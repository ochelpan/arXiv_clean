QCalEval: Benchmarking Vision-Language Models for Quantum Calibration Plot Understanding

Authors: Shuxiang Cao, Zijian Zhang, Abhishek Agarwal, Grace Bratrud, Niyaz R. Beysengulov, Daniel C. Cole, Alejandro Gómez Frieiro, Elena O. Glen, Hao Hsu, Gang Huang, Raymond Jow, Greshma Shaji, Tom Lubowe, Ligeng Zhu, Luis Mantilla Calderón, Nicola Pancotti, Joel Pendleton, Brandon Severin, Charles Etienne Staub, Sara Sussman, Antti Vepsäläinen, Neel Rajeshbhai Vora, Yilun Xu, Varinia Bernales, Daniel Bowring, Elica Kyoseva, Ivan Rungger, Giulia Semeghini, Sam Stanwyck, Timothy Costa, Alán Aspuru-Guzik, Krysta Svore
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25884v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5 · Rydberg arrays 1/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The lack of a systematic evaluation for how well Vision-Language Models (VLMs) can interpret and diagnose quantum calibration plots, which is essential for automating quantum hardware management.

Main result. The study identifies a 'two-step capability gap' where models detect visual features but fail at scientific reasoning, and an 'MM-ICL gap' where many open-weight models degrade under multi-image in-context learning.

Method. The authors introduce QCalEval, a benchmark of 243 samples across 22 experiment families, and evaluate 18 VLMs using zero-shot and in-context learning settings across six task types.

Summary. This paper introduces QCalEval, the first benchmark designed to test if Vision-Language Models can understand quantum calibration plots. It evaluates models on tasks ranging from simple data extraction to complex scientific diagnosis of superconducting and neutral atom systems. The researchers found that while models are good at seeing features, they struggle with high-level reasoning and often exhibit an 'optimistic bias' toward successful results. The work also presents a fine-tuned model, NVIDIA Ising Calibration 1, which shows improved performance in hardware-specific diagnostics.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The benchmark covers various quantum platforms, specifically superconducting qubits (e.g., Rabi, Ramsey, DRAG) and neutral atoms (e.g., Rydberg spectroscopy, MOT loading).

Key observables. Peak locations, fringe spacing, contrast, linewidth, clustering, geometric structures (ridges, hotspots, oscillations, decays), and operational statuses (SUCCESS/FAILURE).

Important parameters / regimes. Transition frequencies (f01), power regimes, T1/T2* relaxation times, and DRAG coefficients.

Assumptions / limitations. The benchmark uses a mix of simulated and real hardware data; the evaluation assumes that LLM-based judges can accurately assess free-text scientific reasoning.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows diverse calibration plots (1D, 2D, scatter); Figure 2 illustrates the zero-shot vs. in-context learning setup; Figure 3 shows task taxonomy using DRAG calibration; Case studies show failures in readout and Ramsey fringes.

Paper structure. Introduction of the calibration automation problem, presentation of the QCalEval benchmark and task taxonomy, evaluation of various VLMs (closed and open-weight), SFT ablation studies, release of the Ising Calibration 1 model, and discussion of limitations.

Abstract

Quantum computing calibration depends on interpreting experimental data, and calibration plots provide the most universal human-readable representation for this task, yet no systematic evaluation exists of how well vision-language models (VLMs) interpret them. We introduce QCalEval, the first VLM benchmark for quantum calibration plots: 243 samples across 87 scenario types from 22 experiment families, spanning superconducting qubits and neutral atoms, evaluated on six question types in both zero-shot and in-context learning settings. The best general-purpose zero-shot model reaches a mean score of 72.3, and many open-weight models degrade under multi-image in-context learning, whereas frontier closed models improve substantially. A supervised fine-tuning ablation at the 9-billion-parameter scale shows that SFT improves zero-shot performance but cannot close the multimodal in-context learning gap. As a reference case study, we release NVIDIA Ising Calibration 1, an open-weight model based on Qwen3.5-35B-A3B that reaches 74.7 zero-shot average score.

Quantum Error Correction Exploiting Quantum Spatial Distribution and Gauge Symmetry

Authors: Ryo Asaka
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25747v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Developing a quantum error correction (QEC) scheme that exploits Quantum Spatial Distribution (QSD) and gauge symmetry to protect against noise affecting both spin and position states.

Main result. The authors prove that gauge symmetry provides resilience against a unified noise model (spin/position decoherence and dephasing) and demonstrate that QSD allows for scalable, space-efficient quantum gates and adders using only nearest-neighbor interactions.

Method. The study utilizes the stabilizer formalism and operator quantum error correction, specifically employing Poulin's stabilizer formalism to implement a subsystem structure with gauge qubits.

Summary. This paper introduces a quantum error correction scheme that utilizes both the spin and position states of particles (Quantum Spatial Distribution). By leveraging gauge symmetry, the authors demonstrate that the system can be made resilient to noise that affects both spin and spatial coherence. The architecture is shown to be highly flexible, allowing for the 'stacking' of systems to implement universal quantum gates and even a quantum adder using only local, short-range interactions. This approach provides a potential path toward space-efficient and scalable quantum computing architectures.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A system of particles arranged on nested squares where each particle possesses both a spin (coin) state and a position (spatial) state, encoding Shor's nine-qubit code through superpositions of these states.

Key observables. Stabilizer generators (s0-s5), logical Pauli operators (X-bar, Z-bar), and gauge transformations.

Important parameters / regimes. Error probabilities (p) for spin-flip, phase-flip, and tunneling/position-related noise; interaction range limited to nearest and next-nearest neighbors.

Assumptions / limitations. Gate operations are assumed to act exclusively on either spin or position states to reduce circuit depth; the error analysis assumes single-event errors do not involve simultaneous Pauli-X operations on both axes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the physical arrangement of particles on nested squares and the concept of gauge symmetry; Figure 2 illustrates the stabilizer measurement scheme; Figure 3 shows a gate-teleportation circuit for the logical Hadamard gate; Figure 4 displays circuits for Toffoli and quantum adder implementations; Figure 5 shows the CX gate procedure and two-layer architecture.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the QSD architecture and encoding, introduces a unified noise model, proves the error-correcting capabilities provided by gauge symmetry, and concludes by demonstrating the implementation of universal quantum gates and a quantum adder.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum computing as it proposes a novel way to use spatial degrees of freedom (positional states) as a resource for error protection and architectural flexibility.

Abstract

We explore what the integrated use of quantum spatial distribution (QSD), or more specifically, superposition of both spin and position states of particles, and gauge symmetry (GS) within stabilizer formalism provides for quantum error correction. The exploration employs $3+2$ particles on nested squares proposed in the companion letter (arXiv:2504.07941), where three of them encode Shor's nine-qubit code and the remaining two detect errors in this code through their spin state measurements (unlike the letter's quantum walk model, each particle evolves by gate operations acting exclusively on either its spin or position state). The first result is that the GS offers resilience against three types of noise acting on a particle: arbitrary decoherence of its spin or position state, and dephasing of both states, which partly or completely destroys its QSD. To show that, we formulate a noise model unifying the above noise and prove the correctability of this unified model under our error-correcting scheme. The second result is that QSD provides architectural flexibility allowing us to stack the error-correcting systems both vertically and horizontally. Indeed, we show implementations of the error detection (stabilizer measurement), logical Hadamard and Toffoli gates, and a quantum adder with the required interactions only between nearest-neighbor and next-nearest-neighbor particles.

Robustness of fiber-optic attenuators to 1061-nm sub-nanosecond pulsed laser radiation in quantum key distribution systems

Authors: Daria Ruzhitskaya, Irina Zhluktova, Anastasiya Ponosova, Fedor Ushakov, Andrey Zverev, Galina Tertyshnikova, Tianyi Xing, Kirill Min'kov, Daniil Trefilov, Anqi Huang, Vladimir Kamynin, Vladimir Tsvetkov, Vadim Makarov
Type: experiment · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25620v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5 · quantum optics experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the vulnerability of fiber-optic attenuators in QKD systems to laser-damage attacks (LDAs) using 1061-nm sub-nanosecond pulsed lasers, which may create hidden side-channels.

Main result. MEMS-based attenuators suffered irreversible damage (up to 3.8 dB reduction), and pulsed irradiation was found to 'prime' absorption-based fixed attenuators, significantly lowering their optical damage threshold for subsequent CW attacks.

Method. Experimental exposure of four types of fiber-optic attenuators to 1061-nm pulsed laser radiation (up to 1 W average power) and monitoring changes in 1550-nm transmission, accompanied by microscopic and spectroscopic analysis.

Summary. This paper investigates how high-power pulsed lasers at 1061 nm can damage or 'prime' optical components in QKD systems. The researchers found that MEMS-based attenuators can suffer permanent damage, and that pulsed light can lower the damage threshold of fixed attenuators for subsequent continuous-wave attacks. These findings reveal a potential hidden side-channel for eavesdropping that existing countermeasures might not address.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An experimental setup involving a 1061-nm pulsed laser (master oscillator and YDFA stages) and a 1550-nm DFB laser simulating QKD signals, testing various attenuators including MEMS-based, mechanical, and fixed-gap/absorption types.

Key observables. Attenuation coefficient changes at 1550 nm, transmission spectra (1061 nm vs 1550 nm), surface temperature, and microscopic damage patterns.

Important parameters / regimes. 1061-nm wavelength, 1550-nm operating wavelength, 1 MHz repetition rate, 260-410 ps pulse duration, and laser average power up to 1030 mW.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the observed attenuation changes (reduction >= 1 dB or increase >= 3 dB) are sufficient to constitute a successful eavesdropping or denial-of-service attack.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Experimental setup schematic; Fig 2: Laser power vs. pulse duration; Fig 3: Transmission spectra comparison; Fig 4: Mechanical VOA photo; Fig 5-7: MEMS VOA attenuation changes and damage; Fig 8: Microscopic damage comparison.

Paper structure. Introduction of the LDA threat, description of the experimental setup and laser parameters, results for different attenuator types (mechanical, MEMS, fixed), analysis of damage mechanisms (carbonization/priming), and conclusions on security implications.
