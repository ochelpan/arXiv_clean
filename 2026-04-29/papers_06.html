Efficient Complex-Valued State Preparation on Bucket Brigade QRAM

Authors: Alessandro Berti, Francesco Ghisoni
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25644v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Efficiently preparing complex-valued quantum states (amplitude and phase encoding) from large classical datasets while minimizing the computational overhead of reversible arithmetic on the QPU.

Main result. The authors present a framework that achieves polylogarithmic BBQRAM query complexity for complex matrices by replacing expensive quantum subroutines with classical precomputation of rotation angles and phases.

Method. A two-step procedure involving magnitude preparation via a cascade of controlled Ry gates and phase encoding via a cascade of controlled P gates, utilizing a precomputed 'complex angle tree' structure.

Summary. This paper improves the efficiency of loading complex-valued classical data into quantum computers using a specific QRAM architecture. By precomputing rotation angles and phases classically, the authors eliminate the need for complex reversible arithmetic (like square roots) on the quantum processor. The resulting method maintains efficient polylogarithmic query time while reducing the required quantum hardware resources. This is a significant step toward practical quantum machine learning and quantum linear algebra applications.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A hardware-aware Bucket Brigade QRAM (BBQRAM) architecture interacting with a Quantum Processing Unit (QPU) using a segment tree data structure.

Key observables. Query complexity, QPU qubit count, gate count, and state fidelity/precision.

Important parameters / regimes. Matrix dimensions (M, N), total entries (K), fixed-point precision (t), and target error (eta).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a fault-tolerant architecture, fixed-point precision, and that classical preprocessing/loading of the QRAM is amortized over repeated preparations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows controlled rotation cascades; Figure 2 depicts the BBQRAM architecture; Figure 3 illustrates the Angle Tree and sign bit integration; Figure 4 shows the complex angle tree structure; Figure 5 shows the BBQRAM mapping for complex entries.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of state preparation, presents an improved architecture-aware framework for complex values, details the classical precomputation and two-step quantum algorithm, provides resource complexity comparisons, and discusses limitations and future work.

Abstract

Efficient quantum state preparation is a critical component in quantum algorithms that process large classical data, and it is fundamental to realizing quantum advantage in domains such as machine learning, quantum linear algebra, and quantum finance. Building on the framework of~\cite{berti2025efficient}, which integrates Bucket Brigade QRAM (BBQRAM) with a segment tree to achieve amplitude encoding in polylogarithmic query time, we present two improvements within the same architecture-aware framework. First, we remove the $U_{2\mathrm{CR}}$ subroutine by classically precomputing the rotation angles determined by the segment tree and storing these angles directly in the BBQRAM cells. The tradeoff is that the classically loaded QRAM stores precomputed fixed-point angles rather than raw subtree weights. Second, we extend the construction to complex-valued matrices $A \in \mathbb{C}^{M \times N}$ by storing a leaf phase alongside each precomputed rotation angle and using a two-step magnitude-then-phase procedure; the real signed case is naturally subsumed as a one-bit phase specialization. At unchanged $\mathcal{O}(\log_2^2(MN))$ BBQRAM query complexity, the QPU procedure reduces to BBQRAM retrievals and controlled-rotation cascades, with $\mathcal{O}(MN)$ memory cells per matrix and no reversible arithmetic on the QPU.

Electrohydrodynamic lubrication theory

Authors: Anirban Chatterjee, Yacine Amarouchene, Thomas Salez
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25350v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Characterizing the electrohydrodynamic motion and the emergence of symmetry-breaking lift forces for a charged cylinder moving near a charged boundary in an electrolyte.

Main result. The development of an electrohydrodynamic lubrication framework that predicts a generalized electroviscous lift force and an extended mobility matrix incorporating surface charges and ions.

Method. The authors use lubrication theory, Debye-Huckel electrostatics, and Nernst-Planck electrokinetics to derive coupled nonlinear ordinary differential equations, which are analyzed via numerical simulations and asymptotic analysis.

Summary. This paper establishes a theoretical framework for understanding how electric charges on particles and surfaces influence their motion in fluids. By combining fluid mechanics with electrokinetics, the authors show that moving ions create an extra 'lift' force that pushes a cylinder away from a wall. The study provides new mathematical tools to predict how particles will move, slide, or rotate in industrial and biological environments where electric fields and ions are present.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An infinite rigid cylinder of radius 'a' moving in a Newtonian electrolyte near a rigid flat wall, considering the coupling of hydrodynamic, electrostatic, and electrokinetic forces.

Key observables. Normal distance (gap height), longitudinal position, rotation, streaming potential, and electroviscous lift force.

Important parameters / regimes. Debye screening length (kappa inverse), Péclet number (Pe), Hartmann number (Ha), surface potentials (zeta potentials), and the lubrication parameter (epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. Small-gap lubrication approximation (epsilon << 1), linearized Poisson-Boltzmann/Debye-Huckel electrostatics, and an open-circuit condition (no net current).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the cylinder, wall, and electrolyte; Figure 4 displays the time evolution of dimensionless distance, longitudinal velocity, and angular velocity showing damped oscillations toward equilibrium.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical problem, defines the mathematical framework (hydrodynamics, electrostatics, electrokinetics), derives the coupled equations of motion, presents numerical results for various motion regimes (sedimentation and sliding), and concludes with asymptotic analysis and validation.

Abstract

The free motion of charged colloids within ionic solutions and in the vicinity of charged boundaries, is a phenomenon that occurs in various natural, biological and industrial settings. Here, we develop an electrohydrodynamic lubrication theoretical framework, in order to characterize such a motion in the case of an infinite rigid cylinder near a rigid wall. Combining hydrodynamic lubrication theory, Debye-H\''uckel electrostatics, and Nernst-Planck electrokinetics, we derive the three coupled equations of motion for the normal, longitudinal and rotational degrees of freedom of the cylinder, which are then investigated numerically and through asymptotic analysis. Our results reveal complex behaviours, beyond existing asymptotic electroviscous-lift expressions, and extend the classical Faxen-Brenner-like mobility matrix when surface charges and dissolved ions are incorporated.

Electronic structures of spin-orbit-coupled metal candidate PbRe$_2$O$_6$: one dimensionality and molecular orbital formation

Authors: Yuki Yanagi, Michi-To Suzuki
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25539v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic origin of the electronic structure, one-dimensionality, and successive structural phase transitions in the spin-orbit-coupled metal candidate PbRe2O6.

Main result. The study identifies that the coexistence of quasi-1D Fermi surfaces (from d_yz and d_zx orbitals) and flat bands (from molecular orbital formation of d_v orbitals) provides a microscopic origin for the material's observed phase transitions and anisotropic transport.

Method. First-principles density functional theory (DFT) calculations using the WIEN2K code, combined with Wannier function construction (Wannier90) and a 36-orbital tight-binding model.

Summary. This paper investigates the electronic properties of the spin-orbit-coupled metal PbRe2O6 using first-principles calculations. It reveals that the material's electronic structure is characterized by both one-dimensional Fermi surfaces and flat bands arising from molecular orbital formation. These features are shown to be responsible for the highly anisotropic charge transport and the successive structural phase transitions observed experimentally. The study provides a microscopic link between orbital physics and macroscopic transport phenomena.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system is PbRe2O6, a 5d-electron compound with a centrosymmetric trigonal structure that undergoes structural phase transitions. The model includes Re-t2g orbitals and describes molecular orbital formation on Re-hexagon networks.

Key observables. Fermi surfaces, density of states (DOS), charge transport anisotropy, and hopping parameters.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin-orbit coupling (SOC) strength, hopping parameters (e.g., |t_v,v(1)/t_v,v(2)| ~ 0.08), and structural transition temperatures (T_s1 ~ 265 K, T_s2 ~ 123 K).

Assumptions / limitations. The molecular orbital picture remains valid even when spin-orbit coupling lifts the degeneracy of the Eg states.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows crystal structures; Fig 2 displays band structures and DOS with and without SOC; Fig 3 shows orbital-weighted Fermi surfaces; Fig 4 illustrates molecular orbital wavefunctions and DOS.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the material and its properties, presents DFT-calculated band structures and DOS, analyzes the orbital-dependent hopping and dimensionality, describes the formation of molecular orbitals, and concludes by linking these electronic features to observed phase transitions.

Abstract

We present a first-principles investigation of the electronic structure of the inversion-symmetry-broken spin-orbit-coupled metal candidate PbRe$_2$O$_6$. Our calculations reveal that the Fermi surfaces derived from the $d_{yz}$ and $d_{zx}$ orbitals exhibit pronounced one-dimensional characteristics, which naturally account for the highly anisotropic charge transport observed experimentally. In addition, the $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbitals on each Re haxagon form molecular orbitals, where the resulting $E_g$ molecular states generate nearly dispersionless bands in close proximity to the Fermi level. The coexistence of these quasi-1D Fermi surfaces and molecular-orbital-induced flat bands provides a possible microscopic origin for the successive phase transitions observed in PbRe$_2$O$_6$.

Elucidating mechanism of optical cavities in superconducting strip single photon detectors using transmission line and impedance models

Authors: Hiroki Kutsuma, Taro Yamashita
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25425v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The physical mechanism by which optical cavities in superconducting strip single photon detectors (SSPDs) achieve maximum photon absorptance is not clearly understood, particularly regarding how different cavity configurations affect performance.

Main result. The study demonstrates that maximum absorptance is achieved through impedance matching between the SSPD and the input medium, and provides analytical formulae for optimal layer thicknesses.

Method. The authors use a transmission line model (transfer matrix formalism) and an impedance model to derive analytical results, which are then validated against numerical simulations using RCWA (S4) and FEM (COMSOL).

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical framework to understand and design optical cavities for superconducting single-photon detectors. By applying transmission line and impedance matching principles, the authors derive analytical expressions for maximizing photon absorption. They show that the optimal performance occurs when the detector's input impedance matches the incident medium. The findings are validated through rigorous numerical simulations and offer a practical design methodology for high-efficiency superconducting detectors.

Detailed structure
