**Main problem.**
Investigating how the competition between the translation velocity of a moving cooling source and the radial propagation velocity of thermal fronts determines the pattern morphology and kinetics of phase separation in binary liquids.

**Main result.**
The study identifies distinct morphological regimes (concentric rings, asymmetric stripes, and leaf-like structures) controlled by the velocity ratio and absolute magnitudes of the source and front velocities, showing that pattern engineering is possible by tuning these scales.

**Method.**
Numerical simulations using a finite-difference method to solve a modified Cahn-Hilliard-Cook framework.

**Summary.**
This paper explores how the motion of a cooling source influences the patterns formed during the phase separation of a binary liquid. By comparing the speed of the moving source to the speed of the spreading cold front, the authors demonstrate that different geometric structures, such as rings or leaf-like domains, can be produced. The findings suggest that one can precisely engineer specific patterns by controlling these two velocity scales. This work provides a framework for understanding non-equilibrium phase separation in driven, inhomogeneous systems.

**Model / system.**
A 2D binary mixture undergoing phase separation, modeled via a Cahn-Hilliard-Cook equation that explicitly couples a time-dependent temperature field (generated by a moving source) with a concentration field.

Order parameter (concentration difference), domain morphology (shape and symmetry), number of stripes (N), domain width (w), and the two-point equal-time correlation function.

**Assumptions / limitations.**
The temperature field is driven by a moving source with radial propagation; the study focuses on the non-equilibrium dynamics of the demixing transition.

**Figures summary.**

**Paper structure.**

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We investigate phase separation dynamics in a binary mixture subjected to a moving cooling source from which cold temperature fronts propagate radially outward into the mixture. The motion of the source introduces two distinct velocity scales: $v_s$ associated with the translation of the source, and $v$ related to the propagation of the cooling thermal fronts. Competition between the two velocities determines how long a region of the fluid experiences a temperature change, which directly controls phase separation. A modified Cahn Hilliard Cook framework is employed, incorporating explicit coupling between the time-dependent temperature and concentration fields. Our numerical simulation results reveal that the evolving patterns and kinetics strongly depend on both the ratio and absolute magnitudes of these two competing velocities. Same value of $v_s/v$ yields distinctly different patterns for different $v$. The temperature profile delineating spatial regions with local temperatures above and below the demixing temperature controls the shape of the patterns formed. The rich parameter space enables one to engineer desired pattern structures by tuning the two velocities.

**Main problem.**
The lack of a systematic evaluation for how well Vision-Language Models (VLMs) can interpret and diagnose quantum calibration plots, which is essential for automating quantum hardware management.

**Main result.**
The study identifies a 'two-step capability gap' where models detect visual features but fail at scientific reasoning, and an 'MM-ICL gap' where many open-weight models degrade under multi-image in-context learning.

**Method.**
The authors introduce QCalEval, a benchmark of 243 samples across 22 experiment families, and evaluate 18 VLMs using zero-shot and in-context learning settings across six task types.

**Summary.**
This paper introduces QCalEval, the first benchmark designed to test if Vision-Language Models can understand quantum calibration plots. It evaluates models on tasks ranging from simple data extraction to complex scientific diagnosis of superconducting and neutral atom systems. The researchers found that while models are good at seeing features, they struggle with high-level reasoning and often exhibit an 'optimistic bias' toward successful results. The work also presents a fine-tuned model, NVIDIA Ising Calibration 1, which shows improved performance in hardware-specific diagnostics.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Quantum computing calibration depends on interpreting experimental data, and calibration plots provide the most universal human-readable representation for this task, yet no systematic evaluation exists of how well vision-language models (VLMs) interpret them. We introduce QCalEval, the first VLM benchmark for quantum calibration plots: 243 samples across 87 scenario types from 22 experiment families, spanning superconducting qubits and neutral atoms, evaluated on six question types in both zero-shot and in-context learning settings. The best general-purpose zero-shot model reaches a mean score of 72.3, and many open-weight models degrade under multi-image in-context learning, whereas frontier closed models improve substantially. A supervised fine-tuning ablation at the 9-billion-parameter scale shows that SFT improves zero-shot performance but cannot close the multimodal in-context learning gap. As a reference case study, we release NVIDIA Ising Calibration 1, an open-weight model based on Qwen3.5-35B-A3B that reaches 74.7 zero-shot average score.

**Main problem.**
Developing a quantum error correction (QEC) scheme that exploits Quantum Spatial Distribution (QSD) and gauge symmetry to protect against noise affecting both spin and position states.

**Main result.**
The authors prove that gauge symmetry provides resilience against a unified noise model (spin/position decoherence and dephasing) and demonstrate that QSD allows for scalable, space-efficient quantum gates and adders using only nearest-neighbor interactions.

**Method.**
The study utilizes the stabilizer formalism and operator quantum error correction, specifically employing Poulin's stabilizer formalism to implement a subsystem structure with gauge qubits.

**Summary.**
This paper introduces a quantum error correction scheme that utilizes both the spin and position states of particles (Quantum Spatial Distribution). By leveraging gauge symmetry, the authors demonstrate that the system can be made resilient to noise that affects both spin and spatial coherence. The architecture is shown to be highly flexible, allowing for the 'stacking' of systems to implement universal quantum gates and even a quantum adder using only local, short-range interactions. This approach provides a potential path toward space-efficient and scalable quantum computing architectures.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We explore what the integrated use of quantum spatial distribution (QSD), or more specifically, superposition of both spin and position states of particles, and gauge symmetry (GS) within stabilizer formalism provides for quantum error correction. The exploration employs $3+2$ particles on nested squares proposed in the companion letter (arXiv:2504.07941), where three of them encode Shor's nine-qubit code and the remaining two detect errors in this code through their spin state measurements (unlike the letter's quantum walk model, each particle evolves by gate operations acting exclusively on either its spin or position state). The first result is that the GS offers resilience against three types of noise acting on a particle: arbitrary decoherence of its spin or position state, and dephasing of both states, which partly or completely destroys its QSD. To show that, we formulate a noise model unifying the above noise and prove the correctability of this unified model under our error-correcting scheme. The second result is that QSD provides architectural flexibility allowing us to stack the error-correcting systems both vertically and horizontally. Indeed, we show implementations of the error detection (stabilizer measurement), logical Hadamard and Toffoli gates, and a quantum adder with the required interactions only between nearest-neighbor and next-nearest-neighbor particles.

**Main problem.**
Investigating the vulnerability of fiber-optic attenuators in QKD systems to laser-damage attacks (LDAs) using 1061-nm sub-nanosecond pulsed lasers, which may create hidden side-channels.

**Main result.**
MEMS-based attenuators suffered irreversible damage (up to 3.8 dB reduction), and pulsed irradiation was found to 'prime' absorption-based fixed attenuators, significantly lowering their optical damage threshold for subsequent CW attacks.

**Method.**
Experimental exposure of four types of fiber-optic attenuators to 1061-nm pulsed laser radiation (up to 1 W average power) and monitoring changes in 1550-nm transmission, accompanied by microscopic and spectroscopic analysis.

**Summary.**
This paper investigates how high-power pulsed lasers at 1061 nm can damage or 'prime' optical components in QKD systems. The researchers found that MEMS-based attenuators can suffer permanent damage, and that pulsed light can lower the damage threshold of fixed attenuators for subsequent continuous-wave attacks. These findings reveal a potential hidden side-channel for eavesdropping that existing countermeasures might not address.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We present a validation of the asdf method, an information-theoretic framework for computing thermodynamic entropy from molecular configurations. The method reformulates entropy estimation as the Shannon entropy of a residual mapping distribution defined between two decorrelated microstates. We demonstrate analytically that for the closed-form Hamiltonians with known solutions, the classical ideal gas and the one-dimensional harmonic oscillator's entropy obtained from the compressibility of the residual mapping object reproduces the exact thermodynamic entropy. In each case, the conditional entropy of the residual mapping object with respect to an uncorrelated microstate is shown to coincide with the ensemble entropy derived from the canonical partition function. These results establish consistency between the asdf formalism and classical statistical mechanics for analytically solvable systems. We further discuss how the framework generalizes to interacting Hamiltonians. The analysis supports the interpretation of thermodynamic entropy as an information measure encoded geometrically in inter-microstate mappings and motivates application of the method to complex condensed phases.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation by learning the score function, the gradient of the log-probability density, on Bohmian trajectories. In Bohm's formulation of quantum mechanics, particles follow deterministic paths under the classical potential supplemented by a quantum potential depending on the score function of the evolving density. These non-crossing Bohmian trajectories form a continuous normalizing flow governed by the score. We parametrize the score with a neural network and minimize a self-consistent Fisher divergence between the network and the score of the resulting density. We prove that the zero-loss minimizer of this self-consistent objective recovers Schrödinger dynamics for nodeless wave functions, a condition naturally met in quantum vibrations of atoms. We demonstrate the approach on wavepacket splitting in a double-well potential and anharmonic vibrations of a Morse chain. By recasting real-time quantum dynamics as a self-consistent score-driven normalizing flow, this framework opens the time-dependent Schrödinger equation to the rapidly advancing toolkit of modern generative modeling.
