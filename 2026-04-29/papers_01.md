**Main problem.**

Measuring entanglement dynamics and the transition from quantum chaos to many-body localization (MBL) in analog quantum simulators that lack universal local gate control.

**Main result.**

The authors demonstrate a randomized measurement protocol using global controls and local detuning to extract Rényi entropy, successfully resolving the transition from linear to logarithmic entanglement growth in a neutral-atom processor.

**Method.**

A modified randomized measurement toolbox using a phi-quench protocol (switching between non-commuting Hamiltonians) to approximate local rotations, combined with numerical simulations to account for decoherence and readout errors.

**Summary.**

This paper presents a new method for measuring entanglement in analog quantum simulators using only global controls and local energy tuning. Using the QuEra Aquila neutral-atom processor, the authors demonstrate that they can observe the transition from a quantum chaotic regime to a many-body localized regime. The protocol bypasses the need for local single-qubit gates by using a sequence of phase quenches. The results show distinct entanglement growth patterns (linear vs. logarithmic) that match numerical predictions even in the presence of noise.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**

A 1D chain of Rydberg atoms (N=6 to N=12) in the QuEra Aquila platform, governed by a long-range interacting transverse-field Ising model with programmable local and global detuning.

**Key observables.**

Second-order Rényi entropy (S2,A), von Neumann entropy (S1,A), subsystem purity, level spacing ratio (r-parameter), and Spectral Form Factor (SFF).

**Figures summary.**

Fig 3: Experimental entanglement growth across low, moderate, and high disorder regimes. Fig 4: Numerical signatures of the transition (level statistics and SFF).

**Paper structure.**

Introduction to entanglement dynamics and MBL; description of the hardware and Hamiltonian; development of the global-to-local randomized measurement protocol; experimental implementation on Aquila; results showing the disorder-induced transition; and error analysis/numerical validation.

**Why it may be interesting.**

It provides a practical way to perform complex many-body measurements (like Rényi entropy) on analog hardware that lacks the high-fidelity local gates required by standard randomized measurement toolboxes, bridging the gap between analog simulation and quantum information protocols.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

The development and spread of entanglement in complex quantum systems is central to exploring many-body phenomena out of equilibrium. Measuring entanglement dynamics can shed light on information scrambling and thermalisation, namely on transitions from many-body quantum chaos to localisation in disordered, interacting systems. In quantum computing systems, entanglement entropy and other nonlinear functions of the density matrix have been recently measured, in particular by using the randomised measurement toolbox. However, it is difficult to implement the required arbitrary unitary rotations on specific subsystems without universal local control. Here we devise and demonstrate the measurement of entanglement entropy in a programmable analogue quantum simulator using a randomised measurement protocol that leverages local energy tuning together with a global field to bypass the need for local gate control. We implement this on a commercially available neutral-atom quantum simulator, QuEra's Aquila, and use it to show how programmable disorder in the local Hamiltonian parameters leads to a transition from chaotic to localised entanglement dynamics. Given current decoherence times, we clearly resolve disorder-specific, time-dependent entanglement spreading in small systems. Our work extends the utility of programmable analogue quantum simulators, and opens further opportunities for wider randomised measurement toolboxes in a range of other analogue systems.

**Main problem.**

The paper addresses the fundamental tension between using coherent driving to minimize noise and maintaining continuous internal symmetries in driven-dissipative quantum systems. It seeks to realize quantum limit cycles with continuous symmetries that are robust and exhibit reduced phase noise.

**Main result.**

The authors identify a class of multi-mode bosonic models with O(N) symmetry that can be exactly solved, demonstrating that coherent parametric driving can yield symmetry-enriched limit cycles with phase noise suppression below the Schawlow-Townes limit.

**Method.**

The study employs a combination of exact analytical solutions via a 2N-mode purification method, semiclassical analysis using Langevin equations, and a mechanical analogy mapping quadrature dynamics to a particle in a potential.

**Summary.**

This paper presents a new class of multi-mode bosonic models that realize quantum limit cycles with continuous O(N) symmetries using coherent parametric driving. By using coherent rather than incoherent driving, the authors show that phase noise can be suppressed below the standard Schawlow-Townes limit. The models are exactly solvable, allowing for a precise study of steady-state entanglement and the emergence of quantum limit tori. The findings are applicable to experimental platforms like superconducting circuits and quantum optics.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**

A multi-mode bosonic system featuring Kerr-type nonlinearities, subject to resonant parametric (two-photon) driving and single-photon loss, compatible with superconducting circuits and quantum optical setups.

**Key observables.**

Steady-state entanglement (log-negativity), phase diffusion coefficient, Fano ratio, photon number, and the existence of quantum limit tori.

**Important parameters / regimes.**

Kerr nonlinearity (u), parametric drive amplitude (D), photon loss rate (kappa), and antisymmetric coupling strength (h).

**Assumptions / limitations.**

The analysis of phase-locking assumes a weak symmetry-breaking term; the semiclassical Fano ratio is valid in the limit cycle phase.

**Figures summary.**

Figures show log-negativity and its ratio to a thermal squeezed state as a function of drive strength, and the behavior of the Fano ratio across different regimes.

**Paper structure.**

The paper introduces the problem of noise-symmetry trade-offs, presents the O(N) symmetric model and its exact solution, performs semiclassical stability and noise analysis, explores higher-dimensional limit tori, and concludes with the effects of symmetry-breaking perturbations.

**Why it may be interesting.**

It provides a unified framework for understanding how coherent driving can achieve high-precision quantum oscillations with reduced phase noise and non-Gaussian entanglement, which is highly relevant for quantum optics and open quantum systems.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

There is widespread interest in many-body quantum systems that exhibit limit-cycle or time-crystalline behaviour. An ideal quantum limit cycle would be realized using fully coherent driving (to minimize noise) and also have a continuous internal symmetry (to ensure generation of monochromatic radiation). While these two requirements may seem incompatible, we introduce in this work a large class of multi-mode bosonic limit cycle models based on coherent parametric driving which possess an O(N) continuous symmetry. Surprisingly, the full quantum dissipative steady state of these models can be found exactly. They exhibit rich physics, including steady state entanglement, reduced phase diffusion and the possibility of realizing quantum limit tori. The basic mechanism we identify provides a unified way to understand how coherent parametric driving can yield symmetry-enriched limit cycles, and also helps us understand related models where the relevant symmetries are weakly broken. The models we study are compatible with a range of different experimental platforms, including quantum optical setups and superconducting quantum circuits.

The difficulty of scaling Quantum Neural Networks (QNNs) due to exponentially large Hilbert spaces, barren plateaus, and measurement noise in the NISQ era.

**Main result.**

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Quantum neural networks generalize classical artificial neural networks into the quantum domain. They are formulated as parameterized quantum circuits which are optimized by measuring and minimizing a suitably chosen loss function. The core challenge in understanding, implementing and ultimately using quantum neural networks is that they represent many-body systems with an exponentially large Hilbert space, in combination with a large parameter search space. Moreover, noise -- which is inherent to any quantum measurement -- sets practical limits for the estimation of training loss. Here, we study physics-informed large-scale quantum neural networks that are trained through a finite number of noisy loss function measurements. We show that this architecture permits the construction of nontrivial decision boundaries that enable the classification of quantum states through measuring an order parameter. Our approach can directly process quantum data that is output from quantum simulators and computers and is well suited for implementation on current hardware. Moreover, owed to a close link between the neural network dynamics and the evolution of Markovian open many-body quantum systems, one may expect a certain robustness to noise, which is ubiquitous in the current NISQ era.

**Main problem.**

The paper addresses the failure of traditional 't Hooft anomaly formulations and spectral flow mechanisms when applied to mixed states, where charge exchange with the environment and state impurity invalidate standard diagnostics.

**Main result.**

The authors derive a universal 'symmetry-charge flow' for Abelian symmetries, which generalizes spectral flow to mixed states by describing how applied fields redistribute statistical weight across symmetry-resolved charge sectors.

**Method.**

The study employs an algebraic approach using flux-insertion operators and large gauge transformations, supplemented by tensor network formalisms (MPS/MPO/PEPS) and K-matrix effective field theories.

**Summary.**

This paper resolves the problem of non-universal anomalies in mixed states by introducing the concept of symmetry-charge flow. Unlike traditional spectral flow, which relies on ground-state level crossings, symmetry-charge flow describes the redistribution of charge sectors in a density matrix under flux insertion. This mechanism is shown to be universal for both bosonic and fermionic systems and is insensitive to temperature or state purity. The work establishes a deep link between mixed-state anomalies and the presence of long-range entanglement.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

The $(1+1)$-dimensional chiral anomaly is a paradigmatic exact result in quantum field theory, traditionally formulated for zero-temperature pure states where it arises from spectral flow induced by external gauge fields and captures universal ground-state properties. In mixed states, however, the participation of many states and charge exchange with the environment invalidate this mechanism. Naive extensions yield model-dependent anomaly coefficients, calling its universality into question. In the dispersive regime, characterized by a large detuning between the transmons and the cavity, the two transmons interact via virtual excitation of the cavity, giving rise to an effective transmon-transmon coupling.
