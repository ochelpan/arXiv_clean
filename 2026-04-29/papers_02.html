Near-identical photons from distant quantum dot-cavity devices

Authors: Thibaut Pollet, Victor Guilloux, Duc-Duy Tran, Anton Pishchagin, Stephen Wein, Joseph A. Sulpizio, William Hease, Petr Stepanov, Petr Steindl, Nico Margaria, Samuel Mister, Martina Morassi, Aristide Lemaître, Thi Huong Au, Sébastien Boissier, Pascale Senellart
Type: experiment · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25615v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · 🔥 quantum optics experiment 4/5 · interference shaping light 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Achieving high-visibility quantum interference (indistinguishability) between single photons emitted from spatially distant and independent semiconductor quantum dot-cavity sources.

Main result. Demonstrated a record mutual indistinguishability of 88 +/- 1% between two distant sources, reaching the upper bound set by the intrinsic performance of the individual emitters.

Method. The researchers used a combination of strain-tuning (via piezo-driven fiber) and electrical tuning (via PIN diode Stark shift) to align wavelengths, followed by Hong-Ou-Mandel interference measurements.

Summary. This paper reports on the successful creation of near-identical photons from two separate, distant quantum dot-cavity devices. By using a dual-tuning approach involving both strain and electric fields, the researchers were able to overcome wavelength mismatches and spectral noise. They achieved a high mutual indistinguishability of 88%, which is nearly the maximum possible value allowed by the individual sources. This achievement is a significant milestone for scaling up photonic quantum technologies and building large-scale quantum networks.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The platform consists of two independent InGaAs quantum dot-cavity micropillars embedded in a PIN diode structure, cooled to 5 K, using longitudinal acoustic (LA) phonon-assisted excitation.

Key observables. Two-photon indistinguishability (M12), Hong-Ou-Mandel visibility (V_HOM), second-order correlation function (g2(0)), and spectral noise/fluctuations.

Important parameters / regimes. Indistinguishability of 88%, cavity Q factor of 8000, charge noise at 4.8% of natural linewidth, and photon collection efficiency between 15.6% and 27.5%.

Assumptions / limitations. The calculation of the theoretical upper bound assumes perfect temporal overlap (s12 = 1) between the two photons.

Figures summary. Fig 1: SEM images and fabrication details showing wavelength/cavity distribution and low charge noise. Fig 2: Principles of phonon-assisted excitation and lifetime/Purcell effect measurements. Fig 3: Extraction of mutual indistinguishability and comparison to theoretical bounds. Extended Data: Fabrication process, g2(0) measurements, and optical setup schematics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of remote interference, describes the fabrication and tuning architecture, presents the characterization of individual source stability, demonstrates the successful interference of distant sources, and concludes with the scalability and future potential of the approach.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum networks as it demonstrates a scalable method to bridge the gap between single-source performance and remote-source interference, a prerequisite for photon-mediated entanglement in quantum computing.

Abstract

Scalable optical quantum technologies require interference between large numbers of indistinguishable single-photons emitted by independent sources. Semiconductor quantum dots are known to be excellent on-demand sources of single-photons. They show record efficiency when inserted into optical cavities to control their spontaneous emission and generate trains of near identical photons over microsecond timescales. However, generating perfectly identical photons from distant cavity-based sources has remained a long-standing challenge. It requires precise matching of the emission wavelengths and emission dynamics, while simultaneously minimizing spectral noise across all time scales for distant emitters in uncorrelated environments. Here, we report on the nanofabrication of a large number of quantum dot-cavity sources with ultra-low spectral noise and wavelength dispersion. The high source efficiency and the use of two tuning mechanisms enable precise optimization of the spectral overlap between distant sources. With this approach, we demonstrate a two-photon indistinguishability of $88\pm1$ % between photons emitted from two distant sources. Remarkably, this value reaches the upper bound set by the intrinsic indistinguishability of photons emitted successively by each source. These results represent a key milestone for scaling photon-based quantum technologies.

Determination of the Fermi Energy of Diamond using Photoluminescence Spectral Analysis

Authors: Yifan Song, Sina Ilkhani, Leah Webb, Helen Highland, Shunki Nakamura, Stephen B. Cronin, Susumu Takahashi
Type: experiment · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25097v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 3/5 · quantum optics experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The difficulty of experimentally determining the Fermi energy in diamond due to the highly non-linear relationship between Fermi energy and donor-acceptor concentrations.

Main result. The development of a non-destructive method to determine the Fermi energy by analyzing the relative populations of NV and SiV charge states via photoluminescence spectroscopy.

Method. The method combines photoluminescence (PL) spectral decomposition with Density Functional Theory (DFT) models to relate charge state populations to the Fermi energy.

Summary. This paper presents a new experimental method to precisely determine the Fermi energy in diamond using photoluminescence spectroscopy. By measuring the ratio of different charge states of NV and SiV centers and applying DFT-based models, the researchers can map the Fermi energy across different diamond types. The study also demonstrates how this energy level affects spin coherence times and charge state stability. This technique is highly advantageous because it offers high spatial and temporal resolution and can function in extreme environments.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system consists of various diamond crystals (CVD, HPHT, and polycrystalline) containing nitrogen-vacancy (NV) and silicon-vacancy (SiV) defect centers.

Key observables. Relative populations of NV- and NV0 centers, spin coherence time (T2), and PL intensity.

Important parameters / regimes. Fermi energy (Ef), nitrogen donor concentration (P1 centers), and laser power dependence.

Assumptions / limitations. The method relies on the accuracy of DFT-derived formation energies and assumes the ability to extrapolate populations to zero laser power to avoid laser-induced charge state conversion.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows energy diagrams and DFT-calculated formation energies; Figure 2 displays PL spectra and the results of spectral decomposition analysis; Figure 3 shows the dependence of parameters on specific variables.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Fermi energy determination, presents the proposed PL-based method using DFT integration, demonstrates the method on various nitrogen-doped and thermal-grade diamond samples, investigates the correlation between Fermi energy and spin coherence (T2), extends the method to SiV centers, and concludes with the method's advantages and potential applications.

Why it may be interesting. This is relevant for quantum information because controlling the charge state stability and spin coherence of NV centers is critical for the development of robust solid-state qubits and quantum sensors.

Abstract

Electronic band structures and the Fermi energy provide essential information for understanding the electronic properties of solids. In semiconductors, the Fermi energy level is determined by the donor and acceptor concentrations. For diamond, the relationship between the Fermi energy level and the donor-acceptor concentrations is highly nonlinear; therefore, experimental determination of the Fermi energy level is important. Here, we report a method to determine the Fermi energy of diamond based on photoluminescence (PL) measurement. The density-functional-theory (DFT) study by Deák et al.~\cite{deak2014formation} showed the relationship between the Fermi energy and the formation energies of nitrogen-vacancy centers in the negatively charged (NV-) and neutrally charged (NV0) charge states. In the present method, we measure the relative populations of the NV- and NV0 centers from PL spectral analysis and, using these populations and the DFT result, determine the Fermi energy of the diamond samples. Moreover, we show the application of the method to study the spin coherence and the stability against the charge state conversion of the NV centers on several diamond samples. We also extend the method for the Fermi energy determination using the silicon-vacancy (SiV) center in diamond. The PL-based method is advantageous for determining the Fermi energy with high spatial and fast time resolutions, even in extreme environments, and can be extended to determine various wide band gap semiconductors.

Nonreciprocal magnon blockade based on nonlinear effects

Authors: Han-Qiu Zhang, Shuang-Shuo Chu, Jian-Song Zhang, Wen-Xue Zhong, Guang-Ling Cheng
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25141v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 spintronics-quantum-optics interface 4/5 · interference shaping light 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Achieving nonreciprocal unconventional magnon blockade (NUMB) in a hybrid cavity-magnon system to prepare single-magnon resources.

Main result. The study demonstrates that nonreciprocity can be achieved by adjusting the sign of the Kerr nonlinearity, providing a method to induce magnon blockade through either destructive interference or anharmonic energy splitting.

Method. The authors use adiabatic elimination of the signal cavity mode, a rotating reference frame, and the probability amplitude method to find analytical solutions, validated by numerical simulations of the master equation.

Summary. This paper proposes a scheme to achieve nonreciprocal magnon blockade in a hybrid system of two microwave cavities and a YIG sphere. By utilizing nonlinear effects like Kerr nonlinearity and parametric driving, the researchers show how to control the magnon blockade effect. The study identifies two distinct regimes: unconventional blockade via destructive interference and conventional blockade via energy splitting. This work offers a new method for generating single-magnon resources essential for quantum information tasks.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A hybrid system consisting of two microwave cavities (pump and signal) and one Yttrium Iron Garnet (YIG) sphere, featuring nonlinear photon-photon coupling, magnetic dipole interaction, and Kerr nonlinearity.

Key observables. The second-order correlation function g(2) of the magnon mode, used to identify magnon bunching, antibunching, and perfect blockade.

Important parameters / regimes. Kerr nonlinearity (chi), coupling strengths (g, J, g_ms), detunings (Delta_p, Delta_m, Delta_s), and decay rates (kappa, gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The signal cavity mode is eliminated using second-order perturbation theory under the condition that its detuning is much larger than the coupling strengths.

Figures summary. Figure 2 plots the log of the second-order correlation function against detuning, showing the transition between weak and strong coupling regimes and identifying specific blockade frequency conditions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical platform, derives an effective Hamiltonian via adiabatic elimination, presents analytical solutions for the blockade conditions, and uses numerical simulations to demonstrate nonreciprocity and the transition between different blockade regimes.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel mechanism for controlling quantum interference and nonreciprocity in hybrid systems, which is highly relevant for developing single-photon/magnon sources and quantum information processing.

Abstract

We present an alternative scheme to achieve nonreciprocal unconventional magnon blockade (NUMB) in a hybrid system formed by two microwave cavities and one yttrium iron garnet (YIG) sphere, where the pump and signal cavities interact nonlinearly with each other and the signal cavity is coupled to the YIG sphere.
