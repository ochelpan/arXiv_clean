Control of relaxation properties of a macroscopic nuclear spin ensemble

Authors: János Ádám, Andrew J. Winter, Deniz Aybas, Dmitry Budker, Derek F. Jackson Kimball, Arne Wickenbrock, Alexander O. Sushkov
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25548v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 3/5 · QC/QI experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Controlling the nuclear spin population relaxation time (T1) in macroscopic spin ensembles at cryogenic temperatures, where phonon freeze-out leads to prohibitively long relaxation times.

Main result. Demonstrated that 405 nm laser illumination can optically control the 207Pb nuclear T1 relaxation time by creating transient paramagnetic centers (Pb3+ and Ti3+) in ferroelectric crystals.

Method. Used X-band Electron Paramagnetic Resonance (EPR) spectroscopy and saturation-recovery Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) at 10 K under 405 nm laser illumination.

Summary. This paper demonstrates a method to optically control the nuclear spin relaxation time in lead-based ferroelectric crystals. By using a 405 nm laser, the researchers induce paramagnetic centers that act as a spin bath, effectively shortening the long T1 times caused by cryogenic temperatures. This technique allows for faster thermal polarization and dynamic nuclear polarization. Such control is a critical step toward improving the sensitivity of NMR-based searches for dark matter, such as axion-like particles.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The system consists of 207Pb nuclear spin ensembles in lead-containing ferroelectric crystals (PbTiO3 and PMN-PT). The model relates the nuclear relaxation rate to the density of photoinduced paramagnetic centers using a Zeeman and hyperfine interaction Hamiltonian.

Key observables. EPR spectra (voltage signal), g-factors, linewidths, spin number densities, and nuclear T1 relaxation times.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature of 10 K; 405 nm laser wavelength; spin densities of approximately 10^17 cm^-3; reduction of T1 from 1550 s to 850 s at 40 MHz.

Assumptions / limitations. Isotropic hyperfine interaction assumption for certain centers; powder-like orientational averaging for Ti3+ due to lattice disorder; treating unresolved hyperfine structure as Gaussian broadening.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup; Figure 2 shows the EPR spectrum of PT; Figure 7 shows the spatial dependence of the microwave field along the cavity axis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of long T1 times, describes the experimental setup and materials, details the EPR characterization of paramagnetic centers, presents NMR results for T1 control, and concludes with a model relating relaxation to spin density.

Why it may be interesting. The ability to optically manipulate relaxation rates in a solid-state spin bath is highly relevant to open quantum systems and the development of efficient initialization protocols for quantum sensing and precision metrology.

Abstract

Macroscopic spin ensembles in solids are powerful platforms for quantum sensing and precision metrology. A key challenge is controlling the nuclear spin population relaxation time $T_1$, which can become prohibitively long at cryogenic temperatures due to phonon freeze-out. We demonstrate optical control of the $T_1$ relaxation time of the $^{207}$Pb nuclear spin ensemble in lead-containing ferroelectric crystals PbTiO$_3$ (PT) and (PbMg$_{1/3}$Nb$_{2/3}$O$_3$)$_{2/3}$-(PbTiO$_3$)$_{1/3}$ (PMN-PT). Using X-band electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy at 10 K, we characterize light-induced paramagnetic centers created by 405 nm laser illumination. In PT, we observe paramagnetic Pb$^{3+}$ centers and their hyperfine interaction with nearby nuclear spins. In PMN-PT, we identify two populations: isotropic Pb$^{3+}$ centers and anisotropic Ti$^{3+}$ centers occupying $d$-orbitals, with spin number densities of $(2.5 \pm 1.0) \times 10^{17}$ cm$^{-3}$ and $(4.1 \pm 1.7) \times 10^{17}$ cm$^{-3}$, respectively. Power-dependent EPR measurements enable extraction of spin relaxation times. We investigate the ionization and recombination dynamics of these transient paramagnetic centers. Using saturation-recovery nuclear magnetic resonance, we demonstrate that laser illumination reduces the $^{207}$Pb nuclear $T_1$ by approximately a factor of two, from $(17 \pm 2)$ s to $(7 \pm 1)$ s at 4.6 MHz, and from $(1550 \pm 40)$ s to $(850 \pm 70)$ s at 40 MHz. We develop a model relating the nuclear relaxation rate to the density of photoinduced paramagnetic centers. This optical control of nuclear spin relaxation provides a pathway toward accelerated thermal polarization and dynamic nuclear polarization in solid-state NMR-based precision measurements, including searches for axion-like dark matter.

From Ultrafast Demagnetization to Ultrafast Spintronics : a 30 years story

Authors: Quentin Remy, Stéphane Mangin
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25431v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic mechanisms of angular momentum transfer and redistribution in magnetic systems during ultrafast (femtosecond to picosecond) demagnetization and its evolution into ultrafast spintronic applications.

Main result. The paper traces the 30-year evolution from observing sub-picosecond magnetization quenching to developing deterministic all-optical switching and ultrafast spin-injection devices, highlighting that angular momentum is redistributed among spin, orbital, and lattice reservoirs rather than destroyed.

Method. A review of experimental techniques including femtosecond pump-probe spectroscopy, MOKE, THz emission, and XMCD, alongside theoretical frameworks like the three-temperature model, TDDFT, and superdiffusive transport models.

Summary. This review paper provides a comprehensive 30-year history of the field of ultrafast magnetism. It explains how the discovery of sub-picosecond magnetization quenching led to new ways of controlling spin via light. The authors detail the transition from fundamental studies of spin-lattice relaxation to the engineering of ultrafast spintronic devices like all-optical switches. The work highlights the complex interplay between electrons, spins, and phonons in non-equilibrium states. Ultimately, it outlines the potential for high-speed, energy-efficient information processing using femtosecond-scale magnetic control.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The review covers various magnetic systems including ferromagnets (Ni, Fe), rare-earth transition-metal (RE-TM) ferrimagnets (GdFeCo), and heterostructures like spin valves and magnetic tunnel junctions.

Key observables. Magnetization dynamics (quenching and recovery), spin currents, spin accumulation, THz radiation, and magnon/electron/phonon temperatures.

Important parameters / regimes. Femtosecond to picosecond timescales, femtojoule energy scales, spin-orbit coupling, spin-mixing conductance, and non-equilibrium regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The review discusses the limitations of the three-temperature model, noting it fails to capture certain non-equilibrium features like magnon generation without demagnetization.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Ni magnetization quenching via MOKE; Figure 2 illustrates the three-temperature model; Figure 3 depicts superdiffusive spin transport; Figure 4 shows THz emission from bilayers; Figure 6 compares experimental data.

Paper structure. The paper follows a historical and thematic progression: starting with the discovery of ultrafast demagnetization, moving to the identification of microscopic angular momentum transfer mechanisms, then to the development of all-optical switching (AOS), and finally to the integration of these phenomena into ultrafast spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. The study of rapid angular momentum redistribution between coupled reservoirs (spin, orbital, and lattice) and the dynamics of strongly out-of-equilibrium quantum materials provides insights relevant to open quantum systems and non-equilibrium many-body dynamics.

Abstract

The discovery of femtosecond laser-induced ultrafast demagnetization in 1996 opened a new field, femtomagnetism, in which magnetic order can be quenched on timescales shorter than a picosecond. This seminal observation revealed that angular momentum can be transferred out of the spin system with unprecedented speed, launching intense efforts to disentangle the roles of electrons, phonons, and spins in the non-equilibrium regime. Soon it became evident that ultrafast demagnetization generates spin-flips, spin polarization, magnons and spin currents, providing new channels for angular-momentum flow. These insights laid the foundation for linking femtomagnetism with spintronics. An emblematic breakthrough in this evolution is the helicity-independent single-pulse all-optical switching (AOS) observed in rare-earth transition-metal (RE-TM) ferrimagnets such as GdFeCo. This mechanism, operating at femtojoule-scale energies and without external magnetic fields, establishes RE-TM alloys as benchmark systems for understanding and exploiting angular-momentum flow at the femtosecond timescale. Building on these concepts, the combination of ultrafast optical excitation with spintronic devices has demonstrated deterministic magnetization reversal driven by femtosecond pulses in spin valves and tunnel junctions, including rare-earth-free systems. Ultrafast spin injection, acting analogously to spin transfer torque but operating three orders of magnitude faster, allows reversal of both ferromagnetic and ferrimagnetic layers. By enabling ultrafast and energy-efficient switching, ultrafast spintronics promises scalable technologies for high-speed information processing while raising fundamental questions about angular momentum transfer in strongly out-of-equilibrium quantum materials.

Nonlocal Cooper pairs in finite topological superconductors and their relation to Majorana nonlocality

Authors: Hiroto Mizoguchi, Yutaro Nagae, Yasuhiro Asano, Satoshi Ikegaya
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25169v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates the manifestation of Majorana nonlocality through the lens of pair correlations (anomalous Green's functions) in finite topological superconductors, addressing the gap between quasiparticle-based and pair-correlation-based perspectives.

Main result. The authors identify that in the low-frequency regime, normal and anomalous Green's functions become identical up to a phase factor and exhibit pronounced nonlocality, where nonlocal correlations grow exponentially with system length while local correlations vanish.

Method. The study employs an analytical derivation of the Matsubara Gor'kov Green's function using the Dyson equation and the hard-wall limit, which is then verified numerically using the recursive Green's function method.

Summary. This paper explores how Majorana nonlocality is reflected in the pair correlations of finite topological superconductors. The authors show that at low energies, the single-particle and Cooper-pair correlations become essentially identical and exhibit a striking nonlocality where correlations between opposite ends of the system grow exponentially with length. This discovery links the unusual transport properties, such as positive noise cross-correlation, directly to the emergence of unconventional nonlocal Cooper pairs. These findings advance the understanding of Majorana-based states for potential use in topological quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The system is a finite one-dimensional Kitaev chain model representing topological superconductors, such as semiconductor-superconductor hybrid nanowires or magnetic atom chains.
