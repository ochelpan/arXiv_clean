**Main problem.**

The weak magnon-phonon coupling in conventional bulk platforms like YIG spheres limits the realization of efficient, coherent magnomechanical systems for hybrid information processing.

**Main result.**

Suspended 2D van der Waals ferromagnetic membranes, such as CrGeTe3, exhibit magnomechanical coupling rates up to three orders of magnitude higher than YIG spheres and are highly tunable via electrostatic gating.

**Method.**

The authors use a theoretical framework involving the Holstein-Primakoff transformation and linearized magnetoelastic interactions, supplemented by COMSOL Multiphysics simulations to model electrostatic-induced deformation.

**Summary.**

This paper proposes using suspended 2D van der Waals ferromagnets as a new platform for magnomechanics. By utilizing the large magnetoelastic coupling and the ability to induce pre-strain via electrostatic gating, the authors demonstrate coupling rates significantly higher than those found in traditional YIG-based systems. The study shows that the coupling strength can be tuned by orders of magnitude through voltage-induced deformation. This makes these membranes a robust candidate for developing chip-scale hybrid quantum information processing devices.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**

A suspended, doubly-clamped 2D van der Waals ferromagnetic membrane (specifically CrGeTe3) with in-plane magnetization, subject to out-of-plane mechanical oscillations and electrostatic pre-strain.

**Key observables.**

Single magnon-phonon coupling rate (g0), mechanical eigenfrequency, cooperativity (C), and static deformation (h).

**Important parameters / regimes.**

Magnon frequencies in the GHz scale, mechanical frequencies in the 10-100 MHz range, coupling rates in the Hz to kHz range, and pre-strain scaling linearly with coupling.

**Assumptions / limitations.**

The model assumes the thin membrane limit (Lz << Ly < Lx), focuses on the Kittel mode, neglects higher-order strain components, and assumes small displacement amplitudes.

**Figures summary.**

Figure 1 shows the 3D and 2D geometry of the membrane; Figure 2 illustrates ferromagnetic resonance and spin alignment; Figure 4 shows coupling vs. pre-strain; Figure 5 compares COMSOL simulations to analytical models for frequency and coupling strength.

**Paper structure.**

The paper introduces the problem of weak coupling in bulk systems, proposes the 2D vdW membrane platform, derives the analytical coupling mechanism and scaling laws, validates the model with numerical simulations of electrostatic tuning, and concludes with the potential for hybrid quantum technologies.

**Why it may be interesting.**

The platform offers a highly tunable, high-cooperativity interface between magnons and phonons, which is a key building block for hybrid quantum transducers and quantum memory in quantum networks.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Magnomechanical systems provide a promising route for exploring coherent hybrid magnon-phonon interactions and hybrid information processing, but their realization has so far been limited by weak magnon-phonon coupling in conventional bulk platforms. We show that a suspended membrane of a two-dimensional van der Waals ferromagnet with in-plane magnetization and out-of-plane mechanical oscillations exhibits large magnomechanical coupling dominated by magnetoelastic interactions. The parametric single magnon-phonon coupling rate scales linearly with pre-strain and can reach hundreds of Hertz to low kiloHertz in suspended membranes of van der Waals ferromagnets such as CrGeTe_3 under experimentally realistic conditions. This rate exceeds typical values reported for YIG spheres by more than three orders of magnitude. Our results demonstrate that suspended membranes of van der Waals magnets provide a robust and highly tunable platform for magnomechanics.

**Main problem.**

Controlling the nuclear spin population relaxation time (T1) in macroscopic spin ensembles at cryogenic temperatures, where phonon freeze-out leads to prohibitively long relaxation times.

**Main result.**

Demonstrated that 405 nm laser illumination can optically control the 207Pb nuclear T1 relaxation time by creating transient paramagnetic centers (Pb3+ and Ti3+) in ferroelectric crystals.

**Method.**

Used X-band Electron Paramagnetic Resonance (EPR) spectroscopy and saturation-recovery Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) at 10 K under 405 nm laser illumination.

**Summary.**

This paper demonstrates a method to optically control the nuclear spin relaxation time in lead-based ferroelectric crystals. By using a 405 nm laser, the researchers induce paramagnetic centers that act as a spin bath, effectively shortening the long T1 times caused by cryogenic temperatures. This technique allows for faster thermal polarization and dynamic nuclear polarization. Such control is a critical step toward improving the sensitivity of NMR-based searches for dark matter, such as axion-like particles.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**

The system consists of 207Pb nuclear spin ensembles in lead-containing ferroelectric crystals (PbTiO3 and PMN-PT). The model relates the nuclear relaxation rate to the density of photoinduced paramagnetic centers using a Zeeman and hyperfine interaction Hamiltonian.

**Key observables.**

EPR spectra (voltage signal), g-factors, linewidths, spin number densities, and nuclear T1 relaxation times.

**Important parameters / regimes.**

Temperature of 10 K; 405 nm laser wavelength; spin densities of approximately 10^17 cm^-3; reduction of T1 from 1550 s to 850 s at 40 MHz.

**Assumptions / limitations.**

Isotropic hyperfine interaction assumption for certain centers; powder-like orientational averaging for Ti3+ due to lattice disorder; treating unresolved hyperfine structure as Gaussian broadening.

**Figures summary.**

**Paper structure.**

The paper introduces the problem of long T1 times, describes the experimental setup and materials, details the EPR characterization of paramagnetic centers, presents NMR results for T1 control, and concludes with a model relating relaxation to spin density.

**Why it may be interesting.**

The ability to optically manipulate relaxation rates in a solid-state spin bath is highly relevant to open quantum systems and the development of efficient initialization protocols for quantum sensing and precision metrology.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Macroscopic spin ensembles in solids are powerful platforms for quantum sensing and precision metrology. A key challenge is controlling the nuclear spin population relaxation time $T_1$, which can become prohibitively long at cryogenic temperatures due to phonon freeze-out. We demonstrate optical control of the $T_1$ relaxation time of the $^{207}$Pb nuclear spin ensemble in lead-containing ferroelectric crystals PbTiO$_3$ (PT) and (PbMg$_{1/3}$Nb$_{2/3}$O$_3$)$_{2/3}$-(PbTiO$_3$)$_{1/3}$ (PMN-PT). Using X-band electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy at 10 K, we characterize light-induced paramagnetic centers created by 405 nm laser illumination. In PT, we observe paramagnetic Pb$^{3+}$ centers and their hyperfine interaction with nearby nuclear spins. In PMN-PT, we identify two populations: isotropic Pb$^{3+}$ centers and anisotropic Ti$^{3+}$ centers occupying $d$-orbitals, with spin number densities of $(2.5 \pm 1.0) \times 10^{17}$ cm$^{-3}$ and $(4.1 \pm 1.7) \times 10^{17}$ cm$^{-3}$, respectively. Power-dependent EPR measurements enable extraction of spin relaxation times. We investigate the ionization and recombination dynamics of these transient paramagnetic centers. Using saturation-recovery nuclear magnetic resonance, we demonstrate that laser illumination reduces the $^{207}$Pb nuclear $T_1$ by approximately a factor of two, from $(17 \pm 2)$ s to $(7 \pm 1)$ s at 4.6 MHz, and from $(1550 \pm 40)$ s to $(850 \pm 70)$ s at 40 MHz. We develop a model relating the nuclear relaxation rate to the density of photoinduced paramagnetic centers. This optical control of nuclear spin relaxation provides a pathway toward accelerated thermal polarization and dynamic nuclear polarization in solid-state NMR-based precision measurements, including searches for axion-like dark matter.

**Main problem.**

Understanding the microscopic mechanisms of angular momentum transfer and redistribution in magnetic systems during ultrafast (femtosecond to picosecond) demagnetization and its evolution into ultrafast spintronic applications.

**Main result.**

**Method.**

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

The discovery of femtosecond laser-induced ultrafast demagnetization in 1996 opened a new field, femtomagnetism, in which magnetic order can be quenched on timescales shorter than a picosecond. This seminal observation revealed that angular momentum can be transferred out of the spin system with unprecedented speed, launching intense efforts to disentangle the roles of electrons, phonons, and spins in the non-equilibrium regime. Soon it became evident that ultrafast demagnetization generates spin-flips, spin polarization, magnons and spin currents, providing new channels for angular-momentum flow. These insights laid the foundation for linking femtomagnetism with spintronics. An emblematic breakthrough in this evolution is the helicity-independent single-pulse all-optical switching (AOS) observed in rare-earth transition-metal (RE-TM) ferrimagnets such as GdFeCo. This mechanism, operating at femtojoule-scale energies and without external magnetic fields, establishes RE-TM alloys as benchmark systems for understanding and exploiting angular-momentum flow at the femtosecond timescale. Building on these concepts, the combination of ultrafast optical excitation with spintronic devices has demonstrated deterministic magnetization reversal driven by femtosecond pulses in spin valves and tunnel junctions, including rare-earth-free systems. Ultrafast spin injection, acting analogously to spin transfer torque but operating three orders of magnitude faster, allows reversal of both ferromagnetic and ferrimagnetic layers. By enabling ultrafast and energy-efficient switching, ultrafast spintronics promises scalable technologies for high-speed information processing while raising fundamental questions about angular momentum transfer in strongly out-of-equilibrium quantum materials.

**Main problem.**

The paper investigates the manifestation of Majorana nonlocality through the lens of pair correlations (anomalous Green's functions) in finite topological superconductors, addressing the gap between quasiparticle-based and pair-correlation-based perspectives.

**Main result.**

The authors identify that in the low-frequency regime, normal and anomalous Green's functions become identical up to a phase factor and exhibit pronounced nonlocality, where nonlocal correlations grow exponentially with system length while local correlations vanish.

**Method.**

The study employs an analytical derivation of the Matsubara Gor'kov Green's function using the Dyson equation and the hard-wall limit, which is then verified numerically using the recursive Green's function method.

**Summary.**

This paper explores how Majorana nonlocality is reflected in the pair correlations of finite topological superconductors. The authors show that at low energies, the single-particle and Cooper-pair correlations become essentially identical and exhibit a striking nonlocality where correlations between opposite ends of the system grow exponentially with length. This discovery links the unusual transport properties, such as positive noise cross-correlation, directly to the emergence of unconventional nonlocal Cooper pairs. These findings advance the understanding of Majorana-based states for potential use in topological quantum computing.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We identify two fundamental properties of the Gor'kov Green's function of finite one-dimensional topological superconductors. <details><summary>Abstract</summary>

**Main result.**

**Method.**

**Summary.**

This paper demonstrates that energy deposition in silicon can be precisely tuned using two-color laser pulses. By adjusting the wavelength and the order of the pulses, one can switch between mechanisms like multiphoton absorption and intraband acceleration. <details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We theoretically show that energy absorption in crystalline silicon can be controlled by two-color femtosecond double-pulse irradiation, in which two temporally separated pulses with different wavelengths interact sequentially with the system. Using time-dependent density functional theory, we systematically examine the wavelength and intensity dependence of the absorbed energy over peak intensities of $2\times10^{11}$-$10^{13}$ W/cm$^2$ and wavelengths of 515, 1030, and 2060 nm. We find that the mechanism governing energy absorption and the optimal wavelength combination strongly depend on the intensity regime. In the low-intensity regime, multiphoton interband absorption is dominant, and energy absorption is enhanced for pulse pairs composed of shorter wavelengths. In contrast, in the high-intensity regime, the contributions of tunneling ionization and intraband acceleration become significant, leading to enhanced absorption for longer-wavelength combinations. In the intermediate-intensity regime, a pronounced enhancement is observed when a short-wavelength pulse precedes a long-wavelength pulse. Our analysis reveals that the nonequilibrium electronic state prepared by the first pulse modifies the excitation process induced by the second pulse, thereby enhancing the absorbed energy through an increased energy gain per excited electron. In this regime, the energy absorption is governed not only by the number of excited carriers but also by the energy gain per excited electron, which can be strongly modified by the pulse sequence. These results indicate that ultrafast energy transfer in semiconductors is tunable by appropriately designing the wavelength and intensity combination of the two pulses, and provide microscopic insight into two-color strong-field excitation.
