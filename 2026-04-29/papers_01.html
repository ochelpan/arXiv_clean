⭐ Randomised measurements of a disorder-induced entanglement transition in a neutral atom quantum processor

Highlighted author(s): Andrew J. Daley
Authors: Apollonas S. Matsoukas-Roubeas, Oscar Scholin, Lucas Sá, Arinjoy De, Majd Hamdan, Alexei Bylinskii, Andrew J. Daley, Dorian A. Gangloff
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24854v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 Rydberg arrays 5/5 · 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · 🔥 analog quantum simulation 4/5 · 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · quantum measurements 3/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 2/5 · measurement-induced transitions 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5 · scars & prethermalization 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Measuring entanglement dynamics and the transition from quantum chaos to many-body localization (MBL) in analog quantum simulators that lack universal local gate control.

Main result. The authors demonstrate a randomized measurement protocol using global controls and local detuning to extract Rényi entropy, successfully resolving the transition from linear to logarithmic entanglement growth in a neutral-atom processor.

Method. A modified randomized measurement toolbox using a phi-quench protocol (switching between non-commuting Hamiltonians) to approximate local rotations, combined with numerical simulations to account for decoherence and readout errors.

Summary. This paper presents a new method for measuring entanglement in analog quantum simulators using only global controls and local energy tuning. Using the QuEra Aquila neutral-atom processor, the authors demonstrate that they can observe the transition from a quantum chaotic regime to a many-body localized regime. The protocol bypasses the need for local single-qubit gates by using a sequence of phase quenches. The results show distinct entanglement growth patterns (linear vs. logarithmic) that match numerical predictions even in the presence of noise.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 1D chain of Rydberg atoms (N=6 to N=12) in the QuEra Aquila platform, governed by a long-range interacting transverse-field Ising model with programmable local and global detuning.

Key observables. Second-order Rényi entropy (S2,A), von Neumann entropy (S1,A), subsystem purity, level spacing ratio (r-parameter), and Spectral Form Factor (SFF).

Important parameters / regimes. Rabi frequency (Omega), local detuning (Delta_local), global detuning (Delta_global), and interaction strength (J).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is small-scale (N=6) to stay within coherence times; the protocol assumes the phi-quench sequence effectively approximates a 2-design.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of the atom array and Hamiltonian parameters. Fig 2: Validation of the randomized rotation protocol via Bloch vector trajectories. Fig 3: Experimental entanglement growth across low, moderate, and high disorder regimes. Fig 4: Numerical signatures of the transition (level statistics and SFF).

Paper structure. Introduction to entanglement dynamics and MBL; description of the hardware and Hamiltonian; development of the global-to-local randomized measurement protocol; experimental implementation on Aquila; results showing the disorder-induced transition; and error analysis/numerical validation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical way to perform complex many-body measurements (like Rényi entropy) on analog hardware that lacks the high-fidelity local gates required by standard randomized measurement toolboxes, bridging the gap between analog simulation and quantum information protocols.

Abstract

The development and spread of entanglement in complex quantum systems is central to exploring many-body phenomena out of equilibrium. Measuring entanglement dynamics can shed light on information scrambling and thermalisation, namely on transitions from many-body quantum chaos to localisation in disordered, interacting systems. In quantum computing systems, entanglement entropy and other nonlinear functions of the density matrix have been recently measured, in particular by using the randomised measurement toolbox. However, it is difficult to implement the required arbitrary unitary rotations on specific subsystems without universal local control. Here we devise and demonstrate the measurement of entanglement entropy in a programmable analogue quantum simulator using a randomised measurement protocol that leverages local energy tuning together with a global field to bypass the need for local gate control. We implement this on a commercially available neutral-atom quantum simulator, QuEra's Aquila, and use it to show how programmable disorder in the local Hamiltonian parameters leads to a transition from chaotic to localised entanglement dynamics. Given current decoherence times, we clearly resolve disorder-specific, time-dependent entanglement spreading in small systems. Our work extends the utility of programmable analogue quantum simulators, and opens further opportunities for wider randomised measurement toolboxes in a range of other analogue systems.

⭐ Quantum limit cycles with continuous symmetries from coherent parametric driving: exact solutions and many-body extensions

Highlighted author(s): Aashish A. Clerk
Authors: Sihan Chen, Aashish A. Clerk
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.25864v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 driven-dissipative phase transition 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper addresses the fundamental tension between using coherent driving to minimize noise and maintaining continuous internal symmetries in driven-dissipative quantum systems. It seeks to realize quantum limit cycles with continuous symmetries that are robust and exhibit reduced phase noise.

Main result. The authors identify a class of multi-mode bosonic models with O(N) symmetry that can be exactly solved, demonstrating that coherent parametric driving can yield symmetry-enriched limit cycles with phase noise suppression below the Schawlow-Townes limit.

Method. The study employs a combination of exact analytical solutions via a 2N-mode purification method, semiclassical analysis using Langevin equations, and a mechanical analogy mapping quadrature dynamics to a particle in a potential.

Summary. This paper presents a new class of multi-mode bosonic models that realize quantum limit cycles with continuous O(N) symmetries using coherent parametric driving. By using coherent rather than incoherent driving, the authors show that phase noise can be suppressed below the standard Schawlow-Townes limit. The models are exactly solvable, allowing for a precise study of steady-state entanglement and the emergence of quantum limit tori. The findings are applicable to experimental platforms like superconducting circuits and quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A multi-mode bosonic system featuring Kerr-type nonlinearities, subject to resonant parametric (two-photon) driving and single-photon loss, compatible with superconducting circuits and quantum optical setups.

Key observables. Steady-state entanglement (log-negativity), phase diffusion coefficient, Fano ratio, photon number, and the existence of quantum limit tori.

Important parameters / regimes. Kerr nonlinearity (u), parametric drive amplitude (D), photon loss rate (kappa), and antisymmetric coupling strength (h).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis of phase-locking assumes a weak symmetry-breaking term; the semiclassical Fano ratio is valid in the limit cycle phase.

Figures summary. Figures show log-negativity and its ratio to a thermal squeezed state as a function of drive strength, and the behavior of the Fano ratio across different regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of noise-symmetry trade-offs, presents the O(N) symmetric model and its exact solution, performs semiclassical stability and noise analysis, explores higher-dimensional limit tori, and concludes with the effects of symmetry-breaking perturbations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified framework for understanding how coherent driving can achieve high-precision quantum oscillations with reduced phase noise and non-Gaussian entanglement, which is highly relevant for quantum optics and open quantum systems.

Abstract

There is widespread interest in many-body quantum systems that exhibit limit-cycle or time-crystalline behaviour. An ideal quantum limit cycle would be realized using fully coherent driving (to minimize noise) and also have a continuous internal symmetry (to ensure generation of monochromatic radiation). While these two requirements may seem incompatible, we introduce in this work a large class of multi-mode bosonic limit cycle models based on coherent parametric driving which possess an O(N) continuous symmetry. Surprisingly, the full quantum dissipative steady state of these models can be found exactly. They exhibit rich physics, including steady state entanglement, reduced phase diffusion and the possibility of realizing quantum limit tori. The basic mechanism we identify provides a unified way to understand how coherent parametric driving can yield symmetry-enriched limit cycles, and also helps us understand related models where the relevant symmetries are weakly broken. The models we study are compatible with a range of different experimental platforms, including quantum optical setups and superconducting quantum circuits.

⭐ Getting large-scale quantum neural networks ready for quantum hardware

Highlighted author(s): Igor Lesanovsky
Authors: Mario Boneberg, Simon Kochsiek, Igor Lesanovsky
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24886v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The difficulty of scaling Quantum Neural Networks (QNNs) due to exponentially large Hilbert spaces, barren plateaus, and measurement noise in the NISQ era.

Main result. The development of physics-informed QNNs that use dissipative many-body dynamics to successfully classify quantum states and reduce the complexity of required observables.

Method. Tensor network simulations (MPS and MPO) are used to train the network via a Nadam stochastic gradient descent variant using a contrastive loss function.

Summary. This paper proposes a new class of physics-informed quantum neural networks designed for large-scale hardware. By mimicking the dissipative dynamics of open many-body systems, these networks can learn to classify complex quantum states using only a single observable. The approach effectively addresses the challenges of barren plateaus and measurement noise. Simulations on systems up to 550 qubits demonstrate that the network can reduce the complexity of tasks by mapping multi-observable problems into a single-observable measurement.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A large-scale layered quantum many-body system on a rectangular lattice, where evolution is modeled via a Lindblad master equation with parameterized Hamiltonian and jump operators.

Key observables. Magnetization (specifically the x-component, m_x) used as an order parameter for classification.

Important parameters / regimes. System size up to 550 qubits (50 sites, 11 layers), learning rate 0.05, and 5000-shot measurement scale.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of SVD truncation in tensor networks is assumed to capture the essential physics without qualitatively altering the training results.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the QNN architecture and the training process from untrained to trained states; Figure 2 shows the mapping of input datasets to distinct magnetization ranges; Supplementary figures demonstrate the convergence of bond dimensions and the stability of training losses.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QNN scaling challenge, describes the physics-informed architecture based on Lindbladian dynamics, presents training results on two distinct datasets, and provides a validation of the numerical methods via tensor network truncation analysis.

Why it may be interesting. It bridges quantum machine learning with open quantum systems, showing how dissipative many-body dynamics can be leveraged to create robust, noise-resilient architectures for feature extraction.

Abstract
