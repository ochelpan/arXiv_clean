**Main problem.**
Achieving high-visibility quantum interference (indistinguishability) between single photons emitted from spatially distant and independent semiconductor quantum dot-cavity sources.

**Main result.**
Demonstrated a record mutual indistinguishability of 88 +/- 1% between two distant sources, reaching the upper bound set by the intrinsic performance of the individual emitters.

**Method.**
The researchers used a combination of strain-tuning (via piezo-driven fiber) and electrical tuning (via PIN diode Stark shift) to align wavelengths, followed by Hong-Ou-Mandel interference measurements.

**Summary.**
This paper reports on the successful creation of near-identical photons from two separate, distant quantum dot-cavity devices. By using a dual-tuning approach involving both strain and electric fields, the researchers were able to overcome wavelength mismatches and spectral noise. They achieved a high mutual indistinguishability of 88%, which is nearly the maximum possible value allowed by the individual sources. This achievement is a significant milestone for scaling up photonic quantum technologies and building large-scale quantum networks.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**
The platform consists of two independent InGaAs quantum dot-cavity micropillars embedded in a PIN diode structure, cooled to 5 K, using longitudinal acoustic (LA) phonon-assisted excitation.

**Key observables.**
Two-photon indistinguishability (M12), Hong-Ou-Mandel visibility (V_HOM), second-order correlation function (g2(0)), and spectral noise/fluctuations.

**Important parameters / regimes.**
Indistinguishability of 88%, cavity Q factor of 8000, charge noise at 4.8% of natural linewidth, and photon collection efficiency between 15.6% and 27.5%.

**Assumptions / limitations.**
The calculation of the theoretical upper bound assumes perfect temporal overlap (s12 = 1) between the two photons.

**Figures summary.**
Fig 1: SEM images and fabrication details showing wavelength/cavity distribution and low charge noise. Fig 2: Principles of phonon-assisted excitation and lifetime/Purcell effect measurements. Fig 3: Extraction of mutual indistinguishability and comparison to theoretical bounds. Extended Data: Fabrication process, g2(0) measurements, and optical setup schematics.

**Paper structure.**
The paper introduces the challenge of remote interference, describes the fabrication and tuning architecture, presents the characterization of individual source stability, demonstrates the successful interference of distant sources, and concludes with the scalability and future potential of the approach.

**Why it may be interesting.**
This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum networks as it demonstrates a scalable method to bridge the gap between single-source performance and remote-source interference, a prerequisite for photon-mediated entanglement in quantum computing.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Scalable optical quantum technologies require interference between large numbers of indistinguishable single-photons emitted by independent sources. Semiconductor quantum dots are known to be excellent on-demand sources of single-photons. They show record efficiency when inserted into optical cavities to control their spontaneous emission and generate trains of near identical photons over microsecond timescales. However, generating perfectly identical photons from distant cavity-based sources has remained a long-standing challenge. It requires precise matching of the emission wavelengths and emission dynamics, while simultaneously minimizing spectral noise across all time scales for distant emitters in uncorrelated environments. Here, we report on the nanofabrication of a large number of quantum dot-cavity sources with ultra-low spectral noise and wavelength dispersion. The high source efficiency and the use of two tuning mechanisms enable precise optimization of the spectral overlap between distant sources. With this approach, we demonstrate a two-photon indistinguishability of $88\pm1$ % between photons emitted from two distant sources. Remarkably, this value reaches the upper bound set by the intrinsic indistinguishability of photons emitted successively by each source. These results represent a key milestone for scaling photon-based quantum technologies.

**Main problem.**
The difficulty of experimentally determining the Fermi energy in diamond due to the highly non-linear relationship between Fermi energy and donor-acceptor concentrations.

**Main result.**
The development of a non-destructive method to determine the Fermi energy by analyzing the relative populations of NV and SiV charge states via photoluminescence spectroscopy.

**Method.**
The method combines photoluminescence (PL) spectral decomposition with Density Functional Theory (DFT) models to relate charge state populations to the Fermi energy.

**Summary.**
This paper presents a new experimental method to precisely determine the Fermi energy in diamond using photoluminescence spectroscopy. By measuring the ratio of different charge states of NV and SiV centers and applying DFT-based models, the researchers can map the Fermi energy across different diamond types. The study also demonstrates how this energy level affects spin coherence times and charge state stability. This technique is highly advantageous because it offers high spatial and temporal resolution and can function in extreme environments.

**Model / system.**
The physical system consists of various diamond crystals (CVD, HPHT, and polycrystalline) containing nitrogen-vacancy (NV) and silicon-vacancy (SiV) defect centers.

**Key observables.**
Relative populations of NV- and NV0 centers, spin coherence time (T2), and PL intensity.

**Important parameters / regimes.**

**Paper structure.**
The paper introduces the problem of Fermi energy determination, presents the proposed PL-based method using DFT integration, demonstrates the method on various nitrogen-doped and thermal-grade diamond samples, investigates the correlation between Fermi energy and spin coherence (T2), extends the method to SiV centers, and concludes with the method's advantages and potential applications.

**Why it may be interesting.**

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Electronic band structures and the Fermi energy provide essential information for understanding the electronic properties of solids. In semiconductors, the Fermi energy level is determined by the donor and acceptor concentrations. For diamond, the relationship between the Fermi energy level and the donor-acceptor concentrations is highly nonlinear; therefore, experimental determination of the Fermi energy level is important. Here, we report a method to determine the Fermi energy of diamond based on photoluminescence (PL) measurement. The density-functional-theory (DFT) study by Deák et al.~\cite{deak2014formation} showed the relationship between the Fermi energy and the formation energies of nitrogen-vacancy centers in the negatively charged (NV-) and neutrally charged (NV0) charge states. In the present method, we measure the relative populations of the NV- and NV0 centers from PL spectral analysis and, using these populations and the DFT result, determine the Fermi energy of the diamond samples. Moreover, we show the application of the method to study the spin coherence and the stability against the charge state conversion of the NV centers on several diamond samples. We also extend the method for the Fermi energy determination using the silicon-vacancy (SiV) center in diamond. The PL-based method is advantageous for determining the Fermi energy with high spatial and fast time resolutions, even in extreme environments, and can be extended to determine various wide band gap semiconductors.

**Main problem.**

**Main result.**

**Method.**

**Summary.**

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We present an alternative scheme to achieve nonreciprocal unconventional magnon blockade (NUMB) in a hybrid system formed by two microwave cavities and one yttrium iron garnet (YIG) sphere, where the pump and signal cavities interact nonlinearly with each other and the signal cavity is coupled to the YIG sphere. It is found that the nonlinear coupling occurs between the pump cavity and magnon modes due to the dispersive interactions among three bosonic modes. Meanwhile, the Kerr nonlinearity is present in the pump cavity. Based on these nonlinear effects, a nonreciprocal magnon blockade could be achieved with the help of weak parametric driving of the pump cavity. The present work provides an alternative method to prepare single magnon resource, which may be helpful for quantum information processing.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy provides unparalleled access to molecular structure and dynamics but is traditionally limited by weak signal strength, requiring large sample volumes and high magnetic fields. Powder-like NV detected 2H NMR establishes a powerful tool for probing molecular dynamics on the nanoscale and, in the ultimate limit, at the single molecule level - capabilities beyond those of most existing spectroscopic techniques.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Although random matrix theory provides a fundamental framework for characterizing quantum chaos, encompassing both ergodic and localized phases, a comprehensive understanding of the universal features governing the critical transition remains elusive in many disordered and quasi-random systems. In this study, we explore the ergodic-to-many-body localization transition in the generalized Aubry-André model with interacting spinless fermions. Using the concept of Frobenius norm of an adiabatic gauge potential, we construct a phase diagram that captures the sensitivity of the eigenspectrum to infinitesimal adiabatic gauge deformations. To examine the stability of the critical disordered strength with respect to system size, we perform an unbiased finite-size scaling analysis via cost-function minimization techniques. Additionally, by analyzing the adjacent gap ratio and spectral form factor, we determine the scaling behavior of the Thouless time as a function of the disorder strength.
