**Main problem.**

The study investigates how odd-parity altermagnetism (ALM) reconstructs the momentum-space topology, edge states, and Hall transport in the presence of electronic interactions in a Chern insulator.

**Main result.**

The transition from a conventional Chern-insulating phase to an ALM-induced phase redistributes Berry curvature and alters edge state symmetry without changing the total Chern number, while maintaining quantized low-frequency Hall conductivity.

**Method.**

The authors employ the cluster slave-spin method, utilizing a 2-site cluster with exact diagonalization and a mean-field decoupling approach to treat strong electronic correlations.

**Summary.**

This paper explores how odd-parity altermagnetism affects the topological properties of the Haldane-Hubbard model. It demonstrates that while the global Chern number remains invariant during the transition to an altermagnetic Chern-insulating phase, the local Berry curvature is significantly redistributed. This reconstruction leads to spin-valley-locked edge states in zigzag ribbons and specific signatures in the optical Hall response. The findings establish the Haldane-Hubbard model as a key platform for studying the interplay of symmetry, interactions, and topology.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Odd-parity altermagnetism(ALM) extends compensated collinear magnetism beyond the even-parity spin splitting of conventional altermagnets, but its role in correlated topological phases remains largely unexplored. Using the cluster slave-spin method, we show that the odd-parity ALM appearing in the ALM Chern-insulating phase of Haldane-Hubbard model significantly reconstructs the local topology in the conventional Chern-insulating phase, while the total Chern number remains unchanged compared to the Chern-insulating phase. The Berry curvature becomes spin and valley selective; zigzag ribbons develop chiral-symmetry-breaking edge states; while armchair ribbons remain inversion symmetric. The optical response mirrors this separation between the local reconstruction and the global topology: low-energy spectra are governed by quasiparticles near the gap, whereas the low-frequency Hall conductivity stays quantized, $σ_{\rm T\uparrow}(Ω\to 0)=σ_{\rm T\downarrow}(Ω\to 0)=e^2/h$. These results establish the Haldane-Hubbard model as a minimal correlated platform for odd-parity altermagnetic topology.

**Main problem.**

Developing a unified theoretical framework for Anderson localization on the hyperbolic plane that interpolates between 2D-like behavior at short scales and infinite-dimensional behavior at large scales.

**Main result.**

The derivation of a two-parameter renormalization group flow for conductivity and curvature, identifying a critical line (separatrix) that separates metallic and insulating phases.

**Method.**

A combination of a non-linear sigma-model using the supersymmetry formalism, renormalization group analysis, and lattice discretization using Poisson-Delaunay random graphs.

**Summary.**

This paper provides a complete theory of Anderson localization on a surface with negative curvature. It explains how the geometry of the hyperbolic plane causes a crossover from standard 2D localization to a high-dimensional regime where metallicity can be protected. By using both field theory and graph-based models, the authors derive a unified renormalization group flow that predicts a true phase transition between metallic and insulating states.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**

The two-dimensional hyperbolic plane (H2) with constant negative curvature, modeled via a Hamiltonian with a delta-correlated random potential and discretized using various graph structures like Bethe lattices and Poisson-Delaunay graphs.

**Key observables.**

Scale-dependent conductivity (sigma), scale-dependent curvature (u), resistance (R), and critical exponents (nu and beta).

**Important parameters / regimes.**

Disorder strength (W), curvature radius (L), conductivity (sigma), and the scaling variable (u).

**Assumptions / limitations.**

One-loop approximation in the renormalization group integration; use of a delta-correlated random potential; assumption that the Poisson-Delaunay discretization accurately captures the continuum spectrum in the high-density limit.

**Figures summary.**

Figure 4 shows a generation of a cactus graph; Figure 5 is mentioned in the context of discretization/graph properties.

**Paper structure.**

The paper begins with a continuum sigma-model to establish the 2D-like localization physics, moves to a lattice discretization (Bethe lattice and random graphs) to capture the high-dimensional transition, and concludes by deriving the coupled RG flow equations and the critical line.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

The two-dimensional hyperbolic plane, $\mathbb{H}^2$, is an unusual system in that dimensionality changes with scale: locally two-dimensional and planar at short distances, but effectively infinite-dimensional at large scales, it provides an interesting paradigm for the study of (quantum) phase transitions, notably the disorder-driven Anderson transition. Generalizing previous work, which treated short and large distance scales separately, we develop a unified framework interpolating between the principles of low- and high-dimensional Anderson localization. As a main result, we derive a two-parameter flow in a plane spanned by scale-dependent curvature (setting the system's effective dimensionality) and conductivity, with an extended critical line separating metallic and insulating phases.

</details>

**Main problem.**

The existence of an unresolved internal superconducting phase boundary in UTe2 for magnetic fields along the b-axis lacked definitive bulk thermodynamic evidence.

**Main result.**

Ultrasound measurements provide the first bulk thermodynamic evidence for this internal phase boundary, identifying a tetracritical point near 13.5 T and 1.25 K and revealing symmetry-selective coupling to lattice strain.

**Method.**

Pulse-echo ultrasound spectroscopy of various elastic modes (C33, C44, C55) and transverse magnetostriction measurements.

**Summary.**

This paper provides the first thermodynamic confirmation of an internal superconducting phase boundary in UTe2 for magnetic fields applied along the b-axis. Using ultrasound spectroscopy, the authors observed a significant softening in the C33 elastic mode and a kink in magnetostriction at approximately 14 T. They identified a tetracritical point where the phase boundary terminates and demonstrated that the anomaly is symmetry-selective. This discovery confirms the boundary is a bulk thermodynamic feature and supports the existence of multicomponent superconductivity in this material.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.**

The physical system is UTe2, an orthorhombic heavy-fermion paramagnet and unconventional superconductor, studied in high magnetic fields (up to 18 T) and low temperatures (down to 0.33 K).

**Key observables.**

Elastic constants (C33, C44, C55), relative frequency shift (delta f/f), and transverse magnetostriction (delta lc/lc).

**Important parameters / regimes.**

Magnetic field H parallel to the b-axis, temperature down to 0.33 K, and the phase boundary near 14-15 T.

**Assumptions / limitations.**

The study assumes the use of ultraclean single crystals to ensure the observed features are intrinsic bulk properties.

**Figures summary.**

Figure 1 shows the field dependence of elastic responses: (a) C33 softening and magnetostriction kink at 14 T, (b) C44 hardening anomaly, and (c) no resolvable anomaly in C55.

**Paper structure.**

The paper introduces the unresolved phase boundary problem, describes the experimental setup using ultrasound and magnetostriction, presents the identification of the thermodynamic boundary via elastic anomalies, discusses the symmetry-selective coupling and the tetracritical point, and concludes with implications for the order-parameter structure.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

The $H$--$T$ phase diagram of UTe$_2$ for magnetic field along the hard $b$ axis contains an unresolved internal boundary near $μ_0H \sim 14$--15~T, previously inferred from ac susceptibility and transport experiments but lacking thermodynamic evidence. We report ultrasound results for several elastic modes in an ultraclean UTe$_2$ single crystal with $T_c>2$~K for $H \parallel b$ down to 0.33~K and up to 18~T. A pronounced anomaly in the longitudinal $C_{33}$ mode, with a weaker response in $C_{44}$ and no resolvable anomaly in $C_{55}$, establishes this feature as a bulk thermodynamic phase boundary and reveals a symmetry-selective coupling to lattice strain. The phase line remains nearly constant in field near 14~T and terminates near 13.5~T and 1.25~K at a tetracritical point, providing the thermodynamic evidence for the fourth phase boundary in the $H$--$T$ phase diagram. The results constrain the order-parameter structure of the high-field phase and support field-induced multicomponent superconductivity in UTe$_2$.

**Main problem.**

The study addresses the lack of systematic research on the topochemical fluorination of bulk single crystals of La2NiO4+d, aiming to understand the intrinsic structure-property relationships and the mechanism of fluorine incorporation.

**Main result.**

The researchers discovered that topochemical fluorination preserves the Ruddlesden-Popper framework while inducing a previously unreported fluorine-intercalated superstructure (C2/m symmetry) and altering the antiferromagnetic ordering.

**Method.**

Single crystals were grown via the optical floating zone method and subjected to fluorination using agents like PTFE, PVDF, and CuF2. Characterization included SC-XRD, SEM-EDX, TGA, and magnetic susceptibility measurements.

**Summary.**

This paper investigates the topochemical fluorination of La2NiO4+d single crystals using various fluorine-containing agents. It demonstrates that fluorine can be intercalated into the rock-salt layers of the Ruddlesden-Popper structure without destroying the crystal framework. The study reveals the emergence of a new monoclinic superstructure and changes in the material's magnetic properties. This work provides a new route for tuning the electronic and structural properties of nickelates in bulk form.

**Model / system.**

**Key observables.**

**Important parameters / regimes.**

**Assumptions / limitations.**

**Figures summary.**

**Paper structure.**

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Topochemical fluorination offers a low--temperature route for modifying the anion chemistry and electronic ground states of layered transition-metal oxides, providing access to metastable phases and functionalities that are not able to be achieved through conventional solid--state synthesis. Despite extensive work on polycrystalline samples and thin films, topochemical fluorination of bulk single crystals has not been studied, limiting insights into intrinsic structure property relationships. Here, we investigate the topochemical fluorination of optical float zone grown (OFZ) La$_2$NiO$_{4+δ}$ single crystals using polymer-based PTFE, PVDF and inorganic CuF$_{2}$ fluorination agents and compare it to our topochemical pathways of reduction of LaNiO$_{3-x}$. By systematically investigating direct and indirect contact reaction pathways, we can understand fluorination mechanisms, quantify the degree of fluorine incorporation, and evaluate the resulting structural and magnetic modifications in a detail that was not possible in powder and thin films. Powder and single--crystal X-ray diffraction reveal that fluorination proceeds without destroying the Ruddlesden--Popper framework, while inducing lattice parameter changes consistent with anion intercalation in the bulk and ion exchange on the surface. This even induces a clear superstructure, which was not reported before and extends the understanding of anion insertion reactions beyond what is known on stage ordering in nickelates. Energy-dispersive X--ray spectroscopy confirms strong fluorine incorporation on the surface and reduced homogeneity in the bulk. Magnetic susceptibility measurements demonstrate a change in antiferromagnetic ordering upon fluorination.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

CrCl3, a layered van der Waals (vdW) magnet, exhibits in-plane magnetic anisotropy and enhanced interlayer coupling upon stacking, making it an ideal platform to host exotic nanoscale magnetic phenomena such as magnon hydrodynamics and meron-like topological spin defects. When interfaced with other vdW materials, its antiferromagnetic-to-ferromagnetic and ferromagnetic-to-paramagnetic phase transitions and magnetic anisotropy can be tuned by voltage, strain, and layer stacking. Understanding the spin dynamics of CrCl3 at its magnetic phase transitions is crucial to its applications in magnonics. Here, we investigate the spin dynamics of CrCl3 nanoflakes using cryogenic diamond quantum sensing microscopy, based on measuring optically detected magnetic resonance, Rabi oscillations, and spin-lattice relaxation time (T1) of shallow nitrogen vacancy (NV) centers in diamond. In the ferromagnetic regime, we observe a pronounced reduction in the NV spin resonance contrast, a collapse of the Rabi oscillations, and a strong enhancement by two orders of magnitude of the relaxation rate, G1 = 1/T1. These observations indicate intensified spin fluctuations in the gigahertz range. Broadband ferromagnetic resonance spectroscopy on CrCl3 microcrystals reveals resonance frequencies in the 4-15 GHz range together with a linewidth of ~24 mT, further supporting the NV measurements. A phenomenological model of magnetic-noise-induced NV relaxation reproduces the temperature dependence of G1 by combining antiferromagnetic, ferromagnetic, and paramagnetic fluctuation channels, indicating that magnetic noise is strongest in the ferromagnetic regime and evolves markedly across the phase diagram. These results are crucial for using CrCl3 in 2D magnonics and hybrid quantum-magnon systems.
