Two-orbital $e_g$ model with bond-dependent spin-orbit coupling: A playground for emergent band topology, Kitaev magnetism, and magnetoelectricity

Authors: YuZheng Xie, Manoj Gupta, Arun Paramekanti, Tanusri Saha-Dasgupta
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02716v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 1/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 1/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work develops a theoretical model for NiX2 compounds using a two-orbital e_g Hamiltonian that includes bond-dependent spin-orbit coupling. It demonstrates that this model hosts rich physics, including topological band structures and Kitaev magnetism in the strongly correlated limit. The research extends the understanding of magnetoelectric effects by incorporating orbital-dependent spin currents.

Why it may be interesting. The explicit derivation of magnetoelectric coupling terms beyond single-orbital models, incorporating orbital degrees of freedom, provides a rich theoretical framework for understanding charge-spin coupling in correlated materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates emergent phenomena, including band topology, Kitaev magnetism, and magnetoelectricity, in layered transition metal compounds like NiX2.

Main result. The model predicts topological bands with non-trivial spin Chern numbers and reveals a spin-1 Hamiltonian exhibiting bond-dependent Kitaev interactions in the Mott limit.

Method. The study uses a two-orbital e_g model derived from DFT calculations, analyzing different limits (non-interacting, Mott insulator) and employing symmetry-based perturbation theory.

Model / system. The system is modeled by an effective two-orbital e_g Hamiltonian incorporating bond-dependent spin-orbit coupling (SOC) derived from ligand p-orbitals, relevant to NiX2 compounds.

Key observables. Spin-Chern numbers (Cs), higher-order topological indices, bond-dependent Kitaev exchange interactions, and spin-induced electric polarization (P).

Important parameters / regimes. Ligand SOC strength (lambda), Coulomb repulsion (U), and various hopping parameters (t_1a, t_3b, etc.).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on mapping the full electronic structure onto a minimal two-orbital e_g model and using perturbation theory in the strong coupling limit.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate phase diagrams (SOC vs U), band structures showing topological edge/corner states, and magnetic phase diagrams (Luttinger-Tisza).

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the effective two-orbital model via DFT/Wannierization, analyzing the non-interacting topological properties, deriving the effective spin Hamiltonian in the Mott limit, and finally calculating the generalized magnetoelectric response.

Abstract

Inspired by the electronic structure of compounds like nickel dihalides Ni$X_2$ ($X$=Cl, Br, I), we propose a low-energy two-orbital $e_g$ model featuring bond-dependent spin-orbit terms, driven by atomic spin-orbit coupling on the ligand $X$. We demonstrate that this model hosts a rich array of phenomena. In the non-interacting band limit, spin-orbit-derived spin-dependent and spin-flip hopping terms produce topological bands with spin-Chern numbers $C_s=\pm 2, \pm 4$, and higher order topological states with fractional corner charges, respectively. In the half-filled Mott insulator limit, we recover a spin-$1$ Hamiltonian with bond-dependent Kitaev exchange interactions. We explore the magnetoelectric effect in this two-orbital model using symmetry-based perspective and microscopic calculations, going beyond the generalized Katsura-Nagaosa-Balatsky theory for the single-orbital case. Our work may be relevant to study of doping, strain, or pressure on Ni$X_2$ and related materials.

Game, Set, Quantum: Parameterized Quantum Circuit for Correlated Equilibrium in Bayesian Games

Authors: Param Pathak, Vidhi Oad, Nouhaila Innan, Adarsh Ganesan, Muhammad Shafique
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03109v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes using a Parameterized Quantum Circuit (PQC) within a hybrid quantum-classical framework to approximate Bayes correlated equilibria in Bayesian games. By parameterizing the conditional strategy distribution, the method bypasses the exponential memory bottleneck of classical formulations. The results show that this quantum approach yields superior performance compared to established classical algorithms for moderate numbers of players.

Why it may be interesting. While the application is in game theory, the core methodology—using a PQC to compress an exponentially large state space into a polynomially parameterized variational form—is a powerful technique relevant to simulating complex quantum many-body states or high-dimensional quantum processes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the computational challenge of finding equilibria in Bayesian games, where the explicit representation of the joint type-action space grows exponentially.

Main result. The proposed quantum solver achieves lower mean clipped regret than MCCFR across all tested player counts for up to eight players, demonstrating a viable variational representation for approximate equilibrium computation.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical framework is used, employing a Parameterized Quantum Circuit (PQC) to represent the conditional strategy distribution, trained via gradient-based regret minimization.

Model / system. The model is a binary-type, binary-action Bayesian game, and the quantum computation is performed on a PQC operating on $2n$ qubits (type and action registers).

Key observables. Mean clipped regret, maximum positive deviation gain ($\epsilon_{\max}$), and memory usage scaling ($\mathcal{O}(2^{2n})$ vs $\mathcal{O}(nL)$).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of players ($n$), circuit depth ($L$), learning rate ($\eta_t$), and entropy regularization coefficient ($ au_t$).

Assumptions / limitations. The method is a variational approximation, meaning convergence to an exact equilibrium is not guaranteed unless the target distribution lies within the PQC's reachable family.

Figures summary. Figures compare the mean clipped regret of the quantum solver, MCCFR, and DCFR across increasing player counts ($n$), showing the quantum solver's advantage up to $n=8$.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem, details the PQC architecture and the hybrid quantum-classical training workflow (including regret minimization and entropy regularization), presents comparative results against classical baselines, and discusses scaling and noise effects.

Abstract

Strategic decision-making among many agents under incomplete information is central to economics, security, and multi-agent artificial intelligence (AI). Computing equilibria in such settings is challenging because the joint type-action space grows exponentially with the number of players. In binary-type, binary-action Bayesian games, an explicit representation over type-action profiles requires O(22n) entries, making direct linear-programming (LP) formulations memory intensive at moderate player counts. We propose a hybrid quantum-classical framework for approximating Bayes correlated equilibrium using a parameterized quantum circuit (PQC). The PQC represents the conditional strategy distribution with O(nL) trainable parameters, where n is the number of players and L is the circuit depth; for the largest setting studied here, n = 10 and L = 2, this corresponds to 60 trainable angles. The circuit is trained by gradient-based regret minimization with a negative entropy regularizer and a curriculum schedule over player counts. On a poker-style Bayesian game with two to ten players, the proposed solver achieves lower mean clipped regret than MCCFR across all tested player counts and lower regret than DCFR up to eight players, while DCFR performs best at ten players. These results show that compact PQC parameterizations can provide a viable variational representation for approximate equilibrium computation, while highlighting the roles of ansatz expressivity, optimization strategy, and classical simulation cost.

A proof of an identity for the critical exponents of jamming

Authors: Giorgio Parisi, Francesco Zamponi
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03300v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous, analytic proof that a key identity, $a+b=1$, holds for the critical exponents describing the jamming transition in dense hard spheres. The proof connects the mathematical structure of the full Replica-Symmetry-Breaking theory to the physical stability criteria of the system. This establishes a deep equivalence between theoretical scaling laws and mechanical stability arguments in the jamming limit.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on classical statistical mechanics, the rigorous mathematical techniques involving functional analysis (maximum principle) and the analysis of complex scaling equations are highly relevant to modern theoretical physics, including quantum many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To provide an analytic proof of the identity $a+b=1$ for the critical exponents governing the jamming transition in dense hard spheres.

Main result. The authors analytically prove the identity $a+b=1$ by showing that the phase-space marginal stability condition derived from the fullRSB equations is equivalent to the mechanical marginal stability condition.

Method. The proof combines analyzing the asymptotic behavior of coupled non-linear differential equations with applying the maximum principle to establish bounds on auxiliary functions derived from the fullRSB profile.

Model / system. The system is amorphous packings of hard or soft spheres undergoing a jamming transition, modeled using the full Replica-Symmetry-Breaking (fullRSB) Ansatz in the infinite-dimensional limit ($d o \infty$).

Key observables. Critical exponents $a, b, c$ (from fullRSB) and physical exponents $\alpha, heta, \kappa$ (controlling gap, force, and overlap distributions).

Important parameters / regimes. The jamming transition point, the infinite dimension limit ($d o \infty$), and the scaling exponents $a, b, c$.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes the validity of the scaling equations and the marginal-stability identities established in previous work (CKPUZ).

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by revisiting the eigenvalue condition, deriving an algebraic identity from the fullRSB equations, and combining this with the marginal-stability identity to force the conclusion $a+b=1$.

Abstract

Within the full replica-symmetry-breaking (fullRSB) solution of dense hard spheres in infinite dimension, Charbonneau, Kurchan, Parisi, Urbani, and Zamponi (CKPUZ; J.Stat.Mech.P10009, 2014) introduced three critical exponents $a$, $b$, $c$ governing the matching region of the fullRSB profile near the jamming transition. These exponents satisfy two scaling relations. The first, $b=(1+c)/2$, was established analytically by the diffusion-drift balance in the scaling ansatz. The second, $a+b=1$, was observed numerically to arbitrary precision but could not be proven. The exponents $a,b,c$ of the scaling fullRSB ansatz are related to the physical exponents $α, θ, κ$ that control the gap, force, and overlap distributions by the relations $α=a/b$, $θ=(c-a)/(b-c)$, $κ=c+1$. Crucially, the relation $a+b=1$ yields the scaling relations $α=1/(2+θ)$ and $κ=2-2/(3+θ)$ predicted on independent grounds by the mechanical-marginal-stability arguments of Wyart and collaborators. Here, we give an analytic proof of the identity $a+b=1$ from the scaling fullRSB equations. The proof was obtained through interaction with Claude (Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.7) and verified by us.

Generalised simultaneous transmission of arbitrary quantum states and classical information

Authors: Timothy C. Ralph, Nicholas Zaunders
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03181v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5

Summary. This paper details a Continuous-Variable Quantum Communication (CMQC) protocol enabling the simultaneous, low-error transmission of arbitrary quantum states and classical data. By encoding classical information via phase-space displacements and using continuous-variable teleportation, the scheme demonstrates high fidelity reconstruction. The results show that the protocol is robust, maintaining quantum correlations strong enough to violate the Bell inequality even under realistic channel losses.

Why it may be interesting. This work directly addresses quantum networking by proposing a robust method to multiplex classical data and quantum entanglement over one physical channel, which is crucial for future quantum internet architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a protocol for the simultaneous transmission of arbitrary quantum states and classical information over a single quantum channel without losing the integrity of either information type.

Main result. The protocol can achieve perfect reconstruction of both the classical signal and the quantum state in the limits of high classical signal strength and high squeezing, and it can violate the CHSH inequality under realistic conditions.

Method. The scheme uses continuous-variable quantum teleportation combined with classical phase-space displacements for encoding and subsequent inverse displacements for decoding.

Model / system. The system models optical quantum states transmitted through a lossy bosonic channel, utilizing continuous-variable teleportation and encoding classical data via coherent-state displacements.

Key observables. Symbol-Error Rate ($e_S$), CHSH expectation value ($S$), and the output state ($\hat{ ho}_{ ext{out}}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Transmissivity ($\eta$), squeezing parameter ($r$), and classical signal strength ($\alpha$).

Assumptions / limitations. The protocol assumes the ability to probabilistically infer the true classical displacement and relies on the channel being a pure-loss channel for some derivations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the protocol structure, the dependence of the CHSH value ($S$) on channel loss ($\eta$) for varying classical signal strength ($\alpha$) and squeezing ($r$), and the success probabilities ($p_s$) for post-selection.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the CMQC protocol, analyzes its effective channel map in various limits (high SNR, infinite squeezing), and rigorously tests its capability to maintain quantum correlations by calculating the CHSH value for transmitted Bell states.

Abstract

We present a protocol which allows for arbitrary optical quantum states to simultaneously carry and transmit classical data, without sacrificing the integrity of either the quantum or classical information. Our scheme encodes classical information via displacements in the phase space prior to transmission and retrieves each classical symbol via a Gaussian continuous-variable teleportation. The original quantum state is then restored by guessing the the original displacement and performing the appropriate inverse operation. In the limit of sufficiently high classical signal and high squeezing, we show that our scheme is capable of perfectly reconstructing both the input classical signal and the input quantum state without loss of coherence. An example is given in terms of the transmission of a dual-rail Bell state.

Magnetic Symmetries and the Structure of Correlation Functions in Quantum Field Theory

Authors: Masaru Hongo, Kentaro Nishimura
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03082v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical paper develops a model-independent framework to analyze quantum field theories in strong magnetic fields by exploiting magnetic translation and rotation symmetries. It shows that these symmetries force correlation functions to factorize and acquire a Schwinger phase. Ultimately, the authors derive a rigorous spectral representation for these correlation functions, applicable to various physical systems in the presence of a magnetic field.

Why it may be interesting. The derivation of spectral representations and correlation function factorizations based purely on symmetry principles provides a powerful, non-perturbative tool applicable to strongly correlated systems in external fields, which is highly relevant for understanding magnetotransport phenomena.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To investigate the general consequences of magnetic symmetries (translations and rotations) on correlation functions in Quantum Field Theory when subjected to a static, uniform external magnetic field.

Main result. The magnetic translation symmetry forces correlation functions to acquire a Schwinger phase and leads to a factorized form, allowing for a model-independent spectral representation based on the magnetic translation algebra.

Method. The analysis uses symmetry constraints derived from the projective structure of the magnetic translation algebra, avoiding specific models or perturbative expansions.

Model / system. The system is a Quantum Field Theory (QFT) describing charged particles coupled to a background U(1) gauge field with a constant magnetic field (B) along the z-axis.

Key observables. Two-point and N-point Wightman/Green's functions, the Schwinger phase, and the spectral representation of the reduced correlator.

Important parameters / regimes. The magnetic field strength (B), the charge of the particles (q), and the transverse coordinates ($x_\perp$).

Assumptions / limitations. The magnetic field is assumed to be static and uniform; the analysis assumes the magnetic symmetries are global symmetries acting on the dynamical fields.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by first establishing the magnetic symmetries and their non-Abelian algebra (Chunk 2). It then applies these symmetries to constrain N-point correlation functions, deriving the Schwinger phase and factorization (Chunk 3). Finally, it derives the spectral representation of the reduced two-point function using the magnetic translation algebra, yielding basis-independent forms for both Landau and symmetric gauges (Chunks 4 & 5).

Abstract

Quantum field theories in the presence of a static and uniform external magnetic field possess two characteristic spatial symmetries: magnetic translations and magnetic rotation. We investigate general consequences of these symmetries on correlation functions from a model-independent perspective, without relying on specific models or perturbative expansions. The projective structure of magnetic translation symmetry constrains correlation functions of charged operators to acquire the Schwinger phase and leads to a factorized form into a gauge-covariant phase factor and a reduced correlator depending only on relative coordinates. We further derive the spectral representation of two-point functions in terms of representations of the magnetic translation algebra, in which the Landau- and symmetric-gauge descriptions arise as different choices of basis. Our results provide a unified symmetry-based framework for quantum field theories in external magnetic fields.

Phase transitions through excited-state level crossings and topological indicators: the case of the XXZ chain with staggered Ising interaction

Authors: B. F. Márquez, K. Hallberg, A. A. Aligia
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02845v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work determines the phase diagram of a staggered XXZ spin chain by comparing two advanced diagnostic techniques: topological indicators and energy level crossings. The authors find that while both methods are powerful, the energy level crossing method provides a more reliable determination of the Néel-dimer transition boundary, especially when the system exhibits broken translational symmetry.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous comparison of multiple theoretical diagnostics (topological vs. spectral) to pinpoint a quantum critical point in a complex, interacting 1D model is highly valuable for understanding the robustness of phase transition detection in strongly correlated systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to determine the phase diagram, specifically the transition between Néel and dimerized phases, in the spin-1/2 XXZ chain subjected to a staggered Ising interaction.

Main result. The combination of two diagnostic methods—topological indicators and energy level crossings—provides a robust and efficient way to locate the phase boundary, with the level crossing method being particularly accurate despite the loss of translational symmetry.

Method. The authors employ exact diagonalization on finite systems, comparing results from topological indicators (based on generalized position operators) with those derived from the crossing of excited energy levels (MCEL), which is justified by conformal-field theory.

Model / system. The system is a spin-1/2 XXZ chain Hamiltonian incorporating a staggered Ising interaction ($\delta eq 0$) and a uniform transverse exchange. This alternating interaction doubles the unit cell and drives the competition between Néel and bond-dimerized orders.

Key observables. Topological indicators ($\alpha_s$, $\alpha_\uparrow$), the pseudo-critical point $\delta_c(L)$ extracted from energy level crossings ($E^{(1)}_+(L, \delta_c) = E^{(1)}_-(L, \delta_c)$), and the correlation length ($\xi$).

Important parameters / regimes. Anisotropy parameter ($\Delta$), staggering parameter ($\delta$), and system size ($L$). The analysis distinguishes between the homogeneous ($\delta=0$) and staggered ($\delta eq 0$) regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The topological indicator method's accuracy is compromised when the energy gap is very small near the transition. The analysis relies on mapping the low-energy physics to the double sine-Gordon theory and using finite-size scaling extrapolation.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the extraction of the pseudo-critical point from the crossing of first excited energy levels for finite systems, and show the finite-size scaling of this critical point across different anisotropy values.

Paper structure. The paper systematically compares two diagnostic tools—topological indicators and MCEL—to map the phase diagram. It analyzes the underlying physics using bosonization and relates the finite-size corrections to known critical theories (Ising universality class).

Abstract

We combine two ways of determining the phase diagram of the spin-$1/2$ XXZ chain with a staggered Ising interaction and uniform transverse exchange, based on exact diagonalization. The model realizes a competition between Néel order and bond-dimerized phases generated by the alternating Ising interaction. The simplest approach to determine the phase boundaries is to use topological indicators based on generalized position operators. We show that in general, the bosonized and numerical results for the topological indicators agree. The second is based on crossings of excited energy levels and justified by conformal-field theory. In spite of the partial loss of translational symmetry caused by the alternating Ising interaction, we find that the latter method provides an accurate determination of the boundary between Néel and dimerized phases. Instead, while the jumps of a topological indicator provide a qualitatively correct phase diagram, its accuracy is affected when the gap is very small (or the correlation length very large) at one side of the transition, as we show using field-theoretical arguments. The combination of both methods provides a more efficient way of calculating phase diagrams for correlated one-dimensional models than other widely used conventional approaches.

The bulk spectral gap is semi-decidable: a convergent family of certified upper bounds

Authors: Xiangling Xu, Matthias Schötz, Jie Wang, Victor Magron, Igor Klep, Omar Fawzi, Marc-Olivier Renou
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03836v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a powerful, algorithmic method using semidefinite programming to compute certified upper bounds on the bulk spectral gap of quantum many-body systems. By showing that the gap is semi-decidable, the authors provide rigorous mathematical tools to analyze ground state properties in the thermodynamic limit. This is significant because it offers a systematic way to quantify spectral gaps, contrasting with previous undecidability results for finite-volume systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a rigorous, algorithmic tool to study fundamental properties of quantum ground states in the thermodynamic limit, bridging quantum many-body theory with computational complexity.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the bulk spectral gap of quantum many-body systems in the thermodynamic limit is a central, challenging problem.

Main result. The authors show that the bulk spectral gap is semi-decidable by constructing a complete family of certified upper bounds using semidefinite programming (SDP).

Method. The problem is reformulated into a sequence of necessary conditions tested via solving increasingly complex semidefinite programs (SDPs) that relax the non-linear gap definition.

Model / system. The analysis applies to general quantum many-body systems, using the spin-1/2 kagome lattice Heisenberg antiferromagnet and the transverse-field Ising model as key examples.

Key observables. Bulk spectral gap ($\gamma$), KMS ground state expectation values ($\omega(a)$), and the gap parameter $\gamma$.

Important parameters / regimes. The lattice structure (e.g., kagome, 1D chain), the coupling constants ($J$), and the computational resources/relaxation levels $(L, d)$.

Assumptions / limitations. The method relies on the equivalence between the existence of a KMS ground state and the feasibility of the SDP relaxation hierarchy; the bounds are certified upper bounds, not necessarily the true gap.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the convergence of certified upper bounds for the TFIM chain and the kagome lattice, showing how increasing relaxation levels tighten the bounds.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the formal definition of the bulk spectral gap via KMS states, develops the SDP relaxation hierarchy to test necessary conditions, and applies this machinery to specific lattice models to demonstrate semi-decidability.

Abstract

Determining spectral gaps in the thermodynamic limit is a central challenge in quantum many-body physics. Existing rigorous methods are largely limited to special settings, while variational numerical approaches typically provide estimates rather than certified bounds. Here we introduce a complete family of certified upper bounds on the bulk spectral gap of quantum many-body systems. These upper bounds are obtained by solving a series of semidefinite programs and they become arbitrarily tight at the cost of more computational resources. This shows that the bulk spectral gap is semi-decidable, in contrast to undecidability results for alternative notions of spectral gap based on sequences of finite systems with prescribed boundary conditions. As a proof of principle, we apply our algorithm to the spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ kagome lattice Heisenberg antiferromagnet and obtain, to our knowledge, the first nontrivial certified upper bounds on its bulk spectral gap.

A Voxel-Based Quantum Computing Method (VBQC) for Solid Mechanics Problem

Authors: Feng Wu, Yuxiang Yang, Li Zhu, Chen Li, Yansong Guo, Xu Guo
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03515v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces the Voxel-Based Quantum Computing Method (VBQC) to overcome the challenge of simulating solid mechanics Hamiltonians on irregular grids. It achieves this by exploiting the fractal structure of voxel-discretized matrices, leading to a specialized KCQ decomposition. The method is successfully demonstrated by calculating natural vibration frequencies and mode shapes for 1D, 2D, and 3D structures with high accuracy.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a concrete, structured quantum algorithm for a major class of physical problems (structural dynamics) that traditionally rely on complex numerical methods, demonstrating the potential of quantum simulation for continuum mechanics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge is efficiently simulating the system matrix (Hamiltonian) for solid mechanics problems, which typically involve irregular spatial grids unsuitable for direct quantum simulation.

Main result. The authors propose the Voxel-Based Quantum Computing Method (VBQC), which successfully simulates the time evolution operator $e^{iAt}$ for 1D, 2D, and 3D solid mechanics problems with high accuracy.

Method. The method involves discretizing the domain using voxel grids to induce a tridiagonal fractal property in the system matrix. This allows for a decomposition (KCQ decomposition) which is then implemented using quantum circuits involving the Quantum Fourier Transform and quantum multiplexers.

Model / system. The physical system is solid mechanics (e.g., beams, plates) whose dynamics are governed by a Hamiltonian derived from the system matrix $A$. The method handles complex geometries by using voxel grids and virtual nodes to maintain a structure suitable for quantum circuit encoding.

Key observables. Natural vibration frequencies ($\omega_i$) and corresponding mode shapes ($\mathbf{x}_i$), as well as the time evolution operator $e^{iAt}$ itself.

Important parameters / regimes. The complexity scales polynomially with the number of nodes/variables ($n_x + n_y + d$), and the accuracy is verified by comparing results across different time steps ($\Delta t$).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that the quantum multiplexer can be implemented with polynomial complexity, and that the basic matrix elements derived from the fractal structure can be mapped onto fundamental quantum gates.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the fractal structure of matrices for 1D, 2D, and 3D voxel grids, and show quantum circuits implementing the matrix exponential for various dimensions and variable counts.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the problem (solid mechanics on irregular grids) and proposes the VBQC. It details the mathematical foundation through the tridiagonal fractal property and the KCQ decomposition. Subsequently, it develops the quantum circuits for $e^{iAt}$ for increasing dimensions (1D, 2D, 3D) and finally verifies the method by comparing computed natural frequencies and mode shapes against classical reference solutions.

Abstract

Quantum computing presents a promising method to overcome the efficiency and memory constraints in large-scale mechanical problems, with numerous successful applications demonstrated in fluid mechanics. However, solid mechanics problems usually require irregular grids for spatial discretization, due to the Lagrange formulations and complex boundaries, which makes the quantum simulation of the system matrix, e.g., the mass or stiffness matrix which is often referred to as the Hamiltonian in quantum computing, difficult to be effectively conducted. This study proposes a voxel-based quantum computing method (VBQC) for the quantum simulation of Hamiltonians in solid mechanics. VBQC applies voxel grids to discretize the spatial domain, thereby enabling the system matrix to exhibit the tridiagonal fractal property. Based on this property, the system matrix can be decomposed into three groups of fundamental matrices, $\mathbf{k}_{n}$, $\mathbf{c}_{n}$, and $\mathbf{q}_{n}$. This decomposition process is referred to as the KCQ decomposition. By integrating the KCQ decomposition with the quantum Fourier transform and the quantum multiplexer, VBQC enables efficient quantum simulation of Hamiltonians in solid mechanics. Three specific solid problems with different dimensions and numbers of variables are applied to preliminarily verify the correctness of the proposed VBQC for solid mechanics problems.

An Asymptotic Theory of Chain-of-Thought in In-Context Learning

Summary. This paper provides a deep theoretical understanding of how the depth of Chain-of-Thought reasoning affects model generalization in In-Context Learning. Using random matrix theory on a simplified linear model, the authors derive an exact formula that predicts four distinct regimes of performance scaling. This work clarifies that the benefit of deep reasoning is highly dependent on the quality of pretraining and the amount of in-context examples provided.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous application of random matrix theory and asymptotic analysis to model the generalization dynamics of LLMs provides a powerful theoretical bridge between statistical physics/random matrix theory and modern deep learning theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the poorly understood scaling behavior of generalization error when using Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning within In-Context Learning (ICL) for large language models.

Main result. It derives an exact asymptotic formula characterizing four distinct regimes of test-time error dynamics: exponential improvement, polynomial decay, saturation, and error amplification (overthinking).

Method. The analysis employs tools from random matrix theory under high-dimensional asymptotics, modeling the CoT process as an iterative refinement of a weight-parameter estimate in a solvable linear regression model.

Model / system. The system is a theoretically solvable model of CoT applied to in-context weight prediction in linear regression, analyzed via an attention mechanism structure. The analysis tracks the error dynamics as an iterative update $\hat{w}_{t+1} := ext{Read}( ext{Atten}(P_t; heta^*))$.

Key observables. Generalization error (MSE) as a function of reasoning depth ($t$), pretraining data amount, and context length ($\alpha$).

Important parameters / regimes. Reasoning depth ($t$), context length ($\alpha$), task diversity ($ au$), and noise variance ($\sigma^2$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on a theoretically tractable model (linear regression) and the high-dimensional asymptotic limit ($D, M, L o \infty$).

Figures summary. The figures summarize a phase diagram showing the four distinct regimes (exponential improvement, polynomial decay, saturation, overthinking) based on the relative values of task diversity ($ au$) versus a critical threshold ($ au_c$).

Paper structure. The paper builds from establishing the iterative error dynamics using random matrix theory, deriving the asymptotic scaling law, identifying the phase transition points, and finally characterizing the optimal stopping depth ($t^*$) in the overthinking regime.

Abstract

Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning has become a widely used mechanism for eliciting multi-step reasoning in large language models by generating intermediate reasoning steps at inference time. Yet the scaling behavior of generalization with CoT depth remains poorly understood. To address this question, we study a theoretically solvable model of CoT for in-context weight prediction in linear regression, where test-time reasoning is represented as an iterative refinement of the weight-parameter estimate. Using tools from random matrix theory under high-dimensional asymptotics, we derive an exact formula for the generalization error as a function of reasoning depth, pretraining data amount, and context length. Our analysis reveals a sharp phase transition separating exponential and polynomial improvement, saturation, and overthinking, and characterizes how the optimal reasoning depth scales. We further show that deeper reasoning is most effective with sufficiently rich pretraining and in-context information, whereas limited pretraining or context makes longer reasoning prone to error amplification or saturation. We also validate these predictions through experiments on fully learned linear attention and softmax attention models. Our results provide a unified theoretical account of how test-time CoT depth affects generalization.

Summary. This paper presents an efficient quantum algorithm for computing the Hadamard product state of two quantum states derived from functions. By transforming the problem into the Fourier domain, the authors circumvent the exponential scaling issues of conventional methods. This results in a quantum circuit whose complexity depends on the function's smoothness rather than the grid size, enabling simulations of large systems with high efficiency.

Abstract

The coexistence of topological and spin-polarized electronic states within a single material platform provides an attractive route toward emergent quantum phenomena and spintronic functionalities. However, materials simultaneously exhibiting spin-gapless semiconducting (SGS) behavior and Weyl semimetallicity remain exceedingly rare. Here, using first-principles calculations, we identify the half-Heusler compound KCrSe as an ideal spin-gapless Weyl semimetal. Transport calculations reveal a weak temperature dependence of the longitudinal conductivity and relatively small Seebeck coefficients, providing further evidence of its SGS nature. KCrSe hosts a single pair of Weyl nodes-the minimum number permitted in a Weyl semimetal-located in close proximity to the Fermi level (E$_\text{F}$), resulting in exceptionally clean bulk and surface electronic spectra. The nontrivial Berry curvature associated with these Weyl nodes gives rise to sizable anomalous transport responses, including an anomalous Hall conductivity of $σ_{xy}^{A}\sim 90.76~\mathrm{S\,cm^{-1}}$ and an anomalous Nernst conductivity of $α_{xy}^{A}\sim 0.15~\mathrm{A\,m^{-1}K^{-1}}$ at E$_\text{F}$, with substantially enhanced values at lower energies. The combination of an ideal Weyl topology, fully spin-polarized low-energy states, and finite anomalous transport establishes KCrSe as a promising platform for designing high-efficiency topological spintronic devices.

Abstract

We investigate the quantum dynamics of a particle confined to a space curve within the thin-layer quantization framework. For a nondegenerate scalar transverse mode, torsion does not enter the local effective Hamiltonian, which contains only the curvature-induced scalar geometric potential. In contrast, when a degenerate transverse subspace is retained, the rotation of the Frenet normal frame becomes dynamically relevant and generates a matrix-valued Abelian gauge potential. Using a projection-based derivation in a co-rotating Frenet-frame basis, we show that this effective gauge potential is directly determined by the local torsion of the curve. The resulting effective Hamiltonian takes a gauge-covariant form and produces two transverse-mode branches whose parabolic dispersions are shifted in opposite directions in momentum space. For closed curves, the associated holonomy is controlled by the integrated torsion and leads to geometric interference. These results provide a direct realization of a Wilczek--Zee-type connection induced purely by spatial geometry in curved quantum waveguides. We further construct a classical-wave analogue using the degenerate bending modes of an isotropic elastic rod, demonstrating that the same torsion-induced gauge structure appears in continuum wave physics.
