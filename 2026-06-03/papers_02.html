Efficient Quantum Error Mitigation for Unitary k-Designs

Authors: Ayush Pancholy, K. Birgitta Whaley
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03891v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 1/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

Summary. This paper presents a novel, overhead-free error mitigation protocol for quantum circuits approximating unitary k-designs. It combines 'circuit balancing' with Pauli twirling to estimate and invert global depolarization caused by both depolarizing and coherent noise. The technique demonstrates significant fidelity improvements on superconducting quantum hardware, making complex simulations more feasible on noisy devices.

Why it may be interesting. The focus on mitigating errors in circuits designed to mimic complex physical dynamics (like quantum chaos) is highly relevant for simulating strongly correlated or non-equilibrium quantum systems using current NISQ hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing efficient quantum error mitigation techniques for quantum circuits representing unitary k-designs, especially in the presence of both depolarizing and coherent errors, without incurring significant quantum overhead.

Main result. The proposed method significantly reduces average random circuit infidelity on both simulated and real superconducting quantum hardware (IBM Fez) without adding two-qubit gate overhead.

Method. The authors introduce 'circuit balancing' to estimate global depolarization by leveraging the structure of k-design Pauli support distributions, followed by inverting the noise model using Pauli twirling.

Model / system. The work analyzes quantum circuits approximating unitary k-designs, relevant for simulating quantum chaos (e.g., near black holes). Testing is performed on superconducting quantum computers (IBM Fez) and simulated for various connectivity graphs.

Key observables. Mean Hellinger distance, Loschmidt echo survival probability, and estimated global depolarizing parameters ($\lambda_{ ext{avg}, 2Q}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Depolarizing parameter ($\lambda$), coherent error angle ($ heta$), circuit depth, and the number of twirls ($t$).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the availability of regular device benchmarking data and relies on asymptotic scaling arguments for the required number of twirls.

Figures summary. Figures compare Hellinger distance reduction across different error models (depolarizing only vs. depolarizing + coherent) and show that circuit balancing and twirling stabilize fidelity on real hardware.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the need for mitigation in k-designs, deriving theoretical bounds for global depolarization on structured graphs, introducing circuit balancing to optimize local error variance, and finally applying Pauli twirling to invert the noise model for practical error suppression.

Abstract

Quantum circuit ensembles that have the properties of unitary k-designs represent applications where there is no obvious bias toward any particular Pauli support, as is the case in simulating systems exhibiting ''quantum chaos,'' which range from quantum dynamics near black holes to gapless spin fluid analysis. However, noisy hardware makes quantum circuits prone to a myriad of error sources, of which depolarizing and coherent error can be particularly destructive. To combat depolarizing error, popular techniques typically involve circuit or gate folding, which can be time-intensive procedures due to increased circuit depth and shot overhead. Other tensor-network-based mitigation techniques suffer from intractability in high-entanglement regimes. In this work, we leverage the structure of unitary k-design Pauli support distributions by introducing a technique we name ''circuit balancing,'' along with gate benchmarking data, in order to estimate circuit-wide depolarization. We describe how to invert the diagnosed circuit depolarization even in the presence of coherent error, via Pauli twirling. We provide asymptotics to estimate the number of twirls needed to maintain a desired output fidelity. We test our method numerically in a variety of simulation settings and find that it can significantly reduce average random circuit infidelity. Further, we employ our methods to find significant infidelity reductions when running a random circuit ensemble on a contemporary superconducting quantum computer, IBM Fez. Overall, we show that the method effectively reduces gate-based error for unitary k-designs without incurring any two-qubit gate overhead.

Exact Solutions for Spin Conserving Models and the Wigner-Araki-Yanase Theorem

Summary. This paper re-examines the Wigner-Araki-Yanase theorem by developing a general model based on exact total angular momentum conservation. Using both density matrix tracing and Kraus operators, the authors show that measurement outcomes are constrained by apparatus uncertainties, providing a deeper physical understanding of measurement limitations.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum measurement theory, providing a rigorous, generalized framework for understanding how conservation laws constrain measurement outcomes beyond standard assumptions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the fundamental limitations of quantum measurement in the presence of additive conservation laws, specifically addressing the Wigner-Araki-Yanase (WAY) theorem.

Main result. By developing a general angular momentum conserving model of measurement, the authors provide a simple explanation for the major implications of the WAY theorem, showing that measurement errors depend on apparatus uncertainty.

Method. The analysis uses both tracing out the apparatus from the density matrix and developing a system-only channel model based on Kraus operators to analyze the measurement process.

Model / system. The model involves a quantum system (e.g., spin) interacting with an apparatus (like a Stern-Gerlach device) under the assumption of exact total angular momentum conservation.

Key observables. Expected values of angular momenta ($E(L_{i,z,SA})$), the final system density matrix ($ ho_{f,S}$), and the correlation parameters ($eta_x, eta_z$).

Important parameters / regimes. The correlation parameters $eta_x$ and $eta_z$ characterizing the non-orthogonality of apparatus states.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption is the exact conservation of total angular momentum ($L_T$) during measurement, moving beyond the standard von Neumann model assumptions.

Paper structure. The paper first reviews the WAY theorem and then develops a general angular momentum conserving model. It proceeds by calculating the evolution using unitary operators and then deriving the system-only channel model via Kraus operators to analyze the resulting state.

Abstract

The Wigner-Araki-Yanase (WAY) theorem is a well-known theorem regarding limitations of quantum measurement in the presence of additive conservation laws. Under the assumptions of the von Neumann measurement model, for which the system conserved quantity $L_{S}$ is bounded, given a conserved total additive system plus apparatus quantity $L_{SA}$, the measurement operator $E_{S}$ must commute with $L_{S}$. Prior proofs have exploited the properties of unitary evolution constrained by momentum conserving operations that tend to obscure the physical nature of the WAY theorem and as well lead to bounds on performance. As it is generally agreed that momentum is always exactly conserved in measurement, we instead develop a general angular momentum conserving model of measurement. This model is shown to lead to a simple explanation of the major implications of the WAY theorem and provides exact results of the effects of measurement based on the apparatus model. This is shown by both tracing the apparatus from the density matrix and also via a system-only channel model based on Kraus operators.

Informational completeness of qubit measurements and IC preservability of qubit channels: Characterization and Quantification

Summary. This paper develops quantitative measures for informational completeness of quantum measurements and the ability of quantum channels to preserve this property. Using qubit systems, the authors establish that SIC measurements saturate the upper bound for IC. Crucially, they derive bounds linking this preservation measure to the channel's absolute output coherence, providing a deep conceptual link between information theory and quantum coherence.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides rigorous, quantitative tools to measure how much information a quantum channel preserves about the initial state, linking fundamental concepts like measurement distinguishability (IC) to coherence measures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to quantify the informational completeness (IC) of quantum measurements and characterize the ability of quantum channels to preserve this property (IC preservability).

Main result. The authors establish a quantitative framework showing that the IC of qubit SIC measurements is $1/\sqrt{6}$, and they derive bounds relating IC preservability ($ ilde{D}(\Lambda)$) to the channel's absolute output coherence ($C(\Lambda)$).

Method. The work involves defining and characterizing faithful measures for informational completeness, analyzing the action of quantum channels in the Heisenberg picture, and using singular value decompositions of the channel map.

Model / system. The primary focus is on qubit systems ($d=2$), analyzing general quantum measurements (POVMs) and arbitrary quantum channels ($\Lambda$) acting on these qubits.

Key observables. Informational Completeness ($D(A)$), Informational Completeness-Preservability ($ ilde{D}(\Lambda)$), and Absolute Output Coherence ($C(\Lambda)$).

Important parameters / regimes. The dimension $d=2$ (qubits); the singular values ($\lambda_i$) of the channel $\Lambda$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is heavily focused on the qubit case; the derivation of bounds for $ ilde{D}(\Lambda)$ relies on assuming the measurement $A'$ is a qubit SIC measurement.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first quantifying IC for measurements (establishing the $1/\sqrt{6}$ bound via SIC measurements), then defining and analyzing IC preservability ($ ilde{D}(\Lambda)$) for channels, and finally establishing the relationship between $ ilde{D}(\Lambda)$ and the absolute output coherence $C(\Lambda)$.

Abstract

Informationally complete (IC) measurements are a useful class of measurements, as their outcome statistics uniquely determine an unknown quantum state. Hence, they are important for certain tasks such as quantum state tomography, quantum process tomography, etc. In this work, we study the quantification of informational completeness for arbitrary quantum measurements by introducing and characterizing a faithful measure for it. We explicitly evaluate the informational completeness of qubit symmetric informationally complete (SIC) measurements and show that it is an upper bound for all qubit minimal informationally complete measurements. Furthermore, by introducing a faithful measure, we try to quantify and characterize the ability of an arbitrary quantum channel to preserve informational completeness of any IC measurement when the channel acts on it in the Heisenberg picture. We call this measure informational completeness-preservability (IC preservability) of quantum channels. After studying its properties, we finally establish its relation to another quantity, namely, the absolute output coherence of a quantum channel, which quantifies the minimum amount of coherence (w.r.t. an arbitrary incoherent basis) that can always be obtained from the output of that channel. Thus, in this work, not only do we try to provide a quantitative framework for studying both the informational completeness of quantum measurements and the ability of quantum channels to preserve it, but we also try to offer key insight into the conceptual relation between informational completeness and quantum coherence.

Operator spreading in random circuits with orthogonal or symplectic symmetry

Summary. This work analyzes how symmetry constraints (orthogonal or symplectic) modify operator spreading in random quantum circuits compared to the standard unitary case. It shows that these symmetries force the system's state representation into a ternary structure and predict specific, symmetry-dependent values for key dynamical parameters like the butterfly velocity. This is vital for understanding quantum thermalization in physical systems with underlying symmetries.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a detailed, mathematically rigorous framework for understanding how fundamental symmetries (like time-reversal or particle-hole symmetry) constrain the scrambling dynamics in quantum many-body systems, which is crucial for understanding thermalization and quantum chaos.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates operator spreading in random quantum circuits when the gates are constrained by orthogonal or symplectic symmetries, contrasting these results with the standard unitary-invariant case.

Main result. For orthogonal/symplectic circuits, the Pauli-string weights relax to a ternary-valued structure, and the domain wall separating trivial and scrambled regions has a finite width, unlike the sharp wall in the unitary case.

Method. The analysis uses the Heisenberg picture evolution, deriving closed Markovian equations for projected ternary-string distributions and modeling the dynamics via drift-diffusion equations.

Model / system. The system is a random quantum circuit acting on qudits ($q$-dimensional degrees of freedom) arranged in a 1D lattice, with gates drawn from orthogonal or symplectic invariant ensembles.

Key observables. Operator spreading, butterfly velocity ($v_B$), diffusion constant ($D$), and the structure of the steady-state Pauli-string weights ($ar{ ho}_{ar{p}}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Qudit dimension ($q$), symmetry type (orthogonal/symplectic), and the degree of randomness (Haar vs. Brownian).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on projecting the full state space onto a simplified binary or ternary string distribution to derive tractable Markovian evolution equations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the front position and width of operator spreading for random unitary circuits, comparing analytical approximations with numerical simulations.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the general problem and the difference between symmetry classes, deriving the Markovian evolution equations for the projected ternary distribution, and finally deriving the drift-diffusion equation governing the long-time dynamics.

Abstract

We investigate operator spreading in random quantum circuits with gates drawn from orthogonal-invariant or symplectic-invariant ensembles, revealing several key distinctions from the well-studied unitary-invariant case. We find that the ensemble-averaged Pauli-string weights relax to a ternary-valued structure, instead of the binary structure of unitary-invariant circuits. For orthogonal- or symplectic-invariant circuits, the domain wall separating trivial and scrambled regions has a finite width even for Haar-random gates, whereas domain walls are sharp for Haar-distributed random unitary circuits. We further find a fundamental dichotomy between random circuits with two-qubit gates from the two disconnected components of the orthogonal group: While the butterfly velocity for the special orthogonal ensemble lies between zero and the Haar value, the negative-determinant sector exhibits a non-zero lower bound for any gate distribution. Moreover, for qudit size $q=2$, the butterfly velocity can exceed that of the Haar-random ensemble.

Summary. This paper proposes that spatial subregions in quantum gravity can be described by pure quantum states, not mixed ones. It introduces a novel holographic formula for calculating the entanglement entropy of these subregions. The key achievement is demonstrating that this formula is mathematically consistent with core principles of quantum information theory, such as strong subadditivity.

Why it may be interesting. While highly theoretical, the rigorous derivation of entanglement entropy in a gravitational context provides deep insights into how quantum information principles (like SSA) must constrain the structure of spacetime itself, which is relevant for understanding quantum correlations in complex many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses whether spatial subregions in quantum gravity can be assigned pure quantum states, moving beyond the standard mixed reduced density matrix description.

Main result. It proposes a holographic prescription for the entanglement entropy that satisfies fundamental consistency conditions like strong subadditivity and complementarity, and reduces to known formulas like Ryu-Takayanagi.

Method. The authors use a partially frozen gravitational path integral (GPI) to prepare the state and conjecture a holographic formula involving minimizing over surfaces constrained by the frozen region.

Model / system. The system is quantum gravity, modeled using a gravitational path integral coupled to a QFT, focusing on the entanglement structure of spatial subregions within a larger, fixed gravitational background.

Key observables. Entanglement Entropy ($S_{|F}A$), the pure state $|\Psi( ext{MF} / \mathbb{Z}_2) angle_{ ext{F} \cup \partial ext{F}}$, and the area of minimal surfaces ($ ext{Area}(\gamma_{|F}(eA))$).

Important parameters / regimes. The gravitational constant ($G_N$), the geometry of the frozen region ($ ext{F}$), and the specific subregions ($ ext{A}, ext{B}$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the partially frozen GPI is dominated by a saddle geometry, and the derivation relies on specific constraints related to the frozen subregion.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the pure state nature of the subregion using the GPI, then conjectures the holographic EE formula, and finally verifies its consistency by checking fundamental properties like SSA and complementarity.

Abstract

It is proposed that spatial subregions in quantum gravity can be assigned pure states, rather than mixed reduced density matrices. The state is prepared by a partially frozen gravitational path integral, in which a spacetime subregion containing the spatial subregion is fixed while the field configurations and ambient geometry are summed over. In the semiclassical regime, we further propose a holographic prescription for the entanglement entropy of bipartitions of this state, with a frozen-region analogue of the homology constraint. The prescription satisfies nontrivial self-consistency conditions, including strong subadditivity, complementarity, and entanglement wedge nesting, and reproduces several known entropy formulas in holography and gravity as special cases. The construction suggests an observer-dependent entanglement wedge labeled by the frozen subregion.

Testing the ${\rm ER=EPR}$ conjecture with entangled photons

Authors: Kimet Jusufi, Francisco S. N. Lobo, Douglas Singleton
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02943v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5

Summary. This work provides a rigorous theoretical framework to test the ER=EPR conjecture using entangled photons. By calculating the gravitational self-energy within a regularized wormhole geometry, the authors show that the energy scales match predictions derived from entanglement entropy. The results suggest that quantum gravity effects for photons are extremely weak, leading to an immunity from collapse over cosmological timescales.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a concrete, quantitative link between quantum entanglement (information theory) and spacetime geometry (general relativity) using ultra-relativistic quanta, which is a major frontier in theoretical physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper tests the ER=EPR conjecture by investigating the gravitational self-energy associated with entangled photon pairs.

Main result. The gravitational self-energy is found to be suppressed by the photon's wavelength ($1/L$), and the entanglement entropy calculation successfully reproduces this scaling, confirming the conjecture.

Method. The authors use a regularized Aichelburg-Sexl shock-wave metric to model the geometry and calculate the self-energy, complementing this with an entanglement-entropy interpretation derived from CFT.

Model / system. The system involves entangled photon pairs generated in $e^+e^- o 2\gamma$, modeled using a regularized shock-wave metric that forms a zero-throat Einstein-Rosen wormhole.

Key observables. Gravitational Self-Energy (EGSE), Entanglement Entropy (SEE), and the collapse time ($ au_{ ext{collapse}}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Longitudinal extent ($L$), transverse separation ($d$), and the string-inspired smearing length scale ($l_0$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on linearizing the metric and using a T-duality-inspired smearing scale ($l_0$) to regularize divergences.

Paper structure. The paper develops the regularized geometry, calculates the EGSE for separated pairs, and then provides a complementary calculation using entanglement entropy to confirm the scaling and normalization of the self-energy.

Abstract

We regularize the Aichelburg-Sexl shock-wave metric for massless particles by smearing the point-like source over a string-inspired length scale $l_0$, obtaining a singularity-free gravitational potential. A coordinate transformation reveals that the transverse geometry is a zero-throat Einstein-Rosen wormhole, providing an explicit geometric realization of the ER=EPR conjecture for entangled photons. Crucially, we show that the gravitational self-energy depends on the photon's longitudinal extent $L$ (its wavelength) and, for a transversely separated photon pair, is suppressed by a factor $1/L$, giving $E^{\rm GSE}\sim 4G(\hbarω)^2/(c^4 L)\ln(d^2/l_0^2)$. For the coincident back-to-back pair created in $e^{+} e^{-}\to2γ$, the wormhole carries no additional binding energy; the logarithmic interaction energy emerges only after the entangled photons separate to a distance $d$, stretching the ER bridge. We further provide an entanglement-entropy interpretation: by computing the entanglement entropy of null intervals in the shock-wave geometry and introducing an effective entanglement temperature $k_B T_{\rm ent}\sim\hbar c/(2πL)$, we recover the same scaling and normalization of the gravitational self-energy. For optical photons the corresponding collapse time exceeds $10^{30}$ years, making isolated photons immune to gravity-induced wave-function collapse. These findings establish a rigorous playground for testing ER=EPR and reveal a deep suppression of quantum-gravity effects for ultra-relativistic quanta.

Abstract

The interplay between disorder and topology has become a central theme in condensed matter physics. Disorder can not only destroy topological phases but also induce them, as exemplified by the topological Anderson insulator (TAI). Here we show that, in close analogy, disorder can drive the clean-limit, time-reversal-broken(T-broken) quantum spin Hall state of ferromagnetic(FM) monolayer MnBi4Te7 into a quantum anomalous Hall phase, which was called topological Anderson Chern insulator (TACI). Using density functional theory (DFT) and nonequilibrium Green's func tion (NEGF) calculations in the presence of disorder, we identify disorder induced phases-including T-broken TAI, TACI, Normal insulator, etc., then construct a comprehensive phase diagram. To discriminate multiple phases in the strong disorder regime, we further use the density of states computed within the self-consistent Born approximation (SCBA), which in particular distinguishes gapped and ungapped topological phases. We find that the two effective band inversions of Hamiltonian are suppressed at distinct critical disorder strengths; the survival of a single inversion over a finite disorder window stabilizes the TACI. Remarkably, at strong disorder, we further propose a zero Hall plateau insulating state characterized by an insulating bulk and edge channels subject to diffusive scattering that can coexist with the TACI. This behavior is distinct from a conventional band-gap Chern insulator and provides a clear experimental signature.

Summary. This paper develops a rigorous theory for generating complex quantum state ensembles by implementing a reverse-time quantum diffusion process. It shows that the reverse dynamics is a controlled quantum diffusion whose Hamiltonian acts as a quantum score function. This provides a general, principled method for quantum state preparation from simple noise distributions.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful, general mathematical tool for quantum state preparation and tomography, linking modern machine learning generative models (diffusion models) directly to fundamental open quantum system dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper establishes a principled framework to generate complex quantum state ensembles by reversing a quantum diffusion process, analogous to classical score-based generative models.

Main result. It derives the exact reverse-time dynamics, showing it is a feedback-controlled quantum diffusion governed by a state-dependent Hamiltonian that acts as the quantum analogue of the score function.

Method. The derivation involves analyzing the reverse-time transition kernel using Bayes' formula and solving the corresponding backward Kolmogorov equation to find the required stochastic differential equation (SDE).

Model / system. The system involves quantum states evolving via continuous measurement, modeled by a forward Stochastic Schrödinger Equation (SSE) leading to an invariant distribution. The reverse process is a controlled quantum diffusion.

Key observables. The Wasserstein distance $W_1( ilde{\mu}_t, \mu_0)$ is used to confirm convergence to the target distribution $\mu_0$. The mean Frobenius norm of the reverse Hamiltonian $\mathbb{E}[\| ilde{H}_t( ilde{\psi}_t)\|^2]$ is also monitored.

Abstract

We establish a reverse-time denoising theory for quantum diffusions of continuously measured quantum systems. Starting from the stochastic Schrödinger equation of a forward noising dynamics, we derive the exact reverse-time dynamics for quantum trajectories, whose law coincides with the time-reversal of the original process. We prove that the denoising dynamics is a physically admissible quantum diffusion, with the same measurement-induced noise but a state-dependent feedback Hamiltonian, a direct analogue of the "score function" of generative classical diffusion models. This provides a principled framework for converting samples of a simple distribution into those of a more complex ensemble of quantum states. We show how the denoising dynamics can be directly learnt from forward trajectory data, and how to exploit purification to initialise the denoising process.

Abstract

Identifying a materials platform for creating qubits that are both tunable and resilient towards environmental noise is one of the main hurdles that need to be overcome to realize quantum computation that is practically useful. One pursued avenue to this end is to use superconducting qubits with intrinsic spin-dependent interactions, such as spin-orbit coupling or magnetism. However, the recently discovered class of materials known as altermagnets remain largely unexplored in this context. We here use microscopic calculations to determine how the properties of superconducting qubits are modified when altermagnetic Josephson junctions are included. The key qubit performance parameters, including splitting, anharmonicity, decoherence, and single/coupled-qubit gate operation times, display rich behavior depending on the characteristic properties of the altermagnetic material, such as the strength of the Néel field and the crystallographic orientation of the altermagnetic relative interfaces in the system. We focus in particular on the transmon design and show that the qubit is very well protected against decoherence and simultaneously shows superior anharmonicity both near 0-$π$ transition points and when it is in a $φ$-state. We propose that by using strain, the altermagnetic qubit can be moved out of its protected regime to enable faster gate-operation times, and then moved back to its protected state. We also discuss how the altermagnetic properties influence flux qubits and fluxonium. Our results suggest that integration of altermagnetic materials into existing superconducting qubit design can substantially improve their performance due to the unique properties of the altermagnetic band-structure.
