Summary. This paper develops a Global Adiabatic Criterion (GAC) to explain fast topological photon transfer in Fock-state lattices. It proves that the key to speed is minimizing the variance of the nonadiabatic coupling, not just maintaining a constant energy gap. The optimal protocol uses a sinusoidal coupling profile, predicting significantly faster and more efficient transfer times than previously reported.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a quantitative, general theoretical framework for optimizing quantum light transport, moving beyond simple gap-closing arguments to focus on the uniformity of nonadiabatic dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To provide a rigorous explanation for the fast topological photon transfer observed in Fock-state lattices and establish a general framework for fast topological photonics engineering.

Main result. The essential condition for fast transfer is the vanishing temporal variance of the nonadiabatic coupling factor ($\sigma_Q^2 = 0$), which is achieved optimally using a sinusoidal coupling profile ($\alpha=1$).

Method. The authors developed a Global Adiabatic Criterion (GAC) to bound infidelity using the mean and variance of the nonadiabatic factor, analyzing power-law coupling profiles.

Model / system. The system is modeled using a two-mode Jaynes-Cummings (JC) model applied to Fock-state lattices (FSLs), which simulates topological photon transfer.

Key observables. Transferred photon number ($ar{n}_2$), infidelity ($\epsilon$), and the nonadiabaticity variance ($\sigma_Q^2$).

Important parameters / regimes. Optimal transfer duration for a five-photon state is predicted to be 161 ns, showing a linear scaling with photon number $N$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis incorporates experimental decoherence parameters and relies on the validity of the Global Adiabatic Criterion.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the setup (JC model, FSL), compare fidelity/photon number vs. coupling exponent ($\alpha$), and show the variance $\sigma_Q^2$ vanishing only for the sinusoidal case ($\alpha=1$).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical model and the GAC, analyzes power-law coupling profiles, determines the optimal profile by minimizing nonadiabaticity variance, and finally predicts significantly faster transfer times compared to previous experimental benchmarks.

Abstract

Topological state transfer in Fock-state lattices has been demonstrated with high speed using sinusoidal profiles of coupling, yet the underlying reason has remained unclear. A global adiabatic criterion (GAC) is developed to bound the infidelity by the mean and variance of the nonadiabatic factor. The GAC reveals that the key to fast transfer is not a constant energy gap but the vanishing nonadiabaticity variance. For power-law coupling profiles, the variance vanishes only for the sinusoidal shape, which is thus globally optimal. Incorporating experimental decoherence parameters, it is predicted that the optimal transfer duration for a five-photon state is 161 ns, far shorter than 600 ns used in the experiment, reducing time by over 73% while increasing transferred photons by 29%. The optimal duration follow a simple linear scaling with photon number, providing a practical guideline. Through constructing an alternative constant-gap coupling family, it is confirmed that a constant gap alone is not sufficient for fast topological photon transfer. The essential condition is uniformity of nonadiabaticity. This work offers a rigorous explanation for the observed speed and a general framework for fast topological photonics engineering.

Summary. This work applies scaling laws from deep learning to variational quantum simulations of spin models. Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether the scaling laws observed in modern neural networks can be used to characterize the computational complexity of solving physical problems using Neural-Network Quantum States (NQS).

Main result. The accuracy measure ($V$-score) decays as a power law in training compute, and the scaling exponent systematically decreases with physical frustration, suggesting it quantifies the ground state's representational difficulty.

Method. The authors use a variational approach with transformer wave functions to approximate ground states, fitting the resulting error metric ($V$-score) to a power law dependent on training compute and system size.

Model / system. The study focuses on the $J_1$-$J_2$ Heisenberg model on 2D lattices (square and triangular) to generate target ground states spanning different levels of magnetic frustration.

Key observables. The $V$-score (a measure of variational accuracy), and the scaling exponents ($\alpha, eta$) derived from the power-law fit of the $V$-score vs. compute.

Important parameters / regimes. Frustration level ($J_2/J_1$ ratio), system size ($L imes L$ up to $20 imes 20$), and training compute ($f$).

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption is that the scaling behavior follows a power law, and that the training compute scales linearly with system size ($f \approx aN$).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of applying ML scaling laws to physics, details the use of transformer wave functions for the $J_1-J_2$ model, presents the power-law fitting and data collapse, and concludes by interpreting the scaling exponent as a measure of physical complexity.

Abstract

Scaling laws, the power-law relations between loss, architecture size, and compute observed in modern neural networks, offer a quantitative way to characterize the complexity of a learning problem, with the exponent governing the decay of the loss reflecting how rapidly additional resources translate into improved accuracy, and thus how hard the target is to learn. Whether an analogous framework can characterize the complexity of physical problems remains open. We address this question for Neural-Network Quantum States, a leading variational approach for strongly correlated quantum many-body systems. Using transformer wave functions to approximate ground states of the $J_1$-$J_2$ Heisenberg model on triangular and square lattices with up to $20\times 20$ sites, we find that the $V$-score, a measure of accuracy of a variational state, decays as a power law in training compute. Under an appropriate rescaling of compute, results for different system sizes collapse onto a single curve, analogous to scaling collapse in critical phenomena. The resulting power law is, to a good approximation, independent of the number of sites, showing that the transformer Ansatz is size-consistent for the systems considered. The exponent decreases systematically with frustration, identifying it as a quantitative measure of representational difficulty of the ground state and establishing scaling laws as a general framework for benchmarking variational ansätze.

Abstract

We present a novel method for full vector magnetometry using nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers. Simulated data is analyzed using deep neural networks, whose efficacy we expect to translate very well to experiments. Our method yields sensitivities between $5~\mathrm{pT}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ and $100~\mathrm{pT}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ across different magnetic field vector components, while achieving approximately $\mathrm{nT}$ accuracy at a signal-to-noise (SNR) ratio of $70~\mathrm{dB}$. Summary. This paper develops a theory for an energy-selective quantum search using the natural phase accumulation from an Ising Hamiltonian's time evolution. It shows that this method achieves Grover-like speedups, while also providing crucial theoretical tools to correct for spectral shifts caused by real-world Hamiltonian correlations. This advances the understanding of quantum computation on disordered physical systems.

Why it may be interesting. The work provides a deep theoretical link between quantum search algorithms and the spectral properties of disordered Hamiltonians, offering insights into how physical constraints (like spectral correlations) affect quantum computational scaling.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates an energy-selective quantum search primitive that uses the physical evolution of an Ising Hamiltonian as a continuous phase oracle, contrasting it with standard Boolean oracles.

Main result. The algorithm successfully achieves a Grover-type amplification peak, and the required oracle calls scale according to a square-root law in the annealed Gaussian limit.

Method. The analysis employs spectral theory, generating functions, and recurrence relations to model the quantum amplitude evolution in energy space.

Model / system. The system is modeled by the Ising Hamiltonian, $H(\mathbf{s})$, which describes disordered magnets and is used in optimization problems. The oracle is the time evolution operator $U_T = \exp(-iTH)$, which imparts a phase proportional to the state's energy $E_s$.

Key observables. The primary observables are the resonance peak position, width, and height, which determine the success probability of the search.

Important parameters / regimes. Key parameters include the evolution time $T$, the target energy $E^*$, and the correlation-induced spectral shift $\varphi_0 = O(n^{-3})$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on approximations such as the annealed Gaussian density of states and the ability to correct for spectral correlations using spectral symmetrization or iterative calibration.

Figures summary. Figures compare the discrete random Ising spectrum response against the annealed Gaussian prediction, illustrating the measurable shift and distortion caused by spectral correlations.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the energy-selective oracle to deriving the recurrence relations, analyzing the idealized annealed Gaussian case, and finally tackling the complexities introduced by spectral correlations through calibration techniques.

Abstract

Ising Hamiltonians are basic models of disordered magnets and a standard language for quantum and classical optimization. We study an energy-selective quantum search primitive in which the physical evolution \(\exp(-\mathrm{i} T H)\) is used directly as a Hamiltonian phase oracle. Unlike a Boolean oracle, this oracle marks configurations continuously by their phases and selects a finite resonance band rather than a preassigned marked set. We show that alternating it with the Grover diffusion operator nevertheless produces a Grover-type amplification peak. An exact spectral recurrence and a generating-function representation determine the peak position, width, and height. For an annealed Gaussian density of states, target energies in a high-density tail require \(Θ(\sqrt{2^n/M})\) oracle calls when the resonance contains \(M\) configurations. For random Ising spectra, overlap-induced correlations shift and distort the peak; spectral symmetrization and iterative calibration remove this detuning for prescribed-energy targeting.

Abstract

Nonreciprocal interactions in active matter provide interesting structure and dynamics. Here we investigate chemophoretic systems in which nonreciprocity arises from the asymmetric coupling between agents: first species produces certain chemicals and the other phoretically responds to it. This leads to phase separation at varying scales. Our study uncovers a re-entrant steady-state phase diagram as the nature of the coupling changes from chemoattractive to chemorepulsive character. Chemoattraction provides sustained domain growth, leading to macrophase separation via cluster coalescence. Aggregation in the chemorepulsive case, on the other hand, leads to a steady-state situation that displays phase separation only at a microscale, owing to strong caging effect and frequent fragmentation. The overall far-from-steady-state dynamics is quantified via calculations of growth exponents, cluster transition matrices, and mean-squared displacements.

Abstract

We present the results of a resonant X-ray diffraction experiment, resolving both charge and spin textures in the intermetallic topological magnet Eu(Al$_{1-x}$Ga$_{x}$)$_4$, $x$ = 0.1. Below $\approx$ 75 K the system develops a charge density wave (CDW) with propagation vector kCDW ~ (0, 0, 0.18). The CDW order parameter grows monotonically on cooling until ~ 15 K, when a sudden decrease in the CDW amplitude occurs. Pairs of magnetic satellites of the (0, 0, 8) Bragg reflection corresponding to two distinct domains, k = ($\pm δ_\text{m}$, 0, 0), k2 = (0, $\pmδ_\text{m}$, 0), $δ_\text{m} = 0.2002(4)$ were studied at the Eu L3 edge, appearing below TN = 14.8 K. This behaviour is unlike the x = 0 material, in which a spin density wave forms first, transitioning to a helical ground state on cooling through intermediate phases. Future theoretical work on the Eu electronic ground state, supported by related experiments, will help understand the effects of Ga substitution on the evolution of the magnetic structure.

Abstract

In chiral magnets, magnetic skyrmions are typically stabilized by the competition between exchange and Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya interactions under an external magnetic field, while the role of lattice degrees of freedom has received comparatively less attention. Here we study how spin-phonon (SP) coupling modifies magnetic interactions and the resulting spin textures in a two-dimensional skyrmion model in the square lattice. Using Monte Carlo simulations, we compare two simplified models describing the SP coupling: the Einstein site-phonon (ESP) and bond-phonon (BP) models. We find that ESP coupling stabilizes skyrmion crystals in field regimes that are topologically trivial in the uncoupled model and also induces additional textures, including meron-antimeron (M-aM) crystals and mixed skyrmion-bimeron (SkX-Bm) phases. Furthermore, for sufficiently strong phonon coupling, the conventional triple-$\textbf{q}$ hexagonal skyrmion lattice is distorted into a double-$\textbf{q}$ square skyrmion lattice. Overall, our results show that lattice effects provide a simple mechanism to tune topological magnetic phases.
