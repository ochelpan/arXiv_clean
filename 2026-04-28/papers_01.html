⭐ Dynamical preparation of U(1) quantum spin liquids in an analogue quantum simulator

Highlighted author(s): Immanuel Bloch
Authors: Simon Karch, Melissa Will, Irene Prieto Rodriguez, Nikolas Liebster, SeungJung Huh, Michael Knap, Frank Pollmann, Clemens Kuhlenkamp, Immanuel Bloch, Monika Aidelsburger
Type: experiment · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24744v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 analog quantum simulation 5/5 · 🔥 exotic spin models in light-matter 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 3/5 · QC/QI experiment 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of experimentally preparing and verifying the many-body coherence and emergent gauge structure of U(1) quantum spin liquids, which are otherwise difficult to detect due to their fragile nature.

Main result. The researchers successfully prepared extended regions of U(1) quantum spin liquids using non-equilibrium semi-adiabatic ramps and directly observed large-scale many-body coherence and characteristic pinch-point signatures.

Method. An analogue quantum simulation using a caesium quantum gas microscope with a 2D optical superlattice, employing semi-adiabatic sweeps and round-trip interferometric protocols for coherence detection.

Summary. This paper reports the experimental realization and verification of U(1) quantum spin liquids in a large-scale quantum gas microscope. By using semi-adiabatic ramps, the authors prepare extended regions of the spin liquid and confirm the validity of Gauss's law. They observe hallmark signatures of the U(1) gauge structure, such as pinch points in the structure factor, and use interferometry to prove large-scale many-body coherence. This work establishes non-equilibrium protocols as a vital tool for exploring exotic phases of matter.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A large-scale (>3,000 sites) 2D Bose-Hubbard model in a square optical superlattice, which implements an effective 2D monomer-dimer model equivalent to a (2+1)D U(1) lattice gauge theory.

Key observables. Gauss's law validity, real-space dimer-dimer correlations, momentum-space pinch points, and many-body coherence via subsystem return probabilities.

Important parameters / regimes. Tunnel coupling J/h approx 130 Hz, staggered potential delta/J approx 12, on-site interaction U/J approx 24, and effective coupling J_eff.

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonian does not support the target RK wavefunction as its ground state, necessitating a non-equilibrium preparation approach; 2D U(1) QSLs are theoretically unstable to perturbations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the monomer-dimer model and preparation protocol; Figure 2 illustrates the experimental setup and Gauss's law verification; Figure 5 details the round-trip interferometric protocol and phase imprinting; Figure 6 presents monomer-monomer correlations and correlation length estimates.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of QSL preparation, describes the experimental platform and effective gauge theory model, details the non-equilibrium preparation and detection methods, presents results on Gauss's law and gauge signatures, and concludes with a quantification of many-body coherence and spatial scales.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a powerful route for accessing highly-entangled, exotic states via non-equilibrium dynamics that are not accessible in thermal equilibrium, providing a blueprint for simulating gauge theories using ultracold atoms.

Abstract

Locally constrained gauge theories underpin our understanding of fundamental interactions in particle physics and the emergent behaviour of quantum materials. In strongly correlated systems, they can give rise to quantum spin liquids that lack conventional order and are defined by coherent superpositions of an extensive number of many-body configurations. Realising and probing such exotic states experimentally is an outstanding challenge both in solid-state and synthetic quantum systems, not least due to the difficulty of detecting the fragile coherences between many-body states. Here, we report a large-scale (>3,000 sites) realisation of a two-dimensional U(1) lattice gauge theory with ultracold atoms in a square optical superlattice and demonstrate non-equilibrium preparation of extended regions of U(1) quantum spin liquids. We demonstrate Gauss's law validity in a quench experiment, enabled by a new microscopy technique for detecting doubly occupied sites. We observe characteristic real-space correlations and momentum-space pinch points, hallmarks of the emergent U(1) gauge structure. Using round-trip interferometric protocols, we directly observe large-scale coherence between many-body configurations, providing strong evidence for quantum spin liquid regions extending over ~100 lattice sites. Our results establish non-equilibrium quantum simulation protocols as a powerful route for accessing and probing exotic, highly-entangled states beyond those hosted by the engineered Hamiltonian in thermal equilibrium.

⭐ Optical depth dictates universal bounds on many-body decay in atomic ensembles

Highlighted author(s): Simon B. Jäger, Ana Asenjo-Garcia
Authors: Cosimo C. Rusconi, Eric Sierra, Wai-Keong Mok, Avishi Poddar, Simon B. Jäger, Ana Asenjo-Garcia
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24680v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 free-space correlated emission 5/5 · 🔥 Dicke superradiance 4/5 · 🔥 correlated / nonlocal dissipation 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Determining the universal scaling laws for the maximum photon emission rate and collective decay in spatially extended, free-space atomic ensembles, across both ordered arrays and disordered clouds.

Main result. The authors derive a universal law where the maximum emission rate scales as the product of the atom number and the system's optical depth (R* ~ N * OD), unifying different dimensionalities and regimes from independent emission to the Dicke limit.

Method. The study uses a mathematical mapping of the maximum decay rate to a ground-state problem of an effective spin Hamiltonian, employing integral operator approximations, Gelfand's formula, and semidefinite programming (SDP) relaxation.

Summary. This paper establishes that the optical depth is the fundamental parameter governing many-body cooperative emission in atomic ensembles. It proves a universal scaling law that applies to both ordered arrays and disordered clouds across 1D, 2D, and 3D geometries. The work also reveals that the observed emission rate depends heavily on the detector's numerical aperture, which can lead to different scaling observations compared to total emission. These findings provide a rigorous benchmark for predicting superradiant behavior in extended atomic systems.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Ensembles of N two-level atoms with a resonant dipole transition interacting via a Markovian master equation in free space, including both ordered lattices and disordered clouds in 1D, 2D, and 3D.

Key observables. Maximum emission rate (R*), maximum decay rate (Gamma_max), superradiant lifetime, and directional detection intensity.

Important parameters / regimes. Optical depth (OD), atomic density (rho), dimensionality (D), numerical aperture (NA), and interatomic separation (d/lambda_0).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes uniform or Gaussian atomic density distributions, large-density/large-system limits (k0L >> 1), and a scalar model for the dissipative interaction kernel.

Figures summary. Plots showing the scaling exponent alpha vs. dimensionality, numerical confirmation of decay rate scaling with detector aperture angle, and comparisons between exact and approximate integral evaluations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of cooperative emission limits, establishes the universal scaling law via optical depth, extends the derivation to different dimensionalities and geometries, analyzes the impact of detector aperture on observed rates, and validates results via numerical SDP and asymptotic analysis.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental, unified framework for understanding many-body cooperative effects in realistic, extended systems, bridging the gap between idealized Dicke models and experimental atomic clouds.

Abstract

Cooperative emission is well understood for idealized symmetric systems, but its limits in spatially extended, free-space ensembles remain an open question. Here, we derive a universal law for the scaling of the maximum photon emission rate with system size that unifies both ordered arrays and disordered atomic clouds in arbitrary dimensions at fixed density. We demonstrate that, for a fixed atomic density, the maximum emission rate scales universally as the product of the atom number and the system's optical depth, with the latter encoding the dimensional scaling across all regimes from independent emission to the Dicke limit. Furthermore, we establish a scaling law for directional detection, revealing that the observed rate depends on the detector's numerical aperture: small apertures yield Dicke-like quadratic scaling, whereas large apertures recover our integrated universal bound. Our results establish optical depth as the parameter governing many-body cooperative emission in both ordered and disordered ensembles, and reveal that directional and total-emission scalings must be carefully distinguished in experimental settings.

⭐ Realizing multi-orbital Emery models with ultracold atoms

Highlighted author(s): Ana Maria Rey
Authors: Conall McCabe, Jamie Boyd, Kaizhao Wang, Martin Lebrat, Cindy Regal, Adam Kaufman, Ana Maria Rey, Lukas Homeier
Type: both · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22955v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 analog quantum simulation 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The difficulty of simulating the complex multi-orbital physics of cuprate superconductors, specifically the interplay between copper and oxygen orbitals, using simplified one-band Hubbard models in ultracold atom platforms.

Main result. The authors propose an optical superlattice architecture that implements the three-band Emery model, demonstrating that the metal-insulator crossover and antiferromagnetic correlations are experimentally accessible and that effective single-band parameters can be learned via quantum quenches.

Method. The study combines an experimental proposal for a bichromatic optical superlattice with ab initio Wannier function construction, Determinant Quantum Monte Carlo (DQMC) simulations, and a Hamiltonian learning protocol based on quantum quenches.

Summary. This paper proposes a new way to use ultracold atoms to simulate the complex three-band Emery model, which is essential for understanding high-temperature superconductors like cuprates. By using a specialized bichromatic optical lattice, the researchers show how to control copper and oxygen orbital interactions independently. They demonstrate that the key physics, such as the metal-insulator transition, is reachable with current experimental temperatures. Additionally, they introduce a method to learn effective single-band parameters from experimental data, providing a bridge between complex multi-orbital simulations and simpler Hubbard models.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 2D optical superlattice using ultracold fermionic 6Li atoms in a bichromatic (short and long wavelength) lattice that creates a Lieb lattice geometry, implementing a three-band Emery model with Cu (d) and O (p) orbitals.

Key observables. Charge-ordering temperature, antiferromagnetic spin-spin correlations, incompressibility, Fermi surface shape, and effective single-band Hubbard parameters (t, t', U).

Important parameters / regimes. Charge-transfer energy (pd gap) delta_pd, Hubbard interactions Ud and Up, tunneling amplitudes t_pd and t_pp, and the ratio of lattice depths V_S/V_L.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that the low-energy physics can be captured by an effective single-band model in the large delta_pd limit and that the 4th band leakage is minimal in the cuprate-relevant regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the cuprate electronic structure and proposed superlattice; Figure 2 details the bichromatic lattice and parameter accessibility; Figure 3 compares the exact potential to the tight-binding model and shows band structure; Figure 5 illustrates the Hamiltonian learning protocol and spectral agreement; Figure 6 shows the gauge transformation for tunneling signs; Figure 9 shows incompressibility and spin correlations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific motivation (cuprate physics), proposes the experimental superlattice architecture, provides ab initio parameter extraction via Wannier functions, uses DQMC to investigate thermodynamic properties and phase transitions, presents a Hamiltonian learning protocol for parameter extraction, and concludes with an assessment of experimental feasibility and limitations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical pathway to bridge the gap between ultracold atom simulators and real-world strongly correlated materials by implementing a more realistic multi-orbital model and offering a method to map complex physics back to simpler, interpretable models.

Abstract

Strongly-correlated electrons in transition-metal oxides give rise to intriguing emergent phenomena, including high-temperature superconductivity in cuprates. While simplified one-band Hubbard models capture some aspects, explicitly describing the interplay of copper and oxygen orbitals -- as in the three-band Emery model -- is essential to capture the full phenomenology of cuprates. Quantum simulators based on ultracold atoms offer a promising route to study such systems in a controlled setting, but realizing realistic multi-orbital Hubbard models remains challenging. Here we propose an optical superlattice architecture that implements the three-band Emery model with ultracold fermions. By combining lattice beams with controllable interference, we engineer orbital degrees of freedom that reproduce key features of the cuprate band structure, while enabling independent control of orbital-dependent interactions and charge-transfer energy. We show that single-particle quantum walks can benchmark the resulting tight-binding model. Using determinant quantum Monte Carlo, we further investigate thermodynamic properties in the undoped regime and find a finite-temperature metal-insulator crossover accompanied by the onset of antiferromagnetic correlations accessible in current experiments. Finally, we apply a Hamiltonian learning protocol enabling to infer effective single-band Hubbard models from experimental realizations of Emery models. Our results provide a practical pathway to simulate multi-orbital Hubbard physics with quantum gas microscopes.

⭐ Enhanced Atom Capture via Multi-Frequency Magneto-Optical Trapping

Highlighted author(s): Nathan Cooper
Authors: Benjamin Hopton, Alexander Abbey, David Johnson, Daniele Baldolini, Matt Overton, Nathan Cooper, Joseph Aziz, Richard Howl, Lucia Hackermuller
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23221v1

Main problem. The need to increase the atom number and loading rate in Magneto-Optical Traps (MOTs) to improve the sensitivity and bandwidth of quantum technologies and fundamental physics tests.

Main result. Demonstrated that using multi-frequency cooling light can double the steady-state atom number and increase the loading rate by up to a factor of 4 compared to single-frequency MOTs.

Method. The researchers used acousto-optic modulation to create a single-sided multi-frequency spectrum and axicon lenses to create a ring-shaped beam, combined with PyLCP numerical simulations to model atomic deceleration.

Summary. This paper demonstrates a technique to significantly enhance the efficiency of Magneto-Optical Traps by using multiple, closely spaced optical frequencies in the cooling light. By avoiding previous pitfalls like heating and collision-induced losses through specific beam geometries, the authors achieved a fourfold increase in loading rate and a doubling of the steady-state atom number. This method is highly scalable and has direct implications for improving the sensitivity of portable quantum sensors and tests of fundamental physics like dark matter searches.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A Rubidium-87 (87Rb) magneto-optical trap utilizing a six-beam configuration with multi-frequency cooling light and a quadrupole magnetic field.

Key observables. Loading rate (R), steady-state atom number (Nss), capture velocity (vc), and loading time constant (tau).

Important parameters / regimes. Frequency detunings, frequency spacing (~5 MHz), magnetic field gradient (up to 22 G/cm), and beam waist radius (15 mm).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that the enhancement in MOT atom numbers carries over proportionally to the Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC) stage.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the frequency spectrum and velocity distribution; Fig 2 shows atom number as a function of frequency; Fig 3 compares loading rates and atom numbers vs magnetic gradient; Fig 4 shows predicted gains vs beam diameter; Fig 5 shows projected CSL parameter bounds; Fig 6 compares beam profiles.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MOT loading efficiency, describes the experimental setup for multi-frequency light generation and beam shaping, presents experimental results for atom number and loading rate, provides numerical simulations to validate and extrapolate results, and discusses applications in quantum sensing and fundamental physics.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a practical method for scaling up cold-atom sources, which is critical for improving the signal-to-noise ratio in atom interferometry and the precision of quantum-enhanced sensors.

Abstract

Magneto-optical traps are central to atomic and molecular quantum technologies and precision tests of fundamental physics, where both sensitivity and bandwidth scale strongly with atom number and loading rate. We demonstrate that employing multiple, closely spaced optical frequency components in the cooling light of a $^{87}$Rb magneto-optical trap -- without utilizing any additional slowing techniques -- can double the steady state atom number and increase the loading rate by up to a factor of 4, compared to a conventional single-frequency implementation. Subsequently, we capture up to $1.0(1)\times10^{10}$ atoms with a loading rate of up to $1.3(2)\times 10^{11}\,\mathrm{atoms\,s^{-1}}$ from a thermal background. Numerical simulations reproduce the observed trends and predict substantially larger gains for increased trap sizes beyond our experimental bounds. By re-examining earlier studies of multi-frequency atom capture in the context of modern experimental hardware and emerging applications, we show that previously identified limitations can be avoided and establish multi-frequency cooling as a practical and scalable route to high-flux cold-atom sources. These results have immediate applications in portable atom-based quantum sensing, where higher bandwidth and precision can be achieved without forgoing compactness, and in atom-interferometric tests of fundamental physics, which benefit from access to larger-mass quantum systems.

⭐ Dichroic Raman probes for chiral edge modes

Highlighted author(s): Johannes Knolle
Authors: Avedis Neehus, Johannes Knolle
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24208v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The difficulty of identifying and detecting charge-neutral chiral edge modes (CEM) in topological systems like Kitaev spin liquids using Raman spectroscopy, which is typically forbidden by momentum conservation selection rules.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that broken translational symmetry at realistic, curved edges allows for a Raman circular dichroism (RCD) signal that evades selection rules, providing a unique spectroscopic fingerprint of the Kitaev spin liquid phase.

Method. Theoretical calculation of the low-frequency Raman circular dichroism response using the Loudon-Fleury operator and numerical analysis of a punctured disk geometry with disorder averaging.

Summary. This paper proposes a new way to use Raman circular dichroism to detect the elusive chiral edge modes in Kitaev quantum spin liquids. It shows that while these modes are normally Raman-inactive due to momentum conservation, the broken symmetry at real physical edges allows a detectable signal to emerge. The study highlights that the signal's dependence on the Zeeman field and edge geometry serves as a unique fingerprint for the spin liquid phase. This provides a potential experimental roadmap for identifying fractionalized excitations in topological materials.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The Kitaev quantum spin liquid (KSL) model on a honeycomb lattice, including effects of an out-of-plane magnetic field (Zeeman field) and boundary-induced symmetry breaking.

Key observables. Raman circular dichroism (RCD), Raman intensity (I++), two-particle density of states (2DOS), and Raman center of energy (RCE).

Important parameters / regimes. Bulk gap (Delta), Zeeman field (h), edge geometry/length scale (L), energy scale (EL), and anisotropy (kappa).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglect of certain non-Kitaev terms on zig-zag edges due to sublattice imbalance; use of the Loudon-Fleury form for non-resonant Raman scattering.

Figures summary. Figure 1 sketches the experimental protocol with nano-structured holes; Figure 2 shows RCD in disorder-free and disordered disks; Figure 4 shows frequency dependence of RCD; Figure 5 illustrates RCE on hexagonal flakes; Figure 6 and 7 show Raman intensity dependence on Zeeman field and geometry.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of detecting CEMs, presents a theoretical framework using the Kitaev model, analyzes the impact of edge geometry and symmetry breaking on Raman selection rules, evaluates the robustness of the signal to disorder, and concludes with the implications for experimental identification of spin liquids.
