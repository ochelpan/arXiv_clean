Non-Hermitian corner skin effect in a two-dimensional photonic crystal

Authors: Huyen Thanh Phan, Katsunori Wakabayashi
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23209v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the second-order non-Hermitian skin effect (corner localization) in a continuous 2D photonic crystal, moving beyond traditional tight-binding models.

Main result. Demonstrated that a point gap in the complex eigenfrequency spectrum leads to the emergence of corner skin modes in truncated structures, distinguishing them from Hermitian topological corner states.

Method. Numerical simulations using the Finite Element Method (FEM) and extended plane-wave expansion, combined with non-Bloch band theory and complex Berry phase analysis.

Summary. This paper explores the non-Hermitian corner skin effect in a 2D photonic crystal made of lossy magneto-optical materials. The authors show that point gaps in the complex frequency spectrum cause electromagnetic waves to localize at the corners of finite structures. Unlike traditional topological corner states, these skin modes are driven by non-Hermitian topology and their number scales with the system size. This work is significant because it moves the study of non-Hermitian skin effects from simplified tight-binding models to more realistic, continuous photonic platforms.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 2D non-Hermitian photonic crystal composed of lossy magneto-optical materials arranged in a square unit cell, featuring broken inversion, mirror, and time-reversal symmetries.

Key observables. Complex eigenfrequencies, winding numbers, Chern numbers, and spatial field profiles of edge and corner states.

Important parameters / regimes. Point gaps in the complex spectrum, winding number (w), and the presence of nonreciprocity via the permittivity tensor.

Assumptions / limitations. The lattice constant is fixed at a=1, and the magnetization is assumed to be parallel to the z-direction.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows 1D vs 2D band structure topology; Figure 2 displays the unit cell, dispersion, and ribbon edge state profiles; Figure 3 shows complex eigenfrequency spectra; Figure 5 illustrates corner skin mode localization and field profiles; Figure 6 compares infinite and ribbon-based complex wave vector dispersions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the non-Hermitian skin effect, describes the photonic crystal model and its symmetry breaking, presents numerical results for ribbon (edge) and finite (corner) structures, provides topological characterization via winding numbers, and concludes with a comparison to existing models.

Why it may be interesting. The study of non-Hermitian topology and skin effects is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum optics, as it provides a realistic continuous-system platform for studying how dissipation and non-reciprocity drive novel localization phenomena.

Abstract

We numerically study topological effects of electromagnetic (EM) waves in a two-dimensional (2D) non-Hermitian photonic crystal (PhC) composed of lossy magneto-optical materials. In this system, not only the EM wavefunctions but also the complex eigenfrequencies exhibit nontrivial topological properties. We demonstrate that the non-Hermitian skin effect, protected by point gaps in the complex eigenfrequency spectrum, emerges at both the edges and corners of truncated structures. This phenomenon has no counterpart in Hermitian systems. In addition, we identify non-Hermitian topological edge states originating from the nontrivial topology of the bulk bands. While most previous studies of non-Hermitian topology have focused on tight-binding models, our work addresses a continuous photonic system, providing a more realistic platform and offering a concrete route toward experimental realization of non-Hermitian effects.

A practicable method for the analysis of complex motion of biological and soft matter

Authors: Jun Ma
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24387v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The lack of an analytical method to decipher the complex, stochastic 'fingerprint' trajectories of biological and soft matter to extract underlying dynamical information.

Main result. The development of a method that maps the Gaussian probability distribution of Brownian particle displacements onto a propagating wave packet, allowing for the extraction of microscopic dynamical information from irregular trajectories.

Method. A quantization-inspired approach that transforms the probability density of particle displacement into a wave function, using Fourier transforms and Taylor expansions of the angular frequency to relate ballistic motion to wave packet dynamics.

Summary. This paper presents a new analytical method for studying the complex, random motions found in biological and soft matter. By treating the stochastic trajectories of particles like Brownian motion as propagating wave packets, the author can extract hidden dynamical information from seemingly random paths. The method bridges the gap between microscopic ballistic steps and macroscopic diffusion. This approach allows for a systematic study of how spatio-temporal microstructures evolve in complex fluids.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 1D model of a spherical Brownian particle (e.g., proteins, bacteria, or silica beads) undergoing a series of random ballistic motion steps in a fluid, driven by a random thermal field.

Key observables. Mean-squared displacement, probability distribution P(x,t), group velocity (vg), mean displacement, and diffusion constant (D).

Important parameters / regimes. Ballistic step length (l), step time (tb), diffusion exponent (alpha), diffusion constant (D), and effective mass (m*).

Assumptions / limitations. The initial distribution of the wave function is Gaussian, the number of extra steps follows a Gaussian distribution, and the time intervals are integer multiples of the ballistic step time.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows irregular Brownian trajectories and a 1D model of a silica bead performing ballistic motion with direction flipping. Figure 2 illustrates the wave packet propagation mechanism, showing how oscillating motion and drift lead to net displacement.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of analyzing irregular trajectories, proposes a wave packet quantization model, derives the mathematical framework for the wave function, maps microscopic parameters to macroscopic observables, and concludes with the implications for studying soft matter dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. The paper uses a mathematical analogy between Brownian motion uncertainty and quantum mechanical wave packets, providing a framework for treating stochastic classical trajectories using tools similar to those used in open quantum systems and wave mechanics.

Abstract

Biological function of living matter is fulfilled by complex motions of biological and soft matter. Unlike general motion is deterministic described by Newton's laws, these motions are mostly random and uncertain for the position in stochastic process, being characterized as irregular trajectories of movement without a defined velocity. Like human fingerprint, the trajectory is the identity of the motion containing fundamental dynamical information. Such irregular trajectories randomly inter-wind and twist to each other to produce a complicated turmoil configuration in which so far the unrealized mechanism of motion is hidden. Nowadays, the analytical method for this fingerprint trajectory is still missed. Here we develop a practicable method to decipher complicated trajectory configuration, which uncovers abundant dynamical information hiding in irregular trajectories, revealing the remarkable evolution of spatial-temporal micro-structure, thus leading to the novel systematic study of the dynamics of biological and soft matter.

Spin Seebeck Effect in Normal-Metal--Chiral-Insulator Heterostructure

Authors: Jiayan Zhang, Gaoyang Li, Gaomin Tang, Yanxia Xing
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23111v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the generation and control of spin currents via the phonon-mediated Spin Seebeck Effect (SSE) in a normal-metal-chiral-insulator heterostructure.

Main result. The study identifies two nonlinear phenomena: negative differential SSE, where increasing thermal bias decreases spin current, and spin-current rectification, which enables a thermally controlled spin diode.

Method. The authors utilize the nonequilibrium Green's function (NEGF) formalism to calculate spin transport and solve for Green's functions and phonon self-energies self-consistently.

Summary. This paper explores how the angular momentum of chiral phonons can be converted into electron spin current in a metal-insulator heterostructure. By using the NEGF formalism, the authors demonstrate that this process can lead to unexpected nonlinear effects like negative differential spin Seebeck effect and spin-current rectification. These findings suggest that such structures could function as thermally controlled spin diodes. The work provides a theoretical foundation for developing new types of phonon-driven spintronic devices.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A two-terminal heterostructure consisting of a normal metal (NM) and a chiral insulator (CI), where the central region is modeled as a quantum dot with spin-microrotation coupling between electrons and chiral phonons.

Key observables. Spin current (Is) in the left lead and the rectification ratio (eta).

Important parameters / regimes. Thermal bias (delta T), average temperature (T0), chemical potential (muL), on-site potential (epsilon_d), and phonon coupling strength (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. The central region is simplified to a quantum dot model to capture essential physics, and the phonon reservoir is modeled using a super-Ohmic spectral density.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows device schematics for phonon-to-spin conversion; Fig 2 illustrates spin current dependence on thermal bias, chemical potential, and interfacial potential; Fig 3 plots effective spectral and spin current densities; Fig 4 shows the rectification ratio.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical mechanism of phonon-mediated SSE, presents the theoretical NEGF framework, analyzes the effects of various parameters (bias, chemical potential, interface), identifies nonlinear transport phenomena, and concludes with implications for spintronic devices.

Abstract

Phonons can carry angular momentum and exhibit chirality through the circular polarization of atomic motion. This enables a phonon-mediated spin Seebeck effect (SSE) via the conversion of phonon angular momentum into electron spin angular momentum. In this Letter, we develop a theoretical framework for calculating the spin current in a normal-metal (NM)-chiral-insulator (CI) heterostructure within the nonequilibrium Green's function formalism. We discuss the influence of (i) the thermal bias across the NM-CI interface, (ii) the chemical potential of the NM, and (iii) the insertion of an additional interfacial layer, on the spin transport properties. We identify two remarkable nonlinear spin transport phenomena: negative differential SSE and spin-current rectification. The negative differential SSE arises from the competition between the thermal bias and the thermally excited electron density. The spin-current rectification suggests the possibility of realizing a thermally controlled spin diode. We also find that the spin transport behavior is closely associated with an effective interfacial spectral density. This work provides a novel route toward thermally controlled spintronic devices using chiral phonons.

Main problem. Addressing the limitations of conventional polycrystalline metal films, such as high Ohmic losses and surface roughness, by utilizing crystalline metal flakes for advanced plasmonics and hybrid 2D material architectures.

Main result. Crystalline metal flakes provide superior structural definition and reduced scattering, enabling enhanced plasmonic performance, precise nanostructuring via FIB/EBL, and effective integration with 2D materials.

Method. The paper reviews chemical synthesis methods (polyol, Brust-Schiffrin, etc.), nanofabrication techniques (FIB, EBL, etching), and characterization tools (SEM, TEM, SNOM, ellipsometry).

Summary. This review examines the emergence of crystalline metal flakes as a superior alternative to traditional polycrystalline films for nanophotonics. It highlights how their high crystallinity and atomic flatness reduce optical losses and enable precise nanostructuring. The paper covers various chemical synthesis routes, fabrication methods like FIB and EBL, and the integration of these flakes with 2D materials. Ultimately, it positions these flakes as essential platforms for advanced applications in sensing, optoelectronics, and quantum plasmonics.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The primary system consists of quasi-monocrystalline noble metal flakes (Au, Ag, Al, Cu) characterized by atomically flat surfaces, well-defined {111} facets, and stacking faults.

Key observables. Surface Plasmon Polariton (SPP) propagation length, complex permittivity, surface roughness, aspect ratio, and nonlinear optical responses.

Important parameters / regimes. Thickness (from ~1.9 nm to >1 um), lateral size (up to ~250 um), and crystal facets ({111} vs {100}).

Assumptions / limitations. The flakes are treated as quasi-monocrystals due to the presence of twin planes/stacking faults required for anisotropic growth.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Morphology and crystal structure of Au flakes; Fig 2: Linear optical properties and SPP propagation; Fig 3: Comparison of Ga-FIB vs He-FIB milling quality; Fig 4: EBL fabrication flow and patterned nanostructures; Fig 5: Examples of micromanipulation techniques.

Paper structure. The review begins by contrasting polycrystalline vs. crystalline films, moves to the structural and chemical synthesis of flakes, discusses fabrication and nanostructuring techniques, explores integration with 2D materials, and concludes with future prospects.

Why it may be interesting. The paper is highly relevant to quantum optics and nanophotonics due to its discussion on quantum plasmonics, nonclassical light generation, and the manipulation of light-matter interactions in hybrid architectures.

Abstract

Crystalline noble metal flakes are emerging as versatile platforms in nanophotonics, enabling a broad range of optical phenomena and applications. Their atomically flat surfaces, high crystallinity, and superior optical quality open new avenues in advanced plasmonics, quantum light generation, and hybrid photonic systems. In contrast to conventional polycrystalline metal films, which typically suffer from higher optical losses due to grain boundaries, surface roughness, and structural disorder, these monocrystalline flakes provide minimal scattering and enhanced performance. They serve as templates for precise nanostructuring through techniques like focused-ion beam (FIB) milling and are crucial for advanced applications in sensing and optoelectronics. Additionally, they facilitate frontier research in quantum plasmonics, enabling fundamental studies of nonlocal optical effects and the generation of nonclassical light. Furthermore, the well-defined $\{111\}$ facets of these flakes host Tamm--Shockley surface states that support 2D plasmons coexisting with bulk modes. At near-infrared wavelengths and beyond, crystalline flakes act as nearly ideal metallic mirrors, featuring surface roughness limited only to atomic terrace steps, making them highly suitable for integration with 2D materials in hybrid photonic architectures. This review surveys the key roles these flakes play, highlighting recent developments and discussing future prospects while emphasizing their unique benefits in addressing fundamental and applied challenges in modern nanophotonics.

Accelerating Quantum Materials Characterization: Hybrid Active Learning for Autonomous Spin Wave Spectroscopy

Authors: William Ratcliff

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of automating neutron spectroscopy, specifically balancing the tasks of signal detection, Hamiltonian inference, and parameter refinement while avoiding 'algorithmic myopia' where a controller over-refines an incorrect model.

Main result. The TAS-AI framework successfully uses a hybrid agnostic-to-physics-informed workflow to accelerate characterization, reducing wall-clock time by 32% and using an LLM-based audit layer to prevent model lock-in.

Method. A hybrid active learning framework (TAS-AI) combining Log-Gaussian Process mapping for discovery, physics-informed planning for refinement, and an LLM-based 'audit committee' for strategic falsification.

Summary. This paper presents TAS-AI, an autonomous framework designed to accelerate the characterization of quantum materials via neutron spectroscopy. It addresses the inefficiency of using a single controller for all experimental tasks by proposing a hybrid approach: model-agnostic methods for initial signal discovery and physics-informed methods for parameter refinement. The authors also introduce an LLM-based audit layer to prevent the system from getting stuck on incorrect physical models. The framework demonstrates significant reductions in experimental wall-clock time and improved robustness in model selection.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study uses a high-fidelity digital twin of a Triple-Axis Spectrometer (TAS) to simulate spin-wave spectroscopy on square-lattice magnetic systems, including ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic Hamiltonians with J1-J1 and anisotropy parameters.

Key observables. Momentum-energy coordinates (Q, E), intensity (I), uncertainty (sigma), and AIC-derived evidence ratios for model selection.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange constants (J1, J2), anisotropy (D), motor speeds (v_H, v_E), and counting time (t).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework relies on fast Laplace/Levenberg-Marquardt approximations for real-time decision-making and assumes the use of a digital twin for validation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the TAS-AI workflow stages; Figure S1 shows the effect of energy tapering on edge-locking; Figure 9 shows the transition between controller phases.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the TAS-AI framework, details the three-stage hybrid workflow (agnostic discovery, physics-informed refinement, and strategic audit), presents benchmarks on model discrimination and parameter accuracy, discusses the mitigation of algorithmic myopia via LLMs, and concludes with software implementation details.

Abstract

Autonomous neutron spectroscopy must solve three distinct tasks: detection (where is the signal?), inference (which Hamiltonian governs it?), and refinement (what are the parameters?). No single controller solves all three equally well. We present TAS-AI, a hybrid agnostic-to-physics-informed framework for autonomous triple-axis spin-wave spectroscopy that separates these tasks explicitly. In blind reconstruction benchmarks, model-agnostic methods such as random sampling, coarse grids, and Gaussian-process mappers reach a global error threshold more reliably and with fewer measurements than physics-informed planning, supporting the claim that discovery and inference are distinct tasks requiring distinct controllers. Once signal structure is localized, the physics-informed stage performs in-loop Hamiltonian discrimination and parameter refinement: in a controlled square-lattice test between nearest-neighbor-only and J1-J2 Hamiltonians, TAS-AI reaches a decisive AIC-derived evidence ratio (>100) in fewer than 10 measurements, while motion-aware scheduling cuts wall-clock time by 32% at a fixed measurement budget. We also identify a failure mode of posterior-weighted design, algorithmic myopia, in which the planner over-refines the current leading model while under-sampling low-intensity falsification probes. A constrained falsification channel sharply reduces time spent committed to the wrong model and accelerates correct model selection without modifying the Bayesian inference engine. In controlled two-model ablations, both a deterministic top-two max-disagreement rule and an LLM-based audit committee achieve this gain under identical constraints. We demonstrate the full workflow in silico using a high-fidelity digital twin and provide an open-source Python implementation.

Emergence of dual axion response in condensed matter

Main problem. Investigating whether the recently predicted dual axion response, a nonreciprocal magneto-electric effect involving magnetic charge, can emerge in condensed matter systems like antiferromagnets rather than just in engineered metamaterials.

Main result. The authors prove that a periodic antiferromagnetic spin lattice exhibits an emergent dual axion response, which can be distinguished from the conventional axion response by analyzing the frequency dependence of polarization rotation from an internal current source.

Method. The study uses linear response theory, the semi-classical Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation, and the Discrete Dipole Approximation (DDA) for numerical validation, alongside an effective medium approach.
