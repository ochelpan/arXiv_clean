Mesoscopic Josephson effect in graphene disk at magnetic field

Authors: Adam Rycerz
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23470v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the mesoscopic Josephson effect in a graphene Corbino disk under a perpendicular magnetic field, specifically looking for deviations from the standard tunneling-limit current-phase relation.

Main result. In the limit of vanishing conductance, the system exhibits an enhanced I_cR_N product (~1.85 Delta_0/e) and positive skewness (S ~ 0.14) compared to the standard tunneling limit.

Method. Quantum-mechanical mode-matching analysis using the Dirac-Bogoliubov-De-Gennes equation, compared against an incoherent scattering approximation.

Summary. This paper explores how the geometry and magnetic field of a graphene Corbino disk influence the Josephson effect. It demonstrates that even in the limit of very high resistance, the junction maintains mesoscopic features like an enhanced I_cR_N product and a skewed current-phase relation. The study provides a detailed comparison between exact quantum-mechanical calculations and simpler scattering models. These findings highlight the persistence of non-trivial transport properties in graphene-based superconducting junctions.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A superconductor-graphene-superconductor (S-g-S) junction in a Corbino (disk) geometry, featuring an annular graphene sample with two circular superconducting electrodes.

Key observables. Critical current (I_c), normal-state resistance (R_N), current-phase relation (CPR), skewness (S), and conductance (G).

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic field (B), chemical potential (mu), radii ratio (a), and the short-junction limit (junction width much smaller than coherence length).

Assumptions / limitations. Short-junction limit (width << coherence length); use of an incoherent scattering approximation for comparison; averaging datasets to mitigate Landau level resonance effects.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the Corbino geometry and the current-phase relation; Fig 2 displays conductance, I_cR_N, and skewness as functions of chemical potential; Fig 3 shows the relationship between chemical potential and magnetic field-induced resonances and compares averaged results to incoherent scattering predictions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Corbino geometry, presents the theoretical framework (DBdG equation), details the numerical mode-matching method, compares results with an incoherent scattering model, and analyzes the evolution of Josephson observables under varying magnetic fields and doping.

Abstract

Unlike for tunneling Josephson junctions, for which the current-phase relation is given by the sine function, with the critical current ($I_c$) and normal-state resistance ($R_N$) following the relation $I_cR_N=(π/2)\,Δ_0/e$ (where $Δ_0$ is the superconducting gap and electron charge is $-e$), mesoscopic Josephson junctions show more complex current-phase relations, with the skewness $S>0$, what is related to the presence -- in case the leads are in the normal state -- of transmission probabilities taking the values comparable to $1$. Here, we show that these features also appear for a superconductor-graphene-superconductor (S-g-S) junction in the disk-shaped (Corbino) geometry, when the magnetic field is adjusted such that $I_c\rightarrow{}0$ and $R_N\rightarrow{}\infty$. In such a case, the product $I_cR_N\approx{}1.85\,Δ_0/e$, and the skewness $S\approx{}0.14$. The results obtained from quantum-mechanical mode-matching analysis for the Dirac-Bogoliubov-De-Gennes equation are compared with simpler model assuming incoherent scattering between two circular interfaces separating the sample and the leads.

Multi-photon schemes for mid-infrared detection

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Main problem. Developing efficient mid-infrared (MIR) detection methods that avoid cryogenic temperatures by comparing two multi-photon absorption schemes using non-degenerate two-photon absorption (ND-2PA).

Main result. The study finds that Ge(1-x)Sn(x) alloys (Scheme II) exhibit a substantially larger nonlinear response and three-color injected current compared to GaAs (Scheme I) in the mid-infrared window.

Method. Theoretical calculations using first- and second-order time-dependent perturbation theory and a 30-band k.p model.

Summary. This paper investigates theoretical methods for detecting mid-infrared light using multi-photon processes in semiconductors. It compares a scheme using wide-gap GaAs to one using narrow-gap Germanium-Tin alloys. The researchers demonstrate that the GeSn-based approach provides much higher sensitivity and larger nonlinear current generation. This work is significant for developing room-temperature infrared detectors.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical systems studied are bulk GaAs and Ge(1-x)Sn(x) alloys, modeled using a 30-band k.p Hamiltonian to account for the entire Brillouin zone and anisotropic nonlinear responses.

Key observables. Nonlinear absorption coefficient (alpha), three-color injected current response tensor, linear electric susceptibility, and anisotropy/dichroism parameters.

Important parameters / regimes. Photon energies (signal and pump), alloy composition (x), band gap energy, and pump intensities up to 1 TW/cm2.

Assumptions / limitations. Independent particle approximation, continuous wave limit, neglect of many-body effects, neglect of phonon-mediated indirect absorption, and neglect of local inversion symmetry breaking in GeSn alloys.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of current generation via interference between degenerate 2PA and 1PA; Figure 2 shows schematics of the two ND-2PA schemes (Scheme I and Scheme II).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MIR detection, defines two multi-photon schemes, details the 30-band k.p modeling approach, compares the nonlinear responses and current injection of GaAs and GeSn, and provides technical parameters for the alloys.

Abstract

We calculate the theoretical non-degenerate two photon absorption and three color injected current response tensors for bulk GaAs and Ge$_{1-x}$Sn$_x$ for a range of alloy compositions. In particular, by including a ''pump'' beam we compare two ''schemes'' that are sensitive to mid-infrared photons. In ''scheme I'' we consider GaAs and a pump photon with energy greater than half the band gap, and in ''scheme II'' we consider Ge$_{1-x}$Sn$_x$ with a pump photon with energy less than half the band gap. We find that for certain pump and alloy concentrations Ge$_{1-x}$Sn$_x$ has a substantially larger nonlinear response and three-color injected current than GaAs in the mid-infrared frequency window where both materials can absorb photons via non-degenerate two-photon absorption.

Nearly Isotropic Magnon Transport in Epitaxial Lithium Aluminum Ferrite Thin Films

Authors: Yiming Li, Katya Mikhailova, Lerato Takana, Daisy O'Mahoney, Sauviz P. Alaei, Guanxiong Qu, Dominic Petruzzi, Samuel Crossley, Harold Y. Hwang, Ian R. Fisher, Clare C. Yu, Yuri Suzuki
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23943v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to determine if the anisotropic magnon transport observed in magnesium aluminum ferrite (MAFO) is a general feature of spinel ferrites or if isotropic in-plane magnon propagation can be achieved for information transport.

Main result. The researchers found that epitaxial lithium aluminum ferrite (LAFO) thin films exhibit nearly isotropic magnon diffusion lengths along the [100] and [110] directions at 250 K, despite having pronounced fourfold in-plane magnetic anisotropy.

Method. Nonlocal transport measurements using platinum electrodes were used to detect electrically (Spin Hall Effect) and thermally (Spin Seebeck Effect) generated magnons, combined with SQUID magnetometry and broadband Ferromagnetic Resonance (FMR).

Summary. This paper investigates magnon transport in lithium aluminum ferrite (LAFO) thin films to address the need for low-loss, isotropic magnetic waveguides. While previous spinel ferrites showed anisotropic transport, LAFO demonstrates nearly identical magnon diffusion lengths along different in-plane directions. This isotropy is attributed to isotropic exchange stiffness and potential angular averaging. These findings suggest that LAFO is a promising platform for developing efficient, isotropic magnon-based information interconnects.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system consists of epitaxial (001) lithium aluminum ferrite (LAFO) thin films grown on MgAl2O4 substrates, characterized by a spinel structure with significant in-plane magnetic anisotropy.

Key observables. Magnon diffusion length (lambda), nonlocal resistance (R_SHE and R_SSE), saturation magnetization (Ms), anisotropy field (H4,parallel), and Gilbert damping (alpha).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature of 250 K, magnetic field of 300 mT, and film thickness of approximately 12 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a 1D magnon diffusion model and attributes the observed isotropy to nearly isotropic exchange stiffness and effective angular averaging due to inhomogeneous broadening.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows magnetization field dependence and hysteresis loops; Fig 2 shows FMR frequency dependence; Fig 3 demonstrates the angular dependence of nonlocal resistance (cos^2 and cos dependence).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of magnon anisotropy, describes the growth and structural characterization of LAFO films, presents magnetic property measurements (SQUID and FMR), details the nonlocal transport experiments, and concludes with a discussion on the mechanism of isotropic transport.

Abstract

Low-loss magnetic insulating thin films are promising for information transport via magnons, where isotropic in-plane magnon propagation is desirable. We report nonlocal measurements of electrically and thermally generated magnons in epitaxial (001) lithium aluminum ferrite Li$_{0.5}$Al$_{0.7}$Fe$_{1.8}$O$_4$ thin films with pronounced fourfold in-plane magnetic anisotropy. By measuring the inverse spin Hall signal as a function of the magnon diffusion distance, we deduce magnon diffusion lengths that are nearly identical along the [100] and [110] directions at 250~K. This isotropy is consistent with a nearly isotropic exchange stiffness. These results highlight spinel ferrites as viable platforms for isotropic magnon transport.

Non-Bloch band theory of nonlinear eigenvalue problems

Authors: Kota Otsuka, Kazuki Yokomizo
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24284v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The breakdown of conventional Bloch band theory and topological characterization in nonlinear eigenvalue problems due to extreme sensitivity to boundary conditions.

Main result. The establishment of a non-Bloch band theory framework that uses Generalized Brillouin Zones (GBZs) to accurately reproduce open boundary condition spectra and predict topological edge states in nonlinear systems.

Method. Development of a non-Bloch framework using complex-valued Bloch wavenumbers and the construction of Generalized Brillouin Zones (GBZs) via the characteristic equation of the nonlinear system.

Summary. This paper introduces a non-Bloch band theory specifically designed for nonlinear eigenvalue problems. It addresses the failure of standard Bloch theory in systems where parameters depend on the eigenvalue, which typically causes a discrepancy between periodic and open boundary spectra. By using Generalized Brillouin Zones, the authors can accurately predict the spectra and the emergence of the nonlinear skin effect. The framework is successfully extended to 2D to establish a bulk-boundary correspondence for nonlinear Chern insulators.

Detailed structure

Model / system. 1D and 2D tight-binding models featuring nonlinear eigenvalue equations where system parameters depend on the eigenvalue, including nonreciprocal hopping models and nonlinear Chern insulators.

Key observables. Complex eigenvalue spectra, winding numbers, Chern numbers, and the biorthogonal inverse participation ratio (bi-IPR).

Important parameters / regimes. Nonreciprocity strength (tL/tR, gamma_x), nonlinearity strength (delta), and mass parameter (m).

Assumptions / limitations. The nonlinearity follows specific analytical forms (e.g., f(omega) = omega or -omega^2), and the 2D extension assumes mirror symmetry to suppress skin effects in one direction.

Figures summary. Fig 1 compares Bloch vs. non-Bloch bands and PBC vs. OBC spectra; Fig 3 shows the reproduction of bulk and edge spectra in 2D, the disappearance of edge states, and the calculation of Chern number transitions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of nonlinear eigenvalue sensitivity, develops the 1D non-Bloch framework and GBZ construction, investigates the nonlinearity-induced skin effect and pseudo-Hermiticity, and extends the theory to 2D topological Chern insulators.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a critical theoretical tool for understanding nonlinear dynamics in optics, mechanical oscillators, and weakly interacting bosonic systems where boundary-induced spectral shifts are prevalent.

Abstract

Nonlinear eigenvalue problems arise in a wide range of physical systems, in which system parameters depend on the eigenvalue. Such systems have been proposed to exhibit an extreme sensitivity of their spectra to boundary conditions, which leads to the breakdown of conventional topological characterizations. In this work, we establish a non-Bloch framework for calculating continuum bands that reproduce the spectra of the nonlinear system with open boundary conditions. This non-Bloch band theory enables us not only to calculate the eigenvalues but also to reveal phenomena unique to the nonlinear system. We further investigate the topological bulk-boundary correspondence in a nonlinear Chern insulator within an extended version of this framework.

Nonintegral Flux Trapping in Frustrated Josephson Networks of Triplet Superconductors

Authors: Grayson R. Frazier, Colton Lelievre, Yi Li
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24734v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating how anisotropic Josephson coupling between spin-triplet Cooper pairs in a junction network leads to frustrated d-vector textures and spontaneous nonintegral flux trapping.

Main result. The authors identify a critical coupling strength D* = |J0|/sqrt(3) above which the system enters a frustrated regime characterized by chiral 120-degree d-vector textures and the spontaneous trapping of pi-flux.

Method. The study uses a theoretical framework involving the minimization of the Josephson free energy and the analytical derivation of an emergent geometric phase arising from d-vector orientations.

Summary. This paper explores how the internal spin structure of triplet superconductors can induce frustration in Josephson junction networks. By analyzing the competition between different types of anisotropic coupling, the authors show that a transition occurs to a state with chiral d-vector textures. This transition leads to the spontaneous trapping of fractional (pi) magnetic flux. The findings suggest new ways to engineer frustrated superconducting states using magnetic textures.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A Josephson junction network of superconducting grains (such as polycrystals or single-crystal networks) with spin-triplet pairing, modeled via a three-grain ring with Heisenberg-like and Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya-type interactions.

Key observables. Nonintegral/fractional flux trapping (specifically pi-flux), spontaneous Josephson currents, and chiral d-vector textures.

Important parameters / regimes. Anisotropic coupling amplitudes (J0, D0, Gamma), the dimensionless ratio x = D0/J0, and the critical value D* = |J0|/sqrt(3).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes unitary triplet pairing (d x d* = 0) and neglects the self-inductance of the network.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of a Josephson junction network illustrating superconducting grains, d-vector orientations, and a three-grain ring demonstrating flux trapping.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of flux trapping in triplet networks, defines the Hamiltonian and coupling terms, performs an analytical derivation of the critical coupling regime, compares the results to half-quantum vortices, and discusses experimental candidates and implications for glassy phases.

Abstract

In a Josephson junction network, anisotropic coupling between spin triplet pairing correlations can lead to frustrated $d$ vector textures that support spontaneous Josephson currents and nonintegral flux trapping. Such networks can appear in superconducting polycrystals, as well as single-crystal superconductors. In analogy to classical spin systems, in which the presence of geometric frustration and anisotropic superexchange can lead to nontrivial spin textures, Josephson networks with anisotropic Josephson couplings cannot simultaneously optimize their $\mathrm{U}(1)$ superconducting phase difference and relative $d$ vector orientations. The internal pairing structure of Cooper pairs twists as they tunnel across the Josephson junction, and the $d$ vector texture enters as an emergent geometric phase which can spontaneously trap fractional flux. For unitary triplet pairing order, this mechanism can support $π$-flux trapping above a critical value of antisymmetric Josephson coupling, and is distinct from usual half-quantum vortices. The results of this work reveal new routes to engineer frustrated Josephson networks from the interplay of magnetic textures and spin triplet superconducting pairing order.

Optical Properties of Indium-Gallium-Oxide Microcrystalline Alloy Films: From the Visible to the Deep-UV

Authors: HM Borhanul Alam, Dipak Oli, You Qiang, Bisheswor Acharya, Jesse Huso, Matthew D. McCluskey, Leah Bergman
Type: experiment · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23884v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the tunable optical properties and the onset of phase separation in (InxGa1-x)2O3 microcrystalline alloy films.

Main result. The study identifies the onset of incipient phase separation at x ~ 0.3, where phase-segregated precipitates act as a major defect type, and finds that large Urbach energies are driven by strong hole-phonon coupling.

Method. Wet chemical synthesis followed by UV-Vis transmission spectroscopy, spatial photoluminescence mapping, AFM, and EDS analysis.

Summary. This paper explores how changing the indium content in (InxGa1-x)2O3 films tunes their optical properties from the deep-UV to the visible spectrum. The researchers discovered that at a composition of x ~ 0.3, the material begins to undergo phase separation, creating defects that significantly impact the optical properties. They also found that the material exhibits much larger Urbach energies than similar alloy systems due to strong coupling between holes and phonons. This work is important for designing transparent conducting oxides and wide-bandgap semiconductors.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Microcrystalline (InxGa1-x)2O3 alloy films ranging from beta-Ga2O3 to In2O3, characterized using a saturation model for the optical gap and an Urbach model for absorption band tails.

Key observables. Optical gap (E_OG), Urbach energy (E_u), self-trapped hole (STH) photoluminescence emission, and compositional-dependent absorption tails.

Important parameters / regimes. Indium composition (x), solubility limit/saturation composition (x0 ~ 0.3), and hole-phonon coupling strength.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a tanh-based saturation model to identify the onset of phase separation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows normalized transmission spectra; Figure 3 and 5 include transmission spectra showing absorption features and the In2O3 optical gap.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the alloy system, describes the synthesis and characterization methods, presents the compositional dependence of the optical gap and STH PL, analyzes phase separation via saturation modeling, compares Urbach energies with the MgxZn1-xO system, and concludes with the impact of hole-phonon coupling.

Abstract

The tailored optical properties of $(In_xGa_{1-x})_2O_3$ microcrystalline films were studied as a function of composition x via transmission, Urbach energy analysis, and spatial photoluminescence (PL) mapping of the self-trapped hole (STH) emission, with the objective of addressing material characteristics specific to this alloy system. Up to x = 0.46, the optical gap exhibited a redshift of 1 eV from the deep to the near-UV range, while the STH PL was redshifted by 0.5 eV in the visible range. For higher composition, x = 0.63, the transmission spectra indicated the co-existence of two optical gaps attributed to Ga-rich and to In-rich domains, implying that this sample is phase-separated.
