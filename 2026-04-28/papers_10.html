Bottom-up realization of a type-II organic-TMD heterointerface: Pentacene on monolayer WS2

Authors: Michele Capra, Christian S. Kern, Mira S. Arndt, Karl J. Schiller, Max Niederreiter, Francesco Presel, Iolanda Di Bernardo, Marco Gruenewald, Torsten Fritz, Stefan Tappertzhofen, Martin Sterrer, Peter Puschnig, Mirko Cinchetti, Giovanni Zamborlini
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24671v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of creating high-quality, electronically homogeneous interfaces between organic semiconductors and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) using bottom-up synthesis to enable efficient charge separation.

Main result. The researchers successfully demonstrated the bottom-up growth of a highly ordered pentacene monolayer on WS2, establishing a Type-II (staggered) band alignment critical for optoelectronic applications.

Method. The study combines reactive molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) for synthesis with STM-STS, photoemission orbital tomography (POT), and G0W0 electronic structure calculations for characterization.

Summary. This paper reports the successful bottom-up synthesis of a pentacene/WS2 heterostructure using molecular beam epitaxy. By using a non-toxic sulfur source (DMDS), the authors achieved atomically flat WS2 layers that allow for a highly ordered organic molecular overlayer. Using advanced spectroscopy and many-body calculations, they confirmed a Type-II band alignment, which is essential for separating charges in future solar cells and spintronic devices. This system serves as a model platform for studying charge transfer and energy-level renormalization in 2D hybrid interfaces.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A hybrid van der Waals heterostructure consisting of a self-assembled monolayer of pentacene (5A) on a monolayer of WS2, which is grown epitaxially on an Au(111) substrate.

Key observables. Band alignment (Type-II), HOMO-LUMO gap, WS2 transport gap, moiré periodicity, and molecular orbital momentum maps.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature regimes of 4K, 77K, and 950K; WS2 lattice constant of ~0.314 nm; moiré periodicity of ~3.43 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. The computational model uses a supercell approach that ignores the Au substrate to manage lattice mismatch.

Figures summary. Figure 7 shows UPS spectra comparisons for pristine WS2 and pentacene, experimental and DFT-calculated momentum maps for orbital identification, and symmetrized POT data.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of interface quality, describes the MBE synthesis of WS2 using DMDS, details the structural and chemical characterization (XPS, LEED, STM), presents the electronic structure analysis (STS, POT, ARPES), validates results with G0W0 calculations, and concludes with the implications for hybrid heterostructures.

Abstract

Stacked van der Waals heterostructures based on transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) exhibit a rich variety of exotic interfacial phenomena. Substituting one component with an organic semiconductor (OSC) enables the design of hybrid heterostructures with tunable functionalities for optoelectronic, photovoltaic, and spintronic applications. In this work, exploiting scanning tunneling spectroscopy (STS), photoemission orbital tomography (POT) and G0W0 electronic structure calculations, we experimentally and theoretically demonstrate the self-assembly of an ordered single layer of pentacene (5A) above monolayer WS2, exhibiting a type-II (staggered) band alignment in the hybrid 5A/WS2 interface. Central to this result is the synthesis of extended, atomically flat WS2 - an essential prerequisite for a highly ordered and electronically homogeneous OSC/TMD interface - which can only be reliably achieved via bottom-up growth, most notably molecular beam epitaxy (MBE). We realize this by leveraging Au(111) as an atomically clean and conductive sample for epitaxial growth - a necessary requirement for reliable and comparable STS/POT characterizations. The high quality of the synthesized heterostructure, together with its type-II band alignment, establishes pentacene/WS2 as a model system for orbital-resolved studies of charge transfer, energy-level renormalization, and non-equilibrium interfacial processes in hybrid organic-inorganic-2D heterostructures.

Characterizing Fill Factor Limitations in Perovskite-Silicon Tandem Solar Cells

Authors: Yueming Wang, Nan Sun, Chris Dreessen, Gaosheng Huang, Alexander Eberst, Kaining Ding, Thomas Kirchartz
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24098v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Identifying and characterizing the mechanisms responsible for Fill Factor (FF) losses in perovskite-silicon monolithic tandem solar cells, specifically distinguishing between series resistance and the 'photoshunt' effect.

Main result. The study identifies 'photoshunt'—an illumination-dependent reduction in parallel resistance due to low charge transport layer mobility—as a critical FF loss mechanism, and shows that operating in a 'bottom-cell limited' regime can mitigate this effect.

Method. The authors combine experimental characterization (electroluminescence spectroscopy, EQE, and J-V measurements) with equivalent circuit modeling and analytical derivations to quantify resistive losses.

Summary. This paper investigates why perovskite-silicon tandem solar cells fail to reach their theoretical efficiency limits, focusing on the Fill Factor. The researchers identify a phenomenon called 'photoshunt,' where low mobility in the charge transport layers causes an apparent drop in parallel resistance under light. They demonstrate that this loss can be minimized by designing cells that are slightly limited by the bottom silicon cell's current. This work provides a roadmap for improving the efficiency of next-generation tandem photovoltaics.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A monolithic two-terminal perovskite-silicon tandem solar cell consisting of a perovskite top cell (approx. 1.7 eV) and a silicon heterojunction bottom cell.

Key observables. Fill Factor (FF), short-circuit current density (Jsc), open-circuit voltage (Voc), series resistance (Rs), and photoshunt resistance (Rp,photo).

Important parameters / regimes. Charge transport layer mobility (mu_TL), bandgap (Eg), current matching conditions, and the radiative limit (Shockley-Queisser limit).

Assumptions / limitations. The Shockley-Queisser model assumes radiative recombination is the only recombination mechanism and assumes a standard AM1.5G solar spectrum.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows efficiency losses relative to the SQ limit; Figure 2 shows SQ limits for varying bandgaps; Figure 3 shows EL spectra and reconstructed J-V curves; Figure 4 shows tandem efficiency and FF as functions of top-cell bandgap and photoshunt resistance; Figure 5 shows simulated J-V curves.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the efficiency gap relative to the SQ limit, introduces the experimental characterization via EL, presents the discovery of the photoshunt mechanism through analytical modeling, and concludes with design strategies for high-efficiency tandem cells.

Abstract

Perovskite-silicon tandem technology has exceeded the single junction theoretical efficiency limit. However, there is still distance to the thermodynamic limit mainly caused by the fill factor. This work presents a methodology to illustrate the mechanisms of FF loss in perovskite-Si monolithic tandem solar cells. Apart from the series resistance related loss characterized by electroluminescence, another loss factor is from the photoshunt, a phenomenon in which the parallel resistance apparently reduces under illumination in perovskite solar cells due to the moderate charge transport layer mobility. In addoition, the two-diode property of the Si cell can also influence the FF of tandem devices. The photoshunt can be hidden when the bottom cell is over illuminated, which explains highly efficient tandem solar cells are usually bottom cell limited. This work outlines strategies that overcoming the photoshunt issue can move the perovskite top cell closer to low FF losses in tandem solar cells.

Chirality Transfer to the Centrosymmetric Magnetic Sublattice in the Hybrid Perovskite (R)-/(S)-3-Fluoropyrrolidinium Copper(II) Chloride

Authors: Zheng Zhang, Mingyu Xu, Jose L. Gonzalez Jimenez, Stephen Zhang, Weiwei Xie, Xianghan Xu, Daniel B. Straus
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22952v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the mechanism of chirality transfer from chiral organic cations to a structurally centrosymmetric (achiral) inorganic magnetic sublattice in a hybrid perovskite.

Main result. The chiral organic cations induce chiral magnetic order in the achiral Cu-Cl sublattice, evidenced by a second-order magnetoelectric effect present only in the enantiopure forms.

Method. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction, Circular Dichroism (CD) spectroscopy, magnetic susceptibility, specific heat capacity, and magnetoelectric current measurements.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that chirality can be transferred from organic molecules to an inorganic magnetic lattice in a hybrid perovskite. While the copper-chloride sublattice is structurally centrosymmetric, the presence of chiral cations induces chiral magnetic order. This is experimentally verified through the observation of a second-order magnetoelectric effect in the enantiopure crystals, which is absent in the racemic mixture. This finding provides a method for designing new materials with tailored chiral magnetic and optical properties.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 2D magnetic S=1/2 metal halide perovskite, (R)- and (S)-3-fluoropyrrolidinium copper(II) chloride, consisting of Cu-Cl inorganic layers separated by chiral organic cations.

Key observables. Néel temperature (TN), magnetoelectric signal, circular dichroism (CD) spectra, magnetic susceptibility (DC and AC), and specific heat capacity.

Important parameters / regimes. Néel temperature TN = 2.23 K, spin S=1/2, space groups P212121 (chiral) and P21/c (racemic).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the observed magnetoelectric effect is a direct consequence of the induced toroidal moment from structural chirality.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the crystal structures of the (R), (S), and racemic variants; Figure 6 displays AC susceptibility components; Figure 7 shows the magnetoelectric signal in chiral vs. racemic samples.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the chirality transfer problem, describes the synthesis and structural characterization of the hybrid perovskite, presents magnetic and optical measurements to demonstrate chirality transfer, and concludes with the observation of the magnetoelectric effect.

Abstract

Incorporating chiral organic cations into organic-inorganic hybrid materials has been shown to enable the inorganic sublattice to display chiroptical properties. We report a new two-dimensional magnetic (S=1/2) chiral metal halide material, (R)- and (S)-$(C_4H_9FN)_2CuCl_4$ (where $(C_4H_9FN)^+$ is 3-fluoropyrrolidinium), which consists of Cu-Cl inorganic layers separated by $(C_4H_9FN)^+$ organic cations. The presence of the chiral $(C_4H_9FN)^+$ organic cation induces formation of chiral magnetic order, even though the inorganic sublattice itself is structurally centrosymmetric. We also report the racemic variant, containing an equal amount of (R)- and (S)- cations, which shows no evidence of chiral magnetic order. When the magnetic susceptibility is measured perpendicular to inorganic Cu-Cl layer propagation direction, an antiferromagnetic phase transition at Néel temperature $T_N = 2.23~K$ is observed in both the chiral and racemic materials, and the existence of the magnetic phase transition is supported by specific heat capacity measurements. Field-induced magnetic chirality is observed through the existence of a second-order magnetoelectric effect in the chiral variant, while no magnetoelectric signal is observed for the racemic material, indicating the absence of magnetic chirality. Our findings demonstrate that materials exhibiting chiral magnetic order can be created through the incorporation of a chiral cation into an organic-inorganic hybrid magnetic material, potentially allowing for the design of tailored materials that combine chiral magnetism with other desirable optical and electronic properties that come from structural chirality.

Comparative analysis of nonlinear elastic moduli of polystyrene, polycarbonate and PMMA

Authors: A. V. Belashov, A. A. Zhikhoreva, Y. M. Beltukov, I. V. Semenova
Type: experiment · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23244v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study addresses the lack of experimental data regarding the frequency and temperature dependence of nonlinear viscoelastic properties in glassy amorphous polymers.

Main result. Nonlinear elastic moduli exhibit much more profound frequency dependence (1.5-2.5x) than linear moduli, with significant variations occurring below 1 MHz due to relaxation processes. PMMA showed higher absolute values for both linear and nonlinear moduli compared to PC and PS.

Method. The researchers used the acousto-elastic effect to measure variations in longitudinal and shear ultrasonic wave velocities under applied static pressure (0-12 MPa) in the frequency range of 0.4-3 MHz.

Summary. This paper provides a comparative experimental analysis of the linear and nonlinear elastic properties of three common glassy polymers: PS, PC, and PMMA. Using ultrasonic wave measurements, the authors demonstrate that nonlinear moduli are much more sensitive to frequency changes than linear moduli, particularly below 1 MHz. The study identifies that PMMA's polar groups lead to higher moduli values and highlights how molecular structures influence relaxation processes. The findings help validate nonlinear viscoelastic models for amorphous materials.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system consists of three glassy amorphous polymers: Polystyrene (PS), Polycarbonate (PC), and Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA). The mechanical behavior is modeled using Murnaghan's model for nonlinear elasticity, which includes five parameters (two Lamé and three Murnaghan moduli) derived from the energy density function.

Key observables. Longitudinal and shear ultrasonic wave velocities, wave packet transition times, and phase shifts under static pressure.

Important parameters / regimes. Lamé moduli (lambda, mu), Murnaghan moduli (l, m, n), frequency range (0.4-3 MHz), and the relaxation exponent (gamma between 2.4 and 2.9).

Assumptions / limitations. The polymers are assumed to have a broad spectrum of relaxation times; the applied pressure range is assumed to be within the elastic regime without irreversible deformation; and the effective elastic moduli are assumed to depend linearly on static pressure.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup; Figure 2 is a flowchart of data processing; Figure 3 displays frequency dependence of wave velocities; Figure 4 shows molecular structures of the polymers; Figure 5 shows frequency dependence of moduli.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of nonlinear viscoelasticity, describes the experimental setup and materials, details the mathematical models for calculating moduli, presents the frequency-dependent results and material comparisons, and concludes with a discussion on the molecular origins of the observed nonlinearities.

Abstract

We present the comparative experimental analysis of frequency dependencies of linear (Lamé) and nonlinear (Murnaghan) elastic moduli of polystyrene, PMMA and polycarbonate. The measurement methodology, based on the acousto-elastic effect, provided data on variations of these moduli in block samples of the polymers in the frequency range of 0.45-3 MHz. In all the three polymers the linear Lamé moduli demonstrated moderate rise with frequency, most pronounced rise was observed in modulus $λ$ of PMMA in about 35%. The frequency dependencies of Murnaghan moduli were considerably nonlinear. At higher frequencies above ~1 MHz no significant variations of the Murnaghan moduli occurred, while at lower frequencies the absolute values of the moduli $l$ and $m$ demonstrated rapid rise, more pronounced for the modulus $l$. At the same time the absolute values of the modulus $n$ decreased and demonstrated a tendency to become positive at lower frequencies. Both linear and nonlinear moduli of PMMA had higher values than those of PC and PS, with the latter two demonstrating close values of both types of moduli. The potential origins of the differences in nonlinear elastic properties of the three polymers are discussed.

Complementary-polarity double-layer LiTaO3 resonators for symmetry-selective SH2 excitation with ultrahigh electromechanical coupling (kt^2 = 25.7%)

Authors: Hao Yan, Zhen-hui Qin, Zhi-Wen Wang, Shu-Mao Wu, Chen-Bei Hao, Hua-Yang Chen, Sheng-Nan Liang, Ke Chen, Si-Yuan Yu, Yan-Feng Chen
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24309v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The need to increase the electromechanical coupling coefficient (kt^2) in LiTaO3 resonators for wider bandwidth applications while simultaneously suppressing unwanted spurious modes and maintaining temperature stability.

Main result. The development of a double-layer LiTaO3 resonator with complementary polarization that achieves an ultrahigh electromechanical coupling of 25.7% and suppresses parasitic modes through symmetry-selective excitation.

Method. The researchers used rotation-bonding of two 31 deg Y-oriented LiTaO3 films with complementary (+X/-X) polarization, followed by finite-element analysis (FEA) to predict frequency scaling and temperature compensation.

Summary. This paper presents a new way to build lithium tantalate (LiTaO3) acoustic resonators by bonding two layers with opposite polarities. This specific arrangement allows for the selective excitation of a desired mode (SH2) while canceling out unwanted interference modes. The result is a record-high electromechanical coupling of 25.7%, which is crucial for creating wideband ultrasonic filters and sensors. The design is also scalable to much higher frequencies and can be made temperature-stable using a compensation layer.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A double-layer bulk acoustic resonator consisting of two 365 um thick LiTaO3 plates rotation-bonded with an in-plane relative rotation of 121 degrees, featuring aluminum electrodes for longitudinal electric field driving.

Key observables. Electromechanical coupling coefficient (kt^2), resonance frequency (fs), quality factors (Qs and Qp), and temperature coefficient of frequency (TCF).

Important parameters / regimes. kt^2 = 25.7%, resonance frequency = 5.24 MHz, rotation angle = 121 degrees, and predicted TCF of -25 ppm/deg C with SiO2 compensation.

Assumptions / limitations. The frequency scaling assumes the ability to reduce film and electrode thickness while preserving the coupling coefficient; temperature modeling assumes known temperature-dependent material constants.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the 3D and cross-sectional diagrams of the resonator; Fig 2 displays piezoelectric coefficients at different rotation angles, admittance spectra for single and double-layer resonators, and displacement-field distributions for primary and spurious modes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of coupling and spurious modes, describes the fabrication and structural design of the bilayer resonator, presents experimental measurements of coupling and frequency, provides FEA simulations for scaling and temperature stability, and concludes with potential applications.

Abstract

We report a structurally simple double-layer lithium tantalate (LiTaO3) bulk acoustic resonator that enables symmetry-selective excitation of the second-order thickness-shear (SH2) mode with ultrahigh electromechanical coupling. Two 31 deg Y-oriented single-crystal LiTaO3 films are rotation-bonded with complementary polarization (+X/-X) and driven by a longitudinal electric field. Matching between the effective piezoelectric symmetry and the SH2 mode yields an effective electromechanical coupling coefficient of kt^2 = 25.7% at 5.24 MHz. To our knowledge, this is the highest kt^2 reported for a LiTaO3 resonator architecture to date. The measured response is dominated by the target SH2 mode, with only weak parasitic features in the operating band. The structure is also tunable: the resonance frequency and coupling can be adjusted through geometric parameters while maintaining stable modal behavior, indicating good process tolerance. Finite-element analysis further suggests straightforward frequency scaling beyond 5 GHz by reducing the film and electrode thickness while preserving approximately 25% kt^2. In addition, introducing a SiO2 compensation layer is predicted to improve the temperature coefficient of frequency to approximately -25 ppm/deg C. These results establish complementary-polarity double-layer LiTaO3 as a practical platform for high-coupling, spurious-suppressed acoustic resonators and provide a scalable route toward wideband ultrasonic resonators, filters, and related transducers.
