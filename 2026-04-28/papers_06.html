Gate-dependent offset charge shifts and anharmonicity in gatemon qubits in the weak tunneling regime

Authors: Utkan Güngördü, Rusko Ruskov, Silas Hoffman, Kyle Serniak, Andrew J. Kerman, Charles Tahan
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24716v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Quantifying how gate-dependent offset charge shifts and capacitance renormalization impact the energy spectrum and anharmonicity of gatemon qubits in the weak tunneling regime.

Main result. The study predicts that junction asymmetry induces new gate-voltage-dependent charge offsets and that capacitance renormalization significantly influences qubit anharmonicity, providing a protocol for experimental detection.

Method. The authors use a many-body derived effective Hamiltonian and a WKB approach (including an extended WKB method) to solve the Schrödinger equation and analyze Andreev bound state dynamics.

Summary. This paper investigates the impact of gate-dependent effects on the performance of gatemon qubits. By using a many-body treatment of S-QD-S junctions, the authors show that tunneling asymmetry leads to new, detectable charge offsets. They also demonstrate that capacitance renormalization plays a crucial role in determining qubit anharmonicity. These findings provide a theoretical framework for understanding and controlling the energy spectrum of gatemon-based quantum processors.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A gatemon qubit consisting of a superconductor-quantum dot-superconductor (S-QD-S) junction in the weak tunneling regime, modeled via an effective Hamiltonian incorporating Andreev bound states and continuum energy shifts.

Key observables. Qubit transition energy oscillations (E01), charge dispersion, qubit anharmonicity, and effective charge offset shifts.

Important parameters / regimes. Gate voltage (epsilon_g), tunneling rates (Gamma), junction asymmetry (delta_Gamma), charging energy (EC), and superconducting gap (Delta).

Assumptions / limitations. Weak tunneling regime, slow phase approximation, and the assumption that the even and odd parity sectors are well-separated.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows junction transparency vs. gate voltage; Figure 3 compares WKB theory to exact numerics for charge dispersion; Figure 5 compares relative contributions to anharmonicity; Figure 6 shows numerical anharmonicity vs. gate voltage.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical problem of gatemon dynamics, presents a many-body derived effective Hamiltonian, applies WKB and numerical methods to study charge dispersion and offsets, analyzes anharmonicity drivers, and concludes with experimental implications.

Why it may be interesting. This work is relevant for researchers in open quantum systems as it explores how the coupling to a continuum and quantum phase fluctuations lead to renormalization of fundamental qubit parameters like capacitance and charge offsets.

Abstract

Gatemon qubits are based on a superconductor-quantum dot-superconductor (S-QD-S) junction which enables in situ electrostatic tuning via a gate electrode. For a single-channel QD this structure gives rise to two subgap Andreev bound states (ABSs), and generally leads to a richer quantum phase dynamics as compared to conventional transmons. In a recent work [Phys. Rev. B 111, 214503 (2025)] we derived the quantum phase dynamics from a many-body treatment which leads to an effective gate voltage-dependent Hamiltonian that self-consistently incorporates the phase quantization. It predicts (i) a renormalization of the junction's effective capacitance and (ii) the presence of gate voltage and occupation-dependent charge offsets in junctions with tunneling asymmetry. Here, we quantify the observable impact of these effects on the qubit's energy spectrum and anharmonicity, by studying the interplay of the two Andreev branches as a function of dot-gate voltages and junction transparencies. We show the relation of these predictions to simplified gatemon models and propose a protocol to experimentally detect the predicted charge offsets.

Gauge-covariant projected entangled paired states for interacting systems in a magnetic field

Authors: Wei Tang, Gunnar Möller, Frank Verstraete, Laurens Vanderstraeten
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24574v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Simulating interacting many-body systems in a uniform magnetic field is difficult because the choice of gauge breaks standard translation symmetry, necessitating large, computationally expensive magnetic unit cells.

Main result. The authors developed a gauge-covariant PEPS ansatz that uses virtual flux tensors to maintain translation invariance of physical observables, allowing for efficient simulations using a single unit cell regardless of the magnetic flux value.

Method. A new Projected Entangled Pair State (PEPS) ansatz is constructed by imprinting virtual U(1) flux tensors onto the tensor bonds, optimized via a single-site boundary MPS algorithm and gradient-based variational optimization.

Summary. This paper introduces a new tensor network method (PEPS) designed to simulate many-body systems in magnetic fields. By implementing magnetic flux at the virtual level of the tensors, the authors create an ansatz that is gauge-covariant and preserves translation symmetry in physical observables. This approach allows researchers to treat magnetic flux as a continuous parameter and avoids the need for large, extended unit cells. The method's accuracy is validated against established MPS simulations for the bosonic Harper-Hofstadter-Hubbard model.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on the bosonic Harper-Hofstadter-Hubbard model on a two-dimensional square lattice, featuring hopping strength t, on-site repulsion U, and a uniform magnetic flux per plaquette phi.

Key observables. Ground state energy per site and physical expectation values.

Important parameters / regimes. Hopping strength (t), interaction strength (U), magnetic flux per plaquette (phi), and particle filling (rho).

Assumptions / limitations. The ansatz construction is motivated by the strong interaction (infinite-U) limit and first-order perturbation theory; the method is demonstrated for bosons but is applicable to fermions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the Peierls phase pattern in the Landau gauge; Figure 2 compares the ground state energy per site calculated with the proposed PEPS method against MPS results on cylinders.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of gauge-dependent symmetry breaking, proposes a gauge-covariant PEPS ansatz based on perturbative expansion, demonstrates the method's gauge independence and symmetry preservation, provides numerical benchmarks against MPS, and includes an appendix proving gauge covariance via flux tensor reconfiguration.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a powerful new tool for simulating topological phases and fractional Chern insulators in lattice models without the computational bottleneck of large magnetic unit cells.

Abstract

The Hamiltonian for a system of itinerant particles on a two-dimensional lattice in a uniform magnetic field reduces the translational symmetry to a magnetic translation group, because of the need to choose a particular gauge for the vector potential. Nonetheless, in many situations all physical observables of the ground state remain entirely translation invariant. In this work, we introduce a projected entangled-pair state (PEPS) wavefunction with a pattern of virtual flux tensors, for which all physical expectation values are translation invariant by construction, possibly within an enlarged unit cell reflecting any symmetry breaking in the target state. Moreover, we show that the usual contraction and optimization methods for translation-invariant PEPS can be used, with the magnetic flux per plaquette only entering as a continuous parameter in the tensor network contractions. Therefore, our approach provides a method for simulating an interacting many-body system in a uniform magnetic field independently of the gauge choice for the vector potential and bypassing the need to consider extended magnetic unit cells.

Linear equivalence of nonlinear recurrent neural networks

Authors: David G. Clark
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23489v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper seeks to analytically derive and verify the 'linear equivalence' ansatz, which posits that the full $N imes N$ covariance matrix of a large nonlinear recurrent neural network can be approximated by a linear network driven by independent noise.

Main result. The author provides a rigorous derivation using the two-site cavity method, proving that the nonlinear covariance matrix matches the linear-equivalent form with controlled error scales of $O(1/N)$ for the error matrix and $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ for relative precision.

Method. The study employs the two-site cavity method and Dynamical Mean Field Theory (DMFT), utilizing a linear-residual decomposition and a self-consistent matrix equation approach, supplemented by large-scale numerical simulations.

Summary. This paper provides a formal analytical proof for the 'linear equivalence' of nonlinear recurrent neural networks. It demonstrates that the complex, high-dimensional covariance structure of a nonlinear system can be accurately captured by a simpler linear model with effective noise. By using the two-site cavity method, the author shows that the nonlinearities only affect the system through specific scalar order parameters. This result bridges the gap between the well-understood theory of linear random matrices and the more complex dynamics of nonlinear, chaotic networks.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Large-scale nonlinear recurrent neural networks with $N$ units and random, quenched, i.i.d. asymmetric couplings $J_{ij}$. The dynamics follow a rate model (Sompolinsky et al.) with a nonlinear activation function such as $ anh$ or $ ext{erfen}$.

Key observables. The $N imes N$ time-lagged covariance matrix $C_\phi(\omega)$, the response matrix $S_\phi(\omega)$, and scalar order parameters (single-site response and covariance).

Important parameters / regimes. Network size $N$ (thermodynamic limit), coupling strength $g$ (specifically in the chaotic regime $g > 1/f'(0)$), and the sampling ratio $\alpha$.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the large $N$ limit, i.i.d. quenched couplings, and that the network reaches a stationary state; it also assumes the absence of macroscopic collective modes in the reservoir.

Figures summary. The notes mention Figure 1 shows time-lagged dynamics, and the paper includes numerical comparisons of covariance prediction errors and residual cross-covariances across different network sizes.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing the problem of characterizing high-dimensional covariance; it reviews single-site DMFT; it presents two distinct cavity-based derivations (linear-residual decomposition and self-consistent matrix equation); it addresses the failure of naive Gaussian closure; it provides error scaling analysis; and concludes with numerical verification.

Abstract

Large nonlinear recurrent neural networks with random couplings generate high-dimensional, potentially chaotic activity whose structure is of interest in neuroscience, machine learning, ecology, and other fields. A fundamental object encoding the collective structure of this activity is the $N \times N$ covariance matrix. Prior analytical work on the covariance matrix has been limited to low-dimensional summary statistics, not the full high-dimensional object for a specific realization of the couplings. Recent work proposed an ansatz in which, at large $N$, the covariance matrix for a typical quenched realization takes the same form as that of a linear network with the same couplings, driven by independent noise, with mean-field order parameters setting the effective transfer function and the noise spectrum. Here, we derive this ansatz using the two-site cavity method, providing two distinct derivations that offer complementary perspectives. The first decomposes each unit's activity into a linear component and a nonlinear residual, and shows that cross-covariances between residuals at distinct sites are strongly suppressed, so that residuals act as independent noise within a linear network. The second writes a self-consistent matrix equation for the covariance matrix. A naive Gaussian closure for the joint statistics of activity at distinct sites gives the wrong equation; the cavity method separates Gaussian and non-Gaussian contributions, which enter at the same order, and produces the correct one. We verify the predictions numerically across a range of network sizes. These results extend linear equivalence from feedforward high-dimensional nonlinear systems, where the weights being analyzed are independent of their inputs, to recurrent networks, where the activities depend on the same couplings that generate them.

New non-Euclidean neural quantum states from additional types of hyperbolic recurrent neural networks

Authors: H. L. Dao
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24337v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to extend non-Euclidean Neural Quantum States (NQS) by introducing new hyperbolic architectures, specifically Poincaré RNN, Lorentz RNN, Lorentz GRU, and Poincaré GRU, to better approximate quantum many-body ground states.

Main result. All four new hyperbolic RNN/GRU variants consistently outperformed their Euclidean counterparts in 100-spin Heisenberg models, with the Lorentz RNN emerging as the most efficient and high-performing ansatz despite having significantly fewer parameters.

Method. The researchers used Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) to train the neural network ansatzes and compared their ground state energy estimates against benchmarks from the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) method.

Summary. This paper introduces new hyperbolic neural network architectures (Poincaré and Lorentz RNN/GRU) for use as Neural Quantum States. By applying these to 1D Heisenberg spin chains, the author demonstrates that hyperbolic geometries significantly outperform standard Euclidean networks in approximating ground state energies. Notably, the Lorentz RNN achieves superior performance with much fewer parameters. The work also addresses critical numerical stability issues inherent in hyperbolic manifolds.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on 1D Heisenberg J1-J2 and J1-J2-J3 spin models with 100 spins, exploring various coupling regimes that introduce hierarchical structures and frustration.

Key observables. Ground state energy, local energy, and probability distribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling constants J2 and J3; spatial constraints Rmax (for Poincaré) and Lmax (for Lorentz) used to prevent numerical instabilities.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the applicability of the Marshall sign for unfrustrated systems and focuses on 1D geometries; the optimal spatial constraints were determined via trial-and-error rather than exhaustive scanning.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the autoregressive calculation process for real and complex NQS, the impact of spatial constraints (Rmax and Lmax) on numerical stability, and training curves comparing energy convergence across different architectures.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the mathematical framework for hyperbolic RNN/GRU architectures, details the VMC implementation for real and complex wavefunctions, presents benchmarking results against Euclidean models and DMRG, and discusses numerical stability and future directions.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and simulation as it demonstrates that non-Euclidean geometries can capture complex, hierarchical correlations in quantum systems more efficiently than standard Euclidean neural networks.

Abstract

In this work, we extend the class of previously introduced non-Euclidean neural quantum states (NQS) which consists only of Poincaré hyperbolic GRU, to new variants including Poincaré RNN as well as Lorentz RNN and Lorentz GRU. In addition to constructing and introducing the new non-Euclidean hyperbolic NQS ansatzes, we generalized the results of our earlier work regarding the definitive outperformances delivered by hyperbolic Poincaré GRU NQS ansatzes when benchmarked against their Euclidean counterparts in the Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) experiments involving the quantum many-body settings of the Heisenberg $J_1J_2$ and $J_1J_2J_3$ models, which exhibit hierarchical structures in the forms of the different degrees of nearest-neighbor interactions. Here, in particular, using larger systems consisting of 100 spins, we found that all four hyperbolic RNN/GRU NQS variants always outperformed their respective Euclidean counterparts. Specifically, for all $J_2$ and $(J_2,J_3)$ couplings considered, including $J_2=0.0$, Lorentz RNN NQS and Poincaré RNN NQS always outperformd Euclidean RNN NQS, while Lorentz/Poincaré GRU NQS always outperformed Euclidean GRU NQS, with a single exception when $J_2=0.0$ for Poincaré GRU NQS. Furthermore, among the four hyperbolic NQS ansatzes, depending on the specific $J_2$ or $(J_2, J_3)$ couplings, on four out of eight experiment settings, Lorentz GRU and Poincaré GRU took turns to be the top performing variant among all Euclidean and hyperbolic NQS ansatzes considered, while Lorentz RNN, with up to three times fewer parameters, was capable of not only surpassing the Euclidean GRU eight out of eight times but also outperforming both Lorentz GRU and Poincaré GRU four out of eight times, to emerge as the best overall hyperbolic NQS ansatz.

No cloning with unitary scaling

Authors: Dafa Li
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22960v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates whether it is possible to create a 'U-copy' of an unknown quantum pure state, where the copy is the original state transformed by an arbitrary unitary operator U, using unitary evolution.

Main result. The author proves a general no-cloning principle stating that it is impossible to create a U-copy of an arbitrary unknown pure state via unitary evolution, establishing that U-copying is only possible for identical or orthogonal states.

Method. The proof utilizes the inner product method to compare the transformation of states under unitary evolution and employs linearity tests and counter-examples using CNOT gates.

Summary. This paper proposes a generalization of the fundamental quantum no-cloning principle. Instead of looking at identical copies, it examines the possibility of creating copies that have been transformed by a unitary operator U. The author proves that such 'U-copying' is impossible for arbitrary unknown states, effectively showing that the constraints of the original no-cloning theorem extend to any unitary scaling of the state.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The model involves an unknown quantum pure state |psi> and a target system initially in a ready state |0>, subjected to a unitary transformation T designed to produce the state |psi>U|psi>.

Important parameters / regimes. The identity of the unitary operator U and the inner product between states |psi> and |phi>.

Assumptions / limitations. The proof assumes that the copying process is governed by unitary evolution and focuses specifically on unknown pure states.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of U-copying, provides an analytical proof using inner products, demonstrates failure via a CNOT gate counter-example, performs a linearity test, and concludes with an equivalence theorem to standard cloning.

Abstract

It is known that the classical information like strings of bits can be copied. In 1982, Wootters and Zurek proposed the quantum no-cloning principle [1]. No-cloning principle says that it is impossible to make an identical copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum pure state by using unitary evolution. In this paper, we propose a general no-cloning principle. We call U|psi>, where U is any unitary operator, a U-copy of the state |psi>. The general no-cloning principle states that it is impossible to make a U-copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum pure state by using unitary evolution.

Noise-aware selection of circuit cutting strategies under hardware noise non-uniformity

Authors: Debarthi Pal, Ritajit Majumdar, Padmanabha Venkatagiri Seshadri, Anupama Ray, Yogesh Simmhan
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24422v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Large quantum circuits often require execution across high-noise regions of hardware, and existing circuit cutting strategies lack systematic guidance on how to align cuts with non-uniform hardware noise to balance sampling overhead and fidelity.

Main result. The proposed Hardware-Informed Circuit cutting (HIC) framework achieves 5-54x reductions in execution overhead for 20-qubit circuits and enables tractable cutting for 50-qubit circuits by exploiting spatial noise non-uniformity.

Method. A three-stage workflow involving hardware 'puncturing' to remove noise outliers, identifying connected components to define subcircuit sizes, and using a weighted layout score to select optimal device constraints.

Summary. This paper introduces a new framework called Hardware-Informed Circuit cutting (HIC) designed to optimize how large quantum circuits are decomposed into smaller subcircuits. By specifically accounting for the non-uniform noise patterns (islands of low noise) found in modern NISQ hardware, the method selects cutting strategies that avoid high-error regions. This approach significantly reduces the exponential sampling overhead typically associated with circuit cutting, making the execution of much larger circuits (up to 50 qubits) practically feasible on current hardware.

Detailed structure
