Quantum echo-enabled high harmonic generation using ultrafast electrons

Authors: Yangfan He, Chenhao Pan, Bin Zhang, Yiming Pan
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23569v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 interference shaping light 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The need for compact, coherent, and tunable sources of EUV and X-ray radiation, specifically addressing the lack of selectivity and efficiency in existing PINEM-based high-harmonic generation.

Main result. The development of a Quantum Echo-Enabled High-Harmonic Generation (QEEHG) scheme that uses quantum interference to selectively enhance specific harmonic orders while suppressing others.

Method. A theoretical framework using a multi-path quantum interferometer approach and numerical simulations via a quantum wavepacket evolution algorithm (FFPG) to model electron-laser interactions.

Summary. This paper proposes a new method called Quantum Echo-Enabled High-Harmonic Generation (QEEHG) to create tunable, coherent X-ray and EUV radiation. By using a sequence of laser modulations and dispersive drift sections, the researchers show how to manipulate the quantum phase of electron wavepackets. This allows for the selective enhancement of specific high-order harmonics through constructive interference while suppressing unwanted radiation. The technique provides a promising path toward compact, high-brightness radiation sources for electron microscopy and diffraction.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An ultrafast electron beamline interacting with two optical modulation stages and two dispersive drift (chirp) sections, modeled using a time-dependent Schrödinger equation with momentum-dependent chirping and position-dependent modulation operators.

Key observables. The q-th harmonic bunching factor b(q), the Wigner distribution of the electron wavepacket, and the harmonic spectrum amplitude.

Important parameters / regimes. Modulation strengths (g1, g2), laser frequencies (omega1, omega2), chirping coefficients (xi), drift lengths (d0, df), and the large recoil condition.

Assumptions / limitations. The interaction time is much shorter than the free-drift times (impulsive limit), the laser field is co-propagating, and the large recoil condition (momentum kick much larger than momentum spread) is assumed for analytical derivation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the QEEHG setup and quantum interference network; Figure 2 displays the harmonic spectrum, Wigner distribution, and path selection maps; Figure 3 illustrates the optimization of global phase conditions and tailored spectra (periodic and plateau regimes).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific problem of radiation source development, presents the proposed QEEHG scheme and its physical components, derives the mathematical operator-based framework for electron wavepacket evolution, provides numerical validation and optimization protocols, and concludes with the demonstration of spectrally tailored harmonic outputs.

Why it may be interesting. The paper presents a sophisticated application of quantum interference and wavepacket shaping, using a 'quantum echo' to control the interference of multiple quantum pathways, which is highly relevant to the control of coherent states in quantum optics.

Abstract

Controlling and generating ultrafast free-electron wavepackets via laser is pivotal for photon-induced near-field electron microscopes (PINEM) and also for developing compact, coherent free-electron radiation sources. Here, we present a quantum echo-enabled high-harmonic generation (QEEHG) scheme that manipulates the quantum phase of electron wavepackets to produce tunable, coherent high-harmonic radiation at ultrashort wavelengths. This framework, inspired by the EEHG concept for free-electron lasers by Stupikov et al. (2009), leverages multiphoton PINEM scattering followed by dispersive chirp sections to induce quantum interference among photon sidebands. Such interference selectively enhances a targeted harmonic order - for instance, the 60th harmonic at 13.3nm from an 800nm seeding - while suppressing unwanted radiations. The optimization of harmonic orders and its non-classical spectral characteristics are analyzed. This quantum echo technique establishes a promising paradigm for compact coherent sources and provides new perspectives for quantum wavefunction shaping in ultrafast electron microscopy and diffraction.

DiffQEC: A versatile diffusion model for quantum error correction

Authors: Tianyi Xu, Qinglong Liu, Maolin Wang, Fei Zhang, Zhe Zhao, Yang Wang, Ye Wei
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24640v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of decoding quantum error correction (QEC) by inferring physical error configurations from syndrome patterns, specifically addressing the loss of uncertainty information in existing point-estimation decoders.

Main result. DiffQEC reduces logical error rates by up to 10.2% relative to MWPM and 5% relative to tensor-network decoding on Google's Sycamore processor, while providing useful posterior distributions for post-selection and error analysis.

Method. A generative decoder using discrete denoising diffusion probabilistic models that treats decoding as an iterative process of refining error hypotheses conditioned on syndrome history.

Summary. This paper introduces DiffQEC, a generative decoder for quantum error correction based on discrete denoising diffusion models. Unlike traditional decoders that provide a single error hypothesis, DiffQEC learns the full posterior distribution of errors. When tested on Google's Sycamore hardware, it significantly outperforms standard decoders like MWPM and provides actionable uncertainty estimates for error post-selection. The model scales well to large code distances and maintains efficiency suitable for real-time applications.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study uses Google's Sycamore superconducting quantum processor data (rotated surface codes) and simulated depolarizing noise for scalability tests up to distance d=17.

Key observables. Logical error rate (LER), posterior distribution of errors, decoding latency, and saliency maps for error attribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Code distances (d=3, 5, up to 17), circuit depth (1 to 5), and error models (hardware-specific and depolarizing noise).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that simulated noise at large distances can sufficiently represent real hardware-specific error mechanisms for scalability testing.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the diffusion-error analogy and pipeline; Fig 2 details the 3D syndrome processor and modulation architecture; Fig 3 presents performance benchmarks (LER reduction, latency, and scalability); Fig 4 demonstrates error analysis via saliency maps and post-selection utility.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the decoding problem and the diffusion analogy, details the DiffQEC architecture (syndrome processor and modulation), presents performance benchmarks on experimental and simulated data, and concludes with error analysis and utility for post-selection.

Abstract

Quantum computers could solve problems beyond the reach of classical devices, but this potential depends on quantum error correction (QEC) to protect fragile quantum states from noise. A central challenge in QEC is decoding: inferring likely physical errors from syndrome patterns generated by repeated stabilizer measurements. Existing decoders, including graph-based and neural approaches, typically return a single correction hypothesis and therefore discard the richer posterior structure of the error distribution conditioned on the observed syndrome. Here we recast QEC decoding as posterior inference using discrete denoising diffusion, exploiting the analogy between stochastic error accumulation and the forward diffusion process. We introduce DiffQEC, a generative decoder that combines a syndrome processor for multi-round spatial-temporal syndrome histories with syndrome feature modulation to condition denoising on the observed syndrome throughout inference. On experimental data from Google's superconducting quantum processor, DiffQEC reduces logical error rates by up to 10.2% relative to minimum-weight perfect matching and by about 5% relative to tensor-network decoding. These improvements persist for larger code distances up to 17 under depolarizing noise and for logical circuits of increasing depth. Beyond accuracy, the learned posterior provides confidence estimates for post-selection and reveals physically meaningful error structure, establishing posterior generative decoding as a practical framework for QEC.

On the complexity of quantum numerical integration: an angle-structure characterization

Authors: Francisco Chinesta, Antonio Falco, Daniela Falco-Pomares
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24289v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Determining whether the theoretical quadratic speedup of Quantum Amplitude Estimation (QAE) for numerical integration is preserved when the computational cost of constructing the amplitude oracle (state preparation) is included.

Main result. The authors introduce an angle-structure hierarchy of function classes where the gate count scales with the multilinear degree d, proving that for d=1, quantum integration can asymptotically outperform classical Monte Carlo and achieve a quantum-classical separation for certain Sobolev-regular functions.

Method. The study uses a multilinear expansion of the angle map via the Walsh-Hadamard Transform to decompose the encoding operator into multi-controlled RY gates, combined with Maximum Likelihood Amplitude Estimation (MLAE) for error analysis.

Summary. This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in quantum numerical integration: the cost of preparing the quantum state representing the integrand. By classifying functions based on the multilinear degree of their 'angle map,' the authors show that certain classes of functions allow for highly efficient quantum circuits. They demonstrate a clear quantum advantage over classical methods for specific function regularities and validate these findings using both NMR and superconducting quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The framework uses Quantum Amplitude Estimation applied to numerical integration on the interval [0, 1], with experimental validation performed on the SpinQ Triangulum (3-qubit NMR processor) and IBM Kingston (127-qubit superconducting processor).

Key observables. The encoded amplitude (Riemann sum), ancilla qubit measurement probabilities (pk), and the target integration accuracy (epsilon).

Important parameters / regimes. Multilinear degree (d), Sobolev regularity (s), target accuracy (epsilon), number of qubits (n), and quadrature order (p).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the cost of building the oracle is a structural component of the total complexity, rather than treating it as a black box; it also assumes the multilinear degree is the primary driver of circuit depth.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a log-log plot of the depth-versus-accuracy trade-off highlighting the quantum advantage region; Figure 2 illustrates the quantum-classical separation gap; Figure 3 depicts the amplitude-encoding circuits for n=2; and Figure 4/5 show hardware-specific performance and probability comparisons.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of oracle complexity, defines the angle-structure hierarchy and its multilinear expansion, establishes the encoding-circuit theorem and depth-accuracy trade-offs, proves quantum-classical separation for specific function classes, and concludes with experimental validation on NMR and superconducting hardware.

Abstract

We study numerical integration on $[0,1]$ by quantum amplitude estimation (QAE), focusing on the cost of constructing the amplitude oracle. Although QAE improves the statistical component of the integration error, this advantage is relevant only when the integrand has low encoding complexity. We introduce a hierarchy of grid function classes $\mathcal{G}_n^{(d)}$, defined by requiring the angle map $Θ_g:\{0,1\}^n\to[0,π]$ to be multilinear of degree at most $d$. Membership is classically checkable in $O(n2^n)$ time by the Walsh--Hadamard transform. For $g\in\mathcal{G}_n^{(d)}$, the encoding operator factorises into $\sum_{k=0}^d\binom{n}{k}$ multi-controlled $R_Y$ gates, interpolating between an affine $O(n)$ regime and the generic exponential regime. Combining this structure with classical discretisation estimates for $g\in C^α[0,1]$, we obtain a depth-versus-accuracy trade-off: gate count $O((\log(1/\varepsilon))^d\varepsilon^{-1})$ suffices to achieve $\varepsilon$-accuracy with constant probability. For $d=1$ this becomes $O(\varepsilon^{-1}\log(1/\varepsilon))$, improving over classical Monte Carlo for every $α\ge1$. We also prove an unconditional separation: $\mathcal{G}_n^{(1)}$ contains functions of Sobolev regularity $s<1/2$ for which the quantum oracle cost is $O(1/\varepsilon)$, whereas classical deterministic or randomised quadrature requires $Ω(\varepsilon^{-1/s})$ evaluations. These results identify explicit integrand classes for which the full cost of QAE-based integration, including state preparation, is asymptotically better than classical methods. Experiments on SpinQ Triangulum and IBM Kingston illustrate the hierarchy at $n=2$: circuits inside $\mathcal{G}_n^{(d)}$ run successfully, while those exceeding the Triangulum coherence budget fail as predicted.

Adaptive Tensor Network Sampling for Quantum Optimal Control

Authors: Zeki Zeybek, Rick Mukherjee, Peter Schmelcher
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24467v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Efficiently navigating high-dimensional, non-convex optimization landscapes in Quantum Optimal Control (QOC) to avoid the curse of dimensionality and the limitations of existing gradient-based and gradient-free methods.

Main result. The proposed TT-EDA method exhibits stable convergence and competitive performance against established gradient-free baselines, showing particular advantages in high-dimensional regimes.

Method. A gradient-free sampling heuristic called TT-EDA that uses a Matrix Product State (MPS/Tensor Train) to define an unnormalized score function over discrete control parameters, which is iteratively updated via gradient ascent on the log-scores of elite samples.

Summary. This paper presents TT-EDA, a new optimization heuristic for quantum optimal control that uses Tensor Trains to represent and sample from control parameter distributions. By using a Matrix Product State to define a score function, the method efficiently searches high-dimensional, non-convex landscapes. The authors demonstrate that the method can successfully recover complex control structures, such as 'bang-bang' pulses and STIRAP protocols, with performance comparable to or better than existing gradient-free algorithms. It is particularly effective at scaling to higher-dimensional control problems.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The method is tested on various benchmarks including single-qubit state transfer, Bell-pair preparation in coupled spin systems, qutrit gate implementation, and population transfer in three-level open quantum systems governed by the Lindblad master equation.

Key observables. Fidelity, infidelity, and population dynamics.

Important parameters / regimes. Bond dimension (chi), number of time steps (L), number of amplitude levels (d), learning rate (eta), and the lambda parameter for controlling the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.

Assumptions / limitations. The method is specifically designed for discrete control parameter spaces and relies on the ability of the Tensor Train to provide a compressed representation of the control space; it also assumes a discretization of continuous control fields.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the TT-EDA optimization loop; Figure 2 illustrates the MPS sampling and update mechanism; Figure 3 and 4 show convergence and pulse profiles for single-qubit tasks; Figure 7 displays convergence and STIRAP-like dynamics for open systems; Figure 8 compares scaling behavior across different dimensions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the TT-EDA algorithm, describes the MPS-based sampling and update mechanism, presents benchmarks across various quantum control tasks (single-qubit, Bell-pair, qutrit, and open systems), evaluates performance against standard optimizers, and analyzes the exploration-exploitation tradeoff using high-dimensional benchmark functions.

Why it may be interesting. It introduces a novel way to use tensor networks not just for simulating many-body states, but as a generative tool for optimizing complex control pulses in open and multi-level quantum systems.

Abstract

Quantum optimal control (QOC) provides a systematic framework for achieving high-fidelity operations in quantum systems and plays a central role in tasks such as gate synthesis, state transfer, and pulse design. Existing QOC methods broadly fall into two categories: gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms. The associated optimization landscape is often high-dimensional, non-convex, and populated by numerous local minima, making efficient gradient-free search strategies essential. To address this, we introduce a gradient-free matrix product state/tensor train (MPS/TT) sampling heuristic for discrete quantum optimal control. In our approach, the MPS defines a score function over the space of discrete control parameters, which in turn induces a sampling distribution over candidate control sequences. This distribution is iteratively refined through selection of better performing sequences and local tensor updates to bias the search toward high-performing sequences. We evaluate the method on a range of benchmark problems, including single-qubit state transfer, Bell-pair preparation, qutrit gate implementation, and open-system population transfer. Across these tasks, the method exhibits stable convergence behavior and competitive empirical performance relative to established gradient-free baselines. These results suggest that tensor network sampling offers a viable heuristic framework for discrete quantum control.

AutoQResearch: LLM-Guided Closed-Loop Policy Search for Adaptive Variational Quantum Optimization

Authors: Monit Sharma, Hoong Chuin Lau
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24283v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The difficulty of manually configuring variational quantum algorithms (VQAs) for combinatorial optimization, which requires coordinating complex, interacting design choices like ansatz, optimizer, and objective.

Main result. The AutoQResearch framework successfully discovers adaptive, scale-dependent solver policies that outperform static baselines on MIS and CVRP problems, with validation on real IBM quantum hardware.

Method. An LLM-guided closed-loop experimentation framework using a 'scout-promote-confirm' protocol to perform sequential policy search over a curated design space.

Summary. This paper introduces AutoQResearch, an automated framework that uses Large Language Models to discover optimal configurations for variational quantum algorithms. Instead of just tuning hyperparameters, the system learns adaptive policies that change solver settings based on real-time performance diagnostics. The researchers demonstrated that the agent can discover complex, scale-dependent strategies for solving combinatorial optimization problems. Crucially, the discovered policies were validated on real IBM quantum hardware, proving they are not just simulation artifacts.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The framework is applied to combinatorial optimization problems, specifically the Maximum Independent Set (MIS) and the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP), using VQA families such as QAOA, VQE, and QRAO on IBM quantum processors.

Key observables. Optimality gap, feasibility rate, convergence stagnation, sampling concentration, and hardware-specific metrics like gate counts and depth.

Important parameters / regimes. Problem scales (16 to 64 nodes for MIS), CVaR parameter (alpha), sampling budgets, penalty coefficients, and qubit compression ratios.

Assumptions / limitations. The design problem can be modeled as a sequential decision process, and the LLM's search is restricted to a curated, auditable design space to prevent overfitting.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows stage-wise search trajectories; Figure 2 illustrates the discrepancy between cheap proxy (scout) evaluations and confirmed results; Figure 3 shows stage-wise CVRP performance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the AutoQResearch framework, details the LLM-guided sequential policy search and evaluation protocol, presents case studies on MIS and CVRP, validates results on real IBM hardware, and discusses methodological findings regarding proxy reliability.

Abstract

Configuring variational quantum algorithms for combinatorial optimization remains a difficult, expert-driven process requiring coordinated choices over solver family, ansatz, objective, and optimizer. We present AutoQResearch, an LLM-guided closed-loop experimentation framework that casts this task as sequential policy search over a curated design space. Instead of a single static configuration, the framework searches for adaptive solver-control policies that condition future decisions on diagnostics such as feasibility, optimality gap, and convergence stagnation. The system operates through a structured workflow: an LLM agent edits a small policy surface under a fixed evaluation harness, candidate policies are screened using cheap scout evaluations, and only the strongest candidates are promoted to full confirmation. This enables controlled autonomous exploration while guarding against proxy overfitting and unstable selection. We evaluate the framework on Maximum Independent Set (MIS) and the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP). On MIS instances (16--64 vertices), discovered policies substantially outperform static baselines and reveal scale-dependent behavior: CVaR objectives are effective at small scale, while QRAO-based qubit compression provides the most effective explored scaling path. On CVRP curricula (8--12 customers) and a held-out E-n13-k4 benchmark, the framework discovers adaptations involving sampling budget, penalty design, and hybrid repair protocols, yielding high-quality solutions.
