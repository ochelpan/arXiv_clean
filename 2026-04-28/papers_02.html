How Quantum Contextuality disappears in the Classical Limit

Authors: Arthur C. R. Dutra, Roberto D. Baldijão, Marcelo Terra Cunha
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24735v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates how Kochen-Specker contextuality disappears in the classical limit, specifically resolving the paradox of why state-independent contextuality vanishes despite persisting in the maximally mixed state.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that while state-dependent contextuality is erased by state degradation, state-independent contextuality is suppressed by noise acting between sequential measurements, which shrinks the observable correlations.

Method. The study uses open-system dynamics, analyzing sequential measurement implementations under depolarizing channels using both Schrödinger and Heisenberg pictures.

Summary. This paper explores the mechanism by which quantum contextuality vanishes as a system interacts with its environment. It addresses the apparent paradox where state-independent contextuality seems to resist classicalization because it persists even in maximally mixed states. By modeling noise via depolarizing channels in sequential measurements, the authors show that decoherence suppresses the specific correlations needed to witness contextuality. Ultimately, they prove that environmental interaction effectively drives the system into a classical, noncontextual regime.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The framework involves open quantum systems modeled via depolarizing channels and the GKSL master equation, applied to the KCBS and Peres-Mermin (PM) square scenarios.

Key observables. Sequential correlators and noncontextuality inequalities (KCBS and Peres-Mermin bounds).

Important parameters / regimes. The depolarizing parameter (p), representing the level of noise/decoherence, and the critical threshold (p_crit) for the disappearance of contextuality.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes Markovian dynamics, isotropic noise (unital noise), and that decoherence acts both on state preparation and during the measurement sequence.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining classicality and contextuality, introduces the paradox of state-independent contextuality, applies depolarizing channels to the KCBS and PM scenarios, and concludes by showing how noise-induced suppression of correlations leads to the classical limit.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental link between the emergence of classicality through decoherence and the erasure of foundational quantum properties like Kochen-Specker contextuality, which is highly relevant to the study of open quantum systems and quantum foundations.

Abstract

The emergence of classicality is fundamentally driven by the interaction between a quantum system and its environment. Foundational open-system approaches, notably the Caldeira-Leggett model, successfully captured how these interactions lead to macroscopic effects like quantum dissipation and decoherence. However, these approaches often leave the precise definitions of classicality and quantumness ambiguous. In quantum information theory, this boundary is a heavily scrutinized question, and Kochen-Specker contextuality emerges as a hallmark of nonclassicality. It is therefore natural to investigate whether decoherence can actually suppress this property. Taking this path creates an apparent conundrum, once there exist two distinct manifestations of quantum contextuality: state-dependent and state-independent ones. While state-dependent contextuality naturally vanishes under state degradation, state-independent contextuality could persist for any quantum state, since it shows up even for the maximally mixed state! In this paper, we resolve this apparent paradox by analyzing sequential measurement implementations of the paradigmatic Klyachko, Can, Binicioğlu, and Shumovsky (KCBS) and Peres-Mermin prepare-and-measure scenarios under the influence of depolarizing channels. By introducing noise both prior to and in between measurements, and by analyzing the resulting sequential correlators in both the Schrödinger and Heisenberg pictures, we show how open-system dynamics suppress the correlations required to witness contextuality, leading to classicalization.

Dynamical generation of stable optical-microwave squeezing in structured reservoirs

Authors: Chen Wang, Man Shen, Shi-fan Qi, Yan-kui Bai
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24215v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 correlated / nonlocal dissipation 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of generating and maintaining stable, high-level optical-microwave two-mode squeezed states (TMSS) in the presence of noise and the limitations of Markovian environments.

Main result. Non-Markovian (structured) environments can significantly enhance squeezing levels and allow TMS to persist even after external driving is switched off, especially when the spectral densities of the two modes are matched.

Method. The authors use second-order perturbation theory to derive an effective Hamiltonian and employ the Non-Markovian Hierarchical Equation of Motion (NMHL) to analyze the system's dynamical evolution.

Summary. This paper investigates how structured, non-Markovian environments can be used to generate and stabilize two-mode squeezing between optical and microwave modes in a hybrid electromechanical system. The authors demonstrate that non-Markovian noise can enhance squeezing levels compared to standard Markovian noise and even allow the squeezed state to persist after the external driving fields are turned off. They also find that matching the spectral densities of the optical and microwave reservoirs is key to preventing the degradation of the squeezed state. This work offers a way to mitigate the detrimental effects of anti-squeezing in quantum transducers.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A hybrid electro-optomechanical (EOM) system consisting of an optical cavity mode, a microwave LC resonator, and a mechanical oscillator, all coupled to structured (Lorentzian) reservoirs.

Key observables. Two-mode squeezing level (SL), squeezing variance (Delta X), and anti-squeezing variance (Delta Y).

Important parameters / regimes. Effective coupling strength (g_eff), mechanical parametric amplification (r), spectral density width (lambda), and detunings.

Assumptions / limitations. The system operates in a large detuning regime where the effective Hamiltonian is valid, and the use of the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA) for system-reservoir coupling.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of the EOM system; Fig 2: Time evolution of squeezing and anti-squeezing; Fig 3: Parameter space analysis of squeezing level; Fig 4: Squeezing persistence during drive switch-off; Fig 7-9: Validation of the effective Hamiltonian and coupling strength.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a system schematic and effective Hamiltonian derivation, followed by a numerical validation of the model, an analysis of squeezing dynamics in Markovian vs. non-Markovian environments, an investigation of squeezing persistence after driving is removed, and concludes with a comparison of matched vs. mismatched spectral densities.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical framework for using reservoir engineering and non-Markovian memory effects to enhance and stabilize entanglement, which is crucial for quantum transduction and continuous-variable quantum information processing.

Abstract

Two-mode squeezed states as paradigmatic entangled resources have broad applications in quantum information processing. Here, we study the generation of stable optical-microwave squeezing in structured environments within a hybrid electro-optomechanical system, where a mechanical oscillator is simultaneously coupled to an optical cavity mode and a microwave mode of an LC resonator. Specifically, an effective Hamiltonian that captures the optical-microwave squeezing interaction is constructed by combining strongly modulated driving fields applied to both photonic modes with a mechanical parametric amplifier. Based on this effective model, the dynamical evolution of two-mode squeezing in structured environments is analyzed. It is remarkably shown that the non-Markovian noise can substantially enhance the squeezing level in comparison to the Markovian case, and that two-mode squeezing can persist even in the absence of external driving fields under non-Markovian conditions, thereby mitigating the detrimental effects of anti-squeezing. Furthermore, the persistence of the two-mode squeezed state is enhanced when the environmental spectral densities of the microwave and optical modes are identical. Our work provides a theoretical framework for generating and persisting two-mode squeezing in structured environments.

Impact of thermal and dissipative effects in a periodically-kicked quantum battery

Authors: Sebastián V. Romero, Xi Chen, Yue Ban
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24409v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the impact of thermal fluctuations and environmental dissipation (dephasing and relaxation) on the performance and robustness of periodically-kicked quantum batteries.

Main result. The study establishes that while temperature and dissipation reduce energy injection and ergotropy, the charging protocol remains robust under certain regimes, and provides an analytical framework to quantify these effects.

Method. The authors use the Jordan-Wigner and Fourier transformations to map the system to free fermions, solving the Lindblad master equation via a system of coupled linear ordinary differential equations and applying the Cayley-Hamilton theorem.

Summary. This paper explores how realistic environmental factors, such as temperature and decoherence, affect the charging of a quantum battery based on a kicked-Ising model. By using a mathematical framework involving Jordan-Wigner transformations, the authors show how energy injection and extractable work (ergotropy) decay under dissipation. They identify specific regimes where the battery's performance remains robust despite these effects. The work provides a systematic way to assess the viability of quantum batteries in noisy, real-world environments.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A Kicked-Ising Chain (KIC) acting as a quantum battery, modeled by a Transverse-Field Ising Model (TFIM) Hamiltonian subject to periodic kicks and open-system dynamics via the Lindblad master equation (including pure dephasing and thermal dissipation).

Key observables. Ergotropy (extractable work), injected energy, mean energy stored, fermion occupation number, and parity weights.

Important parameters / regimes. Inverse temperature (beta), dephasing rate (gamma_z), dissipation rates (gamma_plus/minus), system size (N), and Ising interaction strength (J).

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics are modeled using the Lindblad master equation, and the dissipation is assumed to follow specific local-spin operators.

Figures summary. Figure 2 shows the evolution of normalized injected energy over time for different temperatures, the scaling of energy injection with system size, and a parameter map of energy injection across temperature and dephasing rate space.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the Kicked-Ising battery model, moves to the analytical derivation of the open-system dynamics using fermionization, presents the results for energy injection and ergotropy under thermal and dissipative effects, and concludes with a discussion on robustness and scaling.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides an exact analytical framework for studying how environmental noise and finite temperature degrade the performance of non-equilibrium quantum energy storage devices.

Abstract

Quantum batteries (QBs) have emerged as a promising route for fast energy storage and on-chip power supply in quantum devices. Given the limited analytical understanding of open Floquet QBs, we employ the kicked-Ising model as a tractable platform to systematically study its performance under realistic conditions, including finite temperature effects and environmental dissipation. Starting from Gibbs states of the transverse-field Ising model, we incorporate thermal and decoherence effects along the evolution, using both analytical and numerical approaches. Taking ergotropy as a central figure of merit, we characterize the injected and extractable energy, and identify regimes where charging remains robust despite environmental effects. Our results provide a systematic framework for assessing QB performance under thermal and dissipative effects.

Entropy Signatures of Collective Modes and Vortex Dynamics in Rotating Two--Dimensional Bose--Einstein Condensates

Authors: L. A. Machado, N. D. Chavda, B. Chatterjee, M. A. Caracanhas, B. Chakrabarti, A. Gammal, R. P. Sagar
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24557v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the nonequilibrium dynamics, stability, and symmetry breaking of rotating 2D Bose-Einstein condensates (BECs) following interaction and trap quenches.

Main result. The study identifies a transition from regular, periodic oscillations in vortex-free states to irregular, chaotic dynamics in giant vortices, using mutual information to detect a clear dynamical phase transition at a specific rotation frequency.

Method. The Multiconfigurational Time-Dependent Hartree method for bosons (MCTDHB) is used to solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a fully correlated many-body system.

Summary. This paper explores how rotating 2D Bose-Einstein condensates respond to sudden changes in their environment. It demonstrates that while low-rotation states undergo regular oscillations, high-rotation 'giant' vortices exhibit chaotic splitting and fragmentation. By using information-theoretic tools like mutual information, the authors can precisely identify the transition between regular and chaotic dynamical regimes. This work establishes entropy-based measures as sensitive probes for many-body correlations in quantum gases.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 2D rotating Bose gas of N=8 identical bosons in a symmetric anharmonic (quartic) trap with a finite-range Gaussian interaction potential.

Key observables. One-body density and phase, Shannon entropies (marginal and joint), mutual information, Kullback-Leibler divergence (including angular-resolved), and angular Fourier components.

Important parameters / regimes. Rotation frequency (Omega), interaction strength (g), trap anisotropy, and number of bosons (N=8).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated in a 2D plane with a finite-range Gaussian potential instead of a pure delta-function contact interaction; the number of orbitals (M) is finite.

Figures summary. Figures show density snapshots of vortex formation and fragmentation, time evolution of entropy and mutual information, and a 3D dynamical phase diagram of correlations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical system and Hamiltonian, describes the MCTDHB method, analyzes initial state characterization, explores dynamics under interaction quenches, examines dynamics under trap quenches, and concludes with a phase diagram of correlations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful framework for using information-theoretic measures to quantify many-body complexity, fragmentation, and the onset of chaos in ultracold atomic systems.

Abstract

We investigate the nonequilibrium dynamics of a two-dimensional rotating Bose gas confined in a symmetric anharmonic trap, employing the multiconfigurational time-dependent Hartree method for bosons (MCTDHB). We study states ranging from vortex-free configurations to multicharged (giant) vortices, prepared by tuning the rotation frequency, and analyze their response to sudden interaction and trap quenches. In vortex-free states, interaction quenches induce regular breathing--like dynamics, whereas in the presence of giant vortices they lead to symmetry-breaking surface excitations. In contrast, trap deformations that excite quadrupole-like modes produce stable oscillations in vortex-free condensates but trigger rapid, irregular, and effectively chaotic splitting dynamics in multicharged vortices. To characterize these processes beyond conventional density and phase observables, we employ information-theoretic measures, including marginal and joint entropies, mutual information, and Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, supplemented by an angular-resolved KL measure that captures symmetry breaking and azimuthal localization. We find that chaotic splitting is accompanied by a pronounced growth of information-theoretic indicators, signaling the buildup of many-body correlations and increasing complexity in the system dynamics. Our results demonstrate the extreme sensitivity of giant vortices to excitation protocols and establish information-theoretic measures as a powerful framework to quantify correlations and complexity in rotating quantum gases.

Floquet engineering of tight-binding Hamiltonians in momentum space lattices

Authors: D. Ronco, F. Arrouas, N. Ombredane, E. Flament, Q. Levoy, B. Peaudecerf, D. Guéry-Odelin
Type: both · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24722v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 analog quantum simulation 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Developing a programmable method for Floquet engineering of tight-binding Hamiltonians in momentum-space lattices by independently tuning on-site energies and tunneling amplitudes.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that quantum resonances in a shaken rotor can be used to engineer various models, including the Rice-Mele model, and show that optimal control can significantly enhance multi-period Floquet fidelity.

Method. The study uses Floquet theory, first-order time-dependent perturbation theory, and a kernel-based inversion method, supplemented by gradient-ascent optimal control techniques.

Summary. This paper presents a method to engineer programmable tight-binding Hamiltonians in momentum space using a shaken rotor of ultracold atoms. By exploiting quantum resonances and optimal control, the researchers can independently tune tunneling amplitudes and on-site energies. They experimentally demonstrate the simulation of the Rice-Mele model, topological edge states, and Bloch oscillations. This approach turns quantum resonances into a versatile resource for quantum simulation.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A Bose-Einstein condensate of Rubidium-87 atoms in a dynamically modulated (shaken) optical lattice, modeled by a generalized 'shaken rotor' Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Momentum distribution, site populations, momentum width, average momentum, and Floquet operator fidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. Effective Planck constant (hbar_eff), kick period (T), lattice depth (s0), and modulation functions (f1, f2).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes a perturbative regime (weak driving) and relies on quantum resonance conditions to maintain discrete translational symmetry.

Figures summary. Figures show the real and imaginary parts of modulation functions, comparison between perturbative and optimal control solutions, and experimental observations of momentum-space Bloch oscillations, topological edge states, and wave packet expansion in superlattices.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from theoretical derivation of the Floquet framework and analytical relations to the implementation of optimal control, followed by experimental verification of various tight-binding models and topological phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful toolkit for simulating complex condensed-matter models like the Rice-Mele model and topological junctions using the highly controllable platform of momentum-space lattices in ultracold atoms.

Abstract

Quantum simulation with ultracold atoms provides a versatile platform to emulate condensed-matter models. In particular, momentum-space lattices enable the realization of programmable tight-binding Hamiltonians. Here, we generalize this approach by exploiting quantum resonances of a periodically driven (shaken) rotor within the Floquet framework. Using first-order time-dependent perturbation theory, we derive analytical relations between the lattice modulation and the effective tight-binding parameters, and identify explicit solutions for several resonances. We further apply optimal-control techniques to enhance the multi-period Floquet fidelity and extend the accessible parameter regimes. Experimentally, we implement this scheme with a Bose-Einstein condensate of rubidium-87 atoms in a dynamically modulated optical lattice. We demonstrate the simulation of the Rice-Mele model, including band-structure measurements and topological edge states, as well as momentum Bloch oscillations, and superlattice configurations with controlled periodicity. Our results establish quantum resonances as a powerful resource for Floquet engineering of tight-binding models in momentum space.

Control of the Néel vector in the quantum antiferromagnetic honeycomb lattice

Authors: Asliddin Khudoyberdiev, Dag-Björn Hering, Vanessa Sulaiman, Götz S. Uhrig
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24695v1

Main problem. Investigating the control and switching of the Neel vector in a quantum antiferromagnetic honeycomb lattice, specifically determining the threshold magnetic fields required for magnetization reorientation.

Main problem. Mitigating decoherence in donor spin systems for scalable silicon-based quantum information processing by identifying and probing magnetic clock transitions.

Abstract

Magnetic clock transitions (CTs), defined by vanishing first-order sensitivity of the transition frequency to magnetic field fluctuations, provide a powerful route to suppress decoherence in donor spin systems. Here, we present the observation of magnetic field CTs from an ensemble of near-surface $^{75}$As ($I = 3/2$) spins in silicon using low-field ($< 10$~mT) continuous-wave electrically detected magnetic resonance (EDMR). As the CT condition is approached, pronounced linewidth broadening is observed, consistent with a donor Hamiltonian informed linewidth model. These results establish low-field EDMR as a sensitive probe of CTs in near-surface donor systems relevant to silicon-based quantum devices.

Abstract

The results show quantum noise can be learned and fine-tuned with minimal samples, and provide a plausible approach to cross-device quantum error mitigation.

Main problem. The paper aims to distinguish the statistical nature of conventional Young interference from a proposed Time-Reversed Young (TRY) geometry, specifically addressing how randomness is handled in each.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that in TRY, the interference fringe is a deterministic response function indexed by a programmed source coordinate, shifting the role of randomness from pattern formation to the precision of response estimation.

Summary. The paper proposes a new way to view Young interference called Time-Reversed Young (TRY) interferometry. Unlike standard interference, which builds a pattern from random detection events, TRY uses a controlled source to measure a deterministic response. This shift allows for more precise parameter estimation by optimizing how measurement time is allocated across different source coordinates. The framework enables advanced sensing techniques like lock-in readout and superresolution by treating interference as a conditional response rather than a spatial histogram.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum information as it provides a new framework for high-precision metrology and sensing by engineering the source-plane basis to optimize Fisher information.

Abstract

Young interference is usually read as the gradual statistical accumulation of random detection events. Here we show that a time-reversed Young (TRY) geometry has a different statistical character: the fringe is not a marginal distribution of detector positions, but a conditional response indexed by a programmed source coordinate. With a fixed detector and a scanned source basis, the observable is an operational hybrid correlator between detector signal and source label. The resulting interference is deterministic at the response-function level, while noise enters only through estimation precision. We formulate this distinction using Fisher information, estimator variance, and noise scaling, clarifying why TRY naturally supports calibration, lock-in readout, null-fringe sensing, and source-plane superresolution.

Main problem. The lack of a quantitative framework to compare how different cluster state topologies (linear, square, and T-shaped) distribute entanglement and affect information propagation in measurement-based quantum computation.

Main result. The authors introduce the Correlation Concentration Ratio (CCR), a new metric that quantifies the concentration of correlations on graph edges, and demonstrate that square topologies are most scalable due to their balanced correlation distribution.

Method. Numerical simulations using the symplectic representation and covariance matrix formalism to model the generation of Gaussian cluster states via controlled-phase gates.

Summary. This paper introduces the Correlation Concentration Ratio (CCR) to quantitatively compare different quantum cluster state topologies. By simulating continuous-variable Gaussian cluster states, the authors show how graph geometry influences the distribution of quadrature correlations. They demonstrate that while squeezing increases correlation intensity, the CCR effectively captures topology-dependent features, such as the 'quantum hub' effect in T-shaped states. Detailed structure

Model / system. Four-mode continuous-variable (CV) Gaussian cluster states with linear, square, and T-shaped (or star-shaped) topologies, constructed from momentum-squeezed vacuum states.

Key observables. Covariance matrix elements (diagonal quadrature variances and off-diagonal inter-mode correlations), nullifier relations, and the Correlation Concentration Ratio (CCR).

Important parameters / regimes. Squeezing parameter (ranging from 3 to 16 dB) and the graph adjacency matrix (topology).

Assumptions / limitations. The study is limited to Gaussian states and focuses on the canonical quadrature basis without considering non-Gaussian elements.

Abstract

In this paper, numerical simulations of four-mode continuous-variable cluster states with different topologies in the framework of measurement-based quantum computation are presented. By utilizing the symplectic representation and covariance matrix, the process of generating cluster states with linear, square, and T-shaped topologies has been systematically modeled. The simulation results show that the cluster graph structure is directly reflected in the pattern of quadrature correlations; in other words, the theoretical nullifier relations of the cluster states are reproduced in the final covariance matrices. Increasing the squeezing parameter leads to the strengthening of the target correlations and the suppression of unwanted components arising from anti-squeezing; such that the off-diagonal elements of the covariance matrix in the linear and square topologies increase to significant values, and in the T-shaped topology a stronger central correlation (similar to GHZ-like behavior in the continuous-variable domain) is observed. In order to quantitatively analyze these structural differences, a metric titled CCR (Correlation Concentration Ratio) is introduced that quantifies the concentration of effective correlations on the graph edges relative to the total correlations of the system. This index enables direct comparison of different topologies from the perspective of structural entanglement distribution and provides a framework for evaluating the efficiency of cluster graphs in MBQC architectures. The results show that CCR can be used as a practical tool for designing and selecting optimal topologies in larger clusters and more complex structures.

Abstract

We characterize the stationary properties of the system, identify the conditions for the appearance of dark states, and show how they structure the spectrum and delineate interacting and noninteracting sectors.
