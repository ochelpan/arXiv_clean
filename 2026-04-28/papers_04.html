Decoherence Mitigation with Local NOT Gates in Multipartite Systems

Authors: Venkat Abhignan, Raghav Sundararaman, Shriram Pragash M, R. Srikanth, Ashutosh Singh
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23556v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating how to mitigate Entanglement Sudden Death (ESD) and preserve the operational utility of multipartite entangled states under amplitude-damping noise.

Main result. Applying local NOT gates can transform entanglement sudden death into asymptotic decay for GMC, though the optimal number of gates depends on the specific noise regime and the metric being preserved (GMC vs. teleportation fidelity).

Method. Analytical derivation of entanglement dynamics and teleportation fidelity using GHZ-symmetric parametrization, twirling operations, and the Horodecki criterion for Bell nonlocality.

Summary. This paper studies how local NOT gates can be used to prevent the abrupt disappearance of entanglement (Entanglement Sudden Death) in 2, 3, and 4-qubit systems under amplitude damping. The authors show that while a single NOT gate can often turn sudden death into a slower asymptotic decay for entanglement, the best strategy for preserving teleportation fidelity may differ. The study highlights a decoupling between the amount of entanglement preserved and the actual utility of the state for quantum communication tasks. The results offer practical guidance for using simple $\pi$-pulses to protect quantum information in noisy hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Multipartite GHZ-type and Bell-type states consisting of 2, 3, and 4 qubits subject to a two-stage Amplitude Damping Channel (ADC).

Key observables. Genuine Multipartite Concurrence (GMC), teleportation fidelity (F), Bell-CHSH parameter (S), and Localizable Entanglement (LE).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (n=2,3,4), number of NOT gates applied (m), damping parameters (p, p'), and initial state population imbalance (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. Local NOT operations are treated as instantaneous relative to the noise timescale; the ADC is modeled as two sequential, independent processes.

Figures summary. Schematics of the noise/control framework and teleportation protocols; plots of GMC and fidelity dynamics showing ESD vs. ADE; geometric projections of 4-qubit state trajectories in the GHZ-symmetric parameter space; and a summary map of optimal NOT-gate strategies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of ESD, defines the noise model and control mechanism, provides analytical derivations for entanglement and fidelity, explores the hierarchy of correlations, classifies states using GHZ-symmetric parameters, and concludes with an optimization strategy for gate application.

Why it may be interesting. It provides simple, experimentally accessible unitary control strategies (local bit-flips) to extend the lifetime of quantum resources in noisy environments, which is highly relevant for superconducting and trapped-ion platforms.

Abstract

We study the entanglement dynamics of $n=2,3,4$-qubit Bell- and GHZ-type states under an amplitude-damping channel (ADC). We quantify multipartite entanglement using the genuine multipartite concurrence (GMC) and evaluate its utility through the optimal teleportation fidelity. For $2$-qubit states, we analyze the standard (Bennett) teleportation protocol. For $3$- and $4$-qubit states, we study controlled quantum teleportation (CQT) with one and two \emph{controllers}, respectively. Entanglement sudden death (ESD) denotes the abrupt, finite-time disappearance of entanglement caused by decoherence in contrast to asymptotic decay. To counteract ESD, we apply local NOT ($\hatσ_x$) operations on $m$ of the $n$ qubits ($m \leq n$) and derive analytic formulae, revealing that a single-NOT operation often suffices to alter ESD into asymptotic decay when handling GMC. In contrast, teleportation fidelity can decay more rapidly for single-NOT flipped states, whereas flipping all qubits is more useful for preserving teleportation fidelity in certain regimes, highlighting that the amount of entanglement alone does not guarantee teleportation utility. Remarkably, in the case of GHZ-type states, ADC-evolved mixed biseparable states can be exploited successfully in the CQT protocol. Further, using the GHZ-symmetric parametrization, we map the 2- and 3-qubit ADC-evolved mixed states onto a $(x,y)$ plane, revealing their SLOCC (Stochastic Local Operations and Classical Communication) entanglement classes. We also explicitly check the Bell-CHSH nonlocality hierarchy in the 2-qubit teleportation alongside localizable-entanglement diagnostics for 3-qubit CQT. Our results clarify the distinct roles of global versus localizable bipartite correlations and suggest simple, experimentally accessible unitary controls for preserving useful quantum resources in noisy channels.

Single-copy stabilizer learning: average case and worst case

Authors: Gyungmin Cho, Dohun Kim
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24099v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 3/5 · measurement-induced transitions 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates the complexity of learning the stabilizer group (Weyl support) of an n-qubit quantum state using single-copy measurements, specifically comparing average-case and worst-case scenarios.

Main result. In the average case, logarithmic-depth local Clifford circuits are sufficient to efficiently learn almost all stabilizer groups with codimension t = O(log n), but in the worst case, any single-copy measurement scheme requires an exponential number of samples in t.

Method. The authors use a block-Clifford ensemble (shallow-depth circuits) and a computational difference sampling technique to extract Pauli symmetries, supported by symplectic geometry and probabilistic analysis.

Summary. This paper addresses the challenge of identifying the hidden Pauli symmetries of a quantum state. It proves that for most states, we can use very shallow, local circuits to learn these symmetries efficiently. However, it also reveals a fundamental bottleneck: for certain 'hard' states like the GHZ state, single-copy measurements are exponentially less efficient than multi-copy methods. This highlights a potential quantum advantage in using entangled measurements for symmetry detection.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on n-qubit quantum states, specifically t-doped stabilizer states and highly structured states like GHZ, Toric, and Color codes.

Key observables. The Weyl support (Pauli symmetries), Pauli collision coefficient, and the trace of the squared density matrix relative to the learned subspace.

Important parameters / regimes. n (number of qubits), t (codimension of the stabilizer group), k (circuit depth/block size), and epsilon/delta (error and failure parameters).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the ability to perform randomized measurements using block-Clifford ensembles and that the block size k divides n.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares three measurement protocols (Bell, Random Clifford, and Shallow Clifford); Figure 2 shows recovery success vs. number of samples and noise; Figure 3 illustrates the reduction of intersection problems to quotient spaces; Figure 4 and 5 show success probabilities under noise and performance across different code types.

Paper structure. The paper is structured into two parts: Part I establishes an upper bound for the average case using probabilistic arguments and subspace counting; Part II establishes a lower bound for the worst case using reduction to property testing and analyzing the GHZ state.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and topological order, as it provides fundamental limits on how efficiently one can identify hidden symmetries and topological features in quantum systems using local, shallow-depth measurements.

Abstract

We study single-copy stabilizer learning, the problem of identifying a stabilizer group of dimension $n-t$ from an $n$-qubit quantum state $ρ$. We obtain two complementary results. First, in the average case, logarithmic-depth local Clifford circuits suffice to efficiently learn almost all stabilizer groups with $t=O(\log n)$, instead of the linear-depth measurements required in previous approaches. We support this result with numerical simulations for systems of up to 100 qubits. Second, we show that, in the worst case, any adaptive single-copy measurement scheme requires a number of samples that scales exponentially in $t$. Together with existing results on two-copy learning, our findings suggest that, for large $t$, identifying Pauli symmetries of a quantum system exhibits a quantum advantage in the learning setting.

Beyond average: heterogeneous first-passage dynamics in many-particle systems with resetting

Authors: Juhee Lee, Seong-Gyu Yang, Ludvig Lizana
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24406v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating how collective stochastic resetting affects first-passage dynamics and heterogeneity in many-particle interacting systems.

Main result. The study finds that group resetting produces broad hitting-time distributions with heavy tails and extended plateaus, leading to a lack of a single characteristic time scale and high trajectory heterogeneity.

Method. The authors use Langevin equation simulations (Euler-Maruyama method) combined with an analytical theory based on asymptotic behaviors and extreme value theory.

Summary. This paper examines how a 'group resetting' mechanism, where all particles in a system reset to the position of the most extreme particle, affects first-passage times. The researchers show that this protocol creates highly heterogeneous dynamics with arrival time distributions that span several orders of magnitude. They demonstrate that as the resetting rate increases, the mean arrival time can actually diverge. Ultimately, the study highlights that in such many-particle systems, average arrival times are insufficient to characterize the system due to the lack of a single characteristic time scale.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A system of N overdamped Brownian particles in a one-dimensional harmonic potential with an absorbing boundary at the origin, subject to a group resetting protocol where all surviving particles relocate to the position of the rightmost particle.

Key observables. First Group Hitting Time (fGHT), Median Group Hitting Time (mGHT), and a Uniformity Index (w_ij) to quantify trajectory dissimilarity.

Important parameters / regimes. Resetting rate (r), group size (N), and the diffusion coefficient (D).

Assumptions / limitations. The analytical derivation assumes the underlying single-particle dynamics follow an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process (harmonic potential).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the resetting dynamics and boundary conditions; Figure 2 illustrates hitting-time distributions and the development of plateaus; Figure 3 shows position distributions of the group center of mass.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of collective resetting, defines the physical model and resetting protocol, presents numerical simulations and analytical approximations for hitting-time distributions, analyzes trajectory heterogeneity using a uniformity index, and concludes with the implications for defining characteristic time scales.

Why it may be interesting. This is relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it explores how non-equilibrium resetting protocols can fundamentally alter the collective transport and arrival statistics of a many-body ensemble.

Abstract

We study how stochastic resetting affects first-passage processes in systems of many interacting particles. While resetting is well understood for single-particle dynamics, its consequences for collective behavior remain less clear. We consider a protocol in which all surviving particles are reset to the position of the most extreme one, motivated by problems in artificial selection and avoidance. Using stochastic simulations of particles diffusing in a confining potential with an absorbing boundary, we examine two notions of arrival: when the first particle reaches the boundary and the point at which half of the particles do. We find that resetting produces broad distributions of arrival times with heavy tails and extended plateaus that span several orders of magnitude. As the resetting rate increases, the mean arrival time grows and diverges beyond a threshold. Trajectory-level analysis also reveals strong heterogeneity, with very short and very long absorption times. These results show that collective resetting lacks a single characteristic time scale and that the definition of arrival is crucial for understanding and controlling such systems.

Energetics of stochastic limit-cycle oscillators: when does coupling reduce dissipation?

Authors: Anton F. Burnet, Vansh Kharbanda, David Tobias, Benedikt Sabass
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24553v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study investigates how different types of mutual coupling (radial, phase, and Cartesian) between stochastic limit-cycle oscillators affect the steady-state entropy production rate (EPR) and energy dissipation.

Main result. The authors find that Cartesian coupling always reduces the EPR relative to the uncoupled system, while radial and phase couplings can either increase or decrease dissipation depending on the effective temperature and system size.

Method. The researchers use analytical derivations via the Fokker-Planck equation and numerical simulations of Langevin equations using the Euler-Maruyama method.

Summary. This paper explores the thermodynamics of coupled stochastic oscillators, specifically focusing on the energy cost of maintaining oscillations. By analyzing different coupling mechanisms, the authors demonstrate that while some couplings can increase dissipation, Cartesian coupling consistently reduces the entropy production rate. The study reveals a complex competition between the suppression of fluctuations and shifts in the radial distribution. These results provide fundamental insights into the energetic efficiency of biological and physical oscillators in noisy environments.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An idealized model of stochastic circular limit-cycle oscillators described by overdamped Langevin equations in polar and Cartesian coordinates, including Stuart-Landau dynamics.

Key observables. Steady-state entropy production rate (EPR/sigma), radial probability distribution, tangential velocity fluctuations, and radial variance.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength (k), inverse effective temperature (beta), intrinsic frequency (omega), system size (N), and frequency detuning (delta omega).

Assumptions / limitations. Noise is interpreted in the Stratonovich sense, and the system is assumed to be in a non-equilibrium steady state.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a single stochastic oscillator, its trajectory, and radial probability distributions. Figure 2 illustrates EPR vs. coupling strength for different temperatures. Figure 3 shows EPR for phase coupling with varying system sizes. Figure 11 displays gradients of the average radius and radial variance.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the single-oscillator model and its energetics, then progresses to analyze radial coupling, followed by phase/Kuramoto-like coupling, and finally examines full Cartesian coupling and network topology effects.

Why it may be interesting. The findings regarding how coupling-induced synchronization can reduce energy dissipation are highly relevant to understanding the energetic costs of maintaining coherence in open quantum systems and many-body driven-dissipative dynamics.

Abstract

Non-linear oscillators serve important functions in many biological systems, including within the inner ear and neuronal networks. The sustainment of oscillations in noisy environments requires continuous energy dissipation, quantified by the steady-state entropy production rate (EPR). We study an idealized, analytically tractable model of a stochastic circular limit cycle and examine how mutual coupling in pairs and populations alters dissipation. For a single oscillator, the EPR depends on three key factors: intrinsic frequency, tangential velocity fluctuations, and mean tangential velocity. The dynamics are characterized by a dimensionless effective temperature given by the ratio of intrinsic relaxation and diffusion timescales. For radial (amplitude), phase (Kuramoto-like), and Cartesian couplings, we derive analytical expressions for the EPR and confirm them numerically. Varying the effective temperature and system size strongly influences how the EPR depends on coupling strength and, in some cases, results in qualitatively distinct behaviors. Moreover, the coupling types affect the tangential velocity distributions differently. Notably, in all cases studied, Cartesian coupling reduces the EPR relative to the uncoupled system, irrespective of effective temperature and system size. The analysis of idealized non-linear oscillators reveals that different classes of coupling interactions and competing timescales present in the oscillators have distinct effects on energy dissipation.

Quantum algorithm for solving high-dimensional linear stochastic differential equations via amplitude encoding of the noise term

Authors: Koichi Miyamoto
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24133v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Developing quantum algorithms to solve high-dimensional linear stochastic differential equations (SDEs) with an exponential speed-up in the dimension N, specifically addressing the challenge of amplitude encoding the noise term.

Main result. The paper proposes two methods (Dyson series-based and Euler-Maruyama-based) that achieve polylog(N) query complexity, providing an exponential speed-up in the dimension N compared to classical methods.

Method. The algorithms utilize amplitude encoding of the noise term via a quantum pseudorandom number generator (PRNG), quantum linear systems solvers (QLSS), and block-encoding techniques like the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU).

Summary. This paper presents new quantum algorithms for solving high-dimensional linear stochastic differential equations. By using amplitude encoding for the noise term and a quantum PRNG, the authors achieve an exponential speed-up in the system dimension N. They propose two distinct approaches: one based on the Dyson series and another based on the Euler-Maruyama discretization. The work also provides methods for estimating expectations of functions of the solution at various time points.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The model consists of high-dimensional linear SDEs of the form dX_t = A(t)X_t dt + B(t)dW_t, where X_t is an N-dimensional random process driven by m-dimensional Brownian motion.

Key observables. Expectations of functions of X_t at multiple time steps (history states) and expectations of quantities defined at the terminal time.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension N, dimension of Brownian motion m, time interval [0, T], error tolerance epsilon, and stability/dissipativity parameter eta.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes access to oracles for arithmetic operations and data loading, a bounded norm for the drift matrix A(t), and a bounded noise coefficient B(t).

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic outline of the Dyson and EM-based methods; Figure 2 illustrates the quantum circuit for block-encoding a product of matrices; Figure 3 shows the circuit implementation of the operator.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SDE problem, presents two algorithmic approaches (Dyson-based and EM-based), details the construction of quantum oracles and block-encodings, provides rigorous error analysis and complexity bounds, and concludes with discussions on limitations and future extensions.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics, as SDEs are fundamental for modeling dissipative dynamics, and efficient quantum algorithms for high-dimensional stochastic processes are crucial for simulating complex quantum trajectories.

Abstract

This work studies quantum algorithms to solve high-dimensional stochastic differential equations (SDEs) $\mathrm{d} \mathbf{X}_t = A(t) \mathbf{X}_t \mathrm{d} t + B(t) \mathrm{d} \mathbf{W}_t$. Aiming for a speed-up in the dimension $N$ of $\mathbf{X}_t$, we generate quantum states that encode $\mathbf{X}_t$ in the amplitudes, while most of the existing quantum methods for SDEs employ binary encoding. A key challenge is the amplitude encoding of the noise term, and we address this by utilizing the quantum circuit implementation of a pseudorandom number generator (PRNG). We propose two methods: the Dyson series-based method and the Euler-Maruyama (EM)-based method. In the former, we express the noise term via the Dyson series approximation of the time evolution operator, while in the latter, it is approximated using the EM time discretization. Both methods use the quantum linear systems solver to generate the amplitude-encoding state of $\mathbf{X}_t$, making only ${\rm polylog}(N)$ queries to the PRNG circuit and the block-encodings of $A$ and $B$. Additionally, going beyond state preparation, we present methods to estimate expectations of functions of $\mathbf{X}_t$ using the state.

Quantum average correlation based on average coherence

Abstract

We investigate the competition between attractive spin-spin interactions and spin-separating external forces in the ground state of a one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard model. We consider a lattice with open boundary conditions, subject to a linear external potential whose gradient is opposite for the two spin components, so that each spin species sees a potential minimum at a different end of the lattice. Our results are directly testable in cold-atom experiments, and demonstrate that a spin-dependent linear potential enables precise, integer-level control of the number of bound fermion pairs.
