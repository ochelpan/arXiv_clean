Noise-robust 1-copy distillation protocol for all distillable Bell-diagonal qutrits

Authors: Tobias C. Sutter, Christopher Popp, Beatrix C. Hiesmayr
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24460v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Determining whether all NPT Bell-diagonal qutrit states are 1-distillable and constructing a noise-robust 1-copy distillation protocol for them.

Main result. The authors prove that for Bell-diagonal qutrit states with Weyl structure, violating the PPT criterion is both necessary and sufficient for 1-distillability, and they provide a noise-resilient distillation scheme.

Method. The study uses a constructive proof involving the partial transpose and the construction of a Schmidt rank 2 witness vector based on the eigenvectors of the partially transposed density matrix.

Summary. This paper solves the distillability problem for a specific class of Bell-diagonal qutrit states, proving that any state violating the PPT criterion can be distilled using a single copy. The authors develop a new distillation protocol that uses local filters to transform qutrit states into qubit states. This protocol is specifically designed to be resilient to white noise by utilizing the properties of the partially transposed density matrix's eigenvectors. These findings make noisy, high-dimensional entangled states more useful for practical quantum technologies.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Bipartite qutrit-qutrit systems (d=3) with Weyl structure, specifically Bell-diagonal states subject to white-noise-affected models.

Key observables. The expectation value of the partial transpose and the smallest eigenvalue of the partially transposed matrix.

Important parameters / regimes. The noise parameter p, the Schmidt rank of the witness vector, and the success probability q of the filtering process.

Assumptions / limitations. The results are specifically applicable to Bell-diagonal qutrit states with Weyl structure and do not extend to general dimensions d > 3.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the distillability problem, defines the Weyl-structure qutrit model, provides a constructive proof for the sufficiency of PPT violation, details the construction of the noise-robust witness and protocol, and discusses limitations regarding higher dimensions.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a fundamental solution to a specific case of the distillability problem and offers a practical method for enhancing the robustness of entanglement against white noise, which is critical for quantum communication and networking.

Abstract

Entanglement distillation is the process of converting noisy entangled states into maximally entangled pure states via local operations and classical communication. A long-standing, unresolved question is which entangled states are amenable to distillation, known as the distillability problem. We solve this for Bell-diagonal qutrits with Weyl structure, and present a noise-robust scheme for entanglement distillation. In particular, we find that violating the positive partial transposition (PPT) criterion is necessary and sufficient for the 1-distillability of these states. For this, we construct a Schmidt rank 2 eigenvector of the partially transposed density matrix associated with its unique, three-fold degenerate negative eigenvalue. This feature makes the derived entanglement distillation protocol resilient to white-noise effects on the quantum states. Our results thus make noisy entangled qutrit pairs more accessible for future quantum technologies.

Quantum Sufficiency for Self-Adjoint Statistical Models via Likelihood-Type Operators on Real $*$-Subalgebras and Real Jordan Algebras

Authors: Koichi Yamagata
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23292v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The existing theory of quantum sufficiency is limited to complex *-subalgebras and requires faithful reference states, creating a gap when dealing with local quantum statistical structures like SLDs.

Main result. The paper develops a unified theory of quantum sufficiency using real *-subalgebras and real Jordan algebras, allowing for degenerate reference states and the inclusion of self-adjoint operators like SLDs.

Method. The author employs algebraic decomposition of real Jordan algebras, operator theory involving rho-modular invariance, and the study of real-linear positive maps and conditional expectations.

Summary. This paper generalizes the theory of quantum sufficiency from the standard complex -algebraic setting to the more flexible framework of real -subalgebras and real Jordan algebras. By doing so, it allows for the treatment of statistical models that include non-state self-adjoint operators, such as symmetric logarithmic derivatives, and accommodates reference states that are not strictly positive. The author establishes new characterizations for minimal sufficient algebras and provides a generalized Koashi-Imoto decomposition. Ultimately, the work suggests that real Jordan structures are the natural language for the statistical aspects of quantum theory.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The framework considers general self-adjoint statistical models on finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, including both absolutely continuous models and local models (e.g., derivatives of states).

Key observables. Square-root likelihood ratios and symmetric logarithmic derivatives (SLDs).

Important parameters / regimes. Finite-dimensional Hilbert space; the distinction between strictly positive and degenerate reference states.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework is presented for finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces and assumes the existence of a supporting operator.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a new framework for sufficiency, establishes a hierarchy of sufficiency (complex, real, and Jordan), characterizes minimal sufficient algebras via likelihood-type objects and modular invariance, provides Koashi-Imlamo type decompositions, and derives bounds for POVM support size.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for quantum estimation and quantum Fisher information, as it provides a more robust mathematical foundation for handling the local statistical structures (SLDs) used in quantum metrology and asymptotic quantum statistics.

Abstract

We develop a theory of quantum sufficiency on real -subalgebras and real Jordan algebras. In contrast to the conventional formulation, which is based on families of states, complex completely positive coarse-grainings, and Radon-Nikodym cocycles associated with faithful reference states, our framework allows models consisting of general self-adjoint operators, including derivatives of states. Within this framework, square-root likelihood ratios and symmetric logarithmic derivatives arise naturally as fundamental self-adjoint likelihood-type objects. This makes it possible to treat ordinary quantum statistical models and local quantum statistical structures within a unified setting. We introduce sufficient real positive maps and show that sufficient complex -subalgebras, sufficient real -subalgebras, and sufficient real Jordan algebras correspond respectively to complex completely positive maps, real completely positive maps, and real positive maps. We characterize minimal sufficient real -subalgebras by the likelihood-ratio set together with rho-modular invariance, and show that the real Jordan algebra generated by the likelihood-ratio set and the projected reference state is the minimal sufficient real Jordan algebra. We also obtain Koashi-Imoto type decompositions for sufficient real -subalgebras and sufficient real Jordan algebras. Our formulation admits degenerate reference states and separates the likelihood-ratio aspect of sufficiency from its genuinely quantum modular aspect. These results suggest that real Jordan structure provides a natural framework for the statistical aspect of quantum theory beyond the conventional complex -algebraic setting.

Principles of relativistic quantum statistical thermodynamics: a class of exactly solvable models

Authors: A. Yu. Zakharov
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23409v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper addresses the lack of a microscopic mechanism for irreversibility in classical mechanics and the difficulty of finding exactly solvable 3D models for the relativistic quantum statistical thermodynamics of interacting atoms.

Main result. The author establishes a relativistic framework where interatomic interactions are represented by an auxiliary field, demonstrating that quantization resolves the classical ultraviolet catastrophe and that a phase transition occurs at a critical temperature.

Method. The study uses a relativistic field theory approach, employing Fourier transformations, Legendre transformations, and the quadratic approximation of the Hamiltonian to reduce the complex partition function to a solvable form.

Summary. This paper develops a theory for the relativistic quantum statistical thermodynamics of interacting atoms. By representing interatomic potentials as an auxiliary scalar field, the author transforms a complex many-body problem into an exactly solvable model of an ideal gas of quasiparticles. The work demonstrates that quantizing this auxiliary field prevents the classical ultraviolet catastrophe and proves the existence of a phase transition at a specific critical temperature.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A relativistic system consisting of two subsystems: a collection of interacting atoms and an auxiliary scalar covariant field (a superposition of Klein-Gordon fields) that mediates the interatomic potential.

Key observables. Total energy, average energy of the field modes, heat capacity, and the dispersion law of the effective field.

Important parameters / regimes. Critical temperature (T_crit), particle density (n), field mass parameters (mu_s), coupling constants (gamma_s, kappa_s), and the high-temperature/low-temperature regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes atoms do not undergo destruction (valid for v << c), the interatomic potential is representable via simple poles in its Fourier transform, and the field is treated as a superposition of Klein-Gordon fields.

Figures summary. The figures show the domain of definition and behavior of a function f(T/T_s) for different coupling parameters and its behavior in the low-temperature region.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the foundational problem of irreversibility, introduces the relativistic atom-field model and its Hamiltonian, applies mathematical transformations to solve the partition function, analyzes the thermodynamic properties (energy, heat capacity), and concludes with the identification of a phase transition.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rare example of an exactly solvable 3D relativistic many-body model and offers a physical mechanism for irreversibility through field-induced 'lag' in interactions, which is relevant to the study of open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Abstract

A system of interacting atoms is represented as an union of two subsystems, one of which is the system of atoms, and the other is an auxiliary scalar covariant field, which is equivalent to a given static interatomic potential of general form only in the non-relativistic approximation. It is shown that the auxiliary field is a superposition of Klein-Gordon fields, the parameters of which are related to singular points of the Fourier transform of the corresponding interatomic potential. The general form of the relativistic Hamiltonian system of interacting atoms is established. It is shown that the exact calculation of the relativistic partition function of a system of interacting atoms, taking into account the field degrees of freedom, reduces to renormalizing the parameters of the auxiliary field. It is established that the field degrees of freedom lead to a divergence in the total energy of a classical relativistic system - an analogue of the ultraviolet catastrophe. Quantization of the auxiliary field eliminates this divergence. The existence of a phase transition within the framework of relativistic quantum statistical thermodynamics has been proven.

Catalytic Enhancement of Coherence Fraction in Noisy Quantum Channels and Characterization of Strictly Incoherent Operations

Authors: Priyabrata Char, Dipayan Chakraborty, Indrani Chattopadhyay, Debasis Sarkar
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24291v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating whether quantum catalysis can be used to pre-process input states to enhance the coherence fraction of an output state after passing through a noisy quantum channel, and characterizing the structural properties of Strictly Incoherent Operations (SIO).

Main result. The authors prove that correlated catalysis can prevent the degradation of the coherence fraction in noisy channels and establish a necessary and sufficient condition for a CPTP map to be a Schur-multiplier type SIO.

Method. The study uses the framework of Quantum Resource Theory (QRT) of coherence, employing analytical proofs, algebraic derivations (such as polynomial interpolation), and numerical simulations using the Ginibre ensemble.

Summary. This paper explores how quantum catalysis can be used to protect or even enhance the coherence fraction of a quantum state when it passes through a noisy channel. By pre-processing the input state using an auxiliary catalyst, the authors show that the output coherence can be higher than if the original state were used. Additionally, the work provides a new structural characterization of a specific class of incoherent operations. These findings offer practical insights for optimizing quantum protocols in realistic, noisy environments.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The work focuses on single-party d-dimensional quantum systems subject to noisy CPTP channels, specifically including the quantum addition channel and the qutrit phase-damping channel.

Key observables. Coherence fraction (CF), l1 norm of coherence, robustness of coherence, and success probability in phase discrimination tasks.

Important parameters / regimes. Noise parameter (p) for dephasing channels, the parameter (alpha) for the quantum addition channel, and the regularized coherence fraction.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the use of the coherence fraction as the primary figure of merit and focuses on the regime of noisy channels that reduce coherence.

Figures summary. Figures 1-3 compare the coherence fraction of standard versus processed states under different noise levels; Figure 4 is a scatter plot showing the Frobenius norm deviation to demonstrate that the multiplicative property fails for SIOs that are not Schur multipliers.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of coherence degradation, defines the catalytic scheme and operational classes (MIO, IO, SIO), provides analytical theorems for coherence enhancement, applies the results to phase discrimination, characterizes the SIO class via a multiplicative condition, and concludes with numerical validation.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum optics as it provides a theoretical method (catalysis) to mitigate the effects of environmental noise on a key quantum resource (coherence).

Abstract

In realistic quantum information processing tasks, quantum states are inevitably affected by environmental noise, leading to decoherence and degradation of useful quantum resources. The coherence fraction, which serves as an important figure of merit for several quantum protocols, may decrease significantly after the action of a noisy channel. Such degradation can result in unsatisfactory performance in real-world applications. In this work, we investigate whether catalysis can be used to pre-process the input state to enhance the coherence fraction of an output state from a quantum channel. Specifically, we study whether using a processed state $ρ_s'$ as the input to a quantum channel $Λ$, instead of the original state $ρ_s$, can yield an output state $Λ(ρ_s')$ whose coherence fraction exceeds that of $Λ(ρ_s)$. We analyze the conditions under which such an improvement is possible. We also provide a practical application of our setup for the phase discrimination task. Furthermore, we establish a necessary and sufficient condition for an incoherent state preserving CPTP(Completely Positive Trace Preserving) map $\mathcal{E}$ to be a particular type of Strictly Incoherent Operation (SIO). This characterization provides a new structural understanding of SIO and clarifies its role in coherence manipulation. Our results offer practical insights into coherence preservation and enhancement in noisy quantum processes and may be useful for optimizing quantum information protocols under realistic conditions. We also provide numerical examples to support our claims.

Dissipative Vortex Binaries in Compact Fluid Domains with Geometric Corrections

Authors: Aswathy K. R., Rickmoy Samanta
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23857v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · analog quantum simulation 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study investigates how the combination of dissipation (mutual friction) and toroidal geometry (periodic boundary conditions) modifies the classical planar dynamics of vortex binaries.

Main result. The researchers derive torus-corrected analytic solutions that capture geometric effects like angular drift in dipoles and a specific nonlinear chirp (frequency blow-up) in unequal-sign pairs, significantly reducing errors compared to planar theory.

Method. The authors use a perturbative expansion of the interaction kernel via the Schottky-Klein prime function and q-special functions, comparing analytical results against full numerical simulations on a flat torus.

Summary. This paper explores how the periodic geometry of a torus and the presence of dissipation change the way vortex pairs move and interact. While planar models predict simple behaviors like fixed-orientation dipoles, the authors show that a torus induces an angular drift and a unique nonlinear frequency chirp in unequal-sign pairs. By using advanced mathematical tools like the Schottky-Klein prime function, they provide new analytical solutions that are much more accurate than standard planar approximations. These findings are particularly important for understanding vortex dynamics in finite-sized quantum fluids like Bose-Einstein condensates.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The system consists of two-dimensional incompressible, dissipative point-vortices in a doubly periodic fluid domain (flat torus), modeled by a mixed symplectic-gradient flow inspired by mutual friction in superfluids and BECs.

Key observables. Radial separation (r or R), angular orientation (theta), orbital rotation frequency (omega), and energy decay rate.

Important parameters / regimes. Circulation strengths (Gamma), dissipation coefficient (gamma), torus modulus/aspect ratio (rho), and the sum/difference of circulations (Sigma and Delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The fluid is incompressible, vortices are treated as point-like, and the analytical solutions are derived in the 'local regime' where vortex separation is small relative to the torus periods.

Figures summary. Figure 3 shows numerical verification for dipole dynamics, including plots of separation, orientation, and error reduction; Figure 4 shows numerical verification for same-sign pairs, demonstrating improved radial and angular error tracking.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the dissipative vortex model in a periodic domain, develops the mathematical framework using q-special functions, derives analytical solutions for different vortex configurations in the local regime, and concludes with numerical validation of the geometric corrections.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers studying the dynamics of quantized vortices in BECs and superfluids, as it provides precise corrections to vortex motion when the system size is comparable to the vortex separation.

Abstract

We study a dissipative extension of vortex-binary motion in a doubly periodic fluid domain. The underlying conservative system admits an exact integrable reduction to a single complex relative coordinate. Dissipation is introduced via a minimal rotated-velocity (mutual-friction) term, as motivated by finite-temperature superfluid dynamics, converting the Hamiltonian evolution into a mixed symplectic--gradient flow with monotonic energy decay for quantized vortices. In the local regime, the dissipative binary remains analytically solvable and admits closed-form solutions, with systematic corrections arising from the toroidal geometry. Equal same-sign vortices execute outward spiraling motion, while equal opposite-sign pairs (dipoles) undergo finite-time collapse in the planar limit. On the torus, however, the dipole orientation is no longer invariant: the geometry induces a slow angular drift, even in regimes where planar dynamics would preserve alignment. For unequal opposite-sign pairs, dissipation induces coupled contraction and rotation, leading to a finite-time nonlinear chirp characterized by $\dotω\proptoω^2$, in contrast with electromagnetic and gravitational inspirals where $\dotω\propto ω^{3}$ and $\dotω\propto ω^{11/3}$. These results highlight the interplay between Hamiltonian structure, dissipation, and geometry in periodic fluid systems.

Complementarity between bosonic and fermionic many-body interferences with partially distinguishable particles

Authors: Marco Robbio, Michael G. Jabbour, Nicolas J. Cerf
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23316v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · interference shaping light 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates the fundamental relationship and complementarity between bosonic and fermionic many-body interference when particles are partially distinguishable.

Main result. The authors establish an exact algebraic identity relating bosonic and fermionic transition probabilities and prove a sum rule where the sum of their covariance matrices equals twice that of classical particles.

Method. The study employs multilinear algebra, generating functions, and the Khatri-Rao product, utilizing MacMahon's master theorem and Weingarten calculus for Haar random averages.

Summary. This paper establishes a formal mathematical complementarity between bosonic and fermionic interference in linear interferometers. It proves that even when particles are partially distinguishable, a fundamental sum rule holds between their correlation matrices and classical particles. The work reveals a metrological trade-off: increasing particle indistinguishability enhances bosonic phase sensitivity but can degrade fermionic sensitivity. Whereas the ground states of such quantum droplets at finite temperature have been studied for three- and one-dimensional configurations, the possible fragmentation of these droplets has so far not been considered in these studies. In this paper, we show that droplets can lower their free energy by splitting or fragmenting in a combination of multiple smaller droplets and/or a gas.
