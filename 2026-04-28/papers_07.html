A Spectral Gap Informed Parameter Schedule for QAOA

Authors: Kieran McDowall, Konstantinos Georgopoulos, Petros Wallden
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24580v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Finding optimal variational parameters for the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) is computationally difficult, and existing 'de-variationalized' schedules like the Linear Ramp (LR-QAOA) fail to account for the closing of the spectral gap.

Main result. The proposed Spectral Gap Informed Ramp (SGIR-QAOA) outperforms LR-QAOA on Grover's problem and the Maximum Independent Set (MIS) problem, requiring shallower depths to reach target success probabilities and demonstrating better exponential scaling.

Method. The authors develop a parameter schedule that uses smooth ramps informed by the spectral gap of an adiabatic Hamiltonian, performing slower evolution where the gap is small, and use extrapolation techniques to scale the method to larger problem sizes.

Summary. This paper introduces a new parameter scheduling strategy for the QAOA algorithm called SGIR-QAOA. By using information about the spectral gap of an adiabatic Hamiltonian, the algorithm slows down its evolution during periods where the gap is small, effectively mitigating diabatic transitions. The authors demonstrate that this approach provides superior performance and better scaling for both Grover's search and the Maximum Independent Set problem compared to standard linear ramp methods. Furthermore, the method proves to be scalable through gap extrapolation and remains effective even in the presence of depolarizing noise.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study utilizes the QAOA framework with alternating mixer and cost Hamiltonians, specifically testing on Grover's search problem, Erdős–Rényi random graphs, and 3-regular sparse graphs for the Maximum Independent Set problem.

Key observables. Optimal solution probability (Ps), required QAOA depth (p), and the minimum spectral gap (gmin).

Important parameters / regimes. QAOA depth (p), problem size (n), spectral gap (g), and depolarizing noise strength.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that the spectral gap information from smaller system sizes can be accurately extrapolated to larger system sizes where exact calculation is impossible.

Figures summary. Figures show the eigenvalue spectrum of Grover's problem, comparisons of success probability (Ps) across different schedules, plots of required depth (p) vs. problem size (n), and the correlation between performance improvement and the minimum spectral gap.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of parameter optimization in QAOA, presents the SGIR-QAOA method, validates it using Grover's problem, extends the analysis to combinatorial optimization (MIS) on various graph types, demonstrates scalability via extrapolation, and tests robustness against depolarizing noise.

Abstract

A challenge with the Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm (QAOA), and variational algorithms in general, is finding good variational parameters, a task which in itself can be NP-hard. Recent work has sought to de-variationalise QAOA by picking well-informed guesses for the variational parameters. The Linear Ramp QAOA (LR-QAOA) achieves this by using parameter schedules inspired by the quantum adiabatic algorithm. We go a step further and use spectral gap information from an adiabatic Hamiltonian, with the QAOA mixer Hamiltonian as our initial Hamiltonian, to make smooth ramps which we call Spectral Gap Informed Ramps (SGIR-QAOA). SGIR-QAOA schedules perform slow evolution where the spectral gap of the adiabatic Hamiltonian is small. We show that SGIR-QAOA has performance improvements over LR-QAOA on Grover's problem at constant depth and that SGIR-QAOA requires shorter depths to achieve the same optimal solution probability. We then show that these performance benefits extend to a problem with potential practical applications -- the Maximum Independent Set (MIS) problem. Finally, we demonstrate the scalability of the SGIR-QAOA method using extrapolated spectral gap information for scales that the spectral gap cannot be exactly evaluated, and show that the advantage appears to persist under mild depolarising noise.

Main result. The algorithm achieves a computational complexity of O(n) relative to bit-width, providing a significant speedup over classical O(M log M) median filters for large arrays, though it performs slightly lower in filtering quality.

Method. The authors develop a quantum circuit model using controlled rotations and a recursive unitary matrix construction to redistribute probability amplitudes, then compare its performance against classical median filters on 1D signals and 2D images.

Summary. This paper proposes a quantum algorithm designed to replace classical median filters for tasks like signal and image denoising. The study includes testing on 1D signals and 2D images across various resolutions and formats.

Architecture-aware Unitary Synthesis

Authors: Frans Perkkola, Arianne Meijer-van de Griend, Jukka K. Nurminen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23777v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The inefficient transpilation of generic n-qubit unitary transformations into hardware-specific gate sequences, which leads to high CNOT gate counts and long compilation runtimes.

Main result. The proposed architecture-aware method achieves up to a 36% reduction in CNOT counts and up to a 553x speedup in transpilation time compared to leading compilers like TKet, Qiskit, and PennyLane.

Method. An integrated approach that embeds transpilation into the recursive block-ZXZ decomposition using greedy qubit mapping, adaptive Gray code selection, and a CNOT reduction heuristic for long-range ladders.

Summary. This paper presents a new way to compile quantum circuits by making the synthesis process aware of the underlying hardware architecture from the start. Instead of treating hardware constraints as a final step, the method integrates qubit mapping and gate optimization directly into the mathematical decomposition of the unitary. This results in significantly fewer CNOT gates and much faster compilation speeds. It is particularly effective for scaling up the synthesis of larger unitaries on real-world superconducting hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The method is benchmarked on superconducting quantum architectures, specifically the 20-qubit IQM Garnet (square lattice) and the 156-qubit IBM Marrakesh (heavy-hex) topologies.

Key observables. CNOT gate count, transpilation runtime, and CNOT gate ratio relative to the theoretical lower bound.

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit counts ranging from 3 to 11; time limit of 30 minutes for scalability testing.

Assumptions / limitations. The method focuses on exact unitary synthesis rather than numerical approximations and assumes hardware connectivity is restricted to adjacent physical qubits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows CNOT merging in a 3-qubit unitary; Figure 2 visualizes a long-range CNOT ladder; Figure 3 compares standard vs. optimized Gray codes; Figure 4 illustrates qubit swapping; Figure 5 shows the logical-to-physical decomposition of a UC gate.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of post-processing inefficiency, details the architecture-aware synthesis algorithm (including mapping, Gray code, and swapping techniques), presents benchmarking results against industry-standard transpilers, and discusses scalability and limitations.

Abstract

We present a novel architecture-aware transpilation method for exact general unitary gate synthesis on superconducting quantum hardware. Our approach is tightly integrated with the optimized block-ZXZ decomposition, exploiting its recursive structure to make hardware-aware decisions at each level of the recursion rather than treating transpilation as an independent post-processing step. The method introduces three key techniques: a greedy qubit mapping strategy that minimizes pairwise distances between physical qubits, an adaptive Gray code selection combined with qubit swapping that optimizes the construction of uniformly controlled Rz gates for the target topology, and a heuristic for reducing CNOT gates by exploiting the structure of long-range CNOT ladders. We benchmark our method against TKet, Qiskit, and Pennylane on the 20-qubit IQM Garnet (square lattice) and the 156-qubit IBM Marrakesh (heavy-hex) architectures with qubit counts ranging from 3 to 11. Our method achieves CNOT count reductions of up to 36 percent on the IQM Garnet and up to 34 percent on the IBM Marrakesh compared to the best competing transpiler, while simultaneously achieving transpilation speedups of up to 553x. Furthermore, our method is the only one capable of transpiling circuits beyond 10 qubits within a 30-minute time limit across both architectures.

CAbLECAR: efficiently scheduling QLDPC codes on a tileable spin qubit chip with shuttling

Authors: Jason D. Chadwick, Frederic T. Chong
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24739v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The difficulty of implementing high-rate QLDPC codes on semiconductor spin qubit architectures due to the mismatch between non-local code connectivity and the physical constraints of qubit shuttling, specifically regarding collision avoidance and shuttling-induced dephasing.

Main result. The development of the CAbLECAR framework and Q-SIPP algorithm enables efficient, collision-free ancilla routing, demonstrating that specific QLDPC codes can outperform surface codes by orders of magnitude in logical error rate when shuttling errors are sufficiently low.

Method. The authors use a noise-aware circuit tailoring technique to mitigate dephasing and a quantum-adapted Safe Interval Path Planning (Q-SIPP) algorithm for automated, time-optimized ancilla scheduling, validated via Stim circuit-level simulations.

Summary. This paper addresses the challenge of implementing high-rate QLDPC codes on spin qubit platforms that rely on physical qubit shuttling. By introducing a new scheduling algorithm (Q-SIPP) and a noise-aware circuit tailoring technique, the authors show how to mitigate dephasing and avoid collisions during ancilla movement. Their results demonstrate that under appropriate noise regimes, these advanced codes can provide massive improvements in both encoding efficiency and logical error rates compared to the standard surface code.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A tileable, modular semiconductor spin qubit architecture featuring a 2D grid of stationary data qubits and mobile ancilla qubits that are physically transported (shuttled) through interaction and readout zones.

Key observables. Logical error rate (pL), encoding efficiency (kd^2/n), shuttling overhead (ancilla movement counts), and physical error rates (p_sh, p_CX, p_H).

Important parameters / regimes. Shuttling error rates (ranging from 10^-1 to 10^-4), code parameters [[n, k, d]], and gate fidelities (approx 99.9%).

Assumptions / limitations. The evaluation assumes a simplified grid layout for data qubits and relies on the assumption that extremely low shuttling error rates (10^-4) are physically achievable.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the CAbLECAR framework overview; Figure 2 illustrates QEC syndrome extraction; Figure 3 depicts the tiled unit cell architecture; Figure 4 compares baseline vs. tailored circuits; Figure 5 shows logical error rate vs. shuttle error; Figure 6 displays an optimized movement schedule; Figure 7 visualizes collision resolution; Figure 8 compares shuttling overhead; Figure 9 shows encoding efficiency vs. overhead; Figure 12 compares various code families across error regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalability bottleneck of surface codes, presents the CAbLECAR framework and Q-SIPP algorithm, details the noise-aware circuit tailoring technique, evaluates various QLDPC code families through circuit-level simulations, and concludes by identifying the performance crossover regime relative to the surface code.

Abstract

Semiconductor spin qubits are a promising platform for large-scale quantum computing, but have yet to take full advantage of the broad class of quantum low-density parity check (QLDPC) codes, which promise high encoding rates and efficient logic but require nonlocal connectivity between physical qubits. In this work, we investigate the implementation of QLDPC codes on a tileable, shuttling-based spin qubit architecture. By tailoring syndrome extraction circuits to the shuttling noise model, we significantly improve on previous surface code proposals and extend the feasible shuttling range of the architecture by 5-10x, enabling the implementation of more complex codes with long-range interactions. Taking inspiration from the field of robotics, we develop a coordinated shuttle scheduling algorithm that supports arbitrary codes and use it to benchmark the logical performance of a variety of promising code families. We find that the optimized schedules are up to 86% faster than hand-optimized schedules for certain code families. Through detailed circuit-level simulations, we identify specific QLDPC codes that improve upon prior surface code implementations by orders of magnitude, increasing encoding efficiency and reducing logical error rates. This work demonstrates the potential of shuttling-based spin qubit hardware platforms for scalable and efficient fault-tolerant quantum computation.

Conformal Invariance of the large-$N$ limit of the $O(N)$ universality class

Authors: Santiago Cabrera, Gonzalo De Polsi, Adam Rançon, Nicolás Wschebor
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.24675v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates whether scale invariance automatically enhances to full conformal invariance at renormalization group fixed points in dimensions d > 2, specifically for the O(N) universality class.

Main result. The authors provide two rigorous proofs—one functional and one vertex-by-vertex—demonstrating that the large-N limit of the O(N) universality class is conformally invariant at the Wilson-Fisher fixed point.

Method. The study utilizes the Non-Perturbative Renormalization Group (NPRG) framework, employing Legendre transforms and analyzing modified Ward identities induced by an infrared regulator.

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous mathematical proof that the large-N limit of the O(N) universality class possesses full conformal symmetry. Using the non-perturbative renormalization group framework, the authors show that the scale-invariant Wilson-Fisher fixed point satisfies the necessary Ward identities for special conformal transformations. The work clarifies how the theory's structure reorganizes to realize this symmetry and provides general insights into the conditions for symmetry enhancement at criticality.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The model is the O(N)-symmetric scalar phi^4 theory, which includes universality classes such as the Ising, XY, and Heisenberg models, analyzed in the large-N limit.

Key observables. Correlation functions, critical exponents (such as nu), scaling dimensions (D_phi, D_sigma), and the energy-momentum tensor.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension d (2 < d < 4), the large-N limit, the renormalization group scale k, and the vanishing anomalous dimension (eta = 0).

Assumptions / limitations. The proofs are specifically restricted to the large-N limit where the anomalous dimension eta is zero, and they assume the system is at the Wilson-Fisher fixed point.

Figures summary. The figures consist of diagrammatic representations of various vertex functions (H2, H3, gamma4) and their constituent loop channels and momentum-dependent contributions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of conformal symmetry in d > 2, establishes the NPRG framework and modified Ward identities, presents a functional proof of conformal invariance, provides a vertex-by-vertex proof in Fourier space, and concludes with detailed mathematical appendices verifying the algebraic identities.

Abstract

Conformal symmetry is expected to be realized in many equilibrium statistical mechanical systems at criticality. Although this is certainly true in two-dimensional systems, the three-dimensional case is subtler, and only a few proofs exist, only so in very specific cases. In this work, we give two proofs for the large $N$ limit of the $O(N)$ universality class within the non-perturbative renormalization group framework: one functional, and one vertex-by-vertex in Fourier space. While doing so, we unveil how the theory is structured in order for conformal symmetry to be realized. As a consequence, we shed light on what to expect, on rather general grounds, for a theory to be conformally invariant.

Do Quantum Transformers Help? A Systematic VQC Architecture Comparison on Tabular Benchmarks

Authors: Chi-Sheng Chen, En-Jui Kuo
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.23931v1

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study addresses the lack of systematic architectural comparisons for Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) on classical tabular data, specifically investigating the trade-off between accuracy and parameter efficiency in quantum transformer architectures.

Main result. FC-VQCs achieve 90-96% of the R2 of attention-based VQCs with 40-50% fewer parameters and outperform equal-capacity MLPs, while Fully Quantum Transformers (FQT) show superior robustness to depolarizing noise compared to hybrid Quantum Transformers (QT).

Method. The authors perform a systematic empirical comparison of four VQC families across five regression and classification benchmarks using PennyLane simulations, evaluating metrics like R2, accuracy, expressibility (KL divergence), and noise robustness.

Summary. This paper provides a systematic comparison of different variational quantum circuit architectures for processing tabular data. It finds that simpler fully-connected VQCs are highly parameter-efficient and often outperform more complex quantum transformer models on small datasets. Notably, it demonstrates that fully quantum architectures are more robust to depolarizing noise than hybrid architectures. These findings offer practical guidance for designing efficient quantum machine learning models for near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study uses various VQC architectures (FC-VQC, ResNet-VQC, QT, and FQT) based on a parameterized ansatz of single-qubit rotations and CNOT entangling layers, applied to classical tabular datasets.

Key observables. R2 (coefficient of determination), classification accuracy, macro-averaged F1 score, KL-divergence (for expressibility), and parameter efficiency.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (d), number of attention heads (H), number of tokens (T), and depolarizing noise rate (pd).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the relevance of NISQ-era constraints and relies on classical CPU simulations of quantum circuits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides an architectural overview; Figure 2 shows circuit diagrams; Figure 3 compares accuracy vs. parameter count; Figure 4 shows training/validation loss curves; Figure 5 shows fidelity distributions; Figure 6 shows convergence on larger datasets; Figure 7 shows prediction vs. ground truth.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the architectural problem, defines the four VQC families, details the experimental setup and benchmarks, presents comparative results on accuracy and efficiency, analyzes expressibility and noise robustness, and concludes with practical guidance for NISQ deployment.

Abstract
