Anomalous tunneling as a low-energy theorem for Nambu-Goldstone modes

Authors: Keisuke Fujii, Daichi Kagamihara, Masaru Hongo
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27489v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. This paper establishes that the phenomenon of anomalous tunneling in broken-symmetry systems is a universal consequence of symmetry and scaling. It demonstrates that while symmetry-preserving defects become invisible at low energies, symmetry-breaking defects effectively block transmission. This result provides a unified theoretical rule for the behavior of Nambu-Goldstone modes like phonons and magnons.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a universal, symmetry-based principle for transport phenomena in many-body systems, which is highly relevant for understanding how excitations move through defects in BECs and other quantum fluids.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to establish the general conditions under which 'anomalous tunneling'—where the transmission coefficient approaches unity as energy tends to zero—occurs in systems with spontaneous symmetry breaking.

Main result. The authors prove that anomalous tunneling is a universal low-energy theorem where symmetry-preserving potentials are irrelevant and lead to perfect transmission, whereas symmetry-breaking perturbations are relevant and suppress transmission.

Method. The study uses a low-energy effective field theory (EFT) framework combined with scaling analysis and dimensional power counting to evaluate the impact of localized potentials.

Model / system. The framework applies to Nambu-Goldstone modes (Type-A and Type-B) in systems with spontaneous symmetry breaking, specifically illustrating the effect with superfluid phonons and magnons in ferromagnets.

Key observables. Transmission coefficient (T), reflection coefficient (R), and energy flux (Q).

Important parameters / regimes. Low-energy/low-momentum limit (k -> 0), symmetry-preserving vs. symmetry-breaking perturbations, and the scaling dimension of localized potentials.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis for Type-B modes neglects subleading omega^2 g_t(x) terms to focus on the low-energy limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the scaling analysis showing how potentials shrink or grow under scaling; Figure 2 shows the transmission coefficient approaching 1 as the momentum-width product (kia) approaches 0.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of anomalous tunneling, establishes a general EFT framework for Nambu-Goldstone modes, performs scaling and power-counting analysis to distinguish perturbation types, and provides explicit examples using phonons and magnons.

Abstract

Anomalous tunneling refers to the phenomenon in which the transmission coefficient through a potential barrier approaches unity as the energy of an incident particle or quasiparticle tends to zero. This counterintuitive effect has been reported in systems exhibiting spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB), such as superfluids, yet the general conditions for its occurrence remain unclear. In this Letter, we establish that anomalous tunneling of Nambu-Goldstone (NG) modes is a universal low-energy theorem dictated solely by symmetry and scaling, using a low-energy effective field theory (EFT) framework. We formulate the scattering of NG modes by external potentials in terms of spatially dependent EFT coefficients and demonstrate that symmetry-preserving localized potentials are irrelevant in the long-wavelength limit, leading to perfect transmission. In contrast, symmetry-breaking perturbations are relevant and suppress transmission, resulting in the absence of anomalous tunneling. We illustrate this universal behavior with explicit examples of superfluid phonons and magnons.

Summary. This paper presents a theoretical framework for the laser excitation and detection of the 229Th nuclear isomer in trapped ions. It proposes specific electronic fluorescence channels to improve detection efficiency and demonstrates that the nuclear transition can be found relatively quickly using current VUV technology. This work is a significant step toward the realization of practical nuclear clocks.

Why it may be interesting. It provides critical theoretical guidance for the development of nuclear clocks and explores the control of hyperfine-resolved dynamics in a system coupling nuclear and electronic degrees of freedom.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving efficient laser excitation and detection of the low-energy nuclear isomeric state of 229Th in trapped-ion systems, specifically overcoming low fluorescence signals and large frequency uncertainties.

Main result. The study identifies that matching laser linewidth, detuning, and irradiation time is essential for excitation and proposes that a 1088-nm detection scheme provides the highest photon rate, enabling the nuclear transition to be located within one month.

Method. A quantum master equation approach in the Lindblad form is used to analyze hyperfine-resolved excitation dynamics and population transfer efficiency.

Model / system. The system consists of 229Th3+ ions in a trap, modeling the nuclear transition between ground and isomeric states via an M1 transition and electronic transitions via E1 transitions using a hyperfine-coupled basis.

Key observables. Isomeric population, electronic fluorescence photon rates (690, 984, and 1088 nm), and total search time for the nuclear transition frequency.

Important parameters / regimes. Nuclear Rabi frequency (~3.4 Hz), laser linewidths (100 Hz to 10 MHz), nuclear isomeric lifetime (~2500 s), and transition energy (~8.4 eV).

Assumptions / limitations. The nuclear isomer decay is neglected for short time scales (up to 500 s) to treat the system as a two-level model, and the rotating-wave approximation is applied.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows nuclear and electronic energy levels and proposed fluorescence wavelengths; Figure 2 displays hyperfine structures; Figure 10 shows electronic population as a function of laser detuning.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific problem, describes the physical system and hyperfine model, details the quantum master equation methodology, presents results for excitation and detection schemes, and concludes with an optimization of the nuclear transition search time.

Abstract

We present a comprehensive theoretical investigation of hyperfine-resolved excitation and detection of the low-energy isomeric state of $^{229}$Th in trapped $^{229}\mathrm{Th}^{3+}$ ions. Using a quantum master equation approach, we analyze the dependence of the isomeric population on laser linewidth, detuning, and irradiation time, showing that their proper matching is essential for efficient excitation. We further propose two nuclear-state detection schemes based on three hyperfine-resolved electronic fluorescence channels at 690, 984, and 1088 nm. Our analysis shows that the 690-nm and 984-nm scheme yields detectable photon rates on the order of $10^4~\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ per ion for each wavelength, whereas the 1088-nm scheme achieves a higher rate on the order of $10^5~\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ per ion. By quantifying the trade-off between irradiation time and scan-step size, we show that the nuclear transition can be located within one month for a 100-MHz uncertainty using currently available vacuum-ultraviolet laser technology. These results provide practical guidance for trapped-ion $^{229}\mathrm{Th}$ spectroscopy and the development of nuclear clocks.

Hypergeometric Functions of Nilpotent Operators: Functional Collapse and Structural Depth at Exceptional Points

Authors: Ramon Moya
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27834v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proves that applying analytic functions to nilpotent operators causes a 'functional collapse' that reduces the complexity of their Jordan structure. It provides a specific criterion to calculate how much the Jordan depth of an exceptional point decreases based on the function's contact order. This has direct implications for understanding the stability and spectral properties of non-Hermitian systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in non-Hermitian quantum mechanics and open quantum systems, as it provides a precise mathematical tool to predict how physical transformations (like time evolution or spectral filtering) can simplify or destroy the complex algebraic structure of exceptional points.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how analytic functional transformations (like hypergeometric functions) affect the Jordan structure and nilpotency index of operators at exceptional points.

Main result. The author establishes a 'nilpotent depth criterion' showing that a function with contact order r reduces the Jordan depth of an exceptional point from m+1 to at most ceil((m+1)/r).

Method. The study uses functional calculus of nilpotent operators, Taylor expansions, and algebraic analysis within C-associative algebras.

Model / system. The mathematical model focuses on non-Hermitian Hamiltonians at exceptional points, represented by H = lambda I + N, where N is a nilpotent operator.

Key observables. Jordan depth (nilpotency index), trace of hypergeometric functions, and the order of the pole in the modified resolvent.

Important parameters / regimes. Nilpotency index (m+1), contact order (r) of the function at the eigenvalue, and the parameter-termination vs. nilpotent-argument termination mechanisms.

Assumptions / limitations. The operators are assumed to be finite-dimensional and nilpotent; functions are assumed to be analytic at the eigenvalue.

Figures summary. The paper includes explicit 3x3 Jordan block computations for various functions (exponential, Kummer, Gauss hypergeometric) to validate the theoretical bounds.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the functional collapse of hypergeometric functions, introduces the nilpotent depth criterion (Theorem 2), applies this to Hamiltonians at exceptional points (Theorem 3), and concludes with explicit 3x3 matrix examples and consequences for the resolvent.

Abstract

We study hypergeometric functions of nilpotent operators in finite-dimensional settings, motivated by the algebraic structure of exceptional points in non-Hermitian quantum mechanics. Our starting point is the following exact result: if N is a nilpotent operator of index m+1 in an associative algebra over C, then every generalized hypergeometric function pFq evaluated at N reduces to a finite polynomial in N of degree at most m, without any analytic convergence requirement. This "functional collapse" is distinct from the classical parameter-termination mechanism and arises purely from the nilpotent structure of the argument. The main result is a "nilpotent depth criterion" (Theorem 2): if the first non-constant coefficient of a formal series F appears in degree r >= 1, then the nilpotent part F(N) - F(0)I has nilpotency index bounded above by ceil((m+1)/r). We apply this criterion to Hamiltonians at exceptional points, where H = lambda I + N with N^{m+1} = 0. Theorem 3 establishes that a function F analytic at lambda reduces the Jordan depth of the exceptional point from m+1 to at most ceil((m+1)/r), where r is the contact order of F at lambda. As consequences: the time evolution operator e^{tH} preserves the full Jordan depth for all t != 0; a function with a zero of order m+1 at lambda annihilates the entire Jordan structure; and the order of the pole of the modified resolvent is reduced from m+1 to at most m+1-r. Results are illustrated with explicit 3x3 Jordan block computations for 1F1, 2F1, and the time evolution operator, confirming sharpness of the bounds.

Quantum integrable matrix models of spinor Bose gases in one spatial dimension

Authors: Hannes Köper, Thomas Gasenzer
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27885v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 2/5

Summary. This paper develops a mathematical framework using matrix-valued bosonic fields to describe 1D spinor Bose gases with arbitrary spin. It successfully maps the spin-1 case to a set of solvable integral equations, allowing for the precise calculation of the ground state phase diagram. The work highlights how interactions can lead to bound states that change the effective exclusion principle for the particles.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful integrable framework for studying complex spin multiplets in 1D, offering new insights into how bound states can alter the fundamental statistics (Pauli exclusion principle) of interacting bosons in cold atom systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to generalize the description of one-dimensional spinor Bose gases to arbitrary spin multiplets using a matrix-based integrable model.

Main result. The authors derive a quantum integrable matrix extension of the nonlinear Schrödinger model and compute the ground state phase diagram for a spin-1 Bose gas, revealing that paired bound states modify the effective Pauli exclusion principle for bosons.

Method. The study employs algebraic Bethe-ansatz techniques to construct eigenstates and derive Bethe equations, alongside the derivation of integral equations for thermodynamic properties.

Model / system. A quantum integrable matrix extension of the nonlinear Schrödinger model where fundamental fields are represented by an m x n matrix of bosonic field operators, specifically applied to spin-1 Bose gases with repulsive density-density and anti-ferromagnetic spin-spin interactions.

Key observables. Ground state phase diagram, spectra of conserved quantities, and equilibrium thermodynamic properties.

Important parameters / regimes. Chemical potential, external magnetic field, and the Lieb parameter (gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is restricted to one spatial dimension with repulsive density-density interaction and anti-ferromagnetic spin-spin coupling.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a matrix extension of the nonlinear Schrödinger model, applies the algebraic Bethe-ansatz to find eigenstates and Bethe equations, focuses on the spin-1 case to derive thermodynamic integral equations, and concludes with a phase diagram analysis and discussion of bound state effects.

Abstract

Degenerate spinor Bose gases with repulsive density-density interaction and anti-ferromagnetic spin-spin coupling in one spatial dimension are shown to be described by a quantum integrable matrix extension of the nonlinear Schrödinger model, whose fundamental fields are described by an $m\,\times\,n$ matrix of bosonic field operators. The eigenstates of this model are constructed for arbitrarily sized matrix field operators by means of algebraic Bethe-ansatz techniques, and the corresponding Bethe equations governing the spectra of conserved quantities are derived. The approach thus generalizes previously chosen techniques to account for arbitrary spin multiplets and their spin-spin interaction. Focusing on the specific case of the $2\times2$ model, which is shown to correspond to a spin-$1$ Bose gas, a set of integral equations is derived, which describe its equilibrium thermodynamic properties. From these, the ground state phase diagram is computed both, numerically and analytically in the parameter plane spanned by the chemical potential and an external magnetic field. Furthermore, the existence of paired bound states is shown to modify the Pauli exclusion principle for interacting bosons in one dimension. In particular, it is found that no two quasiparticle rapidities can coincide, provided that the Lieb parameter satisfies $γ>4/3$.

Reorganizing Quantum Measurement Records Improves Time-Series Prediction

Authors: Markus Baumann, Maximilian Zorn, Thomas Gabor, Claudia Linnhoff-Popien, Jonas Stein
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.28160v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a simple algorithmic technique called split-ensemble training to reorganize quantum measurement shots. By grouping shots into intermediate averages, it balances the trade-off between measurement noise and training sample size, significantly improving the accuracy of quantum-classical hybrid time-series prediction on both simulators and real hardware.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to optimize the use of finite measurement records in Quantum Reservoir Computing (QRC) to improve time-series prediction without increasing the quantum hardware execution budget.

Main result. The proposed 'split-ensemble' training method, which reorganizes shots into partially denoised groups, consistently outperforms standard expectation-value and raw-stacking approaches, especially on noisy real hardware.

Method. A split-ensemble training protocol that partitions $N$ shots into $G$ disjoint groups, each averaged to create a distinct, partially denoised feature vector for a classical linear readout.

Model / system. A Quantum Reservoir Computing framework using a fixed $Q$-qubit circuit (e.g., ring-CNOT entangler) with repeated executions, combined with a classical leaky integrator and a linear ridge regression readout.

Key observables. Single-qubit $Z_i$ and $X_i$ observables derived from measurement bitstrings.

Important parameters / regimes. Total execution budget ($S_{total}$), group size ($k$), training-support ratio ($ ho_{EV}$), reservoir size ($Q$), and leakage rate ($\eta$).

Assumptions / limitations. The total number of quantum circuit executions is a fixed resource; the classical readout is a linear model.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the three data organization strategies; Figure 2 shows NRMSE results for various time-series tasks; Figure 3-5 show performance across reservoir sizes and architectures; Figure 6 compares split-ensemble to EV-dup; Figure 7-8 show hardware validation on IBM hardware.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of shot-noise vs. data scarcity, defines the three data organization protocols, provides a theoretical framework for the split-ensemble advantage, presents numerical benchmarks on various time-series tasks, performs ablation studies to rule out simple data augmentation, and validates the method on real superconducting quantum hardware.

Abstract

Near-term quantum computers are accessed through repeated circuit executions, which produce finite measurement records rather than exact deterministic outputs. In quantum reservoir computing, these records are converted to feature vectors for a classical readout. The standard expectation-value approach averages all shots from one labeled time step into a single feature vector. This reduces finite-shot noise, but it also gives the readout only one training example from many circuit executions. We introduce split-ensemble training: the same shots are split into groups, and each group average is used as a separate, partially denoised feature vector for the same target. The quantum circuit, task, and measurement budget remain unchanged. Across simulated forecasting benchmarks and real hardware experiments, this simple reorganization improves prediction when full averaging leaves the readout with too few training examples, with the strongest gains observed on hardware. Our results establish shot-record organization as a simple, broadly applicable algorithmic lever for improving near-term quantum learning without additional quantum hardware cost.

Summary. This paper introduces a new quantum secret sharing protocol that ensures the identities of the senders remain anonymous during the decoding process. Abstract

Quantum secret sharing schemes are a family of quantum cryptographic protocols which provide secure quantum encodings, mapping one secret to multiple shares of information such that the original secret cannot be accessed without an authorized set of shares present for decoding. In this work, we describe a protocol that enables sender-anonymity during the secret decoding process. By using permutation-invariant QEC codes along with a set of anonymous quantum transmission algorithms, we construct a quantum anonymous secret sharing scheme that achieves sender-anonymity. We quantify information leakage in ramp quantum secret sharing schemes via the quantum conditional min-entropy, justifying it as a valid measure of leaked information by relating it to the Knill-Laflamme quantum error correction conditions. Finally, we evaluate several permutation-invariant codes using this measure to make observations on the information leakage of intermediate shares for each quantum anonymous secret sharing scheme.

Wavelet-based multiresolution analysis of quantum fractals in confined dynamics

Summary. The paper introduces a wavelet-based multiresolution framework to quantify the fractal dimensions of quantum carpets in confined dynamics. This method avoids the biases of traditional geometric or Fourier methods and provides a robust way to characterize space, time, and space-time fractality. It successfully recovers predicted universal fractal dimensions even in numerically limited, pre-fractal regimes.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a new, computationally efficient diagnostic tool for analyzing multiscale features and interference-driven dynamics in any system exhibiting complex, self-similar patterns, such as wave propagation in confined geometries or anomalous transport.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty in quantitatively and robustly characterizing quantum fractals (self-similar structures in quantum states) due to the limitations of traditional Fourier-based or geometric scaling methods which are sensitive to finite-size effects and numerical truncation.

Main result. The development of a wavelet-based multiresolultion analysis (MRA) framework that provides a robust, scale-adaptive, and hypothesis-free method to extract fractal dimensions for space, time, and space-time quantum fractals.

Method. Wavelet-based Multiresolution Analysis (MRA) using discrete wavelet transforms (Haar and Daubechies families) to analyze the scale-dependent distribution of wavelet energies and Bohmian trajectories to probe space-time correlations.

Model / system. A quantum particle confined within a one-dimensional infinite potential well, starting from initial states with spatial discontinuities (e.g., square wave functions).

Key observables. Probability density, probability flux, Bohmian trajectories, and fractal dimensions (spatial, temporal, and space-time).

Important parameters / regimes. Dyadic scales (j), spectral truncation (N), recurrence time (T), and wavelet family type.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is subject to a 'pre-fractal' regime due to finite spectral resolution/truncation, and the scaling exponent is extracted via linear regression within an intermediate scaling window.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows space-time quantum carpets with superimposed Bohmian trajectories; Figure 2 shows spatial profiles and fractal dimension estimates; Figure 3 shows temporal profiles and fractal dimension estimates; Figure 4 shows the convergence of fractal dimensions as a function of spectral truncation.

Paper structure. The paper identifies limitations in existing fractal characterization methods, introduces the wavelet MRA framework, applies it to confined 1D dynamics, validates the results against Berry's predicted universal dimensions, and demonstrates the method's robustness and efficiency.

Abstract

Fractal structures naturally emerge in quantum systems whose initial states exhibit spatial discontinuities, a phenomenon first identified by Berry in the paradigmatic case of a particle confined in an infinite potential well. While previous analyses of quantum fractals have mainly relied on spectral decompositions and geometric scaling arguments, their quantitative characterization often depends on scale choices and truncation effects. Here we present a wavelet-based multiresolution framework that enables a direct and assumption-free quantification of quantum fractality. Fractal dimensions are extracted from the scale-dependent distribution of wavelet energies, without invoking prior power-law hypotheses. The method is applied to space and time quantum fractals arising in confined dynamics, as well as to dynamical curves generated by the associated quantum probability flux. These flux-driven trajectories provide a natural space--time parametrization of the underlying fractal structure and yield scaling properties fully consistent with Berry's predictions for space--time fractals. The resulting fractal dimensions are shown to be robust with respect to the choice of wavelet family, numerical cutoffs, and system parameters. Beyond validating earlier conjectures, the present framework offers a unified and computationally efficient tool for the multiscale analysis of quantum fractality in confined and interference-driven quantum dynamics. That is, it provides an operational, scale-adaptive criterion that unifies the characterization of space, time, and space--time quantum fractals within a single, hypothesis-free approach.

A No-Cloning Trade-off Between Black Hole No-Hair and Horizon Smoothness

Authors: Sudhanva Joshi, Sunil Kumar Mishra

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper establishes a fundamental mathematical trade-off between the ability to distinguish black hole microstates from the outside and the smoothness of the event horizon. It proves that observable quantum hair is quantitatively incompatible with a smooth horizon, meaning any 'hair' requires a violation of the equivalence principle. This provides a rigorous information-theoretic constraint on various black hole microstate models like fuzzballs and soft hair.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the fundamental compatibility between the existence of observable quantum hair (exterior distinguishability) and the smoothness of the black hole event horizon (equivalence principle validity) under unitary evolution.

Main result. The authors derive a quantitative trade-off inequality, epsilon >= D_max^2/8, proving that any model predicting non-zero exterior quantum hair must necessarily involve a violation of horizon smoothness.

Method. The study employs quantum information-theoretic tools, specifically the Stinespring dilation of quantum channels, the diamond norm, and the Kretschmann-Schlingemann-Werner (KSW) continuity theorem.

Model / system. A semiclassical black hole model where the spacetime is partitioned into interior and exterior sectors, treating the horizon crossing as a quantum channel acting on an infalling system.

Key observables. epsilon (horizon smoothness violation/deviation from isometry), D_max (maximum exterior distinguishability/trace distance), and F_I (interior fidelity).

Important parameters / regimes. The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy (S_BH) scale, the regime of perfect isometry (epsilon = 0), and the firewall/fuzzball regimes (epsilon ~ O(1)).

Assumptions / limitations. Global unitarity, horizon causality (no signaling from interior to exterior), and diamond-norm interior accessibility.

Figures summary. A Penrose diagram illustrating the black hole interior and exterior regions, showing an infalling system crossing the horizon and the encoding of states.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing an exact no-hair lemma for perfect isometries, then uses the KSW theorem to extend this to an approximate theorem, ultimately deriving the quadratic trade-off inequality and discussing implications for fuzzballs and soft hair.

Abstract

The black hole no-hair theorem is traditionally derived from the uniqueness theorems of general relativity. We show that a quantitative form follows from unitarity together with the standard semiclassical assumptions of horizon causality and interior accessibility. For a semiclassical black hole, we prove that the trace distance between exterior states corresponding to two same-charge infalling states is bounded by $2\sqrt{2\varepsilon}$, where $\varepsilon$ quantifies the diamond norm departure of the interior channel from a perfect isometry which is a quantitative measure of horizon-smoothness violation that upper-bounds $1 - F_I$, where $F_I$ is the interior fidelity capturing how faithfully the infalling state is retained. Inverting this relation yields a trade-off inequality, $\varepsilon \geq D_{\max}^2/8$, between the maximum exterior distinguishability $D_{\max}$ and the degree of horizon smoothness. This establishes that observable exterior quantum hair is quantitatively incompatible with exact horizon smoothness under unitary evolution: any model predicting nonzero exterior hair must violate the equivalence principle at the horizon by a quantifiable amount. Pre-existing entanglement with the infalling system is the only channel for quantum hair compatible with both unitarity and horizon smoothness.

Constructing Bulk Topological Orders via Layered Gauging

Authors: Shang Liu
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27363v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. The paper introduces 'layered gauging,' a new method to construct higher-dimensional topological orders by stacking and gauging lower-dimensional layers. It successfully derives well-known models like the X-cube and Toric Code and extends the construction to complex non-invertible symmetries. This provides a powerful tool for exploring the landscape of fracton and liquid topological phases.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a systematic way to build complex many-body phases and explores the deep connection between bulk topological order and boundary symmetry, which is fundamental to understanding many-body dynamics and topological phases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a versatile and physically intuitive method to construct (k+1)-dimensional bulk topological orders, including both liquid and fracton types, from k-dimensional generalized symmetries.

Main result. The proposed 'layered gauging' construction successfully generates various higher-dimensional topological orders, such as the X-cube model, anisotropic fracton orders, and the 2D double semion model, from lower-dimensional symmetries.

Method. The method involves stacking k-dimensional quantum systems into a (k+1)-dimensional pile and sequentially gauging a diagonal or bilayer symmetry acting on adjacent layers.

Model / system. The construction uses various building blocks including 1D Z2 Ising chains, 2D Z2 ferromagnets, and the 2D plaquette Ising model, which are stacked and gauged to create 2D and 3D models like the Toric Code and X-cube model.

Key observables. Ground state degeneracy, anyon mobility, and boundary symmetry enforcement.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling constants J, h, and lambda; regimes where symmetries are spontaneously broken (e.g., h=0).

Assumptions / limitations. The method's universality is conjectured but lacks a rigorous mathematical proof; the construction is most effective when the k-dimensional layers spontaneously break their symmetries completely.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the sequential and even-odd layered gauging procedures; Figure 2 shows anyon loops on disk and cylinder geometries; Figure 4 illustrates the construction of the X-cube model; Figure 7/8 show the implementation of non-invertible gauging.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the layered gauging concept, demonstrates its application to conventional and higher-form symmetries (Toric Code), extends it to subsystem symmetries (fracton models), and finally generalizes the procedure to non-abelian and non-invertible symmetries.

Abstract

Understanding quantum phases and phase transitions in the presence of symmetries is a central objective of quantum many-body physics. A powerful modern paradigm for investigating this problem is topological holography, which relates symmetries in $k$ dimensions to "bulk" topological orders in $(k+1)$ dimensions. While conceptually profound, most existing bulk construction methods rely on sophisticated mathematical formalisms and can be difficult to apply to certain symmetry types. In this work, we propose a physically intuitive and versatile method, termed the layered gauging construction, to systematically generate $(k+1)$-dimensional (liquid or fracton) topological orders from $k$-dimensional generalized symmetries. Roughly speaking, the prescription is to stack many layers of $k$-dimensional quantum systems with certain symmetries into a $(k+1)$-dimensional pile, and then sequentially gauge a diagonal symmetry acting on each nearest-neighbor pair of layers. The detailed procedure depends on the specific symmetry types. We have successfully implemented the method in a number of examples in different spatial dimensions, with symmetries that are conventional, higher-form, subsystem, anomalous, nonabelian, or noninvertible. We hence conjecture the method to be very general. For example, from the subsystem symmetry of the $2d$ plaquette Ising model, we derive the X-cube model and also an anisotropic fracton topological order. Additionally, starting from an anomalous $\mathbb Z_2$ symmetry in $1d$, we construct a new square lattice model realizing the double semion topological order.

Abstract

Collective motions in strongly interacting magnets involve many spins and are often described in terms of integer-spin excitations. However, in certain cases, the collective motion can behave as if these integer excitations break apart into smaller, particle-like entities with unusual properties. Such fractionalized excitations in quantum magnets are commonly associated either with topological order in two dimensions or with criticality in one dimension. It remains unclear how these distinct mechanisms are connected across a dimensional crossover. Here we investigate the Ti-based quantum antiferromagnet, $Cs_{8}LiNa_{3}Ti_{12}F_{48}$, in which $Ti^{3+}$ ($3d^{1}$, $S=1/2$) ions interact antiferromagnetically within distorted kagome planes. Our inelastic neutron scattering study on a single crystal reveals a frustrated network of weakly coupled spin-$1/2$ chainsaws, realizing a regime of dimensional frustration in which interchain couplings fail to establish coherent two-dimensional order. The magnetic excitation spectrum exhibits a strong continuum spanning the full measured momentum and energy phase space. In addition, the dynamic spin correlation function displays rod-like scattering in momentum space, indicating a quasi-one-dimensional nature of the magnetic correlations. These results point to fractionalized excitations with intrinsically directional character, demonstrating that signatures of one-dimensional criticality can persist within a two-dimensional lattice. Our findings establish anisotropic fractionalization as a distinct organizing principle for quantum-disordered states.
