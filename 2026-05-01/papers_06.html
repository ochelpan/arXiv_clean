Towards Accelerated SCF Workflows with Equivariant Density-Matrix Learning and Analytic Refinement

Authors: Zuriel Y. Yescas-Ramos, Andrés Álvarez-García, Huziel E. Sauceda
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27256v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper introduces dm-PhiSNet, a machine learning model designed to accelerate quantum chemistry calculations by predicting high-quality initial density matrices. By enforcing physical constraints like idempotency and charge conservation, the model significantly reduces the number of iterations needed for SCF convergence and can even predict accurate forces in a single step.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Accelerating Self-Consistent Field (SCF) workflows in electronic-structure theory by generating physically admissible initial density matrix guesses.

Main result. The dm-PhiSNet model reduces SCF iteration steps by 49-81% and provides accurate one-shot total energies and Hellmann-Feynman forces without explicit force supervision.

Method. A two-stage training protocol using an equivariant PhiSNet backbone combined with a lightweight analytic refinement block that enforces physical constraints like electron-number conservation and generalized idempotency.

Model / system. The model predicts one-electron reduced density matrices (1-RDMs) in an atomic-orbital basis for closed-shell molecular systems including H2O, CH4, NH3, HF, ethanol, and NO3-.

Key observables. One-electron reduced density matrices (1-RDMs), total energies, Hellmann-Feynman atomic forces, and SCF iteration counts.

Assumptions / limitations. The work focuses on closed-shell systems and assumes the effectiveness of projecting learned 1-RDMs onto the admissible manifold at negligible computational cost.

Figures summary. Figure 1 details the dm-PhiSNet architecture, including the equivariant backbone and the analytic refinement sub-blocks. Figure 2 shows the reduction in SCF iterations compared to standard initializations, and Figure 3 presents error distributions for energy and force norms.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the dm-PhiSNet architecture, describes the equivariant backbone and the analytic constraint enforcement, details the two-stage training protocol, presents benchmarks on molecular systems regarding SCF acceleration and one-shot accuracy, and discusses scalability and limitations.

Abstract

We present \textsc{dm-PhiSNet}, a physically constrained \textsc{PhiSNet}-based equivariant model that predicts one-electron reduced density matrices (1-RDMs) directly from molecular geometries in an atomic-orbital (AO) basis for accelerated self-consistent field (SCF) workflows. Training follows a two-stage schedule with progressively introduced physically motivated objectives, and the resulting predictions are refined by a lightweight analytic block. This block enforces electron-number conservation, drives the 1-RDM toward generalized idempotency in the AO metric, and regularizes the occupation spectrum of the Löwdin-orthogonalized density. Across six closed-shell systems -- H$_2$O, CH$_4$, NH$_3$, HF, ethanol, and NO$_3^-$ -- the refined 1-RDMs provide SCF initial guesses that substantially reduce iteration steps by 49--81\% relative to standard initializations. Beyond SCF acceleration, the learned 1-RDMs yield accurate one-shot total energies and Hellmann--Feynman atomic forces without force supervision, indicating that the model captures chemically meaningful electronic structure. These results demonstrate that combining equivariant learning with analytic constraint enforcement provides a simple, general route to solver-ready density-matrix initializations and accelerated SCF workflows.

Summary. The study achieves strong coupling between magnon modes and superconducting resonator photons in micron-scale YIG structures. By using FIB-milled microplatelets, the researchers overcame previous thickness limitations, enabling efficient, low-power magnonic-based quantum devices. This provides a foundation for on-chip studies of numerous long-lived excitations.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems as it demonstrates a platform for scalable, on-chip strong coupling between bosonic excitations (magnons and photons) at extremely low power levels, which is a key requirement for hybrid quantum architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving strong coupling between magnons and microwave photons in sub-10 micron Yttrium Iron Garnet (YIG) structures has been difficult due to insufficient mode volume caused by YIG thickness limitations.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated strong coupling between numerous magnon modes in a YIG microplatelet and a superconducting LC resonator, even at extremely low input powers (<= 10 fW).

Method. The team used focused ion beam (FIB) techniques to create a YIG microplatelet from a single crystal and placed it on a constricted inductive line of an optimized superconducting lumped element LC resonator.

Model / system. The system consists of a micron-scale YIG microplatelet coupled to a superconducting lumped element LC resonator, involving the interaction between magnonic modes and resonator photons.

Key observables. Microwave transmission signal and anti-crossings in the transmission spectrum.

Important parameters / regimes. Sub-10 micron YIG structures, device input powers <= 10 fW.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of small-scale magnonics, describes the FIB fabrication of YIG microplatelets, presents experimental evidence of strong coupling via anti-crossings, validates results with micromagnetic simulations and analytical calculations, and demonstrates low-power operation.

Abstract

Strong-coupling experiments based on magnons enable the exploration into on-chip demonstrations involving numerous long-lived excitations. Yttrium iron garnet (YIG) has been considered for decades as a gold standard material for magnonics due to its low-loss magnonic properties. While YIG has successfully demonstrated strong-coupling in macroscopic device geometries, the strong coupling of magnons in truly sub-10 micron YIG structures to date has not yet been realized. This obstacle is due to the difficulty producing large enough effective magnonic mode volume necessary primarily due to thickness limitations of YIG deposition and device fabrication techniques. Here, we demonstrate the use of a microplatelet of YIG, manufactured from a single crystal of YIG via focused ion beam (FIB) techniques, placed on a constricted inductive line of an optimized superconducting lumped element LC resonator to achieve strong coupling between numerous magnon modes and the LC resonator photons. These experimental findings are qualitatively backed by micromagnetic simulations and quantitatively supported by analytical calculations to identify the magnon modes corresponding to the experimentally observed anti-crossings in the microwave transmission signal. Further, we show that these anti-crossings remain even at incredibly low device input powers ($\leq 10$ fW). The fabrication techniques and device geometry enable the deterministic use of numerous confined magnon modes in micron-scale YIG structures for various magnetic field strengths and orientations at substantially reduced device powers. The results here establish a foundational path forward to achieving efficient magnon-based strong-coupling experiments in micron-scale YIG magnetic elements for effective on-chip studies.

Main result. The study achieved sub-GHz Rabi oscillations and measured spin coherence times of T2* = 13.8 ns and T2 = 108.7 ns using broadband microwave pulses.

Abstract

Negatively charged boron vacancy (VB-) defects in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) are promising for nanoscale-proximity quantum sensing. To evaluate their performance, it is important to characterize the spin coherence times T2 and T2. In this study, we realized sub-GHz Rabi oscillations of VB- using an isotopically enriched hBN thin film directly stamped onto a narrow gold wire. Using these strong microwave pulses, we performed Ramsey interference and Hahn echo measurements. The Ramsey interference signal showed Gaussian-like decay, yielding T2 = 13.8 ns. The Hahn echo measurement gave T2 = 108.7 ns and a stretch factor of α= 1.25. These results experimentally clarify the spin coherence properties of VB- and provide an effective method for evaluating the coherence of spin defects in van der Waals thin films with broad resonance linewidths.

Mobile Exceptional Points Generate Momentum-Space Switching Domains

Authors: Jung-Wan Ryu, Chang-Hwan Yi
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27417v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5

Summary. The paper shows that when exceptional points move during cyclic modulation, they create distinct regions in the Brillouin zone where bands switch or do not switch. This discovery provides a new method for controlling band topology in non-Hermitian systems, such as lossy photonic crystals.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems as it demonstrates a new way to engineer non-Hermitian topological phenomena by controlling the motion of exceptional points.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how the mobility of exceptional points (EPs) under cyclic modulation affects the band topology and creates new momentum-space structures.

Main result. Mobile EPs generate momentum-space switching domains that partition the Brillouin zone into regions with different band-switching behaviors, leading to global band switching at high modulation strengths.

Method. The authors use a minimal two-band lattice model to introduce a band-permutation invariant and demonstrate the effect in a photonic crystal with lossy materials.

Model / system. A minimal two-band lattice model under cyclic modulation and a photonic crystal platform incorporating lossy materials.

Key observables. Band-switching behavior, band-permutation invariant, and EP trajectories in extended parameter space.

Important parameters / regimes. Modulation strength, crystal momentum, and the modulation parameter.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of mobile EPs, presents a theoretical framework using a two-band lattice model and a new band-permutation invariant, describes the formation of switching domains, and validates the predictions using a photonic crystal system.

Abstract

Exceptional points (EPs), non-Hermitian degeneracies where both eigenvalues and eigenvectors coalesce, play a central role in the topology of non-Hermitian spectra. Recent advances have enabled the controlled creation and manipulation of EPs in a wide range of physical systems, raising the question of what new band topology emerges when EPs become mobile under cyclic modulation. Here we show that mobile EPs generate momentum-space switching domains that partition the Brillouin zone into regions with distinct band-switching behavior. Using a minimal two-band lattice model, we introduce a band-permutation invariant that determines whether eigenmodes exchange after one modulation cycle. The boundaries between switching regions arise from the projection of EP trajectories in an extended parameter space combining crystal momentum and the modulation parameter. As the modulation strength increases, the switching domains expand and eventually cover the entire Brillouin zone, resulting in global band switching. The predicted switching-domain structure is further demonstrated in a photonic crystal with lossy materials. These results open a new avenue within non-Hermitian topology by enabling the engineering of EP-driven phenomena through their controlled motion.

Summary. The paper investigates how particle clusters move directionally between compartments in a vibrated granular system. By measuring species-specific fluxes, the authors show that an asymmetry in how small and large particles interact drives this migration. This work provides an experimental basis for modeling collective transport in driven, dissipative systems.

Why it may be interesting. The discovery of asymmetric, population-dependent flux and directional transport in a non-equilibrium system provides insights into collective transport and driven-dissipative dynamics relevant to open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the mechanism behind the directional stepwise migration of particle clusters in a multi-compartment vibrated granular system.

Main result. The study identifies an asymmetric interaction between particle species where large particles enhance small particle flux, while small particles suppress large particle flux, driving directional transport.

Method. Direct measurement of particle species flux as a function of instantaneous populations, followed by the implementation of a minimal flux model.

Model / system. A vertically vibrated granular system composed of small and large particles distributed across multiple compartments.

Key observables. Flux of small and large particle species, particle populations per compartment, and directional migration dynamics.

Important parameters / regimes. Mechanical agitation (vibration), particle size distribution, and instantaneous particle populations.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated as an out-of-equilibrium system driven by energy dissipation through inelastic collisions and friction.

Paper structure. Introduction to granular out-of-equilibrium dynamics, experimental report on directional cluster migration, flux measurement analysis, and validation via a minimal flux model.

Abstract

Granular materials are inherently out-of-equilibrium systems due to energy dissipation through inelastic collisions and friction. When driven by mechanical agitation such as vibration, they exhibit rich collective behaviors including segregation, clustering, and spontaneous oscillations. Here, we report directional stepwise migration of particle clusters from one compartment to the next in a vertically vibrated granular system composed of small and large particles. To clarify the underlying mechanism, we directly measured how the flux of both particle species depends on the instantaneous particle populations. The measurements reveal an asymmetric interaction between particle species: the flux of small particles is enhanced by the presence of large particles, whereas that of large particles is suppressed by small particles. A minimal flux model incorporating these measured fluxes reproduces the observed directional dynamics and provides an experimentally grounded framework for collective transport in vibrated granular systems.

Main result. The authors developed an ML pipeline that achieves 0.92 classification accuracy, successfully resolving a narrow coexistence region and interpolating phase boundaries beyond the original simulation points.

Abstract

In this study, we use machine learning to classify and interpolate the phase structure of the Vicsek flocking model across the three-dimensional parameter space $(η,ρ,v_0)$. We construct a dataset of simulated parameter points and characterize each point using long-time dynamical observables. These observables are then used as inputs to a K-Means clustering procedure, which assigns each point to a disorder, order, or coexistence phase. Using these clustered labels, we train a neural-network classifier to learn the mapping from model parameters to phase behavior, achieving a classification accuracy of 0.92. The resulting phase map resolves a narrow coexistence region separating the ordered and disordered phases and extends the inferred phase boundaries beyond the originally sampled simulation points. More broadly, this approach provides a systematic way to convert sparse simulation data into a global phase diagram for collective-motion models.

Electrically Tunable Terahertz Chirality from Quantum Geometry

Summary. The researchers demonstrated how to electrically tune the polarization of terahertz light by using a gate voltage to reshape the Fermi surface in a Dirac semimetal. By selectively modulating the Berry curvature-driven component of the emission, they achieved programmable control of THz chirality. This provides a new route for creating on-chip, electrically controllable chiral light sources.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving all-electrical, deterministic control over the chirality (polarization state) of Terahertz (THz) emission by manipulating quantum geometry.

Main result. Demonstrated that electrostatic gating of Cd3As2 can reshape Fermi pockets to selectively modulate the Berry curvature-driven THz component, enabling programmable tuning of THz polarization across the Poincaré sphere.

Method. THz emission spectroscopy using circularly polarized femtosecond laser pulses on a gated Cd3As2 thin-film device, combined with Stokes parameter analysis.

Model / system. A 40 nm thin film of the 3D Dirac semimetal Cd3As2 in a field-effect transistor geometry, subject to Floquet Weyl physics via optical excitation.

Key observables. THz electric field components (Ex and Ey), ellipticity angle (chi), azimuth angle (psi), and Stokes parameters (S1, S2, S3).

Important parameters / regimes. Gate voltage (Vg) from -90 V to +90 V, Fermi energy (Ef) shifts, and Berry curvature integral.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a monopole-like divergence for Berry curvature near Weyl nodes and uses an asymmetric screening model for carrier accumulation/depletion.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of Floquet band engineering and device architecture; Fig 2: Evidence of selective gate-dependent tuning of THz components; Fig 3: Evolution of polarization ellipses across voltages; Fig 4: Quantitative mapping of Stokes parameters and ellipticity vs. voltage.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of controlling optical observables via quantum geometry, describes the Floquet-Weyl mechanism in Cd3As2, presents experimental evidence of gate-tunable THz chirality, provides quantitative verification via Berry curvature integration, and concludes with potential applications for programmable THz sources.

Abstract

Quantum geometry encoded in the momentum space structure of electronic wavefunctions, governs charge dynamics through Berry curvature, enabling unconventional transport and optical responses. In topological semimetals, this geometry is sampled over Fermi pockets, suggesting electrical control by Fermi surface tuning, yet such control has remained largely limited to DC transport. Here we show that electrostatic gating of the 3D Dirac semimetal Cd3As2 reshapes Fermi pockets surrounding photoinduced Floquet Weyl nodes, enabling electrical control of terahertz (THz) emission chirality. Gate tuning selectively modulates the Berry curvature driven linearly polarized THz component by up to 60% and 49% at positive and negative bias, respectively, while the orthogonal linearly polarized photon-drag component remains unchanged. With the two orthogonal fields intrinsically phase-locked at \sfracπ{2} by the excitation geometry, the selective gate-tuned amplitude control enables the polarization tuning across the Poincaré sphere, achieving near-circular polarization (χ\approx-42°) at +10 V. These results establish Fermi surface tuning as a general route to programmable quantum geometric control of chiral terahertz emission.

Abstract

Superconductors can transport spin without Joule dissipation, yet their coherent coupling to short-wavelength magnons in insulating magnets remains largely unexplored. Here we demonstrate experimentally and theoretically that a conventional diffusive superconductor can enable the conversion of the uniform ferromagnetic-resonance (FMR) mode into perpendicular standing spin waves (PSSWs) in an adjacent ferrimagnetic insulator. In Bi-substituted iron-garnet/Nb bilayers, the microwave transmission develops an additional resonance feature that appears only below the Nb transition temperature and lies close to the uniform FMR peak. The resulting susceptibility reproduces the measured lineshapes and establishes superconductivity as an active control knob for exchange standing-wave modes in magnetic insulators.

Theory of quantum decoherence in macroscopic topological insulators

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new perspective on how environmental coupling (decoherence) fundamentally alters transport properties and introduces a new mechanism for the spin Hall effect through second-order scattering.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of decoherence in topological insulators, develops a theory for decoherence-induced corrections, identifies a new skew-scattering mechanism, and concludes with a predicted experimental scaling law.

Abstract

Quantum decoherence-the loss of quantum coherence due to interactions with an environment-plays a central role in quantum transport, and controlling this ubiquitous yet inevitable phenomenon is essential for practical quantum technologies. Despite its importance, the microscopic mechanisms of decoherence in infinite-size topological insulators remain poorly understood. Here, we develop a comprehensive theory that quantitatively investigates how quantum decoherence shapes the quantum spin Hall effect in macroscopic topological insulators, and reveal that decoherence-induced corrections scale quadratically with impurity density. Besides, we uncover a previously unidentified mechanism of the extrinsic spin Hall effect: a second-order skew-scattering process intrinsically tied to quantum decoherence-fundamentally distinct from, yet substantially stronger than, the conventional third-order skew-scattering mechanism. Furthermore, we predict a new scaling law in which the decoherence-induced spin Hall conductivity scales quadratically with the longitudinal conductivity, providing a clear experimental signature of decoherence effects. Our results establish the essential role of decoherence in quantum transport of topological insulators and reveal that macroscopic topological insulators offer a promising platform for next-generation spintronic applications.
