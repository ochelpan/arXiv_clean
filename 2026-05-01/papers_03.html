Derivation of the Born Rule and Operational Quantum Formalism in the Accessibility Framework through Boundary Reduction

Authors: Everett Fall, Hironori Kondo
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27125v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes that quantum mechanics is an epistemic consequence of an observer's limited access to a more complex, deterministic algebraic reality. By applying a 'Balance Principle' to spectral triples, the authors derive the Standard Model's gauge group and spacetime dimension, while simultaneously deriving the Born rule as a result of an information bottleneck at the observer's interface.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems as it provides a structural, algebraic derivation of non-Markovian dynamics and the emergence of probabilistic, non-unitary evolution from an underlying deterministic, unitary ontology.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper seeks to derive the fundamental operational features of quantum mechanics (the Born rule, state updating, and interference) and the parameters of the Standard Model from a single, deeper algebraic principle. **Main result.** The authors show that the Born rule and quantum formalism emerge from an 'Aperture'—a structural information bottleneck created by boundary reduction in Accessibility Theory—while the Standard Model parameters are uniquely selected by a 'Balance Principle'. **Method.** The study employs Noncommutative Geometry (NCG) techniques, specifically using spectral triples, boundary reduction of algebras, Gleason's theorem, and Diophantine analysis to solve for complexity constraints. **Model / system.** The framework is 'Accessibility Theory' (AT), which models an observer interacting with a deterministic, unitary 'Continuum' through a commutative, three-outcome 'Aperture' interface derived from a real graded spectral triple. **Key observables.** Aperture record process (sequence of sector labels), transition effect operators, and context-free records. **Important parameters / regimes.** Spacetime dimension (n=4), number of fermion generations (Ngen=3), algebraic complexity (A_disc=144), and the trace reduction ratio (xi=1/4). **Assumptions / limitations.** Assumes the validity of spectral triple axioms, the Principle of Universal Accessibility Balance, and specific observer-level coherence commitments such as noncontextuality and linearity. **Paper structure.** The paper begins by establishing the Accessibility Theory framework and the Balance Principle to select the Standard Model algebra; it then uses boundary reduction to define the Aperture; subsequently, it applies Gleason's theorem and algebraic constraints to derive the Born rule and quantum dynamics; finally, it discusses the emergence of non-Markovianity and the resolution of the measurement problem.
Abstract We show that the operational quantum formalism -- the Born rule, Lüders state updating, quantum interference, non-Markovian effective dynamics, and Bell inequality violation at the Tsirelson bound \(2\sqrt{2}\) -- arises within Accessibility Theory (AT) from the Aperture construction together with explicit coherence and locality assumptions stated in the paper. AT is a framework built on real graded spectral triples and a single algebraic selection principle. The Principle of Universal Accessibility Balance requires three independent measures of the complexity of a spectral triple -- its algebraic, gauge-theoretic, and geometric content -- to be exactly equal and minimized, uniquely selecting the algebra \(\C \oplus \Hbb \oplus M_3(\C)\) and with it the Standard Model gauge group, particle content, four-dimensional Lorentzian spacetime, three generations, and gravitational dynamics. Restriction to a codimension-one geometric boundary reduces this algebra to its commutative center \(\C \oplus \C \oplus \C\) -- the Aperture -- which defines a permanent information bottleneck for any embedded observer. Coherence conditions on inference through this bottleneck, together with Gleason's theorem on the 48-dimensional internal Hilbert space, uniquely determine the Born rule; the remaining operational features follow from the same observer-level framework under the stated assumptions. At the ontological level the theory is deterministic and state-realist, while the operational quantum formalism appears at the observer level as a consequence of structurally limited access to the underlying algebraic reality.

Galilean boost invariance does not survive the trace: symmetry breaking in open quantum systems

Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27459v1

Summary. This paper proves that tracing out a Galilean-invariant environment breaks the boost covariance of the resulting reduced dynamics due to the dissipative term in the master equation. It establishes that this symmetry breaking is unavoidable in any system obeying the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, though it can be suppressed through parametric driving. The work provides a scale, defined by the ratio of damping to temperature, to predict where this violation becomes experimentally significant.

Why it may be interesting. It identifies a fundamental kinematic obstruction in open quantum systems, showing that dissipation and boost invariance are mutually exclusive in standard models, which has direct implications for the validity of covariant approximations in cold atom and optomechanical experiments.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper investigates whether Galilean boost invariance is preserved in the reduced dynamics of an open quantum system after tracing out the environment. **Main result.** The authors demonstrate a 'no-go' result: for bilinearly coupled systems, Galilean boost covariance is broken by the dissipative term of the master equation, and this violation cannot be avoided if the fluctuation-dissipation theorem holds. **Method.** The study uses an operator-level analysis of the exact Hu-Paz-Zhang master equation, algebraic testing of symmetry transformations, and stochastic decomposition of the influence functional. **Model / system.** The analysis focuses on the Caldeira-Leggett model, featuring a system particle coupled to a bath of harmonic oscillators via bilinear-coordinate interaction. **Key observables.** The dissipative anticommutator term, damping coefficient Gamma(t)f(t), and the boost-breaking phase. **Important parameters / regimes.** The dimensionless ratio hbar*gamma/k_B*T, which defines the crossover between negligible and substantial symmetry violation, and the squeezing rate mu. **Assumptions / limitations.** The analysis assumes a factorized initial condition and focuses on the quadratic regime, though extensions to non-quadratic potentials are conjectured via stochastic decomposition. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 provides a survey of the dimensionless boost-violation parameter across various quantum platforms, showing a crossover from covariant regimes (e.g., trapped ions, nanoparticles) to violated regimes (e.g., cold atoms in optical lattices). **Paper structure.** The paper begins by defining the global Galilean-invariant model, proceeds to an algebraic analysis of the master equation to locate the symmetry breaking, explores the fundamental conflict with the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, and concludes by discussing experimental platforms and potential restoration via parametric driving.
Abstract Tracing out a Galilean-invariant Caldeira-Leggett environment breaks Galilean boost covariance of the reduced dynamics, while spatial translations and rotations survive intact. An operator-level analysis of the exact Hu-Paz-Zhang master equation localizes the violation entirely in the dissipative anticommutator term, scaling with the damping coefficient $Γ(t)f(t)$. The fluctuation-dissipation theorem ties this coefficient to the absorptive bath response that drives equilibrium momentum diffusion, so for any non-trivial bath spectral density bilinear-coupled Galilean invariance, the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, and reduced boost covariance cannot hold simultaneously. The stochastic decomposition of the influence functional extends the mechanism beyond the quadratic regime. The dimensionless ratio $\hbarγ/k_\mathrm{B} T$ delineates the crossover: cold atoms in dissipative optical lattices and ultracold molecules sit at its edge. Parametric driving offers a one-directional escape: the squeezing rate that protects nonequilibrium entanglement above the standard quantum limit also suppresses boost-breaking over a driving cycle.

Heisenberg-limited Hamiltonian learning without short-time control

Authors: Myeongjin Shin, Junseo Lee, Changhun Oh
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27838v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that optimal, Heisenberg-limited Hamiltonian learning can be performed even when experimental constraints prevent the use of very short-time pulses. By using a method to emulate continuous control through longer evolution periods and learned corrections, the authors show that high-bandwidth control is not a fundamental requirement for efficient quantum system characterization.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and quantum control because it proves that the experimental difficulty of generating ultra-short, high-bandwidth pulses does not fundamentally prevent achieving the most efficient possible learning of a system's underlying physics.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Determining whether Heisenberg-limited Hamiltonian learning (achieving 1/epsilon scaling in total evolution time) is possible when access to arbitrarily short-time dynamics is restricted by a minimum evolution time T. **Main result.** The authors prove that Heisenberg-limited scaling can be achieved without short-time control, resolving an open problem. They establish that for logarithmically sparse Hamiltonians, any constant T is sufficient, while for polynomially sparse systems, a quantitative tradeoff exists between minimum evolution time and total evolution time. **Method.** The framework emulates continuous quantum control by replacing short-time Trotter steps with long-time queries to the unknown Hamiltonian interleaved with learned correction unitaries. The task is then reduced to sparse pure-state tomography. **Model / system.** An n-qubit, m-sparse, traceless Hamiltonian H expressed as a sum of Pauli operators with unknown real coefficients. **Key observables.** Pauli coefficients of the Hamiltonian, total evolution time, and error in the Hamiltonian estimate (l-infinity norm). **Important parameters / regimes.** m (sparsity), T (minimum evolution time), epsilon (target accuracy), and n (number of qubits). **Assumptions / limitations.** The Hamiltonian is assumed to be m-sparse in the Pauli basis and traceless; the algorithm assumes the ability to simulate the known part of the Hamiltonian and perform sparse pure-state tomography. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 compares previous approaches relying on stroboscopic control/Trotterization with small minimum evolution times against the new framework using long-time evolutions and learned auxiliary operations. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the short-time bottleneck problem, defines the Hamiltonian model and sparsity regimes, presents the long-time emulation framework and correction generator learning, provides mathematical proofs for logarithmic and polynomial sparsity regimes, and concludes with the implications for quantum learning.
Abstract Characterizing quantum systems by learning their underlying Hamiltonians is a central task in quantum information science. While recent algorithmic advances have achieved near-optimal efficiency in this task, they critically rely on accessing arbitrarily short-time dynamics. This reliance poses severe experimental challenges due to finite control bandwidth and transient pulse errors. In this work, we demonstrate that Heisenberg-limited Hamiltonian learning can be achieved without short-time control. We introduce a framework in which every query to the unknown dynamics has duration at least a prescribed minimum time $T$, and show that this restriction does not preclude Heisenberg-limited scaling. The key ingredient is a method for emulating the continuous quantum control required by iterative learning algorithms using only such lower-bounded evolution times. This reduces the learning task to sparse pure-state tomography. Notably, for logarithmically sparse Hamiltonians, our algorithm achieves the information-theoretically optimal $1/\varepsilon$ scaling in total evolution time for any arbitrary constant minimum evolution time $T$. For many-body (polynomially sparse) systems, we uncover a rigorous quantitative tradeoff, showing that the minimum required evolution time can be significantly relaxed from the standard limit at a polynomial cost in total evolution time. Our results affirmatively resolve a prominent open problem in the field and reveal that high-bandwidth, ultra-short pulses are not fundamentally necessary for optimal quantum learning.

Multirate characterization of relaxation mechanisms for two nonequivalent nuclear spins 1/2 in a liquid using maximally entangled pseudo-pure quantum states

Authors: Georgiy Baroncha, Alexander Perepukhov, Boris V. Fine
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27220v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

Summary. The paper presents a method to simultaneously measure eight different relaxation rates in a two-spin NMR system using entangled Bell states. It identifies a universal ratio for diagonal relaxation rates and reveals an unconventional relaxation mechanism caused by weak couplings to distant spins. This approach provides a powerful tool for fingerprinting complex molecular dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems as it demonstrates the ability to selectively initialize and characterize the eigenmodes of off-diagonal relaxation using entangled states, providing insight into complex decoherence mechanisms.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The study aims to characterize multiple relaxation mechanisms in a two-spin system by accessing both diagonal and off-diagonal elements of the density matrix. **Main result.** The authors successfully accessed 8 relaxation rates using Bell pseudo-pure states and identified a universal dimensionless ratio of diagonal relaxation rates determined by intra-pair magnetic dipolar interaction. **Method.** The researchers used a multirate NMR approach, employing a new method based on a detuned Hartmann-Hahn double resonance condition to create maximally entangled Bell pseudo-pure states (Bell PPSs). **Model / system.** A system of two non-equivalent nuclear spins 1/2 (1H and 13C) belonging to a molecule in a liquid, stable against chemical exchange. **Key observables.** 8 relaxation rates, including inversion recovery, nuclear Overhauser effect, and off-diagonal relaxation rates. **Important parameters / regimes.** dimensionless ratio of diagonal relaxation rates, J-couplings with distant nuclear spins, and intra-pair magnetic dipolar interaction. **Assumptions / limitations.** The nuclear spin pair is stable with respect to chemical exchange. **Paper structure.** The paper presents an experimental and theoretical investigation of spin relaxation, introducing a method for Bell PPS creation, demonstrating the selective initialization of relaxation eigenmodes, and providing a microscopic theory validated by parameter-free tests.
Abstract Multirate characterization of spin responses in nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) is a promising approach to fingerprinting complex molecules in the presence of multiple relaxation mechanisms. Here we present experimental and theoretical investigations simultaneously accessing 8 relaxation rates describing the density matrix of two adjacent non-equivalent nuclear spins 1/2 ($^1$H and $^{\ 13}$C) belonging to a molecule in a liquid. The selected nuclear pair is stable with respect to chemical exchange. Some of the rates are obtained from conventional measurements of inversion recovery and nuclear Overhauser effect, while other, less conventional ones, are extracted from the relaxation initialized by the maximally entangled pseudo-pure Bell states (Bell PPSs) of the spin pair. The Bell PPSs are created using a hereby introduced method based on a detuned Hartmann-Hahn double resonance condition. Microscopic theory behind the measured relaxation rates is presented, and its consistency is demonstrated by several parameter-free tests. In particular, it is shown both theoretically and experimentally, that the eigenmodes of the off-diagonal relaxation of the two-spin density matrix can be selectively initialized using Bell PPSs. Our multirate analysis suggests that the measured off-diagonal relaxation is partly due to an unconventional mechanism arising from very weak $J$-couplings of the spin pair with fluctuating distant nuclear spins. Furthermore, we identify a dimensionless ratio of diagonal relaxation rates, which is determined exclusively by intra-pair magnetic dipolar interaction and hence possesses a universal value for a broad class of nuclear spin pairs. This value is consistent with both our experiments and other experiments reported in the literature.

Summary. The paper proposes using Quantum Circuit Learning to replace deep, noisy quantum simulation circuits with shallower, learned versions. By training the circuit to respect the conserved charges of integrable spin chains, the method significantly reduces the impact of hardware noise. This approach allows for more robust and accurate simulation of long-time dynamics on near-term quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a physics-informed way to extend the reach of NISQ devices by using the inherent symmetries of integrable systems to combat decoherence.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Mitigating hardware noise (decoherence and gate infidelity) in the quantum simulation of integrable spin chains on NISQ devices. **Main result.** Quantum Circuit Learning (QCL) can produce shallower, noise-resilient circuits that effectively preserve conserved charges and dynamical observables compared to the original deep, noisy circuits. **Method.** A machine learning framework where a shallow variational circuit is trained to approximate a deeper time-evolution operator by leveraging conserved charges and a small amount of dynamical information as training signals. **Model / system.** Integrable quantum spin chains, specifically the XXX Heisenberg model, studied for two-qubit and three-qubit systems. **Key observables.** Conserved charges (total magnetization Z_tot, SU(2) generators, Hamiltonian H) and dynamical observables (expectation values like , , ). **Important parameters / regimes.** Circuit depth (D and d), noise probability (p), coupling constant (J), and number of qubits (L). **Assumptions / limitations.** The method relies on the existence of conserved charges (integrability) and assumes a sufficiently small time step for the Trotterized approximation. **Figures summary.** Figures compare predictions of learned circuits vs. true data, demonstrate accuracy improvements across different circuit depths, and show the suppression of noise-induced decay in conserved quantities under various noise models (bit-flip, depolarizing, amplitude, and phase damping). **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the noise mitigation problem, proposes the QCL strategy using integrability, details the circuit architecture and training process, presents numerical results for L=2 and L=3 systems under various noise channels, and discusses scalability and future directions.
Abstract We propose a noise-mitigation quantum simulation strategy for near-term quantum devices based on Quantum Circuit Learning (QCL), which is in particular effective for integrable quantum spin chains. The method trains a shallow variational circuit to approximate a deeper time-evolution circuit by learning the conserved charges and only a small amount of dynamical information in the system. Under realistic noise models, the learned circuit maintains both conserved quantities and dynamical observables significantly closer to their true values than the noisy simulation of the original circuit. This demonstrates QCL as an effective, physics-informed error mitigation strategy, producing shorter, more robust circuits without exponential sampling overhead.

Learning quantum disentanglement scheduling from reduced states via modular hybrid policies

Authors: Y. -X. Xiao, J. -Z. Han, Z. Zheng, Z. -H. Zhang, M. Xue, J. Li, X. Lv
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.28009v1

Summary. The paper proposes a hybrid quantum-classical machine learning approach to control the disentanglement of multiple qubits using only partial information from reduced density matrices. It reveals that classical preprocessing is crucial for success and provides design principles for scaling the quantum component of the controller.

Why it may be interesting. This is relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum control as it addresses the practical challenge of managing many-body entanglement using only local, noisy, or incomplete information, which is a hallmark of near-term quantum device operations.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Developing an effective control strategy for multiqubit disentanglement when only partial information (two-qubit reduced density matrices) is available. **Main result.** The study identifies that classical preprocessing is the primary driver of performance, while the quantum module acts as a compact latent representation; it also finds that increasing circuit width is more effective than increasing depth for policy efficiency. **Method.** A modular hybrid quantum-classical policy framework involving classical preprocessing, a parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) as a nonlinear latent block, and classical postprocessing for pair-selection probabilities. **Model / system.** A multi-qubit system (benchmarked on 4, 5, and 6-qubit tasks) undergoing a disentanglement scheduling process controlled by a hybrid agent. **Key observables.** Two-qubit reduced density matrices and pair-selection probabilities. **Important parameters / regimes.** Circuit width and circuit depth of the quantum module, and the availability of model budget. **Assumptions / limitations.** The controller has restricted access to the full wave-function and can only observe reduced density matrices. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of partial-observation control, describes the modular hybrid policy architecture, presents benchmarking results across different qubit numbers, and analyzes the performance-efficiency trade-offs and circuit scaling.
Abstract Quantum control with restricted state access is central to near-term quantum devices, where full wave-function information is unavailable. We study this problem through multiqubit disentanglement scheduling from partial observations, where a controller receives only two-qubit reduced density matrices and selects which qubit pair to disentangle at each step. We introduce a modular hybrid quantum--classical policy framework consisting of classical preprocessing, a parameterized quantum circuit as a compact nonlinear latent block, and classical postprocessing for pair-selection probabilities. Benchmarking 4-, 5-, and 6-qubit tasks, we find that preprocessing is the dominant factor governing performance under reduced-state observations, while the quantum module provides a conditional compact representation whose utility depends on the input features and model budget. We further identify a performance--efficiency trade-off across policy families and find that increasing circuit width is generally more useful than increasing depth. These results provide practical design principles for hybrid policies in reduced-information quantum control.

Quantum Coordination without Conditioning under Restricted Information

Authors: Faisal Shah Khan

Summary. This paper demonstrates that quantum correlations can enable coordination in sequential tasks where classical agents fail due to an inability to condition on past history. It identifies two distinct quantum mechanisms—shared latent variables and quantum discord—that allow for the implementation of otherwise unattainable joint distributions. Ultimately, it positions quantum correlations as a partial substitute for the classical property of perfect recall.

Why it may be interesting. It provides fundamental insights into how quantum resources like discord can compensate for missing information in sequential processes, which is relevant to understanding the limits of quantum control and communication in networked systems.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Investigating whether quantum correlations can implement joint distributions that are classically unattainable when agents are subject to information constraints like imperfect recall. **Main result.** Quantum systems can act as a partial substitute for perfect recall, using both classical latent-variable encoding in separable states and non-commuting local structures (quantum discord) to achieve coordination impossible for classical local rules. **Method.** The study uses a probabilistic framework comparing classical local conditional rules against quantum measurements (POVMs) on shared states, applying extensions of Kuhn's theorem to imperfect recall settings. **Model / system.** A multi-stage sequential decision process involving agents (Alice, Bob, etc.) sharing multipartite quantum states, including both separable/diagonal states and discordant states. **Key observables.** Target joint distributions (specifically anti-correlated distributions), quantum discord, and local measurement outcomes. **Important parameters / regimes.** Information constraints (restricted access to past history $Y_k$), presence of quantum discord, and the degree of non-commutativity in local measurement bases. **Assumptions / limitations.** The model assumes no signaling between agents and that quantum correlations are fixed in advance and cannot reproduce fully adaptive dependence on indistinguishable past outcomes. **Figures summary.** A tree representation of a binary sequential process where dashed lines indicate nodes that are indistinguishable due to information constraints. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of coordination under restricted information, defines the classical and quantum models, proves two theorems regarding diagonal and discordant mechanisms for coordination, and discusses the fundamental limitations of quantum models compared to perfect recall.
Abstract We study coordination under restricted information, where classical local models fail to implement certain correlated distributions because agents cannot condition on past history. We show that quantum systems overcome this limitation even when using only separable states. Both classically diagonal encodings (shared latent variables) and separable states with noncommuting local structure (quantum discord) enable the implementation of joint distributions that are unattainable by any classical local rules under the same information constraints. The quantum advantage arises from enabling latent-variable coordination without requiring agents to condition on the latent variable itself -- a construction that succeeds where no classical local model can. Separable states with nonzero quantum discord provide an alternative mechanism for realizing such coordination. At the same time, quantum models remain strictly limited by the information structure: unlike perfect recall, they cannot reproduce fully adaptive dependence on realized past outcomes that are observationally indistinguishable. Thus, quantum correlations serve as a partial substitute for perfect recall.

Structure-Aware Transformers for Learning Near-Optimal Trotter Orderings with System-Size Generalization in 1D Heisenberg Hamiltonians

Authors: Shamminuj Aktar, Reuben Tate, Stephan Eidenbenz
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27171v1

Summary. The paper presents a Transformer-based machine learning model designed to select the most accurate sequence of Hamiltonian terms for Trotterized quantum simulations. By using a size-invariant input representation, the model successfully generalizes from small training systems to much larger, unseen 1D Heisenberg chains. This approach significantly outperforms standard baseline policies and reduces the computational overhead of finding optimal simulation parameters.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides an AI-driven method to reduce approximation errors in quantum simulations, potentially enabling more accurate long-time evolution of complex spin systems on near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Optimizing the ordering of non-commuting Hamiltonian terms in Trotterization to maximize simulation fidelity, specifically addressing the exponential complexity of the search space and the need for system-size generalization. **Main result.** A structure-aware Transformer encoder can predict near-optimal Trotter orderings for unseen, larger systems (up to 20 qubits) with a mean fidelity gap of only 0.00115 relative to the best of 24 structured candidates. **Method.** The authors use a supervised learning approach with a Transformer encoder that takes size-invariant Hamiltonian features and global context as input to classify the best ordering from a predefined set of 24 candidates derived from commutation graph colorings. **Model / system.** The study focuses on 1D XXZ Heisenberg Hamiltonians with an external transverse field, decomposed into local Pauli terms, using the Néel state as the initial state for time evolution. **Key observables.** State fidelity (squared overlap between exact and Trotter-evolved states), mean fidelity gap, normalized gain, and poor-prediction rate. **Important parameters / regimes.** System size (L), Trotter orders (p=1, 2), step counts (r=3, 5), anisotropy (Delta), and transverse field strength (g). **Assumptions / limitations.** The search is restricted to a predefined set of 24 structured candidate orderings rather than the full permutation space; the study is limited to 1D XXZ Hamiltonians and a fixed target time. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 compares random vs. structured orderings; Figure 2 shows the dataset generation and model learning pipeline; Figure 3 shows the model tracking the oracle fidelity; Figure 4 shows the error landscape across field strengths; Figure 5 shows the generalization sweep across system sizes; Figure 6 shows sample efficiency. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the Trotter ordering problem, describes the construction of the candidate set via commutation graph coloring, details the Transformer architecture and size-invariant feature encoding, presents results on system-size generalization and sample efficiency, and concludes with limitations and future directions.
Abstract Trotterization is a standard approach for simulating quantum time evolution on quantum computers, where the Hamiltonian is split into local terms and each term is applied in sequence. The order of these terms affects the fidelity of the simulation when they do not commute, so the choice of ordering directly impacts the accuracy of the simulation. We study this problem for one-dimensional XXZ Heisenberg Hamiltonians using a structured set of 24 candidate orderings derived from colorings of the Hamiltonian's commutation graph and their group permutations. Finding the best candidate for large systems becomes prohibitive because fidelity evaluation is computationally expensive. In this work, we train a transformer encoder on smaller systems to predict the best candidate ordering for larger systems directly from Hamiltonian and Trotter-configuration features, without computing candidate fidelities at inference time. The model is trained on in-range systems of 3 to 14 qubits with 15-qubit systems held out for validation. Experimental results show that the model reaches a mean test fidelity gap of 0.00115 relative to the best of the 24 candidates on out-of-range systems of 16 to 20 qubits. A training-size sweep further shows that generalization emerges once training includes systems up to L=8 qubits, with validation at L=9, and the gap continues to decrease as the training range grows. To our knowledge, this is the first application of a learned model to Trotter ordering, and it motivates future work on AI-guided Trotter ordering with generalization across Hamiltonian families and system types.

Bosonic Josephson junction dynamics: interplay between quantum and thermal fluctuations

Authors: Andrea Bardin, Francesco Lorenzi, Luca Salasnich
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.27809v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

Summary. This paper extends the mean-field description of Bosonic Josephson junctions by incorporating both quantum and thermal fluctuations. It demonstrates that these two types of fluctuations act in opposition to influence the system's dynamics. The authors conclude that in current experimental setups, quantum fluctuations are the dominant correction.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a precise understanding of how fluctuations modify fundamental nonlinear phenomena like self-trapping and symmetry breaking, which is crucial for controlling many-body dynamics in ultracold gases.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Investigating how thermal and quantum fluctuations affect the superfluid dynamics of a Bosonic Josephson junction beyond the mean-field approximation. **Main result.** The study finds that quantum and thermal fluctuations play opposite roles: thermal fluctuations decrease the Josephson frequency, while both increase the thresholds for macroscopic quantum self-trapping and spontaneous symmetry breaking. **Method.** A semianalytical approach using a formalism that accounts for fluctuations in a homogeneous gas to derive a corrected equation of motion within a two-site approximation. **Model / system.** A Bosonic Josephson junction modeled within a two-site approximation, considering the dynamics of a superfluid gas. **Key observables.** Josephson frequency, strength of macroscopic quantum self-trapping, and the threshold for spontaneous symmetry breaking. **Important parameters / regimes.** Regimes where quantum fluctuations dominate over thermal fluctuations, assessed via parameters from recent experimental realizations. **Assumptions / limitations.** The transport of the non-condensed component is assumed to be negligible. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of fluctuations in Josephson junctions, presents a corrected equation of motion, analyzes the effects on dynamical quantities, and compares theoretical predictions with experimental regimes.
Abstract We investigate the superfluid dynamics of a Josephson junction beyond the mean-field description, incorporating the role of thermal fluctuations as well as quantum fluctuations. Using a formalism that accounts for the fluctuations in a homogeneous gas, and under the assumption that the transport of the non-condensed component is negligible, we derive a corrected equation of motion within the two-site approximation. The resulting corrections for the typical dynamical quantities, like the Josephson frequency, the strength of macroscopic quantum self-trapping, and the threshold for spontaneous symmetry breaking, allow us to predict the effects of both types of fluctuations and assess their relative importance in different regimes in a semianalytical fashion. For all the dynamical quantities, the quantum fluctuations are shown to play an opposite role with respect to the thermal fluctuations. Josephson frequency is decreased by thermal fluctuations and both the critical strenghts of macroscopic quantum self trapping and spontaneous symmetry breaking are increased. We assess the experimentally accessible regimes by calculating the relevant parameters of recent experimental realizations of Bosonic Josephson junction and show that the expected regime is dominated by quantum fluctuations.

Summary. This paper introduces a parallelized version of the HADOF framework to enable High Performance Quantum (HPQ) computing. By decomposing large optimization problems into smaller sub-problems that can be executed concurrently across multiple quantum processors, the method significantly reduces computation time without sacrificing solution quality.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The scalability of quantum optimization algorithms like QAOA is limited by the low qubit counts, hardware noise, and connectivity constraints of NISQ-era devices when tackling large-scale combinatorial problems. **Main result.** The parallelized HADOF framework achieves up to a 3-4x reduction in wall-clock time using multiple IBM QPUs and a 3x speedup on a single QPU, while maintaining competitive solution accuracy for genome assembly tasks. **Method.** The researchers extend the Hamiltonian Auto Decomposition Optimisation Framework (HADOF) by implementing inter-QPU and intra-QPU parallelization using joblib to solve decomposed sub-Hamiltonians independently. **Model / system.** The study uses Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) models applied to genome assembly (TSP-like problems) and benchmarks them on IBM Quantum processors (e.g., IBM_Kingston, IBM_Fez) and classical PennyLane simulations. **Key observables.** Wall clock time (makespan), QPU usage time, solution accuracy (approximation ratio), and probability distribution of sampled solutions. **Important parameters / regimes.** Sub-Hamiltonian size (k=5), circuit depth (5 layers), and problem size (n up to 500 for simulation, up to 50 for real hardware). **Assumptions / limitations.** The framework assumes that large global Hamiltonians can be effectively approximated by replacing inactive variables with their expected values from previous iterations. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows a standard QAOA circuit; Figure 2 illustrates Trotterized decomposition; Figure 3 provides an overview of the HADOF framework; Figure 8 and 9 show the DAG overlap graph and the assembled genome sequence. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the scalability bottleneck, presents the HADOF decomposition and its parallelized extension, details the experimental setup on IBM hardware, reports performance benchmarks across different execution modes, and validates the approach with a genome assembly case study.
Abstract Practical applicability of quantum optimisation on near term devices is constrained by limited qubit counts and hardware noise, which restricts the scalability of quantum optimisation algorithms for combinatorial problems. The simulation of large quantum circuits is also difficult and constrained by memory requirement. The Hamiltonian Auto Decomposition Optimisation Framework (HADOF) addresses this by decomposing large QUBOs into smaller subproblems that can be solved iteratively on quantum or classical backends. This allows the scalability of quantum QUBO algorithms beyond device limits, as well as their simulation on classical devices. In this research, we extend the evaluation of HADOF by benchmarking on real IBM QPUs across sequential, single-QPU parallel, and multi-QPU parallel execution modes, advancing toward High Performance Quantum (HPQ) computing for combinatorial optimisation problems. Experimental results on IBM quantum hardware demonstrate up to 3-4x reduction in wall clock time when utilising four QPUs compared to sequential execution baseline, while maintaining comparable solution quality. Notably, even single QPU execution benefits from parallelised job orchestration and execution, yielding up to 3x speedup. Simulated results predict over 5x speed-up in parallel execution mode. We further validate the practical applicability of the approach on real world genome assembly instances, showing that both sequential and parallel HADOF variants achieve competitive accuracy while significantly improving time to solution. These results highlight the importance of parallelism at both the algorithmic and system levels, positioning HADOF as a viable pathway toward scalable quantum optimisation.
