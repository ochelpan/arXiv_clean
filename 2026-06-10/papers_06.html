Summary. The paper explores how an electron-phonon interaction can induce phase separation within a topological superconductor model. This phase separation creates internal interfaces that host Majorana zero modes, allowing for the realization of these exotic quasiparticles in the bulk material. The findings suggest a pathway to engineer topological superconductivity using lattice coupling.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a concrete mechanism—phase separation driven by electron-lattice coupling—for realizing topological boundary states (Majorana fermions) in a bulk material without needing engineered physical terminations, which is highly relevant for solid-state quantum devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates whether Majorana fermions can emerge within the bulk of a superconducting system, away from physical boundaries, due to self-generated interfaces.

Main result. Majorana fermions are found to exist at internal interfaces formed when the superconducting material phase-separates into regions with distinct topological invariants. These interfaces host zero-energy modes even under periodic boundary conditions.

Method. The analysis involves generalizing the Kitaev model by coupling electron density to a classical elastic field, followed by solving self-consistency equations and analyzing the resulting phase diagram in real space.

Model / system. The system is modeled as a 1D topological superconductor (Kitaev model generalization) coupled to an elastic lattice field. The Hamiltonian includes hopping, pairing, chemical potential terms, and density-dependent coupling to the lattice.

Key observables. Zero-energy modes in the spectrum (Majorana fermions), local spectral density showing zero-bias peaks, and the phase diagram illustrating coexistence regions.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength ($\lambda$), electron density ($n$), stiffness ($K$), and superconducting gap parameters ($\Delta$, $t$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is performed at zero temperature ($T=0$) and often relies on simplifying the problem to homogeneous or dimerized phases in reciprocal space for initial calculations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate phase diagrams showing topological/non-topological coexistence regions, spectral plots confirming zero modes at interfaces, and density profiles demonstrating polaron trapping at these boundaries.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the generalized model Hamiltonian, deriving self-consistency conditions, analyzing the homogeneous phase transition, and finally using real-space numerical solutions to demonstrate Majorana formation within the coexistence region of the phase diagram.

Abstract

The Kitaev model describing a one-dimensional topological superconducting chain is known to support two Majorana fermions localized at the systems endpoints when the parameters are tuned to the topological phase. In this work, we investigate the possibility that Majorana fermions may also emerge away from the physical boundaries of the chain. To this purpose, we generalize the Kitaev model by incorporating a local coupling between the electronic density and a classical elastic (lattice) field. This electron-lattice interaction can induce phase separation between superconducting regions characterized by distinct topological invariants, thereby generating internal interfaces that host Majorana bound states. Under these conditions, a dilute gas of Majorana fermions can be realized in the bulk of the system.

Summary. This theoretical paper analyzes the transition from extended linear modes to localized states in finite lattices subjected to cubic FPUT interaction nonlinearity. By developing a general framework, the authors successfully extend prior methods to this complex nonlinear case. The key finding is the determination of a quantitative convergence radius $\epsilon = \Theta(1/\sqrt{n})$, providing deep insight into the stability and localization scale of these wave solutions.

Why it may be interesting. The study of localization transitions driven by nonlinearity is central to understanding nonlinear wave phenomena in physical systems, which has parallels with Anderson localization and energy band structure analysis relevant to quantum dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the mechanism by which time-periodic, spatially localized states form from an extended linear mode near a band edge when a nonlinearity is gradually introduced.

Main result. It establishes that this formation process can be quantitatively analyzed for the FPUT interaction nonlinearity, yielding a convergence radius $\epsilon = \Theta(1/\sqrt{n})$ below which the mode remains extended.

Method. The analysis employs perturbation theory combined with Lyapunov–Schmidt reduction to continue an orbit from the linear spectrum into the nonlinear regime.

Model / system. The system is a finite one-dimensional diatomic lattice governed by equations of motion involving both linear spring forces and cubic FPUT interaction nonlinearity. The method is claimed to be model-independent for other short-range nonlinearities and higher dimensions.

Key observables. Radius of convergence ($\epsilon$), frequency shift ($\omega_{<2>}$), and the profile concentration (middle vs. edge localization).

Important parameters / regimes. Chain length ($2n$), nonlinearity strength, and spectral gaps which scale as $\Theta(1/n^2)$ for finite lattices.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to the diatomic chain as a test case; it assumes specific structural relations between different nonlinearities (e.g., FPUT vs. on-site).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate schematic representations of the finite FPUT-type diatomic chain, showing the evolution of periodic orbits from extended states towards central or boundary concentrations.

Paper structure. The paper builds by first establishing a framework transferable from simple on-site nonlinearities to complex FPUT interactions. It then uses spectral analysis and perturbation theory (Lyapunov–Schmidt reduction) to calculate convergence radii, culminating in inductive estimates that confirm the $\Theta(1/\sqrt{n})$ scaling.

Abstract

We study how time-periodic, spatially localized states are born from the linear spectrum of a \emph{finite} lattice as the nonlinearity is switched on. In earlier work we treated this question for a diatomic chain with on-site nonlinearity and developed a framework that continues a near-edge linear mode in amplitude and controls the resulting perturbation series uniformly in the chain length. The present paper shows that the same framework applies to the more difficult case of Fermi--Pasta--Ulam--Tsingou (FPUT) interaction nonlinearity. The key is a structural relation between the FPUT and on-site nonlinearities, which allows the estimates obtained in the on-site setting to be transferred to the FPUT setting. As before, the analysis yields a quantitative radius of convergence, $\eps=Θ(1/\sqrt{n})$ for a chain of length $2n$, below which the near-edge mode stays extended and above which the orbit localizes and its frequency leaves the band. The diatomic chain is used only as a test case; both the formation mechanism and the method are model-independent and are expected to extend to other short-range nonlinearities and to higher dimensions.

Summary. This paper develops a symmetry-based framework to classify surface altermagnetism that remains robust even on rough crystal surfaces. It proves that this phenomenon is restricted to PT-symmetric antiferromagnets by analyzing the Surface Antisymmetry Laue Point Group (SALPG). The work provides a detailed classification scheme linking bulk symmetries and surface orientation to observable magnetic transport properties.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on condensed matter magnetism, the rigorous application of group theory (point/space groups) to determine emergent macroscopic properties from averaging over disordered geometries is highly relevant to understanding open quantum systems and boundary effects in AMO physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study addresses the symmetry governing macroscopic spin-momentum correlations (altermagnetism) that persist when averaging over compensated, rough surfaces, moving beyond flat terrace approximations.

Main result. Roughness-robust surface altermagnetism is restricted to PT-symmetric antiferromagnets, whose symmetries are classified by the Surface Antisymmetry Laue Point Group (SALPG).

Method. The analysis employs symmetry group theory, specifically deriving the SALPG from the bulk Antisymmetry Point Group (APG) using the little group of the surface normal.

Model / system. The system under study is PT-symmetric antiferromagnets (PT-AFMs), focusing on how surface termination and roughness affect magnetic symmetry properties. The theory models macroscopic spin-momentum correlations surviving averaging over rough surfaces.

Key observables. Altermagnetic classes (e.g., d-wave, SD-SAM) determined by the SALPG; spin splitting magnitude and direction.

Important parameters / regimes. The surface normal vector ($\hat{n}$); the bulk Antisymmetry Point Group (APG).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes nonrelativistic properties and that macroscopic responses are averaged over an unbiased ensemble of rough surfaces, which restricts the phenomenon to PT-AFMs.

Figures summary. Table I provides a comprehensive classification linking SALPGs, altermagnetic classes, bulk APGs, and surface normals for PT-AFMs.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes that roughness averaging necessitates using the SALPG. It then derives constraints showing that only PT-AFMs support this robust effect. Finally, it provides a detailed classification table linking symmetry groups to observable altermagnetic states.

Abstract

Surface altermagnetism extends spin splitting beyond bulk altermagnets through symmetry reduction at surfaces and interfaces. An existing classification applies to the local symmetry of atomically flat surface terraces. The present paper addresses the symmetry of macroscopic spin-momentum correlations that survive averaging over compensated rough surfaces. These correlations are governed by the surface antisymmetry Laue point group. Rough-surface altermagnetism is forbidden at any surface of a magnet whose antisymmetry space group contains antitranslations, and the classification therefore reduces to $PT$-symmetric antiferromagnets. By restoring all symmetries leaving the surface normal invariant, roughness can generate compensated surface altermagnets from uncompensated flat terminations, increase the surface symmetry, or suppress spin splitting. By combining bulk switchability with altermagnetic surface transport properties, roughness-robust surface altermagnetism in $PT$-symmetric antiferromagnets provides a route toward spintronic functionality.

Summary. This work develops a novel electrical spectroscopy technique to measure the intervalley relaxation time ($\tau_{iv}$) in WSe$_2$ transistors. By analyzing three complementary signatures—AC transconductance, current transients, and hysteresis—the authors provide quantitative access to $\tau_{iv}$ using standard electronic measurements rather than complex ultrafast optics.

Why it may be interesting. The use of dynamic response functions ($g_m(\omega)$) to probe relaxation rates ($\tau_{iv}$) is a classic problem in open quantum systems, linking transport measurements directly to the system's coherence or decay timescales.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To establish a direct electrical spectroscopic method for measuring the intervalley relaxation time ($\tau_{iv}$) in WSe$_2$ transistors, bypassing reliance on ultrafast optical techniques.

Main result. Three distinct electrical signatures—frequency-dependent transconductance, current transients, and sweep-rate hysteresis—were shown to provide quantitative access to $\tau_{iv}$.

Method. The analysis extends an equilibrium valley-thermodynamics framework by modeling the time evolution of the $\Gamma$-valley carrier fraction using a single relaxation equation.

Model / system. The system is multilayer WSe$_2$ field-effect transistors, where the valley population acts as a dynamic internal state variable governed by the relaxation rate $\tau_{iv}$.

Key observables. Frequency-dependent transconductance $g_m(\omega)$, two-stage current transients (overshoot/undershoot), and sweep-rate-proportional hysteresis.

Important parameters / regimes. Intervalley relaxation time ($\tau_{iv}$); characteristic frequency $\omega_c = 1/\tau_{iv}$; temperature dependence following a Bose–Einstein form.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on extending the equilibrium valley-thermodynamics framework and using first-order approximations for slow gate sweeps to derive the hysteresis current.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the schematic measurement setup, the layer-dependent overshoot/undershoot in transients, Lorentzian peaks in $g_m(\omega)$ at $\omega_c$, and linear scaling of hysteresis current with sweep rate $|r|$.

Paper structure. The paper develops a theoretical framework based on a relaxation equation for valley population, predicts three measurable electrical signatures (AC spectroscopy, DC transients, hysteresis), and demonstrates how these signatures allow quantitative extraction of $\tau_{iv}$ using standard RF/DC equipment.

Abstract

We show that the transconductance of multilayer WSe$_2$ field-effect transistors serves as a direct electrical spectrometer of the intervalley relaxation time $τ_{\rm iv}$, previously accessible only by ultrafast optical techniques. Extending an equilibrium valley-thermodynamics framework with a single relaxation equation for the $Γ$-valley carrier fraction $f_Γ(t)$, we predict three signatures: (i)~a Lorentzian transconductance $g_m(ω)=g_{m,0}+g_{m,v}^0/(1+iωτ_{\rm iv})$, whose imaginary part peaks at $ω_c=τ_{\rm iv}^{-1}$ with opposite signs for bilayer and trilayer; (ii)~a two-stage current transient after a gate step, exhibiting bilayer overshoot or trilayer undershoot; and (iii)~sweep-rate-proportional hysteresis whose gate-voltage profile and layer-number sign reversal distinguish valley from trap-induced dynamics. All three signatures provide quantitative electrical access to $τ_{\rm iv}$ with standard rf and dc instrumentation.

Abstract

A distinctive feature of many quasiperiodic systems is the presence of critical states that are neither extended nor exponentially localized. We investigate the geometric effect on the superfluid weight in quasiperiodic systems with critical states at zero temperature. We find that the superfluid weight is dominated by the geometric contribution in quasiperiodic systems with critical states. This finding reveals a fundamental interplay between superconductivity and critical states in quasicrystals.

Summary. This work demonstrates that applying controlled strain to WSe2-MoSe2 moiré heterostructures acts as a powerful tool to break rotational symmetry. This symmetry breaking results in two key effects: the emergence of linearly polarized interlayer exciton emission, and highly anisotropic diffusion pathways for these excitons. Why it may be interesting. The ability to use mechanical strain as a precise knob to engineer electronic band structure symmetries and control quantum transport pathways is highly relevant for designing artificial quantum materials and excitonic devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To explore strain tuning as a versatile tool for designing moiré superlattices and programming excitonic transport in van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures.

Main result. Strain significantly breaks the $C_3$ rotational symmetry of the moiré lattice, leading to measurable linear polarization and anisotropic diffusion of interlayer excitons along the strain direction.

Method. The study combines optical measurements (PL spectroscopy for polarization and diffusion mapping) with theoretical modeling (solving Fokker-Planck equations for exciton density evolution).

Model / system. The physical system is WSe2-MoSe2 heterostructures forming moiré superlattices. The key mechanism involves applying controlled uniaxial or biaxial strain to modify the lattice geometry and break inherent symmetries.

Important parameters / regimes. The magnitude and type of applied uniaxial/biaxial strain (e.g., 0.2% strain).

Paper structure. The paper establishes the potential of moiré heterostructures, details how strain breaks symmetry leading to polarized emission, and then quantifies this effect by showing strain-controlled anisotropic exciton diffusion both experimentally and via simulation.

Abstract

Moiré superlattices in van der Waals heterostructures have recently gained significant attention as an intriguing platform for studying correlated electronic systems and exotic excitonic properties. Previous reports, however, focused on creating and modulating moiré heterostructures through interlayer twisting or lattice constant mismatches, limiting controls on symmetry of heterostructures. In this work, we show that strain significantly alters the geometry of moiré superlattices by breaking the C3 rotational symmetry. We realize strain-induced moiré superlattices by intentionally regulating interlayer strain in WSe2-MoSe2 heterostructures, which is manifested by linearly polarized interlayer exciton emission coupled to the strain direction. Furthermore, interlayer exciton diffusion was preferentially guided along the stretched moiré superlattice orientations over a wide spatial range, reflecting the strain-modified moiré potentials. Our work highlights strain tuning as a versatile tool for designing moiré superlattices and programming excitonic transport, which opens pathways for van der Waals logic and information processing devices.

Summary. This theoretical paper calculates the intrinsic superheating field limit ($B_{sh}$) for clean superconductors like Niobium using advanced Eilenberger theory. Abstract

We calculate the low-temperature superheating field $B_{\rm sh}$ of clean superconductors near the boundary between type-I and type-II superconductivity, with particular emphasis on Nb. The calculation is based on the self-consistent nonlinear nonlocal Eilenberger theory and the linear stability analysis of the Meissner state. For a Nb-like material with $κ_{\rm GL}=0.7$, we obtain $B_{\rm sh}\simeq 290\,{\rm mT}$ at $T/T_c=0.2$, using $B_{c0}\simeq 200\,{\rm mT}$. This value is substantially higher than the value obtained by naively extrapolating the Ginzburg--Landau result near $T_c$ to $T\ll T_c$. For a TESLA-shaped Nb accelerator cavity, it corresponds to an intrinsic Meissner-stability limit of about $67\,{\rm MV/m}$.

Summary. This work analyzes the topology and edge states of extended-range Kitaev chains by calculating the winding number and tracking parity changes. Why it may be interesting. The detailed study of edge mode localization via $ ext{Map}$ provides a novel diagnostic tool that complements standard energy gap measurements for characterizing topological phases in superconducting systems, which is highly relevant to quantum materials research.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the topological properties of extended-range Kitaev chain models, particularly focusing on how these properties are characterized when the topological winding number is preserved.

Main result. The analysis reveals that in truncated-range scenarios, the number of distinct topological phases scales with the number of coupled neighbors. Method. The authors employ analytical derivations to calculate the topological invariant (winding number) and utilize numerical diagonalization on finite-size clusters in the Majorana basis formalism.

Model / system. The system is a spinless fermion model described by an extended-range Hamiltonian, generalized from the Kitaev chain with couplings decaying algebraically ($\Delta_\ell \sim 1/\ell^\alpha$, $t_\ell \sim 1/\ell^eta$).

Key observables. Topological winding number ($ u$), effective parity ($P_{ ext{eff}}$), ground-state fermion parity ($P$), and the Majorana average position ($ ext{Map}$).

Abstract

The topological properties of the Kitaev chain model with extended-range interactions are investigated, focusing on cases where the topological winding number is preserved. We assume that the pairing and hopping terms decay algebraically in space with exponents $α$ and $β$, respectively. We show that in the truncated-range scenario, there are as many distinct topological phases as the number of coupled neighboring sites. In addition, an explicit analytical formulation is provided to evaluate the topological invariant and the phase transitions that emerge in these systems. Besides the analytical description, we introduce a new physical insight into the topological excitations of the ground-state by measuring the spatial distribution of the edge modes with the Majorana average position. Taking the next-nearest neighbor Kitaev chains as a probe, various numerical calculations of Majorana edge states were performed in finite-size clusters to determine the sensitivity of the topological zero energy modes to the parameters of interest. The occupation of edge-to-edge non-local fermion states is computed and defined as an effective parity. Such an effective parity exhibits new interesting features beyond the energetic exchange from the ground-state fermion parity switches, which are related to the distribution of the respective edge modes. Our calculations show a direct correlation between the ground-state fermion parity and the edge occupation numbers, which are translated into localization and delocalization of the Majorana average position.

Summary. This work provides an exact analytical calculation of Cherenkov radiation in anisotropic chiral matter using modified electrodynamics. The key finding is the demonstration of threshold-free CHR for slow charges, which occurs within a specific low-frequency band. Main problem. The study revisits Cherenkov radiation (CHR) in anisotropic chiral matter, aiming to provide an exact calculation that reveals a novel phenomenon: threshold-free emission.

Method. The authors solve modified Maxwell's equations exactly in the space-frequency domain using cylindrical coordinates and separation of variables, deriving dispersion relations from outgoing wave boundary conditions.

Abstract

We explore Cherenkov radiation in anisotropic chiral matter within the framework of Carroll-Field-Jackiw electrodynamics, where the axion angle exhibits a linear dependence on position. By deriving closed-form expressions for the polarization modes of electromagnetic fields in cylindrical coordinates and the space-frequency domain, we solve the modified Maxwell's equations. To enforce causality, we impose outgoing wave boundary conditions at a cylindrical surface at infinity, which yields the dispersion relations. Our analysis uncovers the specific angles and frequency ranges that allow for zero, one, or two Cherenkov cones. We also obtain the spectral energy distribution of the radiation in all cases. Notably, one sector of the model exhibits a novel phenomenon: Cherenkov radiation can be generated by slowly moving charges without a threshold, but only within a specific frequency range. This behavior is not observed in standard materials.
