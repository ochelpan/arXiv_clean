Summary. This paper introduces 'kinetically constrained superradiance,' a phenomenon where atomic interactions split superradiant decay into multiple frequency-dependent channels. This mechanism breaks permutation symmetry, allowing the system to bypass the trivial steady state of standard Dicke superradiance and instead stabilize long-lived, entangled many-body states. It demonstrates that dissipation alone can be used as a resource for engineering correlated quantum states.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a novel mechanism for dissipative state engineering where purely dissipative dynamics (without coherent driving) can generate and stabilize complex, many-body entangled states, which is highly relevant for quantum memory and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to overcome the limitation of conventional Dicke superradiance, where permutation symmetry leads to a trivial steady state, by using configuration-dependent energy shifts to engineer long-lived entanglement and trapped excitations.

Main result. The discovery of kinetically constrained superradiance, where interactions split superradiance into multiple frequency-resolved decay channels, allowing the system to reach a non-trivial steady state with stabilized, long-lived entanglement and trapped excitations.

Method. The authors use a Lindblad master equation formalism derived via the Born-Markov and Redfield approximations, supplemented by stochastic quantum trajectory simulations to study entanglement and steady-state properties.

Model / system. A one-dimensional array of N two-level atoms with nearest-neighbor interactions (Hamiltonian includes a transition energy delta and interaction strength J) coupled to a vacuum electromagnetic field in a subwavelength arrangement.

Key observables. Frequency-resolved emission rates (I_xi), total emission intensity, peak intensity, burst width, delay time, steady-state excitation density, and half-chain von Neumann entropy.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (J), transition energy (delta), number of atoms (N), and the regime of strong interaction (J >= delta) versus weak interaction (J << delta).

Assumptions / limitations. Subwavelength arrangement of atoms (d << lambda), Born-Markov approximation, and the use of the Rotating-Wave/Secular approximation when interaction-induced splittings are large.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the schematic of collective coupling and conditional spin-flip processes; Figure 4 compares entanglement entropy evolution between constrained and Dicke superradiance; Supplementary figures show frequency-resolved emission components, scaling of burst parameters, and steady-state excitation density.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of kinetically constrained superradiance, provides a microscopic derivation of the frequency-resolved Lindblad master equation, analyzes the resulting sequential relaxation dynamics and scaling laws, and concludes with an investigation of steady-state entanglement and excitation trapping.

Abstract

We introduce kinetically constrained superradiance, a form of cooperative emission in which interactions imprint configuration-dependent energy shifts on optical transitions, splitting Dicke superradiance into multiple, frequency-resolved collective decay channels. Each channel selectively radiates from distinct many-body spin configurations, generating a hierarchy of dissipative time scales and sequential relaxation dynamics. Unlike conventional superradiance, where permutation symmetry enforces relaxation to a trivial steady state, configuration-selective emission can trap finite-momentum spin-wave excitations and stabilize long-lived entanglement. Remarkably, these correlations are generated purely by dissipation in the absence of entangling coherent dynamics. Our results point to modern superradiant experiments as scalable resources for dissipative engineering of correlated quantum states.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that quenched disorder does not alter the fundamental existence of measurement-induced phase transitions in fermionic systems. While disorder modifies the effective parameters of the system, the transition remains absent in 1D and present in higher dimensions. The study provides a unified effective field theory describing both clean and disordered monitored fermions.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a rigorous field-theoretic proof that static disorder cannot stabilize a measurement-induced critical phase in 1D, clarifying the robustness of MIPTs.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how quenched disorder affects measurement-induced phase transitions (MIPTs) in noninteracting fermionic systems, specifically whether disorder can induce or stabilize a transition in 1D.

Main result. The authors find that disorder does not change the existence of the MIPT; a transition exists in d > 1 between an area x log law and an area law phase, but the 1D system remains strictly in an area law phase. The effective field theory (NLsigmaM) for disordered fermions is identical to the clean case, with disorder only modifying model parameters.

Method. The work employs a Replica Keldysh Path Integral approach to derive an effective two-matrix nonlinear sigma model (NLsigmaM) and uses Renormalization Group (RG) analysis alongside large-scale numerical simulations of a quantum projection protocol.

Model / system. A d-dimensional noninteracting fermionic system subject to both quenched on-site disorder and continuous monitoring of the local particle density.

Key observables. Von Neumann entanglement entropy, particle number cumulants, particle number covariance, and the average-density response function.

Important parameters / regimes. Measurement strength (kappa), disorder strength (related to elastic scattering time), spatial dimension (d), and measurement-induced vs. disorder-induced decay rates (gamma_m and gamma_D).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is noninteracting (free fermions), the initial state is Gaussian, and the derivation assumes a continuum limit and neglects certain nonlocal-in-time massive modes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows steady-state covariance vs. measurement rate for various disorder strengths and system sizes, with crossing points indicating the phase behavior. Figure 2 tracks the drift of finite-size crossing points toward zero measurement rate to demonstrate the absence of a transition in 1D.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MIPT in disordered systems, develops a Keldysh field theory and NLsigmaM derivation, performs RG analysis to predict phase stability, and concludes with numerical verification in 1D.

Abstract

Measurement-induced phase transitions are nonequilibrium transitions between phases characterized by distinct entanglement scaling behaviors, driven by the competition between unitary dynamics and measurements. Despite recent numerical efforts, how quenched disorder affects these transitions remains unclear. In this work, we study a $d$-dimensional noninteracting fermionic system subject to both quenched disorder and continuous monitoring of the local particle density, and derive an effective field theory describing its long-time universal behaviors. We find that the system is governed by the same nonlinear sigma model as in the case of clean monitored fermions, with disorder entering only through a modification of model parameters. This result suggests that the presence or absence of a measurement-induced phase transition is unaffected by the introduction of disorder: in spatial dimensions d>1, a transition occurs between an area x log law phase and an area law phase, whereas in d=1, the system exhibits only an area law phase and no transition. Numerical results further demonstrate that both clean and disordered one-dimensional free fermions exhibit area-law behavior when the system size is large enough.

Summary. This paper presents a new graph-theoretic method to find and classify quantum many-body scars in frustrated Rydberg atom arrays. It identifies two distinct mechanisms—Type-I and Type-II—that allow non-thermal dynamics to persist even in non-bipartite lattices. The results offer a roadmap for using complex lattice geometries to control and stabilize many-body quantum dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a systematic, dimension-insensitive way to engineer non-thermal dynamics in complex, frustrated geometries, which is highly relevant for designing quantum simulators and protecting information from thermalization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether and how quantum many-body scars (QMBS) survive in frustrated, non-bipartite Rydberg atom arrays and identifying the initial states that lead to non-thermal dynamics.

Main result. The authors introduce a graph-theoretic framework that identifies two distinct scarring mechanisms: Type-I (using local entanglement to overcome mild frustration) and Type-II (using strong frustration to pin part of the lattice).

Method. A graph-theoretic approach using vertex partitioning and clique covers, combined with algebraic construction of an approximate su(2) algebra and numerical simulations of return fidelity.

Model / system. Rydberg atom arrays in various 2D and 3D geometries (e.g., hexagonal, Shastry-Sutherland, asanoha, kagome, and triangular lattices) described by the PXP model in the blockade regime.

Key observables. Return fidelity, entanglement entropy, sublattice occupation, and overlap with dimer Neel states.

Important parameters / regimes. Levels of lattice frustration, connectivity/coordination numbers of sublattices, and driving frequency adjustments.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes that the dynamics can be captured by an approximate su(2) algebra and that the lattice can be partitioned into specific sublattices (A, B, and C).

Abstract

Quantum many-body scars in Rydberg atom arrays have thus far only been observed on bipartite lattices, leaving open the question of whether and how they survive frustration, and what the appropriate initial states are that lead to nonthermal dynamics. We introduce a graph-theoretic framework to find suitable candidates for scarring on arbitrary lattices. Our framework predicts two distinct mechanisms: type-I scars generalize the bipartite case by using locally entangled states to overcome mild frustration, while type-II scars exploit strong frustration to pin part of the lattice, leaving the remainder to oscillate freely. We numerically demonstrate both mechanisms and uncover an exponential family of scarred trajectories on the hexagonal lattice that can encode information protected from thermalization. Our results establish scarring as a generic feature of Rydberg systems beyond one dimension and provide an experimentally accessible route to systematically probing non-thermal dynamics in quantum simulators.

Summary. The paper shows that changing the geometry of a lattice from a 1D chain to a triangle-decorated structure can induce quantum many-body scars. This happens because the new geometry eliminates certain pathways in the Hilbert space, allowing for periodic revivals and slow entanglement growth. The findings are directly applicable to controlling dynamics in programmable Rydberg atom simulators.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a new design principle for engineering non-ergodic dynamics using only lattice connectivity, which is highly relevant for controlling many-body dynamics and information scrambling in programmable Rydberg atom arrays.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Can lattice geometry alone be used as a control knob to induce and stabilize quantum many-body scars (QMBS) in otherwise thermalizing systems without requiring fine-tuned initial states or Hamiltonian perturbations?

Main result. The authors demonstrate that a triangle-decorated lattice geometry enhances scarring by restructuring the constrained Hilbert space into hypercube subgraphs, which stabilizes an emergent approximate su(2) algebra and suppresses the 'bridges' that cause thermalization.

Method. The study uses Exact Diagonalization (ED) for numerical simulations and an algebraic approach involving an approximate su(2) algebra and subspace variance analysis, alongside a proposed experimental implementation using Rydberg atom arrays.

Model / system. The study focuses on the PXP model, which describes Rydberg atom arrays in the nearest-neighbor blockade regime, specifically comparing a standard 1D chain to a quasi-1D triangle-decorated lattice.

Key observables. Fidelity revivals F(t), fidelity density, bipartite von Neumann entanglement entropy, excitation density, and energy-level spacing ratio.

Important parameters / regimes. Rydberg blockade radius of 3.5 micrometers, interatomic spacing of 2.5 micrometers, and the transition from 1D chains to triangle-decorated lattices.

Assumptions / limitations. The PXP model is assumed to be a valid approximation for the Rydberg Hamiltonian in the blockade regime; the study focuses on the polarized state specifically.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the triangle-decorated lattice geometry and its dynamics (revivals and entanglement); Figure 4 illustrates unit cells with d decorations, showing how increasing decorations enhances revivals but leads toward integrability.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of stabilizing QMBS, presents the triangle-decorated geometry, analyzes the mechanism via Hilbert space restructuring and su(2) algebra, proposes an experimental Rydberg implementation, and discusses the scaling of the effect with unit cell size.

Abstract

Quantum many-body scars enable persistent non-ergodic dynamics in otherwise thermalizing systems, yet their stabilization typically relies on fine-tuned initial states or engineered Hamiltonian perturbations. Here we show that lattice geometry alone can serve as a powerful and experimentally accessible control knob for inducing and enhancing scarring. By transforming a one-dimensional chain into a quasi-one-dimensional triangle-decorated lattice, we find that the fully polarized state -- normally thermalizing in the PXP model -- exhibits pronounced fidelity revivals, slow entanglement growth, and strong overlap with a tower of weakly entangled eigenstates. We trace this behavior to a geometry-induced restructuring of the constrained Hilbert space, whereby the adjacency graph decomposes into hypercube subgraphs that enforce coherent population transfer and stabilize an emergent approximate $\mathrm{su}(2)$ algebra. We propose a direct implementation in programmable arrays of tweezer-trapped Rydberg atoms, where the triangle-decorated geometry can be realized using spatial light modulators and the resulting scarring dynamics probed via time-resolved measurements of excitation density. Our results establish lattice connectivity as a design principle for engineering non-ergodic dynamics in constrained quantum systems.

Summary. The paper introduces Universal Analog Quantum Simulation (UAQS), a method to make fixed-interaction analog quantum simulators programmable. By using optimized continuous-time control pulses, it allows hardware to emulate Hamiltonians beyond its native interaction structure. This approach is shown to be effective for simulating complex dynamics and preparing ground states in systems like Rydberg atoms and superconducting circuits.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a systematic way to extend the utility of existing analog hardware (like Rydberg arrays) to simulate a much wider range of Hamiltonians without the heavy overhead of full digital gate decomposition.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Analog quantum simulators are limited by fixed hardware-defined interaction structures, which restricts their programmability and ability to simulate arbitrary Hamiltonians.

Main result. The UAQS framework successfully transforms fixed-interaction analog devices into programmable simulators capable of accurately reproducing complex many-body dynamics, including real-time and imaginary-time evolution.

Method. A hybrid framework using continuous-time quantum control optimization and McLachlan's variational principle to project target dynamics onto a low-dimensional manifold of states.

Model / system. The framework is demonstrated on superconducting circuits (transmon arrays) and Rydberg-atom arrays, simulating models such as the Transverse-Field Ising Model (TFIM), XY model, Heisenberg model, and Fermi-Hubbard model.

Key observables. Nearest-neighbor correlations, fidelity, spectral functions, Out-of-Time-Order Correlators (OTOCs), and ground-state energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Control pulse amplitudes, evolution time (T), number of tunable parameters (d), and control strength (theta_max * T).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the Hamiltonian can be decomposed into controllable Pauli strings and that the initial state has non-zero overlap with the target state.

Paper structure. Abstract

Analog quantum simulators emulate complex many-body dynamics through native continuous-time evolution under hardware-defined interactions. Yet once a platform is specified, its interaction structure is largely fixed by the underlying hardware, restricting the Hamiltonians that can be realized and limiting programmability. Here we introduce universal analog quantum simulation (UAQS), a hybrid framework that systematically expands the range of accessible quantum evolutions within a given analog platform. UAQS employs optimized continuous-time control fields to engineer target dynamics directly, avoiding decomposition into discrete gate sequences. By preserving native analog evolution while extending the set of achievable Hamiltonians, UAQS transforms fixed-interaction analog devices into programmable simulators. Numerical studies on representative architectures, including superconducting circuits and Rydberg-atom arrays, show that UAQS accurately reproduces non-trivial many-body dynamics beyond the intrinsic interaction structure of the hardware. These results establish UAQS as a practical route toward programmable analog quantum simulation.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that second-order quantum criticality can emerge at the spinodal point of first-order quantum phase transitions due to emergent symmetries in a decoupled Hilbert subspace. By mapping FOQPT dynamics to effective SOQPT models, the authors bridge the gap between different types of phase transition dynamics. Abstract

Universality and scaling are hallmarks of second-order phase transitions but are generally unexpected in first-order quantum phase transitions (FOQPTs). We present a microscopic theory showing that quantum criticality can emerge around the quantum spinodal point of FOQPTs where metastability disappears. We demonstrate that, at this instability, resonant local excitations dynamically decouple a Hilbert subspace characterized by an emergent discrete translational symmetry. Projecting the original Hamiltonian onto this subspace yields an effective Hamiltonian that exhibits a genuine second-order quantum phase transition (SOQPT) and the Kibble-Zurek scaling. We validate this framework in the tilted Ising chain which breaks Z_2 symmetry, and predict the absence of criticality in the staggered-field PXP model. This work indicates that the FOQPT dynamics is usually governed by an emergent critical point around the quantum spinodal point. Our study establishes a bridge between the dynamics of the FOQPT and SOQPT, and thus sheds new light on the long-standing conundrum of the dynamics of the FOQPT.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that macroscopic friction and viscosity in isolated quantum systems are emergent phenomena resulting from temporal coarse-graining. Main problem. How macroscopic irreversible hydrodynamics, such as friction and viscosity, emerge from the reversible, unitary dynamics of isolated quantum many-body systems.

Main result. Macroscopic friction and viscosity are not intrinsic microscopic properties but emerge through temporal coarse-gasting, which averages out reversible microscopic fluctuations.

Method. A data-driven framework combining exact-derivative generalized Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (gEDMD) with the Mori-Zwanzig projection formalism.

Abstract

Understanding the emergence of macroscopic irreversible hydrodynamics from the reversible unitary dynamics of isolated quantum many-body systems remains a fundamental challenge. Conventional approaches often force spin density dynamics into purely diffusive models, obscuring the microscopic interplay of pressure, spin current, and local friction. Furthermore, reconciling true irreversibility with strictly unitary evolution raises profound questions about the role of the observer's temporal resolution. In this paper, we introduce a fully data-driven framework based on generalized Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (gEDMD) integrated with the Mori-Zwanzig projection. By expanding the observable dictionary to explicitly include spin currents, we directly extract the Navier-Stokes hydrodynamic coefficients from a chaotic XXZ spin chain across varying temporal coarse-graining scales. Our unconstrained extraction reveals a profound physical dichotomy: the mechanical elasticity ($c^2$) is intrinsically derived from the exact unitary dynamics, preserving strict microscopic reversibility. In stark contrast, the macroscopic friction ($γ$) and kinematic viscosity ($ν$) exhibit zero net dissipation, oscillating rapidly around zero in the exact-derivative limit. We demonstrate that genuine macroscopic transport cannot be established without finite temporal coarse-graining. By introducing a finite observation timescale ($Δt_{\rm cg} > 0$), the system passes through a distinct crossover timescale where these reversible fluctuations average out, establishing an intermediate functional regime that yields strictly positive friction and viscosity. Our results clearly demonstrate that macroscopic friction in isolated quantum systems is not an absolute property, but fundamentally an emergent phenomenon dictated by the temporal resolution of the observer.

Abstract

Abstract

Far-field optical imaging inevitably involves low-pass spatial filtering, limiting the resolution. Moreover, conventional imaging suppresses high spatial frequency components close to the cutoff, making them invisible under noise, particularly the shot noise arising from discrete and random nature of quantum light. Here we propose and implement a method for reducing the effect of this noise by optically pre-processing the incoming light prior to detection, thereby optimizing the quantum measurement performed on it. Our scheme, termed Fourier Domain Division (FDD), partitions the Fourier plane into multiple regions for independent detection and subsequent post-processing for image reconstruction. By analyzing the quantum and classical Fisher information, we show that our method is advantageous with respect to direct imaging for high spatial-frequency components. As a result, the number of photons required to achieve a certain signal-to-noise-ratio in the Fourier domain is reduced, thus enhancing the overall resolution in the photon-starved regime. We demonstrate our method in microscopy, achieving 5-fold improvement of Fisher information on high spatial-frequency components. Unlike active super-resolution methods, FDD is passive, making it broadly applicable in microscopy and other imaging scenarios where active illumination is impractical, including astronomy and remote sensing. Our work establishes a general strategy for designing quantum optimized superresolution imaging systems, bridging fundamental quantum limits, practical image analysis and computer vision applications.
