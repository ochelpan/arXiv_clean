A Residual-Based Quantum Linear System Algorithm with Dynamic Stopping and Applications to Elliptic PDEs

Authors: Xiantao Li
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06414v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces an adaptive quantum algorithm for solving linear systems arising from elliptic PDEs. By incorporating a residual register into the quantum dynamics, the algorithm can stop computation early once convergence is detected, significantly improving efficiency over worst-case-based schedules.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Standard Quantum Linear System Algorithms (QLSAs) lack an a posteriori mechanism to signal convergence, often requiring fixed runtimes based on conservative worst-case spectral information.

Main result. The authors develop a residual-based QLSA with a dynamic stopping rule that uses a co-evolving residual register to detect convergence, potentially reducing circuit depth and gate count for smooth right-hand sides.

Method. The algorithm uses an ODE-based accumulator approach where the linear system is formulated as a time-evolution problem, augmented with residual variables to enable on-the-fly monitoring via checkpoint circuits.

Model / system. The algorithm is applied to the discretization of second-order elliptic Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) using a Galerkin finite element method, specifically targeting the structure A_h = G_h^dagger G_h.

Key observables. The residual probability (p_res), which serves as a convergence indicator, and the relative error of the solution vector.

Important parameters / regimes. The mesh size (h), the condition number of the matrix, the target precision (epsilon), and the normalization scale of the block encoding (alpha_G).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the existence of a sparse-access block encoding for the gradient operator and relies on the exponential stability of the underlying ODE dynamics.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares different QLSA approaches for a 2D Poisson problem; Figure 2 shows the workflow for the checkpoint and production run; Figure 3 displays the finite element mesh; Figure 4 illustrates the decay of error and residual probability.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of fixed-runtime QLSAs, proposes an augmented ODE-based solver, provides mathematical proofs for stability and complexity, and validates the method with numerical experiments on a 2D Poisson problem.

Abstract

Quantum linear-system algorithms (QLSAs) have rigorous worst-case complexity guarantees, but their runtimes are often chosen from spectral information assumed in advance. What is largely lacking is an a posteriori progress flag: most QLSA workflows, unlike the classical counterparts, do not provide a built-in mechanism to signal whether a particular instance has already converged. For discretizations of elliptic PDEs $-\nabla\cdot(a(x)\nabla u(x))=f(x),$ with divergence--gradient structure \[ -\nabla\cdot \big(a(x)\nabla) \approx A_h=G_h^\dagger G_h, \] we formulate a stable first-order ODE whose limiting solution block is the desired Galerkin solution. The PDE-dependent scale is then \(\norm{G_h}=\bigO(h^{-1})\), comparable to factorized QLSA constructions with square-root condition-number scaling. We design an augmented dynamics with residual variables, in which measuring a residual register gives an on-the-fly convergence indicator without reconstructing the solution vector. For smooth right-hand sides, dynamic stopping can reduce the evolution time and gate count relative to a fixed worst-case schedule, and may also reduce exposure to accumulated hardware errors. Numerical experiments for a two-dimensional finite element Poisson problem show that the residual-register probability follows the actual error decay and, for some right-hand sides, can stop the quantum circuit well before a conservative worst-case runtime estimate is reached.

Affine Subcode Ensemble Decoding for Degeneracy-Aware Quantum Error Correction

Authors: Leo Wursthorn, Jonathan Mandelbaum, Sisi Miao, Hedongliang Liu, Holger Jäkel, Stergios Koutsioumpas, Laurent Schmalen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06547v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new decoding technique called Affine Subcode Ensemble Decoding (aSCED) designed to handle the degeneracy issues in QLDPC codes. By using additional parity checks to split large degeneracy sets, the method improves the convergence of Belief Propagation and achieves lower logical error rates than existing decoders.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The inherent degeneracy in Quantum Low-Density Parity-Check (QLDPC) codes causes Belief Propagation (BP) decoders to oscillate or fail to converge, leading to high logical error rates.

Main result. The proposed aSCED framework significantly improves decoding convergence and reduces logical error rates, outperforming standard MWPM and BP-OSD decoders on Toric and Generalized Bicycle codes.

Method. The authors extend the classical Affine Subcode Ensemble Decoding (aSCED) to the quantum setting by appending linearly independent 'splitter' rows to the check matrix and using an ensemble of parallel decoding paths with overcomplete matrices.

Model / system. The study focuses on stabilizer codes, specifically the Toric code and Generalized Bicycle (GB) codes, under a depolarizing error channel where physical qubits are subject to independent Pauli X, Y, and Z errors.

Key observables. Logical Error Rate (LER), Type I failure fraction (non-convergence), and Type II failure fraction (logical errors).

Important parameters / regimes. Physical error probability (p), ensemble size (K), number of batches (L), and number of splitters (delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The error model assumes a depolarizing channel with independent errors per qubit and relies on the stabilizer formalism.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the aSCED batch block diagram; Figure 2 compares LER for various aSCED configurations; Figure 3 compares aSCED to MWPM decoders on the Toric code; Figure 4 shows the reduction in Type I failures; Figure 5 compares aSCED to BP-OSD on GB codes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of degeneracy-induced decoding failure, defines the mathematical framework of degeneracy sets, proposes the aSCED method using splitters and overcomplete matrices, presents Monte-Carlo simulation results on Toric and GB codes, and concludes with a comparison against state-of-the-art decoders.

Abstract

Quantum low-density parity-check codes are promising candidates for low-overhead fault-tolerant quantum computing, but degeneracy is known to impair the convergence of belief-propagation (BP) decoding of these codes. In this work, we show that appending linearly independent rows to a check matrix of a stabilizer code can reduce the search space for a valid degenerate solution. Motivated by this, we extend the recently proposed affine subcode ensemble decoding technique from the classical to the quantum setting. Moreover, we employ overcomplete matrices for each decoding path. Monte-Carlo simulations on toric and generalized bicycle codes demonstrate improved convergence and reduced logical error rate.

Analytical Angle-Finding and Series Expansions for Quantum Signal Processing via Orthogonal Polynomial Theory

Authors: Pierre-Antoine Bernard, Nathan Wiebe
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05321v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a mathematical breakthrough for Quantum Signal Processing by replacing numerical angle-finding with analytical solutions derived from orthogonal polynomial theory. It establishes explicit formulas for various polynomial families and demonstrates highly efficient gate complexity for approximating smooth functions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the difficulty of analytically finding the rotation angles required for Quantum Signal Processing (QSP) and seeks to characterize the classes of polynomials achievable in various QSP variants.

Main result. The authors derive explicit analytical expressions for QSP angles using orthogonal polynomial theory and prove that an epsilon-approximation of smooth functions can be achieved with O(log(1/epsilon)) gate complexity using Hermite series expansions.

Method. The study utilizes the theory of orthogonal and biorthogonal polynomials (including Hermite, Jacobi, and Rogers-Szego), recurrence relations, and complex analysis (residue calculus) to solve the angle-finding problem.

Model / system. The framework is based on Quantum Signal Processing (QSP), a quantum algorithmic protocol for implementing polynomial transformations of a unitary operator U, including variants like SU(1,1)-QSP and bivariate QSP.

Key observables. QSP rotation angles (theta and phi), block-encoding constants, and polynomial recurrence coefficients.

Important parameters / regimes. Polynomial degree n, error tolerance epsilon, and the growth rate of the normalization constant alpha_n.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the existence of quasi-definite linear functionals and that target polynomials have distinct real roots; function approximation assumes specific smoothness/growth constraints.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a generalized QSP circuit iteration; Figure 2 compares LCU architectures; Figure 3 displays the OP-QSP circuit architecture; Figure 5 illustrates bivariate QSP; Figure 6 shows an SU(1,1) rotation implementation circuit.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QSP angle-finding problem, establishes the connection between QSP and orthogonal polynomial theory, provides explicit angle derivations for specific polynomial families, extends the results to SU(1,1) and bivariate QSP, and concludes with complexity analysis for function approximation.

Abstract

Quantum signal processing is a powerful framework in quantum algorithms, playing a central role in Hamiltonian simulation and related applications. The sequence of polynomials implemented at each step of this protocol provides a polynomial basis for block-encoding any polynomial of a unitary. We characterize the achievable polynomial bases in terms of their orthogonality or biorthogonality with respect to a linear functional admitting an integral representation. Explicit expressions for the quantum signal processing angles are derived for families of polynomial sequences, including Hermite, Jacobi, and Rogers-Szegő polynomials. We show that $2n+2$ rotation angles are required to encode a sequence of polynomials in these classes up to degree $n$. We use this result to show that an $ε$-approximation of a smooth function $f$ can be block-encoded using $O(\log(1/ε))$ gates via its Hermite series expansion. The connections established with the theory of orthogonal and biorthogonal polynomials lead to a new method for solving the quantum signal processing angle-finding problem, yielding explicit expressions for the angles. They also provide a complete characterization of the polynomials achievable by $\mathrm{SU}(1,1)$-QSP in terms of their roots. Biorthogonality properties are shown to hold in the bivariate QSP setting, yielding a set of necessary conditions for achievable polynomials.

Architecture Shape Governs QNN Trainability: Jacobian Null Space Growth and Parameter Efficiency

Authors: Michael Poppel, David Bucher, Maximilian Zorn, Markus Baumann, Sebastian Wölckert, Claudia Linnhoff-Popien, Philipp Altmann, Jonas Stein
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05942v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explains why certain quantum neural network architectures fail to train even when they have sufficient expressivity. It identifies 'structural gradient starvation' in serial architectures and demonstrates that expanding the encoding budget is a much more efficient way to improve performance than simply adding more trainable layers.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating why Variational Quantum Circuit (VQC) architectures with identical frequency spectra exhibit significantly different trainability and identifying the mechanism behind structural gradient starvation.

Main result. The authors identify that serial architectures suffer from structural gradient starvation due to a rank-deficient Jacobian caused by phase-locking, whereas parallel architectures avoid this. They also show that increasing the encoding budget (FM route) is more parameter-efficient than adding trainable blocks.

Method. The study uses Fourier analysis of VQCs, Jacobian rank and null space analysis, and comparison of the Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM) and the Data Jacobian.

Model / system. Variational Quantum Neural Networks (VQNNs) using angle encoding (data re-uploading) with serial (N=1) and parallel (N>1) architectures, utilizing a Rot ansatz with CNOT ladder entanglement.

Key observables. Jacobian rank and null space dimension, coefficient matching Jacobian (J), Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM) eigenvalue spectrum, and R-squared (R2) accuracy.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (N), number of encoding layers (L), encoding budget (E = NL), parameter count (P), and the geometric threshold (Rmax).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the circuits implement truncated Fourier series via angle encoding and focuses on the regime of regression tasks.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows success rates vs. parameter count; Figure 2 demonstrates phase-locking in serial vs. parallel gradients; Figure 3 compares the efficiency of the FM route vs. the trainable blocks route; Figure 4 rules out barren plateaus; Figure 5 shows Jacobian rank saturation; Figure 7 shows real-world validation on temperature data.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of architectural trainability, provides a theoretical framework using Fourier analysis, proves the phase-locking mechanism in serial circuits, compares parameter efficiency of different scaling routes, and validates findings with real-world time-series data.

Abstract

Variational quantum circuits with angle encoding implement truncated Fourier series, and architectures arranging $N$ qubits with $L$ encoding layers each -- sharing encoding budget $E = NL$ -- generate identical frequency spectra, identical frequency redundancy, and require the same minimum parameter count for coefficient control. Despite this equivalence, trainability varies substantially with architecture shape $(N,L)$ at fixed $E$. We identify structural rank deficiency of the coefficient matching Jacobian $J$ as the mechanism responsible. For serial single-qubit architectures, we prove $\mathrm{rank}(J) \leq 2L+1$ regardless of parameter count $P$, with $\dim(\ker J) \geq P-(2L+1)$ growing without bound -- a phenomenon we term \emph{structural gradient starvation}: a growing fraction of parameters become structurally decoupled from the loss as $P$ increases at fixed $L$. Parallel architectures avoid this via independent phase trajectories, ensuring $σ_{\min}(J^{(\mathrm{par})}) > 0$ generically for $P \leq 2E+1$, so no parameter lies in $\ker J$. For practitioners, we further show that the two natural routes to increasing parameter count have fundamentally different effects: adding feature map (FM) layers monotonically strengthens the Jacobian QFIM eigenvalue spectrum and achieves $R^2 \geq 0.95$ with $1.6$--$2.2\times$ fewer parameters than adding trainable blocks across all tested architectures, while trainable blocks improve training only through the classical interpolation mechanism with no quantum-specific benefit.

Collective quantum state at the atomic limit

Authors: Fan Zhang, Yanxing Li, Chengye Dong, Ninad Kailas Dongre, Viet-Anh Ha, Yu-Chuan Lin, Yiyuan Luo, Hyunsue Kim, Joshua A. Robinson, Feliciano Giustino, Fan Zhang, Chih-Kang Shih
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05587v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers show that collective quantum states like spin-charge separation can survive even when confined to segments as small as one nanometer. They discovered that while single-particle states hybridize when segments are linked into chains, the collective many-body excitations remain largely localized. This establishes a new platform for engineering strongly correlated quantum phases using atomic-scale building blocks.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unique look at how many-body correlations and fractionalized excitations behave when spatial extent is reduced to the atomic limit, which is relevant to the study of many-body dynamics and the stability of emergent phases in confined geometries.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether collective quantum states, specifically spin-charge separation in Tomonaga-Luttinger liquids, can persist at the extreme atomic scale of ~1 nm.

Main result. The study demonstrates that collective spin and charge excitations remain robust even in atomically confined segments, forming a new class of many-body quantum dots where collective modes are largely decoupled from inter-dot hybridization.

Method. Low-temperature scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) and spectroscopy (STS) combined with Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations and finite-length Luttinger liquid modeling.

Model / system. Atomically confined mirror twin boundary (MTB) segments in monolayer WSe2, modeled as a 'TLL-in-a-box' and organized into periodic 1D chains of many-body quantum dots.

Key observables. Spin-charge separation, energy gap scaling (delta E proportional to 1/L), spatial profiles of spin and charge excitations, and inter-dot coupling/hybridization.

Important parameters / regimes. Luttinger interaction parameter (K), excitation velocities (v_sigma, v_rho), and the atomic-scale confinement limit (~1 nm).

Assumptions / limitations. The study challenges the assumption that collective phenomena require spatial extensivity and investigates the limits of the thermodynamic limit.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of TLL theory in infinite vs finite systems; Fig 2: TLL behavior in extended MTBs and 1/L scaling; Fig 3: STM/STS of atomic-scale segments showing preserved spin-charge separation; Fig 4: Comparison of experimental spectra with DFT to identify interaction-driven states; Fig 5: Hierarchy of coupling in chains.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing TLL behavior in extended systems, moves to testing the limit of confinement at the atomic scale, and concludes by examining how these modes respond to inter-dot coupling in ordered superlattices.

Abstract

Collective quantum states are often associated with extended systems, where spatially extensive degrees of freedom enable emergent many-body behavior; whether such strongly correlated states survive at atomic dimensions remains a fundamental question. Tomonaga-Luttinger liquids provide a paradigmatic example of one-dimensional collective quantum matter characterized by spin-charge separation. Using low-temperature scanning tunneling microscopy and spectroscopy, we directly visualize quantized collective modes in atomically confined mirror twin boundary segments of monolayer WSe2. Distinct standing-wave branches associated with fractionalized spin and charge excitations persist in segments as short as one nanometer, establishing the atomic-scale confinement limit of Luttinger-liquid behavior. These ultrashort segments form a new class of many-body quantum dots whose discrete spectra arise from confined collective bosonic modes rather than single-particle electron states. When assembled into ordered chains, inter-dot coupling reshapes electron-like fundamental states while collective spin/charge excitations remain largely intact, revealing distinct coupling responses of emergent many-body modes. Our results demonstrate that collective quantum matter can persist and exhibit fundamentally distinct coupling behavior at atomic length scales, establishing a novel platform for engineering strongly correlated quantum phases from atomically confined building blocks.

Summary. This paper investigates the mechanism behind colossal magnetoresistance in a new class of europium-based antiferromagnets. It reveals that the resistance change is driven by a magnetic field lifting the degeneracy of frustrated spin configurations, which reduces phonon scattering. This suggests that lattice-mediated exchange dynamics are the primary driver of electron delocalization in this material.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the physical origin of Colossal Magnetoresistance (CMR) in the Eu2+-based antiferromagnet Eu5Sn2As6, specifically the role of the crystal lattice and phonon-driven exchange dynamics.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that CMR is driven by the suppression of phonon scattering caused by exchange frustration; applying a magnetic field lifts the degeneracy of spin/lattice configurations, quenching magnetostrain and enhancing thermal conductivity.

Method. The study combines experimental measurements (thermal conductivity, magnetostriction, neutron diffraction, and resistivity) with computational techniques (DFT+U and classical spin modeling using SU(N)NY).

Model / system. The system is Eu5Sn2As6, a Zintl-phase stoichiometric antiferromagnet with a non-collinear magnetic ground state. The magnetic interactions are modeled using an effective Heisenberg-like Hamiltonian with a small Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya term.

Key observables. Thermal conductivity, electrical resistivity, magnetostriction, magnetization, specific heat, and magnetic Bragg peaks.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic transitions at TN' = 9.4 K and TN = 10.3 K; CMR magnitude up to a factor of 6000; band gap of ~35-38 meV; Debye temperature of ~21 K.

Assumptions / limitations. The thermal conductivity is assumed to be phonon-dominated; the electronic transport is modeled using the semiclassical Boltzmann transport equation assuming a constant relaxation time.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Crystal structure and magnetic neutron diffraction; Fig 2: Temperature/field dependence of thermal conductivity, specific heat, and resistivity; Fig 3: Field dependence of magnetostriction, magnetization, and thermal conductivity; Fig 4: Magnetic rocking scans; Fig 5: Electronic band structure and DOS.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the CMR phenomenon in Eu5Sn2As6, presents experimental characterization of magnetic and thermal properties, details the neutron diffraction and DFT results to establish the magnetic/electronic structure, and concludes with a proposed mechanism involving exchange-striction-driven phonon scattering suppression.

Abstract

The emergence of colossal magnetoresistance in a new generation of Eu$^{2+}$-based antiferromagnets is intriguing given stark contrasts to the archetypal perovskite manganites and doped Eu-chalcogenides. In this study the thermal conductivity and magnetostriction of Eu$_5$Sn$_2$As$_6$ -- one such representative -- have been measured to better understand the role of the crystal lattice. Both properties are strongly field-dependent and mirror the magnetization, saturating once the Eu$^{2+}$ moments are polarized. The field-enhancement of the phonon-dominated thermal conductivity is interpreted through the lifting of a degeneracy of spin configurations, and the subsequent saturation due to quenched magnetostrain in high field. Comparison with spin-glass insulators suggests that this phenomenon is not a byproduct but rather the driver of electron delocalization due to the suppression of strong phonon scattering arising from exchange frustration.

Density diversity in training data governs thermodynamic transferability of machine learning interatomic potentials

Authors: Minwoo Kim, Seungtae Kim, Je-Yeon Jung, Min Young Ha, Won Bo Lee
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05733v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that to make machine learning potentials work across different phases (like gas to liquid), it is more important to train on a variety of densities than a variety of temperatures. This is because density changes the local arrangement of atoms more significantly than temperature does, making density diversity a key design principle for robust atomistic simulations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of thermodynamic transferability in Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials (MLIPs), specifically the inability of models to accurately describe fluid-phase systems when trained on different density regimes.

Main result. Density diversification (DenVar) is more effective than temperature diversification (TempVar) for building thermodynamically transferable MLIPs because local coordination topology is more sensitive to density than temperature.

Method. The study uses controlled 'from-scratch' training and 'distillation' experiments on CO2 and H2O, comparing datasets with varying density and temperature under a fixed computational budget.

Model / system. The study evaluates various MLIP architectures, including foundation models (SevenNet, SO3krates) and from-scratch models (NequIP, MACE), applied to molecular systems like Ar-Ar, CO2-CO2, and H2O-H2O.

Key observables. Pairwise interaction energy profiles, Radial Distribution Functions (RDFs), RDF similarity scores, and coordination numbers.

Important parameters / regimes. Training density, training temperature, reduced density (rho/rho_c), and reduced temperature (T/T_c).

Assumptions / limitations. The MLIP architecture relies on local-energy decomposition where local energy is a function of the topology and geometry within a fixed cutoff radius.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares interaction energy profiles; Figure 3 shows CO2 RDFs at different densities; Figure 6 presents a heatmap of coordination numbers and their sensitivity to temperature and density.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the transferability problem, analyzes failure modes of existing foundation models, presents controlled training experiments (DenVar vs TempVar), provides a physical mechanism via coordination number analysis, and concludes with a validation framework.

Abstract

Machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) offer first-principles accuracy with reduced computational cost, but their transferability across different thermodynamic states remains questionable, particularly for fluid systems where molecules experience local environments far from crystalline equilibrium. Here, we demonstrate that diversifying the density of training configurations, rather than temperature, is the most effective strategy for building thermodynamically transferable MLIPs within a fixed computational budget. We first show that foundation MLIPs trained on solid-state databases accurately describe liquid-like densities but fail at gas-like conditions, while molecular-database-trained models exhibit the opposite behavior. Controlled from-scratch training and distillation experiments confirm that density-diverse datasets resolve both failure modes, whereas temperature-diverse datasets cannot compensate for missing density regimes. Coordination number analysis reveals the physical origin of this behavior: local coordination topology is more susceptible to density than temperature, leading to further structural diversity. These results establish density diversity as a design principle for thermodynamically transferable MLIPs and provide a validation framework for assessing the thermodynamic coverage of both foundation and from-scratch models, enabling reliable atomistic simulation of fluid-phase processes across diverse operating conditions.

Dominant Role of Sulphur divacancy in Charge Trapping Dynamics in MoS$_2$

Authors: Srest Somay, Sitangshu Bhattacharya, Krishna Balasubramanian
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06109v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This study identifies Sulphur divacancies as the primary driver of electron trapping in monolayer MoS2. It demonstrates that the massive disparity in capture rates between different defects is caused by strong lattice relaxation rather than just the energy position of the defect levels. This finding is crucial for understanding and controlling recombination and hysteresis in 2D semiconductor devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The microscopic origin of defect-dependent carrier capture dynamics in monolayer MoS2 is unclear, specifically which intrinsic defects drive charge trapping and recombination.

Main result. Sulphur divacancies (VS2) dominate electron capture due to strong lattice relaxation and enhanced electron-phonon coupling, with capture coefficients seven orders of magnitude larger than Sulphur monovacancies.

Method. First-principles calculations using VASP with HSE and PBE functionals, employing a non-radiative multiphonon (NMP) framework and a 1D configuration coordinate model.

Model / system. Monolayer Molybdenum Disulfide (MoS2) containing intrinsic defects: Sulphur monovacancy (VS), Sulphur divacancy (VS2), Molybdenum vacancy (VMo), and Sulphur antisite (SMo).

Key observables. Capture coefficients (cm3/s), charge transition levels, lattice distortion (mass-weighted displacement delta Q), and Huang-Rhys factors.

Important parameters / regimes. Capture barriers (Enb), electron-phonon coupling matrix elements (Wif), and the ratio of phonon number to Huang-Rhys factor (kappa/S).

Assumptions / limitations. Harmonic approximation for phonon wavefunctions and potential energy surfaces; linear coupling approximation for the electron-phonon coupling matrix element.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows atomic structures of defects and associated lattice distortions; Fig 2 presents charge transition level diagrams using PBE and HSE functionals; Fig 3 illustrates the configuration coordinate diagrams and capture dynamics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of defect-induced trapping, describes the computational methodology (DFT and NMP framework), presents structural and electronic properties of various defects, analyzes the capture coefficients through lattice relaxation analysis, and concludes with the identification of the dominant trapping species.

Abstract

Intrinsic defects govern carrier trapping and recombination in two-dimensional semiconductors, yet the microscopic origin of defect-dependent capture dynamics remains unclear. Here, we compute carrier capture coefficients of vacancy defects, treating monolayer MoS$_2$ as a prototype, from first principles. We find that the single Sulphur vacancy forms a shallow defect with a small capture coefficient of $\sim 10^{-16}\ \mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$, whereas the Sulphur divacancy exhibits a capture coefficient larger by seven orders of magnitude, $\sim 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$, despite being only moderately deeper in energy. This enhancement originates from strong lattice relaxation enabling efficient multiphonon capture. Consequently, single vacancies contribute weakly to trapping, while Sulphur divacancies dominate nonradiative recombination and reduce quantum yield. In contrast, molybdenum vacancies and Sulphur antisites, although deep, show much smaller capture coefficients, indicating a limited role in carrier trapping in n-type devices.

Abstract

The 1T polymorph of NbSe$_2$, long confined to the monolayer limit, has remained inaccessible in bulk. Here, we report the realization of bulk 1T-NbSe$_2$ via electrochemical Sn intercalation. Raman spectroscopy further reveals vibrational modes associated with Sn intercalation and possible charge density wave order. Our results establish electrochemical intercalation as an effective route to stabilize otherwise inaccessible bulk polytypes, positioning bulk 1T-NbSe$_2$ as a new platform for investigating correlated electronic states.

Field-Induced Local Excitations Causing Zero-Magnetization Plateaus in Antiferromagnets of Antiferromagnetic Spin Dimers Under Magnetic Field

Summary. This paper investigates why certain antiferromagnets maintain a zero-magnetization plateau under a magnetic field despite having negligible single-ion anisotropy. It identifies field-induced triplet excitations as the driving mechanism and explains the observed asymmetry in specific heat through the increasing energy cost of breaking interdimer bonds.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Explaining the mechanism behind zero-magnetization plateaus in antiferromagnets composed of spin dimers where single-ion anisotropy is negligible.

Kappa distributions are widely used in space plasma physics to model velocity distribution functions with extended tails. However, parameter estimation in these distributions presents a fundamental challenge: the kappa distribution does not belong to the exponential family, which prevents the direct application of analytically tractable maximum likelihood methods. In this work we propose a solution to this problem based on data augmentation: we introduce the inverse temperature $β$ as a gamma-distributed latent variable, thereby recovering the exponential family structure in the complete-data likelihood. This enables an implementation of the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm in analytically closed form, with E-step and M-step derived from sufficient statistics. Our approach is agnostic with respect to the underlying physical mechanism generating fluctuations of $β$, which is a central aspect of the Beck-Cohen superstatistics framework, allowing a statistically rigorous treatment without compromising physical interpretability. We demonstrate that the method converges to the usual maximum likelihood estimators by applying it to synthetic data. Our results suggest that EM offers a computationally efficient and conceptually clear alternative for inference in superstatistical systems with temperature fluctuations.
