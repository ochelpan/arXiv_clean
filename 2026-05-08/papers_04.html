BARFI-Q: Quantum-Enhanced Block Attention Residual Fusion Framework for Multivariate Time-Series Forecasting in Atom Interferometry

Authors: Muhammad Bilal Akram Dastagir, Omer Tariq, Safaa Alqrinawi, Shaikha Al-Naimi, Ahmed Farouk, Saif Al-Kuwari
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05394v1

Summary. The paper introduces BARFI-Q, a hybrid quantum-classical framework designed to forecast complex, multivariate time-series data from atom interferometers. By using quantum feature mapping and an adaptive residual mechanism, the model effectively handles the periodic and heterogeneous nature of interferometric signals. The results show that this approach significantly improves the accuracy of phase reconstruction compared to standard deep learning models.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a practical application of hybrid quantum-classical machine learning to improve the predictive capabilities of quantum sensors, specifically addressing the non-linear and periodic nature of interferometric signals.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Accurate multivariate time-series forecasting of heterogeneous signals in atom interferometry, specifically addressing phase evolution, fringe dynamics, and complex interactions among multiple sensing sources.

Main result. The BARFI-Q framework consistently outperforms strong classical Transformer-based baselines in predicting wrapped residual phase errors, with the quantum-enhanced version providing superior accuracy and stability.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical framework (BARFI-Q) utilizing patch-based embedding, a dual-branch temporal architecture with adaptive block-attention residual aggregation, and a Quantum Feature Mapping (QFM) module for latent enhancement.

Model / system. The physical system is a light-pulse atom interferometer used for precision sensing (e.g., gravimetry or inertial navigation), generating multivariate temporal streams of phase, fringe behavior, and control variables.

Key observables. Wrapped residual phase error, population ratio, fringe amplitude, and fringe contrast.

Important parameters / regimes. Historical window size (L), number of qubits (N), circuit depth (L), and encoding strategy (Angle, Amplitude, or Phase).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on a one-step-ahead forecasting setting and assumes the availability of a real-time classical phase estimate to resolve phase ambiguity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares existing Transformer models; Figure 2 shows the BARFI-Q architecture; Figure 3 details the dual-branch transformer; Figure 6 compares error metrics across window sizes; Figure 9 shows the quantum weight landscape; Figure 10 provides a qualitative comparison of fringe reconstruction accuracy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of atom interferometry forecasting, proposes the BARFI-Q architecture (including patch embedding, dual-branch modeling, and adaptive residuals), details the quantum feature mapping module, presents ablation studies on fusion and quantum configurations, and concludes with performance comparisons against classical baselines.

Abstract

Atom interferometry generates heterogeneous multivariate temporal streams governed by phase evolution, fringe dynamics, control variables, and auxiliary sensing measurements. Accurate forecasting of these signals is important for predictive monitoring, phase correction, and intelligent quantum sensing, but it requires effective modeling of long-range temporal dependencies and interactions among multiple sensing sources. This paper proposes BARFI-Q, a Quantum-Enhanced Block Attention Residual Fusion framework for multivariate time-series forecasting in atom interferometry. BARFI-Q integrates patch-based embedding, dual-branch temporal modeling, hierarchical fusion, adaptive block-attention residual aggregation, and a quantum feature-mapping module. Unlike conventional Transformer-based forecasting models with fixed additive residual paths, BARFI-Q adaptively reuses cross-depth information and enhances the fused latent representation through quantum feature mapping. To respect phase periodicity, the forecasting target is represented in circular space using sine and cosine components. Experiments show that BARFI-Q consistently outperforms strong baseline models across repeated runs and different historical window sizes. Fusion ablation results further confirm the benefit of jointly modeling channel-wise and spatial feature interactions. These results indicate that multiscale temporal learning, hierarchical fusion, adaptive residual routing, and quantum-enhanced latent transformation provide an effective framework for atom-interferometric time-series forecasting.

Frustrated Fields: Statistical Field Theory for Frustrated Brownian Particles on 2D Manifolds

Authors: Igor Halperin
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05366v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper derives a new statistical field theory (the F2 model) for frustrated Brownian particles on curved surfaces. It demonstrates that quenched disorder can cause particles to spontaneously concentrate on lower-dimensional manifolds, such as rings on a sphere, with dynamics governed by a non-linear sigma model. The theory is shown to be highly predictive, reproducing simulation results without adjustable parameters.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a statistical field theory to describe the large-N thermodynamic limit of Brownian particles with quenched random interactions on 2D Riemannian manifolds.

Main result. The derivation of the F2 model, a non-linear field theory that predicts adiabatic dimension reduction (e.g., particles concentrating on great-circle rings on a sphere) and identifies the low-energy dynamics as a (0+1)-dimensional non-linear sigma model on the real projective plane.

Method. The authors use the MSRJD path-integral formalism, disorder averaging via Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations, and a Born-Oppenheimer-style adiabatic approximation to reduce the many-body dynamics to an effective single-field theory.

Model / system. A system of N Brownian particles on a compact 2D Riemannian manifold subject to overdamped Langevin dynamics and quenched random pairwise interactions (spin-glass type) with various long-range potentials.

Key observables. Density field (rho), orientation vector (n), rotational diffusion coefficient (D_rot), and two-time density-density and current-current correlators.

Important parameters / regimes. Large-N limit, coupling variance (J), friction coefficient (gamma), temperature (T), and the rotational diffusion coefficient (D_rot).

Assumptions / limitations. The large-N limit, the adiabatic separation between slow orientation and fast density fluctuations, and the neglect of certain functional determinant fluctuations in the leading-order approximation.

Figures summary. The notes do not provide specific figure descriptions, but imply the presence of particle simulations showing density concentration on rings and validation of the effective theory against simulation diagnostics.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds from defining the many-body Langevin dynamics and quenched disorder, to constructing the MSRJD path integral, performing disorder averaging to derive the F2 model, applying adiabatic dimension reduction on specific manifolds like S2, and finally validating the effective theory using particle simulations.

Abstract

We develop a statistical field theory that describes the large-N limit of a system of Brownian particles with quenched random pairwise interactions on a compact two-dimensional Riemannian manifold. The resulting Frustrated Fields (F2) model is a non-linear field theory for a smooth self-interacting density field $ρ$ on the manifold, with local and non-local (in space and time) self-interactions characteristic of spin-glass dynamics. Particle simulations show \emph{adiabatic dimension reduction}: on $S^2$, the density concentrates on a slowly precessing great-circle ring whose orientation is a director ($\hat{\mathbf{n}} \sim -\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, even profile). Conditioned on this simulation-supported ring saddle and on a Born-Oppenheimer separation between the slow orientation and the gapped density fluctuations, symmetry fixes the low-energy dynamics to be the nonlinear sigma model (NLSM) on the real projective plane $S^2/\mathbb{Z}_2 = \mathbb{RP}^2$ (the $\mathbb{RP}^2$ NLSM on the projective rotor space) in $(0+1)$ dimensions, governed by a single low-energy constant, the rotational diffusion coefficient $D_{\text{rot}}$. With $D_{\text{rot}}$ and the static ring profile $f_0$ measured from particle simulations, the resulting effective theory reproduces multiple independent orientation- and density-sector diagnostics with no further adjustable parameters.

Practical Log-Depth Quantum State Preparation and Circuit Verification via Tree Tensor Network Compilation

Authors: Angus Mingare, Peter V. Coveney
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06579v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces an analytical method to convert Matrix Product States into quantum circuits with logarithmic depth instead of linear depth. By using a tree tensor network renormalization approach, it allows for much shallower circuits that are more resilient to noise on near-term quantum hardware. The method also extends to verifying unitary operations using Matrix Product Operators.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The high circuit depth of traditional linear-depth (staircase) mappings for loading Matrix Product States (MPS) onto quantum computers makes them impractical for noisy, near-term hardware.

Main result. The authors developed an analytical method to decompose MPS and MPO into log-depth quantum circuits using Tree Tensor Network (TTN) renormalization, enabling efficient state preparation and circuit verification.

Method. A Tree Tensor Network renormalization procedure uses iterative local merges and Singular Value Decompositions (SVD) to transform MPS into a binary tree structure, which is then mapped to quantum gates.

Model / system. The method focuses on N-qubit systems represented by Matrix Product States (MPS) and Matrix Product Operators (MPO) with a bond dimension chi.

Key observables. Circuit depth, fidelity (overlap), gate locality, and the squared overlap magnitude ||^2 for verifier circuits.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth scaling (O(log N) vs O(N)), bond dimension (chi), system size (N), and noise parameter (delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the MPS can be padded to a bond dimension of 2^k and that the unitary U has an efficient MPO representation; it also assumes a trade-off between fidelity and depth via truncation.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate tensor ranks, the structural difference between MPS and MPO, the linear depth of staircase circuits versus the log-depth of TTN, the SVD renormalization process, and performance metrics like fidelity decay and gate size saturation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MPS loading, presents the TTN renormalization method, provides an error analysis for truncation, extends the method to MPOs for circuit verification, and demonstrates utility for device calibration.

Abstract

Matrix product states provide efficient classical descriptions of quantum systems that may be useful as reference states for quantum algorithms such as quantum phase estimation and quantum-selected configuration interaction. Shallow circuit constructions for loading matrix product states onto quantum computers is necessary for this to be practical on near-term hardware. We present a decomposition of matrix product states to log-depth quantum circuits via a simple tree tensor network renormalisation procedure. Our method exposes an explicit parameter which can be used to trade a small amount of fidelity for large savings in circuit depth. We extend this decomposition to the case of matrix product operators allowing us to construct log-depth and ancilla-free circuits to calculate overlaps of the form $\left |\langleφ|U|ψ\rangle\right |^2$. In particular, we demonstrate an interpretation of these circuits as \emph{verifier circuits} with application to circuit-level device calibration.

Pro-Tensor Network

Authors: Gen Yue, Ansi Bai, Linqian Wu, Tian Lan
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06661v1

Summary. This paper introduces 'pro-tensor networks,' a mathematical generalization of tensor networks that uses category theory to study collections of many-body theories simultaneously. By replacing vector spaces with categories, the authors create a framework capable of describing topological order and generalized symmetries without being limited by standard assumptions like finiteness or semisimplicity.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful new mathematical language for studying the renormalization of many-body systems and the emergence of generalized symmetries, which is highly relevant to the study of many-body dynamics and topological phases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for a rigorous mathematical framework to study the universal properties of entire collections of many-body theories (many-many-body theory) rather than individual models.

Main result. The introduction of the 'pro-tensor network,' a categorification of tensor networks using enriched category theory, which generalizes the Levin-Wen model and provides a new classification of topological excitations.

Method. The authors use enriched category theory, specifically V-profunctors (distributors) and coend calculus, to create a framework that replaces vector spaces with categories and linear maps with profunctors.

Model / system. The framework encompasses a hierarchy of models, including classical tensor networks, the Levin-Wen model (as a uniform pro-tensor network), and string-net models representing topological order.

Key observables. Topological order, generalized symmetries, and topological excitations (particles).

Important parameters / regimes. The framework specifically addresses regimes beyond the standard constraints of semisimplicity, finiteness, and rigidity.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes the base category V is a cosmos (symmetric monoidal closed, complete, and cocomplete), and certain results require the category to be Cauchy complete or rigid.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows two interpretations of classical tensor networks; Figure 2 demonstrates tensor contraction; Figure 3 illustrates the two interpretations of pro-tensor networks; later figures show commutative diagrams for probimonad axioms and module actions.

Paper structure. The paper begins by introducing the pro-tensor network and its physical motivation, followed by the mathematical construction using enriched category theory and coend calculus, then applies the framework to recover and generalize known models like Levin-Wen, and concludes with technical proofs regarding module equivalences and probimonad structures.

Abstract

We introduce the pro-tensor network, a categorification of the tensor network, as a fully rigorous yet graphically transparent framework for studying the collection of many many-body theories, which we dub many-many-body theory. We provide a comprehensive toolbox for the graphical calculations using pro-tensor networks. As applications, we recover the Levin-Wen model as a "uniform" pro-tensor network and generalize a result of Kitaev and Kong by characterizing particles as modules over promonads. One can also interpret the string-net pro-tensor network as the space of symmetric tensor networks, thus our framework also applies to the study of generalized symmetry and topological holography. Notably, our generalization dispenses with the assumptions of semisimplicity, finiteness, and rigidity, potentially facilitating the exploration of many-body physics beyond these constraints.

Static-Field Tunneling Ionization in Space-Fractional Quantum Mechanics

Authors: Marcelo F. Ciappina
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05617v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops an analytical model for tunneling ionization in space-fractional quantum mechanics. It shows that non-local kinetic energy changes the scaling laws of ionization and significantly enhances the ionization rate compared to standard quantum mechanics. The work provides a new benchmark for studying how non-locality affects strong-field physics.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new theoretical benchmark for non-local quantum dynamics and explores how non-quadratic kinetic energy fundamentally alters the tunneling mechanism, which is relevant to anyone studying non-local transport or fractional dynamics in atomic systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the lack of a standard strong-field benchmark for tunneling ionization in Space-Fractional Quantum Mechanics (SFQM) and seeks to understand how non-local kinetic energy affects the tunneling exponent.

Main result. The author derives a closed-form fractional-ADK (fADK) tunneling exponent and demonstrates that decreasing the fractional order alpha enhances ionization rates due to genuine non-local transport effects.

Method. The study uses a semiclassical WKB approach to derive the analytical exponent and performs numerical simulations of the 1D time-dependent fractional Schrödinger equation using a split-operator scheme.

Model / system. A 1D model of an electron in a static electric field with a soft-core Coulomb potential, governed by a fractional Hamiltonian featuring the Riesz fractional Laplacian of order 1 < alpha <= 2.

Key observables. Tunneling ionization rate (Gamma), tunneling exponent, survival probability, and the tunneling action (slope of -ln Gamma vs 1/F0).

Important parameters / regimes. Fractional order (alpha), ionization potential (Ip), static electric field strength (F0), and the barrier-suppression threshold.

Assumptions / limitations. The analytical derivation assumes a triangular exit barrier; the model is restricted to one dimension; and the study focuses on the static-field (quasistatic) limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares tunneling rates for different alpha values; Figure 4 benchmarks the 1D-TDSE against standard ADK theory; Figure 5 shows scaling for various alpha values under a fixed potential; Figure 6 compares the numerical 1D-TDFSE results against the derived fADK prediction.

Paper structure. The paper reviews conventional ADK/PPT theory, introduces the fractional Hamiltonian framework, provides the analytical derivation of the fADK exponent, presents numerical validation protocols, and concludes with a comparison of numerical results against the new theory.

Abstract

Tunneling ionization in static or slowly varying electric fields is a cornerstone of strong-field physics and provides the entry point for semiclassical descriptions of above-threshold ionization and high-harmonic generation. In conventional quantum mechanics, the Perelomov--Popov--Terent'ev (PPT) theory and its Ammosov--Delone--Krainov (ADK) form yield an ionization rate whose defining feature is an exponential dependence governed by an under-barrier (imaginary-time) action. Here we develop an analytical ADK-like tunneling model within \emph{space-fractional} quantum mechanics, where the quadratic kinetic energy is replaced by the Riesz fractional Laplacian of order $1<α\le2$. Working in a static electric field in the length gauge, we derive a closed-form tunneling exponent for a triangular exit barrier. The fractional kinetic operator deforms the conventional $I_p^{3/2}$ scaling to $I_p^{1+1/α}$ and introduces a characteristic $\sin(π/α)$ factor encoding the complex-phase structure associated with nonlocal dispersion. We position this benchmark relative to prior tunneling studies in fractional quantum mechanics (primarily scattering through model barriers and fractal potentials) and provide a validation protocol for testing the exponent in time-dependent simulations of the fractional Schrödinger equation under a constant field. The result establishes a transparent reference for static-field ionization in nonlocal quantum dynamics and a baseline for strong-field approaches extensions.

Syndrome resampling enhances quantum error correction thresholds

Authors: Luis Colmenarez, Áron Márton, Markus Müller
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06101v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new method called syndrome resampling that improves quantum error correction performance by reweighting syndrome data. It works for any decoder and can significantly reduce logical error rates in both simulations and real experimental data without needing new hardware. The method effectively pushes the error correction threshold higher by focusing on the most probable error patterns.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of increasing quantum error correction (QEC) thresholds and reducing logical error rates without requiring additional hardware or complex, code-specific decoding modifications.

Main result. The introduction of syndrome resampling, which increases QEC thresholds and reduces logical error rates by up to four orders of magnitude in simulations and two orders of magnitude in experimental data.

Method. A decoder-agnostic method called syndrome resampling that biases syndrome averages towards more likely syndromes by resampling according to powers of the syndrome probability distribution.

Model / system. Quantum error correction codes, specifically surface codes (unrotated and rotated) and the toric code, subject to Pauli bit-flip noise.

Key observables. Logical error rate, syndrome probability distribution (SPD), Renyi Coherent Information (RCI), and threshold (p_th).

Important parameters / regimes. Resampling parameter alpha, physical error rate p, code distance d, and critical exponent nu.

Assumptions / limitations. The noise model is assumed to be Pauli noise, and the method's efficiency is limited by the sampling cost required to estimate the syndrome probability distribution.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the reconstruction of the modified syndrome distribution; Figure 2 displays logical error rates vs. error rates for different alpha values and shows data collapse; Figure 3 compares various decoding strategies; Figure 4 shows the evolution of error rates and acceptance rates; Figure 6 shows finite-size scaling collapse for MWPM.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the syndrome resampling method, establishes its theoretical connection to Renyi Coherent Information and phase transitions, presents numerical simulations on surface codes, demonstrates implementation with finite data and combination with post-selection, and validates the method using existing experimental QEC data.

Abstract

Quantum error correction (QEC) enables fault-tolerant quantum computation but requires operating quantum hardware at physical error rates below code-dependent thresholds, which remains challenging for current devices. We introduce syndrome resampling, a general method that increases QEC thresholds of any decoder and suppresses logical errors without additional hardware, decoding modifications, or code-specific assumptions beyond syndrome statistics. The method exploits the fact that syndromes with low probability are likely to lead to logical failure, therefore biasing syndrome averages towards most likely syndromes effectively increases logical fidelities. We establish a direct connection between the Rényi coherent information (RCI) and powers of the syndrome probability distribution, showing that resampling syndromes according to these powers combined with maximum likelihood decoding (MLD) realizes a family of optimal thresholds associated with phase transitions in the RCI. Numerical simulations of surface codes demonstrate that syndrome resampling substantially increases thresholds for both optimal and suboptimal decoders and reduces logical error rates by up to four orders of magnitude in experimentally relevant regimes. We further show that syndrome resampling can be effectively implemented from finite data and combined with decoding-based post-selection to achieve additional gains. Finally, applying the method to existing experimental QEC data yields up to two orders of magnitude reduction in logical error rates without requiring additional measurements. Our results provide a practical and decoder-agnostic route to improved logical fidelities in near-term QEC experiments.

Summary. This paper generalizes the classical shadows technique by investigating measurement protocols where unitaries are sampled from compact symmetric spaces rather than just groups. It reveals that certain symmetric space protocols can outperform standard methods when estimating observables that are concentrated on the diagonal of the measurement basis.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Extending the classical shadows framework beyond standard group-based sampling to include protocols based on compact symmetric spaces (G/K).

Main result. The authors establish a unifying theory for symmetric space shadows, showing they are invertible and can provide improved sample complexity for observables concentrated on the diagonal in certain regimes.

Method. The study utilizes representation theory, Weingarten calculus, and the study of measurement channels to evaluate integrals over symmetric spaces and analyze the variance of estimators.

Model / system. The model involves an unknown quantum state in a d-dimensional Hilbert space being reconstructed via random measurements sampled from the seven infinite families of type I compact symmetric spaces.

Key observables. Expectation values of quantum observables, specifically focusing on the variance of estimators for diagonal and off-diagonal components.

Important parameters / regimes. The dimension of the Hilbert space (d), the signature of the involution (s), and the scaling regime of the signature (s in O(1) vs s = cd).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes sampling is performed uniformly from the compact symmetric spaces and that the measurement basis is chosen such that involutions take standard forms.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares probability distributions on the Bloch sphere for different ensembles; Figure 2 compares the variance of random symmetric observables across different shadow protocols.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of symmetric space shadows, provides a mathematical framework using group theory and Weingarten calculus, classifies the resulting measurement channels, analyzes the variance in different scaling regimes, and concludes with performance comparisons.

Abstract

Efficiently learning expectation values of unknown quantum states via classical shadows has become an important primitive in both theoretical and experimental aspects of quantum computation. Typically, classical shadow protocols involve randomised measurements induced by sampling uniformly randomly from a compact group, a situation which is now quite well understood. In this work we go beyond this standard assumption, studying the classical shadow protocols occasioned by sampling uniformly randomly from the so-called compact symmetric spaces. We uncover a unifying theory of such protocols, extending the extent to which the general theory of classical shadows is understood at a mathematical level. Interestingly, for the estimation of observables sampled from certain distributions we further find that some of these protocols allow for slight improvements in sample-complexity over existing shadow schemes.

Competing nonlinearities, criticality, and order-to-chaos transition in deep networks

Authors: Omri Lesser, Debanjan Chowdhury
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05294v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper shows that by randomly mixing two different smooth activation functions, one can create a continuous phase transition that achieves the benefits of scale-invariant signal propagation (like ReLU) without losing smoothness. This approach provides a controlled way to navigate neural network universality classes and improves model robustness to label noise.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of analytically controlling the transition between different universality classes of activation functions in deep neural networks, specifically resolving the tension between achieving scale-invariant signal propagation and maintaining function smoothness.

Main result. The discovery of a continuous phase transition in a statistical mixture of smooth activations (Tanh and Swish) that enables emergent scale-invariance and acts as a structural regularizer during training.

Method. Effective field theory and mean-field dynamics in the infinite-width limit, utilizing kernel recursion, Taylor expansions, and finite-size scaling analysis.

Model / system. Deep multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) where each neuron's activation function is drawn independently from a Bernoulli distribution (a mixture of Tanh and Swish) at initialization, creating quenched activation disorder.

Key observables. Preactivation variance propagation, parallel and perpendicular susceptibilities, Lyapunov exponents, and the stability coefficient.

Important parameters / regimes. Mixing fraction (p), network depth (L), network width (N), and initial input variance (K0).

Assumptions / limitations. The infinite-width limit (N to infinity) allows for self-averaging and Gaussian preactivations; the transition is analyzed in the context of quenched disorder.

Figures summary. Figures depict variance propagation through layers, the transition from variance-collapsing to variance-inflating regimes, the smoothness of the transition, and the non-monotonic relationship between the mixing fraction and neural network test performance/robustness.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of activation universality, proposes a statistical mixture model, develops a mean-field theory to predict the critical point, verifies the transition via Lyapunov exponents and susceptibilities, and demonstrates the practical learning advantages and regularization effects through MNIST/Fashion-MNIST simulations.

Abstract

Deep neural networks owe their expressive power to nonlinear activation functions. The effective field theory of signal propagation at initialization reveals a few distinct universality classes of activations that exhibit different depth scaling. Tuning across these, especially with analytical control, is an open problem. We show that a statistical mixture of activations, where each neuron independently and randomly draws its activation from a two-component distribution with mixing fraction $p$, provides a new mechanism for a continuous phase transition. Applied to a mixture of Tanh and Swish, the transition is sharp in the depth scaling of the preactivation variance, separating a variance-collapsing from a variance-inflating phase; at $p_c$, the network acquires statistical scale invariance, with depth-independent variance, without sacrificing smoothness. This resolves a longstanding tension, where scale-invariant propagation has previously required the non-smooth ReLU family, rendering such networks ill-suited to curvature-based optimizers, physics-informed architectures, and neural-network quantum states. We corroborate the transition through variance propagation, parallel and perpendicular susceptibilities, and Lyapunov exponents. Training multilayer perceptrons on real datasets reveals non-monotonic test performance as a function of $p$, with an optimum near the theoretically predicted $p_c$, confirming that the initialization-level transition has direct consequences for learned representations. The quenched activation disorder acts as a structural regularizer, suppressing memorization of corrupted labels while preserving generalization. Our framework establishes statistical activation mixtures as a controlled tool for navigating the phase diagram of deep network universality classes.

Finite-size general security for differential phase shift keying via variable-length quantum key distribution

Authors: Carlos Pascual-García

Summary. This paper provides a new security proof for DPSK quantum key distribution that works with much smaller data blocks than previously possible. By using advanced entropy accumulation and conic optimization, it proves that industrial-grade, practical QKD is achievable even with significant channel loss and experimental noise.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum communications as it provides a rigorous security foundation for practical, high-rate, and low-complexity photonic QKD implementations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving finite-size general security for Differential Phase Shift Keying (DPSK) QKD without requiring extremely large block sizes or low repetition rates.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that DPSK can achieve secure key rates with block sizes as small as 10^5 signals at losses beyond 12 dB, significantly outperforming previous benchmarks.

Method. The study uses the Marginal-Constrained Entropy Accumulation Theorem (MEAT) and Rényi leftover hashing, formulated as a conic optimization problem using the Julia programming language.

Model / system. A DPSK QKD protocol where Alice sends coherent states and Bob performs threshold detection using a Mach-Zehnder interferometer, incorporating channel loss, dark counts, and modulation asymmetry.

Key observables. Secret key rate (bits per pulse), detection outcomes (clicks, double-clicks, no-clicks), and channel loss (dB).

Important parameters / regimes. Block size (n) ranging from 10^5 to 10^9, channel loss (up to 30 dB), dark count probability (pd), and asymmetry factor (gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The security relies on a no-signaling condition between quantum signals and assumes Eve is a general adversary.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows secret key rates vs. channel loss for various block sizes; Figure 2 shows the impact of dark count rates; Figure 3 shows the dependence on the Rényi parameter; Figure 4 shows the impact of modulation asymmetry.

Paper structure. The paper defines the DPSK protocol, establishes an operational equivalence to a simpler relativistic protocol, applies the MEAT framework with dimension reduction via squashing maps, and performs numerical optimization to derive key rates.

Abstract

Differential phase shift keying (DPSK) constitutes a pathway towards practical quantum key distribution by using affordable commercial technologies, and robust theoretical foundations. Recent advances in the security of DPSK have proven its security against general adversaries, albeit requiring limitations, including strong repetition rate constraints at the security proof and costly statistical estimators. In this work, we overcome said limitations by leveraging recent techniques in variable-length general security by using entropy accumulation techniques based on Rényi leftover hashing, together with conic optimization methods. Our approach achieves secret key rates with $10^5$ signals beyond 12 dB, constituting a robust proof of the experimental implementability of industrial-grade DPSK.

Frustrated Dynamics of Distance Matrices

Summary. This paper introduces a framework to detect geometric transitions in a system by analyzing the time-varying spectrum of a distance matrix. It demonstrates that a collapse from a 2D sphere to a 1D ring can be identified through specific shifts in the eigenvalue distribution and rank-decay exponents. This provides a method for solving dynamical inverse problems in complex systems where only pairwise distances are observable.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining if structural changes in an underlying particle configuration, such as dimensional collapse from a 2D sphere to a 1D ring, can be detected using only the time series of a distance matrix without direct access to particle positions.

Main result. The study finds that the static Bogomolny, Bohigas, and Schmit (BBS) spectral template is preserved during dynamics, but structural changes are identifiable through a redistribution of spectral mass, specifically via rank reduction in the l=1 multiplet and shifts in the rank-decay exponent.

Method. Numerical simulations of Langevin SDEs for Brownian particles on a sphere, combined with spectral analysis of the distance matrix, including MLE for power-law exponents and comparison against i.i.d. resampled ensembles.

Model / system. The Frustrated Distance Matrix (FDM) model, consisting of N Brownian particles on a 2-sphere (S2) evolving under quenched random pairwise couplings that are linear in the geodesic distances.

Furthermore, we show that for each of the algebras $A$ from above, such an approximate Fourier transform can be implemented efficiently: we give a quantum algorithm with gate complexity $\mathrm{poly}(n,\log d,\log(1/\varepsilon))$ for approximating the Fourier transform to error $(d^{-1/2} + \varepsilon) \cdot \mathrm{poly}(|A|)$. Along the way, we establish several properties of the Fourier basis of semisimple algebras that may be of independent interest.

Abstract

We formally introduce a class of models inspired by renormalization group (RG) theory, built on additive hierarchical expansions analogous to those appearing in functional ANOVA and mixed-effects models. Like ReLU convolutional neural networks, they are almost everywhere locally linear; unlike ReLU networks, their partition structure is explicit, interpretable, and easy to modify or constrain. In these models, one defines a multidimensional lattice partition of the input space and uses it to scaffold variations in regression parameters. Each dimension of the lattice corresponds to an attribute by which the statistics of the problem may vary. The parameters are themselves expressed in the form of an expansion, where each term captures variations relative to a lower (coarser) interaction scale. These models admit multiple equivalent interpretations: as piecewise GLMs, as hierarchical mixed-effects regressions, or as regression trees with structured parameter sharing. Since RG motivates the design of these models, we use techniques from statistical physics -- specifically replica analysis -- to study their generalization properties. Specifically, we analyze the behavior of the Watanabe-Akaike Information Criterion (WAIC) as a proxy for generalization loss. This analysis yields two practical results: (i) guidance on the lattice design as a function of dataset size and predictor dimensionality; and (ii) a principled scaling law for the regularization prior when adding higher-order terms to the expansion so that one can increase model complexity without an expected increase in generalization loss. We evaluate the methodology on public datasets and find performance competitive against both blackbox methods and other intrinsically interpretable approaches.
