Thermodynamic incompleteness in non-Markovian Majorana transport I: Island dynamics and missing transport statistics

Authors: Yang Tian
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05754v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the non-Markovian dynamics of a Majorana island do not contain enough information to fully determine the transport statistics of its connected leads. It establishes a thermodynamic completeness criterion and shows that different reservoir configurations can produce identical island-state evolution while yielding different heat and charge noise. This highlights a fundamental loss of information when tracing out environmental degrees of freedom.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental limit on the reconstructibility of open quantum system dynamics, specifically showing that observing the system's internal state is insufficient to infer the full thermodynamic record of its environment.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the non-Markovian dynamics of a Majorana island are sufficient to reconstruct the full thermodynamic transport statistics (charge, heat, and noise) measured in the connected leads.

Main result. The author proves that island-state dynamics are thermodynamically incomplete because the memory kernel discards the specific identity of reservoir channels, meaning two devices with identical island relaxation can exhibit different transport noise.

Method. The study uses a Schrieffer-Wolff transformation to derive a cotunneling Hamiltonian, a Born memory expansion for the non-Markovian master equation, and the counting field method to track transport statistics.

Model / system. A floating superconducting Majorana island in the Coulomb blockade regime, featuring M Majorana zero modes coupled to structured reservoirs via tunneling contacts.

Key observables. Charge transfer, heat transfer, charge noise, heat noise, and mixed charge-energy correlations.

Important parameters / regimes. Coulomb charging energy (Ec), gate voltage (ng), cotunneling regime, and Majorana bilinear operators.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to the weak-cotunneling regime and assumes Gaussian reservoirs and analytic counting-field generating functionals.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of thermodynamic incompleteness, defines the Majorana island model in the cotunneling regime, presents a completeness theorem and a geometric/topological criterion for reconstructibility, and concludes with experimental predictions and limitations.

Abstract

We show that the complete knowledge of the non-Markovian island-state dynamics of a floating Majorana island does not, in general, determine the thermodynamic transport statistics measured in the leads. We demonstrate this statement in a Coulomb-blockaded island with $M$ Majorana zero modes coupled to structured reservoirs. In the cotunneling regime, a Schrieffer-Wolff transformation gives reservoir-assisted transitions generated by Majorana bilinears. After the reservoirs are traced out, the island state determines the memory kernel associated with each bilinear, and this is enough to predict all island-state observables within the cotunneling approximation. It is not enough to determine which lead or detector channel supplied the electron, absorbed the electron, or carried the corresponding energy exchange. This is a genuine loss of thermodynamic information, not an error in the island equation. We formulate the result as a thermodynamic completeness criterion: an island memory equation determines a transport observable only when that observable is constant over all assignments of reservoir channels that give the same island memory kernel. The criterion gives a measurable prediction. Two structured-reservoir Majorana devices can have identical island-state tomography and relaxation, but different charge noise measured separately in the leads, heat noise, and mixed charge-energy correlations. The geometry of the projection from reservoir records to island kernels and the topology of the network of tunnel contacts identify which transport information is absent from island-state dynamics.

Beating noise in frequency estimation with squeezing and memory in continuous-variable systems

Authors: Ayan Patra, Manju, Aditi Sen De, Matteo G. A. Paris

Summary. This paper investigates how squeezing and environmental memory can be used to improve frequency estimation precision in noisy continuous-variable systems. It demonstrates that squeezing can change the fundamental scaling of precision with time and that non-Markovianity can temporarily recover lost information. The work also identifies the limits of Gaussian measurements in high-squeezing regimes.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a dual-strategy approach (Hamiltonian engineering and environmental control) to combat decoherence, which is highly relevant for developing robust quantum sensors and understanding the metrological utility of non-Markovianity in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to mitigate the detrimental effects of environmental noise and decoherence on the precision of frequency estimation in continuous-variable quantum systems.

Main result. The study finds that Hamiltonian engineering via squeezing can enhance QFI scaling to $t^3$ in the short-time regime, while non-Markovian memory effects can induce information backflow to temporarily restore estimation precision; however, strong squeezing may eventually require non-Gaussian measurements to saturate the QFI.

Method. The authors use the Gaussian quantum state formalism, calculating Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) and Classical Fisher Information (CFI) using the first and second moments of the system's state under various master equations.

Model / system. A single-mode harmonic oscillator (continuous-variable system) initially in a coherent state, subject to a Hamiltonian that can include squeezing, and interacting with either a Markovian thermal bath or a non-Markovian Quantum Brownian Motion environment.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), Classical Fisher Information (CFI), and the Symmetric Logarithmic Derivative (SLD).

Important parameters / regimes. Frequency ($\omega$), squeezing parameter ($eta$), damping rate ($\gamma$), thermal photon number ($n$), and the non-Markovianity ratio ($r = \Lambda_c/\omega$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis primarily focuses on the Gaussian regime and Gaussian measurement strategies (homodyne, heterodyne, and optimized dyne).

Figures summary. Figure 2 shows time-dependent dissipation and diffusion coefficients; Figure 3 compares QFI under unitary, non-Markovian, and Markovian dynamics; Figure 5 shows CFI for different dyne measurements; Figure 6 illustrates the growing gap between QFI and CFI with increasing squeezing; Figure 7 shows the failure of fixed quadrature measurements in non-Markovian regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of frequency estimation in noisy CV systems, develops the mathematical framework for Gaussian states under Markovian and non-Markovian dynamics, analyzes the impact of squeezing on QFI scaling, evaluates the role of environmental memory, and concludes by assessing the optimality of various Gaussian measurement strategies.

Abstract

Quantum metrology promises precision beyond classical limits, yet environmental noise typically degrades the quantum resources required for such enhancement. In this work, we investigate frequency estimation in noisy continuous-variable systems, focusing on two complementary strategies to mitigate decoherence: Hamiltonian engineering and the exploitation of non-Markovian dynamics. By embedding squeezing directly into the system Hamiltonian, we show that the quantum Fisher information (QFI) may acquire a tunable higher-order time dependence, leading to enhanced sensitivity in the short-time regime. Moving beyond the Markovian approximation, we employ the quantum Brownian motion model to demonstrate that structured environments with finite memory can induce information backflow, temporarily restoring and even improving estimation precision relative to the unitary limit. We further assess the achievability of these bounds via Gaussian measurements, identifying regimes where homodyne, heterodyne, and optimized general-dyne measurements saturate the QFI, and noting that stronger squeezing widens the gap, potentially requiring non-Gaussian measurement strategies. Our results establish that jointly tailoring system Hamiltonian and environmental memory offers a viable route toward robust quantum-enhanced frequency estimation in open systems.

Comparative Study of Potts Machine Dynamics and Performance for Max-k-Cut

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 1/5 · non-equilibrium universality 1/5

Summary. This paper provides a systematic benchmark of various Potts Machine models against Ising Machines for solving multi-state optimization problems. It reveals that current Potts Machine dynamics actually underperform compared to binary Ising approaches and identifies the specific dynamical features needed to improve them.

Why it may be interesting. This is interesting for researchers in many-body dynamics and non-equilibrium systems because it links the macroscopic optimization performance of complex dynamical systems to microscopic dynamical properties like bifurcation structures and phase discretization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study evaluates whether Potts Machines (PMs) outperform Ising Machines (IMs) on multi-state combinatorial optimization problems like Max-k-Cut, specifically investigating if native multi-state representation overcomes the overhead of binary encoding.

Main result. Surprisingly, the reference Ising Machine outperforms all tested Potts Machines, and this performance gap increases when moving from Max-3-Cut to Max-4-Cut. The authors identify that performance correlates with specific dynamical properties like connected origin-to-solution branches and amplitude homogeneity.

Method. The authors use a unified dynamical systems framework of coupled ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and numerical integration (Euler-Maruyama) to simulate adiabatic annealing on GSet and random (ER50) graphs.

Model / system. The study compares several models: Non-Equilibrium Condensate (NEC), q-photon downconversion (q-PDC), q-th sub-harmonic injection locking (q-SHIL), a Polynomial PM, and a Sigmoid PM, against a reference Coherent Ising Machine (CIM) model.

Key observables. Relative optimality gap, success rate, spin amplitudes, spin phases, and cut values.

Important parameters / regimes. Phase discretization strength (gamma), inter-spin coupling strength (beta), linear amplitude gain (alpha), nonlinearity order (n), and annealing rates.

Assumptions / limitations. The study excludes certain discrete-spin PMs and phase-only models with non-Kuramoto-type coupling; it also notes a Hamiltonian mismatch between the planar Potts model and the target Max-k-Cut problem for k > 3.

Figures summary. Figures include time traces of spin evolution, box plots of optimality gaps and success rates across different graph sizes, heatmaps showing hyperparameter sensitivity, and convergence plots for simulation time and integration time steps.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the optimization problem, defines the mathematical framework for various PM and IM models, presents a comparative benchmarking on graph datasets, analyzes the correlation between dynamics and performance, and concludes with a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters.

Abstract

Combinatorial optimization problems in logistics, finance, energy, and scheduling routinely involve multi-state decision variables. Ising machines (IMs) require binary expansions (e.g., one-hot encoding) to encode such variables, whereas Potts machines (PMs) represent them natively. By doing so, PMs are expected to outperform IMs on multi-state problems. To the best of our knowledge, no systematic study of PM models has yet assessed whether this expectation holds. We therefore benchmark five representative PMs against a reference IM on Max-3-Cut and Max-4-Cut, using 800-vertex GSet graphs and random graphs of up to 50 vertices. Surprisingly, the reference IM still outperforms every PM, and the IM supremacy increases significantly in going from Max-3-Cut to Max-4-Cut. These results provide clear evidence that current PM dynamics underperform relative to binary approaches, even in regimes where they are presumed advantageous. We provide a way forward by quantifying the underperformance of current PMs, as well as by identifying three dynamical properties that correlate strongly with their performance ranking. Our work stresses the need for more systematic assessments of algorithmic performance in order to guide the design of more effective Potts machines.

Breakdown of Emergent Chiral Order and Defect Chaos in Nonreciprocal Flocks

Authors: Charlotte Myin, Suropriya Saha, Benoît Mahault
Type: both · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05448v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that chiral order in nonreciprocal active matter is unstable and breaks down into a state of spatiotemporal chaos driven by topological defects. It reveals that the coupling between density and orientation leads to non-universal scaling exponents that deviate from standard KPZ predictions. This provides new insights into how nonreciprocity modifies the large-scale physics of active systems.

Why it may be interesting. The study explores how broken action-reaction symmetry and nonreciprocal interactions drive non-equilibrium phase transitions and unconventional scaling, which is highly relevant to understanding driven-dissipative many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the stability of emergent chiral order in two-dimensional nonreciprocal flocking mixtures and the origin of non-universal scaling behavior in the resulting defect-chaos phase.

Main result. Chiral order is found to be generically unstable due to the proliferation of topological defects, leading to a defect-chaos phase with non-universal scaling exponents driven by density-polarity coupling.

Method. The authors combine large-scale agent-based simulations with a coarse-grained hydrodynamic continuum description and Floquet stability analysis.

Model / system. A binary mixture of two species of Vicsek-like active particles in a 2D plane, characterized by asymmetric (nonreciprocal) alignment interactions and angular noise.

Key observables. Global polarity, phase shift between species, density and orientational correlation functions, and correlation length.

Important parameters / regimes. Nonreciprocity strength (delta-chi), noise strength (eta), and system size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The continuum hydrodynamic description is derived near the ordering threshold and may not be valid far from it; stability is assessed via the eigenvalues of the evolution operator.

Figures summary. Phase diagrams in the (delta-chi, eta) plane, snapshots of band phases, time series of global polarities, and plots of correlation functions and scaling exponents.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the nonreciprocal flocking model, presents a phase diagram, develops a continuum theory, performs stability analysis of chiral states, and uses large-scale simulations to characterize the defect-chaos phase and its scaling properties.

Abstract

We show that chiral order in two-dimensional nonreciprocal flocking mixtures is generically unstable. Combining large-scale agent-based simulations with a coarse-grained continuum description, we demonstrate that rotating chiral states emerging from antisymmetric couplings are destroyed by the proliferation of topological defects. The resulting dynamics is spatiotemporally chaotic and characterized by a finite correlation length that diverges as nonreciprocity vanishes. On length scales below this cutoff, density and orientational order fluctuations remain scale-free, but the associated scaling exhibits nonuniversal exponents. We attribute this atypical behavior to the coupling between density and order, which causes topological defects to act as persistent sources of nonlinear fluctuations.

Operationally Admissible Post-Quantum Correlations from a Standard Quantum Walk

Authors: Marcos C. de Oliveira
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05477v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that a standard quantum walk can generate correlations that exceed the Tsirelson bound if the initial coin state is prepared using an extended, non-positive framework. However, it reveals that these post-quantum features are practically hidden from observers using realistic, coarse-grained position measurements as the system evolves. The work highlights a fundamental gap between the existence of post-quantum resources and their operational accessibility.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a controlled theoretical platform to study the boundary between quantum and post-quantum regimes and highlights how measurement resolution and structure can fundamentally limit the observation of non-classical resources in many-body-like dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether post-quantum correlations (violating the Tsirelson bound) can be operationally accessed within a standard quantum walk framework and distinguishing between their existence in a state versus their accessibility under realistic measurement constraints.

Main result. The study finds that while standard quantum walk dynamics can support post-quantum correlations via an extended coin preparation, these correlations are strongly suppressed and become inaccessible under physically natural, coarse-grained position measurements as the walk time increases.

Method. The authors use a numerical approach involving randomized searches over preparation and measurement directions, comparing optimized Schmidt-aligned measurements against coarse-grained position binning, and employing a quasiprobability reconstruction to emulate non-positive coin preparations.

Model / system. A one-dimensional, discrete-time, coined quantum walk on a lattice (Z) consisting of a two-level coin and a high-dimensional walker subspace, utilizing standard Hadamard coin and nearest-neighbor shift dynamics.

Key observables. CHSH Bell parameter (S), joint probabilities p(a, b|i, j), and Schmidt coefficients.

Important parameters / regimes. Bloch vector magnitude of the coin preparation (||r||), walk time (T), and position binning threshold ratio (x0/T).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes that physical consistency can be maintained via admissibility (non-negative probabilities) and no-signaling at the level of observed statistics, even if the underlying state preparation is non-positive.

Figures summary. Figure 1 summarizes the separation between existence and accessibility; Figure 2 shows CHSH values exceeding Tsirelson's bound for Schmidt-aligned measurements; Figure 3 shows the admissibility margin; Figure 6 and 7 demonstrate the suppression of Bell violations in coarse-grained measurements as walk time increases.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of post-quantum correlations, defines the extended preparation framework and quantum walk model, presents a benchmark using optimized measurements to show existence, then explores the accessibility gap using coarse-grained measurements, and concludes by discussing the scaling effects and experimental implications.

Abstract

It is shown that a standard one-dimensional coined discrete-time quantum walk can generate operationally admissible post-quantum correlations in a coin-position Bell scenario, without any modification of its unitary nearest-neighbor dynamics. Post-quantum features enter exclusively through an extended operational preparation of the coin, described by a complementarity-violating Hermitian trace-one operator, while physical consistency is enforced solely at the level of observable statistics via admissibility and no-signaling. The extended preparation admits an experimental emulation through a two-component quasiprobability reconstruction over physical coin states, at the price of an increased sampling overhead. The walk-generated coin-position entanglement can support CHSH values exceeding Tsirelson's bound, even though the walk dynamics remains fully standard. We also show that physically natural coarse-grained position measurements can render such post-quantum correlations operationally inaccessible, strongly suppressing observable Bell violations. The purpose here is to contrast the separation between the existence of post-quantum behavior and its accessibility under realistic measurement constraints.

Learning Gaussian optical states with quantum computers

Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05325v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · Tavis-Cummings & cavity-many-emitter 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes a method to use quantum computers to efficiently learn the properties of multi-mode Gaussian optical states. It demonstrates that by mapping optical information onto qubits, one can achieve a sample complexity that scales logarithmically with the number of modes and more efficiently with energy than classical methods.

Why it may be interesting. It bridges quantum information theory with quantum optics by providing a rigorous scaling analysis for using quantum hardware to perform high-precision sensing and imaging of electromagnetic fields.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the sample complexity required to learn all parameters (means and covariances) of an n-mode Gaussian optical state using a quantum computer.

Main result. The authors establish that the number of required state copies scales logarithmically with the number of modes n, providing a polynomial advantage in energy dependence compared to existing CV classical shadow protocols.

Method. The protocol uses a Jaynes-Cummings interaction to transduce optical modes into a qubit register, followed by an iterative algorithm and classical shadows to extract Gaussian moments from Pauli measurements.

Model / system. An n-mode continuous-variable bosonic electromagnetic field interacting with an n-qubit register via a local Jaynes-Cummings Hamiltonian.

Key observables. One- and two-qubit Pauli observables (e.g., X, Y, Z, XX, YY) on the transduced qubit register.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of modes (n), maximum energy per mode (E_max), target additive error (epsilon), and failure probability (delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The optical state is Gaussian; the Jaynes-Cummimng interaction is kept in the perturbative regime; and the transduction process is ideal (ignoring experimental noise).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the transduction schematic; Figure 2 illustrates the inductive proof for state selection; Figure 3 demonstrates the rapid convergence of the error reduction algorithm.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Gaussian state learning, defines the QCIS transduction model via Jaynes-Cummings interaction, provides a mathematical reduction of the n-mode problem to a two-mode problem, presents an iterative algorithm for parameter extraction, and concludes with a complexity analysis comparing the results to CV classical shadows.

Abstract

Recent results have established dramatic advantages in learning properties of quantum states when a quantum computer is available to process or jointly measure multiple copies of the unknown quantum state. Learning tasks can be accomplished with exponentially fewer copies of the state when compared to optimized classical learning strategies that are restricted to measuring one copy of the state at a time. While these results were established in abstract settings and for artificial learning tasks, they motivate the application of quantum computers to imaging and sensing of weak electromagnetic fields since these settings are ultimately concerned with the learning of unknown quantum states. In this work we apply these new results in quantum learning to the problem of learning Gaussian states of the electromagnetic field, which are germane since they describe most fields used in imaging and sensing. In order to connect with quantum learning theory, we consider the transduction of an $n$-mode Gaussian state into a register of qubits on a quantum computer followed by optimized measurements on these qubits to extract the parameters defining the original Gaussian state. We rigorously bound the number of copies of the Gaussian state required to achieve worst-case additive error in parameter estimates. The scaling of this bound with $n$ is exponentially better than naïve strategies for characterizing Gaussian states and matches recently derived bounds for characterization of Gaussian states using continuous-variable (CV) classical shadows. In addition, our bound has a polynomially better dependence on the energy of the multimode Gaussian state compared to the CV shadows protocol.

Summary. The paper proposes that ultrafast laser pulses create squeezed states of quasiparticle pairs in superfluid helium. It shows that the observed birefringence oscillations arise from the interference of multiple vibrational modes, including rotons and maxons.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a macroscopic manifestation of quantum squeezing in a condensed matter system and provides a framework for understanding how ultrafast pulses drive non-equilibrium quantum states in many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Explaining the origin of ultrafast birefringence oscillations and the nonzero initial phase observed in superfluid helium following intense femtosecond laser pulse excitation.

Main result. The pump pulse induces an anisotropic squeezed quantum state of quasiparticle pairs, where the observed birefringence signal is a superposition of contributions from various modes (rotons, maxons, and Pitaevskii's plateau).

Method. A quantum mechanical treatment of the permittivity tensor operator and the evolution of density fluctuation operators using Heisenberg equations of motion and the stationary-phase approximation.

Model / system. Bulk liquid 4He below the lambda point, modeled as a gas of polarizable quasiparticles (rotons, maxons, and Pitaevskii's plateau) treated as harmonic oscillators under a pump electric field.

Key observables. Ultrafast optical birefringence oscillations, polarization change of a time-delayed probe pulse, and local refractive-index modulation.

Important parameters / regimes. Pump intensity ~ 10^12 W/cm^2, pulse duration ~ 70 fs, roton energy ~ 17 K, and low-temperature regime where signal is dominated by zero-point vibrations.

Assumptions / limitations. The pump electric field is treated classically, the system starts in thermal equilibrium, and the short-pulse approximation is used for the evolution operator.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a Wigner quasi-probability distribution in phase space, comparing a stationary thermal state (circular symmetry) to a skewed squeezed state produced by the pump.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the problem of birefringence oscillations, introduces a Hamiltonian model for quasiparticle squeezing, applies mathematical approximations (stationary-phase, short-pulse) to calculate the response, and analyzes the resulting signal and phase.

Abstract

Ultrafast birefringence oscillations observed in superfluid helium provide evidence for anisotropic quantum squeezing of quasiparticle pairs. The measured response is a superposition of contributions from all vibrational modes, with dominant contributions from rotons, maxons, and Pitaevskii's plateau. The nonzero initial phase follows naturally from multimode interference.

Tight Contraction Rates for Primitive Channels under Quantum $f$-Divergences

Authors: Matthew Simon Tan, Marco Tomamichel, Ian George
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06452v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper establishes new, tight upper bounds on the rate at which quantum channels contract different measures of distinguishability toward a stationary state. By relating f-divergences to chi^2-divergences, the authors provide a framework to calculate exact contraction rates for specific families of quantum divergences. This advances the understanding of information loss in non-commutative quantum processes.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems as it provides fundamental bounds on how quickly quantum information/distinguishability decays under the influence of noise and environmental interaction.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the tightest possible asymptotic contraction rates for primitive quantum channels under various quantum f-divergences and establishing conditions for the tightness of these bounds in a non-commutative setting.

Main result. The authors prove that the asymptotic contraction rate of a primitive channel to its stationary state is upper bounded by the SDPI constant of any non-commutative chi^2_g-divergence, and establish that this bound is tight under quantum detailed balance conditions.

Method. The study employs Taylor expansions of f-divergences near the identity to establish a local reverse Pinsker inequality, utilizes spectral analysis of channel eigenvalues, and applies the concept of quantum f-detailed balance.

Model / system. The mathematical model consists of primitive, time-homogeneous quantum channels acting on density matrices in a finite-dimensional Hilbert space, assuming a unique, full-rank stationary state.

Key observables. Strong Data-Processing Inequality (SDPI) constants, quantum f-divergences, and the asymptotic contraction rate of the channel.

Important parameters / regimes. The asymptotic regime (n approaching infinity), the primitivity of the channel, and the smoothness (thrice continuous differentiability) of the function f.

Assumptions / limitations. The quantum channels must be primitive, the stationary state must be full-rank, and the function f must be thrice continuously differentiable in a neighborhood of 1.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing a local reverse Pinsker inequality, moves to proving general upper bounds for asymptotic contraction rates, provides conditions for the tightness of these bounds using detailed balance, and concludes with specific applications to Petz, Matsumoto, and Hirche-Tomamichel f-divergences.

Abstract

Data-processing inequalities capture the phenomenon that two probability distributions can only become less distinguishable under any common post-processing. For more fine-grained inequalities, one turns to strong data-processing inequality (SDPI) constants, which give the strongest inequalities for a given channel and reference state for a fixed measure of distinguishability. These quantities have been used to quantify the rate at which time-homogeneous Markov chains contract towards a fixed point both in the classical and quantum setting. In this work, we establish that quantum $f$-divergences satisfy a local reverse Pinsker inequality, which implies the asymptotic contraction rate of a primitive channel to its stationary state is upper bounded by the SDPI constant of any non-commutative $χ^2$-divergence. Using quantum-detailed balance, we establish a sufficient condition for these bounds to be tight. Finally, we apply these results to Petz, Matsumoto, and Hirche-Tomamichel $f$-divergences, establishing new and strengthening previously known results.

Universal Neural Propagator: Learning Time Evolution in Many-Body Quantum Systems

Authors: Zihao Qi, Christopher Earls, Yang Peng

Summary. The paper presents the Universal Neural Propagator (UNP), a machine learning model that learns to predict the time-evolution of many-body quantum systems. Unlike previous methods, it can simultaneously generalize to new driving protocols and new initial states. This approach enables efficient, scalable simulation of driven quantum matter beyond the limits of exact diagonalization.

Why it may be interesting. It proposes a paradigm shift from learning individual quantum states to learning the underlying operators, providing a foundation model approach that could be extended to dissipative dynamics via Liouville-space superoperators.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing neural network approaches for quantum dynamics are limited to transferring across either Hamiltonians or initial states, but not both simultaneously, requiring new simulations to be re-performed if parameters change.

Main result. The introduction of the Universal Neural Propagator (UNP), which achieves simultaneous transferability across driving protocols and initial states, maintaining high accuracy even for system sizes beyond exact diagonalization.

Method. A self-supervised hybrid architecture combining a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) to process driving protocols and an autoregressive Transformer to predict the time-evolution propagator in a doubled Hilbert space.

Model / system. A two-dimensional driven transverse-field Ising model on a square lattice with time-dependent longitudinal and transverse fields.

Key observables. State fidelity, local magnetization (z and x directions), energy per spin, and ZZ-correlators.

Important parameters / regimes. System sizes of 4x4 and 6x6; driving protocols modeled via random Fourier series; training on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes general initial states can be expressed as linear combinations of computational basis states; scalability is limited by VRAM for larger system sizes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the UNP framework; Figure 2 details the FNO-Transformer architecture; Figure 3 demonstrates accuracy and transferability on 4x4 systems; Figure 4 shows fine-tuning efficiency; Figure 5 illustrates error reduction after fine-tuning; Figure 6 compares 6x6 results with tDMRG.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the limitation of single-trajectory simulations, presents the UNP architecture (FNO and Transformer), describes the self-supervised training objective based on the Schrödinger equation, validates the model via 2D Ising model benchmarks (accuracy, transferability, and scalability), and discusses future extensions to open quantum systems.

Abstract

Conventional approaches to simulating quantum many-body dynamics produce a single trajectory: if the Hamiltonian or the initial state is changed, the computation must be re-performed. Recent efforts toward foundation models have begun to address this limitation, yet existing methods transfer across either Hamiltonians or initial states, but not both. In this work, we introduce the Universal Neural Propagator (UNP), a single, unified model that learns the functional mapping from driving protocols to time-evolution propagators. Trained in an entirely self-supervised way, a single UNP model predicts dynamics across a function space of driving protocols and an exponentially large Hilbert space of initial states simultaneously. We benchmark on a two-dimensional driven Ising model and demonstrate the UNP's accuracy and transferability across product and entangled initial states, as well as for both in- and out-of-distribution driving protocols. The UNP remains accurate at system sizes beyond exact diagonalization, and can be efficiently fine-tuned across all initial states using observable data. By shifting the object of learning from quantum states to operators, this work opens a route toward transferable simulation of driven quantum matter.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that when a controller is dissipative and violates detailed balance, the traditional preference for infinitely slow (quasistatic) protocols breaks down. Abstract

The optimal control of passive systems in equilibrium typically favours quasistatic (infinite-time) protocols. We show that a breakdown of quasistatic optimality occurs when the controller itself is dissipative. Concretely, we study a Brownian particle confined by a harmonic trap with stochastically fluctuating stiffness, driven by an external protocol. When these fluctuations violate detailed balance, the probe-controller coupling continuously exchanges work with the system, altering the optimisation landscape. In this regime, optimal protocols are characterised by a finite duration which vanishes above a critical fluctuation strength. This transition can be directly observed in a short-time expansion of the mean work functional. When imposing an endpoint constraint, the transition to zero duration disappears and finite duration protocols remain optimal for all values of the controller fluctuations. These results demonstrate that finite-time optimality can emerge in passive systems under nonequilibrium control.

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical framework to understand how chemical energy can be converted into mechanical motion through feedback. It identifies a specific phase-relation criterion, analogous to Rayleigh's criterion for acoustic instability, that predicts when chemical driving leads to negative friction and sustained active motion.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the conditions under which non-equilibrium chemical driving generates mechanical instability, such as negative friction and sustained rotational motion, through chemo-mechanical coupling.

Main result. The authors derive a Rayleigh-like criterion for the onset of activity based on the phase relation between entropic and frenetic contributions, showing that negative friction can emerge when the frenetic (time-symmetric) component dominates.

Method. The study uses a reduction/integration approach (Born-Oppenheimer-like) to integrate out fast chemical degrees of freedom, applying non-equilibrium linear response theory and singular perturbation theory in the weak coupling limit.

Model / system. A Newtonian mechanical probe (underdamped particle) coupled to a chemical bath of $N$ independent Markov jump processes. The coupling is mediated by a potential $U(x, \sigma)$ that influences both the mechanical force and the chemical transition rates.

Abstract

Instabilities in thermodynamic systems are often undesirable, as they can lead to loss of control or even catastrophic behavior. Yet, the same mechanisms can also generate rich nonequilibrium behavior and may play a constructive role in living systems. We introduce a theoretical framework, inspired by Rayleigh's analysis of thermoacoustic instabilities, to study the emergence of mechanical activity. In particular, we derive Rayleigh-like criteria governing the onset of activity and the generation of rotational motion in a slow Newtonian probe coupled to driven chemical processes, described by Markov jump processes. These criteria are expressed in terms of the phase relation between entropic and frenetic contributions, providing a transparent condition for when chemical driving results in sustained rotational or active mechanical motion.
