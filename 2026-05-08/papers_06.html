Quantum Kernels for Parity-Structured Classification: A Hybrid Pipeline

Authors: Tushar Pandey
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05625v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that quantum kernels can outperform classical methods specifically when tackling high-order parity-structured classification tasks. It identifies a clear complexity threshold where the quantum ZZ feature map's ability to capture multi-feature correlations provides a genuine advantage over classical RBF kernels.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether quantum kernels provide a genuine advantage over classical kernels for high-order parity (XOR) classification tasks, and determining if this advantage stems from the quantum circuit's expressivity or merely the feature encoding.

Main result. A phase transition in performance was identified: for low parity complexity (n <= 8), classical binary encoding dominates, but for high complexity (n >= 9), the quantum ZZ kernel achieves a significant advantage, outperforming classical methods by up to 12 percentage points.

Method. The study uses exact statevector simulations of a hybrid pipeline, comparing a ZZ quantum feature map against classical RBF SVMs and a 'binary encoding ablation' study to isolate circuit effects from encoding effects.

Model / system. A quantum feature map using n qubits with a ZZ structure (comprising single-qucept rotations and entangling Z_i Z_j gates) applied to synthetic 500-dimensional data with n-way XOR parity labels.

Key observables. Test accuracy, Kernel-Target Alignment (KTA), and parity complexity (n).

Important parameters / regimes. Parity complexity (n = 5 to 11), number of ZZ repetitions (r = 3), and label noise (22%).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes exact statevector simulation without hardware noise (shot noise, decoherence) and uses an 'oracle' feature selection method which may not reflect real-world feature discovery.

Figures summary. Figure 2 shows the comparison of Kernel-Target Alignment, highlighting the 7x superiority of the quantum ZZ kernel over RBF at n=11; Table I illustrates the performance transition/complexity sweep.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the parity classification problem, describes the quantum and classical models/encoding, details the simulation methodology, presents the complexity sweep results and ablation study, and concludes with a discussion on the source of quantum advantage and limitations.

Abstract

Parity (XOR) classification requires detecting discrete, high-order feature interactions that smooth classical kernels cannot efficiently capture. We study how quantum kernel advantage depends on parity complexity, the number of features entering the XOR rule, and find a clear threshold behavior. We pair a ZZ quantum feature map with binary {0, pi} encoding (features median thresholded before circuit input) to expose parity structure. A binary encoding ablation, RBF SVM trained on the identical {0, pi} features, separates encoding from circuit effects: at low complexity (n = 5 features), binary RBF achieves 83.4% +/- 1.7% and the quantum kernel 81.2% +/- 1.9%, showing encoding drives performance there. At high complexity (n = 11 features, 11 qubits, r = 3 ZZ repetitions), all classical methods collapse to near-random (approx. 50%), binary RBF reaches only 54.3% +/- 1.1%, and the quantum ZZ kernel achieves 66.3% +/- 3.2% (mean +/- std, 10 seeds), a +12.0 percentage-point margin over the binary ablation and approx. 7x higher kernel-target alignment (0.094 +/- 0.020 vs. 0.013 +/- 0.001). These results identify parity complexity as a concrete axis along which genuine quantum kernel advantage, not attributable to encoding alone, emerges.

Singularity Resolution in Quantum Cosmology via Page-Wootters Formalism

Authors: Vishal, Malay K. Nandy
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06093v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper uses the Page-Wootters relational formalism to show how time and dynamics can emerge from a stationary quantum state in a Bianchi Type-I universe. It demonstrates that quantum effects effectively resolve the classical Big Bang singularity by making the probability of zero volume vanish. The study also establishes necessary constraints on the internal clock to maintain physically valid, positive probability densities.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the problem of time in canonical quantum gravity and the resolution of the classical Big Bang singularity in a timeless Wheeler-DeWitt framework.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the conditional probability density of the universe's volume vanishes at the zero-volume limit, indicating quantum resolution of the singularity, while also identifying constraints on the clock variable to ensure positive probability density.

Method. The Page-Wootters relational formalism is used to derive dynamics by conditioning a global stationary state on a clock subsystem, employing a Gaussian superposition of momentum eigenstates and a Klein-Gordon type inner product.

Model / system. A plane-symmetric Bianchi Type-I universe within the Wheeler-DeWitt framework, modeled using Misner variables where the anisotropy variable beta acts as an internal clock and alpha represents the volume.

Key observables. The conditional probability density of the universe's volume (alpha) relative to the clock reading (beta).

Important parameters / regimes. The mean momentum (k0), the Gaussian wavepacket width (sigma), and the clock reading (beta_0).

Assumptions / limitations. The universe is treated as a closed system without an external observer; the clock Hamiltonian is quadratic in momentum; and the clock does not interact with the remaining degrees of freedom.

Figures summary. Figure 1 displays plots of the conditional probability density for the volume parameter.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of time and singularity, implements the Page-Wootters framework on a Bianchi Type-I model, constructs the wavefunctions and inner products, derives the probability densities, and analyzes the resulting constraints and singularity resolution.

Abstract

We investigate the problem of classical big bang singularity in a plane-symmetric Bianchi type-I universe within the Wheeler-DeWitt (WDW) framework of quantum gravity. To address the problem of time, we employ the Page-Wootters formalism, which provides a relational notion of dynamics by conditioning the global state on a clock subsystem. Using Misner variables, the WDW equation assumes a Klein-Gordon (KG) type form. Its general solution is constructed as a Gaussian superposition of momentum eigenstates, resulting in an entangled global state between the clock and the remaining subsystem. Within this relational framework, we construct conditional states and obtain the corresponding probability density consistent with the KG-type inner product. The resulting conditional probability density vanishes in the limit of zero volume for all clock values, indicating quantum resolution of the classical singularity. We further show that positivity of the probability density imposes constraints on the admissible clock values, which depend on the parameters of the Gaussian wavepacket. These results highlight the essential role of quantum correlations in the emergence of relational dynamics, and demonstrate that the Page-Wootters formalism provides a consistent and nonsingular probabilistic description of quantum cosmology.

Superconducting and correlated phases of an effective Hubbard model on the BCC lattice

Authors: Theja N. De Silva
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06422v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores the complex phase diagram of a 3D Hubbard model on a BCC lattice, which serves as a model for alkali-doped fullerides. It reveals that the transition to superconductivity is first-order and identifies a multicritical region where Mott insulating, antiferromagnetic, and Fermi-liquid phases coexist. The work provides a unified perspective on how lattice geometry and electron-phonon-mediated interactions drive competing electronic orders.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the competition and interplay between superconducting, magnetic, and Mott insulating phases in a 3D BCC lattice, specifically motivated by alkali-doped fulleride molecular solids.

Main result. The study demonstrates a first-order normal-to-superconducting phase transition and identifies a temperature-interaction phase diagram in the strong-coupling regime featuring a narrow region where Fermi-liquid, antiferromagnetic, and Mott insulating phases compete.

Method. The authors employ two approaches: the Hatsugai-Kohmoto model supplemented by a BCS-type pairing term for the intermediate-coupling regime, and a spin-rotationally invariant slave-boson formalism for the strong-coupling regime.

Model / system. An effective Hubbard model on a body-centered-cubic (BCC) lattice, incorporating renormalized on-site Coulomb interactions (U_eff) and an effective inverted Hund's coupling (J_eff) originating from electron-phonon interactions.

Key observables. Superconducting order parameter (delta), critical temperature (T_C), quasiparticle weight, and free energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Effective on-site interaction (U_eff), effective Hund's coupling (J_eff), hopping amplitude (t), and the super-exchange interaction scale (J_ex).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglects off-site Coulomb interactions, spin-flip interactions, and orbital degrees of freedom; assumes diagonal electron hopping between molecular orbitals; uses the anti-adiabatic limit for electron-phonon interactions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows effective on-site interactions as a function of C60 volume; Figure 6 shows a three-phase structure for the BCC lattice similar to a 2D square lattice.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical motivation (fullerides), defines the effective Hubbard model, presents the intermediate-coupling analysis using the HK-BCS model, details the strong-coupling analysis using the slave-boson approach, and concludes with a discussion of the resulting phase diagram and limitations.

Abstract

We investigate the electronic phases of an effective Hubbard model on the body-centered-cubic lattice, motivated by alkali-doped fulleride molecular solids. The model incorporates renormalized on-site interactions and an effective inverted Hund's coupling originating from electron-phonon interactions. To access complementary interaction regimes, we employ two theoretical approaches. In the intermediate-coupling regime, the on-site repulsive interaction is approximated by a long-range interaction in momentum space, yielding an exactly solvable Hatsugai-Kohmoto model supplemented by a BCS-type pairing term. Within this framework, we analyze the superconducting instability and demonstrate a first-order normal-superconducting phase transition, characterized by a discontinuous jump of the order parameter. In the strong-coupling regime, where pairing fluctuations are suppressed, we apply the spin rotationally invariant slave-boson formalism to map out the temperature-interaction phase diagram. This analysis reveals first-order transitions between a Fermi-liquid phase, an antiferromagnetic phase, and a Mott insulating phase, with a narrow intermediate region where all three phases compete. The resulting phase diagram captures the interplay of itinerancy, magnetic order, and Mott localization in three dimensions and provides a unified perspective on superconducting and correlation-driven phenomena in fulleride-inspired lattice systems.

Surface-Code Thresholds and Qubit Footprints in Shuttling-Based Spin-Qubit Railways

Authors: Arun John Moncy, Reza Dastbasteh, Josu Etxezarreta Martinez, Ryo Nagai, Pedro M. Crespo, Normann Mertig, Charles Smith, Ruben M. Otxoa
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05881v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how to implement fault-tolerant surface codes on a 1D-like 'railway' of silicon spin qubits that use electron shuttling to solve wiring constraints. It demonstrates that by moving check qubits instead of data qubits and using the XZZX code to match the inherent dephasing noise, the physical hardware required for large-scale quantum computing can be significantly reduced.

Why it may be interesting. The paper provides deep insights into how tailoring topological error correction to the specific, asymmetric noise profiles of a physical platform (dephasing-biased transport) can drastically reduce the resource overhead for fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the wiring fan-out bottleneck in large-scale 2D spin-qubit arrays by evaluating fault-tolerant surface codes on a restricted 2xN 'railway' architecture using electron shuttling.

Main result. Shuttling check qubits instead of data qubits improves thresholds, and using the XZZX surface code under dephasing-biased noise allows a distance-7 code to reach the 'Megaquop' regime with a 72% reduction in qubit footprint.

Method. Circuit-level noise modeling using the STIM package and Minimum-Weight Perfect Matching (MWPM) decoding to evaluate error thresholds and logical error rates.

Model / system. A 2xN silicon spin-qubit 'railway' architecture utilizing coherent electron shuttling, modeled with a time-dependent Hamiltonian including external magnetic fields, micromagnet gradients, and Spin-Orbit Coupling (Rashba and Dresselhaus).

Key observables. Error thresholds (physical error rate p), logical error rates, noise bias (ratio of Z to X/Y errors), and qubit footprint (hardware overhead).

Important parameters / regimes. Code distance (d=3 to 17), noise bias (eta), idling factor, and physical error rate (p = 10^-3 to 3*10^-3).

Assumptions / limitations. Quasi-static noise limit, Pauli-twirled approximation for uncorrelated errors, and a unidirectional 'Train Schedule' for syndrome extraction.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the 2xN railway architecture schematic; Figure 2 shows a d=5 XZZX lattice; Figure 3 illustrates stabilizer extraction and hook errors; Figure 5/6 demonstrate the optimized extraction order and 'Train Schedule'.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the wiring bottleneck and railway architecture, defines the physical Hamiltonian and noise model, presents the syndrome extraction protocol (Train Schedule), compares CSS vs. XZZX codes under various noise biases, and concludes with hardware footprint analysis.

Abstract

We present a fault-tolerant mapping of rotated surface codes onto a $2\times N$ silicon spin-qubit railway architecture, utilizing electron shuttling to resolve the wiring fan-out bottleneck. Employing circuit-level noise modeling, we evaluate threshold performances across various noise biases. We demonstrate that shuttling check qubits instead of data qubits fundamentally improves system thresholds. Crucially, under a noise model biased towards dephasing for spin-qubit shuttling, the non-CSS XZZX surface code outperforms standard CSS variants. By tailoring the topological code to this specific inherent bias, we show that the Megaquop footprint is achievable with a distance 7 code requiring a $p = 10^{-3}$ physical error rate, highlighting a pathway for substantial hardware reductions in early fault-tolerant quantum processors.

Systematic Extraction of Exact Yang-Mills Solutions via Algebraic Tensor Ring Decomposition

Authors: Yu-Xuan Zhang, Jing-Ling Chen
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06379v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new algebraic method to systematically find exact solutions to the non-linear Yang-Mills equations. By using tensor ring decomposition, the authors derive stable configurations like color waves and flux tubes that provide analytical insights into non-perturbative phenomena like mass gap generation and confinement.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of extracting exact classical solutions from the non-linear partial differential equations of Yang-Mills theory and understanding the stability of these solutions against tachyonic vacuum instabilities.

Main result. The authors successfully extracted exact solutions for SU(2) and SU(3) gauge theories, including relativistic color waves and dynamical flux tubes, and proved their structural stability against the Savvidy instability.

Method. An algebraic tensor ring decomposition framework that maps non-linear PDEs into tractable differential-algebraic systems using differential ideals and quotient rings (elliptic and Artinian).

Model / system. Non-Abelian Yang-Mills theory (specifically SU(2) and SU(3) sectors) in Minkowski spacetime, featuring configurations such as relativistic color waves, helical dyonic flux tubes, and chaotic SU(3) resonances.

Key observables. Energy-momentum tensor (energy density, momentum flux), field strength components, effective mass, and isospin charge.

Important parameters / regimes. Temporal frequency (omega), longitudinal wavenumber (k), azimuthal winding number (n), geometric coupling constant (gamma), and the temporal dominance condition (M^2 > 0).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes the validity of evaluating the system over specific algebraic rings and that certain field components can be constrained to proportional rays.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the workflow from PDEs to exact solutions; Figure 3 displays radial profiles of SU(2) helical flux tubes; Figure 4 shows 3D orbital trajectories of SU(3) chaotic attractors and the kinetic cancellation mechanism.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the algebraic tensor ring decomposition framework, applies it to SU(2) color waves and helical flux tubes to demonstrate mass gap and screening, extends the analysis to SU(3) dynamics and chaotic oscillators, and concludes with a semiclassical stability analysis against the Savvidy instability.

Abstract

The non-linear nature of Yang-Mills theory presents a challenge for extracting exact classical solutions, which are useful for understanding non-perturbative vacuum structures. In this paper, an algebraic tensor ring decomposition framework is introduced to systematically map the non-linear partial differential equations (PDEs) of Yang-Mills theory into tractable differential-algebraic systems. By promoting static pure-gauge backgrounds to dynamical variables, the reference state acts as a geometric template whose Maurer-Cartan forms generate the algebraic cross-terms necessary to stabilize non-linear self-interactions. To analytically resolve the resulting differential ideals, specific differential-algebraic quotient rings are employed as evaluation tools, and the solution space is organized by an algebraic bifurcation analysis. Applying this framework, three distinct classes of exact solutions are extracted: (i) relativistic $SU(2)$ color waves evaluated over an elliptic quotient ring, where the differential ideal bifurcates into a Decoupled Branch and two Coupled Branches, the latter exhibiting mass gap generation; (ii) dynamical dyonic flux tubes obtained from a time-dependent helical template, where the Gauss law ideal bifurcates the system into Coulomb, Dyonic, and symmetric Meissner branches. In the Meissner branch, an Artinian asymptotic truncation yields Bessel-type exponential screening, stabilized by a temporal dominance condition; and (iii) dynamical $SU(3)$ configurations where the Gauss law ideal bifurcates the solution space into four distinct phases. The non-trivial branches enforce a kinetic cancellation mechanism that maps the amplitude dynamics onto a generalized $x^2y^2$ chaotic oscillator. Across these settings, the framework provides a methodical approach to characterize the classical solution space of strongly coupled gauge theories.

The Interplay of Data Structure and Imbalance in the Learning Dynamics of Diffusion Models

Authors: Flavio Nicoletti, Chenxiao Ma, Enrico Ventura, Luca Saglietti, Stefano Sarao Mannelli
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06367v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical and empirical framework to explain how data heterogeneity and imbalance cause diffusion models to learn different classes at different rates. It reveals that class variance is the dominant factor in determining whether a model generalizes or memorizes a specific sub-population first.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how class-specific data structures (variance, geometry) and sampling imbalance shape the training dynamics, specifically the timing of generalization and memorization in diffusion models.

Main result. Established a hierarchy where class variance is the primary driver of learning order, while sampling imbalance acts as a modulator that can reverse this order and delay the learning of minority classes.

Method. A high-dimensional analytical framework using Random Matrix Theory and Gaussian Equivalence, validated against numerical simulations of Random Feature models and U-Net diffusion models on Fashion MNIST and CIFAR.

Model / system. The study analyzes score-based diffusion models (DDPM) and Random Feature models trained on heterogeneous Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) representing imbalanced datasets.

Key observables. Feature-covariance spectrum, class-specific speciation times, generalization and memorization windows, and memorization fraction based on nearest-neighbor distance ratios.

Important parameters / regimes. Class variance, centroid geometry (norms), sampling imbalance (mixing proportions), diffusion time (t), and training time (tau).

Assumptions / limitations. Data follows a Gaussian Mixture Model; the analysis relies on high-dimensional asymptotic limits; the model uses a random feature architecture; classes are assumed to be well-separated.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate how imbalance induces separation in speciation times, show the alignment between theoretical error curves and simulations, and demonstrate the correlation between memorization gaps and class variance/geometry.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of class-dependent dynamics, develops a high-dimensional analytical framework using RMT, provides theoretical derivations for spectral edges and error dynamics, and validates these findings through large-scale experiments on Fashion MNIST and CIFAR datasets.

Abstract

Real-world datasets are inherently heterogeneous, yet how per-class structural differences and sampling imbalance shape the training dynamics of diffusion models-and potentially exacerbate disparities-remains poorly understood. While models typically transition from an initial phase of generalization to memorizing the training set, existing theory assumes homogeneous data, leaving open how class imbalance and heterogeneity reshape these dynamics. In this work, we develop a high-dimensional analytical framework to study class-dependent learning in score-based diffusion models. Analyzing a random-features model trained on Gaussian mixtures, we derive the feature-covariance spectrum to characterize per-class generalization and memorization times. We reveal the explicit hierarchy governing these dynamics: class variance is the primary determinant of learning order-consistently favoring higher-variance classes-while centroid geometry plays a secondary role. Sampling imbalance acts as a modulator that can reverse this ordering and, under strong imbalance, forces minority classes to acquire distinct, delayed speciation times during backward diffusion. Together, these results suggest that diffusion models can memorize some classes while others remain insufficiently learned. We validate our theoretical predictions empirically using U-Net models trained on Fashion MNIST.

Thermodynamics and emergent thermomechanical response of a quantum ring with nonminimal spin--orbit coupling

Authors: João A. A. S. Reis, L. Lisboa-Santos, Edilberto O. Silva
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05533v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how spin-orbit coupling in a quantum ring alters its thermodynamic and mechanical properties. It shows that these interactions can induce measurable effects like mechanical instabilities and anomalous thermal contraction, providing a way to probe spin-orbit coupling through macroscopic measurements.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how nonminimal spin-orbit coupling deforms the energy spectrum of a 1D quantum ring and how this deformation manifests in macroscopic thermodynamic and thermomechanical response functions.

Main result. The coupling parameter xi reorganizes the energy spectrum, leading to mesoscopic de Haas-van Alphen-like oscillations in the grand-canonical ensemble and even anomalous thermal contraction in an interacting model.

Method. The authors use the Euler-Maclaurin summation formula to derive analytical expressions for partition functions in both canonical and grand-canonical ensembles, and introduce a phenomenological exponential resummation for interacting effects.

Model / system. A 1D quantum ring of radius r0 containing fermions, subject to effective spin-orbit interactions induced by nonminimal couplings to antisymmetric tensor fields.

Key observables. Internal energy, entropy, heat capacity, magnetization analog, susceptibility analog, persistent spin current, effective pressure, isothermal compressibility, and thermal expansion coefficient.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin-orbit coupling strength (xi), temperature (T), chemical potential (mu), and ring circumference (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The electrons are initially treated as non-interacting; the interacting model is phenomenological; the ring radius is treated as a quasi-static thermodynamic variable for mechanical analysis.

Figures summary. Figures demonstrate the accuracy of the Euler-Maclaurin expansion, the monotonic decrease of free energy, the sigmoid growth of entropy, the nonmonotonic behavior of magnetization and spin current, and the presence of mechanical instabilities and peaks in compressibility and expansion coefficients.

Paper structure. The paper moves from the derivation of the energy spectrum to the construction of thermodynamic partition functions, followed by the development of the thermomechanical framework and an extension into interacting systems.

Abstract

We investigate the thermodynamic and emergent thermomechanical properties of fermions confined to a one-dimensional quantum ring with effective spin--orbit interactions induced by nonminimal couplings to antisymmetric tensor fields. Using the exact spectrum obtained in the companion work, we develop canonical and grand-canonical descriptions and show that the coupling parameter~$ξ$ deforms the angular-momentum branches, reorganizing the low-energy spectrum and leaving clear signatures in the internal energy, entropy, heat capacity, and spin--orbit response functions. We also formulate an effective thermomechanical description by treating the ring circumference as a quasi-static thermodynamic variable. This leads to a pressure-like quantity and associated response coefficients, directly linked to the microscopic spectrum. In the grand-canonical ensemble, Fermi statistics strongly enhance the response, producing coupling-dependent instabilities and sign changes reminiscent of mesoscopic de~Haas--van Alphen oscillations. Finally, we introduce a phenomenological interacting extension based on an exponential resummation of the free energy, showing that collective effects can sharpen the thermomechanical response and induce anomalous thermal contraction. Our results connect spectral deformation, finite-size thermodynamics, and emergent mechanical behavior in spin--orbit-active quantum rings.

Twisted Kagome Bilayers: Higher-Order Magic Angles, Topological Flat Bands, and Sublattice Interference

Summary. This paper presents a theoretical framework for understanding twisted kagome bilayers, identifying specific 'higher-order magic angles' where electronic bands become extremely flat. It reveals that twisting these layers can induce non-trivial topological phases and higher-order Van Hove singularities, offering a new platform for studying correlated physics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the emergence of higher-order magic angles, topological flat bands, and the role of sublattice interference in twisted kagome bilayers (TBK).

Main result. The study demonstrates the emergence of higher-order magic angles where significant band flattening and higher-order Van Hove singularities occur, and shows that twisting can induce non-trivial topology.

Method. A generalized Bistritzer-MacDonald continuum model is developed by projecting the kagome monolayer Hamiltonian onto the Dirac subspace and truncating the tunneling Hamiltonian.

Important parameters / regimes. Twist angle (theta), interlayer tunneling energy scale (omega_0), layer separation (d_perp), and 1/3 filling regime.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows kagome geometry and MBZ construction; Fig 2 displays band structures, contour plots, and velocity renormalization; Fig 3 shows layer-resolved sublattice projections.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the TBK system and geometry, develops a generalized continuum model, analyzes the emergence of magic angles and higher-order singularities, investigates topological properties and symmetry breaking, and discusses the role of sublattice interference.

Abstract

We develop a low-energy continuum model to describe the moiré physics of heterostructures, which is a generalization of the celebrated Bistritzer-MacDonald (BM) method [R. Bistritzer and A. H. MacDonald, Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. U.S.A. 108, 12233 (2011)]. We take as an example the moiré physics of electrons in twisted bilayer kagomé (TBK) metals near $1/3$ filling where monolayer Dirac cones lie. We demonstrate the emergence of higher-order magic angles where significant local band flattening occurs as a high-order Van Hove singularity emerges and show how twisting alone can induce non-trivial topology. We, furthermore, show that while sublattice interference effects are present, their role is not as prominent as in monolayer kagome.

Two-mode geometry controls multiscale organization in bipartite systems

Authors: Ottavia Falconi, Giulio Cimini, Pablo Villegas
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06208v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new renormalization method for bipartite networks that preserves their two-mode structure during coarse-graining. It shows that traditional one-mode projections fail to capture the true multiscale hierarchy of complex systems, especially when interactions depend on long-range alternating paths.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Standard one-mode projections of bipartite networks collapse the two-mode architecture, leading to a loss of multiscale hierarchical information and a distortion of the underlying diffusion geometry.

Main result. The authors introduce a bipartite-preserving Laplacian renormalization framework (b-LRG) that reveals hidden hierarchies and demonstrates that while structural imbalance reshapes network geometry, it does not alter the universality class of the percolation transition.

Method. A Laplacian-based renormalization group (b-LRG) approach using diffusion dynamics on the graph Laplacian, combined with information-theoretic metrics (AMI/NMI) and finite-size scaling analysis.

Model / system. Bipartite networks with two distinct node classes, including controlled random bipartite ensembles at criticality and empirical networks such as host-virus, plant-pollinator, and social affiliation networks.

Key observables. Giant component size (P_inf), susceptibility (chi), spectral gap function (Psi), spectral specific heat (C), and effective diffusion-mediated distance (D_ij).

Important parameters / regimes. Diffusion timescale (tau), partition asymmetry (alpha = N_A/N_B), and average degree (k).

Assumptions / limitations. The renormalization process assumes that clustering can be restricted to nodes within the same class to prevent the creation of artificial intra-class links.

Figures summary. Comparison of standard vs. bipartite renormalization; finite-size scaling of the percolation transition; dendrograms showing hierarchical organization; and information-theoretic comparisons (AMI/NMI) between bipartite and projected networks.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of projection-induced distortion, presents the b-LRG framework, analyzes universality and geometry in controlled bipartite ensembles, and validates the framework using various empirical bipartite networks.

Abstract

Many complex systems are organized around complementary roles and naturally described as bipartite networks. Unveiling their multiscale structure presents a fundamental challenge because coarse-graining procedures must preserve role separation, whereas standard approaches collapse it via one-mode projections. Here we introduce a Laplacian-based renormalization framework that operates directly on the bipartite architecture, enabling scale transformations while retaining role differentiation. Using controlled bipartite ensembles at criticality, we show that structural imbalance systematically reshapes organization across scales while leaving scaling properties invariant, revealing a separation between universality and geometry. Applying the coarse-graining framework to empirical bipartite networks, we uncover nontrivial multiscale hierarchies for both roles. In contrast, renormalization performed after one-mode projection -- which truncates diffusion paths to nearest neighbors -- yields qualitatively different structures. Our results identify two-mode geometry as a fundamental constraint for revealing multiscale organization in systems with role separation.

Multifrequency Floquet Engineering of Magnon Polaritons

Authors: L. Hackner, A. R. Myatt, W. Wustmann, N. J. Lambert
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05576v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 spintronics-quantum-optics interface 5/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · 🔥 quantum optics experiment 4/5 · interference shaping light 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5

Summary. The paper presents a method for Floquet engineering of magnon-polaritons by modulating the microwave cavity frequency instead of the magnetic field. This approach enables larger modulation depths and allows for complex spectrum manipulation through multi-frequency drives. The study reveals new features like additional anticrossings and the formation of dark modes.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems as it demonstrates a new way to control quasi-energy levels and sideband dynamics in hybrid systems using multi-tone driving, specifically highlighting interference-induced dark modes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Traditional Floquet engineering of magnon-polaritons via magnetic field modulation is experimentally difficult due to the requirement for large-amplitude, rapid frequency tuning. The authors seek an alternative method using cavity frequency modulation to achieve larger modulation depths and bandwidths.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that modulating the microwave cavity frequency allows for effective Floquet engineering, including the creation of new anticrossings between previously uncoupled sidebands using multi-frequency commensurate drives. They also observe the emergence of 'dark' modes due to destructive interference between sidebands.

Method. The researchers use an arbitrary waveform generator and an IQ demodulator to apply sinusoidal frequency modulation to a microwave cavity frequency. The transmission spectrum is measured using a vector network analyzer (VNA) and analyzed using Floquet matrix formalism and cavity input-output theory.

Summary. This paper explores how nematic fluctuations change the nature of collective modes in superconductors. It demonstrates that the resulting highly anisotropic gap leads to a unique dispersion of phase and amplitude modes that differs significantly from standard BCS theory.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the dispersion and spectral structure of collective modes (transverse phase and longitudinal amplitude modes) in a superconductor where pairing is mediated by long-range nematic fluctuations.

Main result. The authors find that the analytic structure of the pair susceptibility is qualitatively different from BCS theory, exhibiting highly unconventional dispersion, including split peaks and logarithmic singularities in the longitudinal channel.

Method. The study uses Gorkov's diagrammatic technique and the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) to calculate the pair-pair susceptibility and its spectral function.

Model / system. A 2D one-band model of electrons with an anisotropic s-wave gap, specifically motivated by nematic-fluctuation-mediated superconductors like FeSe and its doped derivatives.

Key observables. Transverse (phase) susceptibility, longitudinal (amplitude) susceptibility, and their respective real and imaginary spectral functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Fermi velocity (vF), superconducting gap amplitude (delta_0), momentum transfer (q), frequency (Omega), and coupling strength (g).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a zero-temperature limit, a 2D electron system, and neglects long-range Coulomb interactions in certain parts of the derivation.

Figures summary. The notes mention a polar plot (Fig 1) and a frequency dependence plot (Fig 9).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of nematic-mediated pairing, defines the Hamiltonian and anisotropic gap model, derives the pair susceptibility using diagrammatic techniques, analyzes the pole structure and dispersion in both transverse and longitudinal channels, and provides mathematical proofs for the analytic properties of the susceptibility in an appendix.

A Comparative Study of Projected and Unprojected Schemes for Micromagnetic Simulations

Authors: Changjian Xie
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05858v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper compares different numerical schemes for simulating micromagnetics, specifically investigating if the magnetization magnitude constraint can be maintained without an explicit projection step. It finds that the BDF1 method is robust and consistent even without projection, potentially simplifying the numerical implementation of magnetic simulations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study evaluates the necessity and impact of using nonlinear projection steps to enforce constant magnetization magnitude in micromagnetic simulations.

Main result. The BDF1 method is found to be highly consistent whether or not a projection step is used, whereas the Gauss-Seidel method shows significant discrepancies at high dissipation coefficients.

Method. A comparative analysis of two first-order semi-implicit time-discretization strategies: the implicit Gauss-Seidel method (GSPM) and the Backward Differentiation Formula (BDF1), both tested with and without a spherical projection step.

Model / system. The study uses the Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation to model ferromagnetic materials, specifically focusing on ferromagnetic thin films and nanostrips.

Key observables. Energy evolution (decay and stability), magnetization profiles, and domain wall motion.

Important parameters / regimes. Gilbert damping coefficient (alpha) ranging from 0.01 to 0.1, external magnetic field (5 mT), and grid discretization scales.

Assumptions / limitations. The study is limited to first-order numerical schemes and assumes the validity of the LLG equation for the modeled ferromagnetic systems.

Figures summary. Figures compare magnetization profiles and energy evolution for GSPM and BDF1 at different damping levels, alongside time-series snapshots of domain wall motion and various magnetic configurations (C, Diamond, Flower, etc.).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the micromagnetic problem, defines the numerical schemes (GSPM and BDF1) with and without projection, presents stability analysis, compares static and dynamic simulation results across different damping regimes, and concludes with suggestions for future higher-order extensions.

Abstract

In micromagnetic simulations, the constant magnitude of the magnetization can be derived from the continuity equation. Since the time evolution of the magnetization in the continuity equation is perpendicular to the plane determined by the magnetization and the effective field, taking the inner product of both sides of the model with the magnetization shows that the evolution rate of the magnitude of the magnetization is zero, thus keeping the magnitude constant. From this perspective, the equation itself can maintain the constraint of constant magnetization magnitude. We discretized the continuity equation and compared two first-order semi-implicit strategies in time: one is the implicit Gauss-Seidel method, and the other is the semi-implicit Backward Differentiation Formula (BDF) method. We considered the comparison between these two schemes with and without the projection step. The results of micromagnetic simulations show that when the dissipation coefficient is large, the implicit Gauss-Seidel method without the projection step has significant differences from the method with the projection step in both the achieved steady state and domain wall motion. When an appropriate dissipation coefficient is selected, the difference between the two narrows, and both the steady state and domain wall motion can be simulated. For the other method, BDF1, whether the dissipation coefficient is large or small, the results with and without the projection step are quite consistent, and it can effectively simulate the domain wall motion.
