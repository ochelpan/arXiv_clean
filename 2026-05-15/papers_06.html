Winning Lottery Tickets in Neural Networks via a Quantum-Inspired Classical Algorithm

Authors: Natsuto Isogai, Hayata Yamasaki, Sho Sonoda, Mio Murao
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13979v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a classical algorithm that performs a task previously thought to require quantum advantage: efficiently sampling optimal hidden nodes for sparse neural networks. By exploiting the mathematical structure of the ridgelet transform, the authors achieve polynomial scaling, providing a powerful classical alternative to quantum machine learning approaches for subnetwork selection.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently identifying sparse, high-performing subnetworks (winning lottery tickets) within large neural networks by overcoming the exponential computational bottleneck of sampling from an optimized probability distribution.

Main result. The authors developed a 'dequantized' classical algorithm that achieves polynomial-time scaling in the data dimension D, effectively replacing a previously proposed quantum algorithm without exponential overhead.

Method. The method uses a randomized classical rejection sampling algorithm that exploits the isometry structure of the discrete ridgelet transform to avoid dense matrix inversion.

Model / system. A discretized single-hidden-layer neural network using a ridgelet-based architecture over a finite field Z_P^D, where the learning task is supervised regression.

Key observables. Empirical risk, runtime scaling with dimension D, and total variation distance between sampled and target distributions.

Important parameters / regimes. Data dimension D, number of training samples M, regularization parameter lambda, smoothing parameter Delta, and precision parameter P.

Assumptions / limitations. The efficiency relies on the specific ridgelet structure rather than general sparsity; it assumes parameters are polynomially bounded and uses a discretized domain.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares empirical risk across different dimensions, showing the proposed method tracks exact sampling; Figure 2 demonstrates the reduction from exponential to polynomial runtime scaling.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of sparse subnetwork selection, defines the ridgelet-based mathematical framework, presents the new classical dequantized algorithm and an improved quantum version, provides theoretical proofs for complexity and correctness, and concludes with numerical simulations validating the efficiency and performance.

Abstract

Quantum machine learning (QML) aims to accelerate machine learning tasks by exploiting quantum computation. Previous work studied a QML algorithm for selecting sparse subnetworks from large shallow neural networks. Instead of directly solving an optimization problem over a large-scale network, this algorithm constructs a sparse subnetwork by sampling hidden nodes from an optimized probability distribution defined using the ridgelet transform. The quantum algorithm performs this sampling in time $O(D)$ in the data dimension $D$, whereas a naive classical implementation relies on handling exponentially many candidate nodes and hence takes $\exp[O(D)]$ time. In this work, we construct and analyze a quantum-inspired fully classical algorithm for the same sampling task. We show that our algorithm runs in time $O(\operatorname{poly}(D))$, thereby removing the exponential dependence on $D$ from the previous classical approach. Numerical simulations show that the proposed sampler achieves empirical risk comparable to exact sampling from the optimized distribution and substantially lower than sampling from the non-optimized uniform distribution, while also exhibiting exponentially improved runtime scaling compared with the conventional classical implementation. These successful dequantization results show that sparse subnetwork selection via optimized sampling can be achieved classically with polynomial data-dimension scaling on conventional computers without quantum hardware, providing an alternative to the existing quantum algorithm.

Summary. The study demonstrates that the spatial arrangement of plasmonic nanodisks relative to a 2D semiconductor layer is critical for maintaining high-quality excitons. By placing the lattice on top of the semiconductor, the researchers reduced strain and disorder, leading to a 25% increase in light-matter coupling strength. This approach enables the creation of more homogeneous and robust polaritonic platforms.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and cavity QED as it demonstrates a practical method to maximize light-matter interaction strength and maintain high-quality resonances in hybrid plasmonic-semiconductor architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing the strength of light-matter coupling in plasmonic lattices integrated with monolayer semiconductors by addressing strain and dielectric disorder caused by fabrication processes.

Main result. Placing the plasmonic lattice on top of the semiconductor stack (Sample A) achieves a 25% increase in coupling strength and significantly improved exciton quality compared to a bottom-layer configuration.

Method. The researchers used electron-beam lithography, plasma etching, and gold evaporation to embed gold nanodisk arrays into hBN/MoSe2 heterostructures, followed by momentum-resolved differential reflectance and photoluminescence spectroscopy.

Model / system. The system consists of gold nanodisk arrays embedded in hexagonal Boron Nitride (hBN) layers coupled to MoSe2 monolayers, modeled using a coupled oscillator Hamiltonian to describe exciton-polariton formation.

Key observables. Exciton linewidth, exciton energy, spatial homogeneity (energy and linewidth), coupling strength (g), Rabi splitting, and Surface Lattice Resonance (SLR) Q-factors.

Important parameters / regimes. Exciton energy (~1.64 eV), coupling strength (23.5 meV in optimized sample), and exciton linewidth (3.0 meV in optimized sample).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the primary degradation in the control sample is due to fabrication-induced strain and surface contamination/doping.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the fabrication sequence and a schematic comparison of the two sample geometries (top-lattice vs. bottom-lattice).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of light-matter coupling in 2D materials, describes the fabrication of two different layer-order heterostructures, presents spectroscopic characterization of exciton quality and coupling strength, and concludes with the implications for large-area polariton lattices.

Abstract

Exciton-polaritons provide a versatile platform for the study of a wide range of phenomena, including polariton lasers, topological polaritons, and bosonic condensation. Transition metal dichalcogenide monolayers host excitons with large oscillator strength and binding energies constituting a robust matter constituent that forms polaritons from cryogenic to room temperature when embedded in optical microcavities. Plasmonic nanoparticles arranged in lattice geometries offer strong field-confinement and high quality factors. However, the high sensitivity of monolayer excitons to strain and dielectric disorder necessitates encapsulation in atomically flat hBN to ensure a high optical quality, rendering plasmonics more challenging. Here, we employ our recently developed fabrication method for embedding gold nanodisk arrays into van der Waals heterostructures and compare two samples with opposite layer order. We observe that strain and etching-induced surface contamination can reduce the exciton quality and thus the light-matter interaction strength significantly. Our fabrication approach reduces interfacial irregularities and enables homogeneous large-area polariton lattices for a wide range of applications, such as polarization-control or topological polaritonics.

Summary. This paper reports the development of a high-brightness, tunable source of polarization-entangled photons at telecom wavelengths using twisted NbOBr2 van der Waals crystals. By using a 90-degree twisted stack, the researchers achieved entanglement fidelities over 95% and significantly higher brightness than previous 2D material-based sources. This provides a promising new platform for integrated quantum photonic communication networks.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum networks as it presents a new material platform for generating high-fidelity, high-brightness entangled photons at telecom wavelengths using van der Waals engineering.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for scalable, high-brightness, and tunable telecom-wavelength entangled photon sources that are compatible with existing optical fiber infrastructure while overcoming the noise and brightness limitations of current 2D materials like TMDs.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated a high-brightness, tunable polarization-entangled photon source using twisted NbOBr2 crystals, achieving entanglement fidelities exceeding 95% and a brightness an order of magnitude higher than TMD-based sources.

Method. The team used spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC) in a 90-degree twisted stack of NbOBr2 crystals, employing below-bandgap excitation and quantum state tomography to characterize the generated Bell states.

Model / system. The experimental platform consists of a 90-degree twisted stack of two mechanically exfoliated NbOBr2 van der Waals crystals (approximately 600 nm thick each) on a quartz substrate, utilizing the material's second-order nonlinearity (chi-2) for photon pair generation.

Key observables. Entanglement fidelity, coincidence-to-accidental ratio (CAR), entanglement visibility, photon bunching (g2(0)), and brightness (Hz/mW).

Important parameters / regimes. Pump wavelength of 725 nm, telecom-range photon generation (~1450 nm), 90-degree twist angle, and a 150 nm detection bandwidth.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach assumes that the 90-degree twist is precise enough to induce necessary quantum interference and that below-bandgap excitation effectively suppresses photocarrier-induced noise.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the NbOBr2 crystal structure, the 90-degree twisted stack setup, and the experimental configuration for SPDC; Figure 2 displays SPDC coincidence counts as a function of pump polarization.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of telecom quantum light sources, describes the NbOBr2 material and twisted stacking platform, details the experimental setup for SPDC and characterization, presents the results for polarization selection rules and entanglement fidelity, and concludes with a comparison to existing 2D material technologies.

Abstract

Nanoscale quantum light sources are essential building blocks for integrated quantum photonic systems. Here, we report a wavelength-scale entangled-photon source based on van der Waals-engineered NbOBr$_2$, and benchmark its performance for telecom-wavelength quantum light generation. By exploiting the material's second-order nonlinearity, we generate quantum-correlated photon pairs via spontaneous parametric down-conversion. We then use a 90$^{\circ}$ twisted stacking to induce quantum interference in photon-pair generation, yielding polarization-entangled photons. This approach enables tunability of the quantum optical state via control of the excitation laser polarization. We experimentally obtain entanglement fidelities exceeding 95% for Bell states, along with a high coincidence-to-accidental ratio of $\sim$335, and a brightness approximately one order of magnitude higher than recently reported telecom sources based on transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) 2D materials. These results establish twisted van der Waals engineering as a powerful platform for highly tunable, high-brightness quantum light sources at telecom wavelengths.

Monolithic axial InGaAs quantum dot emitters in GaAs-based nanowires via Sb-mediated facet engineering

Summary. Researchers have developed a method to grow high-quality, ultrathin InGaAs quantum dots in GaAs nanowires by using Antimony as a surfactant to suppress structural defects. This technique enables the creation of reliable single-photon emitters suitable for integration into silicon-based quantum photonic circuits.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a scalable material platform for single-photon sources, which are fundamental building blocks for photonic quantum computing and quantum networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving precise thickness control and abrupt interfaces for ultrathin axial InGaAs quantum dots in GaAs-based nanowires is hindered by facet-dependent growth dynamics and rotational twin defects.

Main result. The use of dilute Antimony (Sb) as a surfactant suppresses rotational twins and promotes axial growth on the (111)B facet, enabling high-quality single-photon emission with g2(0) < 0.4.

Method. Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) using catalyst-free selective-area epitaxy, combined with structural analysis (STEM, EDXS) and optical characterization (micro-PL, TRPL, and HBT interferometry).

Model / system. Monolithic axial InGaAs(Sb) quantum dot emitters embedded within GaAs-based nanowires grown on Si(111) substrates.

Key observables. Second-order photon correlation g2(0), radiative lifetimes, emission wavelength, twin density, and elemental distribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Sb incorporation of 3-4%, radiative lifetimes as short as 0.51 ns, and twin density reduction from 1.5 nm^-1 to 0.5 nm^-1.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a rectangular quantum box model with an infinite potential well and effective-mass approximation to estimate the confined bandgap.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of the QD in a nanowire and the growth concept via Sb-mediated facet engineering. Fig 2: STEM and EDXS analysis showing twin-free structure and In/Sb distribution. Fig 3: CL intensity maps and spectra showing localized emission. Fig 4: Photon statistics and correlation measurements.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the growth challenge, describes the Sb-mediated engineering approach, presents structural verification via microscopy, demonstrates optical single-photon performance, and discusses the implications for scalable quantum photonics.

Abstract

GaAs-based nanowires hosting active quantum heterostructures provide a promising route toward monolithic integration of single-photon sources on silicon, a key requirement for scalable quantum photonics. However, ultrathin axial quantum-emitter formation is often hindered by facet-dependent growth dynamics and rotational twins, which induce lateral overgrowth and compromise interface abruptness. Here, we develop InGaAs-based quantum emitters by tailoring facet evolution via dilute Sb incorporation, which efficiently suppresses twins and promotes confined axial insertion at the growth-front facet. This approach significantly enhances the probability of obtaining abrupt, few-nanometer-thin quantum dots at the nanowire tip. Single-nanowire optical spectroscopy reveals intense, spatially localized emission from the active region with lifetimes as short as (0.51 $\pm$ 0.02) ns, and second-order photon-correlation measurements consistently exhibit pronounced antibunching with $g^{(2)}(0)<0.4$, confirming single-photon emission. These results establish a strong correlation between twin density and axial heterostructure formation, identifying defect control as a key factor in realizing monolithically integrated nanowire single-photon sources.

Summary. This paper theoretically explores how a strong, oscillating Knight field from photoexcited electrons can resonantly cool nuclear spins in semiconductors. It demonstrates that this cooling process creates an Overhauser field that alters the Hanle effect, providing a way to identify the sign of the carrier g-factor.

Why it may be interesting. The paper explores the non-equilibrium dynamics of spin systems under periodic driving and the back-action of a polarized reservoir (nuclear spins) on the driving species (electrons), which is relevant to open quantum systems and driven many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the theoretical description of resonant cooling of nuclear spins in semiconductors when the Knight field of photoexcited electrons is strong enough to exceed local nuclear dipole-dipole interaction fields.

Main result. It is shown that the resulting Overhauser field can significantly modify the shape of Hanle effect curves, and the specific shape of these curves can be used to determine the sign of the charge carrier g-factor.

Method. A modified rotating frame method is used to decompose the oscillating Knight field into two components, followed by solving a nonlinear balance equation between dynamic nuclear polarization and spin-lattice relaxation.

Model / system. The system consists of a semiconductor nuclear spin system interacting with spin-polarized charge carriers under illumination with helicity-modulated (alternating circular polarization) light.

Key observables. Hanle curves (magnetic depolarization of photoluminescence), Overhauser field (BN), and electron spin polarization.

Important parameters / regimes. External magnetic field (B), Knight field amplitude (Be) and frequency (omega), nuclear spin-lattice relaxation time (T1), and charge carrier g-factor.

Assumptions / limitations. The frequency of the Knight field modulation is assumed to be far from the frequency range of the nuclear spin correlator, and the Knight field is assumed to be large compared to local nuclear fields.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of effective fields in two different rotating frames; Figure 2 displays numerical examples of Hanle curves at different modulation frequencies demonstrating different shapes for positive vs. negative g-factors.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of resonant cooling, describes the physical model and the rotating frame method, presents the mathematical framework for calculating DNP rates, provides numerical results for Hanle curves, and concludes with the implications for determining the g-factor.

Abstract

Resonant cooling of the nuclear spin system of a semiconductor by spin-polarized charge carriers under pumping with helicity-modulated polarized light is considered theoretically. It is shown that in the case of strong Knight field of charge carriers, exceeding local fields of the dipole-dipole interaction of nuclear spins, the Overhauser field arising as a result of resonant cooling can considerably modify the overall shape of magnetic-field dependences of charge carrier spin polarization, experimentally observed as the Hanle effect.

Summary. This paper investigates how the evaporation of salt droplets leads to the self-organization of complex crystalline structures on a semiconductor substrate. By analyzing transport-driven dynamics, the authors demonstrate how to control the formation of 1D photonic crystals and hybrid crystal-glass phases. The work provides a unified framework for predicting morphological evolution in evaporating systems.

Why it may be interesting. The paper explores non-equilibrium phase transitions, self-organization, and the decoupling of scales in a driven-dissipative system, which are central themes in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the mechanisms of crystallization dynamics and the emergence of ordered, functional phases during the evaporation of NaCl droplets on a Ge(001) substrate.

Main result. The study identifies that diffusion anisotropy, rather than growth kinetics, governs the maximal structure size and discovers a hybrid photonic phase with 1D crystal-like order and 2D glass-like disorder.

Method. Multi-scale investigation using light microscopy, SEM/EBSD, XRD, and quantitative thin-film interference analysis, supplemented by ARIMA and GARCH time-series modeling.

Model / system. The physical system consists of aqueous NaCl droplets evaporating on a Germanium (001) substrate, where growth is driven by the competition between Marangoni effects and capillary flow.

Key observables. NaCl equivalent height (h_eq), structural anisotropy (d1, d2), thickness peaks (M1-M5), surface filling factor, and crystallization front dynamics.

Important parameters / regimes. NaCl concentration (rho), droplet volume, evaporation rate, and the 'homogeneity line' where transport forces balance.

Figures summary. Height maps showing morphology evolution; 2D autocorrelation functions for structure size; interference analysis schematics; thickness distribution histograms; and time-series plots (ARIMA/GARCH) of the crystallization front.

Paper structure. Introduction to crystallization dynamics; experimental setup of NaCl on Ge(001); morphological classification via h_eq; analysis of thickness layers via interference; investigation of structural anisotropy and epitaxial growth; time-series analysis of the crystallization front; and discussion of photonic applications.

Abstract

Crystallization during droplet evaporation gives rise to complex, self-organized structures, yet the mechanisms underlying the emergence of ordered functional phases remain poorly understood. In this study, we present a comprehensive, multi-scale investigation into the crystallization dynamics of NaCl during droplet evaporation on a germanium (001) substrate, relevant for its IR applications. Through systematic microscopic characterization, we identify the formation of diverse microstructures, including 1D photonic crystal nanostructures formed within hybrid crystal-glass photonic system. To enable quantitative comparison across experimental conditions, we introduce the NaCl equivalent height as a unified metric to describe and classify the evolution of crystalline morphology. Our results reveal that diffusion anisotropy, rather than growth kinetics, primarily governs the maximal attainable structure size. Quantitative thin film interference analysis demonstrates the presence of discrete thickness layers in the film. Controlled evaporation experiments yield homogeneous crystallization patterns across the entire droplet area, facilitating the emergence of ordered photonic structures. Time-series dynamics analysis of height profiles uncovered the spatiotemporal evolution of the crystallization front, providing insights into the details of underlying physical mechanisms. Together, these results establish a robust experimental framework for understanding and predicting crystallization behavior in evaporating droplets, with potential applications in materials synthesis, photonics, and microscale pattern formation.

Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14107v1

Summary. This paper theoretically demonstrates how the surface of the magnetic Weyl semimetal Co3Sn2S2 enables a switchable linear photogalvanic effect through magnetization control. The researchers show that the response is driven by surface Fermi arcs and follows a specific power-law frequency scaling. These findings suggest the material is a strong candidate for developing new magnetically controlled optoelectronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the origin, symmetry constraints, and magnitude of the surface Linear Photogalvanic Effect (LPGE) in the magnetic Weyl semimetal Co3Sn2S2.

Main result. The study finds that the LPGE is localized to the surface due to broken inversion symmetry and that the extrinsic contribution is large due to enhanced density of states from Fermi arcs. It also identifies a power-law frequency scaling of |jy| proportional to omega^-2.2 and a magnetization-induced sign reversal.

Method. A Green's-function and diagrammatic formalism is used to evaluate the second-order response function via four Feynman diagrams.

Model / system. The system is the magnetic kagome Weyl semimetal Co3Sn2S2, modeled using a multi-orbital tight-binding Hamiltonian on a 40-layer slab to capture surface inversion symmetry breaking.

Key observables. Linear photogalvanic effect (LPGE) currents (jx, jy), Circular photogalvanic effect (CPGE) currents, and the second-order response tensor elements.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetization direction (m), polarization angle (phi), frequency (omega), temperature (T), and scattering rate (delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The calculation assumes the response originates from the surface and uses an effective tight-binding Hamiltonian derived from previous literature.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows Feynman diagrams for the second-order response; Fig 2b illustrates the rhombohedral lattice structure; Fig 5 shows polar plots of photocurrents as a function of polarization angle.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of LPGE in Co3Sn2S2, details the tight-binding model and diagrammatic formalism, analyzes symmetry constraints on the response tensor, presents results regarding frequency and temperature dependence, and concludes with implications for optoelectronic applications.

Abstract

We investigate the linear photogalvanic effect (LPGE) on the surface of the magnetic Weyl semimetal Co3Sn2S2 using a Green's-function and diagrammatic formalism. While the LPGE vanishes in the centrosymmetric bulk, it is symmetry-allowed on the surface where inversion symmetry is broken. We show that unitary crystal symmetries on the surface produce characteristic sign reversals of the total photocurrent at certain polarization angles upon flipping the magnetization. We further find that the intrinsic contribution to the LPGE is strongly constrained by an antiunitary mirror symmetry, which forces several nonlinear response tensor elements to vanish. In contrast, the extrinsic contribution is not subject to these constraints and displays a large magnitude which, we argue, is due to the enhanced density of states associated with Fermi-arc surface states. The current exhibits an approximately linear temperature dependence and a low-frequency power-law scaling, |jy| proportional to omega^-2.2, with weak temperature dependence of the scaling exponent. Our results identify Co3Sn2S2 as a promising platform for experimentally accessing symmetry-controlled nonlinear transport in realistic systems and for applications in magnetically controlled optoelectronic devices.

Mid-infrared Assisted THz Phonon Amplification in a 2D Semiconductor for Room Temperature Detection

Authors: Christopher Sumner, Jakob Ziewer, Anju Sajan, Fumin Huang, Rohit Chikkaraddy

Summary. This paper introduces a method called MIRAPA to selectively amplify THz phonons in MoS2 using mid-infrared light. This approach is much more energy-efficient than using visible light and avoids the heating issues typically associated with optical phonon excitation. The technique enables highly sensitive mid-infrared detection and provides a platform for future phonon-based coherent devices.

Why it may be interesting. The paper demonstrates a highly efficient, non-thermal pathway for driving specific vibrational modes via exciton-mediated coupling, which is relevant to the study of driven-dissipative open quantum systems and optomechanics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving efficient and selective excitation of lattice vibrations (phonons) in 2D semiconductors without the detrimental heating and decoherence caused by high-energy visible/NIR optical excitation.

Main result. Demonstrated mid-infrared assisted phonon amplification (MIRAPA) in MoS2, achieving over 80% amplification of the A1g phonon mode at room temperature with 300x lower power density than visible light.

Method. The researchers used surface-enhanced resonant Raman scattering (SERRS) to monitor phonon populations in few-layer MoS2 on a gold foil, using a mid-infrared pump to drive phonons and a visible laser as a probe.

Model / system. The experimental platform consists of few-layer MoS2 (approx. 5nm) exfoliated onto a 15nm thick gold (Au) foil, operating in a weak-coupling, anti-damping regime via exciton-mediated optomechanical coupling.

Key observables. Anti-Stokes/Stokes (aS/S) Raman intensity ratios, phonon occupation number (n), phonon linewidths (Gamma), and noise-equivalent power (NEP).

Important parameters / regimes. Room temperature operation, phonon amplification > 80%, effective NEP of ~0.3 nW/sqrt(Hz), and optomechanical coupling rate (hbar G) of 0.07-0.14 meV.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the phonon population follows a Bose-Einstein distribution and utilizes a Lorentzian field-enhancement model for optomechanical coupling.

Figures summary. Figures show the dependence of A1g and E2g1 phonon intensities on MIR and visible power, the modulation of the anti-Stokes signal under periodic MIR switching, and the efficiency comparison between MIR and visible pumping.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the MIRAPA concept, describes the experimental setup (2DoF geometry), details the Raman spectroscopy methodology, presents results on selective phonon amplification and efficiency, demonstrates long-term stability and sensitivity, and concludes with potential applications.

Abstract

Efficient and selective excitation of lattice vibrations is central to controlling energy flow at the nanoscale, yet remains challenging under conventional optical excitation. Here, we introduce a mid-infrared-assisted phonon amplification approach, termed MIRAPA, that enables efficient energy injection directly into vibrational bonds. Using surface-enhanced resonant Raman scattering in few-layer $\mathrm{MoS_2}$, we exploit strong exciton--phonon coupling to monitor phonon populations. When mid-infrared (MIR) light is introduced, it couples directly to out-of-plane lattice vibrations, leading to room-temperature phonon amplification exceeding $80\%$. Crucially, MIRAPA bypasses electronic excitation pathways, allowing the MIR power density to be nearly $300\times$ lower than that required for visible excitation to achieve comparable enhancement. The resulting phonon modulation is robust, persisting over more than $2800$ on/off cycles and exceeding $15$ hours of continuous-wave laser illumination without degradation. Quantitative analysis yields an effective noise-equivalent power of approximately $0.3\,\mathrm{nW}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ for MIR detection, highlighting the sensitivity of the approach. By combining vibrational selectivity, low-power operation, and long-term stability, MIRAPA provides a robust platform for probing and amplifying phonons in two-dimensional semiconductors. These results open new opportunities for nanoscale vibrational sensing, mid-infrared detection, and phonon-based coherent devices, including routes toward phonon lasing.

Anisotropic Surface Spin Waves as Signature of A-type Altermagnets

Authors: Zhoujian Sun, Yiyuan Chen, Tao Yu, Hai-Zhou Lu, X. C. Xie
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14735v1

Summary. The paper identifies a unique signature of A-type altermagnets through the discovery of anisotropic surface spin waves. These waves exhibit properties like chirality-dependent surface localization and frequency splitting that are absent in conventional magnets. These features can be experimentally detected using NV center magnetometry and resonance absorption spectroscopy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Experimental identification of A-type altermagnets is difficult because they lack an anomalous Hall signal, necessitating a new unique signature for detection.

Main result. The discovery of anisotropic surface spin waves that exhibit chirality-dependent top-bottom localization and frequency splitting, providing a definitive signature for A-type altermagnetism.

Method. Linearization of the Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation around a collinear ground state and solving the resulting tridiagonal dynamical matrix eigenvalue problem.

Model / system. An N-layer square lattice of A-type altermagnets with intralayer ferromagnetic and interlayer antiferromagnetic exchange, characterized by broken PT symmetry due to unequal intralayer couplings (J1 != J2).

Key observables. Chirality-dependent surface localization, chiral split constant frequency contours, magnetic stray fields, and resonance absorption spectra.

Important parameters / regimes. Broken PT symmetry condition (J1 != J2), intralayer exchange (J1, J2), interlayer exchange (J3), and easy-axis anisotropy (K).

Assumptions / limitations. Linearization of the LLG equation, assumption of plane-wave excitations in the x-y plane, and neglect of damping for the resonance absorption calculation.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows PT symmetry breaking and anisotropic contours; Fig 2 displays spin wave dispersions, amplitude profiles, and elliptical frequency contours; Fig 3 illustrates the NV center setup and compares stray field magnitudes and scaling with the J1/J2 ratio.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of A-type altermagnet identification, presents a theoretical model of anisotropic surface spin waves, details the symmetry-breaking mechanism, proposes experimental detection methods via NV centers and resonance spectroscopy, and discusses the scaling of these signatures.

Abstract

Altermagnets have attracted intense interest because they have the advantages of both ferromagnets and antiferromagnets. However, their experimental identification remains challenging, in particular for the A-type altermagnets that account for a large group of material candidates. Here, we discover a kind of anisotropic surface spin waves in A-type altermagnets, which is absent in ferromagnets and conventional antiferromagnets. The anisotropic surface spin waves arise directly from the nature of altermagnets, i.e., the spin-opposite sublattices cannot be related by translation or inversion, which breaks the combined spatial-inversion and time-reversal symmetry, leading to the anisotropic surface spin waves with two properties, the chirality-dependent top-bottom positions and chiral split constant frequency contours. Abstract

We propose a geometric mechanism for fractional quantum Hall states based on impurity-induced correlations within a Landau level. A correlated distribution of ionized impurities partially modifies the Landau-level degeneracy through coherent coupling between cyclotron orbits, generating fractional energy sublevels. The odd-denominator hierarchy emerges naturally from the intrinsic guiding-center quantization and the correlated cyclotron motion. The resulting spectrum reproduces the principal experimentally observed fractional sequences and predicts a strong dependence of fractional-state stability on impurity geometry and layer separation. The absence of an incompressible Hall plateau at filling factor 1/2 follows from cancellation of the geometric correlations responsible for odd-denominator states. These results suggest that impurity-induced geometry may constitute an additional organizing principle in quantum Hall systems.

Abstract

In spintronics, both electronic charge and spin are used to process and store information. Generation, manipulation, and detection of spin currents are essential for the development of next-generation spintronic technologies. Here, we investigate the spin Hall effect in electronic Lévy glasses composed of graphene ribbons with randomly distributed circular regions of high spin-orbit coupling. These systems exhibit two transport regimes that can be tuned by adjusting the Fermi energy. The superdiffusive regime is characterized by low Fermi energy, low resistivity, and low magnetoresistivity, resulting in a long spin diffusion length, in contrast to the diffusive regime. Employing the Landauer-Büttiker approach in conjunction with numerically exact tight-binding simulations, we compute spin-resolved transmission coefficients to assess the spin Hall current and the spin Hall angle as functions of Fermi energy, spin-orbit coupling strength, and on-site electrostatic potential. Our findings reveal that, in the superdiffusive regime, a low charge current can be converted into a large spin Hall current, whereas in the diffusive regime, the same charge current generates a modest spin Hall current. Moreover, we observe that the spin Hall angle can reach 30% in the superdiffusive regime, whereas in the diffusive regime it is only 5%. These results demonstrate that electronic Lévy glasses provide a versatile platform for controlling spin transport and optimizing the spin Hall effect for spintronic applications.
