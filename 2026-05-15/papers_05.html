C-Phase-Aware Compilation for Efficient Fault-Tolerant Quantum Execution

Authors: Dhanvi Bharadwaj, Siddharth Dangwal, Yuewen Hou, Gokul Subramanian Ravi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14042v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces Qomet, a compiler designed to optimize fault-tolerant quantum computing by treating the hardware layout as a continuous, dynamic resource. By exploiting the commutativity of C-Phase gates and using a cycle-accurate scheduler, it significantly reduces the time required to execute complex quantum algorithms like QAOA and QFT.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing compilers for fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC) use rigid, slice-based abstractions that ignore microarchitectural details, leading to high latency and inefficient resource utilization due to routing contention and artificial dependencies.

Main result. The Qomet compiler achieves up to a 59.7x reduction in execution time by leveraging C-Phase commutativity for multi-target interactions and using a cycle-accurate, event-driven scheduler.

Method. A microarchitecture-aware co-design engine that uses multi-target grouping, routing-aware packing via Steiner trees, and a dynamic, event-driven scheduler to overlap operations and minimize qubit idling.

Model / system. A fault-tolerant quantum computing architecture based on the surface code, utilizing lattice surgery (LS) for logical operations (merge/split) and magic state distillation/cultivation for non-Clifford gates.

Key observables. Execution latency (clock cycles), instructions per cycle (IPC), resource utilization, and routing footprint.

Important parameters / regimes. Code distance (d), magic state availability (MS-Abundant vs. MS-Starved), and the number of nearest candidates (k) for magic state selection.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach is specifically designed for surface-code-based lattice surgery architectures and assumes a regime where quantum and classical resources are significant constraints.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares existing work to Qomet's speedup; Figure 2 shows the Qomet Co-Design Engine architecture; Figure 3 depicts the surface code 2D layout; Figure 4 details the temporal steps of a CNOT via lattice surgery; Figures 5-6 illustrate gate decomposition and parallelism; Figures 7-13 show performance scaling across various layouts, qubit counts, and magic state regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of inefficient FTQC compilation, presents the Qomet compiler framework (grouping, packing, and rotation realization), details the event-driven scheduling and cost-aware magic state selection, and concludes with extensive benchmarks on QAOA and QFT workloads across different hardware regimes.

Abstract

Achieving practical quantum advantage on fault-tolerant quantum computers (FTQC) is fundamentally constrained by the substantial spatial and temporal overheads required to map logical operations onto physical hardware. Existing compilation approaches typically adopt coarse-grained, slice-based abstractions that overlook fine-grained microarchitectural effects, such as routing contention, leading to inefficient resource utilization and limited alignment between algorithm structure and hardware capabilities. This work presents a microarchitecture-aware compilation approach that integrates algorithmic structure directly with lattice surgery (LS) execution. By leveraging the commutativity of C-Phase operations, the method transforms inherently sequential gate sequences into concurrent multi-target interactions, effectively removing artificial dependencies and exposing significant instruction-level parallelism. To enable this, we design a dynamic, event-driven scheduling strategy that accurately models spatial layout and routing constraints, allowing operations to overlap in time while minimizing contention. Through improved coordination of computation and communication, this approach substantially reduces idle resources and achieves up to a 59.7$\times$ reduction in execution time compared to standard baselines.

Summary. This paper explores how changing the carrier density in the Dirac semimetal CaIrO3 affects its transport properties. It demonstrates that at low densities, the system enters a quantum limit where enhanced Coulomb interactions lead to non-linear magnetoresistance. The results confirm the presence of a robust Dirac dispersion characterized by a constant Fermi velocity.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the carrier-density dependence of magnetotransport properties, specifically the origin of giant magnetoresistance and the transition in MR scaling behavior in the correlated Dirac semimetal CaIrO3.

Main result. The study finds that as carrier density decreases, mobility and magnetoresistance increase, with the MR scaling transitioning from B-linear to B-nonlinear (alpha > 2) due to enhanced long-range Coulomb interactions in the quantum limit. The constant Fermi velocity across different carrier densities provides strong evidence for k-linear Dirac dispersion.

Method. Transport measurements including resistivity, Hall effect, and Shubnikov-de Haas oscillations were performed on single crystals at low temperatures and high magnetic fields, supplemented by DFT+DMFT modeling.

Model / system. The system is CaIrO3, an orthorhombic perovskite characterized as a correlated Dirac semimetal with a line node in its energy dispersion and 5d orbitals featuring a j_eff = 1/2 multiplet.

Key observables. Carrier density (nH), mobility, transverse magnetoresistance (MR) ratio, effective mass (mc), Fermi velocity (vF), and MR exponent (alpha).

Important parameters / regimes. Low carrier density regime (nH ~ 2.2 x 10^16 cm^-3), quantum limit, magnetic fields up to 14 T, and temperatures down to 2 K.

Assumptions / limitations. Difficulty in precisely estimating the Thomas-Fermi screening length and the exact strength of the impurity potential.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the material and its electronic structure, presents transport data across varying carrier densities, analyzes quantum oscillations to characterize the Dirac dispersion, discusses the scaling of magnetoresistance, and concludes with a physical interpretation of the scattering mechanisms.

Abstract

We report the carrier density dependence of the magnetotransport property in the correlated Dirac semimetal CaIrO$_3$. In the dilute carrier density region ($n_{\rm H}$ $\sim 2.2 \times 10^{16} \,@@MATH0MATH@@\rm{cm}^{2}/\rm{Vs}$ at $2 \, \mathrm{K}$, and the transverse magnetoresistance (MR) reaches $2,000 \,$\% at $12 \, \mathrm{T}$. The analysis of quantum oscillations and Hall conductivity shows that the Fermi velocity is nearly independent of the cross-sectional area of the Fermi surface, or equivalently the carrier density, supporting a $k$-linear dispersion of the Dirac node. The field dependence of magnetoresistivity is nearly $B$-linear in the moderate carrier density region ($n_\mathrm{H} \geq 4 \times 10^{16}\,$cm$^{-3}$), but scales with $B^α$ ($α> 2$) in the lower carrier density region. Deforming the Trail: Baseline Quantum Circuitry for $\text{SU(2)}_k$ Lattice Gauge Theory

Authors: Zoë Webb-Mack, Natalie Klco
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15076v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper proposes a method to simulate q-deformed SU(2) lattice gauge theories on quantum computers with significantly lower gate complexity. By using q-deformation and gauge-variant completions, the authors reduce the scaling of required gates from O(d^8) to O(d^5). This approach provides a more efficient and unitary-preserving way to truncate gauge fields for quantum simulation.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and quantum computing as it provides a scalable method for simulating gauge theories, which are fundamental to understanding strongly interacting quantum fields.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of implementing the magnetic term of the SU(2) lattice gauge theory Hamiltonian on quantum hardware due to high gate complexity and the non-unitarity of truncated operators.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that using q-deformed SU(2)k theory and Gauge-Variant Completions (GVC) reduces the quantum resource scaling for generalized-controlled-X gates from O(d^8) to O(d^5).

Method. The study employs a diagrammatic approach using phased F-moves and G-moves, leveraging q-deformed 6j symbols and symmetry analysis (flux hierarchy inversion) to optimize circuit synthesis.

Model / system. A q-deformed SU(2)k Yang-Mills pure-gauge theory on a 1D lattice (plaquette chain) with periodic boundary conditions, utilizing a truncated Hilbert space with dimension d = k + 1.

Key observables. Quantum resource upper-bounds (GCX gate counts), circuit depth, and the ratio of the physical Hilbert space dimension.

Important parameters / regimes. Truncation level k, deformation parameter q, and the dimension of the local Hilbert space d.

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on SU(2) pure gauge theory in 1D and assumes the ability to implement multi-controlled single-qudit unitaries on quantum hardware.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows F-move diagrams and matrix elements; Figure 2 plots the scaling of the physical Hilbert space and adjacency matrix symmetry; Figure 3 illustrates the circuit for k=1; Figure 4 compares gate scaling for different simulation strategies; Figure 6 shows the interleaved control sector strategy.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the SU(2) Hamiltonian and the need for truncation, introduces the q-deformed SU(2)k model, details the F-move diagonalization strategy and GVC technique, provides a resource scaling analysis, and concludes with optimized circuit implementations.

Abstract

Quantifying quantum resources for simulating the fundamental forces of Nature is sensitive to the mapping of gauge fields onto finite quantum computational architectures. When locally truncating lattice gauge theories in the irreducible representation basis, it has been proposed to further deform the theory via quantum groups. The purpose of this deformation is (1) to provide an infinite tower of finite-dimensional ($d = k+1$) groups systematically approximating the infinite-dimensional gauge links and (2) to restore the physical unitarity of a plaquette operator diagonalization procedure analytically derived from the field continuum by recontracting vertex pairs. For the SU(2)$_k$ Yang-Mills pure-gauge theory, we provide a constructive strategy of gauge-variant completions to extend this unitarity to the entire computational Hilbert space, leading to well-defined time evolution unitaries as targets for optimized circuit synthesis. Leveraging basic circuit decompositions and symmetries of the diagonalized plaquette operator, we report resource upper-bounds on the generalized-controlled-X two-qudit gates for arbitrary local truncation $d$, reducing estimates and scaling relative to the non-deformed theory by three polynomial powers from $O(d^8)$ to $O(d^5)$. Examining the stronger q-deformed gauge constraint, which softens the total flux at vertices, we show that the physical Hilbert space dimension of the deformed plaquette operator scales equivalently to its non-deformed counterpart with a constant factor $0.2563(5)$. Thus, despite affecting interactions at all scales as exemplified by the observed flux hierarchy inversion symmetry, q-deformation continues to pass scrutiny as a reliable truncation offering advantages in quantum circuit synthesis.

Discrete-phase-randomized mode-pairing quantum key distribution

Authors: Yuewei Xu, Zeyang Lu, Chan Li, Jian Long, Zhu Cao
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14484v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a quantum key distribution protocol that uses discrete rather than continuous phase randomization. It proves that a small number of discrete phases (around 14) are sufficient to match the performance of the ideal case while significantly reducing the required randomness.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum information as it addresses the practical implementation of quantum communication protocols by bridging the gap between idealized continuous-variable models and realistic discrete-variable hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The existing mode-pairing QKD protocol relies on an experimentally infeasible assumption of continuous phase randomization, which also requires an impractical, infinite supply of random bits.

Main result. The authors propose a discrete-phase-randomized (DPR) protocol that achieves security using only a few random bits and demonstrates that performance converges to the continuous case with as few as 14 discrete phases.

Method. The study uses a mathematical framework of pseudo k-photon states, fidelity-based error estimation, and a discrete version of the decoy-state method to bound phase error rates.

Model / system. A Measurement-Device-Independent (MDI) QKD setup involving two parties (Alice and Bob) and an untrusted third party (Charlie) using coherent state light sources with discretized phases.

Key observables. Key rate, Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER), yield of pseudo single-photon states, and fidelity between bases.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of discrete phases (D), pairing interval (l), intensity (mu, nu), and transmission distance.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes an even number of discrete phases, uniform probability for phase selection, and a symmetrically attenuating channel.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the protocol schematic; Figure 2 plots the key rate versus distance for various D values compared to the PLOB bound.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the security vulnerability of continuous phase randomization, proposes the DPR-MP-QKD protocol, develops the mathematical framework for security analysis using fidelity and decoy states, and concludes with simulation results showing convergence and resource efficiency.

Abstract

Mode-pairing quantum key distribution (MP-QKD) protocol achieves performance beyond the repeaterless rate-transmittance bound and exhibits excellent practicality by avoiding the requirement for difficult global phase locking. However, the source side of MP-QKD still relies on the assumption of continuous phase randomization, an experimentally infeasible requirement in practice. Therefore, the practical security of the protocol cannot be fully guaranteed. In this work, we propose a discrete-phase-randomized mode-pairing quantum key distribution (DPR-MP-QKD) protocol and analyze the basis-dependence of the source side. Then, we introduce a concrete discrete version of the decoy state method that ensures the security of the DPR-MP-QKD protocol. Finally, simulation results indicate that as the number of discrete phases increases, the key rate performance of DPR-MP-QKD progressively approaches that of the continuous case, with convergence achieved at approximately 14 discrete phases. Moreover, our approach drastically lowers the demand for randomness. While conventional continuous phase randomization demands an unlimited supply of random bits, we show that merely a few bits (e.g., 4) are adequate.

Failure-Guided Fuzzing for Hybrid Quantum-Classical Programs

Authors: Lei Zhang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14219v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new testing framework for hybrid quantum-classical algorithms that uses previously discovered failures to guide the search for new errors. By fuzzing quantum parameters around known non-convergent points, the method significantly improves the efficiency of finding bugs compared to random sampling.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Testing hybrid quantum-classical (HQC) algorithms like VQE and QAOA is difficult because failures (non-convergence) are rare events that are hard to find within realistic execution budgets.

Main result. Failure-guided local fuzzing, which perturbs quantum circuit parameters around identified non-convergent seeds, is the primary driver of improvement over random testing, though the benefit of concolic seed discovery depends on the specific workload.

Method. A two-phase fuzzing strategy consisting of (1) seed discovery using random, enumerative, or concolic methods, and (2) local fuzzing by mutating quantum circuit parameters around those seeds.

Model / system. The study uses two hybrid quantum-classical workloads implemented in Qiskit: a 2-qubit Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) and a 4-qubit Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) for the MaxCut problem on a ring graph.

Key observables. Crash count (number of non-convergence events) and crash rate (crashes per execution budget).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (Nq), execution budget (B), classical optimizer hyperparameters (learning rate, iteration limit), and quantum circuit parameters (rotation angles).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes noiseless simulators and focuses on a small-scale, discrete classical hyperparameter space for the concolic exploration component.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the exponential decay of expected crashes as qubits increase; Figure 2 shows the failure-guided fuzzing workflow; Figure 3 depicts the QAOA diagram; Figure 6 shows the distribution of crash counts for QAOA.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the testing challenge of HQC algorithms, defines the hybrid input model, proposes a two-phase failure-guided fuzzing strategy, compares five different testing strategies via VQE and QAOA case studies, and concludes with a discussion on workload dependency and future directions.

Abstract

Hybrid quantum-classical (HQC) algorithms, such as the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) and the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA), are central to near-term quantum computing but remain challenging to test. Sampling-based fuzzing can expose faulty or non-convergent configurations, but under realistic execution budgets, it may miss failure-prone regions in the joint space of classical optimizer settings and quantum circuit parameters. This paper studies failure-guided fuzzing for HQC programs. It models a hybrid input as a pair of classical optimizer hyperparameters and quantum circuit parameters, and evaluates a two-phase strategy that first searches for non-convergent seeds and then locally fuzzes circuit parameters around those seeds. To understand where the gains come from, five budgeted strategies are compared: random hybrid testing, classical enumeration without fuzzing, random-seed local fuzzing, enumeration-seed local fuzzing, and concolic-seed local fuzzing. The study is implemented on a VQE instance and a QAOA MaxCut instance in Qiskit. The results show that failure-guided local fuzzing is the main driver of improvement over random testing, while concolic seed discovery provides additional benefits on VQE but is less stable on QAOA. These findings suggest that reusing failure information is a promising direction for HQC testing, but that the value of concolic seed discovery is workload-dependent.

Fermi Surface Geometry from Charge Fluctuations in Three-Dimensional Metals

Authors: Pok Man Tam, Yarden Sheffer, Xiao-Chuan Wu, F. D. M. Haldane, Shinsei Ryu
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13951v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the shape and quantum geometry of 3D Fermi surfaces are imprinted on the subleading logarithmic scaling of charge fluctuations. It provides a mathematical link between physical observables and topological invariants like the Euler characteristic and Chern number. This establishes a way to probe the complex geometry of multi-band metals through density-density correlations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how the geometric and quantum geometric properties of 3D Fermi surfaces are encoded in the subleading logarithmic term of bipartite charge fluctuations.

Main result. The authors establish that the logarithmic coefficient of charge fluctuations can be decomposed into a geometric part (related to the Euler characteristic and Will and Willmore energy) and a quantum geometric part (related to the quantum metric and Chern number), and they derive a topological lower bound for this coefficient.

Method. The study uses analytical expansions of the structure factor, differential geometry (curvature tensors, Gauss-Bonnet theorem), and the application of Wick's theorem and RPA to evaluate momentum-space integrals.

Model / system. The system consists of three-dimensional non-interacting multi-band metals and Landau Fermi liquids, including specific models like the copper Fermi surface and Weyl metals.

Key observables. Bipartite charge fluctuations, structure factor S(q) (specifically the |q|^3 coefficient), and the logarithmic scaling coefficient Gamma.

Important parameters / regimes. Fermi surface curvature, Euler characteristic, Chern number, quantum metric tensor, Willmore energy, and the Landau parameter F0.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes zero temperature, the thermodynamic limit, non-interacting or Landau Fermi liquid regimes, and that different Fermi surface components are separated and do not intersect.

Figures summary. Figure 1 visualizes the structure factor overcounting and critical lines on the copper Fermi surface; Figure 3 shows the dependence of structure factor coefficients on the Landau parameter F0.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of encoding Fermi surface geometry in charge fluctuations, moves to the analytical derivation of the structure factor expansion, decomposes the logarithmic coefficient into geometric and quantum geometric parts, establishes topological bounds, and discusses implications for interacting Fermi liquids and Weyl systems.

Abstract

For three-dimensional non-interacting multi-band metals, we show that important information about the shape and the quantum geometry of Fermi surfaces is encoded in the subleading logarithmic term of bipartite charge fluctuations. This logarithmic term is related to the dimensionless $|\mathbf{q}|^3$-coefficient of the structure factor in momentum space, and both quantities can be expressed as Fermi surface integrals of the Fermi surface curvature tensor and the quantum metric tensor. When the real-space partition surface is a quadric (i.e., sphere or ellipsoid), the logarithmic coefficient satisfies a topological bound depending only on the Euler characteristic and the Chern number of the Fermi surface, illustrating a non-trivial interplay between topology and quantum topology in multi-band metals.

Finite-size scaling of hetero-associative retrieval in continuous-signal-driven Ising spin systems

Authors: Andrea Ladiana
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14059v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a method to map continuous, real-world data into Ising spin systems for associative memory. It demonstrates that using pseudo-inverse couplings and specific update dynamics allows for robust pattern reconstruction and the ability to disentangle overlapping signals, even when applied to noisy biological EEG data.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Bridging the gap between high-dimensional continuous signals and discrete Ising spin frameworks for associative memory, specifically addressing basin collapse and cross-modal retrieval.

Main result. The authors establish a multi-layer hetero-associative framework that achieves robust cross-modal reconstruction and spontaneous symmetry breaking, with an asymptotic storage capacity of approximately 0.50.

Method. A pipeline of PCA whitening and SimHash random-hyperplane projection is used to encode continuous signals into Ising spins, combined with Kanter-Sompolinsky pseudo-inverse couplings and Monte Carlo dynamics.

Model / system. A tri-layer hetero-associative Ising spin system where layers are coupled pairwise using pseudo-inverse memory couplings to decorrelate patterns and stabilize retrieval.

Key observables. Overlap (intra-class and inter-class), separation gap (delta q), Basin Collapse Index (BCI), Mattis magnetization, and storage capacity (alpha_c).

Important parameters / regimes. Inverse temperature (beta), spin dimension (N), loading parameter (alpha), Tikhonov regularization (gamma), and bit-flip noise rate (p_flip).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes SimHash entries are i.i.d. Gaussian and that PCA whitening can be performed; assumes the ability to perform sign re-binarization.

Figures summary. Figures show magnetization dynamics, temperature-dependent phase diagrams, separation gaps between spin overlaps, and the reconstruction of sleep-state signatures from EEG/EOG data.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the interface problem, describes the multi-layer encoding and coupling architecture, presents analytical proofs for encoding fidelity and scaling, details numerical simulations on synthetic curves, and concludes with an experimental application to human sleep polysomnography.

Abstract

Real-world physical signals are continuous and high-dimensional, yet the statistical-mechanics machinery of associative memory operates on discrete Ising spins. We bridge this divide through a multilayer Ising framework that couples a geometry-preserving continuous-to-Ising encoder (PCA whitening composed with SimHash random-hyperplane projection) to Kanter-Sompolinsky pseudo-inverse memory couplings, embedded directly into the local-field equations of a tri-layer hetero-associative system. The pseudo-inverse correction renders the equal-weight mixture state thermodynamically unstable, so that thermal fluctuations break the cross-modal symmetry and select a single global winner. We further establish a dynamical duality: parallel (Little) updates are structurally required to ignite the cross-modal signal avalanche from a single cued layer, whereas sequential (Glauber) sweeps resolve symmetric superpositions. The operational storage capacity obeys the Amit-Gutfreund-Sompolinsky finite-size correction $α_c(N)=α_c(\infty)-c\,N^{-1/2}$, extrapolating to an asymptotic operational limit $α_c(\infty)\approx 0.50$ under macroscopic-basin retrieval. Applied to multi-channel sleep polysomnography (PhysioNet Sleep-EDF), the architecture reconstructs the macroscopic sleep state on parietal EEG and EOG axes from a single noisy frontal-EEG cue, demonstrating cross-modal recall in the presence of quenched biological disorder.

From Hilbert's Tenth Problem to Quantum Speedup: Explicit Oracles for Bounded Diophantine Systems

Authors: Gabriel Escrig, M. A. Martin-Delgado
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13980v1

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a concrete quantum architectural design for solving polynomial equations over integers. By explicitly synthesizing the arithmetic gates required for the oracle, the authors prove that the quantum advantage is preserved and that the computational overhead remains polynomially bounded. This provides a scalable blueprint for applying Grover-type searches to integer optimization and cryptographic problems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The computational intractability of solving bounded Diophantine systems (polynomial equations over integers) and the need for an explicit, resource-efficient quantum oracle that avoids the 'black-box' assumption.

Main result. The authors provide a gate-level synthesis of a reversible quantum arithmetic oracle that achieves a quadratic speedup over classical search while maintaining polynomial overhead in terms of qubit and gate complexity.

Method. A 'compute-utilize-uncompute' architecture using Grover's algorithm, two's complement arithmetic, and a shift-and-add engine for polynomial evaluation.

Model / system. A digital quantum computing framework operating on a bounded integer domain D = [-N/2, N/2 - 1]^n, utilizing quantum registers for system variables, function accumulation, and result counting.

Key observables. Toffoli gate count, qubit requirements (spatial complexity), and amplitude amplification probability dynamics.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of variables (n), maximum polynomial degree (d), interval length (N), and Hamming weight of coefficients (w_H).

Assumptions / limitations. The problem is restricted to bounded (decidable) Diophantine systems; the analysis focuses on the pre-asymptotic regime; the architecture assumes two's complement binary encoding.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows scaling of gate counts and qubits; Figure 2 displays amplitude amplification success probability; Figure 3 shows the Grover iteration schematic; Figure 4 and 5 illustrate specific arithmetic circuit implementations (rewiring and squaring); Figure 6 and 7 show gate complexity scaling with respect to qubits, equations, and variables.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the mathematical definition of Diophantine systems, moves to the construction of a reversible arithmetic engine, details the gate-level synthesis of linear and quadratic oracles, provides a complexity analysis proving the preservation of quantum speedup, and concludes with empirical validation via state-vector simulations.

Abstract

Solving non-linear Diophantine systems lies at the mathematical core of integer optimization and cryptography. While the general unbounded problem is undecidable, even over bounded integer domains it remains classically intractable in the worst case. In this work, we introduce a fully reversible quantum algorithmic framework tailored to solve arbitrary polynomial Diophantine equations over bounded integer domains. The core of our approach is the explicit, gate-level synthesis of an evaluation oracle for amplitude amplification. By coherently evaluating polynomial constraints via in-place two's complement arithmetic and routing operations into a single recycled accumulator, this garbage-free strategy achieves a compact and scalable synthesis of the underlying non-linear arithmetic. Through analytical derivations and empirical circuit simulations, we prove that the overall spatial complexity is bounded by $q = \mathcal{O}((n + d^2)\log_2 N)$ logical qubits for $n$ variables, maximum degree $d$, and interval length $N$. The non-Clifford Toffoli depth is upper-bounded by $\mathcal{O}(q^2)$. This structural scaling exponent remains invariant to the variable count, modulated linearly only by the coefficients' Hamming weights. By moving beyond abstract black-box assumptions, this explicit architectural synthesis guarantees that the necessary quantum arithmetic acts as a bounded polynomial overhead. This ensures a quadratic speedup over classical exhaustive search, whether retrieving a unique assignment or dynamically enumerating an unknown number of solutions.

Graphical Algebraic Geometry: From Ideals and Varieties to Quantum Calculi

Authors: Dichuan Gao, Razin A. Shaikh, Aleks Kissinger
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13993v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. The paper introduces Graphical Algebraic Geometry (GAG), a new family of diagrammatic languages for reasoning about commutative algebras and affine varieties. It establishes a formal link between GAG and the ZH quantum calculus, showing that the latter is an extension of the former. The work also demonstrates that rewriting in GAG is #P-hard, as it can represent counting constraint satisfaction problems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers working on quantum circuit simplification and the development of new diagrammatic calculi, as it provides a rigorous algebraic-geometric foundation for the ZH calculus and links quantum computation to classical polynomial constraint satisfaction.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the need for a graphical calculus capable of modeling the nonlinear classical backbone of quantum computation, specifically for the ZH calculus and polynomial constraint satisfaction problems.

Main result. The authors introduce Graphical Algebraic Geometry (GAG), proving it is a sound, universal, and complete diagrammatic language for commutative algebras and affine varieties, and establish that the ZH quantum calculus is an extension of GAG.

Method. The researchers use category theory (PROPs, Lawvere theories, and structured spans/cospans) to construct the language and employ structural induction and complexity analysis to prove completeness and #P-hardness.

Model / system. The framework models algebraic structures such as commutative algebras, ideals, and affine varieties, and specifically relates these to the ZH calculus, a diagrammatic language for qudit quantum computation.

Important parameters / regimes. The order of the finite field q, and the dimension of the polynomial rings and varieties.

Assumptions / limitations. The construction relies on the Noetherian property of polynomial rings and assumes the field k is either algebraically closed or finite for certain categorical equivalences.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the recursive definition of diagrammatic terms; Figure 2 presents the generators and rewrite rules of the Lawvere theory; Figure 3 and 4 illustrate scalable notation for polynomial operations; Figure 5 displays additional rewrite rules for GCA_k.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the GAG framework, constructs the GCA, GAG_k, and GAG_q languages, proves their soundness and completeness using category theory, demonstrates the connection to #CSP complexity, and finally characterizes the ZH calculus as an extension of GAG.

Abstract

We introduce Graphical Algebraic Geometry (GAG), a family of diagrammatic languages extending the Graphical Linear Algebra programme. We construct several languages within this family and prove that they are universal and complete for the corresponding (co)span semantics of commutative algebras and affine varieties. This framework provides clear graphical representations of algebraic structures -- such as polynomials, ideals, and varieties -- enabling intuitive yet rigorous diagrammatic reasoning. We showcase two practical viewpoints on GAG. First, we show that instances of counting constraint satisfaction problem (#CSP) are recast as rewrite problems of closed diagrams in GAG. This means that deciding rewritability in GAG is #P-hard, and GAG can be viewed as a complete and compositional rewrite system for networks of polynomial constraints. Second, we characterize the qudit ZH calculus, a diagrammatic language for quantum computation, as an extension of Graphical Algebraic Geometry. This establishes the correspondence that Graphical Algebraic Geometry is to the ZH calculus what Graphical Linear Algebra is to the ZX calculus. Using this construction, we show that computing amplitudes in qudit ZH requires only a constant number of queries to a GAG oracle.

It reveals that the bond is mediated by a delocalized positron cloud that behaves like a covalent bond but possesses van der Waals-like strength. This discovery suggests a new class of quantum-mediated interactions between particles and antiparticles.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides deep insight into matter-antimatter interactions and the emergence of 'proto-bonds' through quantum correlations, which is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and the fundamental classification of chemical interactions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to resolve the enigmatic nature of the chemical bond in the positronium hydride (PsH) dimer, specifically determining whether the interaction is ionic, covalent, or van der Waals-like.

Main result. The researchers find that the bond is characterized by a delocalized molecular orbital of two positrons that envelopes two hydrogen anions, acting as a collective dipole with covalent-like features but a bond strength comparable to van der Waals interactions.

Method. The study employs high-precision Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) methods, specifically Variational and Diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC), using an advanced many-body wavefunction ansatz with an extended dynamical Jastrow factor.

Model / system. The physical system is the (PsH)2 dimer, composed of two protons, two positrons, and four electrons, modeled within the Born-Oppenheimer approximation using a fermionic Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Binding energy, dipole polarizability (including electronic and positronic components), dispersion coefficients (C6, C8), and electron-positron contact density (annihilation rate).

Important parameters / regimes. Internuclear distance (R), equilibrium distance (Req approx 6.0 Bohr), and external electric field strength (F).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the Fixed-Node approximation to handle the fermion sign problem and the assumption that the positronic wavefunction is independent of nuclear positions to reduce computational complexity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares four bonding mechanisms; Figure 2 shows bonding densities across different molecules; Figure 3 plots interaction energies and polarizability vs. distance; Figure 4 illustrates dipole response decomposition and local polarizability; Supplemental figures show Jastrow factor construction and convergence tests.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the bonding puzzle, describes the (PsH)2 system, details the QMC methodology and Jastrow wavefunction construction, presents results on energy and polarizability, and concludes with implications for broader quantum systems.

Abstract

Classifying interactions is key in the physical sciences, and bonding mechanisms in matter-antimatter systems remain particularly enigmatic. Here we focus on a paradigmatic example of positronium hydride (PsH) dimer composed of two protons, two positrons, and four electrons, whose bonding nature has been previously described as either ionic, covalent, or van der Waals-like. Accurate quantum Monte Carlo calculations show that the two positrons occupy a delocalized molecular orbital that envelopes the two hydrogen anions and responds as a collective dipole to an applied electric field. This positronic bonding stems from quantum correlations that resemble a single covalent bond formed between negatively charged pseudo-nuclei, but with a bond strength commensurate with the traditional van der Waals interaction. Our findings suggest that the ability to form delocalized proto-bonds is a more general property of quantum systems, and could be present in a broader class of particles, antiparticles, and quasi-particles interacting with matter.
