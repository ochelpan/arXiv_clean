Transitions as the Native Objects of Dispersive Light-Matter Dynamics

Authors: Meguebel Mohamed, Maxime Federico, Louis Garbe, Nadia Belabas, Nicolas Fabre
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14096v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · Tavis-Cummings & cavity-many-emitter 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new theoretical framework that treats light-matter transitions, rather than states, as the primary objects of dynamics. This approach simplifies the derivation of high-order effective Hamiltonians and reveals that the dispersive regime is a reorganization of resonant polaritonic hybridization. It successfully identifies an intrinsic, photon-number-independent Rabi frequency in the operator-centric description.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a more efficient, transparent, and unified way to derive effective Hamiltonians for complex light-matter interactions without the need for complex unitary transformations or Hilbert space truncation, which is highly relevant for cavity and waveguide QED.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of tracking high-order processes and deriving effective Hamiltonians in the dispersive regime using traditional state-centric methods, which can become computationally overloaded.

Main result. The introduction of a transition-centric framework that unifies resonant and dispersive regimes, revealing a photon-number-independent intrinsic Rabi frequency and persistent polaritonic hybridization.

Method. A transition-operator diagrammatic perturbation theory in Liouville space, using JLM (Jaynes-Cummings-Liouville) transition operators as fundamental building blocks and Laplace space transformations for time-dependent weights.

Model / system. A two-level atomic system coupled to a cavity mode, described by the Rabi and Jaynes-Cummings Hamiltonians, including both rotating-wave and counter-rotating terms.

Key observables. Intrinsic Rabi frequency, Stark shifts, Bloch-Sizable shifts, and the polaritonic joint population operator.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength (lambda), detuning (Delta), atomic frequency, and cavity frequency; regimes include resonant, dispersive, and ultrastrong coupling.

Assumptions / limitations. The expansion is valid in the dispersive regime where coupling is small compared to detunings; the operator subspace is assumed to be closed by including the joint population term.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a 2D representation of JLM transition operators and a zeroth-order diagram for the quantum Rabi interaction showing detunings.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a new transition-centric framework, develops a diagrammatic perturbation theory for higher-order effective Hamiltonians, applies the method to the Jaynes-Cummings and Rabi models, and demonstrates the unification of resonant and dispersive dynamics.

Abstract

We introduce a framework where light-matter transitions, rather than states, are the primary dynamical objects. Successive compositions of elementary transitions yield multiphoton processes with compact diagrammatic bookkeeping of resonant and off-resonant pathways. This approach enables transparent derivations of effective high-order Hamiltonians in the dispersive regime, foundational to quantum-information applications. Applied to the paradigmatic Jaynes-Cummings model, our framework reveals a photon-number-independent intrinsic Rabi frequency and persistent polaritonic hybridization in the dispersive regime, unifying resonant and dispersive limits.

Are free choices absolute, when internalized in Wigner's friend?

Authors: Laurens Walleghem
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14538v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper presents a theoretical argument showing that if an observer is treated as a quantum system, their experimental choices cannot be considered absolute. By merging Wigner's friend paradoxes with the PBR theorem, the author demonstrates that 'free choice' must be a relational property rather than an absolute one.

Why it may be interesting. This is interesting for researchers in quantum foundations and open quantum systems as it challenges the fundamental status of experimental agency and explores the limits of observer-independent reality in complex quantum networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether the 'free choices' (experimental settings) of observers are absolute or relative when those observers are internalized as quantum systems within a Wigner's friend-style setup.

Main result. The author derives a contradiction against the absoluteness of free choices by combining the Local Friendliness (LF) no-go theorem with the Pusey-Barrett-Rudolph (PBR) argument, suggesting that free choices must have a relational nature.

Method. The study uses a theoretical construction that combines the PBR scenario with an extended Wigner's friend setup, employing entanglement swapping and logical contextuality arguments to derive a contradiction at the possibilistic level.

Model / system. The model involves a multi-agent setup with two friends (Alice and Bob) in sealed labs, a super-observer (Wigner), and external observers (Charlie and Debbie) interacting with qubits (TA and TB) that encode the friends' choices.

Key observables. Measurement outcomes for the agents (a, b, c, d, w) and measurement settings (x, y, z).

Important parameters / regimes. The choice amplitudes (alpha and beta) and the distinction between the computational and X-basis measurements.

Assumptions / limitations. The argument relies on the assumptions of Local Agency, Absoluteness of Observed Events (AOE), and the validity of the PBR theorem and quantum universality.

Figures summary. A sketch of the protocol combining the Wigner's friend scenario with the PBR argument.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Wigner's friend problem, defines the extended setup using the PBR framework, presents the mathematical derivation of the contradiction against the absoluteness of free choices, and discusses the implications for quantum interpretations.

Abstract

Wigner's thought experiment illustrates quantum theory's measurement problem by considering an observer who measures a quantum system inside a sealed lab, modeled unitarily by an outsider. Recent extensions of this thought experiment, referred to as extended Wigner's friend arguments, question how different observers can reason consistently about each other in quantum setups, and challenge the absoluteness of the outcome value obtained by the friend under a notion of locality. In this work, we present an argument against the absoluteness of free choices under the same notion of locality, using an extended Wigner's friend scenario based on the Pusey--Barrett--Rudolph theorem. Similar arguments based on other contextuality or nonlocality models are possible.

Corner Charge Fluctuations in Higher Dimensions

Authors: Xiao-Chuan Wu, Pok Man Tam, Xuyang Liang, Zenan Liu, Dao-Xin Yao, Zheng Yan, Shinsei Ryu
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13971v1

Summary. This paper extends the theory of corner charge fluctuations to three and higher dimensions, providing a universal framework to study quantum geometry and criticality. By analyzing trihedral corners and wedges, the authors show how to extract the many-body quantum metric and identify dimension-dependent scaling laws. The results are validated through Quantum Monte Carlo simulations of Heisenberg and Weyl semimetal models.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and AMO physics as it provides a theoretical foundation for using real-space correlation measurements, such as those in quantum gas microscopy, to probe universal quantum geometry and criticality in higher dimensions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to generalize the framework of corner charge fluctuations from two spatial dimensions to higher dimensions (d >= 3) and to develop a protocol for extracting many-body quantum geometry in 3D.

Main result. The authors derived a universal angle dependence for trihedral corners in 3D and identified a wedge-corner contribution that probes the quantum metric, while establishing dimension-dependent scaling laws for insulators, CFTs, and metals.

Method. The study combines analytical derivations using Gram matrices and multi-region subtraction schemes with numerical Quantum Monte Carlo simulations of Heisenberg and Weyl semimetal models.

Model / system. The work investigates various many-body systems including the Columnar-Dimerized (CD) and Double-Cubic (DC) AFM Heisenberg models at the O(3) quantum critical point, as well as a lattice Weyl semimetel model.

Key observables. Bipartite charge fluctuations (second cumulant of the disorder operator), dimensionless corner contribution (C3), universal angle function (f3), and the quantum metric (S2).

Important parameters / regimes. Spatial dimensions (d >= 3), corner/wedge angles, radial moment (Upsilon_d), and the distinction between insulating, conformal (CFT), and metallic regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes continuous translation symmetry and rotationally invariant (isotropic) systems, and assumes the analyticity of the static structure factor in even dimensions.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Heisenberg models used, finite-size extrapolations of the angle function, the real-space partition scheme for wedge-corner fluctuations, and the scaling of the disorder operator.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of higher-dimensional fluctuations, provides analytical derivations for 3D trihedral corners and general d-dimensional parallelotopes, presents numerical validation using QMC on spin models, demonstrates quantum metric extraction via Weyl semimetals, and concludes with scaling analysis across different phases of matter.

Abstract

Measuring charge fluctuations within a subregion provides a powerful probe of quantum many-body systems. In two spatial dimensions, the shape dependence of the dimensionless corner contribution encodes universal data of quantum critical points and reveals observables of quantum geometry in various quantum phases. Here, we systematically extend this framework to higher dimensions. In three dimensions, we derive the universal angle dependence associated with trihedral corners of a generic parallelepiped and benchmark the predictions against Monte Carlo simulations of lattice models at the O(3) quantum critical point. We further identify a wedge-corner contribution that directly probes the quantum metric, supported by numerical results for a lattice Weyl semimetal model. More generally, we obtain angle functions for polyhedral corners of arbitrary parallelotopes in general dimensions and clarify the scaling of the corner contribution across phases of matter. While insulators and conformal critical points exhibit similar behavior across dimensions, metals display a characteristic even-odd dimensional effect.

Generating Non-Decomposable Maps with Differentiable Semidefinite Programming

Authors: Angela Rosy Morgillo, Davide Poderini, Fabio Anselmi, Fabio Benatti, Massimiliano F. Sacchi, Chiara Macchiavello
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14644v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The authors present a new optimization method using differentiable semidefinite programming to systematically generate positive non-decomposable quantum maps. This framework allows for the discovery of new map families and provides numerical evidence for long-standing conjectures in quantum information theory. It effectively automates the search for complex mathematical structures used to detect bound entanglement.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful new numerical tool for exploring the geometry of quantum channels and entanglement, which is directly relevant to understanding the structure of open quantum systems and entanglement detection.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of systematically constructing positive, non-decomposable linear maps, which are essential for detecting bound entangled states.

Main result. The development of a differentiable SDP framework that successfully generates new numerical examples of non-decomposable maps, identifies a new parametrized family of maps, and provides numerical evidence for the PPT square conjecture.

Method. An optimization framework using differentiable semidefinite programming (SDP) layers within a gradient-based loop (Adam optimizer) to minimize a loss function based on positivity and non-decomposability certificates.

Model / system. Linear maps between finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, represented via the Choi-Jamiołkowski isomorphism using Choi matrices.

Key observables. Positivity certificates (via k-symmetric extensions), non-decomposability witnesses (zeta_1), and trace-preservation properties.

Important parameters / regimes. Input and output dimensions (d, d'), extension order (k), and loss function hyperparameters (epsilon, gamma, omega, nu).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of k-symmetric extensions provides a sufficient but not necessary condition for positivity, which may exclude some valid maps.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the optimization framework flow; Figure 2 displays Choi matrices and masks; Figure 3 shows real non-decomposable maps; Figure 4 shows convergence relative to hyperparameters; Figures 5-7 illustrate the evolution of loss functions and SDP objectives during training.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-decomposable maps, presents the differentiable SDP optimization framework, details the loss function engineering and masking techniques, demonstrates results including new map families and conjecture testing, and discusses limitations and future work.

Abstract

Positive maps that are not decomposable are a key resource in entanglement theory because they can detect bound entangled states, yet systematic methods for constructing them remain limited. We introduce an optimization framework based on differentiable semidefinite programming (SDP) for generating positive non-decomposable maps under flexible structural constraints on their Choi matrices. The method combines SDP-based certificates of non-decomposability and positivity with gradient-based optimization, enabling a systematic search over maps with different input and output dimensions. Within this framework, we generate previously unknown numerical examples, identify a parametrized family of maps arising from masked Choi matrices, and construct real non-decomposable maps. We further show that the same approach can be adapted to explore open questions in quantum information theory, including the PPT square conjecture and recently proposed eigenvalue bounds for 2-positive trace-preserving maps.

Singular Asymptotics of SPADE in Quantum Source Discrimination

Authors: Natsuki Kariya
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14432v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · interference shaping light 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper uses Singular Learning Theory to analyze the asymptotic performance of SPADE versus direct imaging for detecting closely spaced, weak light sources. It reveals that while SPADE is theoretically superior in ideal conditions, detector misalignment can lead to a collapse in detection power and allow direct imaging to perform better in practical finite-photon regimes.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum sensing as it provides a rigorous mathematical framework (SLT) to understand why ideal quantum-optimal benchmarks (like SPADE) may fail to provide advantages in practical, misaligned, finite-photon scenarios.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the performance of Spatial-Mode Demultiplexing (SPADE) versus direct imaging for discriminating between one and two incoherent point sources in the singular regime of weak and closely spaced emitters.

Main result. The study identifies that while aligned SPADE has a universal subleading advantage due to different multiplicity in the singular expansion, misaligned SPADE can be outperformed by direct imaging in finite-photon regimes and exhibits a 'blind separation' point where power collapses.

Method. Application of Singular Learning Theory (SLT) to analyze zeta-function poles, real log canonical thresholds, and Bayes free-energy asymptotics, supplemented by Monte Carlo simulations.

Model / system. A one-dimensional diffraction-limited imaging system with a Gaussian point-spread function (PSF) used for discriminating between a single source (null) and two incoherent sources (alternative) with relative brightness epsilon and separation s.

Key observables. Log Bayes factor, Bayes free-energy difference, mode labels (q) in SPADE, and detection positions (Xi) in direct imaging.

Important parameters / regimes. Relative brightness (epsilon), spatial separation (s), detector misalignment (theta), number of photons (n), and PSF width (sigma).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a Gaussian PSF and uses a physically motivated reduction of full SPADE to a binary-SPADE model in the sub-Rayleigh regime.

Figures summary. The figures likely show comparisons of Bayes free energy and power between direct imaging and SPADE, including plots of the 'blind separation' effect and the scaling of power on different intrinsic scales.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the source discrimination problem, defines the aligned and misaligned models, applies Singular Learning Theory to derive asymptotic expansions for both cases, validates these asymptotics with numerical simulations, and concludes with the physical implications of model singularity.

Abstract

We study far-field discrimination between one and two incoherent point sources in the singular regime of weak and closely spaced emitters. Under ideal alignment, spatial-mode demultiplexing (SPADE) attains the quantum-optimal large-sample Stein exponent, but the finite-photon behavior near the one-source boundary and the effect of realistic imperfections remain less understood. Using singular learning theory, we analyze both the aligned and misaligned problems. In the aligned Gaussian case, we derive the zeta-function poles for direct imaging and SPADE, show that both share the same real log canonical threshold $λ=1/2$ but differ in multiplicity, and obtain the corresponding Bayes free-energy asymptotics. This yields a universal subleading advantage of aligned SPADE in the local prior-weighted regime. In the misaligned setting, we study a physically motivated binary-SPADE reduction that retains the full leading $O(s^2)$ leakage contrast near alignment, with corrections from the detailed higher-mode redistribution entering only at $O(s^4)$. We show that misaligned binary-SPADE and direct imaging acquire nontrivial local power on different intrinsic scales, $s=O(n^{-1/4})$ and $s=O(n^{-1/2})$, respectively. However, finite-$n$ Neyman--Pearson comparisons under common physical conditions reveal that direct imaging is stronger on the plotted grids and that misaligned binary-SPADE exhibits an exact blind separation $s^\ast=2θ$, where its power collapses to $α$. These results identify model singularity as a structural organizing principle for finite-photon quantum discrimination and clarify how ideal aligned SPADE benchmarks can fail to translate into finite-$n$ advantages under misalignment.

The influence of strong coupling between single-photon source and spectral filter on photon statistics

Summary. The paper shows that the impact of a cavity on a single-photon source's photon statistics can be accurately modeled as simple spectral filtering. This effective model remains valid even in the strong-coupling regime, except when extreme energy transfer rates trigger significant non-Markovian effects.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems as it provides a simplified analytical tool for predicting photon statistics in integrated photonic circuits, while precisely defining the boundaries where non-Markovian dynamics necessitate more complex models.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether the strong coupling between a single-photon source and a cavity (acting as a spectral filter) significantly alters the photon statistics beyond simple spectral filtering, specifically regarding the influence of the filter on the source's internal dynamics.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that an effective analytical model treating the cavity solely as a Lorentz spectral filter is accurate for most parameter regimes, even in strong coupling, provided that cavity-induced non-Markovian effects do not dominate.

Method. The study uses a three-way comparison between a rigorous numerical Lindblad master equation approach, an effective analytical model with modified relaxation rates, and a simplified model that neglects the filter's influence on the source dynamics.

Model / system. The system consists of a two-level system (TLS) undergoing incoherent pumping, coupled to a single-mode cavity via the Jaynes-Cummings Hamiltonian. The dynamics are described by a Lindblad master equation incorporating TLS decay, pure dephasing, cavity decay, and incoherent pumping.

Key observables. The second-order autocorrelation function g2(0) of the cavity emission.

Important parameters / regimes. Rabi constant (Omega), TLS decay rate (gamma_diss), cavity linewidth (gamma_a), pure dephasing rate (gamma_deph), pumping rate (gamma_pump), and the efficiency parameter (beta) representing the ratio of energy transfer to losses.

Assumptions / limitations. The cavity mode is initially vacuum; the cavity acts as a Lorentz spectral filter; the TLS is incoherently pumped; the cavity frequency is resonant with the TLS transition frequency.

Figures summary. Figure 3 demonstrates the validity of the analytical approach for Rabi frequencies up to 100 times the TLS decay rate.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of coupling single-photon sources to cavities, presents the theoretical framework (Hamiltonian and Master Equation), develops the effective analytical model, compares the three different modeling approaches across various coupling regimes, and identifies the limits of the effective model's validity.

Abstract

One of the most common approaches for coupling optical single-photon sources and photonic integrated circuits is to use a cavity. The cavity acts as a spectral filter that distorts the light spectrum and changes its statistical properties. But in the general case one should take into account not only spectral filtering of light but also the spectral filter influence on the single-photon source dynamics. We build an effective analytical model for description of the cavity influence on the photon statistics of light emitted by the single-photon source as spectral filtering only. We show that this model correctly describes the photon statistics even in a strong-coupling regime between the single-photon source and the spectral filter. Our results can be useful for analytical modeling of photon statistics of quantum emitters strongly coupled to various electromagnetic interfaces.

Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fulde-Ferrell state of spin polarized atomic Fermi superfluid on a spherical surface

Authors: Yan He, Chih-Chun Chien
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14985v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper examines how the LOFF state behaves when a spin-polarized Fermi gas is placed on a spherical surface. It demonstrates that while spatial modulation can accommodate population imbalance, the LOFF phase is fragile and only stable near the breakdown of uniform superfluidity. The study highlights how compact, curved geometries influence the stability of inhomogeneous quantum phases.

Why it may be interesting. This work is relevant to many-body dynamics and AMO physics as it explores how geometric curvature and topological constraints influence the formation of inhomogeneous superfluid phases in ultracold atomic gases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the existence, stability, and spatial structure of the Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fulde-Ferrell (LOFF) state in a population-imbalanced Fermi superfluid confined to a thin spherical shell.

Main result. The researchers found that LOFF states with multiple nodes can become energetically favorable at high spin polarization, though the phase is fragile and exists only near the boundary where uniform superfluidity vanishes.

Method. The Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) formalism is used, expanding quasiparticle wavefunctions in spherical harmonics and solving the resulting matrix equations iteratively.

Model / system. A two-component, spin-polarized atomic Fermi gas with pairing interactions confined to a 2D thin spherical surface, modeled using a BCS mean-field Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Order parameter (gap function) spatial modulation, spin-resolved densities, total density profiles, and the grand potential.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin polarization (p), interaction strength (-ln(k_F a)), chemical potentials, and angular momentum quantum number (l).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes zero temperature (T=0), contact pairing interactions, the BCS mean-field approximation, and azimuthal symmetry.

Figures summary. Figures show phase boundaries for uniform vs. non-uniform solutions, spatial profiles of the order parameter and densities for various LOFF states, and energy comparisons of the grand potential across different polarizations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of LOFF in curved geometry, describes the BdG formalism and spherical expansion, presents the phase diagram and stability analysis, details the spatial structure of LOFF nodes, and concludes with a discussion on the fragility of the state.

Abstract

By implementing the Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) formalism of population-imbalanced atomic Fermi gases with pairing interactions in a thin spherical shell, we characterize the Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fulde-Ferrell (LOFF) state in such a compact geometry. We first construct a phase diagram showing where uniform solutions of spin-polarized Fermi superfluid from the BdG equation cease to exist due to the vanishing order parameter. Near the boundary, various LOFF states with spatially modulating order parameters and density profiles can survive as convergent solutions to the BdG equation. When both uniform and LOFF solutions are present, we compare their grand potentials to determine the energetically favorable state and find that the LOFF states with multiple nodes in the order parameter become more stable at higher spin polarization. However, the LOFF state only survives close to the phase boundary where the uniform solutions vanish, indicating fragility of the LOFF state on a spherical surface. We also briefly discuss possible implications.

Model Checking Matrix Product States against Linear Chain Logic

Authors: Ming Xu, Yihao Chen, Ji Guan
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14356v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new spatial logic called Linear Chain Logic (LCL) to formally verify properties of 1D quantum many-body states as a function of system size. By leveraging the transfer-operator structure of Matrix Product States, the authors develop an efficient algorithm to detect asymptotic patterns like correlation decay and phase transitions. This work bridges the gap between quantum model checking and the numerical analysis of tensor networks.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a formal, logic-based verification layer for tensor network simulations, allowing researchers to systematically certify the asymptotic behavior and stability of many-body states as they approach the thermodynamic limit.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a formal framework for verifying spatial and size-dependent properties of 1D many-body states (Matrix Product States) as the system size grows.

Main result. The introduction of Linear Chain Logic (LCL) and an approximate model-checking algorithm that can automatically verify properties like state validity, correlation decay, and phase transitions in MPS families.

Method. A transfer-operator-based approach that reduces MPS properties to the spectral analysis of a completely positive map, using semilinear over/under-approximations to compute evidence sets for LCL formulas.

Model / system. One-dimensional periodic Matrix Product States (MPS) representing ground states of translationally invariant 1D local Hamiltonians, such as the TFIM, XXZ, and Kitaev models.

Key observables. Squared norms, correlation functions, energy differences, decay ratios, and indicators for phase transitions or symmetry breaking.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (N), bond dimension (D), spectral radius, and peripheral eigenvalues.

Assumptions / limitations. The logic is specifically designed for periodic MPS families; the algorithm relies on approximations due to the undecidability of the Skolem problem; the 'Until' operator is excluded from the logic syntax.

Figures summary. Tables showing the decision results (True/False/Unknown/Timeout) and runtimes for various synthetic and physical MPS models across different bond dimensions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the verification gap, proposes the LCL syntax, establishes the mathematical connection between MPS and CP maps, develops the top-down and bottom-up model-checking algorithms, and validates the method using synthetic and physical spin-chain models.

Abstract

Matrix product states (MPS) are a standard tensor-network representation for ground states of one-dimensional quantum many-body systems, and they underpin widely used simulation tools such as DMRG. However, while quantum model checking has been developed mainly for quantum programs and communication protocols (with properties expressed along a time axis), there is still no comparable framework for systematically verifying \emph{spatial} and \emph{size-dependent} properties of physical many-body states, where the key parameter is the system size. This paper takes a step toward bridging the gap. We propose \emph{Linear Chain Logic} (LCL), a spatial logic designed to specify physically meaningful properties of periodic MPS families as the system size grows, such as nontriviality on rings and large-size asymptotic patterns. Our approach builds on a simple but powerful connection: every periodic MPS naturally induces a completely positive map (a quantum operation) on its virtual space, so many quantitative features of the MPS can be analysed through the repeated application of the operation. Using this perspective, we derive an effective procedure to compute the inner products of an MPS at a given size and to support richer LCL specifications, without relying on brute-force state expansion. We then develop approximate model-checking algorithms that combine sound bounding with asymptotic structural analysis, enabling scalable reasoning about large system sizes. Experiments on representative MPS families illustrate that our method can automatically verify nontriviality and detect asymptotic spatial regimes in a way that complements traditional numerical techniques.

Summary. This paper constructs a family of deformed SYK models using the q-Askey scheme of orthogonal polynomials and maps them to specific gravitational backgrounds like sine dilaton gravity. It provides a holographic dictionary relating quantum chaos (Krylov complexity) and entanglement to geometric features like Einstein-Rosen bridges and ETW branes.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to provide a systematic microscopic construction and bulk holographic interpretation for the full hierarchy of q-Askey deformations of the double-scaled SYK (DSSYK) model.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that these deformations correspond to specific bulk geometries, such as sine dilaton gravity with End-Of-The-World (ETW) branes, and show that Krylov complexity matches the length of Einstein-Rosen bridges in these backgrounds.

Method. The study employs the double-scaling limit, ensemble averaging, chord diagrammatic methods, and the theory of basic hypergeometric orthogonal polynomials (the q-Askey scheme) alongside algebraic approaches using von Neumann algebras.

Model / system. The primary model is the q-Askey deformed Double-Scaled SYK (DSSYK) model, which involves a large number of Majorana fermions with all-to-all interactions and deformations described by the Askey-Wilson scheme.

Key observables. Krylov complexity (spread complexity), entanglement entropy, energy spectrum (continuous and discrete), and the semiclassical partition function.

Important parameters / regimes. Deformation parameters (t_i), the q-deformation parameter, the number of fermions (n_F), the double-scaling parameter (lambda), and temperature (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the large N limit and the double-scaling limit; it also assumes the validity of the chord diagrammatic expansion for evaluating expectation values.

Figures summary. Figure 16 illustrates the entanglement of ETW branes from both Lorentzian and Euclidean perspectives, showing the distance between the boundary and the brane.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the microscopic construction of the q-Askey deformed SYK Hamiltonian, moves to the derivation of the chord algebra and its relation to orthogonal polynomials, explores the holographic interpretation of Krylov complexity and entanglement entropy, and concludes with the study of the adjoint deformation and its connection to gauge theory.

Abstract

We construct families of deformations of the double-scaled SYK (DSSYK) model and investigate their bulk interpretation. We introduce microscopic deformations of the SYK model which, after ensemble averaging and in the double-scaling limit, are described by a transfer matrix encoding the recurrence relations of basic orthogonal polynomials in the q-Askey scheme. For certain families of deformations in the semiclassical limit at finite temperature, the chord number (encoding Krylov complexity) corresponds to the length of an Einstein-Rosen bridge connecting an End-Of-The-World brane to an anti-de Sitter asymptotic boundary. By increasing one of the deformation parameters, the models eventually exhibit discrete energy levels, signaling a new geometric transition in sine dilaton gravity. Via the SYK-Schur duality, Krylov complexity also admits a representation-theoretic interpretation as the spread of the SU(2) spin in the index of an $\mathcal{N}=2$ SU(2) gauge theory. We study the operator algebras of the deformed theories. The algebras can be type II$_1$ or type I$_\infty$ factors, depending on the operators that are included. The entanglement entropy between the type II$_1$ algebras for a pure state manifests as an extremal surface through the Ryu-Takayanagi formula. We discuss connections between our results and the emergence of baby universes in the bulk.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that quantum resource distillation, such as entanglement purification, can be performed universally. It proves that one can achieve the optimal distillation rate even when the input state is completely unknown, provided the protocol uses a specific sequence of operations. This result is achieved by extending the quantum Stein's lemma to handle unknown i.i.d. source states.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for quantum information theorists as it provides a robust, 'black-box' way to perform entanglement purification and other resource manipulations without needing to characterize the source state beforehand.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the non-universality of quantum resource distillation, where existing optimal protocols require precise prior knowledge of the input noisy state to achieve optimal rates.

Forming ensembles of such point sets with fixed multiplicity, we develop simulation techniques leading to the desired value of correlation dimension in a vast continuous interval of scales. In particular, we demonstrate the possibility to produce ensembles of data sets with a low number of points with the needed properties. Furthermore, we show that the positive $ν$ distributions, apart from a region near the upper scale limit, show fractal behaviour that extends to infinitesimally low scales. As an example, we apply our findings to producing simulations relevant to the search for critical fluctuations, related to QCD critical endpoint, in heavy-ion collision experiments.

Summary. This paper uses DFT+DMFT to investigate the electronic properties of the different structural phases of Samarium. Detailed structure

Main problem. The electronic structure and strong correlation effects of Samarium (Sm) metal in its alpha, beta, and gamma phases are not fully understood due to the complex behavior of its 4f electrons.

Main result. The 4f electrons are found to be well-localized with weak hybridization to conduction electrons, and the system exhibits significant strong correlation effects, including extremely large effective masses and non-Fermi liquid behavior.

Method. The study uses a first-principles DFT+DMFT approach with the WIEN2K program, employing a hybridization expansion continuous-time quantum Monte Carlo (CT-QMC) impurity solver and the maximum entropy method for analytical continuation.

Model / system. The physical system is Samarium (Sm) metal, specifically investigating the alpha (rhombohedral), beta (hcp), and gamma (bcc) structural phases at ambient pressure.

Key observables. Momentum-resolved spectral functions, total and partial (4f) density of states, self-energy functions, valence state histograms, and quasiparticle weight (Z) and effective mass (m*).

Important parameters / regimes. On-site Coulomb interaction (U) of 6.1 eV, Hund's exchange (JH) of 0.835 eV, and spin-orbit coupling (SOC).

Assumptions / limitations. The study uses a single-site DMFT approximation and a simplified double-counting scheme, which may lead to discrepancies with experimental data.

Figures summary. Figure 3 shows the density of states (total, partial 4f, and orbital-resolved) compared to experimental PES/BIS data; Figure 4 displays the imaginary parts of the Matsubara self-energy functions; Figure 5 presents valence state histograms for occupancy and angular momentum.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Sm electronic structure, describes the DFT+DMFT methodology and parameters, presents results for the three structural phases (DOS, self-energy, and localization), and concludes with a comparison to experimental data.

Abstract

The electronic structure of Samarium (Sm) was calculated using the density functional theory combined with the single-site dynamical mean-field theory. In this work, we investigated the electronic properties of α, \b{eta} and γ phases at ambient pressure, including the band structures, density of states, self-energy functions and valence state histograms. Our results agree with the experimental data.The calculation shows that the 4f electrons in all these phases are well localized, the Kondo peaks are suppressed and the hybridization between the 4f electrons and conduction electrons are quite weak. Our results also show the strong correlation effect is significant in Sm metal.
