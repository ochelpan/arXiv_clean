A Neural-Network Framework to Learn History-Dependent Constitutive Laws and Identifiability of Internal Variables

Authors: Mayank Raj, Lianghao Cao, Andrew Stuart, Kaushik Bhattacharya
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14179v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new neural network architecture designed to learn material constitutive laws while strictly adhering to thermodynamic and mathematical stability requirements. It successfully predicts the mechanical response of magnesium with high accuracy and provides a theoretical proof that the learned internal variables are unique up to a linear transform.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a neural network framework to learn history-dependent constitutive laws that satisfy fundamental physical constraints like the second law of thermodynamics, polyconvexity, and material stability, while addressing the identifiability of learned internal variables.

Main result. The authors present a physically consistent framework that achieves 2% relative error in predicting the response of magnesium and theoretically prove that learned internal variables are unique up to a linear transformation.

Method. The framework uses Input Convex Neural Networks (ICNN) to approximate energy and dissipation potentials, incorporating growth functions to enforce stability and a hydrostatic-deviatoric decomposition to improve accuracy.

Model / system. The system is a polycrystalline magnesium (Mg) unit cell modeled via Taylor-averaged response, exhibiting viscoplastic, finite-deformation, and non-Markovian (history-dependent) behavior.

Key observables. Deformation gradient tensor (F) and first Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor (P).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of internal variables (n=6 found to be optimal), growth/singularity parameters (kappa, Xi), and transition rate (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. The material response is assumed to be viscoplastic; the converse theorem for identifiability requires specific reachability conditions (Assumption 4.9) and sufficient dimensionality (m >= n).

Figures summary. Figures show time-series of stress and strain, distribution of stress components, error analysis regarding the number of internal variables and energy decomposition, and a visual proof of internal variable identifiability via linear regression.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the need for physically consistent models to introducing a Markovian framework with internal variables, followed by detailing the neural network architecture and training, demonstrating application to magnesium, and concluding with mathematical proofs of identifiability.

Abstract

The identification of constitutive laws is ubiquitous in engineering: in modeling of materials where experimental data are fitted to mathematical models or learning surrogate models to beat the FE\textsuperscript{2} computational cost of multiscale numerical simulations. However, these models of constitutive laws, unless equipped with a potential formulation, are not necessarily consistent with (a) the second law of thermodynamics; (b) stability of the material under extreme applied strain; and (c) the mathematical theory underpinning the existence of solutions of the governing equation. In this work, we present a causal and energetic formulation, consistent with aforementioned properties, of learning a history-dependent constitutive law. This characterization of the class of internal variables sheds light on the equivalence class of equivalent surrogate models for the constitutive law. We show that the internal variables that are learned from the data are unique up to a linear transform. The framework is deployed to learn the Taylor-averaged response of a polycrystalline magnesium unit cell. We achieve 2\% relative error in the prediction of the Taylor-averaged response.

A study of variational single solitary waves governed by the conservative-extended KdV equation with applications to shallow water dispersive shocks

Authors: Saleh Baqer, Hamid Said
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14024v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a simplified variational method to find solitary wave solutions for the extended KdV equation. These solutions are used to accurately predict the behavior of complex dispersive shock waves, such as resonant radiation and undular bores, in shallow water systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to derive simpler, more practical approximate solitary wave solutions for the conservative-extended KdV (eKdV) equation to better describe complex dispersive hydrodynamic phenomena like undular bores and resonant dispersive shock waves.

Main result. The authors successfully derived variational solitary wave solutions that are simpler than previous asymptotic methods and demonstrated excellent agreement with numerical simulations for both classical and non-classical (resonant) dispersive shock wave regimes.

Method. A variational approach based on the method of averaged Lagrangians was used to construct the solutions, which were then validated against direct numerical simulations using Fourier spectral and Runge-Kutta methods.

Model / system. The study focuses on the conservative-extended KdV (eKdV) equation, a nonlinear dispersive wave model including cubic nonlinearity and fifth-order dispersion, applied to shallow water gravity-capillary waves.

Key observables. Solitary wave amplitude (As), velocity (Vs), inverse width (ws), resonant wavenumber (kr), and Whitham shock velocity (Us).

Important parameters / regimes. Small parameter epsilon (representing weak nonlinearity/dispersion), coefficients c1, c3, and c4 (governing higher-order effects), and the jump magnitude delta.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a conservative system where c2 = 2c3 to maintain Hamiltonian structure, and relies on the weak nonlinearity and long-wave (shallow water) approximations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the structure of a DSW; Figure 2 compares numerical and theoretical predictions for lead edge velocity and height; Figures 3 and 4 show numerical vs. theoretical comparisons for resonant wavenumber, shock velocity, and wave height while varying model coefficients.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the eKdV model, derives solitary wave solutions using averaged Lagrangians, validates these solutions against numerical simulations, and extends the analysis to study the properties of classical and non-classical (resonant) dispersive shock waves using Whitham modulation theory.

Abstract

The extended KdV equation is a nonlinear dispersive wave model that is asymptotically or variationally derived from the full dispersive Euler shallow water waves equations when gravity-capillary and higher order nonlinear effects are taken into account, under weakly nonlinear and long-wave approximations. This reduction introduces four additional terms beyond the classical KdV equation: a nonlinear term (quadratic nonlinearity), two nonlinear-dispersive terms, and a fully dispersive term (fifth order dispersion). In this paper, we employ a variational approach based on averaged Lagrangians to analyze the accuracy of single solitary wave solutions governed by a particular extended KdV equation where energy conservation is a key feature. Compared with solitary wave solutions previously obtained through higher order asymptotics and algebraic methods, the present variational solutions are notably simpler and more readily applicable to practical problems. The solitary wave solutions obtained through this method are then systematically compared with direct numerical simulations, and the corresponding results are critically discussed. We further demonstrate the applicability of these single solitary waves to problems in the field of non-convex dispersive hydrodynamics. These problems include shallow water classical undular bores, commonly known as dispersive shock waves, and non-classical (resonant) dispersive shocks which are additionally analyzed using the concept of Whitham shocks. Theoretical predictions show excellent agreement with numerical simulations.

Accurate computation of the electron-phonon interaction contribution to the total energy

Authors: Shilpa Paul, Mogadalai Pandurangan Gururajan, Amrita Bhattacharya, Tiramkudlu R. S. Prasanna
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14472v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a more accurate way to calculate how electron-phonon interactions affect the total energy of crystals. By deriving new mathematical formulas that better handle numerical stability, the authors achieved the most precise energy difference calculations for diamond and lonsdaleite to date.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty in accurately computing the electron-phonon interaction (EPI) contribution to the total energy in crystalline solids, specifically addressing the theoretical invalidity of the adiabatic approximation and the computational challenges of the partial-FM-occupied term.

Main result. The authors derived new, computationally stable expressions for the partial-FM-occupied term within the non-adiabatic approximation and demonstrated the most accurate ab initio total energy calculation to date for carbon polymorphs (diamond and lonsdaleite).

Method. The study uses Rayleigh-Schrödinger perturbation theory and the non-adiabatic approximation, implemented via the TDepES module in the ABINIT software package.

Model / system. The system consists of crystalline solids, specifically carbon polymorphs (diamond and hexagonal lonsdaleite), modeled using a coupled electron-phonon Hamiltonian including DFT energy, zero-point vibrational energy, and the electron-phonon interaction (Debye-Waller and Fan-Migdal terms).

Key observables. Total energy, band-structure energy, partial-FM-occupied contribution, and relative stability of polymorphs.

Important parameters / regimes. Electron-phonon matrix elements, phonon frequencies, occupation numbers, and the imaginary smearing parameter (delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the non-adiabatic approximation (including phonon energy in the denominator) is required for accuracy, particularly for IR-active materials.

Figures summary. Table I shows how the EPI contribution to band-structure energy varies with different smearing parameters; Table II reports various energy contributions to the standard Hamiltonian at 0 K.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the limitations of the standard Hamiltonian and adiabatic approximation, derives new mathematical expressions for the EPI terms, applies these to carbon polymorphs, and discusses the implications for finite-temperature free energy.

Abstract

The standard Hamiltonian of a coupled electron-phonon system is based on second-order perturbation theory. The EPI contribution in the standard Hamiltonian consists of two terms, the EPI contribution to the band-structure energy and the partial-Fan-Migdal (FM)-occupied contribution. Within the non-adiabatic approximation, we derive a new expression for the partial-FM-occ contribution and show that it has the structure of a higher-order term, and not a second-order term. Along similar lines, we derive new expressions for the computation of the partial-FM-occ term. The new expressions for the partial-FM-occ term must be preferred over the standard expressions, in theoretical and computational studies, because they incorporate the complete physics underlying this term. Unlike the EPI contribution to individual eigenstates, the EPI contribution to the total energy must be computed in the non-adiabatic approximation for all materials, Infra-red (IR) active and IR-inactive. We report the computation of the standard Hamiltonian, for the first time, for Carbon polymorphs (diamond and hexagonal lonsdaleite) by including the EPI contribution to the total energy. This is the most accurate ab initio total energy reported till date. The present work also opens the way to compute the ab initio free-energy more accurately at finite temperatures by including the EPI contribution.

Agentic Design of Compositional Descriptors via Autoresearch for Materials Science Applications

Authors: Matteo Cobelli, Stefano Sanvito
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14671v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces an autonomous AI agent framework called AUTOMAT that can perform scientific feature engineering for materials science. By iteratively designing chemical descriptors from formulas, the agent achieves higher predictive accuracy for electronic and magnetic properties than traditional manual methods. This demonstrates the potential for 'autoresearch' agents to automate complex scientific discovery tasks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of designing effective, task-specific numerical descriptors for materials property prediction, which currently relies on manual and expertise-dependent feature engineering.

Main result. The AUTOMAT framework successfully uses an AI agent to autonomously design compositional descriptors that outperform standard baselines (Magpie, fractional composition) for predicting band gaps and Curie temperatures.

Method. An 'autoresearch' framework (AUTOMAT) where an LLM-based coding agent iteratively proposes, implements, and evaluates descriptor strategies using a Random Forest workflow and a greedy accept/reject optimization loop.

Model / system. The study focuses on chemical compounds and inorganic materials, specifically using chemical formulas to predict experimental band gaps and Curie temperatures in ferromagnetic compounds.

Key observables. Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and R-squared (R2) values for property prediction.

Important parameters / regimes. The agent is restricted to information derivable solely from chemical formulas; the optimization uses a held-out validation set for stopping criteria.

Assumptions / limitations. The agent is restricted to composition-only information (no structural data); the machine learning model architecture (Random Forest) and hyperparameters are held constant.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the AUTOMAT workflow schematic; Figure 2 and 3 illustrate the trajectory of model performance and descriptor dimensionality over search iterations; Figure 4 compares final test performance across different descriptor representations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of manual feature engineering, presents the AUTOMAT agentic framework, details the experimental setup for band gap and Curie temperature prediction, reports performance gains and descriptor evolution, and discusses limitations such as dimensionality explosion and redundancy.

Abstract

Autoresearch offers a flexible paradigm for automating scientific tasks, in which an AI agent proposes, implements, evaluates, and refines candidate solutions against a quantitative objective. Here, we use composition-based materials-property prediction to test whether such agents can perform a task beyond model selection and hyperparameter optimization: the design of input descriptors. We introduce Automat, an autoresearch framework where a coding agent based on a large language model generates composition-only descriptors for chemical compounds and evaluates them using a random forest workflow. The agent is restricted to information derivable from chemical formulas and iteratively proposes, implements, and tests chemically motivated descriptor strategies. We apply Automat, with OpenAI Codex using GPT-5.5 as the coding agent, to the prediction of experimental band gaps in inorganic materials and Curie temperatures in ferromagnetic compounds. In both tasks, Automat improves over fractional-composition, Magpie, and combined fractional-composition/Magpie baselines, while producing descriptor families that are chemically interpretable. These results provide a demonstration that autoresearch agents can generate competitive, task-specific materials descriptors without manual feature engineering during the run. They also reveal current limitations, including descriptor redundancy, sensitivity to greedy feature expansion, and the need for explicit complexity control, descriptor pruning, and more sophisticated search strategies.

Atomically resolved intrinsic superconducting gap in (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 films

Authors: Xinxin Wang, Yaqi Chen, Cui Ding, Lizhi Xu, Jian-Jian Miao, Guangdi Zhou, Zhuoyu Chen, Yu-Jie Sun, Jin-Feng Jia, Qi-Kun Xue
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14806v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper uses atomic-resolution STM to show that bilayer nickelates possess an intrinsic nodeless, two-gap superconducting state. It reveals that oxygen loss during sample handling can create misleading V-shaped spectra that mimic nodal superconductivity. This finding is crucial for correctly identifying the pairing symmetry in this emerging class of high-temperature superconductors.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether the superconducting pairing symmetry in Ruddlesden-Popper bilayer nickelates is nodal or nodeless, and understanding how oxygen stoichiometry affects the observed gap structure.

Main result. The study identifies an intrinsic nodeless, two-gap superconducting state in (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 films, demonstrating that previously observed V-shaped spectra are artifacts of oxygen loss rather than evidence of nodal symmetry.

Method. Atomic-resolution scanning tunneling microscopy and spectroscopy (STM/STS) were used on films grown via gigantic-oxidative atomic-layer-by-layer epitaxy, utilizing a cryogenic UHV transfer technique to preserve oxygen content.

Model / system. The physical system consists of 1.5-unit-cell (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 ultrathin films grown on SrLaAlO4 substrates, characterized by a sqrt(2) * sqrt(2) surface reconstruction.

Key observables. Superconducting gap scales (inner and outer), spectral line shapes (U-shaped vs. V-shaped), and temperature-dependent conductance evolution.

Important parameters / regimes. Gap energies of ~14 and ~20 meV, superconducting transition temperature (Tc) near 53 K, and oxygen loss controlled by UHV transfer time.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of transfer time as a proxy for oxygen stoichiometry and the assumption that the U-shaped spectra represent the intrinsic electronic state.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Crystal structure, STM topography, and resistivity; Fig 2: Atomic-resolution imaging and double-gap U-shaped spectra; Fig 3: Temperature evolution of the gap and Dynes model fits.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the nickelate system, describes the growth and UHV transfer method, presents atomic-scale spectroscopic evidence for a nodeless two-gap state, analyzes the impact of oxygen loss on spectral shape, and concludes with the identification of the intrinsic superconducting state.

Abstract

Ruddlesden-Popper bilayer nickelates provide an emerging platform for studying high-temperature superconductivity, yet the superconducting pairing symmetry remains under debate. Here, we use atomic-resolution scanning tunnelling microscopy and spectroscopy to investigate superconducting 1.5-unit-cell (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 films grown on SrLaAlO4. A cryogenic ultrahigh-vacuum (UHV) sample transfer preserves an ordered sqrt(2) * sqrt(2) surface and yields reproducible U-shaped spectra with two gap scales of ~14 and ~20 meV and extended flat zero-conductance bottoms. By contrast, samples exposed for a longer time in UHV without cooling during transfer show V-shaped spectra despite retaining the surface reconstruction and a transport superconducting transition onset above 40 K. Wide-energy-range spectra indicate that oxygen loss can mix density-wave-related spectral weight. Our measurements provide an atomic-scale observation of the intrinsic nodeless superconducting gap in bilayer nickelate ultrathin films.

Summary. This paper explores the magnetic behavior of Fe/Ti multilayers, highlighting how biquadratic exchange coupling drives transitions between collinear and non-collinear magnetic states. By combining structural analysis with magnetometry and FMR, the authors show that interface roughness and temperature-dependent coupling are critical to understanding the system's magnetism.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how structural characteristics like interface roughness and interdiffusion influence the static and dynamic magnetic properties, specifically the role of biquadratic exchange coupling in Fe/Ti multilayers.

Main result. The study demonstrates that including biquadratic exchange coupling is essential to reproduce experimental hysteresis and FMR data, revealing a transition from antiferromagnetic to non-collinear magnetic configurations as temperature increases.

Method. Experimental techniques include HRTEM, XRD, XRR, SQUID magnetometry, and VNA-FMR, complemented by a macrospin model and numerical relaxation algorithms to minimize free energy.

Model / system. The system consists of weakly antiferromagnetically coupled Fe/Ti superlattices (Fe/Ti/Fe trilayers and N=1 to 6 superlattices). The model uses a macrospin approach incorporating Zeeman energy, uniaxial and effective perpendicular anisotropy, and both bilinear and biquadratic exchange coupling.

Key observables. Hysteresis loops (major/minor), FMR absorption spectra, resonance field vs. frequency dispersion, and total magnetization.

Important parameters / regimes. Bilinear (J_bl) and biquadratic (J_bq) exchange constants, temperature (10-300 K), layer thicknesses, and anisotropy fields.

Assumptions / limitations. The macrospin model treats each Fe layer as a single magnetic domain; the FMR modeling uses the Smit-Beljers approach without damping.

Figures summary. Fig 1: HRTEM and electron diffraction for structure/texture; Fig 2: XRR profiles for thickness; Fig 3: Model schematic and M-H loop comparison; Fig 5: Temperature dependence of coupling and magnetization; Fig 6: Theoretical phase diagram; Fig 7: FMR frequency dependence.

Paper structure. The paper begins with structural characterization (TEM, XRD, XRR), moves to static magnetic measurements (SQUID) and their temperature dependence, then presents dynamic magnetic studies (FMR), and concludes with a theoretical phase diagram and model validation.

Abstract

This work explores the static and dynamic magnetic properties of weakly antiferromagnetically coupled Fe/Ti superlattices, emphasizing the link between magnetic behavior and structural characteristics. HRTEM and XRD analyses confirm alternating Fe and Ti layers with rough interfaces, especially in upper layers. Magnetic measurements reveal two-step hysteresis loops and a temperature and thickness-dependent interlayer exchange coupling (IEC). A macrospin model incorporating bilinear and biquadratic coupling reproduces the experimental data and supports a phase diagram analysis identifying non-collinear configurations. The results underscore the impact of structural imperfections and highlight the crucial role of biquadratic exchange in Fe/Ti/Fe multilayers.

Chiroptical Ternary Entropy Harvesting from Self-Assembled Block Copolymer Nanopatterns

Authors: Wookjin Jung, Serin Jeong, Kyulim Kim, Dongkyu Lee, Sang Ouk Kim, Jihyeon Yeom
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14353v1

Summary. This paper presents a method to turn fabrication defects in self-assembled nanostructures into a high-density source of random numbers. By using the local chirality of gold nanopatterns, the researchers can generate ternary (three-state) random sequences that are more information-dense and secure than traditional binary methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The research aims to repurpose unavoidable fabrication-induced stochasticity in nanostructures, traditionally viewed as defects, into a functional resource for high-density ternary entropy harvesting for information security.

Main result. The authors demonstrated a platform that harvests ternary random sequences (trits) from the local chiroptical response of gold nanopatterns, achieving near-ideal Shannon entropy (1.585 bits per trit) and passing NIST statistical and machine-learning-based predictability tests.

Method. The method involves using block copolymer self-assembly to create stochastic gold nanopatterns, followed by Raman Optical Activity (ROA) mapping to extract handedness-dependent optical signals, and statistical/ML analysis to validate randomness.

Model / system. The physical platform consists of gold (Au) nanopatterns formed via the self-assembly of polystyrene-block-poly(methyl methacrylate) (PS-b-PMMA) on silicon, PDMS, or ITO substrates, characterized by a fingerprint-like, locally chiral morphology.

Key observables. Chiroptical contrast (positive, negative, or neutral), Raman Optical Activity (ROA) signal intensity, Shannon entropy, and Hamming distance.

Important parameters / regimes. Average lateral period of ~64 nm, Au thickness of ~11.6 nm, and ternary encoding density of log2(3) bits per trit.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption is that the local structural stochasticity is sufficiently uncorrelated and unpredictable to serve as a valid entropy source.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows structural characterization (SEM, TEM, GISAXS); Figure 2/3 presents the ternary ROA maps and statistical validation (entropy, autocorrelation); Figure 4 shows machine-learning predictability analysis; Figure 5 demonstrates robustness under environmental stressors.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of repurposing defects, describes the fabrication and characterization of the Au nanopatterns, details the chiroptical readout and ternary digitization, provides rigorous statistical and ML-based validation of the entropy quality, and concludes with robustness and scalability assessments.

Abstract

Nanoscale fabrication inevitably produces local stochasticity that is commonly treated as a defect, but can instead be harnessed as a material resource for information security. Here we report a chiroptical platform for ternary entropy harvesting based on stochastic Au nanopatterns formed by block copolymer self-assembly. By transducing fabrication-induced stochastic microstates into handedness-dependent optical responses through chiroptical mapping, our platform enables native ternary digitization rather than conventional binary encoding, allowing physically harvested ternary random sequences to be used for key generation. This raises the information density to log2(3) = 1.585 bits per trit, approximately 58.5% higher than the binary limit, enabling more entropy to be harvested from a limited physical footprint. The harvested outputs exhibit near-balanced symbol populations, negligible spatial and inter-sample correlations, Shannon entropy approaching the ternary ideal, and resistance to statistical and machine-learning-based prediction. These results establish self-assembled chiroptical nanostructures as a scalable platform for cryptographic key generation, secure edge devices, and distributed Internet-of-Things platforms.

Abstract

As photovoltaics (PVs) scale from one to multiple terawatts over the next decade, ensuring sustainable deployment is urgently required. Crystalline silicon (c-Si) PVs, the current industry standard, will generate an estimated 160 million tonnes of waste by 2050, and there remains complex technoeconomic challenges associated with their recycling. Metal-halide perovskite (MHP)-based tandem PVs not only promise higher power conversion efficiencies than single-junction c-Si devices, but also offer intrinsic advantages for circularity, including simpler device architectures, low-temperature processing, and more accessible materials recovery routes. At this pivotal juncture when perovskite PVs begin to enter the market, this review examines the critical circularity challenges that must be addressed: substitution of scarce raw materials, scalable recycling protocols, cost-effective stack delamination, safe lead sequestration, and policy frameworks to encourage circularity across the device lifecycle with effective incentives. By integrating the materials, technoeconomic and policy dimensions that go beyond conventional lifecycle assessments, we outline actionable strategies to co-optimize device performance and sustainability. This review aims to guide researchers, policymakers, and industry stakeholders in steering perovskite-based tandem PVs towards a circular and responsible commercialization pathway within the global clean-energy transition.

Current induced magneto-optical Kerr effect as a probe of Dirac carriers in Bi$_{1-x}$Sb$_x$ alloy

Authors: Ryota Miyazaki, Shunzhen Wang, Guanxiong Qu, Yukihiro Marui, Yuta Kobayashi, Masashi Kawaguchi, Masamitsu Hayashi
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14975v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the current-induced MOKE in Bi-Sb alloys can effectively distinguish between Dirac and parabolic electronic structures through specific scaling relations with resistivity and mobility. It also explores how the interplay between spin and orbital Hall effects influences the MOKE signal magnitude.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the microscopic origin of the current-induced magneto-optical Kerr effect (MOKE) in Bi-Sb alloys and seeks to determine if the signal can serve as a probe for Dirac carriers.

Main result. The MOKE signal scales with resistivity as rho^1.7 and mobility as mu^2.0, a scaling behavior that supports a Dirac electron model over a conventional parabolic free-electron model.

Method. The researchers combined experimental MOKE and transport measurements on Bi-Sb thin films with numerical modeling using Maxwell's equations, the Kubo formula, and tight-binding calculations.

Model / system. The physical system consists of Bi(1-x)Sb(x) alloy thin films in the semimetallic regime, specifically focusing on the Bi-rich regime where carriers exhibit Dirac-like dispersion near the L-point.

Key observables. Kerr rotation angle (theta_K), Kerr ellipticity (eta_K), resistivity (rho_xx), carrier density (n_c), and mobility (mu_c).

Important parameters / regimes. Sb concentration (x), spin diffusion length (ls approx 18 nm), and the scaling exponent (alpha) relating mobility to carrier density.

Assumptions / limitations. The model calculations may neglect contributions from topological surface states, which could account for discrepancies in certain Sb concentrations.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the device schematic and spin accumulation concept; Fig 2 presents transport properties (resistivity, density, mobility) vs x; Fig 5 compares scaling exponents for different models; Fig 7 shows the x-dependence of SHE and OHE contributions to the MOKE signal.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the MOKE problem, describes the experimental setup and material growth, presents transport and MOKE scaling results, compares Dirac vs. free-electron models, analyzes the interplay between spin and orbital Hall effects, and concludes with a discussion of model limitations.

Abstract

We study the current-induced magneto-optical Kerr effect (MOKE) in Bi$_{1-x}$Sb$_x$ semi-metalic alloys. The MOKE signal is found to be the largest in pure Bi ($x=0$), exceeding that of transition metals by nearly four orders of magnitude, and decreases monotonically with increasing Sb concentration. We find the MOKE signal scales with the resistivity ($ρ$) as $ρ^{1.7 \pm 0.6}$ and with the mobility ($μ_\mathrm{c}$) as $μ_\mathrm{c}^{2.0 \pm 0.2}$. Model calculations show that such exponent can be accounted for if the Dirac electrons are responsible for the generation of spin current. This is in contrast to the $ρ^{2}$ and $μ_\mathrm{c}^{-2}$ scaling of the MOKE signal induced by the free electrons in parabolic band. The scaling of the MOKE amplitude with the resistivity also partly accounts for the order of magnitude differences of the signal observed between metals, semimetals, and semiconductors. These results demonstrate that current induced MOKE serves as an effective means to characterize the nature of spin current in materials with diverse electronic structures.

To lower the barrier to developing MLIPs for non-experts, we propose Lang2MLIP, a multi-agent framework that takes natural-language input and formulates end-to-end MLIP development as a sequential decision-making problem solved by large language models (LLMs). At each step, a decision-making agent observes the current dataset, model, evaluation results, and execution log, and then automatically selects an appropriate action to improve the model. This removes the need for a predefined pipeline and enables the agent to self-correct by revisiting earlier subsystems when new failures arise. We evaluate this approach on a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) system with multiple components and interfaces. These results suggest that LLM-based multi-agent systems are a promising direction for automating MLIP development and making it more accessible to non-experts.

Summary. This paper studies how individual differences in bird size affect the stability and shape of migratory flocks. It finds that the formation is most robust to changes in birds near the leader and that the leader's properties can trigger a global reorganization of the entire group.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how localized individual differences, specifically variations in wingspan, affect the geometric structure and dynamical stability of migratory bird formations.

Main result. The stability of bird formations is position-dependent, being much broader near the leader and narrower near the outer wing; furthermore, modifying the leader causes a global reorganization of the formation, while other positions cause only localized deformation.

Method. A minimal dynamical framework using a lifting-line model with a horseshoe-vortex representation, combined with linear stability analysis and numerical integration using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method.

Model / system. A formation of N migrating birds modeled as a system of coupled equations of motion accounting for mass, velocity, thrust, self-induced drag, and interaction-induced drag via the Biot-Savart law.

Key observables. Formation geometry (U-shaped structure, streamwise and transverse spacing), steady-state flight velocity, drag reduction rate, and stability range of wingspan variations.

Important parameters / regimes. Wingspan (b), span ratio (lambda), number of birds (N), and the spatial position of the modified bird (i).

Assumptions / limitations. The air is treated as incompressible and inviscid; unsteady wake dynamics are neglected; aerodynamic interactions are approximated as primarily local (nearest-neighbor); and birds fly in a higher-speed regime.

Abstract

We investigate how localized inhomogeneity affects the geometry and stability of migratory bird formations. We use a lifting-line model with a horseshoe-vortex representation to describe the longitudinal dynamics of aerodynamic interactions. As a reference case, we first analyze homogeneous formations and show that their steady states exhibit a U-shaped geometry with hierarchical streamwise spacing, in which adjacent birds become progressively closer toward the leader. We then introduce localized inhomogeneity by modifying the wingspan of a single bird, with its physical properties determined by scaling relations. We determine the range of wingspan variation that preserves a stable formation. The stability range depends strongly on the position of the modified bird, being narrower near the outer wing and broader near the leader. These findings provide a minimal dynamical framework for understanding how local aerodynamic interactions and localized individual differences affect collective flight structures.
