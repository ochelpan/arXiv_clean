Dual Shapiro steps and fundamental transconductance in dc driven Bloch transistor

Authors: A. B. Zorin
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14080v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a method to use a DC-driven Bloch transistor to create a quantum resistance standard. By phase-locking Josephson and Bloch oscillations, the device exhibits quantized transconductance related to the resistance quantum. This approach offers a way to perform quantum metrology without the need for high magnetic fields.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the possibility of achieving phase-locking between Josephson oscillations and Bloch oscillations in a DC-driven Bloch transistor to establish a new quantum standard of resistance.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that DC-driven phase-locking leads to the formation of dual Shapiro steps and a fundamental transconductance of 1/Rq, providing a resistance standard that does not require strong magnetic fields.

Method. The study uses a modified Resistively Shunted Junction (RSJ) model, a double Fourier series expansion of the ground-state energy, and the slowly-varying phase method to analyze the dynamics.

Model / system. A Bloch transistor (BT) consisting of two small-capacitance Josephson junctions in series with a superconducting island, coupled to a Josephson circuit and driven by DC voltage and current sources.

Key observables. Josephson frequency (fJ), Bloch frequency (fB), transconductance (IB/VJ), current steps (dual Shapiro steps), and island voltage (VB).

Important parameters / regimes. Resistance quantum (Rq), asymmetry parameter (a), modulation depth (mu), blockade voltage (Vc), and the high-frequency/low-impedance regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to remain in its ground state (adiabatic approximation), the Josephson part is overdamped, and the backaction of the Bloch transistor on the Josephson circuit is negligible.

Figures summary. Figure 5 shows transconductance curves with slanted steps; Figure 6 illustrates a BT design using a dc SQUID for tuning; Figure 7 presents a proposed superconducting version of the quantum metrology triangle.

Paper structure. The paper begins by proposing the device and its physical mechanism, moves to the Hamiltonian and circuit dynamics, develops the mathematical framework for phase-locking and Fourier analysis, calculates specific observables like step width and transconductance, and concludes with implications for quantum metrology.

Abstract

We propose a superconducting circuit based on the Bloch transistor, a quantum device consisting of two small-capacitance Josephson junctions connected in series and having a small island in between. This device is driven by two dc electrical sources controlling Josephson oscillations of frequency $f_J = 2e\overline{V_J}/h$, related to the average voltage $\overline{V_J}$ on the transistor, and Bloch oscillations of frequency $f_B = \overline{I_B}/2e$, related to the average current $\overline{I_B}$ injected into the transistor island. Due to the Bloch transistor properties, these two types of oscillations can mutually phase lock, i.e., $f_J = f_B$. This leads to formation of current steps on the current-voltage curve at $\overline{I}_B = 2ef_J$, which are similar to the dual Shapiro steps appearing at current $\overline{I}=2ef$ under microwave irradiation of frequency $f$. Moreover, transconductance $\overline{I_B}/\overline{V_J}$ takes the fundamental value of $1/R_Q$, where $R_Q = h/4e^2$ is the resistance quantum. The obtained results pave the way to the alternative quantum standard of resistance, based on the superconducting circuit and operating without applying strong magnetic field.

Dynamical scaling near the pseudogap quantum critical point of the two-dimensional Hubbard model

Authors: Mathias Pelz, Gabriel Kotliar, Jan von Delft, Andreas Gleis
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15060v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper uses DCA and NRG to show that the 2D Hubbard model exhibits $\omega/T$ scaling and strange-metal transport near a quantum critical point. It demonstrates that the optical response is dominated by vertex contributions and identifies Marginal Fermi Liquid behavior in the nodal regions, providing a theoretical basis for observations in cuprate superconductors.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the dynamical scaling and the microscopic origin of the pseudogap-to-Fermi-liquid transition and the associated strange-metal behavior in the 2D Hubbard model.

Main result. The study finds $\omega/T$ scaling in spin and cluster-current susceptibilities, a dominant vertex contribution to optical conductivity leading to $1/T$ conductivity, and evidence for Marginal Fermi Liquid behavior in the nodal self-energy.

Method. Four-patch Dynamical Cluster Approximation (DCA) combined with the Numerical Renormalability Group (NRG) as a cluster impurity solver.

Model / system. A two-dimensional square-lattice $t$-$t'$-$U$ Hubbard model representing hole-doped cuprates, specifically focusing on the regime near critical doping $p^* \simeq 0.14$.

Key observables. Local spin susceptibility, cluster-current susceptibility, optical conductivity (including bubble and vertex contributions), and nodal/antinodal electronic spectral functions and self-energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Critical doping $p^* \simeq 0.14$, temperature scales $T_{FL}$ and $T_{NFL}$, and the scaling variable $x = \omega/T$.

Assumptions / limitations. The self-energy is approximated as momentum-independent within four patches; the results may require more accurate computations to solidify the MFL claim.

Figures summary. Phase diagrams showing $T=0$ transitions; scaling collapses of spin and current correlators; $\omega/T$ scaling of susceptibilities and optical conductivity; and decomposition of current-current correlations by type.

Paper structure. The paper establishes a phase diagram, identifies the non-Fermi-liquid regime, demonstrates dynamical scaling in spin and current responses, analyzes the vertex contribution to transport, and examines nodal/antinodal spectral dynamics.

Abstract

We study dynamical scaling in the quantum-critical fan of the pseudogap-metal to Fermi-liquid transition of the two-dimensional Hubbard model. Using a four-patch dynamical cluster approximation with the numerical renormalization group as a cluster impurity solver, we access real-frequency dynamics over several decades at arbitrary temperatures. Close to the critical doping, the local spin and cluster-current susceptibility spectra exhibit $x=ω/T$ scaling of the form $χ''(ω,T)\sim \tanh(x/2)$, and the cluster contribution to the optical conductivity obeys $Tσ'_{\mathrm{cl}}(ω,T) \sim \tanh(x/2)/x$, implying a $1/T$ cluster dc conductivity. In the scaling regime, the vertex contribution to the cluster optical response is much larger than the bubble contribution. We further find evidence for a marginal-Fermi-liquid nodal self-energy. This, together with the $1/T$ vertex contribution to the conductivity, implies strange-metal optical transport in the quantum critical region. Our results describe several qualitative aspects of several experimental observations.

Energy efficiency of quantum computers

Authors: Miquel Carrasco-Codina, Pau Escofet, Paul Hilaire, Ariane Soret, Sam Nerenberg, Victor Champain, Gerard Milburn, Klara Theophilo, Sophie H. Li, Irais Bautista, Andrés Gómez, Jose Miralles, Sergi Abadal, Carmen G. Almudéver, Eduard Alarcón, Raja Yehia
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15090v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a framework to benchmark the energy efficiency of different quantum computing architectures. By analyzing the power profiles of superconducting, spin, ion, atom, and photonic platforms, it highlights the trade-offs between hardware complexity and computational throughput. The work is significant for assessing the environmental and economic sustainability of future quantum technologies.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a critical resource-based perspective on the scalability of quantum hardware, which is essential for understanding the long-term viability of large-scale quantum architectures and their integration with classical control systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Quantifying the energy consumption and energy efficiency of various quantum computing platforms to determine their potential sustainability compared to classical computers.

Main result. The authors establish a standardized framework for benchmarking energy efficiency (computations per Joule) and identify that while cooling is the primary energy driver in NISQ-era devices, control electronics will likely dominate as architectures scale.

Method. The study uses a hardware-aware approach, defining efficiency as the ratio of algorithms performed to energy consumed, and incorporates platform-specific compilation models (accounting for routing and gate sets).

Model / system. The analysis covers five representative platforms: superconducting qubits, silicon spin qubits, trapped ions, neutral atoms, and photonic qubits, using power values from existing hardware components.

Key observables. Energy efficiency (computations per Joule), total power consumption (P), circuit depth (D), and number of samples (N_samples).

Important parameters / regimes. Two-qubit gate time, reset time, qubit connectivity graphs, and the ratio of two-qubit gates to total layers.

Assumptions / limitations. The model focuses on NISQ-era power consumption, excludes manufacturing/life-cycle costs, and assumes power consumption remains relatively constant between idle and active states.

Figures summary. A power breakdown chart showing the percentage of energy used by cooling, control, and classical components; and plots of energy efficiency versus circuit depth and number of possible computations over 24 hours.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining a new energy efficiency metric, followed by a platform-by-platform analysis (solid-state, atom-based, and photonic), and concludes with a discussion on scaling, limitations, and the potential rebound effect.

Abstract

How much energy does a quantum computer consume? Are they more efficient than their classical counterparts? In this work, we make a step towards answering these questions. We define the energy efficiency of a quantum computer as the ratio of the number of algorithms it can perform during a given time over the energy consumed by the hardware during this time. We analyze the most representative physical platforms currently envisioned to be used as building blocks of quantum computers: superconducting qubits, silicon spin qubits, trapped ions, neutral atoms and photonic qubits. Including insights from experts in all these technologies and taking into account algorithm compilation constraints, we discuss the advantages and inconveniences of each platform from an energy standpoint. Beyond providing concrete values of the energy consumption of current quantum computers, we lay the foundation of a framework to benchmark the energy efficiency of any future quantum computing architecture.

Fraxonium: Fractional fluxon states for qudit encoding

Authors: Luca Chirolli, Valentina Brosco, Uri Vool, Gianluigi Catelani, Luigi Amico
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14586v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper proposes a new superconducting circuit called 'Fraxonium' that uses engineered Josephson potentials to create stable, multi-level quantum systems (qudits). By using fractional fluxon states, these qudits are naturally protected from leakage errors, offering a scalable path for more efficient quantum computing.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a novel way to engineer multi-level quantum systems with geometric protection, which is highly relevant for researchers working on holonomic quantum computing and controlled quantum dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need to expand the local Hilbert space for quantum computing using qudits (d > 2) that are protected from leakage errors, which is a major limitation in current transmon-based architectures.

Main result. The proposal of 'Fraxonium,' a superconducting circuit architecture that uses engineered multi-harmonic Josephson potentials to create d degenerate low-lying 'fraxon' states protected from leakage.

Method. Fourier engineering of the Josephson potential using a multi-harmonic approach, combined with WKB approximations for tunneling and numerical diagonalization of the Hamiltonian.

Model / system. A generalized fluxonium circuit featuring a capacitor, inductor, and a tailored Josephson element. The Hamiltonian includes a multi-harmonic potential U_d(phi) created via 'kite' building blocks.

Key observables. Energy spectrum, tunneling matrix elements, dipole matrix elements for phase and charge operators, and transition energies.

Important parameters / regimes. Energy scales EJ, EC, and EL; the regime EJ >> EC, EL; and the periodicity of the engineered potential.

Assumptions / limitations. The regime EJ >> EC, EL is assumed; higher energy excitations (plasmons) and non-nearest-neighbor tunneling are neglected; and the potential is treated as a sum of Fourier harmonics.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the circuit diagram and the engineered potential with low-energy eigenstates. Figure 3 displays the spectrum for a ququart system. Figure 6 and 7 show dipole matrix elements and transition energies for the qutrit system.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the qudit problem, proposes the Fraxonium architecture, details the Fourier engineering method for potential sculpting, analyzes specific d=3, 4, and 5 cases, and proposes a STIRAP protocol for gate implementation.

Abstract

We propose a superconducting circuit hosting $d$ low-lying states, well separated from the rest of the spectrum, that naturally realizes a qudit system protected from leakage errors. The system represents a generalization of the fluxonium and the low-energy states are constituted by fractional fluxon states, that we call {\it fraxons}, localized in the minima of a suitably designed Josephson potential. The latter is tailored through a Fourier engineering approach, that employs multi-harmonic Josephson building block elements composed by a Josephson junction and an inductance connected in series. We present the spectrum of a $d=4$ and a $d=5$ qudit system and study in detail the qutrit case. We analyze the dipole matrix elements for coupling to radiation and propose a non-Abelian, stimulated Raman adiabatic passage (STIRAP) protocol for single-qutrit gates, that is particularly suited for the present system. The proposed platform opens novel perspectives in circuit engineering and quantum computing beyond the qubit paradigm.

From Chaos to Synchrony in Recurrent Excitatory-Inhibitory Networks with Target-Specific Inhibition

Authors: Carles Martorell, Rubén Calvo, Alessia Annibale, Miguel A. Muñoz
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14916v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper extends the classic Sompolinsky-Crisanti-Sommers theory to E/I networks with structured inhibition. It demonstrates that tuning how inhibition targets different populations can fundamentally reorganize the network's phase diagram, even suppressing chaos in favor of synchronized oscillations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how target-specific inhibition in recurrent excitatory-inhibitory (E/I) networks regulates the emergence of macroscopic dynamical regimes like asynchronous chaos, persistent activity, and coherent oscillations.

Main result. The study identifies three distinct classes of phase diagrams based on inhibitory coupling, showing that target-specific inhibition can suppress chaotic fluctuations and drive the system into coherent oscillatory activity.

Method. The authors use Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (DMFT) and the Martin-Siggia-Rose-De Dominicis-Janssen formalism to derive self-consistent equations for macroscopic observables and perform linear stability analysis.

Model / system. A two-population recurrent neural network consisting of segregated excitatory and inhibitory neurons with sparse, asymmetric Gaussian connectivity and target-specific inhibitory couplings.

Key observables. Mean activities, two-time autocorrelation functions, Largest Lyapunov Exponent (LLE), Kuramoto order parameter, and phase lag between populations.

Important parameters / regimes. Target-specific inhibition parameters (beta and delta), coupling strength (g), connectivity mean (J0) and variance (J2), and the fraction of excitatory neurons (f).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is performed in the thermodynamic limit (N to infinity), assumes a tanh activation function, and uses a sparse connectivity construction to preserve Dale's principle.

Figures summary. Figures show phase diagrams and heat maps of mean activity and autocorrelation, trajectories of E/I activity, eigenvalue spectra, and the Largest Lyapunov Exponent across parameter spaces.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the E/I network model, develops the DMFT framework for sparse networks, classifies phase diagrams based on inhibitory parameters, analyzes stability transitions (Type I and II), and validates results with numerical simulations.

Abstract

Biological neural networks can operate in qualitatively distinct dynamical regimes, and transitions between these regimes are thought to underlie changes in computation and behavior. The seminal work of Sompolinsky, Crisanti, and Sommers (SCS) showed that random recurrent networks undergo a transition from quiescence to asynchronous chaos, establishing a paradigmatic link between random connectivity, dynamical instability, and internally generated fluctuations in neural circuits. Here, we extend this framework to two-population firing-rate networks with segregated excitatory and inhibitory neurons and target-specific inhibitory couplings that break excitation--inhibition balance. Using dynamical mean-field theory, we derive self-consistent equations for the macroscopic mean activities and autocorrelations, together with stability criteria distinguishing mean-driven and fluctuation-driven instabilities. We show that target-specific inhibition organizes the phase diagram into three qualitative classes: inhibition-dominated or strictly balanced networks display only quiescent activity and asynchronous chaos; excitation-dominated networks display persistent activity together with either synchronous chaos with non-vanishing mean activity or coherent oscillations, depending on the stability-matrix eigenvalues. Crucially, coherent oscillations do not coexist with chaotic fluctuations around the periodic mean trajectory; rather, their onset suppresses the chaotic component, reminiscent of input-induced suppression of chaos. These results generalize SCS theory to recurrent networks with explicit excitatory--inhibitory structure and identify target-specific inhibition as a key control parameter for large-scale neural dynamics.

Generalized Suzuki-Chin Factorization in Bosonic Path Integral Molecular Dynamics

Authors: Jacob Higer, Barak Hirshberg
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14687v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the fourth-order Generalized Suzuki-Chin Factorization can be applied to bosonic and fermionic Path Integral Molecular Dynamics. By using a re-weighting technique, the method achieves significantly faster convergence of energy estimators with respect to the number of beads. This allows for more efficient simulations of quantum statistics in trapped particle systems.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a highly efficient numerical tool for simulating the thermodynamic properties of ultracold quantum gases and many-body bosonic/fermionic systems without increasing the complexity of the underlying sampling algorithm.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need to accelerate the convergence of Path Integral Molecular Dynamics (PIMD) simulations for indistinguishable particles (bosons and fermions) as the number of imaginary-time beads increases.

Main result. The Generalized Suzuki-Chin Factorization (GSF) can be successfully integrated into bosonic PIMD, providing a 2-4x speedup in convergence with respect to the Trotter number with only modest computational cost.

Method. The authors implement the fourth-order GSF scheme as a re-weighting factor for observables, allowing the use of standard second-order primitive factorization for sampling.

Model / system. The study focuses on non-interacting indistinguishable particles (bosons and fermions) in harmonic traps and sinusoidal potentials, modeled via ring polymers in a discretized path integral framework.

Key observables. Kinetic energy, potential energy, total energy, radial distribution functions, and the fermionic sign/weight.

Important parameters / regimes. Trotter number (P), inverse temperature (beta), tuning parameter (alpha), and particle number (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The re-weighting efficiency may decrease at very low Trotter numbers due to lack of overlap between distributions; the analytical derivations are specific to harmonic potentials.

Figures summary. Figures show the number of beads required for convergence, the speedup of GSF over PF as a function of temperature, and the stability of the fermionic sign under GSF re-weighting.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the GSF implementation for bosons, demonstrates its efficiency through convergence tests in harmonic and sinusoidal potentials, provides analytical derivations for exact partition functions using polynomial theory, and evaluates its performance for fermionic systems.

Abstract

Modern implementations of path integral molecular dynamics (PIMD) simulations of distinguishable particles frequently make use of high order factorization schemes for the Boltzmann operator to expedite convergence of equilibrium averages. Among these methods is the generalized Suzuki-Chin factorization (GSF), which is accurate up to fourth order in the imaginary-time step. In this work, we show that the GSF decomposition of the Boltzmann operator is applicable to bosonic PIMD, and results in an improved convergence of estimators. In particular, we show that the recently developed quadratic scaling bosonic PIMD need not change when using the GSF. The GSF scheme is implemented as a re-weighting factor for observables, without affecting the sampling generated by the standard, second-order, primitive factorization. We study the effect of this factorization for bosons in a harmonic trap and a sinusoidal potential. We also assess the effectiveness of GSF in calculating fermionic expectation values for harmonically-trapped atoms. In all these cases, we find that the GSF speeds up convergence with the Trotter number by a factor of $\sim 2-4$ across a wide temperature range, at only a modest computational cost.

HQTN-SER: Speech Emotion Recognition with Hybrid Quantum Tensor Networks

Authors: Mahad Mohtashim, Nouhaila Innan, Muhammad Shafique
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14523v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a hybrid quantum-classical framework for speech emotion recognition that uses a Matrix Product State (MPS) inspired quantum circuit. By using a structured tensor network approach, the model efficiently captures correlations in acoustic features using very few qubits and remains robust to simulated hardware noise. It provides a scalable, hardware-aware design for applying quantum machine learning to complex signal processing tasks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) is fragile in real-world conditions due to subtle cues and high parameter requirements of classical models, while existing quantum machine learning approaches lack the architectural inductive bias needed to model correlated acoustic features.

Main result. The HQTN-SER framework achieves high accuracy (up to 80.12% on RAVDESS) and demonstrates significant robustness to hardware noise, showing that MPS-inspired quantum circuits can effectively model speech correlations with very low qubit counts.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical pipeline using PCA for dimensionality reduction, an MPS-inspired variational quantum circuit for feature extraction, and a fusion strategy that combines quantum measurement statistics with classical latent embeddings.

Model / system. A hybrid quantum-classical model utilizing a 1D chain of 3 to 4 qubits with an MPS-style architecture featuring angle encoding (Ry rotations), parameterized Ry and Rz gates, and nearest-neighbor CNOT entanglement.

Key observables. Expectation values of single-qubit Z operators.

Important parameters / regimes. Small qubit counts (n=3, 4), shallow circuit depth, and 32-dimensional PCA-compressed input features.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the structured connectivity of a tensor network can act as a sufficient inductive bias for speech features and does not claim unconditional quantum advantage.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the HQTN-SER workflow; Figure 2 details the MPS-structured variational circuit; Figures 3-5 display training/validation loss and accuracy curves across datasets.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of SER fragility, proposes the HQTN-SER framework, details the MPS-inspired architecture and fusion strategy, presents experimental results on three datasets, performs ablation and hardware-noise studies, and concludes with future directions.

Abstract

Speech emotion recognition (SER) remains fragile in real-world conditions because emotional cues are subtle, speaker-dependent, and easily confounded by recording variability, while high-performing deep models typically rely on large and carefully curated training sets. Quantum machine learning offers an alternative way to introduce nonlinear correlation modeling with compact modules, yet existing quantum SER studies remain limited and the impact of circuit structure is not well understood. This paper presents HQTN-SER, a hybrid quantum-classical framework that investigates how quantum tensor network connectivity can support SER under small-qubit settings. HQTN-SER introduces (i) an MPS-inspired quantum tensor network module that enforces structured interactions to model correlations in speech representations with a small number of trainable parameters, and (ii) a fusion strategy that combines quantum measurement features with a learned classical latent embedding for end-to-end emotion classification. We evaluate HQTN-SER on three public benchmarks (RAVDESS, SAVEE, and MDER) under a unified preprocessing and training protocol. The proposed model achieves consistent performance across datasets, RAVDESS = 80.12%, SAVEE = 78.26% and MDER = 73.51% accuracy, with stable convergence and low qubit counts, showing that tensor network structure can be an effective and hardware-aware design choice for quantum-assisted SER. The results provide a reproducible baseline and clarify when structured quantum modules can add value to affective computing today.

Interference of dynamical arrest, thermodynamic instabilities and energy-scale competition in symmetric binary mixtures

Authors: Ricardo Peredo-Ortiz, Edilio Lázaro-Lázaro, Magdaleno Medina-Noyola, Luis Fernando Elizondo-Aguilera
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14064v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a unified non-equilibrium description of symmetric binary mixtures by exploring how the competition between different energy scales dictates the interplay between phase separation and glass formation. It introduces a new structural metric to distinguish between gels formed by density fluctuations versus those formed by concentration fluctuations. This work is significant for understanding the complex phase behavior of soft matter systems like colloids and proteins.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study addresses the inadequacy of equilibrium phase diagrams to describe soft-matter mixtures where kinetic barriers and dynamical arrest prevent equilibrium, specifically investigating the competition between thermodynamic instabilities and kinetic arrest.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that energy-scale competition (alpha) partitions the phase diagram into distinct kinetic regimes, and introduce a structural order parameter (chi) to resolve 'structural blindness' in distinguishing condensation-driven from demixing-driven arrested states.

Method. The study utilizes the Non-Equilibrium Self-Consistent Generalized Langevin Equation (NESCGLE) theory and a number-concentration formalism to analyze structural relaxation and stability.

Model / system. Symmetric binary mixtures (SBM) of particles with equal diameters using a Hard-Sphere plus Square-Well (HSSW) interaction potential.

Key observables. Partial structure factors (Sii), density fluctuations (Srho-rho), concentration fluctuations (Scc), localization length (gamma), and the structural order parameter (chi).

Important parameters / regimes. Energy-scale ratio (alpha) of cross-species to self-species attraction, density (rho), temperature (T), and concentration (c).

Assumptions / limitations. The direct correlation functions are approximated using the Ornstein-Zernike Equation with a closure relation, and the attractive tail is treated as a perturbative term.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the classification of kinetic regimes (Type I-IV) based on alpha, the resolution of structural blindness using density/concentration fluctuations, and the evolution of instability surfaces (spinodal and lambda-lines).

Paper structure. The paper moves from defining the problem of incomplete phase diagrams to establishing a theoretical framework (NESCGLE), classifying topological regimes via energy-scale competition, and finally introducing a structural metric to distinguish between different types of arrested states.

Abstract

The equilibrium behavior of binary mixtures can be understood through the competition of energy scales, which classifies their corresponding phase diagrams into distinct topological regimes (Types I-IV). However, in many soft-matter mixtures, strong competing interactions and kinetic barriers often promote dynamical arrest, disrupting the formation of equilibrium and metastable states, and thus rendering conventional phase diagrams incomplete. Here we extend the description and classification of binary systems inside regions of thermodynamical instability. Specifically, we discuss how the interplay between two kind of instabilities and kinetic arrest generates a variety of amorphous states driven by different underlying mechanisms. For strong cross-attraction, for example, dynamical arrest suppresses demixing, whereas in competitive regimes, a mixture may display either condensation-driven or demixing-induced arrested states. The crossover between these regimes can be described by a structural order parameter $χ$, providing a unified non-equilibrium description that reconciles theoretical predictions with experimentally observed arrested states.

Non-Invertible Symmetries on Tensor-Product Hilbert Spaces and Quantum Cellular Automata

Authors: Rui Wen, Kansei Inamura, Sakura Schafer-Nameki

Summary. The paper establishes that any weakly integral fusion category can be realized as a symmetry on a 1D tensor-product Hilbert space when allowing for Quantum Cellular Automata refinements. It provides a systematic way to calculate the indices of these symmetries and demonstrates the construction using anyon chains.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a fundamental link between abstract algebraic structures (fusion categories) and concrete lattice models, which is essential for understanding the classification of non-invertible symmetries in many-body dynamics and topological phases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the realizability of (1+1)-dimensional fusion category symmetries on tensor-product Hilbert spaces, specifically addressing how Quantum Cellular Automata (QCAs) refine these realizations.

Main result. The authors prove that any weakly integral fusion category admits a QCA-refined realization on a tensor-product Hilbert space and show that the indices of the symmetry operators and QCAs are uniquely determined by the categorical data.

Method. The study uses fusion category theory, diagrammatic calculus, and the construction of anyon chain models to analyze the relationship between non-invertible symmetries and GNVW indices.

Model / system. The paper focuses on (1+1)-dimensional quantum many-body systems, specifically 1D spin chains and anyon chains with tensor-product Hilbert spaces and finite-dimensional on-site degrees of freedom.

Key observables. Quantum dimensions, GNVW indices, left and right defect dimensions, and the index of Quantum Cellular Automata.

Important parameters / regimes. Weak integrality of the fusion category, grading group E, and the lattice quantum dimension.

Assumptions / limitations. The proof relies on five physical assumptions regarding the locality of defects, the behavior of symmetry compositions, and the homogeneity of the index.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the rearrangement of local Hilbert spaces in anyon chains, the structure of the Ising category sector, and the sequential circuits used to map different Hilbert space sectors.

Paper structure. The paper begins by investigating constraints on QCA-refined realizations, moves to a general construction of lattice models for weakly integral categories, and concludes with specific applications to Tambara-Yamagami and Metaplectic categories.

Abstract

We investigate realizations of (1+1)-dimensional fusion category symmetries on tensor-product Hilbert spaces, allowing for mixing with quantum cellular automata (QCAs). It was argued recently that any such realizable symmetry must be weakly integral. We develop a systematic analysis of QCA-refined realizations of fusion categories and prove two statements. First, we show that, under certain physical assumptions on defects, any QCA-refined realization has QCA and symmetry-operator indices determined by the categorical data, up to the freedom of redefining the symmetry operators. Second, we construct a lattice model that provides a QCA-refined realization for any weakly integral fusion category symmetry on a tensor product Hilbert space. We also compute indices of the QCAs in our lattice model and show agreement with the first result. As an application of the general construction, we give an explicit QCA-refined realization of general Tambara-Yamagami categorical symmetries.

Observation of Switchable Chiral Magnons in an Altermagnet

Authors: Zheyuan Liu, Hodaka Kikuchi, Zijun Wei, Shinichiro Asai, Mechthild Enderle, Ursula B. Hansen, Vasile O. Garlea, Manh D. Le, Gøran J. Nilsen, Igor A. Zaliznyak, Takatsugu Masuda

Summary. This paper provides the first direct experimental observation of switchable chiral magnons in the altermagnet MnTe. By using polarized neutron scattering, the authors show that the handedness of spin waves can be controlled by magnetic field cooling, providing a foundation for future altermagnetic spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The researchers aimed to experimentally verify the existence of chiral magnons in altermagnets, which are a predicted but previously unobserved hallmark of the altermagnetic state arising from non-relativistic exchange.

Main result. The study directly observed chiral magnons in the altermagnet MnTe and demonstrated that their chirality can be reversibly switched using a magnetic-field cooling process.

Method. The team used polarized inelastic neutron scattering (PINS) on the HYSPEC and IN20 spectrometers, combined with polarized neutron diffraction (PND) to control and measure domain imbalance.

Main result. The Worldvolume Hybrid Monte Carlo (WV-HMC) method successfully predicts physical observables with controlled statistical errors in the doped regime where standard methods fail.

Method. The Worldvolume Hybrid Monte Carlo (WV-HMC) method, which uses an anti-holomorphic flow to deform the integration surface into a continuum union of surfaces (the worldvolume) to mitigate the sign problem.

Model / system. A two-dimensional Hubbard model on a periodic, bipartite square lattice, characterized by hopping amplitude (t), on-site repulsion (U), and chemical potential (mu).

Key observables. Number density operator and energy density operator .

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice size 8x8, temperature T/t = 1/6.4, interaction strength U/t = 8.0, and the tuning parameter alpha for balancing sign suppression and ergodicity.

Assumptions / limitations. Uses a symmetric Trotter decomposition up to O(epsilon^2) and assumes the validity of the continuum limit as the Trotter step epsilon approaches zero.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares number density against ALF results; Figure 2 shows finite-epsilon effects on number density; Figure 3 presents number density and energy density in the continuum limit.

Paper structure. The paper defines the sign problem, introduces the WV-HMC method, details the Hubbard model Hamiltonian and path integral implementation, presents simulation results for density and energy, and discusses the continuum limit and ergodicity.

Abstract

We apply the Worldvolume Hybrid Monte Carlo (WV-HMC) method [arXiv:2012.08468] to the two-dimensional Hubbard model, which is known to suffer from a severe sign problem when the system is doped (away from half filling). We show that the method predicts physical observables with controlled statistical errors on an $8 \times 8$ lattice at temperature $T/t = 1/6.4 \approx 0.156$ and interaction strength $U/t = 8.0$ ($t$ is the hopping amplitude), for which the standard determinant quantum Monte Carlo fails.
