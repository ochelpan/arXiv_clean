Summary. The researchers successfully realized a Z3 discrete time crystal using a chain of 15 superconducting qutrits. By utilizing native chiral interactions, they were able to stabilize period-tripling oscillations and prevent the destabilizing effects of domain wall degeneracies. This work highlights the potential of qudit-based processors for exploring complex non-equilibrium phases.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates how engineering specific interaction symmetries (chirality) and utilizing higher-dimensional qudit (qutrit) hardware can access and stabilize exotic non-equilibrium phases of matter that are otherwise unstable in qubit-based systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of stabilizing higher discrete symmetry (Z3) discrete time crystals (DTCs) due to domain wall degeneracies that prevent the formation of stable, long-range order.

Main result. Demonstration of a robust Z3 DTC in a 15-qutrit superconducting chain, where native chiral interactions are shown to stabilize the period-tripling phase and prevent initial state dependence.

Method. Implementation of a Floquet chiral clock model using a 20-transmon processor with tunable couplers, utilizing microwave-driven gates, flux-pulsing for interactions, and many-body localization (MBL) to prevent heating.

Model / system. A 1D chain of 15 superconducting qutrits (spin-1) implemented on a 2D grid of 20 flux-tunable transmons and tunable couplers, following a Floquet chiral clock model with Z3 symmetry.

Key observables. Spin-1 magnetization, Fourier transform of magnetization (to detect period tripling), autocorrelators, and the Edwards-Anderson order parameter (chi_EA).

Important parameters / regimes. Kicking strength (g), chiral angle (theta), coupling strength (J), and system size (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The system relies on many-body localization to prevent Floquet heating and assumes that the experimental decay is primarily limited by intrinsic system decoherence rather than gate errors.

Figures summary. Figures show the processor layout, coherence statistics, single-qutrit gate calibration workflow, magnetization spectroscopy (FFT), and the scaling of the Edwards-Anderson order parameter across different chain lengths.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Z3 DTC stability, describes the superconducting qutrit hardware and chiral clock model implementation, details the calibration and control methods, presents the experimental observation of period tripling and the role of chirality, and concludes with phase transition analysis and scaling.

Abstract

Periodically driven quantum many-body systems can spontaneously break discrete time-translation symmetry, realizing discrete time crystals. To date, both experimental and theoretical efforts have largely focused on the simplest case of spontaneous period-doubling in $\mathbb{Z}_2$ discrete time crystals realized with qubits. This owes, in part, to the challenge of stabilizing eigenstate order in higher discrete symmetry ($\mathbb{Z}_n$) time crystals, due to the presence of richer domain wall physics. Here, we demonstrate the realization of a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ discrete time crystal by implementing a Floquet chiral clock model in a chain of 15 superconducting qutrits. Unlike the conventional Ising setting, our system features a tunable chiral angle that governs domain-wall dynamics, spectral degeneracies, and crucially, the stability of time-crystalline order. Using disordered nearest-neighbor chiral interactions, we observe robust subharmonic period tripling that persists across a wide range of drive strengths and is independent of initial state. Finally, we highlight the special role that chirality plays in our $\mathbb{Z}_3$ discrete time crystal -- in its absence, the system's Floquet dynamics exhibit a marked initial state dependence governed by domain wall degeneracies. Our results establish native qudit hardware as a powerful platform to access a broader landscape of non-equilibrium phases.

Summary. This paper shows that time crystals can spontaneously emerge in coupled exciton-polariton condensates using only the inherent gain and loss of the microcavity. It provides a theoretical threshold for this phase based on nonlinearity ratios and proves that the resulting oscillations are robust against quantum noise. This discovery offers a new way to realize time-crystalline behavior without the need for complex external driving.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics as it identifies a novel mechanism for spontaneous time-translation symmetry breaking driven by intrinsic dissipation rather than external periodic driving.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether a time crystal phase can emerge in coupled exciton-polariton condensates without periodic external driving and determining its robustness against quantum fluctuations.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that time crystals can emerge via time-independent gain and dissipation channels, provided the ratio of Kerr nonlinearity to nonlinear dissipation exceeds sqrt(5/4), and show that this phase is robust against leading-order quantum corrections.

Method. The study uses a Lindblad master equation approach, mean-field analysis via coupled Stuart-Landau equations, Jacobian matrix stability analysis, and Bogoliubov perturbation theory to evaluate quantum fluctuations.

Model / system. The system consists of coupled exciton-polariton condensates in semiconductor microcavities, modeled by a Lindblad master equation including local linear gain, dissipation, nonlinear dissipation, and correlated gain/dissipating channels via a shared reservoir.

Important parameters / regimes. Nonlinearity ratio (alpha = eta_R/eta_I), gain/pumping parameter (beta = p/J_R), coupling parameter (gamma = J_I/J_R), and the regime of gain-dominated systems (p > 0, J_I > 0).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a mean-field approximation for the underlying dynamics and relies on the validity of Bogoliubov perturbation theory where quantum corrections remain small.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of driving-free time crystals, describes the microcavity model and master equation, presents mean-field stability analysis and phase diagrams, evaluates quantum corrections using Bogoliubov theory, and concludes with a discussion on the robustness of the phase.

Abstract

In this paper, we show that time crystals can emerge in coupled exciton-polariton condensates without periodic external driving, enabled instead by incoherent gain and dissipation channels inherent to semiconductor microcavities. We present a full quantum description of these processes that recovers the established effective theory at the mean-field level. We analytically determine the mean-field phase diagram for the time-crystalline phase and find that its emergence requires the ratio of Kerr nonlinearity to nonlinear dissipation to exceed $\sqrt{5/4}$. Within this regime, the periodic oscillation of the particle numbers forms an attractor that is insensitive to the initial conditions. Numerical bifurcation diagrams reveal transitions between the time-crystalline phase and various steady phases, in excellent agreement with the analytical results. Using Bogoliubov perturbation theory, we evaluate the leading-order quantum corrections and find that, over a wide parameter range, these corrections remain periodic and much smaller than the mean-field background, thereby establishing the robustness of the time crystal.

Abstract

Ultrafast continuous-variable quantum states offer new opportunities for advanced quantum technologies, but efficient homodyne detection of these states remains challenging. Here, we present a method for efficient ultrafast homodyne detection by exploiting temporal correlations in detector signals. We analyze the autocorrelations of shot noise and electronic noise and determine the optimal weight by solving a generalized Rayleigh quotient problem. The optimal weight enhances the squeezing and anti-squeezing levels observed experimentally. These results highlight the importance of optimized signal processing for efficient quantum measurements.

Abstract

Cavity-magnon systems, combining magnons and photons, offer a versatile platform for studying quantum entanglement and advancing quantum information science. This work underscores the potential of spinning cavity-magnon systems as a versatile platform for realizing nonreciprocal quantum devices and facilitating the development of quantum technologies.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the universality of a dynamical spin-squeezing phase transition in power-law interacting bilayer spin models across different lattice geometries, dimensions, and coupling strengths.

Main result. The authors establish that the dynamical squeezing transition is universal across various 1D and 2D geometries and identify a new sub-linear scaling regime for the critical aspect ratio in the short-range interaction regime.

Method. The research combines Bogoliubov instability analysis using Holstein-Primakoff transformations with numerical simulations using the discrete Truncated Wigner Approximation (dTWA).

Important parameters / regimes. Power-law exponent (alpha), interlayer coupling ratio (lambda), and the aspect ratio (aZ/L).

Abstract

Recent work has identified a dynamical squeezing phase transition in power-law interacting bilayer XXZ spin models, separating a fully collective phase with Heisenberg-limited squeezing from a partially-collective phase with universal critical scaling. Here we test and establish the universality of this transition along two qualitatively different microscopic axes: lattice geometry, by studying square, triangular, and honeycomb $2\mathrm{D}$ bilayers as well as $1\mathrm{D}$ ladders, and a symmetry-preserving rescaling $λ$ of the interlayer couplings relative to the intralayer ones. Combining a Bogoliubov instability analysis with discrete truncated Wigner simulations, we find that the transition persists across all four lattice geometries and over a wide range of $λ$ with critical exponents consistent within error, providing strong evidence for a genuine non-equilibrium universality class. The Bogoliubov theory recovers the previously identified scaling $a_Z^* \propto L$ in the long-range interacting regime $α< d+2$, and yields an analytical scaling $a_Z^* \propto L^{2/(α-d)}$ for the critical aspect ratio with system size for $α>d+2$, with $α$ the power-law exponent in dimension $d$. This uncovers a previously unrecognized sub-linear regime for short-range interactions. By tuning $λ$ we vary the interlayer coupling strength at fixed layer spacing, demonstrating that the dynamical transition can be driven purely through interaction engineering without modifying the underlying geometry. These findings provide a versatile route toward controlling entanglement generation in Rydberg-array, polar molecule, and trapped-ion platforms with applications in quantum sensing and simulation.

Abstract

Computation of observables in discrete stochastic, possibly conditioned, dynamics over large sparse networks is at the basis of a myriad of applications. The Matrix-Product Belief Propagation method allows a semi-analytical estimation of such observables with a controlled error that depends on the size of the employed matrices, called bond size. For this model, we calculate time auto-correlations, time evolution of energy and magnetization, and finally we estimate the large deviation function of the magnetization at a given future time.

Abstract

Abstract
