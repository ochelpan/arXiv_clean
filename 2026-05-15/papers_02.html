Evolution of Gaussian mixed states under the Markovian master equation for a driven quantum oscillator

Authors: B. A. Tay
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14756v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides analytical solutions for the evolution of Gaussian states in a driven, dissipative quantum harmonic oscillator. It proves that the drift of the state is independent of bath temperature and fast-rotating modes, and characterizes the system's behavior at exceptional points and under various driving protocols.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics as it provides exact analytical solutions for driven-dissipative dynamics and clarifies the role of environmental noise parameters in the evolution of quantum trajectories.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the time evolution of Gaussian mixed states for a linearly driven quantum harmonic oscillator under a generic Markovian master equation, specifically focusing on how different Liouvillian components affect displacement dynamics.

Main result. The study demonstrates that the displacement dynamics (first moments) are independent of bath temperature and fast-rotating modes, and that the exceptional point structure of the driven system remains identical to the non-driven system.

Method. The author uses an analytical approach involving superoperator algebra, displacement superoperators, and biorthogonal eigenbasis solutions to solve the master equation and analyze phase-space trajectories.

Model / system. A single-mode quantum harmonic oscillator subject to a Markovian master equation of the GKSL type, including unitary evolution, dissipation, and linear driving (constant, impulsive, or harmonic).

Key observables. First moments (average position and momentum) and second moments (variances and squeezing parameters).

Important parameters / regimes. Natural frequency (omega_0), decay rate (gamma), bath temperature, driving strength, and the presence of exceptional points (critical damping).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be within the Markovian approximation and the states are restricted to the Gaussian regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1(a) shows phase space trajectories under constant driving across different damping regimes; Figure 1(b) illustrates the effect of impulsive driving (kicks) on the oscillator's position.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the master equation and the driven system, moves to deriving analytical solutions for Gaussian states, explores the relationship between driven and non-driven Liouvillians (including exceptional points), and concludes with the analysis of specific time-dependent driving cases (impulsive and harmonic).

Abstract

We study a generic quantum Markovian master equation for a linearly displaced or driven harmonic oscillator. It was known that the displacement dynamics of Gaussian mixed states depends on the unitary part of the Liouvillian, the decay rate of the system but not on the bath temperature. Here we further show that the fast-rotating modes do not affect the system's displacement dynamics under linear driving forces. Analytical solutions of the quantum master equation are obtained for displaced Gaussian mixed states. Because the non-driven and driven Liouvillians are related by a unitary displacement operator, they are expected to share the same exceptional points structure. At the exceptional points, the displacement of critically damped oscillator displays a characteristics polynomial-in-time prefactor multiplied by an exponential decay. We discuss how external time-dependent forces affect the displacement dynamics using impulsive force and harmonic force as examples. The results obtained for constant driving remain valid in the presence of time-dependent driving.

Generalized Model Fractional Quantum Hall States on Lattices

Authors: Guangyue Ji, Jie Wang
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13978v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 analog quantum simulation 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 3/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a systematic method to generalize continuum fractional quantum Hall wave functions to lattice systems by replacing contact clustering with discrete lattice-displaced zeros. The resulting model states for the Read-Rezayi series are exact ground states of specific density interactions and preserve essential topological features. This provides a blueprint for engineering topological phases in cold-atom and synthetic flat-band systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a constructive route for engineering topological orders in synthetic flat-band platforms like cold atoms, offering a way to realize complex non-Abelian states through controllable density interactions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of constructing exact lattice model wave functions for fractional quantum Hall (FQH) states (like Laughlin, Moore-Read, and Read-Rezayi) beyond simple bosonic systems, specifically overcoming the limitation that particles on a lattice cannot be infinitesimally close to satisfy continuum clustering conditions.

Main result. The authors systematically construct lattice model states for the entire Read-Rezayi series by using a delta-deformation that replaces infinitesimal contact zeros with discrete, distributed lattice zeros. These states are proven to be exact gapped ground states of specific density-density interactions and retain the idealized entanglement features of their continuum counterparts.

Method. The work combines analytical construction using elliptic functions (e.g., modified Weierstrass sigma functions) with numerical exact diagonalization on square and rectangular tori.

Model / system. The study focuses on lattice-based synthetic platforms, such as the Kapit-Mueller model (a Hofstadter model with flat bands) and density-density interaction Hamiltonians where the interaction pattern is matched to the zeros of the wave function.

Key observables. Many-body energy spectrum, pair and three-particle correlation functions (g2, g3), particle-cut entanglement spectrum (PES), projected structure factor, and spectral flow under twisted boundary conditions.

Important parameters / regimes. Filling factor (nu = 1/3, 1/4, 1/2), interaction displacement (delta), magnetic flux density (phi), and interaction range.

Assumptions / limitations. The construction is specific to 'lattice ideal bands' like the Kapit-Mueller model; the broader implications for non-ideal bands are not fully addressed.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows diagnostics for the nu=1/3 Laughlin state including the many-body spectrum, correlation function zeros, and entanglement gap; Figure 2 demonstrates phase transitions induced by increasing interaction range; Figure 3 shows diagnostics for non-Abelian states; Supplemental figures show spectral flow, bosonic states, and the nu=1/4 Moore-Read state.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of lattice FQH construction, presents the delta-deformation theory for Laughlin states, extends the construction to Moore-Read and Read-Rezayi series, analyzes phase transitions via interaction range, and provides detailed numerical verification in the supplemental material.

Abstract

Model wave functions are essential for studying fractional quantum Hall phases, yet lattice model states have so far been limited to bosonic systems with on-site interactions. In this work, by combining analytical and numerical methods, we systematically construct lattice model states for the Laughlin, Moore--Read, and general $\mathbb{Z}_k$ Read--Rezayi series. Our lattice-specific states are characterized by their idealized energy and entanglement features and are distinguished from their continuum counterparts by a modified clustering behavior. Our theory advances the understanding of the stability of topologically ordered phases and illustrates the organizing principles of the conformal Hilbert space on lattices. Practically, this work paves the way for further studying lattice-specific excitations and offers a constructive route for engineering topological orders within density interactions, with potential immediate implications for cold-atom and synthetic flat-band platforms.

Open Quantum Theory of Shot Noise in Dissipative Chiral Transport

Authors: Ming Gong, Masahito Ueda
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14263v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops an open quantum theory to explain how dissipation suppresses shot noise in chiral transport by stacking electrons into lower energy states. It introduces a novel mathematical method to reconstruct the internal particle occupancy distribution of quantum channels using only measurable current noise cumulants.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful analytical tool (the inversion scheme) for extracting hidden many-body statistics from measurable noise, which is highly relevant for researchers studying non-equilibrium dynamics and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic mechanism of shot-noise suppression in dissipative chiral transport and developing a method to reconstruct hidden internal occupancy distributions from measurable noise cumulants.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that current noise is governed by the competition between occupancy distribution and particle-number fluctuations, and they provide a successful inversion scheme to reconstruct the occupancy distribution from higher-order noise cumulants.

Method. The study uses an open quantum theory framework involving a discrete-time master equation, quantum trajectory simulations via Matrix Product States (MPS), and a mathematical inversion scheme based on filter cumulants and combinatorial expansions.

Model / system. An N-channel chiral transport system (such as quantum Hall edge states) modeled as a multi-layer fermionic quantum circuit in energy space, where electrons undergo elastic scattering via Haar-random unitary gates and energy exchange with a bath via dissipative jump operators.

Key observables. Average occupancy distribution, shot-noise spectral density (S11, S12), Fano factor, transmission coefficient, and higher-order noise cumulants.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of channels (N), number of energy levels (M), dissipation rate (gamma_0), source temperature (T_in), and bath temperature (T_bath).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes perfect intra-level channel mixing, inter-level statistical independence, and that dissipative jump operators act primarily on the first channel due to symmetric mixing.

Figures summary. Figure 2 compares exact and effective CGFs and shows noise suppression; Figure 3 demonstrates the sign reversal of inter-channel noise (S12) in different heating regimes; Figure 4 shows the successful reconstruction of the occupancy distribution using the proposed inversion scheme.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of shot-noise in dissipative transport, presents a quantum circuit mapping and master equation model, validates an effective CGF framework, demonstrates a sign reversal in correlation noise, and concludes with a mathematical derivation of an inversion scheme for occupancy reconstruction.

Abstract

We develop an open quantum theory for shot-noise dynamics in dissipative chiral transport. By mapping a system under consideration onto a quantum circuit, we show that current noise is governed by two competing factors: the average occupancy distribution and particle-number fluctuations. With energy fully relaxed, shot noise is strongly suppressed, reflecting the stacking of electrons into lower energy states due to dissipation. This process quenches the partition noise from partially occupied levels, and finally isolates the residual noise protected by strong $U(1)$ symmetry. Moreover, selectively heating the source against the bath uncovers the underlying competition between the noise contributions from the occupancy distribution and those from the particle-number fluctuations. It triggers a sign reversal in inter-channel correlation noise, a signature masked by seemingly identical single-channel thermal noises. We propose an inversion scheme to experimentally reconstruct the hidden occupancy distribution directly from measurable noise cumulants.

Revealing Hidden Correlations in a Fermi-Hubbard system via Interaction Ramps

Authors: Botond Oreg, Carter Turnbaugh, Jens Hertkorn, Ningyuan Jia, Martin Zwierlein
Type: experiment · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14909v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 analog quantum simulation 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers developed a technique using a rapid interaction ramp to make nonlocal pairs visible as local doublons in a 2D Fermi-Hubbard system. This method successfully reveals hidden charge-density-wave correlations and distinguishes between the Fermi liquid and pseudogap regimes. It offers a new way to study complex phases like stripe order or FFLO states in ultracold atoms.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a powerful new experimental tool for observing exotic pair orders and phase transitions in many-body systems by using non-equilibrium dynamics to reveal static correlations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Detecting hidden spatial correlations, specifically charge-density-wave order, in the strongly interacting regime of the attractive Hubbard model where nonlocal pairs are difficult to image.

Main result. An interaction ramp technique significantly enhances the visibility of charge-density-wave correlations by projecting nonlocal pairs into detectable local doublons. This allows for the clear distinction between the Fermi liquid and the pseudogap phase.

Method. A rapid linear magnetic field ramp (1 ms) is used to increase the interaction strength, contracting extended pairs into local doublons before single-site, spin-resolved fluorescence imaging.

Model / system. A 2D spin-balanced Fermi gas of 40K atoms in a square optical lattice, realizing the attractive Hubbard model with controlled tunneling (t) and interaction (U) via Feshbach resonance.

Key observables. Doublon density, density-density correlators, density structure factor, charge-density-wave strength, singlon fraction, and spin/magnetization correlations.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength U/t (ranging from weak/Fermi liquid to strong/local pairs), tunneling t = 340 Hz, temperature T/t ~ 0.5, and lattice depth.

Assumptions / limitations. The ramp is fast enough to prevent many-body evolution but slow enough to allow pair contraction; the density distribution remains frozen during the ramp.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the ramp schematic and enhanced CDW patterns; Fig 2 displays density-density correlation maps; Fig 4 compares magnetization fluctuations and singlon density across different U/t regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of hidden correlations, describes the experimental setup and the interaction ramp technique, presents results showing enhanced CDW visibility and the identification of the pseudogap phase, and concludes with potential applications to spin-imbalanced and repulsive Hubbard models.

Abstract

We observe an enhanced visibility of charge-density-wave correlations in a cold-atom realization of the attractive Hubbard model following a rapid boost of the interaction strength. The interaction boost associates nonlocal pairs into doublons which mark the center of mass of the original pairs. The enhancement is largest in the strongly correlated regime where pairing is nonlocal. We distinguish the unpaired Fermi liquid from the pseudogap phase of preformed pairs by analyzing atom-resolved spin-charge correlations after the ramp. The technique we establish here may facilitate the observation of exotic forms of pair order in spin-imbalanced systems, and of stripe order in the dual case of the doped repulsive Hubbard model.

Toward Covert Quantum Computing

Authors: Evan J. D. Anderson, Kaushik Datta, Boulat A. Bash
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14325v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces 'covert quantum computing,' a method to hide computations from adversaries on shared quantum hardware. While theory predicts that a small buffer of qubits can hide activity, experimental results on IBM and IQM processors show that long-range crosstalk can expose the computation, necessitating better spatial isolation.

Why it may be interesting. It addresses the physical-layer security of quantum hardware, specifically how many-body interactions (crosstalk) can be used as a side channel for information leakage, which is highly relevant to the study of open quantum systems and noise in large-scale quantum architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to perform quantum computations on a multi-tenant cloud QPU such that an adversary cannot detect the ongoing computation via crosstalk-induced noise in spectator qubits.

Main result. The authors derive a 'Square-Root Law' stating that a computation can be made covert using only O(sqrt(n)) additional idling qubits, but experimental tests on IBM and IQM hardware reveal long-range crosstalk that creates a side channel for detection.

Method. The paper uses the quantum-strategy framework (quantum combs) and discrete isoperimetric inequalities for theoretical bounds, alongside Ramsey interferometry to experimentally measure frequency shifts in spectator qubits.

Model / system. Superconducting quantum processors with planar topologies, specifically IQM's 54-qubit Emerald (square lattice) and IBM's 156-qubit ibm_fez (heavy-hex lattice), utilizing tunable-coupler architectures.

Key observables. Frequency shifts (delta f) in spectator qubits, ZZ-crosstalk, and the probability of detection (false alarm and missed detection rates).

Important parameters / regimes. n-qubit circuit size, number of shots (k), diamond-norm distance, and the scaling of the boundary size relative to the computational area.

Assumptions / limitations. The initial theoretical model assumes nearest-neighbor crosstalk; the adversary has access to quantum memory and can perform adaptive operations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows optimal geometric layouts (diamonds/disks) for minimizing boundaries; Figure 2 shows the Ramsey circuit protocol; Figure 3 shows experimental Ramsey oscillation data; Figure 4 shows the physical qubit layouts on Emerald and ibm_fez; Figure 5 presents bar charts of detected frequency shifts.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of covert quantum computing, develops a theoretical framework using information theory and graph theory to derive scaling laws, presents experimental validation using Ramsey interferometry on two different hardware platforms, and discusses the implications of discovered long-range crosstalk.

Abstract

As quantum computers become available through multi-tenant cloud platforms, ensuring privacy against adversaries sharing the same quantum processing unit becomes critical. We introduce and explore \emph{covert quantum computing}, a new concept that ensures an adversary with access to all other quantum computational units (QCUs) of a quantum computer cannot detect computation on the subset that they cannot access. Analogous to covert communication, we employ information theory. However, since here the adversary controls the systems used for detection, we require a richer framework for covertness analysis that accounts for the use of quantum memories and adaptive operations. Thus, we adopt the \emph{quantum-strategy} framework used in quantum game theory and memory channel discrimination. Current quantum computers use planar graph circuit layouts and typically assume nearest-neighbor crosstalk. We derive discrete isoperimetric inequalities to show that, for an $n$-qubit circuit under this model, only $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{n})$ border qubits provide detection information to the adversary. We then explore this scaling law on IQM's 54-qubit \emph{Emerald} processor and IBM's 156-qubit \emph{ibm_fez} machine employing the Heron 2 architecture. We implement Ramsey experiments on qubits not used in computation, and detect nearest-neighbor crosstalk, as expected. However, we also observe long-range coupling effects beyond the border qubits, revealing a side channel that the adversary can exploit. We hypothesize that this long-range crosstalk is induced by leakage from the drive and control lines. Beyond weakening covertness, it exposes co-tenants to both adversarial and unintended crosstalk and degrades circuits that span spatially distributed qubits, motivating further work on spatial isolation and crosstalk characterization.

Translation symmetry-enforced long-range entanglement in mixed states

Authors: Ryan Thorngren, Lei Gioia, Carolyn Zhang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15200v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper demonstrates that translation symmetry can force a mixed state to possess long-range entanglement that cannot be decomposed into short-range entangled components. This effect is particularly interesting because it remains hidden from standard local correlation function measurements. The study provides a rigorous lower bound on the circuit depth required to simulate such states.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it establishes a fundamental link between symmetry, entanglement structure, and the complexity (circuit depth) required for thermalization and state preparation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether translation symmetry can enforce long-range entanglement (LRE) in mixed states, specifically determining if the maximally mixed state of translation-invariant states is LRE.

Main result. The authors prove that the maximally mixed state of translation-invariant states is LRE because the dimension of the zero momentum sector exceeds the dimension of the space spanned by short-range entangled (SRE) states. This LRE is subtle because it cannot be detected by long-range connected correlation functions.

Method. A dimension-based counting argument is used to compare the size of the zero momentum sector to the space of SRE states, supported by a 'Tails Lemma' and circuit decomposition into Matrix Product Operators.

Model / system. 1D quantum chains of length n with periodic boundary conditions and local Hilbert space dimension q, focusing on the zero momentum sector of translation-invariant states.

Key observables. Trace distance (to measure approximation fidelity), connected correlation functions, and circuit depth/evolution time.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (n), local dimension (q), circuit depth (d), and evolution time (tau).

Assumptions / limitations. The primary results are derived for 1D systems; the proof assumes translation symmetry of the resulting state but not necessarily the generating circuit.

Figures summary. A circuit diagram illustrating a depth-4 circuit with a highlighted light-cone (sub-circuit C0) and internal bonds representing Schmidt decompositions.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of LRE in mixed states, introduces a counting argument and dimension bounds, develops a mathematical framework using circuit decomposition and MPS counting, provides the main theorem on LRE, and discusses the implications for correlation functions and higher dimensions.

Abstract

We show by a counting argument that even though translation symmetry admits symmetric short-range entangled (SRE) eigenstates, there are not enough such SRE eigenstates to span the zero momentum sector. This means that the fixed point strong-to-weak spontaneous symmetry breaking state of translation symmetry is long-range entangled: it cannot be written as a mixture of SRE states. This is a subtle form of long-range entanglement in mixed states that cannot be detected by long-range connected correlation functions.

Majorana Constellations: A Geometric Lens on Multipartite Entanglement and Geometric Phases

Authors: Chon-Fai Kam
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15008v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · Dicke superradiance 2/5 · Tavis-Cummings & cavity-many-emitter 2/5

Summary. This review synthesizes the Majorana stellar representation as a geometric framework for studying multipartite entanglement and geometric phases. It demonstrates how mapping quantum states to constellations of points on a sphere simplifies the classification of entanglement and makes the evaluation of many-body invariants computationally tractable. The work also establishes a profound mathematical link between quantum entanglement patterns and the classification of gravitational fields in general relativity.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful geometric tool for analyzing many-body entanglement and phase-space properties, offering a way to bypass computational complexity in symmetric quantum systems and linking quantum correlations to spacetime geometry.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of characterizing multipartite entanglement and geometric phases in large-scale quantum systems using traditional algebraic methods, which often face #P-hard computational bottlenecks.

Main result. The paper establishes the Majorana stellar representation as a unified geometric language that simplifies entanglement classification (SLOCC) and reduces the complexity of evaluating many-body invariants from #P-hard to polynomial time.

Method. The authors use a mapping of spin-S states to a constellation of points (stars) on a Bloch sphere via the roots of a characteristic polynomial, leveraging stereographic projection and spinor calculus.

Model / system. The study focuses on symmetric multi-qubit systems and higher-spin (S) systems, including bosonic ensembles, Dicke states, and continuous-variable (CV) systems in the Bargmann-Fock space.

Key observables. Concurrence, three-tangle, genuine multipartite entanglement (GME), Berry and Hannay phases, and Husimi Q-function zeros.

Important parameters / regimes. The spin magnitude (S), the number of qubits (N=2S), and the thermodynamic limit (j approaching infinity).

Assumptions / limitations. The primary analytical framework assumes permutation symmetry (the symmetric subspace) and focuses on pure quantum states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a roadmap of the article, outlining the progression from mathematical foundations to applications in entanglement, dynamics, and experimental implementations.

Paper structure. The paper moves from mathematical foundations (polynomials and isomorphisms) to geometric quantities and entanglement metrics, then explores unitary dynamics (geometric phases), and concludes with applications in metrology, error correction, and condensed matter.

Abstract

The Majorana stellar representation translates abstract quantum spin states into intuitive geometric constellations on the Bloch sphere, revealing symmetries, degeneracies, and correlations that traditional algebraic methods often obscure. Within quantum information science, this framework provides a powerful lens for characterizing symmetric multi-qubit and higher-spin systems. By encoding entanglement directly into spatial coordinates, the constellation geometry yields exact measures of concurrence, three-tangle, and genuine multipartite entanglement, while its dynamical evolution uncovers internal anomalous contributions to geometric phases. While interest in stellar representations has resurged, existing literature remains fragmented, lacking a unified treatment of these entanglement-specific metrics and their higher-dimensional dynamics. This review synthesizes the entanglement-centric perspective on Majorana representations, bridging discrete algebraic classifications (e.g., SLOCC orbits) with continuous geometric interpretations. Crucially, we highlight how this framework circumvents #P-hard computational bottlenecks, leveraging polynomial-time tractability to evaluate multipartite invariants. We detail the interplay between constellation topology and higher-spin Berry/Hannay phases, explore extensions beyond pure symmetric states, and review applications in quantum metrology, state engineering, and condensed-matter physics. By foregrounding entanglement as the unifying theme, this comprehensive examination establishes Majorana stars as a fundamental geometric language, uniquely positioned to inspire new theoretical and experimental directions in quantum technologies.

Transient dynamics of parametric driving for single-electron image current detection in a Paul trap

Authors: Baiyi Yu, Andris Huang, Isabel Sacksteder, Hartmut Haeffner
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15087v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · QC/QI experiment 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes a new method for detecting single electrons in Paul traps by using a ramped parametric drive to lock the electron's frequency during the transient regime. This approach overcomes the limitations of traditional steady-state detection, such as frequency fluctuations and signal broadening. The method enables faster and more robust nondestructive readout, essential for electron-based quantum information processing.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel way to utilize transient dynamics rather than steady-state assumptions to achieve robust frequency-locking, which is highly relevant for high-fidelity readout in trapped-particle quantum technologies.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The nondestructive detection of single-electron motion in Paul traps is hindered by motional frequency fluctuations caused by trap anharmonicities and RF field instabilities.

Main result. A controlled ramp of the parametric drive strength effectively locks the electron's motional frequency in the transient regime, providing a robust and fast readout method resilient to noise and micromotion.

Method. The study uses numerical simulations of 3D electron dynamics, comparing instantaneous versus ramped parametric driving protocols using high-order stochastic differential equation solvers.

Model / system. A single electron trapped in a three-layer PCB Paul trap, coupled to an RLC resonator for image current detection, modeled with nonlinear oscillator dynamics including 6th-order anharmonic terms.

Key observables. Image current (induced voltage), electron motion amplitude and phase, power spectrum of the resonator voltage, and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).

Important parameters / regimes. Secular frequency of 2pi x 200 MHz, driving strength epsilon = 0.1, temperature T = 4 K, and a parametric drive frequency of approximately twice the secular frequency.

Assumptions / limitations. The model uses a pseudopotential approximation and neglects motional cross-coupling between different axes.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the trap geometry and phase-space trajectories of the separatrix; Fig 2 compares instantaneous vs. ramped driving in time and frequency domains; Fig 3 demonstrates robustness to frequency detuning; Fig 5 shows the probability density distribution of voltage power density.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the detection problem, describes the PCB trap platform and RLC circuit, presents the nonlinear oscillator model, compares driving protocols (instantaneous vs. ramped), analyzes noise impact and statistical distributions, and concludes with implications for experimental realization.

Abstract

Nondestructive detection of single-electron motion is crucial for quantum information processing with electrons trapped in Paul traps. The standard approach in Penning traps is to detect the image current induced on the trap electrodes by the electron's oscillatory motion. However, applying this approach in Paul traps for single electrons is currently hindered by motional frequency fluctuations arising from trap anharmonicities and instabilities in the rf trapping field. In this work, we propose a robust detection scheme exploiting the transient dynamics of parametric driving to overcome these limitations. Distinct from traditional steady-state approaches, our method focuses on the transient regime to break the temporal constraints imposed by steady-state assumptions, thereby enabling fast readout. We show that a controlled ramp of the parametric drive effectively locks the frequency of the electron motion in the transient regime, rendering the signal highly resilient to realistic experimental noise and inherent micromotion. This work paves the way for the experimental realization of nondestructive detection of single-electron motion in Paul traps.

Quantum-enabled complete RF-polarimetry with an optically-wired atomic sensor

Authors: Matthew Chilcott, Laurits N. Stokholm, Matthew Cloutman, J. Susanne Otto, Amita B. Deb, Niels Kjærgaard
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14529v1

Summary. This paper presents a method for complete RF-polarimetry using Rydberg atoms as an optically-wired sensor. By analyzing the spectroscopic fingerprints of RF-dressed atoms, the researchers can determine the full polarization state of an RF field without needing calibration or metallic antennas. This approach leverages the fundamental quantization of angular momentum to create a universal sensing framework.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for quantum optics and AMO physics as it demonstrates a novel way to use the internal quantum structure (angular momentum quantization) of atoms to perform high-precision, non-invasive sensing of external classical fields.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a complete, calibration-free RF-polarimetry method to determine the full Stokes vector of an RF field using Rydberg atoms without the field distortions caused by metallic antennas.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated that the state of polarization (SOP) of an RF field is encoded in the spectroscopic fingerprints of Rydberg atoms, allowing for universal, calibration-free measurement of the full Stokes vector.

Method. The method uses Electromagnetically Induced Transparency (EIT) in a Rubidium vapor cell to probe RF-dressed Rydberg states, employing an analytical inversion of the EIT spectra based on eigenvalue envelope ratios.

Model / system. An optically-wired atomic sensor using 87Rb atoms in a vapor cell, specifically utilizing Rydberg states (n approx 50) and a ladder-type EIT scheme with 780 nm and 480 nm lasers.

Key observables. Atomic eigenenergy spectrum, EIT peak positions, and the eigenvalue envelope ratio R.

Important parameters / regimes. RF phase angle (phi), RF polarization (linear, circular, elliptical), and principal quantum number (n).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the dipole approximation and the rotating wave approximation (RWA).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the mapping of phase angles to EIT spectra, the identification of mathematical ambiguities in the phase angle extraction, and the use of peak intensity/symmetry breaking to resolve those ambiguities.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of RF-polarimetry, describes the Rydberg-based experimental platform, presents the analytical framework for mapping SOP to the Poincaré sphere, details the inversion method and ambiguity resolution, and concludes with experimental demonstrations in Rubidium.

Abstract

Rydberg atomic electrometry leverages the extreme sensitivity of highly excited atoms for calibration-free electric field measurements. The technique uses a non-metallic vapor cell to link properties of an RF field to a spectroscopic readout in the optical domain. Most demonstrations have so far focused on detecting linearly-polarized fields, for which the induced splitting of dressed atomic levels is rotationally invariant. Here we report on Rydberg atomic measurements of RF fields in a general state of polarization (SOP) which we map onto the Poincaré sphere through spectroscopic fingerprints. For a Stokes vector circumnavigating a Poincaré sphere meridian, we witness a continuous transformation of the atomic eigenenergy spectrum. Because the relative positions of eigenenergies are locked in place by quantization of angular momentum, the framework is universal and calibration free. We provide a specific demonstration in rubidium, which generalizes to all systems with a single valence electron.

Summary. This paper demonstrates the first implementation of a blind quantum computation protocol on a modular superconducting processor. By using a server-client architecture, the researchers showed that a client can run algorithms like Deutsch-Jozsa without revealing the task or results to the server. The study quantifies extremely low information leakage, proving the feasibility of secure, privacy-preserving cloud quantum computing.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a practical implementation of a quantum communication/computing protocol (BQC) using a modular hardware architecture, which is highly relevant to the development of quantum networks and distributed quantum computing.

Abstract

Recently, the technique of counterdiabatic driving, which provides an effective strategy for accelerating adiabatic quantum evolution, has been widely applied in the preparation of many-body quantum states. In this work, we propose a theoretical scheme for the efficient preparation of Dicke states in a system of non-interacting two-level atoms. Our approach leverages the one-axis twisting (OAT) interaction to generate non-classical correlations and combines it with time-dependent external fields to achieve precise control over the dynamics of the system. By employing rapid adiabatic passage (RAP), it demonstrates how the system can be steered from an initial coherent spin state to a target Dicke state with high fidelity [S. C. Carrasco, M. H. Goerz, S. A. Malinovskaya, V. Vuletić, W. P. Schleich, and V. S. Malinovsky, Phys. Rev. Lett. \textbf{132}, 153603 (2024)]. To further optimize the preparation process, we introduce counterdiabatic driving (CD), which suppresses non-adiabatic transitions. Numerical simulations confirm that our scheme can achieve high-fidelity Dicke states for a moderate number of particles. Our results provide a scalable and experimentally feasible approach to prepare Dicke states, with potential applications in quantum metrology, quantum communication, and quantum information processing.

Summary. The paper identifies a failure mode in adaptive QFI estimation where algorithms stop too early due to unresolved truncation bias. By introducing a 'guarded' stopping rule that enforces minimum resource thresholds, the authors significantly improve the reliability of error certification, though they note challenges in scaling this approach to larger systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum metrology and open quantum systems, as it provides a critical warning that high-precision sampling does not guarantee the control of systematic truncation errors in randomized measurement-based estimation protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the 'false-stop' pathology in adaptive Krylov-shadow Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) estimation, where a stopping rule prematurely declares convergence because it mistakes low sampling variance for true accuracy while ignoring significant Krylov truncation bias.

Main result. The authors propose a 'guarded' stopping rule using eligibility thresholds and a persistence condition that suppresses false success declarations and significantly reduces median absolute error. Method. The authors develop an adaptive interface (AKS-QFI) that monitors both inter-order change (dK) and bootstrap interval width (wM). They implement a guarded rule with minimum thresholds for Krylov order (K) and sampling budget (M), alongside a persistence requirement (P).

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) estimate, Krylov-stability measure (dK), empirical uncertainty interval width (wM), False-Stop Rate (FSR), and truncation bias (BK).

Important parameters / regimes. Krylov order (K), sampling budget (M), target error tolerance (epsilon), dephasing probability (p_phi), and the persistence parameter (P).

Assumptions / limitations. The estimator assumes Hadamard differentiability of the density operator family and exponential decay of the Krylov truncation bias. It also assumes sub-Gaussian concentration for the sampling-side error.

Abstract

Adaptive quantum Fisher information (QFI) estimation requires a stopping rule that distinguishes accuracy from apparent numerical stability. For Krylov-shadow QFI estimators, finite Krylov order $K$ produces truncation bias, while finite sample budget $M$ produces finite-$M$ sampling-side error. We show that a width-only empirical stopping rule, based on interval width and local Krylov stability, can declare convergence at small $(K,M)$ even when the post hoc error exceeds the requested tolerance; we call this event a \emph{false stop}. The mechanism is a narrow empirical interval centered on a biased low-$K$ estimate. We give a two-component stopping analysis that separates the Krylov and sampling terms, and we implement a guarded rule that permits a success declaration only after minimum thresholds in $K$ and $M$ and a persistence condition are satisfied. On a five-level dephasing benchmark at $n=4$ qubits, the guarded rule suppresses the false success declarations produced by the width-only empirical rule, whose false-stop rates range from $0.16$ to $0.68$ across the tested noise levels. Under the main fixed resource limit, the guarded rule refuses to make success declarations rather than accepting biased low-$K$ estimates; a separate true-relative-tolerance sampling-budget sequence then shows that, after Krylov and sampling recalibration, the same decision principle can make success declarations without observed false stops. These results show that stopping reliability is a distinct design requirement for adaptive QFI estimation: sampling precision at fixed $K$ does not by itself establish that Krylov truncation bias is controlled.
