Summary. The paper introduces the Noncommutative Skin Effect (NCSE), showing that spatial noncommutativity alone can induce boundary localization typically associated with gain/loss terms. By analyzing lattice models and deriving effective 1D Hamiltonians, it establishes rigorous criteria—particularly based on potential symmetry—for the existence of this effect.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a fundamental mechanism linking geometric properties of spacetime (noncommutativity) directly to observable quantum phenomena like topological edge states, which is highly relevant for designing synthetic dimensions in AMO platforms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To demonstrate that spatial noncommutativity can induce the Non-Hermitian Skin Effect (NHSE) without requiring an independent gain/loss term along the skin direction.

Main result. The phenomenon, termed the noncommutative skin effect (NCSE), is characterized by a skin length inversely proportional to the noncommutativity parameter ($\kappa_ u \propto heta$).

Method. The analysis uses mathematical transformations (like the Bopp shift) and asymptotic expansions (Taylor/adiabatic approximations) on effective 1D Hamiltonians derived from the 2D noncommutative lattice model.

Model / system. The system is a quantum mechanical model on a two-dimensional space where spatial coordinates do not commute, $[\hat{x}, \hat{y}] = i heta$. The Hamiltonian involves kinetic terms and an imaginary potential $V(\hat{y})$.

Key observables. Inverse skin length ($\kappa_ u$), spectral non-reciprocity ($\epsilon_ u(k) eq \epsilon_ u(-k)$), and the collective reversal of eigenstate direction at geometric 'flip points'.

Important parameters / regimes. Noncommutativity parameter ($ heta$), lattice size ($L_x, L_y$), and potential strength/geometry.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on approximations such as the adiabatic limit and Taylor truncation to derive solvable effective 1D equations from the full 2D problem.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the 'Sliding-window mechanism' showing momentum shearing into coordinate space, and numerical verification of exponential envelope localization ($\propto e^{ heta m}$) for various potentials.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the general noncommutative framework, deriving effective 1D models (Hatano-Nelson type), proving existence criteria based on potential symmetry, and finally applying these results to specific lattice potentials (quadratic, sine/cosine).

Abstract

In all known schemes for the non-Hermitian skin effect, the non-Hermitian ingredient that drives the skin localization, whether asymmetric hopping or gain and loss, is invariably introduced by hand as an independent model parameter along the skin direction. Here we show that when two spatial coordinates do not commute, the skin effect can break free of this paradigm: a gain-loss potential applied along one coordinate automatically generates non-reciprocity along the other through the coordinate noncommutativity, driving all eigenstates to pile up exponentially at a boundary. We term this phenomenon the noncommutative skin effect. The inverse skin length is proportional to the noncommutativity parameter and is given by an analytic formula, exact in the thermodynamic limit and verified by exact diagonalization of lattice models; the reflection symmetry of the imaginary potential furnishes an exact criterion for the presence or absence of the effect, valid rigorously for finite-size systems. For a sinusoidal imaginary potential, the skin direction of all eigenstates flips collectively at parameter points fixed purely by geometry. Because the flip point is independent of the potential strength, the reversal constitutes a zero-crossing measurement scheme intrinsically robust against systematic errors, from which the noncommutativity parameter can be extracted directly. The qualitative transition of the eigenstates from uniform to exponentially localized renders the effect a nonperturbative probe of spatial noncommutativity, and the Peierls-phase structure of its lattice model is in principle accessible to cold-atom synthetic dimensions, photonic resonators, and topolectrical circuits.

Summary. The paper develops a semi-device-independent method to certify Nonlocality without Entanglement (NLWE) by comparing the maximum confidence achievable through global versus separable measurements. Using SDPs, it demonstrates that a measurable gap exists for antiparallel states, which can be verified even with noisy detectors or by analyzing inconclusive outcomes. This provides a robust, experimentally feasible test for quantum nonlocality.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a highly rigorous, experimentally relevant protocol for certifying fundamental quantum resources like nonlocality using only measurement statistics, which is crucial for practical quantum information applications.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The work investigates Nonlocality without Entanglement (NLWE) by developing a semi-device-independent (sDI) certification method based on maximum-confidence discrimination (MCM). It aims to establish NLWE using only measurement outcomes, making it robust against detector inefficiencies.

Main result. A measurable gap exists between the confidence achievable with global measurements and that achievable with separable measurements for antiparallel states. This gap can be certified using outcome rates or by analyzing inconclusive outcome rates, even in the presence of noise.

Method. The analysis relies heavily on formulating optimization problems as Semidefinite Programs (SDPs) to determine the maximum confidence ($\hat{C}$) under global versus separable measurement constraints. The certification is achieved by comparing these bounds.

Model / system. The study analyzes quantum state ensembles, specifically focusing on antiparallel and parallel qubit states subjected to local depolarizing noise. The measurements are characterized by Global (GLOBAL) or Separable (SEP) Positive Operator-Valued Measures (POVMs).

Key observables. Maximum Confidence ($C_{x, ext{max}}$), Certifiable Confidence ($\hat{C}$), Outcome Rates ($\eta_x$), and the rate of inconclusive outcomes ($\eta_0$).

Important parameters / regimes. Noise parameter $p$, outcome rates $\eta_x$, and optimization parameters like Lagrange multipliers ($\lambda$).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes that maximum-confidence measurements only rely on detected measurement outcomes, making it robust to non-unit detection efficiencies. Separability is tested by comparing global vs. product operator constraints.

Figures summary. Figures compare the certifiable MCM for antiparallel states versus outcome rates, showing distinct regimes (I, II, III) where NLWE can be certified. Another figure compares inconclusive outcome rates ($\eta(G)_0$ vs $\eta(S)_0$) to demonstrate the gap.

Paper structure. The paper systematically develops the MCM framework using SDPs, first establishing the theoretical gap for ideal states (Chunk 1). It then generalizes this to a certifiable, device-independent setting by incorporating outcome rates and noise parameters (Chunks 2, 3, 4), finally exploring certification via inconclusive outcomes (Chunk 5).

Abstract

In this work, we investigate maximum-confidence discrimination, which encompasses minimum-error and unambiguous discrimination, for ensembles of separable states by considering global and separable measurements. We demonstrate that global measurements outperform separable ones, thereby establishing nonlocality without entanglement (NLWE) in terms of confidence in a detection event, a fine-grained state-identification strategy that maximizes the probability of a correct guess given a measurement outcome. Conversely, verifying achievable confidence in measurement outcomes can certify global measurements, namely, semi-device-independent certification of NLWE. Our results make it feasible to experimentally demonstrate NLWE using present-day quantum measurement devices, even with non-unit detection efficiencies, since maximum-confidence measurements rely only on detected measurement outcomes.

Abstract

The periodic table of elements transformed chemistry by revealing simple organizing principles underlying atomic behavior. Despite decades of effort, no analogous framework has emerged for crystalline materials -- their microscopic complexity and vast configurational space have defied reduction to fundamental organizing principles. Here we demonstrate that the materials landscape possesses a hidden geometric organization that can be unveiled through unsupervised nonlinear dimensionality reduction. Our approach uncovers emergent organizing principles that control macroscopic quantum behavior, offering a new paradigm in how we build models of complex quantum materials directly from experimental data.

Abstract

Abstract

Abstract

For general max-k-XORSAT with $k \geq 3$, no polynomial-time algorithm can do substantially better than random guessing on worst-case instances unless $\mathsf{P} = \mathsf{NP}$: approximating beyond the random-assignment value of $1/2$ is $\mathsf{NP}$-hard. The picture changes when each variable appears in at most $D$ constraints. In that bounded-degree setting, polynomial-time algorithms can provably beat the random baseline by an additive amount of order $1/\sqrt{D}$. For Boolean instances, this scaling is known to be optimal: the matching hardness result is due to Trevisan, while the corresponding algorithmic guarantee was established by Barak et al. Whether the same holds over general finite fields, and what it implies for quantum algorithms, has not been established. We make this connection explicit and extend the hardness to max-E$k$-LINSAT$(q,r)$ with bounded degree $D$ and over arbitrary finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$, proving that it is $\mathsf{NP}$-hard to exceed $r/q + \mathcal{O}_{q,r}(1/\sqrt{D})$. These results provide the complexity-theoretic benchmark for the bounded-degree instances targeted by decoded quantum interferometry (DQI), QAOA, and classical heuristics. Any quantum advantage on bounded-degree instances is therefore confined to the constant prefactor. We further show that in the context of DQI and on $(k,D)$-regular instances, this prefactor is sensitive to the nature of the decoder: DQI with classical decoders faces an information-theoretic $1/\sqrt{D \log D}$ barrier that prevents it from matching the hardness scaling, while DQI with quantum decoders is compatible with the $1/\sqrt{D}$ scaling -- identifying quantum decoding as the key ingredient for matching the complexity-theoretic scaling with DQI.
