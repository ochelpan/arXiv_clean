Non-Markovian entropy production fluctuation theorem driven by a time-dependent electric field

Authors: K. S. Rodríguez-Vigil, M. A. Bastarrachea-Magnani, J. I. Jiménez-Aquino
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03191v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proves that the Detailed Fluctuation Theorem holds for a non-Markovian system driven by an electric field that affects both the particle and the bath. It shows that the standard fluctuation-dissipation theorem remains valid even when an induced electric force is present. The study highlights how memory effects can lead to temporary energy backflow and negative entropy production.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the validity of the Detailed Fluctuation Theorem (DFT) for total entropy production in a non-Markovian system driven by a time-dependent electric field.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that Kubo's second fluctuation-dissipation theorem remains unchanged despite induced electric forces, and prove the validity of the Detailed Fluctuation Theorem for total entropy production.

Method. The study uses the Zwanzig-Caldeira-Leggett model, Generalized Langevin Equations, and Laplace transforms to solve for the system's dynamics and probability density.

Model / system. A Brownian particle with charge Q in a harmonic potential, embedded in a charged fluid bath of harmonic oscillators, subjected to a time-dependent electric field.

Key observables. Total entropy production, average work, dissipated heat, and Gaussian probability density of position.

Important parameters / regimes. Electric field frequency (Omega), friction memory correlation time (tau_c), friction coefficient (gamma_0), and the overdamped vs. underdamped regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is initially in thermal equilibrium, the potential is harmonic, and the induced force is approximated based on high-frequency bath oscillations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the time behavior of the system in the overdamped regime under weak and high-frequency driving.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the derivation of the Generalized Langevin Equation from a Hamiltonian, proceeds to solve for the probability density and verify FDT invariance, then analyzes entropy production and work statistics, and concludes with the derivation of the Markovian limit.

Abstract

Fluctuation theorems are key to understanding both fundamental and applied aspects of non-equilibrium thermodynamics of small systems. We study the non-Markovian entropy production fluctuation theorem for the diffusion process of charged particles in a gas inside a harmonic potential and under the action of a time-dependent electric field, using a generalized Langevin equation. By considering the influence of the electric field on both the tagged Brownian particle and the bath particles, an "induced" electric force arises. Despite the additional force, we demonstrate that Kubo's second fluctuation-dissipation theorem (FDT) remains unchanged. The FDT allows us to obtain the Gaussian probability density for the position along a single stochastic trajectory, which is the key to demonstrating the validity of the detailed fluctuation theorem (DFT) for the total entropy production. We study the specific result of an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck-type friction memory kernel and an oscillating electric field, and analyze the average work and entropy production in different parameter regimes.

Quantum work beyond classical (commuting) limits

Authors: Sumit Rout, Aravinth Balaji Ravichandran, Paweł Horodecki, Anubhav Chaturvedi
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04021v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper proves that non-commuting Hamiltonian settings allow for a higher average work extraction across multiple processes than any possible commuting (classical) device. It establishes a new thermodynamic law and identifies Hamiltonian incompatibility as a fundamental resource for work extraction.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and thermodynamics as it identifies a new way to certify quantum advantage using thermodynamic quantities rather than Bell inequalities, and treats non-commutativity as a quantifiable resource.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether Hamiltonian incompatibility (non-commutativity) acts as a thermodynamic resource to extract more average work across multiple tasks than any classical (commuting) device can achieve.

Main result. The authors derive a universal classical average-work law and prove that incompatible Hamiltonians can exceed this limit, establishing Hamiltonian incompatibility as a fundamental thermodynamic resource.

Method. The study uses a thermodynamic framework involving nonequilibrium free-energy differences, quantum relative entropy, and geometric optimization over tracial noncommuting polynomials and Bloch-sphere geometries.

Model / system. A work-extraction task involving a black-box device queried with bounded Hamiltonians (0 <= H <= I) using a source of pure qubit preparations constrained by pairwise maximal-energy data.

Key observables. Average work (W_p), average-work advantage (Delta_av), and visibility (v).

Important parameters / regimes. Hamiltonian commutativity, visibility threshold (v_c), source geometry (theta), and reference energy averages (mu_j).

Assumptions / limitations. The preparations are assumed to be pure, and the work device is treated as a black box with no specific microscopic model or dimension restriction.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the work-extraction task, showing the flow from the constrained source to the work device and comparing the free-energy envelope to the commutativity constraint.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the work-extraction task, establishes the branch-wise free-energy benchmark, derives the classical average-work law for a minimal task, quantifies the quantum advantage, and analyzes the robustness of this advantage against noise and task complexity.

Abstract

Free energy fixes the maximum work of a thermodynamic process once the state and Hamiltonian are specified. A work-extraction task asks a different question: how much average work can a single device realize across several preparations and Hamiltonian settings? A classical work device is one whose Hamiltonian settings are mutually commuting. We place every branch at its best free-energy-limited work envelope and derive the corresponding classical limit on the task average. For pure preparations, the source is specified only by pairwise maximal-energy constraints: for each pair, the intrinsic maximal average energy under one common normalized Hamiltonian is bounded as part of the task data, while the work device is otherwise microscopically unrestricted. The benchmark is optimized over arbitrary-dimensional classical implementations. Incompatible Hamiltonian settings exceed this limit, even though every branch remains bounded by its own free-energy maximum. The advantage therefore does not arise in any single process, but in the average work of the task: incompatible Hamiltonians realize a value that no classical work device can attain. Hamiltonian incompatibility is thus a thermodynamic resource for work extraction.

Sparkling bubbles in chiral active fluids

Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03404v1

Summary. This paper identifies a new 'sparkling' phase in chiral active fluids where bubbles undergo continuous breakup and reformation. Driven by non-equilibrium transverse forces, this phase is explained through a hydrodynamic model showing that high chirality induces oscillations in bubble size. The findings provide a new way to understand complex, self-organizing patterns in active matter.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates a novel, dynamically unstable phase in chiral active fluids called the 'sparkling bubble' (SBIO) phase, characterized by the continuous nucleation, deformation, and breakup of bubbles.

Main result. The authors discover that high chirality induces an underdamped oscillatory regime in bubble radii, leading to a 'sparkling' behavior where bubbles continuously merge and annihilate, distinct from the stable bubble (BIO) phase.

Method. The researchers use 2D numerical Langevin simulations of repulsive particles and develop a coarse-grained hydrodynamic theory, including a generalized Rayleigh-Plesset approach to model bubble evolution.

Model / system. A 2D inertial chiral active fluid composed of N repulsive particles interacting via a WCA potential and 'odd' transverse forces that break mirror symmetry.

Key observables. Vorticity, kinetic energy, bubble radius, normalized variance of bubble size, and the Minnaert frequency.

Important parameters / regimes. Chirality strength (omega/epsilon), packing fraction (phi), reduced temperature (Tr), and reduced inertial time (tau_I/tau).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglect of spatial gradients in the velocity field for steady-state evaluation, assumption of perfectly circular bubbles, and neglect of viscous and odd-viscous contributions in the momentum equation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows snapshots of the three phases (homogeneous, BIO, SBIO), time-series of bubble breakup, and a U-shaped phase diagram. Figure 2 plots vorticity, kinetic energy, and bubble radius against chirality, using bubble size variance as an order parameter. Figure 3 shows vorticity and related dynamics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SBIO phase, describes the microscopic Langevin model and hydrodynamic derivation, presents simulation results and phase diagrams, and concludes with a theoretical explanation of the bubble instability mechanism.

Abstract

We study an inertial chiral active fluid, formed by repulsive particles that transfer angular momentum through odd interactions, i.e. transverse forces. Chirality induces an inhomogeneous phase, consisting of rotating bubbles, whose formation is favored at an optimal packing fraction. In this regime, we discover that bubbles may be dynamically unstable, breaking up and reforming in the steady state, thereby showing a spontaneous sparkling-like behavior reminiscent of supersaturated liquids. Bubbles and sparkling bubbles are predicted by a coarse-grained hydrodynamic theory, revealing the intrinsic non-linearity of these collective phenomena, and call for experimental verifications in granular spinners or spinning colloids.

Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03177v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. This paper presents a method to pinpoint which nuclear spin pairs are responsible for dephasing in molecular qubits. By connecting experimental coherence data to specific spin interactions, the authors provide a workflow to design molecules with longer-lived quantum states. This is a significant step toward using molecular spins for practical quantum sensing and computing.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical framework for understanding open quantum system dynamics (non-Markovian dephasing) in a molecular platform, which is crucial for scaling molecular-based quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Identifying the specific sources of nuclear spin-spin dephasing in molecular qubits to improve electron spin coherence lifetimes.

Main result. The development of a computational workflow that connects experimental dephasing times to specific individual spin pairs, enabling strategic improvements to coherence.

Method. An electronic-structure enhanced, non-Markovian perturbative theoretical method used to analyze decoherence behavior.

Model / system. Two molecular qubit candidates with various ligands placed in different nuclear-spin containing solvents.

Key observables. Electron spin coherence lifetimes and dephasing times.

Important parameters / regimes. Low temperature regime; nuclear spin-spin interactions; spin-pair dephasing pathways.

Assumptions / limitations. The decoherence is dominated by spin-spin dephasing from nuclear spins within the molecule or the surrounding environment.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of molecular spin dephasing, describes the analysis of two molecular qubit candidates, presents a new perturbative theoretical method, and concludes with a computational workflow for improving coherence.

Abstract

Molecular spins offer promise in emerging quantum technologies such as quantum sensing and computing. At low temperatures, nuclear spin-spin interactions affect electron spin coherence lifetimes through pure dephasing. Nuclear-spin noise can originate from spin pairs within a molecule itself, pairs in a surrounding environment system, or pairs in which one spin is on the molecule and the other in the environment. Improving coherence times requires detailed knowledge of the dominant sources of dephasing. Here, we analyze the decoherence behavior of two molecular qubit candidates with various ligands and in different nuclear-spin containing solvents. We apply an electronic-structure enhanced, non-Markovian perturbative theoretical method to connect experimentally comparable dephasing times to individual spin pairs. This analysis allows the development of a computational workflow to strategically improve coherence lifetimes in spin systems where decoherence is dominated by spin-spin dephasing.

Nonlinear Compton scattering in a frequency-modulated field

Authors: Antonino Di Piazza, Kenan Qu
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04011v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · interference shaping light 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how using squeezed light as a background laser field can control the radiation emitted during nonlinear Compton scattering. It demonstrates that the squeezing angle can be tuned to either enhance or suppress the photon yield and energy spectrum. This provides a new mechanism for controlling high-energy radiation processes using quantum optical techniques.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the squeezing of a background electromagnetic field can be used as a control parameter to alter the radiation spectrum and photon yield in nonlinear Compton scattering.

Main result. The authors show that squeezing in the background field effectively acts as a frequency modulation, which can significantly enhance or suppress the emitted photon energy spectrum and total photon yield depending on the squeezing angle.

Method. The study uses the Furry picture of strong-field QED and a numerical kinetic approach to simulate the collision of ultrarelativistic electrons with a squeezed coherent plane-wave field.

Model / system. An ultrarelativistic electron beam colliding with a strong, frequency-modulated electromagnetic plane wave modeled as a squeezed coherent state.

Key observables. Differential energy spectrum of emitted photons (dE/domega), total photon yield, and total emitted energy per electron.

Important parameters / regimes. Quantum nonlinearity parameter (chi_0), nonlinearity parameter (xi_0), squeezing amplitude (zeta_0), squeezing angle (theta_0), and Schwinger field (F_cr).

Assumptions / limitations. Quantum fluctuations induced by squeezing are assumed to be negligible; the background field is treated as a classical external field (no depletion); and the electron dynamics are treated in a one-dimensional approximation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the differential energy spectra for different squeezing angles and the evolution of the nonlinearity parameter; Figure 2 compares the spectrum of a squeezed pulse to a non-squeezed pulse of equal total energy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of nonlinear Compton scattering in squeezed fields, develops the theoretical framework using the Furry picture, demonstrates the equivalence of squeezing to frequency modulation, provides numerical simulations of electron-laser collisions, and concludes with an analysis of enhancement and suppression effects.

Abstract

When an electron is accelerated, it emits radiation. In the relativistic quantum realm the elementary radiation process is the emission of a single photon, a process known as nonlinear Compton scattering in the case of an electron moving in the presence of a strong electromagnetic wave. This process is typically described within the Furry picture, where the electromagnetic wave is described as a classical background field and the electron-positron field is quantized in the presence of that background field. Equivalently one can quantize the electron-positron field in the vacuum but then the photon emission process is described as a transition from an initial state to a final state both featuring, apart from the electron (the initial state) and the electron and the photon (the final state), the same coherent state of photons appropriately related to the electromagnetic wave. Here, we consider a more general situation where the initial and the final state feature the same squeezed coherent state. Then, we specialize to the case where the coherent state corresponds to a plane-wave field and the mostly populated modes of the coherent state are also squeezed. We show that, when quantum fluctuations induced by the squeezing in the coherent field are negligible, a condition well satisfied at available squeezing levels, the squeezing effects effectively reduce to a frequency modulation of the plane-wave field corresponding to the coherent state. By means of numerical examples we show that at already available squeezing levels the emission spectrum of nonlinear Compton scattering and the total photon yield can be altered significantly. Analytical explanations of the main numerical results are also provided.

Fisher-Informational Time: A Causal-Geometric Framework for Emergent Clock Time Physical Distinguishability

Authors: J. Sumaya-Martinez
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03958v1

Summary. This paper proposes that time is not a fundamental background parameter but an emergent property reconstructed from the accumulation of physical distinguishability. By using Fisher information geometry, the author shows how clock time can be calibrated from the measurable differences between causally ordered states. This offers a new perspective on the relationship between information, dynamics, and the arrow of time.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep information-geometric reinterpretation of quantum speed limits and the Schrödinger equation, linking the rate of state evolution directly to energy uncertainty and distinguishability.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The fundamental nature of time and the 'problem of time' in quantum gravity, specifically whether time is a fundamental substance or an emergent property.

Main result. Proposes that clock time is an emergent calibration derived from the accumulation of Fisher distinguishability along causally ordered physical trajectories.

Method. Uses information geometry, specifically the Fisher information metric, Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), and the Fubini-Study metric, to define an informational parameter Lambda_F.

Model / system. The framework applies to classical statistical systems, quantum systems (using density operators and Hilbert space geometry), qubit clocks, and cosmological evolution.

Key observables. Fisher-informational parameter (Lambda_F), energy uncertainty (Delta H), and Fisher distinguishability per tick (eta_C).

Important parameters / regimes. Lambda_F (accumulated Fisher distance), g_ij (Fisher metric), and the energy uncertainty (Delta H) which governs the rate of distinguishability accumulation.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that only physically distinguishable changes have operational meaning, changes must follow a causal order, and clock time requires a monotonic mapping from the accumulated distance.

Figures summary. A conceptual flowchart illustrating the hierarchy from physical states and causal admissibility to Fisher geometry and emergent clock description.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the problem of time, proposes a causal-geometric framework based on distinguishability, establishes mathematical postulates, demonstrates applications in quantum and classical systems (including qubit clocks and decay), and situates the work within the context of existing relational and thermal time theories.

Abstract

We develop a Fisher-informational reformulation of physical time in which clock time is not regarded as a fundamental ontological substance, but as an emergent calibration of causally ordered distinguishability among physical states. The operational starting point is that clocks do not measure time itself; rather, they instantiate reproducible physical processes whose distinguishable states are correlated with other events. We introduce a causal-informational parameter, denoted by Lambda_F, defined as an accumulated Fisher-geometric distance along a causally admissible trajectory in state space. In classical statistical systems, this parameter is generated by the Fisher information metric; in quantum systems, the corresponding construction is associated with quantum Fisher information, the Bures metric, and the Fubini-Study geometry of projective Hilbert space. The manuscript distinguishes model-dependent Fisher information from quantum Fisher information, clarifies the reparameterization of Schrodinger dynamics, and gives explicit examples involving a qubit clock, an exponential decay process, and a Fisher characterization of clock quality. The proposal is positioned relative to relational time, the Page-Wootters mechanism, thermal time, quantum speed-limit relations, information geometry, and the problem of time in quantum gravity. We do not claim that relational or emergent time is new. The specific contribution is the use of Fisher distinguishability as an operational precursor from which ordinary clock time can be reconstructed. In this sense, the central statement of the paper is: time is not measured by clocks; clock time is reconstructed from the Fisher distinguishability accumulated along causally ordered physical changes.

Sequential vs. Simultaneous Entanglement Swapping under Optimal Link-Layer Control

Authors: Priyam Srivastava, Akshat R. Sabavat, Siddharth Jain, Alan Scheller-Wolf, Sridhar Tayur, David Tipper, Prashant Krishnamurthy, Amy Babay, Kaushik P. Seshadreesan
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04047v1

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the performance disadvantage of connection-less quantum networks is not a fundamental architectural limitation but rather a consequence of current-day hardware constraints regarding memory coherence. By using reinforcement learning to optimize link-layer control, the authors show that as memory coherence improves, sequential swapping protocols become as efficient as simultaneous ones.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems as it quantifies how environmental decoherence (memory decay) fundamentally dictates the operational limits and architectural choices for scalable quantum communication networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study compares the performance of sequential (connection-less) versus simultaneous (connection-oriented) entanglement swapping protocols in quantum networks, specifically investigating if the performance penalty in sequential swapping is a fundamental flaw or a transient effect of low memory coherence.

Main result. The researchers find that the performance gap is a near-term phenomenon tied to low memory coherence; at high coherence levels, both protocols are statistically equivalent, and the sequential protocol only collapses when the coherence-to-latency ratio falls below a critical threshold.

Method. A two-layer simulation framework is used where the link layer is controlled by a reinforcement-learning policy (WN2M2) and the network layer implements different swapping protocols, followed by a parameter sweep of external memory coherence time.

Model / system. A multi-hop quantum network chain (n=4 links) consisting of nodes with internal memories and external buffers, utilizing Werner states subject to depolarizing decay and a six-state QKD protocol.

Key observables. Per-pair efficiency (uSKR) in bits per heralding tick, end-to-end entanglement delivery rates, and the dimensionless ratio of external coherence time to heralding latency (T_c_ext/tau).

Important parameters / regimes. External memory coherence time (T_c_ext), per-link heralding latency (tau), link lengths (5 km and 10 km), and the number of links (n=4).

Assumptions / limitations. Local operations (gates, measurements, readout) are assumed to be instantaneous and noiseless; the link-layer RL policy is held fixed to isolate network-layer variables.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the two-layer architecture; Figure 2 shows the convergence of trained RL policies; Figure 3 compares uSKR vs. coherence time showing the sequential protocol collapse; Figures 7 and 8 demonstrate protocol equivalence in symmetric and bottleneck topologies at high coherence.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the two-layer architecture, defines the link-layer RL policy and network-layer protocols, presents a systematic sweep of coherence regimes, analyzes the mechanism of protocol failure, and concludes with a comparison of different network topologies.

Abstract

Connection-less, packet-switched quantum network architectures distribute entanglement across multi-hop paths through sequential entanglement swapping, in which each node acts on purely local state information. The architectural advantages over the connection-oriented alternative -- simultaneous SWAP-ASAP -- are compelling, but sequential swapping holds partial chains in intermediate buffers between successive swaps, exposing them to memory decoherence in a way simultaneous SWAP-ASAP avoids by design. We present a proof-of-principle study at fixed chain length $n = 4$ in which each elementary link is governed by a fixed reinforcement-learning policy optimizing the secret-key rate of the six-state protocol, leaving the network-layer protocol as the sole independent variable. Sweeping the network-layer memory coherence time $T_c^{\mathrm{ext}}$ over four orders of magnitude reveals a clear regime structure governed by the dimensionless ratio $T_c^{\mathrm{ext}}/τ$, where $τ$ is the per-link entanglement heralding latency. Simultaneous SWAP-ASAP delivers a constant rate across the full sweep. Sequential swapping, by contrast, collapses to zero end-to-end deliveries below $T_c^{\mathrm{ext}}/τ= 25$, and begins recovering at $T_c^{\mathrm{ext}}/τ= 50$. It remains limited by the simultaneous rate, which it saturates only at the relaxed end of the sweep. These results suggest that the connection-less penalty is a near-term phenomenon tied to present-day memory coherence rather than a fundamental property of sequential swapping.

Quantum Multi-Level Estimation of Functionals of Discrete Distributions

Authors: Kean Chen, Minbo Gao, Tongyang Li, Qisheng Wang, Xinzhao Wang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03685v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new quantum algorithm for estimating complex functions of probability distributions, such as various types of entropy. It achieves near-optimal efficiency for many cases while using much fewer extra qubits and simpler control logic than previous methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing efficient quantum algorithms for estimating functionals of discrete probability distributions, particularly those with singularities like q-Tsallis and Shannon entropies.

Main result. The authors propose a multi-level estimation framework that achieves near-optimal quantum query complexity for q-Tsallis entropy for q > 1 and provides a quantum speedup for 0 < q < 1, while significantly reducing ancilla and control overhead.

Method. A multi-level framework using logarithmic partitioning of the probability space, non-destructive singular value discrimination, and Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT) for local polynomial approximation.

Model / system. A purified quantum query access model where a unitary oracle provides access to the square roots of the probabilities of a discrete distribution.

Key observables. q-Tsallis entropy, Shannon entropy, alpha-Renyi entropy, and general functionals of the form sum(p_i * g(p_i)).

Important parameters / regimes. The parameter q (for Tsallis/Renyi entropy), the precision epsilon, the distribution size n, and the interval partitioning ratio rho.

Assumptions / limitations. Requires purified quantum query access, the existence of suitable polynomial approximations for the target function, and that the matrix norm is bounded.

Figures summary. Table 1 compares the quantum query complexity of estimating q-Tsallis entropy across various ranges of q against previous classical and quantum results.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a multi-level estimation framework, details the technical implementation using non-destructive discrimination and QSVT, provides complexity analysis for various entropy regimes (Shannon, Tsallis, Renyi), and establishes matching lower bounds via sample-to-query lifting.

Abstract

We propose a quantum multi-level estimation framework for a functional $\sum_{i=1}^n f(p_i)$ of a discrete distribution $(p_i)_{i=1}^n$. We partition the values $p_i$ into logarithmically many intervals whose length decays exponentially. For each interval, we perform non-destructive singular value discrimination to isolate the relevant $p_i$, enabling adaptive estimation of the partial sum over this interval. Unlike previous variable-time approaches, our method avoids high control overhead and requires only constant extra ancilla qubits. As an application, we present efficient quantum estimators for the $q$-Tsallis entropy of discrete distributions. Specifically: (i) For $q > 1$, we obtain a near-optimal quantum algorithm with query complexity $\tildeΘ(1/\varepsilon^{\max\{1/(2(q-1)), 1\}})$, improving the prior best $O(1/\varepsilon^{1+1/(q-1)})$ due to Liu and Wang (SODA 2025; IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 2026). (ii) For $0 < q < 1$, we obtain a quantum algorithm with query complexity $\tilde{O}(n^{1/q-1/2}/\varepsilon^{1/q})$, exhibiting a quantum speedup over the near-optimal classical estimators due to Jiao, Venkat, Han, and Weissman (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 2017). Our results achieve, to our knowledge, the first near-optimal quantum estimators for parameterized $q$-entropy for non-integer $q$.

Analytical two-pulse control of universal single-qubit gates in rotational ultracold NaCs molecules

Summary. This paper presents an analytical method for performing high-fidelity single-qubit gates in ultracold NaCs molecules using a simple two-pulse sequence. By using a Magnus expansion, the authors achieve gate fidelities exceeding 0.9999. The approach also allows for easy gate tomography through molecular orientation detection.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a highly accurate, analytical method for controlling molecular qubits, which is crucial for building scalable molecular quantum processors and understanding coherent dynamics in rotational states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The practical implementation of universal single-qubit gates in ultracold molecules is hindered by complex control protocols and sensitivity to experimental imperfections.

Main result. The authors developed an analytical framework using an optimized two-pulse sequence that achieves gate fidelities above 0.9999 and enables practical gate tomography.

Method. The researchers employ a first-order Magnus expansion to derive closed-form unitary evolution from a two-pulse sequence, establishing precise amplitude and phase conditions.

Abstract

Complex control protocols and sensitivity to experimental imperfections have limited the practical implementation of quantum gate operations. Here, we present an analytical framework for universal single-qubit gates using rotational states of ultracold NaCs molecules. By encoding qubits in the lowest rotational energy levels, we employ a first-order Magnus expansion to derive closed-form unitary evolution from an optimized two-pulse sequence. This approach establishes precise amplitude and phase conditions for arbitrary single-qubit rotations, achieving gate fidelities above 0.9999 in numerical simulations. We further demonstrate that complex multi-gate sequences, including phase-locked operations, can be executed with minimal population leakage into auxiliary states. The time-dependent molecular orientation is shown to faithfully encode both the gate truth table and coherence dynamics, enabling practical gate tomography via weak-field polarization detection. Our analytical method is also applicable to other molecules and physical platforms, offering a potential path to high-fidelity, scalable molecular quantum processors.

Main result. The study establishes a microscopic model that demonstrates violations of the Mott-IOLoffe-Regel and KSS bounds, identifies a non-Boltzmann transport relation, and shows bosonic self-tuning to a quantum critical point.

Method. A non-perturbative approach using the large-N limit of a (2+1)D Yukawa-SYK model, employing saddle-point equations and the Kubo formula for transport coefficients.

Model / system. A (2+1)-dimensional Yukawa-Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev (Y-SYK) model consisting of N electrons coupled to N bosons with spatially random coupling constants.

Key observables. Resistivity, electrical conductivity, shear viscosity to entropy density ratio (eta/s), charge diffusivity, and superconducting critical temperature (Tc).

Important parameters / regimes. Electronic bandwidth (Lambda), temperature (T), coupling strength (lambda), bosonic UV cutoff (omega_D), and impurity scattering rate (Omega_0).

Assumptions / limitations. Large-N limit, momentum-independent self-energies due to local disorder, and use of natural units (hbar = kB = 1).

Figures summary. Plots of resistivity versus temperature for various coupling strengths, showing the transition from strange-metal to bad-metal regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Y-SYK model, derives self-consistent saddle-point equations, analyzes transport regimes (Strange Metal, Bad Metal, Thermal Incoherent Metal), investigates the impact of disorder and self-tuning, and concludes with the derivation of superconducting properties.

Abstract

Numerous unconventional superconductors such as cuprates, heavy-fermions, and twisted-bilayer graphene exhibit incoherent metallic transport above the superconducting critical temperature. This phenomenon cannot be described with Fermi-liquid theory and has presented a significant theoretical challenge to overcome. We utilize the two-dimensional Yukawa-SYK model of fermions with spatially random coupling to quantum-critical bosons to study transport in a manner which is non-perturbative in the coupling strength. Our work provides a microscopic model of quantum-critical incoherent metals and their concomitant properties, including a non-Boltzmann transport formula between resistivity and quasi-particle lifetime, violation of the Mott-Ioffe-Regel resistivity bound, and violation of the Kovtun-Son-Starinets shear viscosity to entropy density bound.
