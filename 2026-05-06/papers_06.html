Quantum Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning via Variational Quantum Circuits

Authors: Yu-Ting Lee, Samuel Yen-Chi Chen, Fu-Chieh Chang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03434v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores integrating variational quantum circuits into hierarchical reinforcement learning to achieve higher parameter efficiency. It finds that while quantum feature extractors can significantly outperform classical models with fewer parameters, using quantum circuits for value estimation currently acts as a performance bottleneck.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the advantages of Quantum Reinforcement Learning, such as parameter efficiency and expressivity, extend to Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) using the option-critic architecture.

Main result. A hybrid agent using a quantum feature extractor outperforms classical baselines while reducing trainable parameters by up to 66%, though using quantum circuits for option-value estimation creates a performance bottleneck.

Method. The authors develop a hybrid quantum-classical option-critic agent that replaces specific classical components with Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) using data re-uploading and hardware-efficient ansatz.

Model / system. The study uses a hybrid reinforcement learning framework applied to classical Gymnasium environments, specifically the CartPole and Acrobot tasks, utilizing VQCs for feature extraction, policies, and value functions.

Key observables. Expectation values of Pauli-Z operators, policy entropy, actor loss, and critic loss.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth, learnable input-scaling parameters (lambda), CNOT entanglement, and the number of options.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that classical state observations can be effectively mapped to quantum states via unitary encoding and does not account for hardware noise in the simulations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the hybrid RL paradigm; Figure 2 details the option-critic architecture; Figure 3 illustrates the VQC structure; Figure 6 shows learning curves; Figure 8 provides a diagnostic analysis of the quantum critic failure; Figure 10 shows ablation studies on circuit depth and entanglement.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of quantum HRL, details the hybrid option-critic architecture and VQC implementation, presents experimental results on CartPole and Acrobot, conducts ablation studies on circuit parameters, and discusses identified bottlenecks and future directions.

Abstract

Reinforcement learning is one of the most challenging learning paradigms where efficacy and efficiency gains are extremely valuable. Hierarchical reinforcement learning is a variant that leverages temporal abstraction to structure decision-making. While parametrized quantum computations have shown success in non-hierarchical reinforcement learning, whether these advantages adapt to hierarchical decision-making remains a critical open question. In this work, we develop a hybrid hierarchical agent based on the option-critic architecture. This hybrid agent substitutes classical components with variational quantum circuits for feature extractors, option-value functions, termination functions, and intra-option policies. Evaluated on standard benchmarking environments, results show that a hybrid agent utilizing a quantum feature extractor can outperform classical baselines while saving up to 66\% trainable parameters. We also identify an architectural bottleneck that quantum option-value estimation severely degrades performance. Further ablation studies reveal how architectural choices of the quantum circuits affect performance. Our work establishes design principles for parameter-efficient hybrid hierarchical agents.

Quantum Metric Localization and Quantum Metric Protection

Authors: Wen-Bo Dai, Jinchao Zhao, Shuai A. Chen, K. T. Law
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03987v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies a new regime of localization where the localization length is determined by the intrinsic quantum geometry of a band rather than the disorder strength. It demonstrates that the quantum metric provides a 'protection' mechanism that keeps the localization length constant even as disorder increases, until the disorder becomes large enough to mix different bands.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how the quantum metric of an isolated band influences electronic localization in the presence of disorder, specifically challenging the standard Anderson localization paradigm.

Main result. The authors discover a 'quantum metric localization' regime where the localization length becomes pinned at a plateau of approximately twice the quantum metric length, providing a form of 'quantum metric protection' against disorder.

Method. The study combines numerical Transfer Matrix Method simulations with analytical approaches, including a physical picture based on Wannier functions and a supersymmetric (SUSY) non-linear sigma model.

Model / system. The primary model is a 1D modified Lieb lattice (applicable to electronic, photonic, and acoustic systems) featuring a central band separated from other bands by a gap $\Delta$, subjected to on-site Anderson disorder.

Key observables. Localization length ($\xi$), quantum metric length ($l_{QM}$), and the ratio $\xi/l_{QM}$.

Important parameters / regimes. Disorder strength ($\Gamma$), band gap ($\Delta$), bandwidth ($4t$), and quantum metric length ($l_{QM}$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes an isolated band where interband mixing is negligible ($\Delta \gg \Gamma$) and utilizes a gradient expansion in the effective field theory.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the 1D Lieb lattice, energy spectrum, and the emergence of the localization length plateau; Fig 3 illustrates the crossover between Anderson and quantum metric localization and the failure of protection at large $\Gamma$; Fig 4 demonstrates the robustness of the plateau in spinful systems.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of localization in flat bands, presents numerical evidence of the localization plateau in a 1D Lieb lattice, provides a physical explanation via Wannier functions, uses a SUSY non-linear sigma model to derive the universal scaling $\xi \approx 2l_{QM}$, explores the breakdown of protection via interband mixing, and tests robustness in multiband spinful systems.

Abstract

The study of disorder effects in electronic systems is one of the central themes in physics. It is well established that in the Anderson localization regime, the localization length of electrons decreases monotonically as the disorder strength increases. Here, we demonstrate that the conventional Anderson localization paradigm fails completely in describing an isolated band with quantum metric, where the quantum metric of the band defines a length scale called the quantum metric length. For an isolated band with a finite bandwidth separated from other bands by a band gap $Δ$, weak disorder results in conventional Anderson localization behavior. However, as the disorder increases, the localization length ceases to decrease and becomes pinned at a value approximately twice the quantum metric length, forming a localization length plateau. We term the regime within this localization length plateau as the quantum metric localization regime. Remarkably, the localization length does not deviate from the plateau until the disorder strength far exceeds $Δ$. We refer to this strong protection against disorder, characterized by the quantum metric length, as quantum metric protection. In this work, we first numerically demonstrate quantum metric localization using a 1D Lieb lattice. We then provide a simple physical picture based on the properties of Wannier functions to explain the origin of the localization length plateau. A supersymmetric field theory approach explains why the localization length is approximately twice the quantum metric length and captures the crossover from Anderson localization to quantum metric localization. Our conclusions are broadly applicable to disordered electronic, photonic, and acoustic systems.

Quantum Resource Estimation for Minimising Energy Grid Losses

Authors: Camille de Valk, Milou van Nederveen, Koen Reerink, Werner van Westering
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03467v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper evaluates the feasibility of using gate-based quantum computing to optimize electricity grid topologies to minimize power losses. By using a higher-order optimization formulation (HUBO), the authors provide a resource estimation that shows how network complexity directly drives the required quantum hardware scale.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Estimating the quantum computational resources (qubits and runtime) required to solve the NP-hard Distribution Network Reconfiguration (DNR) problem for minimizing power losses in electricity grids.

Main result. The study demonstrates that constructing HUBO formulations for real-world networks is feasible, but resource requirements scale extremely rapidly with network size and structural complexity (connectivity and cyclicity).

Method. The authors formulate the DNR problem as a Higher-Order Unconstrained Binary Optimization (HUBO) problem and use the Microsoft Quantum Resource Estimator to perform Quantum Resource Estimation (QRE) for various gate-based and Majorana qubit architectures.

Model / system. The system is a medium-voltage electricity distribution network modeled as a directed graph. The optimization is formulated via a Pauli Hamiltonian (H_HUBO) representing a weighted sum of penalty terms (for vertex, edge, and cycle constraints) and an objective term for power losses.

Key observables. Number of logical qubits, number of rotation gates, number of interaction terms, and estimated execution runtime.

Important parameters / regimes. Network connectivity, cyclicity, number of nodes/edges, and the number of optimization layers.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the use of the surface code for error correction and focuses on the resources for a single optimization layer rather than a full multi-layer pipeline.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the network graph topology and decomposition into biconnected components; Figure 2 shows the trade-off between runtime and physical qubit count for different qubit technologies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the DNR problem, presents the HUBO formulation and graph reduction techniques, details the quantum resource estimation methodology, analyzes the scaling of resources relative to network topology, and discusses future implications for large-scale grids.

Abstract

Distribution network reconfiguration (DNR) can minimise power losses by identifying the optimal topology of the electricity grid. Determining the minimum loss configuration is NP-hard, and classical optimisation methods struggle to scale to real-world distribution grids. This paper explores the use of gate-based quantum computing to solve DNR for power loss reduction. We formulate DNR as a higher-order unconstrained binary optimisation (HUBO) problem, avoiding the need for auxiliary variables, thereby reducing the required number of qubits. This is applied to a real medium voltage (MV) network operated by Alliander, a Dutch distribution system operator (DSO). For each biconnected component in the network graph, we construct the corresponding HUBO, derive the cost and mixer operators, and determine the number of required logical qubits and rotation gates. These are then mapped to physical qubits and execution time estimates using quantum resource estimation (QRE). The results suggest that the quantum resource requirements depend not only on component size but also on structural characteristics such as connectivity and cyclicity. Overall, the novelty of this work lies in directly framing the optimisation problem as a HUBO, applying it to real-world MV networks, and performing a QRE to assess future feasibility.

Quasinormal modes and continuum response of de Sitter black holes via complex scaling method

Authors: Shoya Ogawa, Okuto Morikawa, Takuya Hirose
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03277v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a unified spectral framework using the complex scaling method to study both the discrete resonances and the continuous spectrum of de Sitter black holes. It reveals how the cosmological constant influences the spectral density and shows that the lowest quasinormal mode dominates the continuum response. The method is also shown to be applicable to more complex, higher-dimensional, and string-inspired black hole models.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to extend the complex scaling method (CSM) to Schwarzschild-de Sitter spacetimes to investigate how a non-zero cosmological constant modifies both quasinormal-mode poles and the continuum spectral response.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that CSM provides a unified framework for treating QNM poles and the rotated continuum, showing that the lowest QNM pole is the primary driver of the continuum level density structure and that increasing the cosmological constant shifts these features toward lower frequencies.

Method. The complex scaling method (CSM) is applied to Regge-Wheeler-type perturbation equations, using a numerical variational approach with a polynomial times real-range Gaussian basis expansion.

Model / system. The study focuses on four-dimensional Schwarzschild-de Sitter black holes, including scalar, electromagnetic, and gravitational perturbations, as well as extensions to higher-dimensional (5D) and string-inspired (MSW) black hole models.

Key observables. Quasinormal mode (QNM) frequencies, continuum level density (CLD), transmission phase, and greybody factors.

Important parameters / regimes. Cosmological constant (Lambda), spin (s = 0, 1, 2), angular momentum (l = 2, 3), and the scaling angle (theta).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the weakly curved regime (small Lambda) and assumes the validity of the Regge-Wheeler-type master equations in the de Sitter background.

Figures summary. Plots of the complex frequency plane showing discretized continua and QNM candidates; numerical tables of QNM frequencies; and series of plots showing the continuum level density (CLD) and its partial reconstruction using the lowest QNM poles.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the mathematical framework of CSM in dS spacetimes, proceeds to numerical implementation and validation against known Schwarzschild results, analyzes the dependence of QNMs and CLD on the cosmological constant, and concludes with extensions to higher-dimensional and coupled-channel systems.

Abstract

We apply the complex scaling method to black-hole perturbations in four-dimensional Schwarzschild--de~Sitter (dS) spacetimes. The method converts the outgoing-wave boundary-value problem into a non-Hermitian spectral problem and enables quasinormal-mode poles and the rotated continuum to be treated in a common framework. We focus in particular on the continuum level density, which characterizes the continuum response beyond isolated quasinormal-mode frequencies. Using Regge--Wheeler-type perturbation equations for scalar, electromagnetic, and gravitational fields, we investigate how a nonzero cosmological constant modifies the pole and continuum sectors. We also discuss a possible extension to string-inspired coupled-channel systems, and illustrate that higher-dimensional dS black holes can be treated within the same framework, at least in tensor- and vector-type sectors. Our results indicate that complex scaling offers a useful spectral framework for analyzing both quasinormal modes and continuum response in black-hole physics.

Reducing Postselection Overhead in Magic-State Cultivation by In-Patch Multiplexing

Authors: Dongmin Kim, Jeonggeun Seo, Aniket Patra, Youngsun Han
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03616v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a multiplexing strategy for magic-state cultivation that utilizes idle space within a logical patch to run multiple cultivation trials in parallel. By doing so, it significantly reduces the frequency of discarded shots due to early-stage errors, thereby lowering the computational overhead for implementing non-Clifford gates in fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. High postselection overhead in Magic-State Cultivation (MSC) protocols leads to high discard rates and inefficient use of spacetime volume in fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Main result. The proposed in-patch multiplexing scheme significantly reduces the expected number of attempts required for successful magic-state preparation, with reductions of up to 78.69% in full-cycle attempts at a physical error rate of 2e-3.

Method. The authors propose an in-patch multiplexing protocol that embeds multiple local cultivation sites within a single large logical patch, using a 'grafting' process to forward successful candidates to a standard escape stage.

Model / system. The protocol is modeled within a fault-tolerant quantum error correction framework using color codes and surface codes, subject to a uniform depolarizing noise model including idle noise.

Key observables. Injection-and-cultivation (IC) expected attempts, full-cycle expected attempts, logical error rate, and the decoder-based gap threshold.

Important parameters / regimes. Physical error rate (p = 2e-3), local cultivation distances (d1 = 3, 5), final logical distance (d2 = 15), and the decoder gap threshold (G).

Assumptions / limitations. The efficiency gain relies on an independent-site approximation, which assumes no correlation between the multiplexed sites (e.g., no crosstalk).

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares distillation vs. cultivation; Figure 2 shows resource underutilization in single-site MSC; Figure 3 illustrates the multiplexed protocol structure; Figure 4 shows the 2D lattice embedding of the four sites; Figure 5 shows the end-to-end execution flow; Figure 11 shows cumulative fractions of correct and erroneous shots.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MSC overhead, presents the proposed in-patch multiplexing architecture, details the protocol stages (injection, cultivation, and escape), provides numerical results comparing the scheme to a single-site baseline, and discusses the scaling of benefits and limitations.

Abstract

Fault-tolerant quantum computing requires high-fidelity logical magic states for implementing non-Clifford operations. Magic-state cultivation provides a lower-overhead route to logical magic-state preparation, but its efficiency is limited by postselection loss during the early injection-and-cultivation stages. In this work, we propose an in-patch multiplexing scheme that uses early-stage idle resources within a single logical patch to create multiple local cultivation opportunities. A candidate that passes the early stages is forwarded to the standard escape pathway, while the escape stage and the decoder-based acceptance procedure are kept identical to those of the single-site baseline. Under a uniform depolarizing noise model with idle noise, the proposed protocol substantially reduces the injection-and-cultivation discard rate and the expected number of attempts required to obtain an accepted early-stage candidate. At a physical error rate of \(p=2\times10^{-3}\), the injection-and-cultivation expected attempts are reduced by \(45.46\%\) for \(d_1=3\) and by \(72.91\%\) for \(d_1=5\), relative to the single-site MSC baseline. In the direct full-cycle evaluation including escape, the expected attempts per kept logical output are further reduced by \(49.04\%\) for \(d_1=3\) and by \(78.69\%\) for \(d_1=5\) at the same physical error rate. The full-cycle cost curves are shifted toward smaller expected attempts, while the final logical-error behavior remains governed by the escape-stage gap threshold. These results show that in-patch multiplexing can reduce postselection overhead while preserving the standard magic-state cultivation framework.

Renormalization group analysis for bosonization coefficients in half-odd-integer Kitaev spin chains

Authors: Jianxun Li, Chao Xu, Wang Yang
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03582v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides an analytical framework for the bosonization of spin-S Kitaev-type chains using renormalization group theory. It demonstrates that the effects of broken emergent symmetries vanish in the large-S limit and provides specific predictions for the coefficients that govern magnetic ordering.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the bosonization coefficients for spin-S Kitaev-Gamma and Kitaev-Heisenberg-Gamma chains and understanding how the breaking of emergent continuous symmetries scales with spin S.

Main result. The study finds that symmetry-breaking effects in bosonization formulas scale as 1/S in the large-S limit and identifies ten independent bosonization coefficients in the Kitaev-Heisenberg-Gamma model, five of which are independent of Heisenberg coupling to linear order.

Method. Renormalization group (RG) analysis combined with one-loop diagrammatic calculations and validation via Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) numerical simulations.

Model / system. One-dimensional spin-S Kitaev-Gamma and Kitaev-Heisenberg-Gamma chains, which serve as quasi-1D models for 2D Kitaev materials like alpha-RuCl3.

Key observables. Bosonization coefficients (C and D), spin correlation functions, and scaling of symmetry-breaking terms.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin quantum number S (half-odd-integer), Kitaev coupling (K), Gamma interaction (Gamma), Heisenberg exchange (J), and the parameter delta-Gamma.

Assumptions / limitations. Small delta-Gamma limit, large-S limit for scaling relations, and neglecting higher-order terms in delta-Gamma and J.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the ratio of bosonization coefficients as a function of parameter phi; Figure 3 and 4 show diagrammatic representations of the RG vertex and Feynman diagrams for scaling field renormalization; Figure 5 shows log-log plots of spin correlation components.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the spin-S Kitaev models, performs a microscopic derivation using a fermionic representation, applies RG analysis to calculate the renormalization of scaling fields, validates results with DMRG, and discusses the large-S scaling behavior.

Abstract

Based on a renormalization group (RG) analysis, we study the bosonization formulas in spin-S Kitaev-Gamma and Kitaev-Heisenberg-Gamma chains in the (K < 0, Gamma > 0, J > 0) parameter region, where S is a half-odd integer. We find that the effects associated with the breaking of emergent continuous symmetries in bosonization formulas scale as 1/S in the large-S limit, which is in qualitative agreement with DMRG numerical results for Kitaev-Gamma chains. In Kitaev-Heisenberg-Gamma chains, symmetry analysis reveals ten independent bosonization coefficients, five of which are predicted by the RG analysis to have no dependence on the Heisenberg coupling up to linear order. Our work may offer valuable input for determining magnetic ordering tendencies in two-dimensional Kitaev spin models within a quasi-one-dimensional approach.

Scalar bosonic oscillator fields in LV-wormholes

Authors: Omar Mustafa, Abdullah Guvendi
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03366v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the topology of a wormhole and the breaking of Lorentz symmetry together influence the quantum energy levels of scalar fields. It demonstrates that the wormhole geometry acts as a regulator that removes singularities and creates a discrete, predictable energy spectrum.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the quantum dynamics and spectral properties of scalar bosonic oscillator fields propagating in a (3+1)-dimensional Lorentz-violating wormhole spacetime.

Main result. The researchers found that the wormhole geometry eliminates centrifugal singularities and produces a discrete energy spectrum with particle-antiparticle symmetry, where the spectrum is regulated by curvature and Lorentz-violation strength.

Method. The radial Klein-Gordon equation is mapped to a 1D Schrödinger-type equation and solved using the confluent Heun differential equation framework to find conditionally exact solutions.

Model / system. A 4D traversable, static, spherically symmetric Lorentz-violating wormhole with a nonminimally coupled vector background that generates an effective KG-oscillator interaction.

Key observables. Energy eigenvalues (energy spectrum), effective potential, and particle-antiparticle symmetry.

Important parameters / regimes. Lorentz-violation parameter (zeta), wormhole throat radius (a), oscillator frequency (Omega), and angular momentum quantum number (l).

Assumptions / limitations. The system assumes a specific constant-lapse wormhole metric and requires specific parametric constraints (truncation conditions) for the existence of bound states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the effective potential is regular at the throat; Figure 2 displays the relativistic energy spectrum; Figure 3 illustrates the oscillator frequency as a function of LV deformation and angular momentum.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the LV wormhole geometry, formulates the Klein-Gordon equation with a vector background, maps the radial equation to a Schrödinger-type form, solves the resulting confluent Heun problem, and analyzes the resulting energy spectrum and parameter dependencies.

Abstract

We investigate the quantum dynamics of scalar bosonic oscillator fields propagating in a (3+1)-dimensional Lorentz-violating (LV) wormhole spacetime within a modified gravity framework. The underlying geometry, characterized by a smooth minimal-radius throat and a globally regular redshift sector, induces nontrivial curvature effects that significantly modify the spectral properties of the Klein-Gordon (KG) field. The field dynamics are formulated in the presence of a nonminimally coupled vector background of the form $\mathcal{F}_μ=(\mathcal{F}_t(x),0,0,0)$, which, under the physically motivated ansatz $\mathcal{F}_t(x)=Ω\, r(x)$, generates an effective KG-oscillator interaction intrinsically encoded by the wormhole geometry. The resulting effective potential is regular and finite at the throat, eliminating centrifugal singularities and ensuring globally well-defined propagation across the minimal-radius region. The spectral problem reduces to a confluent Heun structure, leading to conditionally exact solutions and a discrete energy spectrum governed by curvature, Lorentz-violation strength, and oscillator frequency. The associated eigenvalue structure exhibits a relativistic particle-antiparticle symmetry with curvature-induced deformation and parameter-dependent confinement. Our results demonstrate that LV wormhole spacetimes act as effective dispersive quantum gravitational media, in which spacetime topology and spontaneous Lorentz symmetry breaking jointly regulate confinement, spectral quantization, and the global evolution of scalar bosonic modes.

Selecting optimal unrestricted Hartree-Fock trial wavefunctions for phaseless auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo: Accuracy and limitations in modeling three iron-sulfur clusters

Authors: Don Danilov, Brad Ganoe, Leon Otis, Zhi Gong, Zixiang Lu, James Shee
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03981v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how the choice of mean-field trial wavefunctions affects the accuracy of ph-AFQMC in transition metal clusters. It demonstrates that while energy-minimizing trials are not always best, selecting trials that preserve physical symmetries can yield surprisingly accurate results despite significant sampling bias.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insight into the systematic errors and sampling pathologies (like overcorrelation) that occur when using mean-field importance functions in many-body Monte Carlo simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining how to select optimal Unrestricted Hartree-Fock (UHF) trial wavefunctions for phaseless auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (ph-AFQMC) to minimize bias in strongly correlated systems.

Main result. The study finds that choosing trial wavefunctions based on physical symmetries and chemical properties (like spin density) is more effective than minimizing variational energy, though the trial states remain suboptimal importance functions that can lead to sampling bias.

Method. Phaseless auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (ph-AFQMC) benchmarked against DMRG, FCI, and CCSD(T) using spin-density metrics to evaluate UHF trial states.

Model / system. Three iron-sulfur (Fe-S) active space models ([2Fe-2S]2+, [4Fe-4S]2+, and [4Fe-4S]4+) and a stretched helium dimer cation (He2+) used as an analogy for sampling bias.

Key observables. Ground-state energies, atomic spin densities, Mulliken spin density, Spin Density Metric (SDM), overlap between stochastic and trial wavefunctions, and local energy fluctuations.

Important parameters / regimes. Imaginary-time step (0.005), walker populations (1024-2048), and Cholesky decomposition threshold (10^-12).

Assumptions / limitations. The phaseless constraint is assumed to be unbiased if the trial wavefunction is an exact eigenstate; the study explores the breakdown of this assumption.

Figures summary. The figures show ph-AFQMC energy errors relative to DMRG/FCI as a function of the Spin Density Metric, energy evolution over imaginary time, and the decay of wavefunction overlap.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the ph-AFQMC problem, presents investigations of specific Fe-S clusters, uses a He2+ dissociation model to explain the mechanism of sampling bias, and concludes with implications for trial wavefunction design.

Abstract

Phaseless auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (ph-AFQMC) has emerged as a promising electronic structure method for correlated electronic systems. However, the quality of its predictions depends critically on the choice of trial wavefunction, and it is not obvious how to make an optimal choice especially for strongly correlated states of large systems. Mean-field wavefunctions are compelling trial wavefunction candidates as they map directly to chemical concepts and can be obtained with $O(N^4)$ cost. Yet in the strongly correlated regime one faces a symmetry dilemma and the existence of multiple nearly-degenerate solutions. In this work we investigate active space models of [2Fe-2S]$^{2+}$, mixed-valent [4Fe-4S]$^{2+}$, and [4Fe-4S]$^{4+}$ and explore the sensitivity of ph-AFQMC to the choice of unrestricted Hartree-Fock trial wavefunction. We find that chemical properties and physical symmetries, rather than the variational energy, ought to guide the choice of mean-field trial for ph-AFQMC (or reference state for coupled cluster models), and show that surprisingly accurate ground-state energies for these systems can be obtained. However, in all cases we find a rapidly vanishing overlap between the stochastic wavefunction and the UHF trial, indicating that the trials are suboptimal importance functions. By analogy to a similar situation in the stretched helium dimer cation, we show how this sampling bias pushes ph-AFQMC towards artificially negative energies, which evidently can be compensated for by the phaseless bias in certain cases.

Summary. The paper introduces the 'Shortest Path in Pauli Forest' algorithm, which optimizes the synthesis of Pauli exponentials by treating decomposition as a path-finding problem. It achieves significant reductions in gate counts and execution time by being aware of the underlying quantum hardware architecture. This is crucial for making quantum chemistry simulations more viable on noisy, near-term quantum devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently decomposing Pauli exponentials into quantum circuits while minimizing CNOT count, CNOT depth, and runtime on NISQ-era hardware with specific connectivity constraints.

Main result. The proposed Shortest Path in Pauli Forest (SPPF) algorithm significantly reduces CNOT count (up to 71%) and runtime (up to 91%) compared to standard methods like Lexicographical Ordering and Qiskit/TKET for both random Pauli strings and molecular ansätze.

Method. A heuristic algorithm that models decomposition as a shortest-path problem in a graph of Clifford tableaus, combined with an architecture-aware initial qubit mapping strategy.

Model / system. The study focuses on NISQ-era quantum computing architectures with various topologies (Line, Grid, Guadalupe, and IBM Brisbane/Mumbai) and uses UCCSD molecular ansätze (e.g., H2, LiH, H2O) as application benchmarks.

Key observables. CNOT count, CNOT depth, and algorithm runtime.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of Pauli gadgets (g), number of qubits (n), and hardware connectivity/topology.

Assumptions / limitations. The algorithm assumes Pauli gadgets are mutually commuting (common in Trotterized Hamiltonians) and utilizes a specific Clifford gate set.

Figures summary. Figures demonstrate the decomposition of single gadgets on line topologies, illustrate the construction of connectivity trees on grid topologies, and provide comparative performance plots for CNOT count and runtime across different hardware architectures.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Pauli exponential decomposition, reviews existing literature, proposes the SPPF algorithm and mapping strategy, presents performance evaluations on random and molecular systems across various topologies, and concludes with a discussion of results.

Abstract

Decomposing Pauli exponentials efficiently to quantum circuits has been the subject of intense research in recent years. Pauli exponentials are an essential component of many different quantum algorithms. Due to the error-prone nature of current and near term quantum devices, it is crucial that quantum circuits are as compact as possible. Several different types of algorithms have been developed to decompose Pauli exponentials into as short circuits as possible. We propose a novel algorithm for architecture-aware synthesis of Pauli exponentials that also determines the initial qubit placement on the device. We call this the Shortest Path in Pauli Forest algorithm. The results show an improved CNOT count and runtime for both random Pauli exponentials and molecular ansätze.

Summary. This article reviews the current state and future challenges in cluster science, focusing on the dynamics of electrons in nanoclusters. It explores how external fields and molecular structure can be used to engineer complex quantum phenomena, ranging from chirality-induced spin selectivity to topological phases in synthetic lattices.

Why it may be interesting. It highlights the use of structured light and microwave dressing to engineer topological properties and tunable interactions in molecular ensembles, which is highly relevant to quantum control and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Identifying fundamental open problems in cluster science, specifically regarding the dynamics of electrons in atomic and molecular nanoclusters and the control of molecular rotations.

Main result. The paper synthesizes various research frontiers, arguing that minimal models and structured light can effectively bridge the gap between single-particle dynamics and complex many-body phenomena in clusters.

Method. The work reviews experimental techniques like mass-selected cluster beams, structured light manipulation, and microwave dressing, alongside theoretical frameworks such as Floquet theory and effective Hamiltonians.

Model / system. The systems discussed include hybrid organic-inorganic perovskites, chiral molecular systems (CISS), ultracold molecules, and metal/boron clusters.

Key observables. Charge transport, spin polarization, rotational spectra, vibrational electron energy loss (EELS) maps, and mass spectra.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin-orbit coupling strength, molecular geometry, pulse sequence/period, and the transition from microscopic to macroscopic regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of minimal models assumes that essential degrees of freedom (rotation, vibration, spin) can capture complex many-body landscapes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of a Size-Selected Neutral cluster experiment; Figure 2 displays mass spectra of Fe-S clusters produced via different ablation methods.

Paper structure. The paper is organized as a collection of perspectives from the 2025 DEAMN workshop, covering topics from molecular rotation control and field-linked molecules to specific studies on electron energy loss spectroscopy and astrochemical sulfur reservoirs.

Abstract

This article is a collection of contributions from speakers at the 2025 DEAMN [Dynamics of Electrons in Atomic and Molecular Nanoclusters] workshop at the Majorana Centre in Erice. Not ordinary contributions to a conference proceeding, this gives a new and different perspective on the work done by the workshop participants.
