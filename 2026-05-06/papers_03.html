Resource-efficient parallel entanglement generation for multinode quantum networks via time-bin multiplexing

Authors: Wenbo Zhang, Jing Zheng, Yimin Wang, Tao Li
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03682v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · Tavis-Cummings & cavity-many-emitter 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes a resource-efficient protocol for generating multiple GHZ states in parallel across a quantum network using time-bin multiplexing. By using a single photon to entangle multiple nodes simultaneously, the method reduces the complexity of optical modulations and the necessary coherence time for stationary qubits. This approach offers a scalable pathway for building large-scale multinode quantum networks.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a scalable architecture for quantum networks that drastically relaxes the stringent requirements on qubit coherence and experimental control, which is highly relevant for developing practical quantum repeaters and distributed quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Generating multipartite entanglement across multiple remote quantum nodes is limited by exponential photon loss in optical fibers and requires high coherence times and complex control.

Main result. The proposed protocol enables parallel generation of M GHZ states among N nodes using time-bin multiplexing, significantly reducing the required coherence time for stationary qubits and the complexity of photonic modulations.

Method. A time-bin qudit-encoding protocol where a single photon interacts with cavity-qubit units via optical switches, inducing controlled-phase flips, followed by measurement in a generalized X-basis.

Model / system. A network of N spatially separated nodes, each containing M stationary qubits coupled to single-sided cavities, modeled using a four-level atom system and cavity-QED dynamics.

Key observables. Average fidelity (F_M,N) and average efficiency (eta_M,N) of the generated GHZ states.

Important parameters / regimes. Node distance (L0), photon qudit dimension (d=2M), cooperativity (Cs), detuning (Delta_s), and qubit coherence times (T1, T2).

Assumptions / limitations. The dimension of the photonic qudit is strictly tied to the number of parallel GHZ states being generated (d=2M).

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the protocol for M=2 and N=3, illustrating the photon's path through nodes A, B, and C and its interaction with cavity-qubit units.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of scaling quantum networks, proposes a time-bin multiplexing protocol, describes the physical model and interaction mechanism, presents numerical results for fidelity and efficiency, and compares the protocol's advantages against conventional schemes.

Abstract

Nonlocal entanglement generation among multiple remote quantum nodes provides a critical foundation for a variety of counterintuitive quantum applications. The exponential loss of photons transmitting over optical fibers sets an upper limit for entangling these quantum nodes. Here, we propose a resource-efficient and parallel protocol for entangling multiple remote quantum nodes via time-bin multiplexing. The transmission of a single photon with qudit-encoding in the time-bin mode enables entangling multiple stationary qubits in parallel, when single photons and individual stationary qubits interfaces are used and photon-state modulations are properly introduced before subsequently impinging the photon into each interface. Our protocol can generate parallel multipartite entanglement among ($N\geq3$) quantum nodes with the dimension of the photonic time bins independent of $N$, exponentially reducing the requirements for the coherence time of the stationary qubits and for the complexity of the photonic modulations. These distinct features make our protocol particularly advantageous for the development of multinode quantum networks.

The Geometric Part of Decoherence: Quasi-Orthogonality in High-Dimensional Hilbert Spaces

Authors: Karl Svozil
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03807v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper identifies a geometric mechanism for decoherence based on the fact that in high-dimensional Hilbert spaces, almost all states are nearly orthogonal. This 'kinematic' property provides an enormous capacity for environmental records, ensuring that macroscopic quantum superpositions are suppressed. The effect is most robust in chaotic systems where the accessible Hilbert space dimension is exponentially large.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental geometric perspective on the emergence of classicality and the effectiveness of decoherence, which is highly relevant for researchers studying open quantum systems and the transition from quantum to classical regimes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the geometric/kinematic contribution to decoherence, specifically addressing why macroscopic quantum alternatives do not blur into a featureless state in high-dimensional Hilbert spaces.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that high-dimensional Hilbert spaces possess an exponentially large capacity for mutually quasi-orthogonal environmental records, providing a geometric mechanism that suppresses macroscopic interference.

Method. The study employs high-dimensional geometry, including the concentration of measure on spheres, Lévy's Lemma, and the Beta distribution to analyze the overlap of Haar-random vectors.

Model / system. A system coupled to a macroscopic environment modeled as a high-dimensional Hilbert space, where the effective dimension scales with the microcanonical entropy of the environment.

Key observables. The squared overlap between environmental branch states, ||^2, and the survival probability of significant overlaps.

Important parameters / regimes. The dimension (d) of the Hilbert space, the epsilon-quasi-orthogonality threshold, and the effective dimension (deff) related to microcanonical entropy.

Assumptions / limitations. The argument is conditional on the dynamics being able to populate the available geometric reservoir; it assumes the Hamiltonian drives the system into typical regions of the accessible subspace.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the geometric mechanism of quasi-orthogonality, then distinguishes between dynamical and kinematic ingredients of decoherence, and finally examines how dynamical constraints (like integrability or MBL) affect the effective dimension and the validity of the geometric reservoir.

Abstract

We isolate a geometric mechanism that complements the dynamical suppression of macroscopic interference: In a high-dimensional Hilbert space, almost all state vectors are nearly orthogonal, accommodating an exponentially large reservoir of mutually quasi-orthogonal environmental records. This geometry explains why macroscopic alternatives fail to exhibit visible interference once such records are populated. The argument is conditional and finite-dimensional, and it leaves the interpretive core of quantum mechanics untouched: geometry alone does not select a pointer basis, does not guarantee that a given Hamiltonian drives the system into typical regions of the accessible subspace, and does not turn an improper mixture into a proper one. It merely supplies the vast Hilbert-space capacity that makes decoherence so overwhelmingly effective for all practical purposes.

Adversarial Effects on Expressibility and Trainability in Distributed Variational Quantum Algorithms

Authors: Abhishek Sadhu, Sharu Theresa Jose
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03629v1

Summary. This paper explores how an adversary can corrupt the shared entanglement used in distributed quantum computing to sabotage variational algorithms. It introduces a new metric called Kraus expressibility to quantify how noise affects the ability of a circuit to represent solutions and shows that attackers can bias results without triggering the vanishing gradients known as barren plateaus.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum networks as it provides a rigorous framework for understanding how structured noise in quantum communication channels directly degrades the performance and security of distributed quantum algorithms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how adversarial perturbations to shared entanglement in distributed quantum computing impact the expressibility and trainability of Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs).

Main result. The authors demonstrate that an adversary can manipulate entanglement-level noise to bias optimization toward incorrect solutions while avoiding detection via barren plateaus, and they establish a new metric, Kraus expressibility, to quantify this effect.

Method. The study uses a Kraus representation framework to map entanglement perturbations to gate-level noise, introduces the Kraus expressibility norm, and employs gradient variance analysis to link noise to trainability.

Model / system. A distributed quantum architecture consisting of multiple NISQ processors connected via a quantum repeater network using the cat-entangler/cat-disentangler protocol for non-local CNOT gates.

Key observables. Kraus expressibility norm, cost function gradient variance, concurrence, and state purity.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (L), number of qubits (n), degree of parameter initialization confinement (r), and entanglement concurrence (C).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on adversarial attacks via the entanglement-sharing layer and assumes the use of the cat-entangler protocol for non-local gates.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates different adversarial models (network vs. device level) and the restriction of solution space; Figure 2 shows energy convergence failure in VQE under adversarial noise; Figure 3 shows the decay of Kraus expressibility and gradient variance with depth and noise; Figure 4 shows the scaling of gradient variance with qubit number under different noise levels.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the vulnerability of distributed VQAs, introduces a mathematical framework for mapping entanglement noise to Kraus operators, defines the new Kraus expressibility metric, provides analytical bounds on gradient variance, and validates the theory through numerical simulations of VQE and scaling analysis.

Abstract

Distributed quantum algorithms offer a promising pathway to scale variational quantum algorithms beyond the constraints of noisy intermediate-scale quantum hardware. However, existing approaches implicitly assume a trusted entanglement-sharing layer across quantum processors. We show that this assumption introduces a fundamental vulnerability: adversarial perturbations of shared entanglement induce structured gate-level noise that directly impacts quantum learning. We develop a framework that maps entanglement-level perturbations to gate-level noise via an explicit Kraus representation. To quantify their impact, we introduce Kraus expressibility, a metric that generalizes unitary expressibility to noisy quantum channels. We then establish a trade-off between Kraus expressibility and trainability of noisy quantum circuits through gradient variance analysis. Our analysis reveals that an adversary can manipulate Kraus expressibility to maintain sufficiently large cost gradients (avoiding barren plateaus) while systematically biasing optimization toward incorrect solutions. We validate these findings through numerical simulations, demonstrating adversarial degradation of expressibility and trainability.

Summary. The authors present a Graph Neural Network that operates directly on gauge-invariant Wilson loops to simulate Abelian lattice gauge theories. The model efficiently predicts both static properties and semiclassical dynamics, offering a powerful tool for studying complex gauge-matter interactions without the overhead of redundant gauge degrees of freedom.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a scalable way to simulate the time evolution of gauge-matter systems, bypassing the computational bottleneck of repeated exact diagonalization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient machine learning framework for Abelian lattice gauge theories that handles gauge redundancy and captures nonlocal correlations induced by gauge-matter coupling.

Main result. The proposed gauge-invariant GNN accurately predicts local and global observables in Z2 and U(1) models and serves as a scalable surrogate for semiclassical dynamics, capturing statistical properties like autocorrelation functions.

Method. A Graph Neural Network (GNN) architecture using gauge-invariant Wilson loop inputs and message passing, combined with automatic differentiation to reconstruct gauge-covariant forces for dynamics.

Model / system. Abelian lattice gauge theories (Z2 and U(1)) and U(1) quantum link models on a square lattice, featuring gauge-matter coupling via a tight-binding Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Wilson loops (plaquette and winding), local fermion density, total energy, and autocorrelation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Inverse temperature (beta), chemical potential (mu), kinetic scale (tau), electric coupling (g), and magnetic strength (K).

Assumptions / limitations. The architecture is specifically tailored for Abelian theories and assumes the gauge-invariant sector can be captured by local and global Wilson loops; dynamical results are currently limited to the semiclassical regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the GNN architecture, showing the mapping from gauge configurations to Wilson loops and the message-passing process. Figure 2 contains heat maps comparing GNN predictions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a gauge-invariant GNN architecture, benchmarks its performance on static observables in Z2 and U(1) models, and then demonstrates its utility as a surrogate for semiclassical time evolution in quantum link models.

Abstract

Local gauge structures play a central role in a wide range of condensed matter systems and synthetic quantum platforms, where they emerge as effective descriptions of strongly correlated phases and engineered dynamics. We introduce a gauge-invariant graph neural network (GNN) architecture for Abelian lattice gauge models, in which symmetry is enforced explicitly through local gauge-invariant inputs, such as Wilson loops, and preserved throughout message passing, eliminating redundant gauge degrees of freedom while retaining expressive power. We benchmark the approach on both $\mathbb{Z}_2$ and $\mathrm{U}(1)$ lattice gauge models, achieving accurate predictions of global observables and spatially resolved quantities despite the nonlocal correlations induced by gauge-matter coupling. We further demonstrate that the learned model serves as an efficient surrogate for semiclassical dynamics in $\mathrm{U}(1)$ quantum link models, enabling stable and scalable time evolution without repeated fermionic diagonalization, while faithfully reproducing both local dynamics and statistical correlations. These results establish gauge-invariant message passing as a compact and physically grounded framework for learning and simulating Abelian lattice gauge systems.

Phase-Reference Control of Steady-State Entanglement in Open Quantum Systems

Authors: Areeda Ayoub, Alfonso Castillo-Gonzalez, Eric R Bittner
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03978v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper shows that the phase of a squeezed reservoir can be used to control steady-state entanglement in open quantum systems. It demonstrates that local dissipation can generate entanglement when paired with coherent coupling, and that the effectiveness of this process depends on the phase reference of the reservoir. This establishes phase-sensitive reservoir engineering as a viable route for creating entanglement in continuous-variable quantum technologies.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new mechanism for reservoir engineering, showing that the phase of the environment is a controllable knob for generating nonlocal correlations in continuous-variable systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the phase reference of a phase-sensitive reservoir can be used to control and generate steady-state entanglement in open quantum systems.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that local, phase-sensitive dissipation can generate entanglement when combined with coherent coupling, and that the resulting entanglement structure depends explicitly on the reservoir's phase reference.

Method. A covariance-matrix approach for Gaussian-preserving dynamics is used to analyze the steady state of the system.

Model / system. Continuous-variable open quantum systems subject to phase-sensitive (squeezed) dissipation and coherent coupling.

Key observables. Steady-state entanglement (quantified via entanglement measures for Gaussian states) and thermal robustness of entanglement.

Important parameters / regimes. Squeezing strength, coherent coupling strength, and the phase reference of the reservoir (comparing phase-locked/rotating-frame vs. laboratory-frame implementations).

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics are assumed to be Gaussian-preserving, allowing for the use of the covariance-matrix formalism.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of phase-reference control, applies a covariance-matrix approach to Gaussian dynamics, analyzes the role of squeezing and coupling, compares different phase-reference frames, and concludes with the implications for reservoir engineering.

Abstract

We show that steady-state entanglement in open quantum systems is controlled by the phase reference of a phase-sensitive reservoir. Using a covariance-matrix approach for Gaussian-preserving dynamics, we demonstrate that purely local, phase-sensitive dissipation can generate entanglement when combined with coherent coupling. The steady state exhibits a finite entangled region with an optimal squeezing strength that maximizes both the magnitude and thermal robustness of entanglement. We find that coherent coupling does not enhance entanglement monotonically, but instead regulates the conversion of local squeezing into nonlocal correlations. Importantly, the coupling dependence is controlled by the phase reference of the squeezed reservoir: phase-locked (rotating-frame) and laboratory-frame implementations yield qualitatively distinct steady states and entanglement structure. These results establish phase-sensitive reservoir engineering as a controllable route to steady-state entanglement in continuous-variable systems. Steady-state entanglement in phase-sensitive open systems depends explicitly on the reservoir phase reference and is not invariant under changes of that reference.}

Summary. The researchers developed a new way to design optomechanical crystals that are attached to their substrate rather than suspended. By using an advanced inverse-design algorithm, they achieved extremely high light-matter coupling rates that rival suspended devices while preventing the device from overheating. This makes it a much more robust platform for quantum technologies.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a scalable, thermally stable platform for quantum transducers and microwave-to-optical frequency conversion, which are essential for building large-scale quantum networks.

Main result. The demonstration of a release-free silicon optomechanical crystal with a record vacuum coupling rate of 800 kHz, achieving performance comparable to suspended devices while maintaining superior thermal stability.

Method. A combination of the X-HOPE physics-guided technique to match wavevectors and a multiphysics gradient-based inverse-design algorithm using adjoint sensitivity analysis.

Model / system. A release-free silicon-on-insulator (220 nm Si on 2 um SiO2) optomechanical crystal architecture that is mechanically attached to the substrate to allow for efficient heat dissipation.

Key observables. Vacuum optomechanical coupling rate (g_OM), optomechanical scattering rate (Gamma_OM), optical quality factor (Q_o), and thermo-optic frequency shift.

Important parameters / regimes. g_OM/2pi approx 800 kHz, mechanical frequency 5.25 GHz, optical frequency 193.5 THz, and room-temperature operation.

Assumptions / limitations. The design assumes the ability to mitigate fabrication errors through stochastic erosion/dilation and focuses on radiation-limited performance as a benchmark.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows mode overlap and band structure for the X-HOPE design; Figure 2 illustrates the inverse-design algorithm workflow; Figure 3 presents experimental spectral data and coupling measurements; Figure 4 compares thermal stability across different architectures.

Paper structure. Abstract

Interactions between light and mechanics provide a powerful interface between optical and microwave-frequency signals, with applications spanning classical signal processing and quantum technologies. High-performance optomechanical devices require both strong photon-phonon coupling and tolerance to parasitic laser heating. Release-free optomechanical crystals provide improved thermal anchoring compared to suspended nanobeams, but have so far exhibited weaker vacuum optomechanical coupling rates, leaving a trade-off between coupling strength and thermal robustness. Here, we largely close this gap: we design and experimentally demonstrate a release-free silicon optomechanical crystal with a record vacuum optomechanical coupling rate of about $g_\text{OM} / (2 π) = 800$ kHz, comparable to suspended state-of-the-art devices. The resulting optomechanical scattering rate $Γ_\text{OM}/(2 π)= 1.1$ kHz is nearly twice that of previous release-free implementations. This performance is achieved by combining physics-guided human intuition with a multiphysics inverse-design algorithm introduced here for resonant optomechanical structures. Beyond the specific device demonstrated, the inverse-design framework is applicable to co-optimizing optical and mechanical resonances and eigenmodes more broadly. These results strengthen release-free optomechanical crystals as a platform for fast, low-noise classical and quantum optomechanics.

Summary. This paper introduces the Coordination Principle, stating that perfect agreement among multiple parties implies a shared common cause. It proves that quantum mechanics obeys this principle even in complex causal networks and provides new inequalities to experimentally certify common causes in multipartite GHZ states.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum networks and quantum optics as it provides new experimental tools (Bell-like inequalities) to certify the existence of shared quantum resources and common causes in multipartite systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether the 'Coordination Principle'—the idea that perfect agreement on a random output among N parties requires a shared common cause—is a fundamental causal principle or merely a consequence of No-Signaling and Independence.

Main result. The authors prove that quantum theory satisfies the Coordination Principle in any causal network, even with intermediate transformations, and derive noise-tolerant Bell-like inequalities to certify the presence of a common cause.

Method. The study employs the 'Quantum Inflation' technique to expand causal structures, uses the NPA hierarchy for semidefinite programming, and constructs a counterexample using an Operational Probabilistic Theory (OPT) to show the principle is independent of No-Signaling.

Model / system. The framework uses Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) to represent causal structures, specifically studying multipartite GHZ states and complex scenarios like the two-layer tetrahedron model.

Key observables. Correlations between adjacent parties (e.g., ) and end-to-end correlations (e.g., ), as well as marginal probabilities.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of parties (N), the white noise visibility parameter (v), and the fidelity (f) of the GHZ state.

Assumptions / limitations. The proofs assume the use of pure states, unitary transformations, and projective measurements; it also assumes the possibility of device replication in certain contexts.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate various causal DAG structures, the quantum cut inflation construction, diagrammatic representations of tests and circuits in the counterexample OPT, and plots showing the violation of inequalities as a function of noise visibility.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Coordination Principle, proves quantum theory satisfies it using inflation techniques, provides a counterexample OPT where the principle fails, derives noise-robust Bell-like inequalities, and applies these to GHZ state certification.

Abstract

We introduce the coordination principle, which states that perfect coordination, in the form of agreement on a uniformly random output, among N parties is possible only if they share a common cause. This principle is purely causal and can be viewed as a multipartite generalization of Reichenbach's common cause principle. We prove that quantum information theory satisfy the coordination principle in any network, and derive noise-tolerant Bell-like inequalities that certify the presence of a common cause. We further show that the principle is not a consequence of no-signaling and independence alone by constructing a concrete operational probabilistic theory that obeys both principles while still allowing perfect coordination without a common cause. This possibility arises only in fully general causal scenarios with intermediate transformations between preparations and measurements. We also formulate a genuinely quantum coordination task, showing that the preparation of a multipartite GHZ state requires a quantum common cause, which can be certified by Bell-like inequalities which are experimentally testable. Finally, we discuss the open problem of finding a quantitative, noise-tolerant version of the coordination principle that constrains approximate coordination in any reasonable causal theory. This work is the extended version of the more compact letter and provides all the technical details of the proofs.

Authors: Sonia, Satyabrata Adhikari

Summary. This paper presents a method to transform standard linear entanglement witnesses into more powerful non-linear versions. These new operators can detect a wider range of entangled states, including difficult-to-identify positive partial transpose entangled states, and are designed to be implementable using local measurements.

Why it may be interesting. This is relevant for quantum information theorists as it provides a practical, experimentally realizable method to identify complex entanglement (like bound entanglement) that is otherwise invisible to standard linear detection methods.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of detecting all types of entanglement, particularly Positive Partial Transpose Entangled States (PPTES), using traditional Linear Entanglement Witnesses (LEW) which often fail to detect certain entangled states.

Main result. The authors provide a systematic constructive approach to derive Non-Linear Entanglement Witnesses (NLEW) from existing LEWs, demonstrating that these NLEWs can detect a larger class of both NPTES and PPTES.

Method. A constructive mathematical framework using polynomial functional forms of LEWs, involving determinants, partial traces, and trace norms to transform linear operators into non-linear ones.

Model / system. Bipartite quantum systems in arbitrary dimensions (d1 x d2), with specific analyses performed on qubit-qubit (2x2), qubit-qutrit (2x3), and qutrit-qutrit (3x3) systems.

Key observables. Expectation value of the witness operator (Tr(W rho)), which indicates entanglement if negative.

Important parameters / regimes. Parameters defining specific state families such as alpha (isotropic states), p (LEW parameter), gamma (qutrit isotropic states), and x (qutrit PPTES parameter).

Assumptions / limitations. The construction assumes the existence of a known LEW and relies on the assumption that the maximum of a sum of expectation values is bounded by the sum of their individual maximums.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the functional form of NLEWs, provides specific constructions for NPTES, utilizes CCNR and De Vicente criteria for construction, presents a new method for PPTES detection, and concludes with a discussion on experimental realization via local observables.

Abstract

Entanglement detection is one of the important problems in quantum information theory. To deal with this problem, many entanglement detection criteria have been proposed. Among the proposed criteria, the detection of entanglement through witness operator (also known as linear entanglement witness (LEW) operator) may be considered as the most practical. Although the witness operator approach to detect entanglement is experimentally friendly, the construction of these operators is not a very simple task. Even if we are able to construct a LEW operator, our problem is not solved as it may either detect a few entangled states or not a single entangled state from a given family of entangled states. Thus, we need a constructive approach in order to tackle this type of problem. In this work, we provide a few constructions of the non-linear entanglement witnesses (NLEW) for $d_1\otimes d_2$ dimensional system from any linear entanglement witness (LEW) operator. The advantage of these constructions is that, if a LEW is unable to detect any particular entangled state described by the density operator $ρ^{ent}$ then our construction of NLEW may detect the same entangled state $ρ^{ent}$. Further, we have constructed NLEW operator that may detect not only a class of bipartite negative partial transpose entangled state (NPTES), but also positive partial transpose entangled state (PPTES). Moreover, we have shown that the constructed NLEW operators may be decomposed in terms of the tensor product of local observables and hence may be realizable in an experiment.

Coordination Requires a Common Cause in Quantum Theory

Method. An enhanced asymmetric CV-MDI-QKD protocol using Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) oscillator-to-oscillator codes and a one-by-one concatenation method to extract error syndromes via ancilla measurements.

Model / system. An asymmetric CV-MDI-QKD network topology featuring an untrusted relay connecting Alice and Bob, utilizing coherent states, GKP ancilla modes, and Two-Mode Squeezed (TMS) operations across fiber and wireless (fading) channels.

Key observables. Secret key rate (asymptotic and composable finite-size), residual error variance, mutual information, and Holevo information.

Important parameters / regimes. GKP squeezing levels (e.g., 20 dB), channel transmittance, fiber attenuation (0.2 dB/km), and number of concatenation layers.

Assumptions / limitations. Security is analyzed against collective Gaussian attacks using the quantum de Finetti theorem; the use of a linear estimation function for error syndromes instead of MMSE.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the CV-MDI-QKD limitation, proposes a GKP-based error correction scheme using TMS operations, provides a detailed security analysis under collective attacks, evaluates performance in fiber and free-space (fading) channels, and discusses the optimization of concatenated layers.

Abstract

It is well known that the repeater node is an essential ingredient for the future global quantum network, which will enable high-rate private communication and entanglement distribution over very long distances. The near-term repeater architecture uses the measurement-based node that operate without both entanglement and quantum memory, which is the main idea of the measurement-device-independent quantum key distribution (MDI-QKD) protocol. The MDI-QKD protocol removes the trust condition from the inter repeaters, while its continuous variable (CV) version, when proposed, benefited from its deterministic nature, compatible with the classical devices, and shows a high rate for the short-range local area network (LAN). Whilst the theoretical backbone of CV-MDI-QKD protocol is well established, its secure transmission range is yet limited for practical LAN. In this study, we propose an enhanced scheme for the asymmetric CV-MDI-QKD protocol by using Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) oscillators-to-oscillators codes, where both loss error and operation error are suppressed to below the break-even point, without any delays caused by classical heralding signals. In particular, the proposed scheme, which correlates the noises of the data and ancilla via a pair of symplectic transforms, extracts the error syndromes from stabilizer measurements on the ancilla mode and informs the data mode for a corrective displacement. Numerical analysis shows the composable finite-size security of the protocol under the collective Gaussian attack, encompassing noiseless and noisy GKP states, with both wired (i.e., fiber-based) and wireless (i.e., free-space) configurations. In addition, we demonstrate that the residual errors of the GKP code can be further reduced by the concatenation method but has a trade-off between the layers number and the finite GKP squeezing.
