Preparing High-Fidelity Thermofield Double States

Authors: Brian J. J. Khor, Nadie LiTenn, Martin Sasieta, Brian Swingle
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03017v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · analog quantum simulation 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper presents a method to prepare high-fidelity Thermofield Double states via adiabatic evolution of a parent Hamiltonian. By adding a penalty term to suppress energy leakage, the authors demonstrate that the fidelity loss typically associated with increasing system size can be mitigated. This provides a robust framework for preparing thermal states in both local and non-local quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and quantum computing as it provides a scalable protocol for preparing complex thermal and purified states essential for studying quantum gravity and thermal physics on near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently preparing high-fidelity Thermofield Double (TFD) states on quantum computers, specifically addressing the exponential decay of fidelity with system size in standard parent Hamiltonian constructions.

Main result. The authors propose a modified parent Hamiltonian with an energy-mismatch penalty term that suppresses off-diagonal leakage, thereby reducing or removing the exponential decay of fidelity as the system size increases.

Method. The study uses adiabatic evolution, Matrix Product States (MPS), DMRG, and Exact Diagonalization (ED) to analyze the spectral properties and fidelity of the proposed Hamiltonian.

Model / system. The method is applied to various models including 1D and 2D mixed-field Ising models, spin Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev (SYK) models, and the Maldacena-Qi wormhole model.

Key observables. Fidelity (overlap between ground state and target TFD), optimal inverse temperature (beta*), spectral gap, and off-diagonal leakage weight.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength (J), number of qubits (N), number of coupling operators (K), penalty strength (xi), and scaling dimension (Delta_O).

Assumptions / limitations. The target systems must obey the Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis (ETH) and have a bounded energy spectrum without thermal phase transitions at the target temperature.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the parent Hamiltonian construction; Figure 3 shows scaling of temperature and fidelity in 1D Ising chains; Figure 4 shows ED results for SYK; Figure 6 demonstrates the saturation of fidelity during adiabatic evolution.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the TFD preparation problem, presents the parent Hamiltonian construction, identifies the fidelity decay problem, proposes a penalty-term solution, and validates the method across 1D, 2D, and non-local (SYK) models using numerical and analytical techniques.

Abstract

A major promise of quantum computers is the controlled preparation of many-body quantum states beyond the reach of efficient classical computation. Among the most important targets are thermal mixed states and their thermofield double (TFD) purifications, which play central roles in quantum many-body physics and quantum gravity. For target systems with a bounded energy spectrum that obey the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH), we present a parent Hamiltonian built from two copies of the target Hamiltonian and ultra-local couplings between the copies, which we argue is gapped with a ground state that approximates a TFD state of the target Hamiltonian. By adiabatically evolving down from strong coupling, we can thus prepare a high-fidelity TFD state. We study two variants of the parent Hamiltonian using numerical methods in two classes of models: mixed field Ising models in one and two dimensions and non-local "spin Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev'' models. In the simpler variant, the parent Hamiltonian ground state has high overlap with a TFD for system sizes accessible to near-term quantum devices. However, the global overlap decays exponentially with the number of qubits, with a small error per degree of freedom. The second variant introduces an additional penalty term which can be tuned to reduce or remove the decay of the overlap with system size. Together with a general ETH-based analysis, these results suggest a broadly applicable method for TFD preparation that is not limited to particular temperatures or geometric locality.

Summary. The paper addresses the estimation bias in time-dependent variational Monte Carlo caused by zeros in the wavefunction. By introducing a cutoff-based importance sampling method, the authors achieve more accurate and efficient simulations of quantum quench dynamics. They also investigate using Tensor Cross Interpolation as an alternative, though its effectiveness is currently limited by the complexity of the neural network ansatz.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a way to maintain accuracy in real-time evolution simulations where wavefunctions encounter nodes, a common occurrence in non-equilibrium quantum dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Traditional time-dependent variational Monte Carlo (t-VMC) suffers from a systematic estimation bias when the wavefunction contains nodes (roots), which leads to inaccurate real-time dynamics.

Main result. The authors propose an unbiased variant of t-VMC using a cutoff-based deformation of the Born distribution that significantly improves accuracy and reduces sample requirements. They also explore a Tensor Cross Interpolation (TCI) strategy, though they find it limited by the lack of low-rank properties in certain architectures.

Method. Two approaches are investigated: (1) self-normalized importance sampling with a cutoff-based deformation of the Born distribution, and (2) an active learning strategy using Tensor Cross Interpolation (TCI) to approximate the TDVP equations.

Model / system. The study focuses on the 1D Transverse Field Ising Model (TFIM) undergoing quench dynamics, as well as a single-spin system undergoing Rabi oscillations.

Key observables. Quantum Geometric Tensor (QGT), force vector, local energy, magnetization, and infidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. The cutoff parameter epsilon (where epsilon > 0 avoids bias), bond dimension (chi), and lattice size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The TCI-based approach assumes the existence of a low-rank property in the tensor network architecture, which the authors find is not present in their specific ResNet/RBM setup.

Figures summary. Figure 1 and 2 illustrate the MPS and TCI structures; Figure 3 shows the failure of standard Born sampling in single-spin dynamics; Figure 6 demonstrates the relative error of the force vector and QGT as a function of time and system size.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the bias problem, proposes two solutions (importance sampling and TCI), tests them on a single-spin system and the TFIM, evaluates error metrics and scalability, and discusses limitations of the TCI approach.

Abstract

When combined with highly expressive ansatz functions such as neural quantum states, variational Monte Carlo (VMC) constitutes a versatile numerical approach to tackle the quantum many-body problem in and out of equilibrium. However, its traditional formulation exhibits a subtle estimation bias leading to inaccuracies, which can be particularly detrimental when addressing real time dynamics. In this work, we investigate two avenues to circumvent said estimation bias. First, we propose an unbiased variant of time-dependent VMC using self-normalized importance sampling with respect to a cutoff-based deformation of the Born distribution. We demonstrate the feasibility and accuracy of the approach in pathological and generic cases of quench dynamics. Furthermore, we explore an alternative sampling strategy based on active learning via the tensor cross interpolation (TCI). While we find that our choice of tensor network architecture lacks the required low rank property, the proposed TCI-based algorithm complements the conventional importance sampling paradigm, providing an alternative perspective that may be further explored in future work.

General perturbation theory for local quantum uncertainty and its formulation in the linear-response regime

Authors: A. A. Jimenez-Romero, F. Rojas
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03248v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes a general perturbative framework to calculate Local Quantum Uncertainty under external driving. It demonstrates that periodic perturbations can resonantly enhance quantum discord at temperatures where entanglement has already vanished, showing that frequency can be used to tune nonclassical correlations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical tool to control and tune quantum correlations (discord) using external driving frequencies, which is highly relevant for controlling nonclassicality in driven-dissipative systems and quantum optomechanics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a general perturbation theory for Local Quantum Uncertainty (LQU) and characterizing how external periodic perturbations modulate discord-type nonclassicality in the linear-response regime.

Main result. The authors derive an explicit first-order expansion for the density matrix square root and show that an external field can resonantly enhance quantum discord even above the entanglement critical temperature, acting as a tunable modulator of nonclassicality.

Method. The study uses a Stieltjes integral representation (based on the gamma function) to expand the density matrix square root and reduces the LQU optimization to the diagonalization of a matrix defined via SU(d1) generators within the quantum linear-response framework.

Model / system. The theory is applicable to bipartite composite systems of arbitrary dimension, with a specific illustrative application to an isotropic Heisenberg model of two coupled spins subject to a local periodic magnetic field.

Key observables. Local Quantum Uncertainty (LQU), Wigner-Yanase skew information, and concurrence.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), driving frequency (omega), exchange coupling (J), perturbation strength (xi), and the broadening parameter (delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes a small perturbation (first-order expansion in epsilon) and that the optimization is performed over local observables within the SU(d1) algebra.

Figures summary. Figures show LQU as a function of temperature and frequency, highlighting resonant enhancement near the exchange coupling frequency and a comparison between LQU and concurrence to demonstrate discord-type correlations above the entanglement threshold.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the LQU framework, develops the mathematical perturbation theory for the density matrix square root, applies the theory to the linear-response regime, and concludes with an application to the driven Heisenberg model to demonstrate frequency-dependent modulation of nonclassicality.

Abstract

We develop a general perturbation theory for the local quantum uncertainty (LQU), a discord-type quantifier of nonclassicality based on the Wigner-Yanase skew information. Starting from a perturbed density matrix $ρ= ρ_0 + ερ_1$,we derive an explicit first-order expansion of $ρ^{1/2}$ using an integral representation based on the gamma function, and reduce the LQU optimization to the diagonalization of a $(d_1^2-1) \times (d_1^2-1)$ matrix $w = w^0 + w^1$ defined in terms of the $\mathrm{SU}(d_1)$ generators. The framework is valid for composite systems of arbitrary dimension $d_1 \times d_2$ and provides a direct computational route to the LQU from the spectral decomposition of the unperturbed state. We further specialize the theory to the quantum linear response regime, where the perturbation is generated by a time-dependent external field, and $w^1$ acquires explicit dependence on the driving frequency $ω$, the eigenstates and occupation probabilities of the equilibrium Hamiltonian $H_0$, and the matrix elements of the coupling operator $\hat{A}$. As an illustration, we apply the formalism to the isotropic Heisenberg model of two coupled spins driven by a local periodic magnetic field, obtaining closed-form expressions for the LQU as a function of temperature $T$ and frequency $ω$. Comparison with the concurrence shows that above the entanglement critical temperature $T_c$, the external field induces a resonantly enhanced quantum discord without generating entanglement, demonstrating that frequency acts as a tunable modulator of nonclassicality -- an effect of purely quantum-discord type inaccessible to entanglement-based quantifiers.

Path integral quantization of the electromagnetic field in nonlinear dielectric materials

Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03836v1

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical framework for the quantum electrodynamics of nonlinear, dispersive, and absorbing media. By using path integrals to integrate out matter and bath degrees of freedom, the authors derive the Feynman rules and effective action for light in a nonlinear dielectric. The result connects microscopic anharmonicity to macroscopic nonlinear optical responses like the Kerr effect.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems as it addresses a fundamental gap in macroscopic QED: the simultaneous treatment of nonlinearities and dissipation within a unified, consistent framework.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a consistent quantum field theory for light in nonlinear dielectric media that simultaneously accounts for dispersion, absorption, and nonlinear optical processes (like Kerr nonlinearity) within a consistent fluctuation-dissipation framework.

Main result. The authors derive an effective action and Feynman rules for the electromagnetic field in a 'dressed vacuum,' establishing a relation between the third-order susceptibility and the matter-field anharmonicity that is consistent with Miller's rule.

Method. Path-integral quantization using a functional integral formalism to integrate out matter and reservoir degrees of freedom, followed by perturbation theory and the use of the Legendre transform to derive the effective action.

Model / system. A mesoscopic extension of the Huttner-Barnett model where the electromagnetic field interacts with a matter field (harmonic oscillators) which is further coupled to a reservoir (bath) to model dissipation, including a quartic self-interaction term to represent Kerr nonlinearity.

Key observables. Nonlinear susceptibility (chi-3), displacement field (D), memory kernel (Gamma), and photon-photon propagators.

Important parameters / regimes. Nonlinear coupling constant (lambda), linear susceptibility (chi-1), and the memory kernel (Gamma) representing temporal nonlocality.

Assumptions / limitations. The medium is assumed to be isotropic (vanishing chi-2), the nonlinearity is weak (lambda << 1) to justify perturbation theory, and the derivation uses the Weyl gauge.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the Huttner-Barnett model (EM field, matter, and reservoir coupling); Figure 2 displays the tree-level propagators (AA, XX, and AX*); Figure 3 illustrates the four-point vertex function.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the microscopic Huttner-Barnett model, proceeds to perform path-integral quantization by integrating out the reservoir and matter fields, derives the effective action and Feynman rules, and concludes by expressing the macroscopic displacement field and nonlinear susceptibilities.

Abstract

We construct a quantum theory of light in nonlinear dielectric media with dispersion and absorption. We employ a mesoscopic model for the light-matter interaction that include a fourth-order nonlinearity in the material response. Quantization is performed by constructing an effective action in a path-integral formalism by integrating out matter and bath degrees of freedom. We show how a nonlinear response function associated with Kerr nonlinearity is obtained through the model and, after full field quantization, we derive the Feynman rules from this theory.

Variational Joint Magnetometry and Gradiometry on Dipolar Spin Chains

Authors: Priyam Srivastava, Xin Jin, Junyu Liu, Gurudev Dutt, Tom Purdy, Kang Kim, Kaushik P. Seshadreesan
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03906v1

Summary. The paper presents a variational method to optimize quantum probes for the simultaneous sensing of magnetic fields and gradients in spin chains. It demonstrates that by using a specific 'four-string' entangled state, one can significantly outperform the Standard Quantum Limit. The study also quantifies how the complexity of the quantum circuit and measurement decoder affects sensing precision.

Why it may be interesting. It addresses a fundamental multi-parameter estimation challenge in quantum sensing, providing a concrete example of how entanglement can be structured to overcome the limitations of standard GHZ-based protocols in many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Simultaneous estimation of a uniform magnetic field and its spatial gradient in a dipolar-coupled spin chain, specifically avoiding the 'GHZ collapse' where the GHZ state becomes blind to one parameter.

Main result. The variational approach achieves a 4.2x advantage over the Standard Quantum Limit (SQL) at N=5 and identifies a 'four-string motif' as the optimal probe state structure.

Method. A variational framework using a hardware-motivated layered dipolar circuit ansatz, optimized via the CMA-ES algorithm, with a multi-tier decoder hierarchy.

Model / system. A 1D chain of N dipolar-coupled spin-1/2 systems (modeled after NV-center arrays) with a magnetic field model consisting of a bias B0 and a gradient g.

Key observables. Determinant of the Classical Fisher Information Matrix (det(F)), Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM), and the Standard Quantum Limit (SQL).

Important parameters / regimes. Chain length N, circuit depth L, and the encoding generators G_B0 and G_g.

Assumptions / limitations. The encoding generators are assumed to be diagonal in the computational basis, and the system is analyzed in the linear regime of the field expansion.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the linear chain geometry; Figure 3 illustrates the probability distribution of the optimized probe state across Dicke sectors; Figure 4 and 5 show the performance scaling and saturation deficit across different decoder tiers and system sizes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the joint estimation problem and the failure of GHZ states, describes the dipolar spin chain model and variational ansatz, presents the optimization methodology (CMA-ES and simplex reduction), details the numerical results regarding quantum advantage and state motifs, and concludes with an analysis of encoder/decoder expressivity and scaling limits.

Abstract

Estimating a uniform magnetic field B0 and its spatial gradient g on a dipolar-coupled spin chain calls for a multiparameter figure of merit. The GHZ state, optimal for single-parameter Heisenberg-limited sensing, has a rank-one quantum Fisher information matrix with det(Q^GHZ) = 0 at every chain length N, ruling it out for the two-parameter problem. We present a variational framework that takes det(F) as the objective and a hardware-motivated layered dipolar circuit as the ansatz. Both encoding generators are diagonal in the computational basis, which reduces the search for the quantum Fisher information benchmark to a probability-simplex optimization and yields a tractable best-found benchmark det(Q*) against which variational performance is compared. The same diagonal structure makes the classical Fisher information depend only on basis-state probabilities under any single-qubit decoder, so encoder and decoder parameters are co-trained with CMA-ES in a single run. Decoder optimization past fixed Ramsey adds at most a few percentage points across the grid, in contrast to the persistent decoder gains seen in our prior single-parameter work. Variational probes at L = 3 reach 0.92 of the best-found benchmark at N = 5, a 4.2x SQL advantage in det(F), and concentrate on a four-string motif of the two GHZ extrema and two half-chain-flip strings whose structure follows from the Dicke-sector decomposition of the two generators.

BBQ-mIS: a parallel quantum algorithm for graph coloring problems

Authors: Chiara Vercellino, Giacomo Vitali, Paolo Viviani, Edoardo Giusto, Alberto Scionti, Andrea Scarabosio, Olivier Terzo, Bartolomeo Montrucchio
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03524v1

Summary. The paper presents BBQ-mIS, a hybrid algorithm that uses classical Branch and Bound to decompose large graph coloring problems into smaller subproblems solvable on Rydberg atom quantum hardware. By parallelizing these tasks across multiple quantum resources, the method achieves optimal coloring solutions despite limited qubit counts. This work provides a blueprint for integrating quantum processing units with high-performance computing clusters.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a practical strategy for using Rydberg-based quantum simulators to solve combinatorial optimization problems by leveraging the natural mapping of graphs to atom-atom interactions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of solving large-scale graph coloring problems on current quantum hardware with limited qubit counts.

Main result. The BBQ-mIS algorithm effectively decomposes large graphs into smaller tasks, achieving coloring solutions that match the optimal results of the Gurobi classical solver.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical approach combining a classical Branch and Bound (BB) algorithm with a quantum routine (QAOA) to identify maximal independent sets.

Model / system. The algorithm is designed for neutral atom quantum machines using the Rydberg blockade effect, where qubits represent vertices and edges are defined by the blockade radius.

Key observables. The occupancy of the Rydberg state (n_i) and the distribution of maximal independent set solutions.

Important parameters / regimes. Rydberg blockade radius (r_b), Rabi frequency (Omega), detuning (delta), and pulse sequence duration.

Assumptions / limitations. The graph representation relies on a Unit-Disk Graph (UDG) model, which may limit generality for non-UDG structures.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the Branch and Bound node structure, pruning criteria, and priority score; Figure 2 compares the coloring performance of BBQ-mIS and Greedy-it-MIS against the Gurobi benchmark.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the graph coloring problem and hardware limitations, describes the BBQ-mIS algorithm and its hybrid architecture, details the Rydberg atom physical model and emulation setup, presents benchmarking results against classical solvers, and discusses HPC-QC integration challenges.

Abstract

Among the limitations of current quantum machines, the qubits count represents one of the most critical challenges for porting reasonably large computational problems, such as those coming from real-world applications, to the scale of the quantum hardware. In this regard, one possibility is to decompose the problems at hand and exploit parallelism over multiple size-limited quantum resources. To this purpose, we designed a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm, i.e., BBQ-mIS, to solve graph coloring problems on Rydberg atoms quantum machines. The BBQ-mIS algorithm combines the natural representation of Maximum Independent Set (MIS) problems onto the machine Hamiltonian with a Branch&Bound (BB) approach to identify a proper graph coloring. In the proposed solution, the graph representation emerges from qubit interactions (qubits represent vertexes of the graph), and the coloring is then retrieved by iteratively assigning one color to a maximal set of independent vertexes of the graph, still minimizing the number of colors with the Branch&Bound approach. We emulated real quantum hardware onto an IBM Power9-based cluster, with 32 cores/node and 256 GB/node, and exploited an MPI-enhanced library to implement the parallelism for the BBQ-mIS algorithm. Considering this use case, we also identify some technical requirements and challenges for an effective HPC-QC integration. The results show that our problem decomposition is effective in terms of graph coloring solutions quality, and provide a reference for applying this methodology to other quantum technologies or applications.

Summary. The researchers developed a neural-network-based method called Distance Encoder Networks to map complex graphs onto the physical constraints of neutral atom quantum computers. They successfully applied this to solve graph coloring for antenna networks and protein classification tasks. The approach is much faster than traditional optimization methods, though its success rate decreases as the number of atoms increases.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant as it bridges the gap between abstract graph problems and the physical constraints of Rydberg-based quantum hardware, providing a scalable heuristic for mapping complex networks to real-world atom arrays.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing efficient methods to embed Unit-Disk Graphs (UDGs) onto the physical registers of real-world neutral atom QPUs while satisfying hardware-specific constraints like fixed lattices and row-spacing.

Main result. The Distance Encoder Networks (DEN) approach successfully embedded antenna network graphs on the Orion Alpha QPU and achieved up to 76% success for protein classification tasks on the Aquila QPU, significantly outperforming traditional optimization solvers in speed.

Method. A neural-network-based optimization solver (DEN) using an Embedding Loss Function (ELF) to learn feasible embeddings, adapted with specific penalty terms for hardware constraints like row-spacing and lattice mapping.

Model / system. Neutral atom quantum processing units (QPUs) using Rubidium-87 atoms, specifically the QuEra Aquila and PASQAL Orion Alpha platforms, utilizing the Rydberg blockade mechanism to represent graphs.

Key observables. The gap between non-adjacent and adjacent vertex distances (d_non-adj - d_adj), and the feasibility rate of embeddings.

Important parameters / regimes. Rydberg blockade radius, atom register dimensions (e.g., 75um x 76um for Aquila), minimum row spacing (4um), and vertex count (n).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that the Rydberg blockade can effectively represent the edges of a Unit-Disk Graph and that the graph can be mapped to the available qubit-based register.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of graph embedding on neutral atom hardware, describes the DEN architecture and its modifications for specific QPU constraints (Orion Alpha and Aquila), presents experimental results on antenna networks and protein datasets, and discusses scaling challenges and future replication strategies.

Abstract

We present our work on effectively representing unit-disk graphs on the registers of neutral atom quantum machines. Specifically, we aimed to embed graphs corresponding to proteins and cellular antenna networks into unit-disk graphs, ensuring compatibility with the registers of two real QPUs: Orion Alpha by PASQAL and Aquila by QuEra. To address machine-specific constraints, we made adjustments and integrated Distance Encoder Networks (DEN) from our previous work. Despite these challenges, we successfully embedded up to 76% of protein-representing graphs for a quantum machine learning classification task on the Aquila QPU, and all subgraphs derived from 90 antenna geographical positions in Turin, Italy, on the Orion Alpha QPU. In the latter case, the graphs represented instances of the graph coloring problem, which we tackled using the hybrid quantum-classical algorithm BBQ-mIS. These promising results underscore the effectiveness and versatility of our embedding approach for representing unit-disk graphs on neutral atom quantum computers across diverse applications.

Information in Many-body Eigenstates: A Question of Learnability

Authors: Maksymilian Kliczkowski, Jarosław Pawłowski, Masudul Haque
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03043v1

Summary. This paper introduces 'learnability' as a new way to quantify the information content of many-body eigenstates using machine learning. It demonstrates that while low-energy states can be used to accurately reconstruct Hamiltonian parameters, mid-spectrum states are intrinsically unlearnable due to their random-like nature. This finding suggests that the difficulty of Hamiltonian identification is rooted in the underlying physics of the quantum states.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel information-theoretic perspective on the structure of many-body spectra, linking the physical concept of thermalization/randomness to the fundamental limits of machine learning-based Hamiltonian identification.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how much information about a Hamiltonian's parameters is encoded within individual many-body eigenstates and how this 'learnability' depends on their spectral position.

Main result. The authors find that eigenstates near the spectral edges are highly learnable, whereas mid-spectrum states, which resemble random vectors, lack the intrinsic information necessary for accurate Hamiltonian reconstruction regardless of model capacity.

Method. An unsupervised encoder-decoder neural network architecture is used, employing a physics-inspired Rayleigh loss function to reconstruct Hamiltonian parameters from eigenstates obtained via Exact Diagonalization.

Model / system. A one-dimensional spin-1/2 J1-J2 chain with periodic boundary conditions, featuring nearest-neighbor and next-nearest-neighbor couplings and local magnetic fields.

Key observables. Learnability (reconstruction precision), Rayleigh loss, parameter error (MSE), spectral error, entanglement entropy, and participation ratio.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling constants J1 and J2, number of input eigenstates (M), number of training samples (N_sam), and neural network width (w_H).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the failure to reconstruct mid-spectrum states is due to intrinsic physical properties (information loss) rather than limitations in neural network expressivity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates spectral differences via density of states and entanglement; Figure 2 sketches the ML protocol; Figure 3 shows reconstruction error vs. eigenstate index; Figure 5 demonstrates that increasing network width does not fix bulk reconstruction failure; Figure 6 shows generalization failure on unseen parameter intervals; Figure 9 shows stable convergence for multi-parameter inference.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of learnability, describes the J1-J2 model and the autoencoder architecture, presents results comparing spectral edge vs. mid-spectrum states, investigates the impact of model capacity and training sample size, tests generalization capabilities, and extends the analysis to multi-parameter reconstruction.

Abstract

To what extent do individual eigenstates encode information of their underlying Hamiltonian, and how does this depend on their spectral position? For many-body quantum systems, this issue is widely understood in terms of the differing nature of the eigenstates near the spectral edges (low-entanglement, highly-structured eigenstates) and those far from the spectral edges (high-entanglement, near-random eigenstates). Utilizing the availability of machine learning tools, we introduce a new way to quantify the information contained in eigenstates: for a particular learning architecture, how precisely can the Hamiltonian be reconstructed from a single eigenstate? We refer to this property as learnability; it serves as a new, alternative measure of the information content of eigenstates, made possible by machine learning. Using an encoder-decoder neural network and a physics-inspired loss function, we demonstrate how the distinction between two types of eigenstates is manifested as a difference in learnability. For spectral-edge eigenstates, the prediction accuracy is much better, and fewer eigenstates are required to learn the Hamiltonian, compared to mid-spectrum eigenstates.

Summary. This paper introduces a new technique to study multi-parameter quantum metrology for mixed states by using their purifications. It proves that the precision limits (QCRB and HCRB) of mixed states are linked to those of their purifications and provides a practical measurement protocol to reach these limits using individual measurements.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful theoretical tool for researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems to translate well-understood pure-state metrology results into the more complex regime of mixed-state parameter estimation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of achieving optimal estimation precision in multi-parameter quantum metrology for mixed states, where incompatible measurements often make the Quantum Cramér-Rao Bound (QCRB) and Holevo Cramér-Rao Bound (HCRB) unattainable.

Main result. The paper establishes a connection between the metrological bounds of mixed states and their purifications and proposes a two-stage measurement protocol using random purification channels to asymptotically attain either the HCRB or twice the QCRB.

Method. A purification-based formulation that relates mixed-state bounds to pure-state bounds, combined with a two-stage measurement strategy involving a coarse estimation via random purification channels followed by optimal local estimation.

Model / system. A d-dimensional parameterized mixed state rho_theta (the probe system) and its purification involving an environmental system E of dimension r.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM), Holevo Cramér-Rao Bound (HCRB), Quantum Cramér-Rao Bound (QCRB), and Weighted Mean Squared Error (WMSE).

Assumptions / limitations. The density matrix has a non-degenerate positive spectrum, the rank r is invariant for the parameter range, and the rank r is known a priori.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the measurement protocol involving the probe and environment systems and the random purification channel; Figure 2 depicts the parameterization of the environmental system and the kappa-ball regime for identifiability.

However, a general framework for applying these methods across arbitrary parameters and laser configurations has been missing. In this thesis, we introduce Picard--Lefschetz theory and develop practical numerical methods for its application. These enable the evaluation of oscillatory integrals and identification of contributions from individual critical points. We apply these techniques to strong-field ionisation and HHG, focusing on caustics -- enhancement features where trajectories coalesce and standard approximations fail. Our methods remain valid in these regions, allowing systematic analysis of parameter regimes and revealing previously inaccessible features. This work improves the understanding and control of ultrafast light--matter interactions.

Abstract

Parametrically driven cavity solitons (PDCS), unlike single-pumped cavity solitons, are localized optical pulses arising from parametric processes. These cavity solitons, recently discovered in pure-Kerr media, offer great promise for nonlinear dynamics studies and metrology. Here, we present the first multimode quantum description of pure-Kerr PDCS. In the below threshold regime, we verify single- and two-mode squeezing, while above threshold we uncover novel "quantum" dispersive waves - the quantum analog of soliton Cherenkov radiation. Besides revealing these unexplored quantum properties, we show that PDCS generates up to 20 dB of squeezing, only limited by overcoupling and intrinsic losses for experimentally routine parameters. We therefore provide a pathway to observe strong multimode quantum noise reduction in these systems.
