Summary. The paper identifies a phase transition in the entanglement scaling of 2D translation-invariant tensor networks driven by non-unitarity. It connects the computational cost of network contraction to the spectral properties of the transfer matrix, specifically showing how a dense ring of eigenvalues in the volume-law phase gives way to a spectral gap in the area-law phase.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and open quantum systems as it links the complexity of classical tensor network contraction to fundamental physical phenomena like entanglement transitions and non-unitary purification dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the complexity of contracting translation-invariant 2D tensor networks and identifying the transition between volume-law and area-law entanglement in boundary states.

Main result. The authors discover a transition from a volume-law to an area-law entanglement phase driven by non-unitarity, characterized by a spectral transition in the transfer matrix from a dense ring of eigenvalues to a distinct leading eigenvalue.

Method. Numerical simulations of row-state evolution, Matrix Product State (MPS) approximations, transfer matrix spectral analysis, and comparison with Random Matrix Theory (RMT) and mean-field theory.

Model / system. A 2D cluster state on a square lattice with periodic boundary conditions, modeled as a non-unitary version of the kicked Ising model where the transfer matrix acts as a 1D time evolution operator.

Key observables. Half-chain entanglement entropy, antipodal mutual information, purification time, radial eigenvalue density, and the spectral gap of the transfer matrix.

Important parameters / regimes. Non-unitary field strength (h_I), real field (h_R), and interaction strength (J).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on translation-invariant networks, though results are tested for robustness against spatial disorder.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the tensor network contraction schematic; Figure 2 displays the entanglement entropy and mutual information transitions; Figure 3 illustrates the transfer matrix spectral gap and eigenvalue density scaling; Figure 4 discusses computational tractability via bond dimension scaling.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the tensor network contraction problem, describes the non-unitary kicked Ising model, presents numerical results for entanglement and purification transitions, analyzes the spectral properties of the transfer matrix, tests the universality/robustness of the results, and concludes with implications for computational complexity.

Abstract

We study the complexity of approximately contracting translation-invariant tensor networks. The computational cost of row-by-row tensor network contraction, which defines a discrete time evolution governed by a fixed transfer matrix, is associated with the entanglement of the state of a row. By analyzing a family of tensor networks whose transfer matrices interpolate between chaotic Floquet and strongly non-unitary limits, we uncover a transition between volume- and area-law entanglement in states evolved under the transfer matrix. We show that deep in the volume-law phase the spectrum of the transfer matrix in the complex plane consists of a dense ring with a sharp outer edge, reminiscent of behavior identified for non-unitary random matrices. At late times an evolving row state therefore has significant contributions from many eigenvectors with nearly degenerate eigenvalue magnitudes. In the area-law phase, there is instead a distinct leading eigenvalue. Our results establish connections between contraction complexity, spectral properties of the transfer matrix, and purification under non-unitary dynamics.

Summary. This paper establishes how the geometry and disorder of a quantum network determine the possibility of scalable spin squeezing. It identifies that squeezing can occur via two different mechanisms depending on the network's spectral dimension. The findings offer a guide for engineering robust quantum sensors using platforms like Rydberg arrays or trapped ions.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unifying theoretical framework for designing scalable quantum sensors by linking quantum metrology to fundamental concepts like graph universality, spectral dimension, and percolation theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the necessary and sufficient conditions for achieving scalable spin squeezing in interacting spin ensembles embedded in arbitrary, inhomogeneous, and disordered network geometries.

Main result. The study identifies two distinct squeezing regimes (OAT-like and critical) governed by the spectral dimension of the network, and establishes that the scaling of the spin-squeezing critical point arises from the interplay between XY-ferromagnetic and percolation universality.

Method. The authors use a generalized rotor/spin-wave theory based on the graph Laplacian, linear spin-wave theory, and the Discrete Truncated Wigner Approximation (DTWA) to analyze the dynamics.

Model / system. The model is the XXZ Hamiltonian on generic graphs and networks, including site-diluted lattices and engineered geometries, applicable to platforms like trapped ions, Rydberg atom arrays, and ultracold molecules.

Key observables. Spin-squeezing parameter (xi^2), phase sensitivity, transverse variances, and collective magnetization.

Important parameters / regimes. Spectral dimension (ds), interaction exponent (alpha), anisotropy (Delta), dilution probability (p), and the percolation threshold (pp).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis utilizes the rotor/spin-wave approximation and assumes the dominance of isolated dimers near the percolation transition for certain scaling derivations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows different classes of inhomogeneous systems; Figure 2 presents a hierarchy of conditions for metrological advantage; Figure 3 relates spectral dimension to random walk properties; Figure 4 compares spin-wave spectra via exact diagonalization versus graph approximations; Figure 9 shows spin-squeezing dynamics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of squeezing in networks, develops a low-energy theory using the graph Laplacian, identifies two metrological regimes based on spectral dimension, analyzes scaling near the percolation threshold, and applies the framework to experimental platforms.

Abstract

We establish the conditions under which scalable spin squeezing can be achieved in interacting spin ensembles embedded in arbitrary, inhomogeneous network geometries. We identify two different forms of squeezing: OAT-like scalable squeezing is governed solely by the universal properties of the interaction graph and is controlled by its spectral dimension. In critical squeezing, on the other hand, the value of the spectral dimension only furnishes the necessary condition for scalable metrological gain, while the sufficient condition requires the model to lie below the symmetry breaking transition. Therefore, in quantum networks, the scaling of the spin-squeezing critical point emerges from a nontrivial interplay between xy-ferromagnetic universality and percolation universality. We apply this general theoretical framework to several experimental scenarios and discuss sharp and experimentally relevant conditions for achieving robust metrological gain on generic inhomogeneous structures, giving a unifying perspective for designing scalable quantum sensors across diverse quantum simulation platforms.

Summary. This paper proposes a method to characterize electronic transport regimes and scattering rates in 2D materials using simple multi-terminal resistance measurements. By analyzing how current splits between different contacts, researchers can distinguish between ballistic, hydrodynamic, and diffusive flow without needing complex imaging techniques. This provides a robust way to study electron liquids and identify the onset of the tomographic regime.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of quantitatively distinguishing between various electronic transport regimes (ballistic, hydrodynamic, tomographic, and diffusive) and extracting microscopic scattering rates without using space-resolved imaging.

Main result. A five-terminal 'fan' geometry allows for the extraction of momentum-relaxing and momentum-conserving scattering rates through current partition measurements, providing a simpler alternative to imaging.

Method. Numerical solution of the linearized Boltzmann transport equation using the FermiSea.jl framework and a spectral method with harmonic truncation.

Model / system. Electrons in clean, two-dimensional (2D) conductors modeled as an isotropic Fermi liquid near T = 0.

Key observables. Current partition (branching ratios) among drain contacts, specifically the fractions I_a/I_N, and nonlocal resistances.

Important parameters / regimes. Momentum-relaxing scattering rate (gamma_mr), momentum-conserving electron-electron scattering rate (gamma_ee), and the parameter gamma_3 for higher-order mode relaxation.

Assumptions / limitations. Focuses on the T -> 0 limit, assumes an isotropic Fermi liquid, and primarily assumes purely diffuse boundary conditions.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the device geometry and current density; Fig 2 displays heatmaps of current fractions as functions of scattering rates and Jacobian diagnostics; Fig 4 shows nonlocal resistance and sensitivity/condition number analysis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of transport regime identification, describes the five-terminal device geometry and the Boltzmann modeling approach, presents results for regime differentiation and parameter extraction, and concludes with a discussion on the sensitivity of the inverse problem.

Abstract

Strongly interacting electrons in clean two-dimensional devices are theorized to exhibit many distinct transport regimes, such as ballistic, hydrodynamic, or diffusive. Realistic samples often lie in crossover regimes between these idealized limits. We show how a single experiment on a multiterminal device can distinguish these regimes and constrain the relevant scattering rates without space-resolved imaging. Using a linearized Boltzmann model in a five-terminal geometry, we find that current partition among the drain contacts diagnoses the ballistic-hydrodynamic-Ohmic crossover and allows extraction of momentum-relaxing and momentum-conserving scattering rates in the crossover regime. The same geometry also exhibits clear signatures of the tomographic regime, potentially allowing for a quantitative discrimination between viscous and tomographic flow in experiments. Our results demonstrate that multiterminal devices are a simpler experimental route to characterize transport regimes in electron liquids, relative to space-resolved imaging experiments.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that tuning the frequency of a cavity can protect the extractable energy in a collective quantum battery from environmental noise. It reveals that protecting quantum coherence is more important than relying on environmental memory and provides a scaling law for optimal protection as the number of qubits increases.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical, passive control mechanism (detuning) to mitigate superradiant decay in many-body quantum systems and offers a counter-intuitive insight into the role of non-Markovianity in quantum thermodynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to protect the extractable work (ergotropy) in collective quantum batteries from energy loss caused by superradiant decay and environmental decoherence.

Main result. A passive cavity detuning strategy is proposed that spectrally isolates the system, achieving up to 1088% ergotropy improvement and resolving a 'non-Markovian paradox' by showing that suppressing environmental memory preserves coherence.

Method. Analytical derivation using the Nakajima-Zwanzig projection operator and TCL2 approximation, complemented by numerical simulations using the QuTiP framework.

Model / system. A collective quantum battery consisting of N identical two-level systems interacting with a single electromagnetic mode in a bosonic cavity, described by an extended T-Cummings model subject to superradiant decay.

Key observables. Ergotropy (E), BLP non-Markovianity measure (N), Collective Quantum Advantage (A), and effective decay rate.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (N), coupling strength (g), cavity detuning (Delta), cavity decay rate (gamma_0), and noise types (Ohmic/RTN and Thermal).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA) and the validity of the TCL2 approximation, which may break down in the ultra-strong coupling regime.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows ergotropy dynamics over time; Fig 2 presents ergotropy survival heatmaps; Fig 3 illustrates the scaling of optimal detuning, the non-Markovian paradox, and the quantum advantage metric.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of ergotropy loss, derives an analytical optimal detuning strategy, explores the relationship between coherence and non-Markovianity, compares different noise environments, and establishes the limits of the model in the ultra-strong coupling regime.

Abstract

This study investigates the performance and ergotropy protection of open collective quantum batteries subject to superradiant decay. By employing a passive spectral detuning strategy within an intermediate cavity, an optimal detuning value ($Δ^*$) is analytically derived and numerically verified to spectrally isolate the system and protect quantum coherence, achieving up to 1088% ergotropy improvement for single qubits and superextensive collective advantage for $N \ge 3$. Our analysis resolves a "non-Markovian paradox," revealing that maximizing ergotropy does not strictly require non-Markovian memory; rather, suppressing environmental memory via detuning optimally preserves coherence, which serves as the fundamental resource. Survival maps across different environments demonstrate that thermal noise dissipates coherence more severely than telegraph noise. Finally, we establish that collective amplification of the effective coupling ($g_{\rm eff} = g\sqrt{N})$ inevitably drives large qubit arrays into the ultra-strong coupling regime, providing a quantitative ceiling $N_{\rm max}$ on the validity of the Tavis-Cummings description and the current ergotropy protection protocol.

Summary. This paper presents a new mathematical framework to find exact Matrix Product State eigenstates for Hamiltonians that are not frustration-free. By using a local error cancellation technique, the authors demonstrate how to construct quantum many-body scars in 1D and 2D spin systems. This expands the toolkit for studying non-thermalizing quantum dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. The discovery of exact, low-entanglement eigenstates (scars) in non-frustration-free systems provides a powerful tool for studying non-thermal dynamics and offers states that are potentially observable on near-term quantum computers.

Main problem. Developing a general method to construct exact Matrix Product State (MPS) eigenstates for quantum many-body systems where the Hamiltonian is not frustration-free.

Main result. The authors successfully constructed exact zero-energy MPS eigenstates (quantum many-body scars) in both 1D and 2D by using a local error cancellation ansatz.

Method. A generalized Matrix Product Ansatz (MPA) based on a local error cancellation condition, inspired by the DEHP method for the ASEP, which reduces the problem to finding representations of a quadratic algebra.

Model / system. The study covers various models including 1D spin-S chains with Dzyalimoshinskii-Moriya interactions, 1D spin-1 chains, and 2D square lattice models (spin-2 and spin-1 XYZ models).

Key observables. Energy eigenvalues (specifically zero-energy states), two-point correlation functions, and correlation length.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin magnitude S, Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya interaction components (Dx, Dy, Dz), magnetic field strengths (hz, hy), and a complex parameter 'a' used in the expansion.

Assumptions / limitations. The construction relies on the existence of a local error cancellation mechanism and translational invariance; the method is demonstrated for specific fine-tuned boundary conditions in open chains.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a generalized DEHP-based ansatz, applies it to 1D spin chains (Model I and II), extends the construction to 2D square lattice models, and discusses the implications for quantum many-body scars and computational utility.

Abstract

We construct exact eigenstates of quantum many-body systems with Hamiltonians that are not frustration-free in matrix product form, based on a local error cancellation ansatz motivated by the Derrida-Evans-Hakim-Pasquier method for finding the stationary state of the asymmetric simple exclusion process. We demonstrate the approach with explicit examples in both one and two spatial dimensions.

Summary. This paper theoretically explores how a dual-species Rydberg atom array on a Kagome lattice can be driven into a Quantum Spin Liquid state. By using a specific laser-tuning protocol, the authors show that the system can exhibit topological order and long-range entanglement. This work provides a framework for using different atomic species to control many-body physics in quantum simulators.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates how heteronuclear interaction asymmetry can be exploited to engineer exotic topological phases like the Z2 spin liquid, offering a new pathway for preparing highly entangled states in Rydberg simulators.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the emergence and properties of a Quantum Spin Liquid (QSL) state in a dual-species (Rb and Cs) Rydberg atom array arranged on a Kagome lattice.

Main result. The study identifies specific detuning and quenching conditions to achieve a QSL state and proves the existence of topological order via Kitaev-Preskill topological quantum entanglement entropy.

Method. Numerical simulations using the Bloqade.jl Julia package, employing a sweep-quench-sweep protocol and analyzing correlation length, mutual information, and entanglement entropy.

Model / system. A programmable quantum simulator consisting of a dual-species (Rb and Cs) ultracold neutral atom array on a Kagome lattice, governed by a Hamiltonian with Rydberg blockade and long-range van der Waals interactions.

Key observables. Rydberg density (filling fraction), correlation length, mutual information, and Kitaev-Preskill topological quantum entanglement entropy (TQEE).

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice spacing (a), detuning ratio (nu), detuning parameter (Delta Rb/Omega), quenching time (tq), and interatomic interaction energies (V_ij).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is studied within the Rydberg blockade regime using a truncated subspace approach to manage Hilbert space dimensionality.

Abstract

Dual-species arrays of ultracold neutral atoms have recently attracted increased interest due to the ability to independently control different atomic species and tune the interatomic interactions. This capability provides additional flexibility essential for both quantum computing and quantum simulation. In this work we theoretically investigate a quantum spin liquid (QSL) state to be simulated on a programmable quantum simulator based on a dual-species atomic array, arranged on a Kagome lattice. The Kagome lattice is formed by corner sharing triangles. This specific spatial arrangement enhances the competing interactions between atoms and is often considered as a model for realising QSL states. When the atoms are excited into Rydberg states, long-range interactions result in Rydberg blockade. The geometric frustration of the Kagome lattice, combined with the Rydberg blockade, drives the system into exotic phases with topological order and long-range entanglement. To drive an array into the QSL state, we use a sweep-quench-sweep protocol, when the atoms are quasiadiabatically excited into Rydberg state with individually controlled detuning from the resonance for each atomic species. The filling fraction, indicating emergence of a QSL state, is represented by a density of Rydberg excitations. We identified the conditions required for QSL state in a dual-species array with non-uniform interaction energies. We calculated the correlation length and studied the mutual information as a function of the size of the subset of the system. The existence of a topological order was proved by estimating the Kitaev-Preskill topological quantum entanglement entropy.

Summary. The paper presents a systematic method to identify how nonlinear interactions in effective field theories lead to power-law relaxation at late times. By using Landau singularity analysis, the authors can determine the late-time decay behavior without performing explicit loop integrations. This provides a unified description of long-time tails in a wide range of macroscopic systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a universal, systematic framework for predicting long-time tails and non-exponential relaxation in any system governed by nonlinear hydrodynamic-like effective field theories.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how nonlinear hydrodynamic interactions cause the relaxation of fluctuations to transition from exponential decay to power-law decay (long-time tails) at late times.

Main result. The authors establish a systematic way to identify nonlinear decay modes and power-law exponents using Landau singularity analysis, showing that nonlinear interactions generate new decay modes and universal power-law behavior in the presence of gapless modes.

Method. Landau singularity analysis applied to Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory, using Landau equations to identify loop integral singularities and inverse Fourier transforms to extract time-domain asymptotic behavior.

Model / system. A broad class of macroscopic effective theories described by the Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory (SK-EFT) framework, specifically focusing on near-equilibrium systems with gapless hydrodynamic modes and nonlinear mode coupling.

Key observables. Two-point correlation functions (retarded, advanced, and symmetrized correlators) and the time-domain signal decay.

Important parameters / regimes. Late-time regime (t -> infinity), long-wavelength limit (k -> 0), spatial dimension (d), and the minimum loop number (L_min) determined by the interaction power.

Assumptions / limitations. The free two-point functions possess simple poles in the complex frequency plane, and the functions vanish sufficiently fast at infinity to ensure the convergence of the inverse Fourier transform.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides an illustration of a 'banana diagram' (a 1PI topology) used to represent the loop integrals and the structure of the self-energy analysis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of nonlinear relaxation, defines the SK-EFT framework, applies Landau singularity analysis to identify loop-induced singularities, derives the resulting power-law decay in the time domain, and provides a unified description for a broad class of theories.

Abstract

Nonlinear hydrodynamic interactions can change the relaxation of fluctuations from exponential to power-law decay at late times. Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory provides a standard framework for describing such fluctuation effects, where the nonlinear late-time behavior is encoded in loop corrections. Extracting this behavior requires identifying the singularities of loop integrals, whose structure becomes increasingly intricate beyond simple models. We apply Landau singularity analysis to two-point functions in effective field theories and determine the singularities induced by nonlinear interactions without performing the loop integrations explicitly. From these frequency-space singularities, we extract nonlinear relaxation modes that control the late-time behavior. When gapless modes are present, these modes produce power-law decay at late times. Our results give a systematic singularity-based description of nonlinear late-time relaxation in a broad class of macroscopic effective theories.

Summary. The paper introduces a method called ensemble engineering to prevent the cancellation of signals during quantum measurements by preparing states that concentrate probability mass in specific regions of the computational basis. By aligning the sampling weights with the operator's sign structure, the authors show that previously hidden physical signals can be recovered on real IBM quantum hardware. This approach offers a practical way to improve measurement efficiency in the NISQ era.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and open quantum systems as it provides a new tool for extracting complex correlation functions and signatures of quantum chaos (like OTOCs) from noisy, near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Estimating expectation values on NISQ devices via sampling suffers from destructive cancellation when near-uniform ensembles cause positive and negative operator contributions to cancel out, making signals unresolvable.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that 'ensemble engineering'—preparing quantum states with non-uniform, localized weights—can align the sampling distribution with the operator's sign structure to overcome this cancellation, a result validated on IBM quantum processors.

Method. The framework uses two circuit constructions: a Grover-type amplitude amplification protocol for benchmarking and an oracle-free shallow circuit for practical, low-depth hardware implementation.

Model / system. The study utilizes IBM quantum processors (up to 20 qubits) in the NISQ regime, focusing on the infinite-temperature correlation function (ITCF) and diagonal observables like Pauli-Z strings.

Key observables. Infinite-temperature correlation function (ITCF), sector contrast (CE(0)), sector mass, and operator-resolved diagonal profiles.

Abstract

On noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, expectation values of many observables are obtained through sampling-based approximations to trace-like quantities. A central limitation of this approach is destructive cancellation under near-uniform ensembles, which can render physically relevant signals effectively unresolvable. Here we show that this limitation is not simply statistical, but reflects a structural mismatch between ensemble weights and the operator-dependent sign structure of the measured correlator. We introduce a general framework for mitigating this effect through quantum ensemble engineering, in which the sampling distribution is encoded directly in the prepared quantum state. By reformulating correlators in a basis-resolved representation, we make the origin of cancellation explicit and derive strategies for aligning ensemble weights with operator structure. We realize this approach using two complementary circuit constructions: a Grover-type amplitude amplification protocol that provides a structure-aligned benchmark, and an oracle-free shallow circuit designed for near-term hardware constraints. Using the infinite-temperature correlation function as a representative setting, we demonstrate on IBM quantum processors with up to 20 qubits that engineered ensembles expose operator-resolved contributions that are strongly suppressed under uniform averaging. We identify a practical tradeoff between amplification strength and noise robustness, extend the framework to multi-qubit diagonal observables, and outline a path toward non-diagonal generalizations. These results position ensemble engineering as a new tool for improving measurement efficiency in near-term quantum algorithms.

Summary. The paper introduces an information-geometric method to detect non-equilibrium driving by measuring a 'relaxation gap' in the system's entropy-like divergence. It establishes a new thermodynamic lower bound on the entropy production rate that can be estimated from relaxation behavior. This provides a powerful tool for characterizing dissipation in systems where the full driving force is not explicitly known.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems as it provides a way to detect non-equilibrium driving and bound dissipation using only the observable relaxation dynamics of the system's density matrix.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a criterion to detect violations of detailed balance (nonconservative driving) and establishing thermodynamic lower bounds on the steady-state entropy production rate using information-geometric signatures.

Main result. The authors identify a 'relaxation gap'—the discrepancy between the acceleration of the Kullback-Leibler divergence and twice the squared intrinsic speed—as a signature of nonconservative driving, and derive a new lower bound on the steady-state entropy production rate based on this gap.

Method. The study uses information geometry, specifically the Kullback-Leibler divergence and Fisher information, combined with perturbation analysis and the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to relate relaxation dynamics to dissipation.

Model / system. The analysis covers Markov jump processes (discrete-state) and Fokker-Planck dynamics (continuous-state), including specific test models like a uniform biased cycle and a 2D hierarchical system.

Key observables. Steady-state entropy production rate (sigma*), Kullback-Leibler divergence, intrinsic speed (v_info), and the relaxation gap.

Important parameters / regimes. Mixing rate (kappa), asymmetry parameter (gamma or delta), perturbation magnitude (epsilon), and network dimension (n).

Assumptions / limitations. Results are primarily derived in the weak-perturbation (near-equilibrium or near-steady-state) limit and assume ergodic, locally reversible dynamics.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows schematic probability trajectories and examples of relaxation with and without a relaxation gap; Figure 2 presents numerical analysis of the bound's tightness using random rate matrices; Figure 3 shows the bound's performance for a biased cycle model.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the relaxation gap concept, provides analytical derivations for Markov jump processes, extends the results to Fokker-Planck dynamics, and concludes with numerical validation of the thermodynamic bounds.

Abstract

We propose an information-geometric signature of nonconservative driving that detects violations of detailed balance using the Kullback--Leibler divergence and the Fisher information. For Markov jump processes satisfying detailed balance, we show that, near equilibrium, the acceleration of the Kullback--Leibler divergence relative to the equilibrium state is given by twice the Fisher information with respect to time. In contrast, for relaxation toward a nonequilibrium steady state, this relation is generally violated even near the steady state. We refer to the resulting discrepancy as the relaxation gap and derive a lower bound on the steady-state entropy production rate in terms of this gap. We demonstrate that this bound is particularly tight for networks with simple cyclic topologies. Finally, we show that analogous relations and bounds hold for Fokker--Planck dynamics.

Abstract

We report experimental digital quantum simulation of the one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard model on a superconducting quantum processor at a scale beyond the reach of exact statevector simulation and challenging for state-of-the-art tensor-network methods. We encode this problem using up to 120 qubits through an efficient mapping that reduces circuit complexity, and we improve accuracy through error suppression to simulate dynamical evolution using up to 90 Trotter steps. From a vacancy defect introduced in the middle of an $L=31$-site (62-qubit) Néel initial state, we directly observe spin-charge separation to $t=9$ in natural units using up to 90 Trotter steps, and quantitatively extract velocity ratios $v_c/v_s$ which match classical simulations across a range of model parameters. We then extend experiments to $L=60$ (120 qubits) and long evolution times to $t=6$ using 30 Trotter steps; Quantum-processor outputs agree quantitatively with approximate classical simulations performed using a time-dependent variational principle (TDVP) solver; increasing the TDVP bond dimension through $χ= 4096$ expands the range of evolution times within which agreement has RMSE $\sim 1\%$ before the approaches diverge. Owing to the large scale of the simulation and the use of efficient overhead-free error-suppression techniques, for simulated evolution times at the limit of quantum/classical agreement ($t\gtrsim 5$ in natural hopping units), the wall-clock runtime of the quantum processor is up to $3000\times$ faster than an optimized TDVP simulation using $χ= 4096$. These results establish contemporary digital quantum processors as a versatile, quantitatively accurate, and competitive platform for the study of fermionic many-body dynamics in regimes where leading classical methods can become prohibitively expensive.

Abstract

We present a general method to determine the energy minimum of spin Hamiltonians over separable states when the single-particle reduced density matrices are fixed. For ferromagnetic Ising and Ising-like models with nearest-neighbor interactions on lattices of any dimension and on a fully connected graph in an external field, this minimum is given by a compact analytic formula involving the quantum Fisher information. For the ferromagnetic Heisenberg chain of spin-1/2 particles, the minimum is expressed via the Uhlmann-Jozsa fidelity. These relations enable the direct extraction of both the quantum Fisher information and the fidelity from correlation measurements on the ground states of suitably engineered spin models.
