Enhanced Valley Polarization via Nonlinear Cascaded Quantum-Geometric Selection Rules

Authors: Quentin Courtade, Sotirios Fragkos, Dominique Descamps, Stéphane Petit, Yann Mairesse, Michael Schüler, Samuel Beaulieu
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03663v1

Summary. This paper shows that using a nonlinear, two-photon cascaded process in MoTe2 can create much stronger valley polarization in high-energy electronic states than standard single-photon methods. By leveraging a real intermediate state, the researchers found they could effectively 'double' the chiroptical selection rules. This discovery provides a new pathway for controlling valley degrees of freedom in ultrafast valleytronics.

Why it may be interesting. The use of a Lindblad master equation to model nonlinear, multi-photon light-matter interactions and the exploration of quantum-geometric selection rules in a driven-dissipative regime are highly relevant to researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to understand how to enhance valley polarization in high-lying conduction bands of transition-metal dichalcogenides beyond the limits of conventional linear optical response.

Main result. The researchers demonstrate that a doubly resonant, cascaded nonlinear pathway via a real intermediate state significantly enhances valley polarization in high-lying states compared to the first conduction band.

Method. The study combines time- and angle-resolved extreme-ultraviolet photoemission spectroscopy (trARPES) with a time-dependent Lindblad master-equation formalism and DFT calculations.

Model / system. The physical system is monolayer 2H-MoTe2, modeled as a three-level ladder-type electronic structure (valence band, first conduction band, and a high-lying conduction band) using a Lindblad master equation to account for dynamics and dissipation.

Key observables. Energy- and momentum-resolved photoemission intensity, band-resolved local orbital angular momentum (Lz), and valley asymmetry (normalized difference between LCP and RCP pump intensities).

Important parameters / regimes. Pump wavelength (1.2 eV), XUV probe energy (21.6 eV), carrier lifetimes (ranging from ~47 fs to ~1.4 ps), and peak electric field strength (1.6 MV/cm).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a three-level ladder system and uses a Lindblad formalism to approximate dissipative decay and intervalley scattering.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup and orbital angular momentum textures; Figure 2 presents energy-momentum maps, time-resolved intensities, and Lindblad simulations; Figure 3 displays momentum distribution curves for LCP and RCP pumps.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of valley polarization enhancement, describes the experimental trARPES setup and theoretical Lindblad framework, presents the observation of enhanced polarization in high-lying states, and provides a microscopic explanation based on cascaded selection rules.

Abstract

The quantum geometric properties of Bloch electrons fundamentally govern light-matter interactions and optical selection rules in solids. In semiconducting transition-metal dichalcogenides, circularly polarized excitation near the band edge enables valley-selective interband transitions, providing the basis for valleytronics. While nonlinear optical protocols are being developed to manipulate and probe valley selection rules, they largely rely on band-edge transitions that proceed via virtual intermediate states. Here, we demonstrate a doubly resonant cascaded nonlinear pathway from the valence band to high-lying states, mediated by a real intermediate state whose participation substantially reshapes the valley optical selection rules. Using time- and angle-resolved extreme-ultraviolet photoemission spectroscopy in combination with a time-dependent Lindblad master-equation formalism, we show that this cascaded nonlinear photoexcitation produces a substantially enhanced high-lying valley polarization compared to the conventional linear optical response near the band edge. The extension of the quantum-geometry-based selection rules to the nonlinear regime and high-lying bands offers new perspectives for ultrafast valleytronics and should play a determinant role in strong-field-driven phenomena in quantum materials.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the gauge-field component of spin-orbit coupling is crucial for capturing the true spin dynamics in materials under external electric fields. It shows that neglecting this term leads to an incorrect 'symmetry-frozen' description of spins, whereas including it reveals essential symmetry-breaking effects and spin accumulation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the gauge-field contribution to the spin-orbit coupling (SOC) Hamiltonian is essential for describing symmetry breaking and spin dynamics under external electromagnetic fields.

Main result. The gauge-field term is the primary driver of symmetry breaking in the SOC Hamiltonian; without it, spins remain 'symmetry-frozen,' whereas its inclusion allows for the development of dynamical spin states.

Method. Real-time time-dependent density functional theory (rt-TDDFT) using the Quantum ESPRESS and the Kleinman-Bylander scheme to incorporate gauge-field-dependent SOC into pseudopotentials.

Model / system. The study examines a 2D Rashba-type square lattice model and real materials including the topological insulator Bi2Se3, the Dirac semimetal ZrSiS, and a 1D trigonal Se chain.

Key observables. Spin expectation values (Sx, Sy, Sz), spin texture, spin-projected band structures, and spin accumulation.

Important parameters / regimes. External electric field strengths (e.g., 0.5 mV/A and 1.0 mV/A), presence/absence of the gauge-field term, and various crystal symmetries (mirror, glide, and screw-rotational).

Assumptions / limitations. The relativistic expansion of the electron Hamiltonian is considered up to the (v/c)^2 order.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates mirror planes and compares spin evolution with and without the gauge-field term; Figure 2 shows the crystal structure of ZrSiS; other figures show spin-resolved 3D band structures.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of gauge-invariance in SOC, defines the decomposed Hamiltonian, presents rt-TDDFT methodology, applies the framework to specific models and real materials (Bi2Se3, ZrSiS), and concludes with the necessity of the gauge-field term for nonequilibrium dynamics.

Abstract

When a condensed-matter system is subjected to external electromagnetic fields, the gauge-invariant formulation of physical operators must explicitly incorporate the gauge-field contribution. However, in the context of spin-orbit coupling (SOC), this gauge-field term is often regarded as negligible or merely additive compared to the canonical SOC, which is typically localized near atomic cores. Here, we demonstrate that the symmetry breaking and consequent spin dynamics are governed by the gauge-field term, without which the spins remain symmetry-constrained. We perform real-time time-dependent density functional theory calculations to investigate spin-orbit dynamics, focusing on representative cases with mirror, glide, and screw-rotational symmetry. We demonstrate that when the gauge-field term in the time-dependent Hamiltonian perturbs the symmetry of the canonical term, a dynamical spin state gradually develops during the time evolution, beyond the symmetry-frozen states. We suggest that, for nonequilibrium spin-orbit dynamics, the gauge-invariant formulation of SOC is not only formally required but also quantitatively essential, even for a weak external field.

Tunable Odd-Parity Spin Splittings in Altermagnets

Summary. This paper proposes a method to engineer new spin-splitting symmetries in altermagnets using light or loop-current orders. By inducing odd-parity spin textures, it enables new ways to manipulate spin-polarized currents for spintronic applications. The work also predicts a dissipationless spin Hall effect in driven PT-symmetric magnets.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a theoretical framework to induce tunable odd-parity spin splittings in collinear altermagnets using two-color light driving or coupling to loop-current orders.

Main result. The study demonstrates that two-color light driving or P-odd loop-current orders can create mixed-parity spin textures in altermagnets and induce a nonrelativistic dissipationless anomalous spin Hall conductivity in PT-symmetric magnets.

Method. The work employs group-theoretical analysis, Landau theory, and microscopic Floquet theory (using the high-frequency and weak-intensity limits) alongside the Kubo formula for transport properties.

Model / system. The study focuses on collinear altermagnets (P,T = +, -) and PT-symmetric magnets (P,T = -, -) on square (D4h) and honeycomb (D6h) lattices, using a two-sublattice Hamiltonian with k-dependent hopping and exchange interactions.

Key observables. Momentum-dependent spin splitting (even and odd parity), spin-polarized currents, and dissipationless anomalous spin Hall conductivity.

Important parameters / regimes. Two-color light driving with a 2:1 frequency ratio, relative phase between light components, high-frequency limit (omega >> bandwidth), and weak light intensity.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the high-frequency limit, small driving amplitude (A << 1), and collinear spin ordering along the z-axis.

Figures summary. Figures show Fermi surface evolution (undriven vs. driven), the honeycomb lattice structure for PT-symmetric magnets, and different types of loop-current orders in square lattices.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of spin-splitting symmetry, develops a theoretical framework using group theory and Floquet theory, analyzes the response of altermagnets and PT-symmetric magnets to light and loop-currents, and concludes with transport property calculations.

Abstract

Momentum-dependent spin splitting and its relation to inversion ($P$) and time-reversal ($T$) symmetries are central to nonrelativistic spintronics. Representative examples include collinear altermagnets with $(P,T)=(+,-)$ and non-collinear odd-parity magnets with $(P,T)=(-,+)$. In this work, we develop a theoretical framework to induce odd-parity spin splittings in the more abundant collinear altermagnets through two mechanisms: driving by a two-color linearly polarized light field or coupling to a $P$-odd loop-current order. Properly phase-locked two-color driving induces a static $(P,T)=(-,-)$ order, symmetry-equivalent to a translationally invariant $P$-odd loop-current order. Coupling this order to an altermagnet produces a controllable mixed-parity spin texture, opening new avenues for the electrical and optical manipulation of spin-polarized currents in spintronics applications. The same mechanism applied to a collinear $PT$-symmetric magnet induces a distinct $(P,T)=(+,+)$ state with a nonrelativistic dissipationless anomalous spin Hall conductivity. We present group-theory and microscopic Floquet theory to highlight the emergent responses.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the twist angle is a critical control parameter for charge separation in WS2-graphene heterostructures. Using trARPES, the authors show that while 0-degree alignment enables efficient separation, a 30-degree misalignment leads to simultaneous electron and hole transfer. These findings provide essential insights for engineering next-generation optoelectronic and photovoltaic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how the twist angle between layers in van der Waals heterostructures influences the efficiency of ultrafast charge separation.

Main result. At a 0 degree twist angle, efficient charge separation is observed, whereas at 30 degrees, electron and hole transfer occur at similar rates, preventing separation. The electron transfer rate itself appears independent of the twist angle, potentially due to tunneling through sulfur vacancies.

Method. The researchers used time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (trARPES) to probe population dynamics and band shifts following photoexcitation.

Model / system. The physical system consists of epitaxially grown WS2-graphene heterostructures with twist angles of 0 and 30 degrees, where WS2 islands are grown via CVD on hydrogen-intercalated epitaxial graphene on SiC(0001).

Key observables. Population dynamics (gain/loss in graphene Dirac cone and WS2 bands), transient band gap renormalization, and transient band shifts (upward/downward shifts).

Important parameters / regimes. Twist angles of 0 and 30 degrees; pump photon energy of 3.1 eV; temporal resolution of 200 fs; energy resolution of 350 meV.

Assumptions / limitations. Fitting of WS2 valence bands assumed fixed energy differences between spin-split bands and fixed spectral weights based on equilibrium data.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows LEED data for twist angle confirmation; Fig 2 displays equilibrium and pump-induced ARPES spectra; Fig 3 shows population dynamics and Dirac cone changes; Fig 4 tracks evolution of band positions and charging shifts; Figs 5-7 detail the methodology for extracting band positions from EDCs and MDCs.

Paper structure. The paper begins with structural characterization via LEED, moves to the presentation of trARPES spectral snapshots, analyzes population dynamics and band shifts for both twist angles, compares results to theoretical energy barrier models, and concludes with a discussion on the mechanism of charge transfer.

Abstract

Van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures, formed by stacking two-dimensional materials, offer highly tunable electronic and optical properties, with the twist angle between layers acting as a critical tuning parameter. While its impact on moiré patterns, band structure, and correlated states is well-established, the influence of twist angle on ultrafast charge transfer remains controversial. Here, we employ time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (trARPES) to directly probe ultrafast charge transfer in epitaxially grown WS\textsubscript{2}-graphene heterostructures with twist angles of 0$^{\circ}$ and 30$^{\circ}$. Upon photoexcitation at $\hbarω= 3.1\,\mathrm{eV}$, we observe efficient charge separation at 0$^{\circ}$, while at 30$^{\circ}$, electron and hole transfer occur at similar rates. Our results highlight the crucial role of the twist angle in controlling charge separation efficiency, offering valuable insights for designing vdW heterostructures for applications in photovoltaics and optoelectronics.

Quantum Geometric Quadrupole of Cooper Pairs

Authors: Wenqin Chen, Kaijie Yang, Ting Cao, Shi-Zeng Lin, Jiabin Yu, Di Xiao
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03133v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5

Summary. This paper develops a new framework to calculate the size of Cooper pairs in flat-band superconductors by analyzing the quantum geometric quadrupole moment. It reveals that Berry curvature and the quantum metric impose a fundamental lower bound on the pair size, which can dominate the coherence length in systems like rhombohedral graphene. This identifies quantum geometry as a central driver of the microscopic length scales in correlated electronic systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep connection between topological invariants (Berry curvature/metric) and the microscopic spatial structure of many-body bound states, which is relevant to understanding the stability and scale of many-body correlations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the fundamental length scale (size) of Cooper pairs in flat-band superconductors where conventional BCS theory fails due to quenched band dispersion.

Main result. The authors establish that the Cooper pair size is determined by a geometric lower bound arising from the quantum metric and Berry curvature, with the latter providing a significant contribution when time-revanl symmetry is broken.

Method. A momentum-space derivation using a band-projected BCS framework, decomposing the pair quadrupole moment into amplitude, quantum metric, and phase structure components.

Model / system. The framework is applied to (nearly) flat-band systems, specifically focusing on rhombohedral graphene and twisted moiré systems using a two-band effective Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Cooper pair size (trace of the quadrupole moment), pair Berry curvature, pair quantum metric, and pair geometric dipole.

Important parameters / regimes. Berry curvature, quantum metric, winding number, momentum cutoff, and the presence/absence of time-reversal symmetry.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes a band-projected BCS framework for intraband pairing, neglects band dispersion in the limit of local attractive interaction, and utilizes a tight-binding approximation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a semiclassical illustration of the two-body bound state and the roles of anomalous velocity and intrinsic spread; Figure 2 shows the two-band model for rhombohedral graphene and its low-energy dispersion.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of pair size in flat bands, develops a formal mathematical framework for the pair quadrupole moment, decomposes the pair size into three gauge-invariant components, establishes a geometric lower bound, and validates the theory using rhombohedral graphene and the 'Berry trashcan' model.

Abstract

The size of Cooper pairs defines a fundamental length scale of superconductivity, conventionally set by band dispersion and the superconducting gap. This picture breaks down in flat bands, where quenched dispersion makes quantum geometry essential. Here we develop a general framework based on the Cooper pair quadrupole moment, whose trace gives the pair size. The framework holds for both dispersive and flat-band cases, and provides a unified description of the geometric origin of this length scale. In particular, when time-reversal symmetry is broken, Berry curvature enters through the phase structure of the pair wavefunction and gives an essential contribution absent from previous quantum-metric theories. Together, Berry curvature and quantum metric impose a geometric lower bound on the pair size. Applying this framework to rhombohedral graphene, we find that the Berry-curvature-induced contribution can dominate and yields pair sizes comparable to experimentally inferred coherence lengths. These results identify Berry curvature as a central geometric ingredient controlling the microscopic length scale of superconductivity.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that superconducting islands on suspended nanowires experience a thermal bottleneck where phonons cannot efficiently cool to the bath temperature. Detailed structure

Abstract

We investigate the heat balance in superconducting islands (S-islands) formed in epitaxial Al/InAs nanowires (NWs) freely suspended above the substrate. We employ a Joule spectroscopy approach, which traces the superconductor-normal transition in the S-island mediated by heating of the neighboring InAs NW segments via transport current. The temperature of the surrounding 3He bath is varied with nearby mesoscopic heaters and controlled with the NW Johnson noise thermometry. The experiment reveals a substantial thermal relaxation bottleneck associated with the cooling via surrounding 3He, which gives rise to phonon heating in the S-island. Our results uncover the role of environmental cooling in non-equilibrium experiments in S-islands in NW devices.

A Correction Method for Crack Area Overestimation in Phase-Field Fracture

Authors: M. Castillón, J. Segurado, I. Romero
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.03731v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new method to correct the overestimation of crack area in phase-field fracture simulations. By leveraging the stability of the gradient energy term, the proposed method provides a mesh-independent way to recover physical fracture properties and eliminate artificial force peaks. It is highly effective for complex 3D geometries where traditional correction methods fail.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Phase-field fracture models suffer from an overestimation of crack area due to numerical artifacts like strain localization, where the phase-field variable artificially saturates across finite elements.

Main result. The authors propose the Double Gradient Correction Method (DGCM), which uses the principle of energy equipartition to accurately approximate the true crack area as twice the gradient-dependent energy, effectively suppressing non-physical peak force overshoots.

Method. A novel a posteriori correction framework (DGCM) based on the energy equipartition principle, where the crack area is calculated from the gradient term of the energy functional, which is less sensitive to mesh-induced saturation.

Model / system. The study utilizes phase-field fracture models, specifically the AT2 (and AT1) regularization formulations, applied to 1D, 2D, and 3D mechanical specimens such as center-cracked tension and compact tension specimens.

Key observables. Crack surface area, peak force during crack initiation, force-displacement response, stiffness, and energy release rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Phase-field length-scale parameter (l), mesh size (h), critical energy release rate (Gc), and the mesh resolution ratio (l/h).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the principle of energy equipartition holds (valid as l approaches zero) and that the phase-field profile is sufficiently developed and free from boundary effects.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the transition from sharp to diffuse cracks, energy component convergence, the distortion of phase-field profiles due to localization, and validation of the DGCM against LEFM and other correction methods in 2D and 3D.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the problem of crack area overestimation, introduces the mathematical principle of energy equipartition, derives the DGCM framework, validates it with 1D analytical benchmarks, compares it against existing mesh-based and skeletonization methods, and demonstrates its robustness in complex 2D and 3D simulations.

Abstract

Phase-field fracture models are known to overestimate the crack area, a discrepancy that compromises the accuracy of fracture predictions. This issue stems from the diffuse crack representation and numerical artifacts, such as strain localization, where the phase-field variable artificially saturates across finite elements. Existing correction strategies, including mesh-dependent factors and skeletonization algorithms, have significant limitations. Mesh-based corrections are often unreliable for unstructured meshes, while skeletonization can be complex and inaccurate for intricate crack topologies, especially in three dimensions. This paper introduces a novel and robust framework to correct this overestimation. Our approach is founded on the principle of energy equipartition, where the energy contributions from the phase-field and its gradient are equal as the length-scale parameter approaches zero. Since numerical artifacts primarily affect the phase-field term while leaving the gradient term largely unperturbed, we propose that the crack area can be accurately approximated as twice the gradient-dependent energy. This method is inherently mesh-independent and readily applicable to the entire domain, including 3D simulations. The proposed methodology is validated against benchmarks with analytical solutions and compared with established methods like skeletonization to demonstrate its accuracy. It is then applied to complex geometries with curvilinear crack paths and evaluated in a three-dimensional simulation.

Adjacent Sink Strengths Used in Multiscale Kinetic Rate Equation Simulations of Defects and Impurities in Solids

Summary. This paper introduces a new 'adjacent sink strength' formulation for Kinetic Rate Equation simulations to account for the high probability of defects being retrapped immediately after detrapping. By incorporating this effect, the model corrects significant errors in predicting thermal desorption peaks and ensures that fitted kinetic parameters remain physically consistent. This provides a more accurate multiscale bridge between atomistic simulations and experimental observations in materials science.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Conventional Kinetic Rate Equation (kRE) models fail to capture the enhanced retrapping of defects/impurities released adjacent to traps, leading to systematic errors in predicting Thermal Desorption Spectroscopy (TDS) peak temperatures and physically inconsistent kinetic parameters.

Main result. The derivation of analytical expressions for 'adjacent sink strength' (Ka) allows kRE simulations to achieve quantitative agreement with kinetic Monte Carlo benchmarks and prevents the use of non-physical parameters when fitting experimental data.

Method. The author derives analytical expressions for adjacent sink strength including jump length corrections and implements a numerical iterative scheme (using Crank-Nicolson and Picard iteration) to solve the coupled nonlinear kREs.

Model / system. A mean-field Kinetic Rate Equation (kRE) framework modeling the evolution of defect and impurity concentrations (e.g., hydrogen/helium in solids) subject to diffusion, trapping, and detrapping.

Key observables. Thermal Desorption Spectroscopy (TDS) peak temperatures, impurity flux to the surface, and defect concentrations.

Important parameters / regimes. Trap radius (Rt), trap concentration (Ct), diffusion jump length (lambda), trapping energy (Et), detrapping frequency (nu_detr), and the sink strength enhancement factor (epsilon_k).

Assumptions / limitations. The analytical expressions assume spherical trap geometries, a small jump length limit (lambda/Rt <= 0.5), and a low total trap volume fraction (VF_tot <= 0.1).

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares random vs. adjacent sink strengths and shows the enhancement factor; Figure 2 shows TDS peaks with and without adjacent sink strengths; Figure 3 shows TDS peak shifts with concentration and jump length; Figure 6/7 show analytical vs. kMC sink strength comparisons.

Paper structure. Introduction of the kRE deficiency, derivation of the adjacent sink strength and jump length corrections, numerical implementation strategy, validation via kMC benchmarking, and discussion of parameter estimation risks.

Abstract

Kinetic Rate Equation (kRE) modeling is widely used to simulate defect and impurity evolution in solids over experimentally relevant time and length scales. However, conventional kRE formulations include only random-position sink strengths, which adequately describe trapping of defects created at random lattice sites but fail to capture the enhanced retrapping of defects released directly adjacent to traps during detrapping or dissociation events. This omission leads to systematic errors, including underestimated thermal desorption (TDS) peak temperatures and incorrect kinetic parameters when fitting to experimental data. In this work, we derive for the first time analytical expressions for the adjacent sink strength, including correction for finite impurity diffusion jump length. We provide a practical implementation strategy for integrating these expressions into kRE simulations. Comparisons with kinetic Monte Carlo (kMC) benchmarks demonstrate that adjacent sink strengths dominate the retrapping probability and are essential for reproducing the correct temperature dependence of TDS release peaks. Simulations that employ only random sink strengths can still be tuned to match TDS spectra; however, the resulting fitted trapping energies, detrapping frequencies, and diffusion parameters are often physically inconsistent. The adjacent sink strength formulation introduced here significantly improves the predictive capability of kRE modeling, enabling accurate multiscale simulations of defect and impurity behavior in materials. This framework also establishes a foundation for future extensions, including adjacent sink strengths associated with extended defects such as dislocations and grain boundaries, offering new opportunities to resolve persistent discrepancies between experimental and simulated trapping energetics.

Abstract

Axion dark matter searches require superconducting radio-frequency (SRF) cavities on copper (Cu) substrates with quality factors Q > 10^5 in multi-tesla magnetic fields. Copper reduces thermal noise and allows complex geometries. Nb3Sn is a strong candidate due to its superior superconducting properties. However, uniform high-Tc Nb3Sn thin films on Cu are challenging due to Sn loss and substrate strain. This work uses solid-state diffusion of Sn from high-Sn Cu-Sn alloys into Nb layers to form Nb3Sn at Cu-compatible temperatures (650-750°C), avoiding the traditional ~1100°C vapor method. Varying Cu-Sn composition yielded an optimal alloy that maintains high Sn activity. Compositional and thermal expansion analyses showed Tc is suppressed below 18 K by Cu substrate strain. Experiments on Nb and sapphire substrates isolated the strain effects. Two routes were developed: (1) Cu-Sn on Ta-coated Cu with hot Nb sputtering (Tc = 16 K), and (2) Nb on Ta/Cu with Cu-Sn evaporation and ex-situ reaction. Route 2 gave uniform Nb3Sn and was chosen for cavity coating. A hexagonal cavity combining designs from the University of Washington and Center for Axion and Precision Physics was coated using Route 2 and tested to 50 mK and 9 T. At zero field it reached Q = 77,000 (40% above bare Cu's Q = 55,000), but Q dropped sharply in field. Nb3Sn coatings on Cu cavities outperform bare Cu at zero field and provide practical routes for improved axion detectors.
