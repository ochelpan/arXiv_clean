All Quantum Probability viewed in Complex Projective Geometry

Authors: Stephen Bruce Sontz
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17578v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a purely geometric foundation for quantum mechanics by showing that all probability rules can be derived from the intrinsic metric properties of complex projective space. By removing the need for an underlying linear Hilbert space, it supports a broader program of geometrizing physics and allows for potential generalizations of quantum theory in other geometric settings.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to reformulate all quantum probability formulas (single and consecutive events) using only the intrinsic geometric properties of complex projective space, removing the dependency on the underlying linear Hilbert space structure.

Main result. The author demonstrates that the Born rule and Wigner's rule for consecutive events can be expressed purely through the Fubini-Study metric and geodesics of the Kähler manifold CP(H), establishing a projection theorem for projective space.

Method. The study employs the mathematical framework of Riemannian submersions, the Fubini-Study metric, and the theory of geodesics within complex projective geometry to reinterpret Hilbert space operators as geometric subspaces.

Model / system. The framework applies to a general quantum system represented by a complex Hilbert space (finite or infinite-dimensional) where pure states are identified with points in the complex projective space CP(H).

Key observables. Quantum transition probabilities (Born rule), single event probabilities, and consecutive event probabilities.

Important parameters / regimes. The Fubini-Study metric distance d(x, y), the projective angle, and the dimension of the Hilbert space (dim >= 2).

Assumptions / limitations. The Hilbert space is assumed to be complex with a Hermitian inner product; the paper notes potential non-compactness issues in infinite-dimensional settings.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a review of Hilbert space structures, transitions to the geometric properties of the Kähler manifold CP(H), proves a projection theorem for projective subspaces, and concludes by demonstrating how probability rules and von Neumann algebras are recovered geometrically.

Abstract

In a recent paper it was shown that all the Hilbert space formulas for quantum probabilities can be realized as functions of geometric properties of the associated projective space, but those functions were expressed using the structures of the associated Hilbert space. In this paper a direct description of all these probabilities is given as formulas involving only the geometric properties of the projective space itself without referring to the associated Hilbert space theory. In large part this depends on a projection theorem for complex projective space which is analogous to the projection theorem for Hilbert spaces. The importance of this is that this exhibits quantum probability in terms of the geometry of a Riemannian metric in a non-linear Kähler manifold without any reference to a linear Hilbert space. As such this is a part of a larger program of the geometrization of physics. This opens the possibility of generalizations of quantum theory in other similar geometric settings. The theory presented includes projective spaces of both finite and infinite dimension. Some comments explain how quantum theory based on a von Neumann algebra is compatible with this approach.

An Entropy-Governed Speedup for Quantum Algorithms on Local Hamiltonians

Authors: Ranitha Mataraarachchi, François Le Gall, Suguru Tamaki
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18241v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a quantum algorithm that outperforms the standard Grover bound for energy estimation and state preparation of k-local Hamiltonians. By shifting the target from the absolute ground state to an energy threshold reachable by low-depth circuits, the authors leverage the high density of states to achieve a speedup governed by the binary entropy function. This provides a fundamental distinction between highly entangled states and those with efficient classical descriptions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently estimating the ground-state energy and preparing low-energy states for general k-local Hamiltonians, specifically aiming to break the Grover bound of O*(2^{n/2}).

Main result. The authors present a quantum algorithm that achieves an even faster runtime than previous methods, with a scaling governed by the binary entropy function, by targeting an energy threshold achievable by low-depth circuits.

Method. The approach uses spectral analysis, the Cauchy Interlacing Theorem, and a perturbation strategy on a 'quiet set' of qubits to prove an exponentially dense spectrum of low-energy states near a specific energy threshold.

Model / system. The study considers n-qubit k-local Hamiltonians, which are general all-to-all interacting systems without specific geometric or spatial structure assumptions.

Key observables. Ground-state energy estimation, energy threshold (Ed), and cumulative spectral count N(E).

Important parameters / regimes. k-locality, circuit depth (d), total interaction strength (M), and the binary entropy function H(.).

Assumptions / limitations. The algorithm assumes the target energy threshold is achievable by low-depth circuits and relies on the k-local nature of the Hamiltonian.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a visual representation of applying a local unitary perturbation (Pauli X operator) to a qubit within a quiet set to transform the zero state to the one state.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of energy estimation, defines an effective Hamiltonian via low-depth circuits, provides a spectral density lower bound using perturbation theory, and concludes with the construction and complexity analysis of the new quantum algorithm.

Abstract

Low-energy estimation and state preparation for general $k$-local Hamiltonians are fundamental challenges in quantum complexity theory. For constant relative accuracy, Buhrman et al. (PRL 2025) recently broke the natural Grover bound $O(2^{n/2})$, where $n$ denotes the number of qubits, for both problems. In this paper, for any sufficiently small parameter $d\ge 0$, we present an even faster quantum algorithm that outputs a quantum state with energy bounded by the minimum energy over all depth-$d$ states (i.e., states obtained by applying a depth-$d$ circuit to the all-zero state), together with an estimate of this energy. For the class of Hamiltonians with depth-$d$ ground states, our algorithm furthermore achieves exactly the same energy guarantees as Buhrman et al. Our results also provide insight into the distinction between strongly entangled states and those admitting efficient classical descriptions.

Bounds on quantum conference key agreement in pair-entangled networks

Authors: Justus Neumann, Hermann Kampermann, Dagmar Bruß, Anton Trushechkin

Summary. This paper establishes fundamental limits on how much secret key can be distilled in quantum networks where connections are made of bipartite entangled pairs. It proves that for these specific networks, the most efficient way to create a group key is simply to perform standard bipartite QKD on each link and then merge the results.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the upper bounds on the distillable conference key rate in quantum conference key agreement (QCKA) within near-term quantum networks.

Main result. The authors prove that for pair-entangled networks, multipartite strategies do not outperform bipartite-based strategies, and they derive upper bounds on the key rate based on network topology and the regularized entanglement of formation.

Method. The study uses information-theoretic tools including the multipartite Devetak-Winter formula, von Neumann entropy, and graph theory (cuts and partitions) to derive and prove the tightness of bounds.

Model / system. The model consists of pair-entangled networks (PENs) where nodes are connected by sources of bipartite entangled states (e.g., Bell states) distributed along the edges of a graph.

Key observables. Distillable conference key rate (r_key), multipartite Devetable-Winter key rate (r_DW), and von Neumann entropy.

Important parameters / regimes. Network topology (graph structure), degree of entanglement of the sources, and the number of secrecy-seeking parties (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The network operates under local operations and shared randomness (LOSR) without quantum memory, and helper nodes are assumed to be trusted.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the network graph and participants; Figure 2 shows the 'weakest cut' bound; Figure 3 demonstrates how vertex partitions provide stronger bounds; Figure 4 illustrates tree networks.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QCKA task in PENs, establishes upper bounds for the Devetak-Winter rate, analyzes specific network topologies like tree and 3-node networks, and generalizes the bounds to mixed states using regularized entanglement of formation.

Abstract

We investigate the task of conference key agreement in near-term quantum networks, where the nodes are connected by sources of bipartite entangled states, under the class of local operations not requiring quantum memory. We derive upper bounds on the distillable conference key depending on the network topology and degree of entanglement of the sources, and prove tightness of these bounds for some particular cases. In these cases, we show that pairwise bipartite key distillation followed by merging the bipartite keys into the conference key is optimal.

Branching under First-Passage Resetting

Authors: Aanjaneya Kumar, James Holehouse
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16693v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a framework for branching processes where replication is triggered by an internal stochastic variable reaching a threshold. It demonstrates that timing fluctuations can optimize population growth by balancing the benefit of higher offspring yield against the cost of delayed reproduction. The theory is successfully validated against experimental data from bacteriophage lysis.

Why it may be interesting. The framework of first-passage resetting and the mapping between microscopic stochasticity and macroscopic growth rates are highly relevant to the study of non-equilibrium dynamics and the stability of populations in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how stochastic fluctuations in replication timing, driven by internal first-passage dynamics, affect the macroscopic population growth rate and the trade-off between offspring yield and reproduction delay.

Main result. The authors derive an exact renewal equation showing that for fixed yields, timing fluctuations necessarily enhance growth, while for time-dependent yields, fluctuations can either enhance or reduce growth depending on a specific yield-delay trade-off.

Method. The study uses a renewal approach, Laplace transforms, and a generalized Euler-Lotka equation, supplemented by small-fluctuation expansions and the use of the Lambert W function for analytical optimization.

Model / system. A branching process where replication events are triggered when an internal stochastic variable reaches a threshold (First-Passage Resetting). Models include drift-diffusion processes and multi-state Erlang/Gamma chains, with applications to bacteriophage lysis.

Key observables. Population growth rate (lambda), first-passage time (TL), offspring number/yield (m), and the coefficient of variation (CV^2) of the timing.

Important parameters / regimes. Threshold (L), drift velocity (vd), diffusion constant (D), power-law yield exponent (beta), and the yield-delay trade-off regime.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the existence of a probability density for first-passage times and that the population dynamics can be described by a generalized Bellman-Harris/Euler-Lotka framework.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the core model and phage lysis application; Figure 2 compares population density and growth rate vs. variability for different stochastic clocks; Figure 3 illustrates the impact of diffusion on growth.

Paper structure. Introduction of the BFPR framework, derivation of the exact renewal equation, analysis of fixed-yield vs. time-dependent yield regimes, optimization of the yield-delay trade-off, and experimental validation using bacteriophage data.

Abstract

Many biological processes, from cell division to viral lysis, are triggered when an internal stochastic variable reaches a threshold. Here we introduce Branching under First-Passage Resetting, a general framework in which replication events arise endogenously from first-passage dynamics rather than from externally imposed lifetime clocks. We show that the resulting population dynamics obey an exact renewal equation linking single-trajectory first-passage statistics to the population growth rate. This mapping shows that, for fixed offspring number and fixed mean replication time, stochastic timing fluctuations necessarily enhance growth relative to a deterministic clock. When offspring yield depends on the first-passage time, however, fluctuations have non-trivial effects and expose a fundamental yield-delay trade-off: waiting longer can increase the number of descendants, but delays all future lineages. Our framework allows us to address this optimization problem analytically, and upon application to bacteriophage lysis, gives an optimal lysis time and growth rate consistent with empirical data.

Charge order on a triangular lattice with Mott physics and arbitrary charge density

Authors: Aleksey Alekseev, Agnieszka Cichy, Konrad Jerzy Kapcia
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18575v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper uses DMFT to map out the phase diagram of the extended Hubbard model on a triangular lattice. It discovers various charge-ordered and pinball-liquid phases, highlighting how Mott physics and intersite repulsion compete to drive different types of electronic localization.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the complex phase diagram of charge-ordering phenomena and the interplay between Mott physics and intersite interactions on a triangular lattice.

Main result. The study identifies a rich phase diagram containing various pinball-liquid (PL) phases, distinguishing between Mott-localization-driven and charge-transfer-driven types, and reveals strong particle-hole asymmetry.

Method. Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (DMFT) using the Lanczos algorithm to solve the Anderson Impurity Model, supplemented by Mean-Field Approximation (MFA) and Atomic-Limit (AL) analysis.

Model / system. The Extended Hubbard Model (EHM) on a triangular lattice, incorporating onsite interaction (U), nearest-neighbor interaction (V), and hopping amplitude (t).

Key observables. Sublattice occupation numbers, quasiparticle weights (Z), spectral functions, and grand potential.

Important parameters / regimes. Onsite interaction (U), intersite interaction (V), chemical potential (mu), and bandwidth (D).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglects magnetic ordering, neglects spatial correlations by treating intersite interaction at the Hartree level, and focuses on the zero-temperature ground state.

Figures summary. Visualizations of charge-order types, the atomic-limit phase diagram, DMFT phase diagrams with color-coded regions, and sublattice-specific spectral functions.

Paper structure. The paper defines the mathematical framework and model, reviews established limits (AL and MFA), presents the new DMFT phase diagrams, and analyzes specific spectral properties and phase transitions.

Abstract

Triangular-lattice systems attract a lot of attention due to various frustration-induced and strongly correlated effects. Here, we focus on the charge-ordering phenomenon by means of investigation of the extended Hubbard model with dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT). By considering the intersite nearest-neighbor interaction we have found a very rich phase diagram that contains large number of features, phases, and phase transitions. Among them are pinball-liquid (PL) phases where we distinguish charge-transfer-driven and Mott-localization-driven PLs; phase transitions that change their order as model parameters evolve (from discontinuous to continuous); very strong particle-hole asymmetry. Various features of the phase diagram are found to be better understood by means of the simple mean-field approximation (MFA). Moreover, besides helping with interpretation of the phase diagram, the MFA results together with results for the atomic-limit model are found to be able to set rather good expectations on how the DMFT phase diagram should look like. Nevertheless, a few features were not expected and are found within the DMFT, such as a small-region intermediate metallic phase on an electron-doped side of the phase diagram.

Covariant extrinsic curvature expansion of the nonlocal effective action for a massless scalar field on a manifold with boundary

Authors: A. Boasso, C. D. Fosco, B. C. Guntsche, F. D. Mazzitelli
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17124v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a new covariant method to calculate the nonlocal effective action for quantum fields near curved boundaries. It successfully applies this framework to predict particle production in oscillating geometric structures, such as spheres and rings, providing a more general approach than previous coordinate-dependent methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Deriving a covariant expansion of the nonlocal effective action for a massless scalar field on a manifold with a curved boundary, extending beyond previous Monge-patch-based methods.

Main result. The authors establish a geometric framework for the nonlocal effective action in terms of the extrinsic curvature tensor, providing specific results for particle creation rates in oscillating 2+1D rings and 3+1D spheres.

Method. The study utilizes a heat-kernel approach and the Barvinsky-Vilkovsky reconstruction method to derive the expansion, applying it to time-dependent geometries via Wick rotation and perturbation theory.

Model / system. A massless real scalar field in a flat (d+1)-dimensional bulk with a smooth, curved boundary subject to Dirichlet or Neumann boundary conditions, including specific models of oscillating rings and spheres.

Key observables. Nonlocal effective action, particle creation rate, and the mean number of created particles.

Important parameters / regimes. Extrinsic curvature tensor (K_ab), dimension (d), oscillation frequency (omega), deformation amplitude (epsilon), and boundary conditions (Dirichlet/Neumann).

Assumptions / limitations. The bulk manifold is assumed to be flat, the boundary is smooth, and the expansion is valid in the regime where curvature gradients dominate over nonlinear curvature effects.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the dimensionless mean number of created particles per unit time as a function of omega*R for various multipoles, illustrating a threshold frequency for radiation and a universal high-frequency scaling.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the heat-kernel expansion and geometric framework to deriving the quadratic order expansion in extrinsic curvature, followed by applications to oscillating ring and sphere geometries and validation against existing models.

Abstract

We study the nonlocal effective action of a massless scalar field defined on a flat manifold with a curved boundary. Using a heat-kernel approach, we derive a covariant expansion of the nonlocal contribution to quadratic order in the extrinsic curvature tensor. Our construction provides a geometric framework that both reproduces earlier results obtained for Monge-patch embeddings and extends them to more general surfaces that need not admit a global Monge-patch description. The expansion is valid in the regime where gradients of the extrinsic curvature dominate over nonlinear curvature effects. As an application, we compute the particle-creation rate for an oscillating deformed ring in $2+1$ dimensions and an oscillating deformed sphere in $3+1$ dimensions.

Detecting nonclassicality in randomly-displaced copies of a squeezed state

Authors: Mehmet Emre Tasgin
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18708v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper presents a method to identify quadrature squeezing even when the signal's amplitude fluctuates randomly between pulses. By using a specific interaction to convert quadrature squeezing into number squeezing, the authors show that the nonclassical signature becomes detectable and remains invariant under signal attenuation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a robust protocol for quantum state verification in noisy, fluctuating channels, which is critical for quantum communication and sensing in real-world environments like atmospheric links.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to detect quadrature squeezing in a signal when each received copy has a different, unknown random displacement due to environmental noise or channel fluctuations.

Main result. The authors propose an interaction Hamiltonian that converts quadrature squeezing into number squeezing, allowing for detection via the second-order correlation function g(2)(0) which remains robust against random displacements and attenuation.

Method. The paper uses an operator formalism (Pegg-Barnett) and the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff lemma to derive a beam-splitter-like transformation that maps quadrature variables to number-phase variables, supplemented by numerical simulations in a finite Fock space.

Model / system. A two-mode system consisting of an incident squeezed mode and a number-phase mode, with potential implementations in circuit QED (Josephson junctions), atomic ensembles (Raman transitions), and nonlinear optics (Kerr nonlinearities).

Key observables. Second-order correlation function g(2)(0), normalized number variance, and the g(2)(tau) histogram.

Important parameters / regimes. Large photon number regime (n >> 1), squeezing rates, displacement amplitudes, and transmission coefficient (eta).

Assumptions / limitations. The large-occupation approximation is used for the validity of the number-phase commutation relation, and the environment/loss mode is assumed to be in the vacuum state.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the detection protocol schematic (displacement, conversion, and detection); Figure 2 illustrates the conversion of quadrature squeezing to number squeezing; Figure 3 shows the enhancement of signal visibility via counter-displacement.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of random displacements, defines the conversion Hamiltonian and its operator properties, proves the non-generation of nonclassicality, demonstrates the conversion mechanism and entanglement generation, provides an analytical proof of g(2)(0) invariance under loss, and discusses physical implementations.

Abstract

We address a fundamental question: Can one determine whether a received signal is squeezed when each copy arrives with a different displacement/amplitude? We introduce an interaction Hamiltonian that converts quadrature squeezing into number squeezing. Using this conversion, we test whether the copies satisfy $g^{(2)}(0)<1$. The Hamiltonian itself does not create nonclassicality; it only transfers it from quadrature squeezing to number squeezing. This allows us to identify squeezing even when individual copies have random displacements.

Energy-Weighted Site Percolation in Two Dimensions

Authors: Sayan Sircar, Kabir Ramola
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18312v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper generalizes 2D site percolation by introducing a bond energy cost, demonstrating that this parameter can continuously tune the system between different universality classes. By using renormalization group and Monte Carlo methods, the authors show how connectivity and critical exponents evolve as a function of the energetic cost.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how assigning an energy cost to bonds between nearest-neighbor occupied sites affects the percolation threshold, critical exponents, and universality classes in 2D site percolation.

Main result. The bond energy epsilon acts as a tuning parameter that shifts the percolation threshold and drives a continuous evolution of the correlation length exponent nu from 1/2 to 4/3 to 1, effectively interpolating between different universality classes.

Method. The authors employ Monte Carlo simulations in a grand-canonical ensemble, real-space renormalization group (RG) methods using Kadanoff block recursions, and a Coulomb-gas formulation to interpret scaling behavior.

Model / system. An energy-weighted site percolation model on a 2D square lattice where each bond between nearest-neighbor occupied sites incurs an energy cost epsilon, creating a competition between entropy and energetic suppression.

Key observables. Percolation threshold (pc), correlation length (xi) and its exponent (nu), cluster size distribution, and susceptibility (chi).

Important parameters / regimes. Site occupation probability (p), bond energy cost (epsilon), and anisotropic energy components (epsilon_x, epsilon_y).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the validity of real-space RG approximations and uses specific renormalization rules (e.g., R0, R2) for block-based calculations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate lattice configurations with energy costs, phase diagrams of cluster configurations, RG flow diagrams around fixed points, and scaling collapses of wrapping probability and density.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the energy-weighted model, details the Monte Carlo and RG methodologies, presents results for isotropic and anisotropic cases, provides a Coulomb-gas interpretation, and concludes with a discussion on lattice gas RG approaches.

Abstract

We study a generalization of two-dimensional site percolation by assigning an energy cost $\varepsilon$ to bonds between nearest-neighbor occupied sites. This leads to a competition between entropy-driven cluster growth and energetic suppression (or enhancement) of connectivity. Varying $\varepsilon$ continuously interpolates between dense ferromagnetic-like clusters, ordinary classical percolation, and a dilute regime of minimally connected isolated clusters. Using Monte Carlo simulations and real-space renormalization-group (RG) methods, we show that bond energy shifts the percolation threshold smoothly. We define an energy-weighted correlation length that remains finite at the classical site occupation threshold ($p_c(\varepsilon=0)$) and shrinks with increasing $\varepsilon$, capturing the energetic suppression of large-scale connectivity. The cluster size distribution exhibits an energy-dependent cutoff that drives the transition from percolation-like clusters to isolated clusters. A real-space RG with Kadanoff block recursions reveals a systematic evolution of the correlation-length exponent $ν$ from $ν=1/2$ (dense clusters) to $ν=4/3$ (classical percolation), approaching $ν=1$ (minimally connected isolated clusters), in agreement with Coulomb-gas predictions for loop models where bond energy renormalizes loop fugacity. For large values of \(\varepsilon\) (isotropic case), the suppression of nearest-neighbor bonds results in the emergence of antiferromagnetic sub-lattice ordering at high densities. Additionally, anisotropic bond energies lead to directionally selective cluster growth. Finally, we also discuss a lattice gas RG approach and scenarios where bond energy is renormalized across different scales.

Finite-Temperature Spin Exchange-Correlation Kernel of the Uniform Electron Gas

Authors: Pengcheng Hou, Zhiyi Li, Youjin Deng, Kun Chen

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

Summary. This paper uses variational diagrammatic Monte Carlo to provide the first-principles spin exchange-correlation kernel for the uniform electron gas across a wide temperature range. It identifies discrepancies in current warm-dense matter models and demonstrates how thermal effects lead to a transition toward a local, momentum-independent spin response.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The finite-temperature spin response of the uniform electron gas (UEG) is poorly constrained, particularly regarding the momentum-resolved spin exchange-correlation kernel across quantum-degenerate, warm-dense, and classical regimes.

Main result. The study provides first-principles data for the spin XC kernel, revealing that heating suppresses spin-correlation structures and Stoner enhancement, and identifying a 'warm-dense residual' error in current LSDA parametrizations.

Method. Variational diagrammatic Monte Carlo (VDMC) using a screened-interaction formulation and the Principle of Minimal Sensitivity (PMS) to ensure convergence.

Model / system. A 3D unpolarized uniform electron gas (UEG) at metallic densities, covering regimes from quantum-degenerate to classical temperatures.

Key observables. Static spin exchange-correlation kernel Kxc(q;T), spin stiffness (alpha_xc), Stoner enhancement, and the spin-interpolation exponent (p*).

Important parameters / regimes. Wigner-Seitz radius (rs) between 1 and 4, reduced temperature (theta = T/TF) from 0.25 to 8, and momentum transfer (q/kF).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a Yukawa-type screening scale for the VDMC stability and the reliance on the PDW spin-interpolation ansatz for evaluating the spin-polarization dependence.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the dimensionless spin XC kernel for various densities; Figure 2 compares spin stiffness and kernel values against LSDA models (GDB, corrKSDT); Supplemental figures show VDMC convergence and the temperature-driven flattening of the kernel.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of spin-response constraints, describes the VDMC methodology and convergence, presents results for the spin XC kernel across temperature and density, benchmarks these results against existing LSDA models, and discusses the high-temperature classical limit.

Abstract

The finite-temperature spin response of the uniform electron gas (UEG) is a fundamental reference for spin-polarized and magnetized electron liquids, including warm dense matter (WDM), yet it remains far less constrained than charge response. Using variational diagrammatic Monte Carlo, we compute the static spin exchange--correlation (XC) kernel $K_{xc}(q;T)$ of the unpolarized UEG at metallic densities across the quantum-degenerate, warm-dense, and classical regimes. The kernel connects smoothly to zero-temperature spin-response parametrizations at low temperature, while heating suppresses the Fermi-surface-scale spin-correlation structure and weakens the XC-driven Stoner enhancement. Its long-wavelength limit provides a direct response test of the spin stiffness implied by thermal local-spin-density-approximation (LSDA) parametrizations, showing low-temperature consistency while exposing a resolved warm-dense residual in current LSDA parametrizations. In the classical regime, the spin XC kernel becomes nearly local on the Fermi-momentum scale, in sharp contrast to the corresponding charge XC kernel. These results provide a first-principles basis for finite-temperature spin-response theory and magnetized WDM modeling.

Maximum Likelihood Decoding of Quantum Error Correction Codes

Authors: Hanyan Cao, Ge Yan, Yuxuan Du, Feng Pan
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17230v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This review explores the optimal strategy for quantum error correction, known as Maximum Likelihood Decoding, which is computationally difficult but essential for high-fidelity quantum computing. It synthesizes recent breakthroughs from statistical mechanics, tensor networks, and machine learning to show how we can approximate this optimal decoding efficiently. The paper highlights the critical need for low-latency decoders to enable real-time fault-tolerant quantum computation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The computational intractability (#P-hard) of Maximum Likelihood Decoding (MLD) in quantum error correction, which is necessary to account for error degeneracy and achieve optimal error thresholds.

Main result. The paper provides a unified review of MLD approximation strategies, demonstrating how statistical mechanics, tensor networks, and artificial intelligence offer complementary paths to approach MLD accuracy with polynomial complexity.

Method. A survey of three analytical lenses: mapping decoding to disordered spin models (statistical mechanics), approximate contraction of factor graphs (tensor networks), and learning error distributions via generative models (AI).

Model / system. Quantum stabilizer codes, including surface, color, and qLDPC codes, subject to various noise models such as Pauli, phenomenological, and circuit-level noise.

Key observables. Logical error rate, error threshold (p_th), and coset partition function Z(l, s).

Important parameters / regimes. Code distance (d), error rate (p), code rate (k/n), and the Nishimori line in the phase diagram.

Assumptions / limitations. The review focuses primarily on stabilizer codes and Pauli-type noise models, though it discusses the challenges of extending these to non-Pauli effects like leakage and crosstalk.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the surface code decoding pipeline and detector error models; Figure 2 provides a roadmap of the three MLD perspectives; Figure 3 compares different decoding approaches.

Paper structure. The paper is structured as a topical review, first establishing the MLD objective and its complexity, then analyzing it through the lenses of statistical mechanics, tensor networks, and AI, and concluding with hardware integration challenges and future directions.

Abstract

Quantum error correction (QEC) is indispensable for realizing fault-tolerant quantum computation, yet its effectiveness hinges critically on the classical decoding algorithm that interprets noisy syndrome measurements. Among all possible decoding strategies, maximum likelihood decoding (MLD) is provably optimal, since it identifies the logical group with largest likelihood by summing over all possible errors within logical class consistent with the observed syndrome. Despite its optimality, MLD is computationally intractable in general (#P-hard), motivating a rich landscape of exact and approximate algorithms. In this topical review, we provide a unified perspective on MLD by surveying recent advances through three complementary lenses: statistical mechanics, tensor networks, and artificial intelligence. From the statistical mechanics viewpoint, the MLD problem maps onto evaluating partition functions of disordered spin models, enabling exact solutions for certain codes and noise models as well as threshold estimation via phase-transition analysis. From the tensor network perspective, approximate contraction of tensor networks on the code's factor graph yields decoders that closely approach MLD accuracy with polynomial computational cost. From the artificial intelligence perspective, neural-network-based decoders, including autoregressive generative models and recurrent transformers, learn to approximate the MLD distribution from data, achieving high accuracy with the parallelism afforded by modern hardware accelerators. We discuss the connections among these three approaches, review their application to both simulated and experimental quantum hardware, and outline open challenges including real-time decoding, scalability to large code distances, and generalization to high-rate quantum low-density parity-check codes.

McLachlan-projected reduced dynamics for ill-posed Schrödingerized backward diffusion

Summary. The paper demonstrates that projecting Schrödingerized dynamics onto a low-dimensional subspace provides a natural regularization mechanism for ill-posed problems. It proves that this method remains stable even under statistical sampling noise, outperforming unregularized numerical integration.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum algorithms, as it provides a rigorous framework for using subspace-based reduced dynamics to stabilize the simulation of non-unitary or dissipative processes on noisy quantum hardware.

Assumptions / limitations. The channel is quasi-static within each clock period, the transmitter is an ideal single-photon source, and background noise is spatially uniform.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows statistical model fitting (Gamma, lognormal, Gamma-Gamma) to intensity distributions; Fig 2 illustrates focal-plane speckle patterns under different turbulence and focal lengths; Fig 3 compares QBER and SKR for different strategies across turbulence regimes.

Paper structure. Introduction of the SatQKD problem, description of the physical system and turbulence model, presentation of the proposed adaptive detection strategies, statistical analysis of intensity distributions, performance evaluation via Monte Carlo simulations, and discussion of results and limitations.

Abstract

Quantum key distribution (QKD) provides information-theoretic security and satellite-based quantum key distribution (SatQKD) has demonstrated the potential to extend this communication security to intercontinental scales. However, atmospheric turbulence induces significant distortion in the spatial distribution of received optical beams, while background noise remains approximately uniform across the detector plane. As a result, single-element qubit (quantum bit) detection can be frequently dominated by noise due to the random spatial pattern of the imaged wavefront, thereby degrading the system performance. To address this limitation, we propose to exploit the spatial degrees of freedom of single-photon detector arrays to reject the excessive noise while adapting to channel variations induced by turbulence. We develop a threshold-based selection method that only activates detector elements that have higher probability of registering qubits. We evaluate the performance of the proposed noise-rejection QKD schemes using Monte Carlo simulations considering the impact of diffraction and atmospheric turbulence on the transmitted optical beam in the presence of background and dark noise. The results show that, compared to conventional schemes, the proposed noise-rejection strategy effectively reduces the quantum bit error rate (QBER) and improves the secret key rate (SKR) performance, while the performance gains depend on the turbulence condition. These findings demonstrate the potential of adaptive array receiver design to enhance the robustness of the SatQKD system under realistic atmospheric conditions.
