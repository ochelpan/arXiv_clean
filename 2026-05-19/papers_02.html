A Wafer-Scale Heterogeneous III-V-on-Silicon Nitride Quantum Photonic Platform

Patel, Yang Shen, Theodore Morin, Igor Kudelin, Bowen Song, Kaustubh Asawa, John E. Bowers, Kerry Vahala, Nergis Mavalvala, Xinghui Yin, Steven Bowers, Minh A. Tran, Tin Komljenovic, Galan Moody

Summary. The researchers have developed a new way to manufacture quantum photonic chips by bonding III-V materials directly onto silicon nitride wafers. This platform allows for the integration of lasers, nonlinear light sources, and high-efficiency detectors on a single, large-scale chip. This advancement is a major step toward building complex, large-scale quantum computers and communication networks.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum information as it provides a scalable hardware architecture for generating and detecting complex quantum states like squeezed light and entangled photons with extremely high fidelity and low loss.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of creating scalable, wafer-scale quantum photonic circuits that simultaneously provide ultra-low-loss passive routing, high optical gain, and strong nonlinearities without the losses or scalability issues of previous integration methods.

Main result. The demonstration of a wafer-scale III-V-on-SiN platform that integrates high-efficiency nonlinear elements, lasers, and detectors, achieving 15x brighter entanglement sources and near-unity quantum efficiency in photodetectors.

Method. Wafer-scale manufacturing using direct bonding of III-V epitaxial stacks onto foundry-fabricated SiN circuits, utilizing adiabatic inverse tapers for interlayer coupling and self-injection locking for laser noise reduction.

Model / system. A heterogeneous photonic platform consisting of ultra-low-loss Silicon Nitride (SiN) waveguides integrated with III-V materials (InGaP and InP) for gain, nonlinearity, and detection.

Key observables. SiN waveguide loss, InGaP waveguide loss, interlayer coupling loss, SHG efficiency, entangled-photon brightness, two-photon entanglement fidelity, laser linewidth, and photodetector quantum efficiency.

Important parameters / regimes. SiN loss < 1 dB/m, InGaP SHG efficiency > 35,000 %/W, entanglement fidelity of 99.9%, photodetector quantum efficiency > 99%, and laser linewidth in the hertz-level regime.

Figures summary. Figure 4b shows the integrated homodyne detector setup and a model for squeezing degradation; Figure 4c displays photodetector bandwidth and noise; Figure 5a illustrates the vision for a fully integrated system.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of scalable quantum photonics, describes the fabrication and architecture of the III-V-on-SiN platform, details the characterization of nonlinear sources and active components, analyzes noise and efficiency in the detection system, and concludes with a roadmap for large-scale integration.

Abstract

Heterogeneous integration of gain and strongly nonlinear materials with ultra-low-loss silicon nitride (SiN) photonics offers a route to scalable quantum circuits, but concurrent wafer-scale manufacturability, low interlayer loss, and high performance have been challenging to realize. Here we demonstrate a wafer-scale III-V-on-SiN quantum photonic platform that directly integrates III-V layers to foundry-fabricated SiN circuits. The SiN layer provides 200-300 nm thick waveguides with $<1$ dB/m loss and a mature passive photonics ecosystem, while III-V materials provide large $χ^{\left(2\right)}$ and $χ^{\left(3\right)}$ nonlinearities for parametric gain, frequency conversion and quantum light generation. Adiabatic interlayer couplers yield $<25$ mdB loss to InGaP waveguides and resonators with intrinsic quality factors exceeding $10^6$, enabling $15\times$ brighter entanglement sources and efficient nonlinear conversion on SiN. Integrated components--including low-loss beam splitters, waveguide crossers, and tunable interferometers--are complemented by III-V lasers and InP photodetectors with amplifiers achieving up to $99^{+1}_{-12}\%$ quantum efficiency and $3$ GHz bandwidth. This architecture unites ultra-efficient sources, nonlinear elements and detectors on a wafer-scale, low-loss platform, establishing a path toward large-scale, low-noise quantum photonic systems.

Summary. The paper presents an adaptive filtering framework designed to track dynamic magnetic fields in real-time using spin-noise spectroscopy. By dynamically estimating measurement noise, the method overcomes the limitations of traditional filters and can reconstruct weak signals even when the noise levels change abruptly. This allows for the detection of magnetic field variations that are otherwise hidden below the intrinsic sensitivity of standard spectroscopic methods.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in quantum sensing and open quantum systems as it provides a robust way to perform real-time parameter estimation in the presence of non-stationary noise and unknown signal dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Extracting weak, time-varying magnetic field signals from high-noise environments where the signal-to-noise ratio is extremely low and the noise statistics may be non-stationary.

Main result. The development of an Adaptive Extended Kalman Filter (AEKF) that successfully tracks dynamic magnetic fields, even with massive measurement noise mismatch and without prior knowledge of the signal waveform, outperforming conventional spin-noise spectroscopy.

Method. An Adaptive Extended Kalman Filter (AEKF) utilizing the Sage-Husa algorithm and innovation-based adaptive estimation (IAE) with a sliding window to dynamically update measurement noise covariance.

Model / system. An 87Rb atomic vapor cell in a magnetic shield using Faraday rotation for detection, where the spin dynamics are governed by the Bloch equations.

Key observables. Photocurrent (capturing polarization fluctuations via the Faraday effect) and Larmor precession frequency.

Important parameters / regimes. Larmor frequency, relaxation term, spin-noise fluctuation matrix, and measurement noise intensity.

Assumptions / limitations. The magnetic field is assumed to be a slowly time-varying variable, and measurement noise is assumed to be Gaussian-distributed.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the experimental setup and agreement between experimental and simulated spin noise spectra; Fig 2 demonstrates AEKF robustness to noise mismatch and sudden disturbances; Fig 3 shows tracking of various field models; Fig 4 presents experimental reconstruction of random-walk and earthquake-like magnetic signals.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of weak signal extraction, proposes the AEKF framework, validates the method through numerical simulations of various field dynamics, and concludes with experimental demonstration using Rb vapor cells.

Abstract

The extraction of weak signals plays a crucial role in quantum precision measurement, where the estimation results are often limited by low signal-to-noise ratios. Here, we demonstrate a parameter-estimation framework based on the adaptive extended Kalman filter for dynamic magnetic-field estimation in quantum systems using spin-noise measurements -- a challenging regime characterized by weak signals. By modeling the magnetic field as an unknown parameter, the proposed approach alleviates model dependence in state estimation. Furthermore, by introducing an adaptive algorithm with real-time noise estimation, our method overcomes the measurement noise intensity constraints of conventional extended Kalman filtering and enhances its practical applicability. Numerical simulations covering three representative magnetic-field dynamics validate the capability of the proposed framework, while experimental results demonstrate successful tracking of a seismo-magnetic-like signal beyond the intrinsic sensitivity of conventional spin-noise spectroscopy.

Basis- and Channel-Selective Quantum Photodetection

Summary. This paper proposes a generalized theory of photodetection that includes both electric and magnetic field sensitivities. It demonstrates that by engineering these responses, one can actively control the measurement basis of single photons and create detectors that absorb light without emitting detectable radiation. This offers a new design principle for advanced quantum sensors and metamaterial-based detectors.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new way to think about detector engineering in quantum optics and circuit QED, showing that the detector architecture itself can be used as a tool to select measurement bases and control light-matter interaction channels.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Standard quantum photodetection models (Glauber theory) rely solely on electric-field response, which fails to account for modern engineered detectors that are sensitive to both electric and magnetic fields.

Main result. The authors develop a generalized framework where the detector's electric and magnetic responses can be used to rotate the measurement basis and selectively suppress specific radiative output channels.

Method. The study uses an extension of Glauber's theory of optical coherence, POVM formalism, and input-output theory to analyze detection probability and absorption.

Model / system. The framework is applied to three scenarios: a far-field two-source dipole geometry, a two-mode single-photon Hilbert space, and a lossy resonant detector model with electric and magnetic coupling.

Key observables. Detection probability, first-order visibility, path distinguishability, and absorption fraction.

Important parameters / regimes. The complex parameter zeta (ratio of magnetic to electric response), radiative linewidths (gamma_e, gamma_m), and internal decay rate (gamma_i).

Assumptions / limitations. The model is presented as an effective description of engineered electromagnetic responses.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the two-dipole geometry and detection amplitude cancellation; Figure 2 illustrates detector-defined interference, visibility/path-bias relations, and Bloch sphere representation; Figure 3 shows radiative rates and the relationship between output channels and absorption.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a generalized detection operator, applies it to far-field interference, extends the analysis to single-photon basis rotation, investigates resonant absorption and channel selection, and concludes with a design principle for engineered detectors.

Abstract

Photodetection converts optical quantum states into measurement events, but the usual electric-field response model becomes restrictive when the detector response is shaped by cavity, superconducting, or metamaterial engineering. We develop a generalized quantum photodetection framework in which electric and magnetic field amplitudes contribute coherently to the detection operator, and analyze it in a far-field two-source geometry, a two-mode single-photon setting, and a lossy resonant detector model. The far-field reference case exhibits complete detector-amplitude cancellation, absent in the electric-only Glauber response, while the single-photon model shows that the detector continuously rotates the effective measurement basis and controls the first-order visibility via an exact closed-form law. In the resonant realization, a monitored radiative output channel can be dark while the detector remains internally excited and absorptive, with unit absorption of the matched input mode at critical coupling. These results identify basis-selective readout and channel-selective absorption as experimentally relevant signatures of engineered electric-magnetic photodetection.

Summary. The paper introduces a new adiabatic quantum algorithm for the Maximum Independent Set problem using local, degree-dependent detuning in Rydberg atom arrays. By engineering the Hamiltonian to favor low-degree nodes, the method suppresses trap states and significantly improves the success probability and scaling compared to traditional global control methods.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in many-body dynamics and quantum control, as it demonstrates how spatially-dependent Hamiltonian engineering can be used to manipulate spectral gaps and suppress transitions into unwanted many-body states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Improving the efficiency of Adiabatic Quantum Computation (AQC) for solving the Maximum Independent Set (MIS) problem by addressing the shrinking spectral gap and the presence of trap states.

Main result. The proposed Local Degree-dependent AQC (LD-AQC) outperforms traditional global controls, achieving higher success probabilities and a 25% reduction in the fidelity decay rate as problem hardness increases.

Method. The authors implement a Hamiltonian engineering approach using degree-dependent local detuning profiles that preferentially excite atoms with low local degrees, combined with a classical preprocessing algorithm (Algorithm 0) to optimize detuning parameters.

Model / system. The study uses Rydberg atom arrays (neutral atom arrays) where the MIS problem is mapped to the preparation of a state with maximal Rydberg excitations under a Hamiltonian with Rydberg-Rydberg interactions.

Key observables. Success probability (P_MIS), approximation ratio error, average infidelity, and the hardness parameter (HP_trad and HP_LD).

Important parameters / regimes. Local degree (d_i), detuning profiles (linear, exponential, power-law), protocol duration (t_f), and the energy difference function (D_k(a)).

Assumptions / limitations. The existence of optimal parameters (a*) is assumed for general detuning functions; the Rydberg blockade interaction is assumed to be sufficiently strong (U > delta_0^2 * f_max) to prevent unwanted excitations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the energy spectrum lifting degeneracy in the IS band, benchmarking plots showing superior success probability and lower error for LD-AQC, and scaling analysis of the success probability metric against hardness parameters.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the MIS problem in AQC, describes the LD-AQC Hamiltonian engineering method, presents a classical algorithm for parameter optimization, provides mathematical proofs for energy gap maintenance, and concludes with numerical simulations and scaling analysis.

Abstract

We present a hybrid adiabatic algorithm for maximum independent set (MIS) using Rydberg atom arrays. We engineer local controls that preferentially excite atoms with few neighbors, which represent graph nodes with small degrees. Numerical simulations show that the designed pulses accelerate convergence to the MIS state and suppress population in trap states. We obtain higher success probabilities than traditional global controls and a $25\%$ reduction in fidelity decay rate as problem hardness increases.

Abstract

Quantum contextuality provides a fundamental signature of nonclassical behavior that cannot be explained by noncontextual hidden-variable models. We propose and experimentally implement a linear-optical setup for demonstrating Kochen-Specker contextuality via a violation of the KCBS inequality using single photons. Our scheme employs sequential measurements realized with linear-optical networks and on-off photodetectors. Our experimental results demonstrate a clear violation of the KCBS inequality and robustness against photon loss. Beyond fundamental investigations, the proposed setup provides a practical tool for probing non-classicality and photon-number statistics of quantum states, which in turn enables the verification of single-photon sources.

Summary. This paper investigates the 'quantum magic' of the Hubbard dimer, demonstrating that non-stabilizerness is a fundamental resource distinct from entanglement and non-Gaussianity. Abstract

We study the non-stabilizerness (quantum magic) content of the Hubbard dimer, an analytically solvable, yet completely non-trivial, model of strongly correlated fermions. We can access zero- and finite-temperature properties as well as the time evolution in a quantum quench protocol. We evaluate local and nonlocal non-stabilizerness using both the robustness of magic and the stabilizer Renyi entropy, demonstrating how the latter often fails in detecting the mixed stabilizer states that are typically found in this kind of systems. Finally, we compare the non-stabilizerness with other genuine resources of quantum-state complexity, i.e., the fermionic non-Gaussianity and the superselected two-site entanglement. Our findings corroborate the notion of non-stabilizerness as a fundamentally different quantum resource, able to give profound insights that are missed by more traditional information-theoretic quantities.

Zeno-Assisted Quantum Heat Engines

Authors: Selma Memić, Rafael Wagner, Susana F. Huelga, Martin B. Plenio
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18367v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

Summary. This paper introduces a new method for 'lubricating' quantum heat engines using Quantum Zeno Dynamics. By coupling the engine to an auxiliary system and monitoring it frequently, the authors show it is possible to suppress quantum friction and achieve high efficiency even during fast, finite-time operations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified framework connecting quantum control (shortcuts to adiabaticity) with quantum thermodynamics and the Zeno effect, offering a new way to suppress dissipation in driven open systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to mitigate 'quantum friction' (non-adiabatic transitions and coherences) in finite-time quantum heat engines to maintain high efficiency and power.

Main result. The authors propose a 'Zeno-assisted' lubrication protocol using strong coupling and frequent monitoring to implement a shortcut to adiabaticity, effectively recovering Otto efficiency at finite driving speeds.

Method. The study uses Quantum Zeno Dynamics (QZD) and the framework of adiabatic transporters to derive an effective Hamiltonian in the strong-coupling and frequent-monitoring limits.

Model / system. A tripartite quantum Otto engine consisting of a single-qubit working medium coupled to two thermal baths and an auxiliary 'lubricant' qubit system.

Key observables. Work extraction, efficiency, power, entropy production, and heat exchange.

Important parameters / regimes. Cycle duration (tau), coupling strength (Gamma), energy gaps (omega, Omega), and temperature (Th, Tc).

Assumptions / limitations. Markovian Lindblad dynamics for thermalization, the strong-coupling limit (Gamma to infinity), and the Zeno limit (frequent monitoring).

Figures summary. Figure 14 illustrates the competition between the strong-coupling regime and the Zeno limit, showing how monitoring in the computational basis can suppress the lubrication effect.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of quantum friction, describes the Zeno-assisted protocol, compares it to dephasing-assisted methods, analyzes thermodynamic costs, and provides mathematical proofs in the appendices.

Abstract

Finite-time quantum heat engines (QHEs) typically extract less work than their quasistatic counterparts because fast driving generates coherences and non-adiabatic transitions during the work strokes, a phenomenon commonly referred to as quantum friction. Quantum lubrication denotes a broad class of strategies that use auxiliary systems or controls to mitigate this loss. In this work, we introduce a lubrication protocol based on the quantum Zeno dynamics (QZD). By coupling the working medium to an auxiliary lubricant system and frequently monitoring the lubricant, we confine the joint evolution to a Zeno subspace and obtain an effective shortcut to adiabaticity during the work strokes of a QHE running an Otto cycle. In the ideal Zeno limit, the protocol reproduces the transitionless dynamics required to preserve populations in the instantaneous energy basis and recover the Otto efficiency at finite stroke duration. We also analyze several implementation-dependent thermodynamic costs, including switching, driving, monitoring, and imperfect thermalization, in order to assess how these costs constrain the practical gains in efficiency and power. Our results identify QZD as a conceptually distinct route to quantum lubrication and highlight quantum heat engines as a useful setting in which to study the interplay between strong coupling, measurement, and quantum thermodynamic control.

Summary. This paper uses ultrafast laser pulses to perform spectroscopy on collective excitations in superfluid helium far from equilibrium. Abstract

Ultrafast dynamics of collective excitations in superfluids remains largely unexplored beyond the roton regime, despite its importance for understanding nonequilibrium processes in these systems. Here, we employ ultrafast coherent control with sequences of femtosecond pulses to perform spectroscopy of multiple branches of the Landau excitation spectrum in superfluid helium far from equilibrium. By measuring the time-resolved optical birefringence, we track the nonequilibrium dynamics of maxon pairs and Pitaevskii plateau excitations alongside the previously studied roton pairs, revealing surprisingly strong binding energy of maxon pairs, their extremely short lifetime, and the influence of the quasiparticle effective mass on the phase of the coherent response. These results demonstrate the ability to extract previously inaccessible information about collective excitations in a strongly interacting quantum fluid by probing its nonequilibrium dynamics on femtosecond and picosecond timescales.

Abstract

Integrated time-bin-entangled photon-pair source with cavity-enhanced nonlinear optical processes is essential for quantum information technologies. By using local droplet etching on GaAs(111)A substrates, the researchers achieved high-quality single-photon emission with low asymmetry. This is a significant step toward building long-distance quantum communication networks using existing fiber technology.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum networks as it provides a scalable material platform for generating high-purity single photons at the telecom C-band, which is essential for integrating quantum nodes into existing fiber-optic infrastructure.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for high-quality, strain-free single-photon emitters operating in the telecommunication C-band (1550 nm) for low-loss, long-distance quantum communication networks.

Main result. Successful fabrication of strain-free, symmetric InGaAs/InAlAs quantum dots on GaAs(111)A substrates that emit in the 1400-1600 nm range with high single-photon purity (g2(0) = 0.141).

Method. The researchers used Local Droplet Etching (LDE) via Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) to create nanoholes, followed by filling them with InGaAs, and validated the results using 8-band k.p simulations and optical spectroscopy.

Model / system. Strain-free, C3v-symmetric In0.7Ga0.3As/In0.7Al0.3As quantum dots grown on a thin metamorphic buffer layer on vicinal GaAs(111)A substrates.

Key observables. Emission wavelength (1400-1600 nm), exciton lifetimes (1.3-1.9 ns), linewidths (approx. 300 ueV), second-order autocorrelation g2(0), and degree of linear polarization (DOLP).

Important parameters / regimes. Target wavelength of 1550 nm, substrate temperature of 450-500 C, and QD height of approximately 6 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the envelope function approximation (EFA) within an eight-band k.p framework to model electronic properties.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the QD fabrication schematic; Figure 2 displays AFM morphology of nanoholes and simulated emission properties; Figure 3 shows micro-photoluminescence spectra and photon statistics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of C-band emission, describes the LDE growth process and metamorphic buffer layer, presents morphological characterization via AFM, details the k.p simulations, and concludes with optical characterization of the emitters.

Abstract

Non-classical photon sources made of semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) emitting in the telecommunication C-band are crucial components for low-loss, long-distance photonic quantum communication networks. Here we designed and fabricated strain--free In$_{0.7}$Ga$_{0.3}$As/In$_{0.7}$Al$_{0.3}$As QDs grown on GaAs(111)A substrates working as single-photon emitters in the 1550 nm window. The QDs were grown via local droplet etching method in a molecular beam epitaxy environment, employing a thin In$_{0.7}$Al$_{0.3}$As metamorphic buffer layer with the same lattice constant of the QD material, thus allowing for a completely strain--free self-assembly of the QDs. The QDs exhibit a C$_{3v}$ symmetry with a ground state emission in the 1400--1600 nm range. The exciton lifetimes of $\approx$ 1.3--1.9 ns and linewidths as low as $\approx$ 300 $μ$eV show the good quality of the fabricated QDs. Second-order autocorrelation measurements under pulsed excitation confirmed the single-photon purity of the emitters, yielding a $g^{(2)}(0)$ value of $0.141 \pm 0.027$

A Compact, Robust, and Tunable Open Microcavity Platform for Solid-State Quantum Electrodynamics

Authors: Thi D. Hoang, Fateme Mahdikhany, Zixuan Wang, Richard Mirin, Kevin Silverman, Poolad Imany, Shuo Sun

Summary. The researchers have created a tiny, vibration-resistant mechanical assembly for open microcavities that works reliably in standard cryostats. This platform allows for easy tuning of cavity resonance and has successfully demonstrated strong coupling with quantum dots. It offers a scalable path toward portable quantum light sources and quantum computing components.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a highly stable and scalable hardware platform for cavity QED, which is essential for implementing spin-photon interfaces and quantum networks in solid-state systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Operating open microcavities at cryogenic temperatures is challenging due to extreme sensitivity to mechanical vibrations and the need for bulky, custom vibration-mitigation setups.

Main result. The authors developed a compact, robust, and tunable mechanical host for an open microcavity that shows no vibration-induced broadening in a standard cryostat and achieves cooperativity exceeding unity with an InGaAs quantum dot.

Method. The platform uses a piezo-driven assembly to tune cavity resonance in situ and employs cross-polarized transmission spectroscopy and photoluminescence to characterize the emitter-cavity coupling.

Model / system. The system is a plano-concave Fabry-Perot resonator consisting of a GaAs/AlAs DBR bottom mirror and a microscale concave top mirror, coupled to InGaAs quantum dots embedded in a p-i-n junction.

Key observables. Cavity resonance, quantum-dot-induced transmission dips (dipole-induced transparency), cavity finesse, and cooperativity.

Important parameters / regimes. Finesse > 1,000, coupling strength g/2pi = 1.37 GHz, cooperativity C > 1, and a tuning range of 3 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. The theoretical model for fitting spectra omits inhomogeneous spectral diffusion, and the model assumes the weak excitation limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the microcavity schematic and electric field profile; Figure 2 shows PL spectra and cavity modes; Figure 3 displays transmission spectra showing QD-induced dips, saturation, and Fano-like features; Supplemental figures show device yield and Stark tuning.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the vibration-mitigation challenge, describes the fabrication and architecture of the microcavity and mechanical host, presents characterization of cavity stability and tuning, demonstrates quantum dot coupling and nonlinear response, and concludes with scalability and device yield analysis.

Abstract

Open microcavities provide a powerful platform for studying cavity quantum electrodynamics in solid-state systems. However, operating open microcavities at cryogenic temperatures, as required for many solid-state quantum emitters, typically demands bulky and cryostat-specific vibration-mitigation setups. Here we report a compact, robust, and tunable mechanical host for an open microcavity. The complete mechanical assembly fits within a footprint of $1'' \times 1'' \times 0.5''$. Using this mechanical host, we observe no vibration-induced cavity broadening for an open microcavity with finesse exceeding 1,000 without cryostat customization or active locking. The assembly also enables in situ tuning of the cavity resonance over 3 nm, and the resonance of the same cavity remains within this range across multiple cooldowns. To further showcase the capability of this assembly, we demonstrate coupling between an InGaAs quantum dot and an open microcavity with a cooperativity exceeding unity. This platform provides a versatile testbed for fundamental cavity quantum electrodynamics and a scalable route to portable quantum light sources and spin-photon interfaces for quantum repeaters, quantum networks, and photonic quantum computing.
