Structural $f$-divergence: Tight universal bounds for cost function moments and gradients in parameterized quantum circuits

Authors: Tomohiro Nishiyama, Yoshihiko Hasegawa
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18051v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new information-theoretic metric called structural f-divergence to quantify how differences in parameter distributions affect the landscape of quantum cost functions. It provides tight, universal bounds on gradient vanishing and cost concentration, offering new design principles for optimizing parameterized quantum circuits.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The vanishing of cost-function gradients (barren plateaus) in variational quantum algorithms is typically analyzed using high-randomness assumptions, which lack design principles for choosing realistic probability measures on parameter spaces.

Main result. The authors introduce 'structural f-divergence' and establish tight, universal analytical bounds that relate the discrepancy between probability distributions to differences in expected gradient magnitudes and cost-function moments.

Method. The study utilizes information-geometric tools, specifically a new symmetric, normalized f-divergence-based measure, to derive trade-off inequalities using properties like the data processing inequality and Hölder's inequality.

Model / system. The framework is applied to Parameterized Quantum Circuits (PQCs) consisting of n qubits and L layers of alternating gates, using unitary operators $U( heta)$ and Hermitian cost function operators $O$.

Key observables. Expected gradient magnitude, cost-function moments (even and odd), and cost concentration.

Important parameters / regimes. Probability measures on the parameter space (P and Q), number of qubits (n), number of layers (L), and the structural f-divergence metric.

Assumptions / limitations. The function f must be twice-differentiable, strictly convex, and satisfy f(1)=0; equality conditions are demonstrated using a minimal one-qubit, one-layer ansatz.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a conceptual diagram showing how structural f-divergence bounds the differences in cost function moments and expected gradient magnitudes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the barren plateau problem, defines the new structural f-divergence metric, establishes analytical trade-off inequalities for gradients and moments, proves the tightness of these bounds using specific ansatz examples, and applies the framework to derive conditions for avoiding barren plateaus and suppressing noise.

Abstract

The barren plateau phenomenon, in which cost-function gradients of variational quantum algorithms vanish exponentially, remains a central obstacle for near-term quantum computing. Existing analyses typically depend on t-design or Haar-random assumptions and bound quantities at the level of unitary distributions, offering limited insight for designing probability measures on the parameter space of parameterized quantum circuits. In this paper, we introduce the structural $f$-divergence, a symmetric $f$-divergence-based measure between probability distributions on the parameter space. We establish analytically trade-off inequalities that bound the discrepancies in the expected gradient magnitude and in the cost-function moments between a distribution on PQC and a reference distribution; equality is attained by a minimal one-qubit, one-layer ansatz. As applications, we derive necessary conditions on probability measures for avoiding BPs and cost concentration, and sufficient conditions that suppress noise-induced deviations.

Systematic dynamical mean-field theory study of 3d perovskite oxides with uniform Coulomb interactions

Authors: Antik Sihi, Caden Ginter, Kristjan Haule, Subhasish Mandal
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16771v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper shows that using the embedded DMFT (eDMFT) framework allows researchers to perform high-throughput calculations of correlated oxides using fixed, universal interaction parameters. By treating screening self-consistently, the method avoids the need for material-specific tuning and accurately reproduces experimental spectral features. This paves the way for creating large-scale, predictive databases for strongly correlated materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. High-throughput electronic-structure discovery for strongly correlated materials is hindered by the need for material-specific, non-transferable Coulomb interaction parameters (U).

Main result. The study demonstrates that the eDMFT framework allows for a parameter-tuning-free approach using uniform U values (6 eV for metals, 10 eV for insulators) that achieves excellent agreement with experimental spectroscopy.

Method. High-throughput charge self-consistent embedded Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (eDMFT) using CTQMC for the impurity problem and the Maximum Entropy Method for analytical continuation.

Model / system. A series of 12 3d transition-metal perovskite oxides with the formula ABO3, where A = Ca, Sr, La and B = V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni.

Key observables. Total density of states (TDOS), momentum-resolved spectral functions, bandwidth renormalization, and Mott gaps.

Important parameters / regimes. Fixed Hubbard U (6 eV for metals, 10 eV for insulators), Hund's coupling J = 0.8 eV, and magnetic phases (paramagnetic and antiferromagnetic).

Assumptions / limitations. The spectral properties are primarily governed by the dynamical self-energy rather than static interaction-induced energy shifts, allowing for a fixed-U approach.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Overview of the 12 compounds and their properties; Fig 2: Comparison of eDMFT vs ARPES/XPS/BIS for Vanadium oxides; Fig 3/4/5: Comparison of eDMFT TDOS with experimental XPS, XAS, and BIS for various Mn, Fe, and Co oxides in PM and AFM phases.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of parameter-dependency in DFT+DMFT, presents the eDMFT framework and the fixed-U hypothesis, details the systematic study of 3d perovskites, benchmarks results against various spectroscopic experiments, and concludes with the implications for high-throughput materials databases.

Abstract

Strongly correlated transition-metal perovskite oxides pose a fundamental challenge for electronic-structure theory and for large-scale, data-driven materials discovery. While DFT+DMFT provides a quantitatively accurate description of such systems, its high-throughput application is hindered by the need to determine material-specific Coulomb interaction parameters ($U$). First-principles approaches such as the cRPA predict a highly nonlinear and non-transferable evolution of the interaction strength across chemically similar ABO$_3$ perovskites. Here we show that this paradigm does not extend to the large-energy-window eDMFT, which employs highly localized orbitals and treats electronic correlations and screening self-consistently within the same many-body framework. As a result, spectral properties are governed primarily by the dynamical self-energy rather than by static interaction-induced energy shifts. Recent constrained-eDMFT calculations demonstrated that, for broad classes of $3d$ transition-metal oxides, the self-consistently screened Coulomb interactions naturally fall within relatively narrow ranges for correlated metals and insulators. Motivated by these findings, we implement a high-throughput eDMFT framework employing physically derived interaction values of $U=6$ eV for metals and $U=10$ eV for insulators together with $exact$ double counting. We test this framework using systematic high-throughput eDMFT calculations for ABO$_3$ compounds (A = Ca, Sr, La; B = V--Ni) and benchmark the resulting spectral functions against photoemission experiments, where we find overall excellent agreement. Our results establish that charge self-consistent eDMFT enables robust, parameter-tuning-free high-throughput many-body calculations for correlated oxides, opening a practical pathway toward predictive electronic-structure databases for strongly correlated materials.

Taming the 3D Wilson-Fisher Fixed Point via Nonlocal Effective Action

Authors: Hyeon Jung Kim, Seung-Jong Yoo, Jinmo Bok, Lemuel John Sese, Semin Park, Ki-Seok Kim
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18148v1

Summary. The authors propose a new Renormalization Group framework that uses a nonlocal effective action to solve the 3D Wilson-Fisher fixed point problem. By treating the scaling dimensions of both primary and auxiliary fields as independent variables, they eliminate systematic truncation errors. This approach yields critical exponents that are highly accurate when compared to established numerical benchmarks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of accurately characterizing the 3D Wilson-Fisher quantum critical point due to severe truncation errors and unphysical fixed-point divergence in standard non-perturbative Renormalization Group frameworks.

Main result. The identification of a robust, non-trivial physical fixed point with critical exponents (eta ~ 0.038, nu ~ 0.6317) that show exceptional agreement with Quantum Monte Carlo and conformal bootstrap benchmarks.

Method. A multi-loop Renormalization Group approach using a nonlocal effective action ansatz and a two-variable master equation system evaluated up to three-loop order.

Model / system. A 3D relativistic phi^4 theory (Wilson-Fisher universality class) where the quartic interaction is decoupled via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation into a primary field and an auxiliary field.

Key observables. Kinematic anomalous dimension (eta_phi), energy operator dimension (Delta_phi^2), and thermal correlation length exponent (nu).

Important parameters / regimes. Scaling dimensions (Delta_phi and Delta_phi_tilde) treated as independent dynamical variables, and the dimensionless coupling (g_tilde^2).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes a scale-invariant action at the critical point and utilizes a three-loop truncation of the effective action.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the intersection of the two independent field flow equations (gamma_phi = 0 and gamma_phi_tilde = 0) on the scaling dimension plane, marking the stable physical root.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of truncation errors in RG, presents a nonlocal effective action ansatz using Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, details the multi-loop diagrammatic calculations, demonstrates the numerical solution of the two-variable master equations, and validates the results against benchmarked data.

Abstract

We present a novel Renormalization Group (RG) framework based on a nonlocal effective action ansatz to tame the strong coupling dynamics of the three-dimensional relativistic $φ^{4}$ theory. By implementing a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, we decouple the quartic interaction into a system of the primary field $φ$ and an auxiliary field $\varphi \sim φ^2$. Rather than freezing the intermediate scaling dimensions, the nonlocality of our effective action allows both exponents $Δ_φ$ and $Δ_{\varphi}$ to act as fully independent, unconstrained dynamical variables.This nonlocal propagator framework plays a critical role in the RG flow: evaluating self-energies and vertex fluctuations up to the three-loop order, the nonlocality drives precise structural cross-cancellations among multi-loop fluctuations near the Gaussian limit. Solving the resulting closed two-variable master equations isolates a robust, non-trivial physical fixed point at $Δ_φ^{*} \approx 0.981$ and $Δ_{\varphi}^{*} \approx 0.415$. These dynamic exponents yield a kinematic anomalous dimension $η_φ \approx 0.038$, an energy operator dimension $Δ_{φ^2} \approx 1.417$, and-via mass deformation-a thermal correlation length exponent $ν\approx 0.6317$, demonstrating exceptional quantitative agreement with high-precision Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) and conformal bootstrap benchmarks. Our results rigorously confirm that unfreezing the nonlocal degrees of freedom successfully eliminates the systematic truncation errors inherent to conventional local ansatz treatments, simultaneously resolving both the static scaling and thermodynamic flows of the Wilson-Fisher universality class.

The problem of time: a path integral view

Authors: Juan Manuel Diaz, Alejandro Perez
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17935v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper addresses the 'problem of time' in quantum gravity by showing that time evolution is an emergent phenomenon. By using a path integral view, the authors demonstrate that a semiclassical clock state can resolve the ambiguity of backward-propagating amplitudes, effectively recovering standard Schrödinger dynamics from a fundamentally timeless theory.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. How time evolution and a directed arrow of time emerge from a fundamentally timeless, generally covariant quantum system, and how to resolve the 'cosine problem' in path integral representations.

Main result. The authors show that Schrödinger evolution and forward-propagating amplitudes emerge naturally when a clock degree of freedom is prepared in a semiclassical 'good-clock state', which breaks time-reversal symmetry.

Method. A path integral approach using a non-relativistic toy model and semiclassical approximations (stationary phase) to analyze transition amplitudes.

Model / system. A non-relativistic, closed, background-independent quantum system with a fixed total energy E, decomposed into a clock subsystem (harmonic oscillator or free particle) and the rest of the universe.

Key observables. Dirac observables (gauge-invariant quantities), relational observables, and the emergent physical time parameter.

Important parameters / regimes. The semiclassical limit (|z| >> 1), the large energy regime, and the energy-time uncertainty relation.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a non-interacting clock and a fixed energy eigenvalue; it also assumes the existence of suitable semiclassical 'good-clock' states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of how clock boundary states select forward-propagating amplitudes; Figure 2 illustrates different spacetime developments related by 4D diffeomorphisms.

Paper structure. The paper reviews general covariance and timelessness, critiques existing classical and quantum clock approaches, defines the cosine problem, and then uses a non-relativistic model to demonstrate the emergence of time via path integrals.

Abstract

We show that the emergence of time evolution in an otherwise timeless nonrelativistic closed quantum system -- viewed as a poor man's model of generally covariant quantum theory -- can be understood from the perspective of the path integral representation. As often happens in the functional integral approach, this viewpoint offers a more intuitive account of features that become cumbersome in the operator/Hilbert-space formulation. We show how Schrödinger evolution emerges once a clock degree of freedom is identified and placed in a suitable semiclassical good-clock state'. Our analysis has a consequence that extends to path integral formulations of generally covariant systems with action $S$ (including gravity). In such theories certain transition amplitudes take the form $\exp(iS/\hbar)+\exp(-iS/\hbar)$ rather than the expectedforward propagating' $\exp(iS/\hbar)$. This feature, known as the {\em cosine problem}, appears in concrete regularizations of the path integral, for example in the spin foam representation defining the physical inner product between spin network states in loop quantum gravity. Both formally and in explicit regularizations, this apparent difficulty has led some authors to seek modifications of the basic amplitudes to eliminate backward propagation. Our model shows that the cosine problem is instead a natural consequence of time-reversal invariance of the fundamental dynamics together with the time-neutral boundary states commonly used in transition amplitudes. When a suitable clock system is identified and placed in a semiclassical good-clock state', it introduces a time arrow selecting theforward propagating' $\exp(iS/\hbar)$, without modifying the fundamental dynamics. The analysis clarifies how time emerges under suitable conditions and emphasizes that, in the canonical formulation, quantum gravity is fundamentally timeless.

Abstract

Quantum machine learning (QML) has attracted considerable research interest, yet whether it offers practical benefits over classical approaches remains an open question. The choice of data encoding significantly influences QML performance, but why certain encodings outperform others remains poorly understood. We employ Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to discover optimal data encoding circuits for a quantum-classical convolutional neural network (QCCNN) combining a non-variational quantum block for feature extraction with a classical classifier. We further analyze metrics to identify predictors of encoding performance. Entanglement capability and Fourier decomposition provide minimal insight, whereas the effective rank of the feature maps exhibits meaningful correlation and can serve as a threshold criterion to accelerate the search for high-performing encodings. Our findings provide both a practical method for encoding discovery and new insights into what makes data encodings effective in QML.

InterQ: Communication-Aware Scheduling Across Modular QPUs with Classical and Quantum Links

Authors: Vinooth Kulkarni, Jaehyun Lee, Lauren Li, Aaron Orenstein, Xinpeng Li, Shuai Xu, Daniel Blankenberg, Vipin Chaudhary

Summary. The paper presents InterQ, a scheduler designed to manage quantum jobs across modular processors with different communication technologies. It demonstrates how to adaptively partition circuits to balance the costs of communication latency and sampling overhead. The study highlights how different hardware platforms like superconducting and trapped-ion systems offer distinct trade-offs between speed and fidelity.

Why it may be interesting. It addresses the critical scaling bottleneck of moving from monolithic to modular quantum computing, specifically focusing on the interplay between hardware-specific interconnects (photonic vs. classical) and computational throughput.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently scheduling and partitioning quantum workloads across modular QPU architectures that use heterogeneous communication links (classical vs. quantum).

Main result. The InterQ scheduler optimizes the trade-off between runtime, fidelity, and communication overhead, revealing that superconducting systems minimize runtime while neutral-atom systems achieve the highest fidelity.

Method. A communication-aware, iterative scheduling framework (InterQ) that jointly optimizes job placement, adaptive circuit cutting, and communication mode selection (LO, LOCC, or QComm) using a discrete-event simulation.

Model / system. Modular quantum architectures including superconducting (LOCC-based), trapped-ion (QComm-based), and neutral-atom (hierarchical/optical link) systems.

Key observables. Throughput, latency (makespan), fidelity (LPST, TiIF), queue time, and communication costs (Bell-pair consumption, synchronization delay).

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit capacity, communication bandwidth, entanglement generation rates, sampling overhead (scaling with number of cuts), and synchronization constraints.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a shared quantum cloud environment where jobs compete for limited communication resources and treats entanglement generation as a resource-constrained service.

Figures summary. Figure 1 contrasts LO vs. LOCC execution semantics; Figure 2 illustrates the QComm abstraction with shared Bell-pair resources.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of modular scheduling, defines the InterQ framework and its optimization objectives, details the hardware-specific communication models (LO, LOCC, QComm), presents a simulation-based evaluation across various hardware platforms and workloads, and concludes with an analysis of architecture-dependent trade-offs.

Abstract

As quantum computing scales toward practical workloads, future systems are expected to move beyond single monolithic processors toward modular architectures that connect multiple QPUs. Different platforms realize this modularity through different communication models: superconducting systems rely on real-time classical links and dynamic-circuit coordination, trapped-ion systems use photonic interconnects for remote entanglement, and neutral-atom systems provide strong intra-core connectivity with proposed optical links for inter-core communication. This heterogeneity makes communication-aware scheduling essential for shared modular quantum cloud environments. We present InterQ, a communication-aware scheduler for modular QPU architectures with heterogeneous communication models. InterQ jointly considers qubit capacity, placement, parallel execution, and communication-driven dependencies across distributed subcircuits, while enabling adaptive circuit cutting to reduce makespan while balancing fidelity and communication overhead. The framework distinguishes classical-link execution, where measurement and feedforward impose synchronization constraints, from quantum-link execution, where entanglement distribution and state transfer determine coordination cost. Using a unified simulation framework to compare superconducting, trapped-ion, and neutral-atom modular systems, InterQ shows how communication models and scheduler-driven cutting decisions affect throughput, latency, and fidelity. Across evaluated workloads, InterQ exposes an architecture-dependent tradeoff: neutral-atom modular QPUs achieve the highest fidelity, superconducting systems minimize runtime, and trapped-ion systems provide a balanced intermediate profile across fidelity and makespan.

qstack: Compositional End-to-End Compilation for Fault-Tolerant Quantum Programs

Authors: Andres Paz, Dan Grossman
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16595v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces qstack, a new compiler framework designed to automate the complex task of composing quantum error correction layers with hardware-specific instructions. By treating classical feedback as opaque callbacks, it allows for a seamless, automated pipeline that handles the transition from high-level logical gates to physical hardware operations. This approach significantly reduces the manual effort required to design and implement multi-layered fault-tolerant quantum programs.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of composing multiple layers of quantum error correction (QEC) and hardware-specific ISA translations without manual, error-prone adaptation of the classical feedback logic required for syndrome measurement.

Main result. The development of qstack, a compiler framework that uses a callback wrapping mechanism to enable automated, compositional, end-to-end compilation from high-level gates to hardware-native instructions.

Method. A kernel-based intermediate representation (IR) where classical logic is accessed via opaque callbacks, which are automatically wrapped by compiler passes to decode measurement results and re-compile dynamically generated kernels.

Model / system. A fault-tolerant quantum computing framework supporting various gate sets (Toy, Clifford) and error-correcting codes (bit-flip, phase-flip, Steane, and Shor codes) targeting platforms like trapped-ion and neutral-atom hardware.

Key observables. Measurement stack (classical outcomes) and kernel stack (hierarchy of nested kernels and qubit allocations).

Assumptions / limitations. The classical logic is treated as an opaque callback; the framework assumes a LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) discipline for measurements.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows gate definitions; Figure 2 shows a histogram of a repeat-until-success program; Figure 3 illustrates the wrapper structure and runtime data flow; Figure 4 defines the operational semantics; Figure 5 shows a compilation graph.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of hybrid quantum-classical compilation, presents the qstack framework and its callback wrapping mechanism, provides formal operational semantics, demonstrates end-to-end compilation pipelines including concatenated QEC codes, and discusses limitations and future integration with OpenQASM 3.

Abstract

Compiling quantum programs for fault-tolerant execution requires transforming high-level operations through multiple abstraction layers: from logical gates to error-corrected encodings to hardware-native instructions. A key challenge is that quantum error correction turns purely quantum programs into hybrid quantum-classical programs, where classical feedback from syndrome measurements drives quantum corrections at runtime. Existing compilation frameworks handle these quantum and classical components separately, requiring manual adaptation of classical logic at each compilation stage, all while preserving program semantics. We present qstack, a compiler framework built around a purely quantum intermediate representation in which classical logic is accessed only through opaque callbacks, written in any classical language. The framework's central mechanism, callback wrapping, enables compositional compilation: each compiler pass automatically adapts both quantum operations and their associated classical callbacks, and any kernel dynamically generated by a callback is compiled through the full pipeline. This allows ISA translation and quantum error correction to be expressed as composable compiler passes, including concatenation of error-correcting codes, without manual intervention. We demonstrate end-to-end compilation from a high-level gate set through Clifford gates to trapped-ion native operations, with bit-flip and phase-flip repetition codes, the Steane code, and the Shor code obtained by composing two repetition passes.

Summary. This paper provides a unified geometric origin for various modified black hole entropy models by linking them to specific scalar-tensor gravity theories. It shows that these information-theoretic entropy proposals correspond to a varying effective gravitational coupling and specific scalar potentials that drive distinct cosmological phenomena like inflation and dark energy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the lack of a consistent geometric/gravitational theory for generalized black hole entropy functionals (such as Barrow or Tsallis-Cirto entropy) and seeks to resolve the thermodynamic inconsistencies that arise when these entropies are used without a proper gravitational framework.

Main result. The author establishes a direct correspondence between various generalized entropy proposals and scalar-tensor gravity theories, demonstrating that these entropies can be realized through a scale-dependent effective gravitational coupling and specific scalar potentials.

Method. The study uses conformal transformations between Jordan and Einstein frames, evaluates the Wald Noether-charge entropy, and maps the Misner-Sharp quasilocal mass to a generalized mass-to-horizon relation.

Model / system. The framework involves scalar-tensor gravity theories in both Jordan and Einstein frames, applied to spherically symmetric spacetimes and spatially flat FLRW cosmologies, incorporating entropy models like Barrow, Tsallis-Cirto, and entanglement-induced corrections.

Paper structure. Abstract

We develop a geometric realization of a broad class of generalized black hole entropy functionals by establishing their direct correspondence with the Misner$-$Sharp quasilocal mass and the Wald Noether$-$charge entropy in scalar$-$tensor theories of gravity. The resulting models feature a scale-dependent effective gravitational coupling, whose functional dependence is determined by the underlying entropy parameters. Within this framework, we derive explicit Einstein-frame scalar potentials: for Barrow entropy, a steep exponential potential; for Tsallis$-$Cirto entropy, an exponential potential governed by the nonextensivity parameter; and for quantum-gravity and entanglement$-$induced corrections, an approximately linear potential. These distinct potentials generate characteristic cosmological phenomenology, with implications for inflationary dynamics, late-time dark-energy behavior, and non-singular bouncing cosmologies. The framework is compatible with current constraints from solar-system tests, big-bang nucleosynthesis, and pulsar-timing observations, and it yields predictions that can be probed by forthcoming observational surveys. In this way, the analysis establishes a unified and geometrically grounded connection between information$-$theoretic entropy proposals and gravitational field theory.

Abstract

We study the quantum Fourier transform (QFT) block-encoding of the semi-discrete fractional Laplacian on bounded domains with open, zero-extension boundary conditions. In the notation of the main construction, the target operator is the finite Toeplitz truncation \(A^{(N)}_{α,h}\) obtained from the full-lattice semi-discrete operator with symbol \(|ξ|^α\). A finite QFT register, however, diagonalizes circulant matrices rather than Toeplitz truncations. The native QFT circuit therefore implements a periodic surrogate \(\widetilde A^{(N)}_{α,h}\), not the open-boundary operator. We identify this mismatch through an exact Toeplitz-to-circulant aliasing identity. To recover the open-boundary action, we zero-pad the state into a larger \(M\)-point QFT register, apply the same Fourier-symbol block-encoding, and compress back to the physical subspace. The resulting compressed block satisfies \(P_{N\to M}^{\dagger}\widetilde A^{(M)}_{α,h}P_{N\to M} = A^{(N)}_{α,h}+E^{(M)}\), where \(E^{(M)}\) is controlled by the tail of the semi-discrete convolution kernel. Thus, the QFT layer implements the fractional symbol, while zero-padding supplies the open-boundary geometry. The construction is an operator-compilation primitive for boundary-aware quantum simulation rather than a complete PDE solver.

Abstract

Reliable early detection of lithium-ion battery degradation requires health indicators that are physically interpretable and computable from routine cycler telemetry without access to the degradation region. We introduce \textsc{CausalHealth}, a framework that applies causal graph discovery and $k$-nearest-neighbour transfer entropy to per-cycle voltage, current, temperature, and resistance time series, and organises twelve resulting anomaly scores into three signal-class bundles (Magnitude-shift, Predictive-residual, Complexity-entropy) -- with Isolation Forest reported separately as it falls below the bundle reliability threshold -- to characterise detection sensitivity across ten commissioning fractions (5--30\,\%). The Magnitude-shift class achieves 100\,\% detection across all seven tested cells spanning LFP (MIT--Stanford MATR) and LCO (NASA PCoE, CALCE CS2) chemistries, with a lead time of up to 402 cycles before conventional capacity-threshold failure on gradual-fade cells. A Reliability-Weighted Master Health Index (RWMHI) -- a cross-bundle fusion of five high-reliability detectors weighted by inverse coefficient of variation -- improves lead time by 15--52 cycles over the class median on long-lived cells while maintaining 100\,\% detection. Validation against electrochemical impedance spectroscopy on an NMC prismatic cell provides independent physical grounding: transfer entropy $\mathrm{TE}(R \!\to\! V)$ correlates with charge-transfer resistance $R_{\mathrm{ct}}$ (pooled $r = +0.990$; temperature-controlled partial $r = +0.898$), and an Arrhenius analysis of both quantities yields an activation energy consistent with published NMC charge-transfer kinetics. These results are evaluated on seven cells across three benchmark datasets.
