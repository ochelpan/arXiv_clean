Localization of a quantum particle in a classical one-component plasma. II. Dynamic Disorder and Temporal Decorrelation

Authors: Yury A. Budkov
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18196v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 3/5 · Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how the movement of ions in a plasma affects the localization of a quantum particle. It demonstrates that while fast particles behave according to static theory, ultra-slow particles experience a suppression of disorder that prevents exponential localization.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental look at how environmental dynamics (temporal decorrelation) can destroy localization, which is highly relevant to understanding decoherence and transport in many-body open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Extending the theory of Anderson localization from a static random potential to a dynamic, time-dependent potential caused by ionic density fluctuations in a plasma.

Main result. The study finds that for slow particles, temporal decorrelation suppresses the Coulomb logarithm and leads to a scaling of the localization length where $\ell \propto k^{-1/3}$, implying that ultra-slow particles are not exponentially localized.

Method. The author uses a path-integral formalism combined with the eikonal approximation, the Random Phase Approximation (RPA), and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem to derive the decay of the Green function.

Model / system. A non-relativistic quantum particle with charge $q_0$ and mass $m$ moving in a classical one-component plasma (OCPS) of mobile ions, where the potential is a Gaussian random field generated by ionic density fluctuations.

Key observables. Averaged retarded Green function, localization length ($\ell$), decay rate ($\Gamma$), and effective dynamic disorder strength ($G_{dyn}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Particle velocity ($v$) vs. ion thermal speed ($v_{th}$), Debye screening length ($\kappa^{-1}$), and ion plasma frequency ($\omega_{pi}$).

Assumptions / limitations. The eikonal (straight-line) approximation is used, neglecting quantum fluctuations of the path, and the plasma is treated within the Random Phase Approximation (RPA).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the dynamic problem, establishes the mathematical framework using path integrals and RPA, analyzes the two velocity regimes (fast vs. slow), and discusses the physical implications and scaling laws.

Abstract

We extend the static theory of disorder-induced exponential decay of the averaged Green function of a quantum charged particle in a classical one-component plasma to the dynamic regime by incorporating the temporal evolution of the ionic density fluctuations within the random phase approximation. The dynamic potential correlator is derived from the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and the Kramers--Kronig relations. Within the eikonal (straight-line) approximation, the effective disorder strength is expressed through the dielectric function of the ion plasma. For particles moving faster than the ion thermal speed, the static Coulomb logarithm is recovered, with the large-distance cutoff replaced by the dynamic scale $v/ω_{pi}$. For slow particles, the Coulomb logarithm disappears completely and the disorder strength becomes proportional to the velocity, leading to a fundamentally different scaling of the localization length. In particular, the strong-disorder length diverges as $k^{-1/3}$ for $v\ll v_{\mathrm{th}}$, whereas it saturates in the static limit, indicating that ultra-slow particles are not exponentially localized in a dynamic plasma. A crossover between the quasi-static and dynamic regimes occurs when the particle speed becomes comparable to the ion thermal speed.

Euler-Maruyama method for non-Wiener processes

Authors: Richard D. J. G. Ho
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16662v1

Summary. This paper introduces a generalized Euler-Maruyama method for simulating stochastic differential equations driven by non-Wiener, non-Gaussian processes. By using increments from Lévy processes like the Gamma distribution, the method more accurately captures the physical reality of systems with skewed fluctuations. It demonstrates that this approach avoids the inaccuracies introduced by standard Gaussian approximations in modeling decay and diffusion.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics who need to simulate non-equilibrium processes where environmental noise is non-Gaussian or heavy-tailed.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the limitations of the standard Euler-Maruyama method when modeling complex systems that exhibit non-Gaussian (non-Wiener) fluctuations.

Main result. The author develops a 'relaxed' Euler-Maruyama method using Lévy processes (such as Gamma processes) that accurately captures the tails and skewness of non-Gaussian noise, providing more physically accurate results for decay processes than standard Brownian motion.

Method. A generalized Euler-Maruyama discretization scheme where increments are drawn from non-Gaussian, infinitely divisible distributions, validated via Kramers-Moyal expansion and comparison with the Central Limit Theorem approach.

Model / system. The method is applied to stochastic differential equations (SDEs) featuring both additive and multiplicative noise, specifically modeling noisy exponential decay and particle decay processes.

Key observables. Probability density functions (PDFs), mean lifetime, variance, skewness, and kurtosis.

Important parameters / regimes. Simulation time step (delta t), decay rates (nu and lambda), and noise variance (sigma^2).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the noise follows a subset of Lévy processes that are infinitely divisible; numerical implementation of the Master equation requires truncation of the Kramers-Moyal expansion.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares PDFs of different processes across methods; Figure 2 shows decay trajectories and the distribution of mean lifetimes; Figure 3 compares the Master equation solution against the exact EM method solution.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the mathematical requirements for a relaxed EM method, moves to testing the method with additive noise models, and concludes with applications to multiplicative noise and validation via the Kramers-Moyal expansion.

Abstract

Descriptions of complex physical or biological systems often include stochastic contributions, and these are commonly simulated using Wiener processes. In many cases however, non-Gaussian fluctuations may originate from non-Wiener processes which remain less explored. The Euler-Maruyama method of discretising stochastic differential equations to non-Wiener processes is generalised. Non-Gaussian noise generated from a subset of Lévy processes can be used simply and often with more physical justification, for both additive and multiplicative noise. An example of this is provided that gives superior physical results compared to using geometric Brownian motion. Finally the results of the additive noise are shown to be equivalent to a derived master equation via the Kramers-Moyal expansion.

Temperature-Controlled Resonance in a Heteronuclear Quantum Gas Mixture

Summary. The paper proposes that temperature can be used to tune single-channel resonances in heteronuclear quantum gas mixtures by reshaping the effective interaction between impurities. This 'Temperature-Controlled Resonance' mechanism explains observed temperature-dependent atom loss in Bose-Fermi mixtures. The study demonstrates that temperature serves as a simple control knob for manipulating effective scattering properties.

Why it may be interesting. It introduces a novel, experimentally accessible way to tune interactions in ultracold gases using temperature rather than magnetic fields, which has implications for controlling many-body dynamics and impurity physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty in systematically controlling single-channel resonances in atomic collisions, which are usually incidental and lack a tuning mechanism like Feshbach resonances.

Main result. The discovery of a Temperature-Controlled Resonance (TCR) where thermal smearing of the Fermi surface reshapes the effective potential, allowing temperature to act as a control knob for resonances.

Method. The authors use a grand potential approach to calculate Casimir-like mediated interactions, the variable-phase equation for scattering theory, and a high-temperature virial expansion to study quench dynamics.

Model / system. A heteronuclear quantum gas mixture consisting of heavy impurities (e.g., 133Cs) immersed in a light, single-component Fermi gas (e.g., 6Li), modeled using the Born-Oppenheimer approximation and a contact pseudopotical.

Key observables. Effective scattering length (a_eff), impurity bound-state energy (E_imp,b), and momentum distribution depletion (delta n_k) following an interaction quench.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), Fermi temperature (T_F), interspecies scattering length (a_IF), mass ratio (eta), and the short-range cutoff (R0).

Assumptions / limitations. The impurities are assumed to be dilute and static, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation is applied, and the interaction is modeled as a contact s-wave potential.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the temperature-dependent Casimir-like interaction; Figure 2 plots the effective scattering length as a function of interaction strength and temperature, highlighting resonance boundaries.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of resonance control, proposes the TCR mechanism via thermal modification of the Fermi sea, provides a theoretical derivation of the effective potential, validates the mechanism against experimental loss data, and explores the dynamical consequences via interaction quenches.

Abstract

Single-channel resonances are fundamental processes in scattering of atoms, yet their occurrence is largely incidental and lacks systematic control. In this Letter, we propose a mechanism to realize a continuously tunable single-channel resonance by controlling the temperature of the heteronuclear mixture. By extending the Casimir-like mediated interaction to finite temperature, we demonstrate that thermal smearing of the Fermi surface reshapes the effective potential between impurities, giving rise to a temperature-controlled resonance (TCR) over a wide parameter range. As a direct consequence, the resonance position shifts systematically with temperature variation, providing a clear experimental signature of this mechanism. We further investigate the quench dynamics of a Bose gas immersed in a Fermi sea and demonstrate that the observed temperature-dependent loss features in recent experiments are consistent with the TCR mechanism. Our results establish temperature as a simple and experimentally accessible control knob for single-channel resonances in ultracold quantum gases.

Localization of a quantum particle in a classical one-component plasma.III. Mutual coherence and coherence degradation in Coulomb-disordered media

Authors: Yury A. Budkov
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18559v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper derives a universal scaling law relating the transverse coherence length of an electron beam to the longitudinal localization length in a Coulomb-disordered medium. It demonstrates that phase decorrelation is driven by the same disorder correlator as single-particle localization. These findings have significant implications for understanding image contrast degradation in liquid-cell electron microscopy.

Why it may be interesting. The paper establishes a formal connection between transverse coherence decay and longitudinal localization, providing a fundamental link between transport phenomena and wave-front decorrelation in disordered media.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the loss of mutual coherence and phase decorrelation in electron beams propagating through Coulomb-disordered media, such as electrolytes and plasmas.

Main result. The derivation of a universal scaling relation for the coherence length rho_c ~ lambda_D * sqrt(ell/L), linking transverse coherence decay to longitudinal localization phenomena.

Method. The study employs the Efimov path-integral formalism, the eikonal approximation, and the saddle-point approximation to evaluate the mutual coherence function.

Model / system. A quantum particle (electron) interacting with a random potential originating from the thermal fluctuations of a classical one-component plasma (Coulomb-disordered medium).

Key observables. Mutual coherence function (gamma), coherence length (rho_c), localization length (ell), and phase structure function (D_phi).

Important parameters / regimes. Debye length (lambda_D), sample thickness (L), electron momentum (k), and the distinction between static and dynamic disorder regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a Gaussian random potential, uses the eikonal (straight-line) approximation, neglects strong multiple scattering and inelastic channels, and assumes a statistically homogeneous medium.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the coherence function via the Green function, applies the Efimov path-integral and disorder averaging, derives scaling laws for static and dynamic regimes, and concludes with an extension to the relativistic regime in an appendix.

Abstract

We derive the mutual coherence function of an electron beam propagating through a static or dynamic Coulomb-disordered medium and show that its decay introduces an intrinsic coherence-reduction mechanism relevant for electron microscopy in Coulomb-disordered media. Using the Efimov path-integral formalism, the coherence length $ρ_c$ is expressed through the same disorder correlator that governs the single-particle localization length $\ell$. For both a static electrolyte and a dynamic plasma we obtain a universal relation $ρ_c \sim λ_D \sqrt{\ell/L}$, where $λ_D$ is the Debye length and $L$ the sample thickness. In the static case $\ell\propto k^{2}$ (electron momentum), whereas in the dynamic slow-particle regime $\ell\propto k$, leading to qualitatively different energy dependences of the coherence scale. The ion thermal velocity cancels out in the final expression, demonstrating a formal connection between transverse coherence decay and longitudinal localization phenomena. Exact analytical results are given for the phase structure function of a model electrolyte, and numerical estimates indicate that disorder-induced phase decorrelation may contribute appreciably to the attenuation of high-spatial-frequency contrast under experimentally relevant liquid-cell electron microscopy conditions. Possible implications for cryo-EM, disordered liquids, soft condensed matter, and biological media are discussed. In an appendix we extend the theory to the relativistic regime relevant for transmission electron microscopy.

Summary. The researchers developed an inverse-design method to create a new type of circular photonic cavity that breaks the trade-off between high photon confinement and efficient light collection. The resulting design significantly improves the Q-factor compared to standard periodic structures while remaining robust to manufacturing imperfections. This is a key step toward building efficient interfaces for quantum information processing.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful computational tool for designing high-cooperativity interfaces, which are critical for scalable quantum networks and light-matter interaction in cavity QED.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The simultaneous optimization of the quality factor (Q-factor) and far-field emission mode in photonic cavities to enable efficient spin-photon interfaces.

Main result. An optimized non-periodic circular cavity achieves a Q-factor approximately ten times higher than traditional bullseye cavities while maintaining a Gaussian-like far-field profile and robustness to fabrication errors.

Method. A gradient-based inverse-design framework using the Guided-Mode Expansion (GME) method combined with automatic differentiation, validated by FDTD simulations.

Model / system. A non-periodic circular ring cavity consisting of a central disk and concentric rings, implemented on a GaAs substrate designed for coupling to InAs quantum dots.

Key observables. Q-factor, far-field mode profile (Gaussian overlap), and collection efficiency.

Important parameters / regimes. Central disk radius, ring widths, ring gaps, and fabrication tolerance (up to +/- 6 nm).

Assumptions / limitations. The GME method is an approximation requiring FDTD validation; the primary demonstration assumes a GaAs material platform.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the bullseye cavity geometry and compares GME/FDTD far-field modes; Figure 2 shows the optimization evolution, resulting geometry, and far-field pattern; Figure 3 presents FDTD resonance spectra and validation; Figure 4 displays single-parameter fabrication tolerance analysis.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the motivation for spin-photon interfaces, validates the GME method against FDTD, describes the multi-objective gradient-based optimization process, presents the final optimized cavity performance, and concludes with a sensitivity analysis of fabrication errors.

Abstract

Spin-photon interfaces, realized by coupling optically active spin systems to photonic cavities, are essential for quantum networking and quantum information processing. Implementing such an interface for polarization-encoded photons requires a cavity that supports arbitrary polarization, provides efficient optical access through its far-field mode, and maintains sufficiently high quality factors to enable high cooperativity with the system's optical transitions. However, inherent trade-offs between the Q-factor and far-field emission mode make the simultaneous optimization of these parameters toward the realization of spin-photon interfaces challenging. In this work, we implement a gradient-based inverse-design framework using guided-mode expansion with automatic differentiation to obtain the geometrical features of a circular ring cavity that supports arbitrary polarization while simultaneously optimizing the cavity quality factor and far-field mode profile. The resulting optimized non-periodic cavity achieves a quality factor of approximately $9,000$, about an order-of-magnitude higher than that of a periodic ("bullseye") cavity while preserving a Gaussian-like far-field emission pattern. Furthermore, by varying the cavity geometry within a $\pm 6$ nm fabrication tolerance, we demonstrate the robustness of the design against fabrication errors and identify the innermost ring width and central disk radius as the parameters with the greatest impact on the quality factor and far-field mode. These results establish guided mode expansion-based inverse design as a powerful and computationally efficient approach for developing high-cooperativity spin-photon interfaces for quantum photonic applications.

Summary. This paper develops a first-principles quantum framework for understanding noise in frequency-comb-based measurements. It demonstrates that the precision of frequency division and spectroscopy depends critically on the comb's spectral shape and the specific type of quantum entanglement used. The work identifies practical routes to surpass the standard quantum limit in next-generation frequency combs.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous bridge between continuous-mode quantum optics and discrete-line metrology, offering new insights into how engineered quantum states like squeezing and entanglement can be utilized in broadband frequency-domain measurements.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a rigorous, first-principles quantum-mechanical framework to account for how optical vacuum fluctuations are transduced into radio-frequency measurement noise in frequency-comb metrology.

Main result. The authors establish a continuous-mode field quantization framework that reveals how spectral envelope shapes and quantum states (squeezing/entanglement) affect the Standard Quantum Limit in optical frequency division and dual-comb spectroscopy.

Method. The study uses continuous-mode field quantization, a comb-line-resolved decomposition of quantum fluctuations, and linearization of the photocurrent operator to trace noise from optical lines to the RF domain.

Model / system. The system is an optical frequency comb modeled as a periodic pulse train with a carrier frequency and repetition rate, analyzed through a 1D continuous-mode electromagnetic field quantization framework.

Key observables. Photocurrent operator, RF/microwave signal noise, phase noise power spectral density, and transmittance estimator SNR.

Important parameters / regimes. Carrier frequency, repetition rate, spectral envelope shape (Gaussian, sech, flat-top), squeezing gain, and absorption depth.

Assumptions / limitations. Negligible dispersion and loss during propagation, wide-sense stationary operators, and a flat detector response where the RF bandwidth is much smaller than the optical carrier frequency.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows time and frequency domain representations of the comb field; Fig 2 illustrates the principle of OFD and noise transduction mechanisms; Fig 3 depicts the DCS experimental setup and noise folding; Fig 4 shows time-domain photocurrent variance for self-referred squeezing.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a fundamental quantization of the electromagnetic field, moves to a comb-line-resolved decomposition, applies this to OFD to analyze spectral shape effects, and then extends the framework to DCS to evaluate quantum enhancement strategies and the impact of sample-induced noise.

Abstract

Frequency combs enable precision measurements across timekeeping, spectroscopy, ranging and astronomy, and are now extending to integrated and field-deployable platforms. Realizing their full performance demands a comprehensive account of the quantum noise that arises when broadband optical fields are converted into finite-bandwidth electrical signals. Here we present a rigorous first-principles quantum-mechanical framework for optical frequency-comb metrology based on continuous-mode field quantization and a comb-line-resolved description of quantum fluctuations. The theory describes how quantum fluctuations of the comb field are transduced into electrical measurement noise. We apply the framework to two canonical settings, optical frequency division (OFD) and dual-comb spectroscopy (DCS), where it reveals two effects beyond semiclassical reach: a dependence of the OFD standard quantum limit on the comb spectral envelope, and a cyclostationary noise penalty that obstructs straightforward squeezing in DCS. These insights identify practical, resource-efficient routes to quantum enhancement through engineered comb states, laying a foundation for the design of next-generation frequency combs operating at and beyond standard quantum limits.

Shortcut-error signatures in coherence-retaining endpoint work quasistatistics

Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18095v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. The paper introduces a new way to detect errors in fast quantum control protocols by using work statistics that retain initial phase information. It proves that these 'coherent' statistics respond linearly to errors, making them much more sensitive than traditional methods that only see quadratic changes in populations. This provides a powerful new diagnostic tool for calibrating shortcuts to adiabaticity.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum control, as it provides a more sensitive, low-overhead method for detecting subtle non-adiabatic errors in fast control protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a sensitive diagnostic to detect and calibrate non-commuting errors in Shortcuts to Adiabaticity (STA) protocols, which traditional population-based measurements fail to detect efficiently.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that coherence-retaining endpoint work statistics provide a first-order (linear) signal of non-commuting errors, whereas traditional population-transfer diagnostics only scale quadratically with the error amplitude.

Method. The study uses perturbation theory and quasiprobability frameworks (Kirkwood-Dirac and Margenau-Hill) to compare the scaling of coherent energy signals against population-based metrics.

Model / system. The theoretical framework is applied to closed-system unitary dynamics, specifically using a qubit and a time-dependent harmonic oscillator as benchmark models for STA protocols.

Key observables. Coherent endpoint work signal (delta W_coh), population excess (Q*^-1), and Kirkwood-Dirac/Margenau-Hill quasiprobability weights.

Important parameters / regimes. Error amplitude (epsilon), error generator (K), initial state phase (theta), and dephasing rate (Gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes closed-system unitary dynamics and focuses on the small-error regime where the linear-versus-quadratic hierarchy is preserved.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the workflow between TPM and coherence-retaining branches; Figure 2 demonstrates the linear vs. quadratic scaling in the oscillator; Figure 5 shows the impact of dephasing on signal contrast; Figure 6 verifies the scaling for waveform distortions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of coherence erasure in TPM, proposes a coherence-retaining diagnostic, provides analytical scaling proofs, validates the method with qubit and oscillator benchmarks, and tests robustness against dephasing and waveform errors.

Abstract

Quantum work statistics differ from classical ones because initial energy coherence matters. The standard two-point measurement (TPM) gives a positive distribution but erases phase information. Coherence-retaining endpoint-work quasistatistics provide a compact probe of shortcut-to-adiabaticity performance. For work defined with respect to a reference Hamiltonian, an exact counterdiabatic shortcut pulls the final reference Hamiltonian back to an operator diagonal in the initial energy basis. Endpoint Kirkwood-Dirac or Margenau-Hill quasistatistics then lose sensitivity to initial coherence and reduce to the TPM result. Imperfect shortcuts restore this sensitivity: a non-commuting control error produces off-diagonal pulled-back Hamiltonian elements at first order in the error amplitude, whereas population-only transition probabilities change only at second order. Harmonic-oscillator and qubit benchmarks confirm this linear-versus-quadratic contrast. The result complements inclusive work-cost analyses: it does not measure the auxiliary field's energetic cost, but provides a phase-sensitive endpoint diagnostic of residual nonadiabaticity.

Where solitons are in a KdV soliton gas

Authors: Benjamin Doyon
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18093v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper solves the problem of identifying individual soliton positions within a dense, interacting gas of KdV solitons. By introducing a fluid-cell projection method, the author proves that local field properties are controlled by nearby solitons, providing a rigorous link between microscopic soliton dynamics and macroscopic kinetic equations.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a rigorous foundation for Generalized Hydrodynamics (GHD), which is a central framework for understanding the non-equilibrium dynamics of 1D integrable many-body systems, including integrable spin chains and quantum gases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Defining the precise spatial positions of individual solitons within a dense KdV soliton gas and establishing a rigorous connection between local field observables and the macroscopic density of states.

Main result. The paper develops a 'fluid-cell projection' method that proves local observables of the KdV field are determined by nearby solitons, and demonstrates that soliton positions satisfy semi-classical Bethe equations, thereby reproducing the kinetic equation of the soliton gas.

Method. The author utilizes a novel tau-function representation for multi-soliton fields, a 'magnifying-glass' construction for local observation, and a 'fluid-cell projection' technique to relate $N$-soliton solutions to the large-$N$ limit.

Model / system. The system is a Korteweg-de Vries (KdV) soliton gas, modeled as the large-$N$ limit of $N$-soliton solutions characterized by spectral parameters (velocities) and impact parameters (positions).

Key observables. Density of states $\rho(\chi, x, t)$, conserved densities $P_k[u]$, and the KdV field $u(x)$ and its derivatives.

Important parameters / regimes. Spectral parameters $\chi$, impact parameters $y$, number of solitons $N \to \infty$, and the mesoscopic scale $L$.

Assumptions / limitations. The results rely on specific conditions regarding the regularity of spectral parameters and the density of solitons; the derivation of the kinetic equation is noted as non-rigorous.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates a 'time-of-flight' thought experiment showing how a localized piece of the field evolves into separated solitons to derive spectral density.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the quasi-particle problem, proposes a fluid-cell projection and magnifying-glass construction, provides mathematical proofs using a new tau-function for support and bounds, and concludes by reproducing the kinetic equation of the soliton gas.

Abstract

The Korteweg-De Vries (KdV) equation is a paradigmatic model of integrable classical fields, admitting solitoning solutions. When many solitons are near to each other, their shapes are modified, and it is not manifest, from the KdV field, where they are. This is a key problem in the analysis of a soliton gas, as its main object, the density of states, is a number of solitons per unit length. How to define solitons' positions at finite densities in the macroscopic limit? A sensible criterium is that, projecting out solitons lying outside a mesoscopic region, the KdV field is unchanged in this region, and the result is a multi-soliton field supported there. In the context of emergent hydrodynamics, this is referred to as a fluid-cell projection. In this paper we solve this problem. We define solitons' positions and a fluid-cell projection, and show that it has these properties, without introducing radiative corrections. We show that the weak limit of conserved densities can be evaluated using the density of states. On large scales the solitons' positions satisfy the semi-classical Bethe equations introduced in the context Generalised Hydrodynamics, that accounts for the two-body scattering shift and encodes factorised scattering. A non-rigorous derivation reproduces the kinetic equation of the KdV soliton gas, first proposed by Gennady El in 2003 using Witham modulation theory from finite-gap solutions. The results hold under simple conditions on spectral parameters, and certain physically natural conditions on impact parameters. No randomness is required. Our proof is based on a novel tau function for the multi-soliton KdV field, which also allows us to obtain new bounds on the growth of the multi-soliton support and on the supremum of the field and its derivatives. We believe the methods are generalisable to other solitonic models.

Network analysis for steady-state current fluctuations under finite affinity: Application to Brownian computation

Authors: Yasuhiro Utsumi
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17838v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new graph-theoretic method to calculate current fluctuations in networks under finite thermodynamic force by using 'twisted' matrices to maintain mathematical orthogonality. It applies this to Brownian computation, demonstrating that the thermodynamic cost of computation is linked to a transition between noiseless and Poissonian noise regimes.

Why it may be interesting. The paper provides a rigorous topological method for calculating fluctuations in non-equilibrium steady states, which is directly applicable to the study of dissipation and noise in open quantum systems and driven many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a graph-theoretic framework to analyze steady-state current fluctuations in Markov master equations under finite thermodynamic force (affinity), where standard orthogonality between cycle and cut spaces is lost.

Main result. The introduction of twisted circuit matrices restores orthogonality and reveals an interference-like effect in the signal-to-noise ratio; specifically, in Brownian computation, a transition from noiseless to Poissonian Fano factor occurs at an affinity equal to the logarithm of the branching factor.

Method. Graph-theoretic analysis using twisted incidence, cycle, and cutset matrices, combined with a duality transformation to express the signal-to-noise ratio as a quadratic optimization problem.

Model / system. A Brownian computation model represented as a directed, tree-like state-transition diagram with exponential backward branching, a single reset cycle, and finite thermodynamic affinity at each transition step.

Key observables. Steady-state edge currents, Fano factor of the reset current, signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and entropy production rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Thermodynamic affinity (or bias beta), branching factor (alpha), and the length of the computation path (l).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a connected graph with a unique steady state, a directed rooted spanning tree, and focuses on the long-measurement-time limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates fundamental cutset vectors and twisted matrix configurations; Figure 3 shows the state-transition diagram of the Brownian computation model; Figure 4 shows the dependence of the average reset current on parameters.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a new twisted matrix formalism for non-equilibrium networks, applies this framework to a specific Brownian computation model, analyzes the resulting phase transitions in current fluctuations and complexity, and compares results against thermodynamic uncertainty relations.

Abstract

A graph-theoretic analysis of the steady-state current noise in master equations under a finite thermodynamic force (affinity) is presented. The incidence matrix twisted by a finite affinity is not orthogonal to the standard cycle space, motivating the introduction of twisted circuit matrices to restore the orthogonality. The resulting twisted-cycle matrix yields an interference-like effect, enabling us to express the signal-to-noise ratio as a quadratic optimization problem in terms of twisted-cycle currents. We apply this framework to a Brownian computation model on a tree-like state-transition diagram with exponential backward branching, finite affinity at each step, and a single reset cycle. In the limit of an infinitely long intended computation path $\ell$, the Fano factor of the reset current undergoes a transition from noiseless to Poissonian behavior at an affinity equal to the logarithm of the number of immediate predecessors $α$. This corresponds to an easy-hard transition in the computational time complexity [K. Okajima, K. Hukushima, arXiv:2512.24728 ], which is not captured by the thermodynamic uncertainty relation. This transition point precisely characterizes the thermodynamic costs of logically irreversible computation: in the absence of affinity, the reset cost scales as $\ln \ell$, whereas reaching the transition point requires a thermodynamic force of order $\ln α$ per step to counteract backward branching.

Authors: Prashant C. Raju

Summary. The study reveals that extreme spatial memory in birds is achieved through a phase transition in the neural population geometry, moving from a disorganized state to a rigid, crystalline structure. This transition, driven by excitatory-inhibitory synergy, allows for a massive increase in memory capacity by using a redundant, topologically stable manifold. This suggests that evolution optimizes memory through the engineering of neural code geometry rather than just increasing neuron counts.

Why it may be interesting. The paper describes a topological phase transition and the emergence of structural rigidity (stiffening) in a high-dimensional network, which is highly relevant to understanding how noise-resilient order emerges in many-body systems and neural networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. How biological memory systems expand information capacity without increasing neural hardware or suffering from catastrophic interference.

Main result. High-capacity memory emerges from a topological phase transition in the neural manifold from a disorganized 'mist' to a rigid 'crystalline' geometry, stabilized by excitatory-inhibitory synergy.

Method. Comparative electrophysiology in chickadees and finches combined with large-scale computational simulations (10,000 configurations) and geometric stability metrics (Shesha).

Model / system. A comparative study of hippocampal populations in food-caching (chickadees) and non-caching (finches) birds, modeled as a transition between a disordered 'mist' regime and a topologically rigid 'crystalline' lattice.

Key observables. Geometric stability (Shesha), intrinsic dimensionality (D), spatial information (bits/spike), Mantel r (topogeographic correlation), and population redundancy.

Important parameters / regimes. Sparsity (rho), topology strength (tau), memory load (M), and excitatory-inhibitory (E-I) subspace orthogonality.

Assumptions / limitations. The computational model is primarily tested on 1D circular tracks; the 2D generalization is assumed based on simulation results.

Figures summary. Figures demonstrate decoding error vs. memory load across different topological regimes, the synergy of E-I populations, and the single-unit signatures (spatial information, regularity) that distinguish the two species.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scaling paradox, presents comparative biological data, details the geometric metrics used, describes the computational phase transition model, and concludes with the implications of the 'geometric tax' for neural coding.

Abstract

Memory systems can store vastly different amounts of information despite similar hardware constraints. Here, we show that superior spatial memory emerges from a discrete stiffening of hippocampal population geometry-a transition from disorganized to crystalline collective coding. Comparing food-caching chickadees to non-caching zebra finches, we found that the caching hippocampus maintains a topologically rigid, "crystalline" geometry with significantly higher geometric stability (Shesha 0.245 v 0.166) and nearly two-fold greater temporal coherence (Shesha 0.393 v 0.209), while the non-caching hippocampus resembles a disorganized "mist." This stability is actively constructed by synergistic circuit dynamics: excitatory neurons form the spatial scaffold while inhibitory populations contribute orthogonal decorrelation, a circuit motif in which excitatory and inhibitory populations occupy largely non-overlapping representational subspaces. A double dissociation with Valiant's Stable Memory Allocator, a model predicting that dedicated neuron ensembles underlie each memory, confirms this advantage reflects continuous topological organization rather than discrete neuron allocation: caching networks exhibit near-zero split-half allocation reliability despite their geometric superiority. Computational modeling across 10k configurations reveals topological rigidity as the mathematical prerequisite for scale: crystalline codes sustain high-fidelity readout beyond M=1k locations while mist codes fail below M=10, a >100-fold capacity advantage. This capacity requires a 169fold representational redundancy: a "geometric tax" stabilizing the manifold against biological noise. These results establish geometric stability as a candidate organizing principle of biological memory: evolution achieves high-capacity memory not by proliferating neurons, but by engineering the geometry of the neural code itself.
