Large-Scale Quantum Kernels for Hyperspectral Data Classification

Authors: A. Delilbasic, A. Miroszewski, A. Wijata, J. Nalepa, J. Mielczarek, M. Riedel, G. Cavallaro
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17587v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that quantum kernel methods can be applied to large-scale hyperspectral imaging tasks without heavy dimensionality reduction. By using tensor network simulations and optimizing kernel bandwidth, the authors show that quantum models can outperform classical baselines in remote sensing classification.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The scalability of quantum kernel methods for high-dimensional hyperspectral data classification and the challenge of 'exponential concentration' where kernel values vanish as qubit counts increase.

Main result. Quantum kernels achieve competitive or superior accuracy compared to classical RBF kernels on Indian Pines and Methane Detection datasets, provided that kernel bandwidth optimization is used to mitigate overfitting and concentration.

Method. Large-scale classical simulation using tensor network contraction (cuTensorNet) and GPU acceleration to evaluate fidelity quantum kernels with a rotation-based encoding ansatz.

Model / system. A quantum kernel support vector machine (QSVM) using a fidelity quantum kernel approach, where hyperspectral bands are mapped to qubits via a parameterized unitary transformation involving single-qubit rotations and CNOT entanglement.

Key observables. Classification accuracy, kernel alignment (A), geometric difference (g), expressibility (epsilon), and kernel value distribution (mean and standard deviation).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (n), kernel bandwidth parameter (c), and penalty term (C).

Assumptions / limitations. The specific family of fidelity kernels used has low entanglement, allowing for efficient classical simulation via tensor networks; the initial state is the vacuum state |0>.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Loschmidt echo approach, the quantum circuit ansatz, the overall processing pipeline, runtime comparisons between statevector and tensor network simulations, and statistical analyses of kernel matrices and accuracy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of high-dimensional QML, details the quantum kernel model and circuit architecture, describes the tensor network simulation strategy, presents experimental results on hyperspectral datasets, and concludes with a discussion on kernel statistics and future directions.

Abstract

Quantum kernel methods have emerged as a promising approach for leveraging high-dimensional feature spaces in machine learning, particularly in domains where classical kernel methods face scalability limitations. In this work, we present the first large-scale study of fidelity-quantum-kernel support vector machines for hyperspectral data classification without requiring heavy prior feature selection or dimensionality reduction. By simulating quantum kernels using tensor network contraction techniques and GPU acceleration, we overcome the computational bottlenecks traditionally associated with quantum models, achieving quadratic scaling O(n^2) in the number of qubits. Our approach enables the evaluation of quantum kernels on hyperspectral data with hundreds of spectral bands, aligning quantum feature spaces with real-world remote sensing applications. We provide an in-depth analysis of kernel bandwidth optimization, demonstrating its crucial role in mitigating exponential concentration effects and ensuring the model's ability to generalize. Experimental results on binary classification (Indian Pines and Methane Detection) and multiclass classification (Indian Pines) demonstrate that quantum kernels achieve competitive performance compared to a broad range of state-of-the-art classical baselines. As illustrative cases, on four 50-band splits selected from Indian Pines, the quantum model achieved a 78.0 pm6.2% accuracy for a binary classification task compared to 72.0 pm5.0% for the standard radial basis function (RBF) kernel. For a four-class classification task, the quantum kernel reached 83.3 pm3.1% accuracy, outperforming several state-of-the-art baselines. On five 75-band splits selected from the Methane Detection dataset, the quantum approach yielded 58.5\pm5.0% accuracy versus 55.1\pm2.5% for the classical counterpart...

LEAD: A Local Ensemble-Assisted Parallel Decoding Framework for Quantum Tanner Codes

Authors: Zhuo-Yan Xiao, Sha Shi, Chen-Peng Huang, Dong-Sheng Wang, Yun-Jiang Wang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17796v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper introduces LEAD, a new decoding framework for Quantum Tanner Codes that uses local, parallelized decoding to estimate error probabilities. By regularizing these local estimates to prevent overconfidence, the method significantly improves error correction performance and reduces decoding time compared to traditional methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing decoding algorithms for Quantum Tanner Codes face a trade-off between exponential computational complexity in local decoders and poor performance in structure-blind iterative decoders due to high degeneracy and short cycles.

Main result. The LEAD framework achieves significantly lower logical error rates and reduced decoding latency compared to standard BP-OSD and BP-LSD baselines by leveraging the code's topological structure.

Method. A two-stage parallel decoding framework that projects the global code onto overlapping local subcodes, aggregates local error probability estimates using a soft-information regularization mechanism, and performs final global decoding.

Model / system. Quantum Tanner Codes, which are a class of quantum LDPC codes constructed via a left-right Cayley complex using a CSS-type structure on vertices, edges, and faces.

Key observables. Logical Error Rate (LER), Frame Error Rate (FER), decoding iterations, and decoding gain (delta_log).

Important parameters / regimes. The scaling parameter alpha (used for regularization), physical error probability p, and the local neighborhood size Delta.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes a depolarizing noise model and relies on the decomposable structure of the underlying Cayley complex.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the quadrangle structure of the Cayley complex; Figure 2 provides a flowchart of the LEAD algorithm; Figure 3 compares LER and decoding iterations for various codes; Figure 5 shows the relationship between decoding gain and subcode FER; Figure 6 demonstrates the impact of the alpha parameter on performance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of decoding Quantum Tanner Codes, describes the geometric structure of the Cayley complex, presents the LEAD decoding framework (local decoding, regularization, and global decoding), provides complexity analysis, and validates the method through numerical simulations and error rate comparisons.

Abstract

Quantum Tanner codes are a recently developed family of quantum error-correcting codes characterized by favorable asymptotic performance characteristics. Despite their theoretical potential, practical decoding algorithms that effectively leverage their structural properties remain limited. This work introduces LEAD (Local Ensemble-Assisted Decoder), a structure-aware decoding framework tailored for quantum Tanner codes. The proposed scheme leverages the decomposable structure of Cayley complexes to project the global code onto overlapping local subcodes defined by vertex neighborhoods, where error probabilities are estimated in parallel. To ensure global consistency, LEAD utilizes the inherent topological symmetry of the complex and introduces a soft-information regularization mechanism to mitigate local overconfidence during information aggregation. This framework enables highly parallelized, low-complexity decoding that is intrinsically compatible with various local search heuristics. Simulation results demonstrate that LEAD achieves significantly lower logical error rates than standard decoding framework while substantially reducing the average decoding latency and iteration count.

Localization Transitions in a Half-Filled Helical Aubry-André Model

Authors: Taylan Yildiz, B. Tanatar, Balázs Hetényi
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18064v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper explores how long-range, helical connections modify the localization transition in a quasiperiodic system. It reveals that helical hopping stabilizes the delocalized phase and that the transition point fluctuates significantly due to resonance effects between the hopping range and the lattice potential.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how long-range helical hopping and the winding range N influence the delocalization-localization transition in a 1D quasiperiodic lattice.

Main result. Increasing helical hopping strength stabilizes the extended phase, and the critical potential exhibits non-monotonic spikes due to resonant helical sampling of the quasiperiodic landscape.

Method. The authors use the modern theory of polarization to construct a geometric Binder cumulant and employ a Zeckendorf-shift construction with Fibonacci system sizes to perform controlled thermodynamic limit extrapolations.

Model / system. A one-dimensional helical Aubry-André model featuring an incommensurate onsite potential and an Nth-neighbor hopping term that connects successive windings of a helical chain.

Key observables. Geometric Binder cumulant (U4), polarization amplitudes (Zq), normalized Fermi gap, and single-particle energy spectrum.

Important parameters / regimes. Quasiperiodic potential strength (Delta), Nth-neighbor hopping strength (JN), helical range (N), and system size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on non-interacting fermions at half-filling and utilizes periodic boundary conditions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the helical lattice geometry; Figure 6 displays the Binder cumulant and Fermi gap for different hopping strengths; Figure 7 illustrates the energy spectrum changes; Figure 8 shows the Binder cumulant for various Fibonacci sizes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the helical Aubry-André model, details the polarization-based diagnostic methods and thermodynamic limit construction, presents results on phase stabilization and commensurability-induced spikes, and concludes with a robustness analysis via finite-size scaling.

Abstract

We study localization in a one-dimensional quasiperiodic lattice obtained by extending the Aubry-André model with an additional $N$th-neighbor hopping term of strength $J_{N}$. This long-range tunneling couples successive windings of an effective helical chain and introduces a second control parameter beyond the quasiperiodic potential strength $Δ$. Working with noninteracting fermions (typically at half filling), we diagnose the delocalization-localization transition using extensions of the modern theory of polarization. Specifically, we compute the polarization amplitudes of the many-body Slater-determinant ground state and construct a geometric Binder cumulant from polarization amplitudes. The critical potential where the localization transition happens is extracted from the sign change (zero crossing) of the geometric Binder cumulant. We map critical potential as a function of $J_N$ and the helical range $N$, finding that stronger helical hopping generally stabilizes the extended phase (shifting critical potential upward), while the $N$-dependence can display pronounced commensurability-induced spikes. We further compare the geometric Binder cumulant with the Fermi gap, which remains near zero at small values of potential and opens in the same parameter regime where the geometric Binder cumulant departs from extended phase. Finally, to take a controlled thermodynamic limit along Fibonacci system sizes, we employ a Zeckendorf-shift construction that fixes the many-body sector consistently as system size goes to infinity.

Summary. This paper introduces a new generative model for growing simplicial complexes using a preferential attachment mechanism that acts on different dimensions. It demonstrates how the model can produce power-law degree distributions and reveals how the interplay between different dimensional components can trigger abrupt transitions in the network's scaling properties.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for generative models of higher-order networks (simplicial complexes) that can reproduce the topological and spectral features found in empirical many-body interaction data.

Main result. The authors develop a dimension-wise preferential attachment model that produces power-law degree distributions and identify an abrupt transition in scaling exponents when moving between different dimensional attachment probabilities.

Method. The study uses algebraic topology (Hodge-Laplacian, boundary operators), continuous differential equation approximations for growth dynamics, and spectral analysis of the $d$-Laplacian.

Model / system. A growing simplicial complex where new nodes and higher-dimensional simplices (links, triangles, tetrahedra) are added at each time step based on dimension-specific attachment probabilities $p_d$.

Key observables. Generalized degree distribution, spectral dimension ($d_s$), Betti numbers ($eta_d$), Fiedler eigenvalue, and scaling exponents ($eta, \gamma$).

Important parameters / regimes. Maximum dimension $D$, attachment probabilities $p_1, \dots, p_D$, and a flavor parameter $s$.

Assumptions / limitations. The growth process assumes a specific probabilistic attachment rule where the probability of selecting a simplex is proportional to its generalized degree.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the basic building blocks (simplices) and their assembly; Figure 2 illustrates the growth process; Figure 4 shows the transition of the growth exponent $eta_0$ as a function of $p_1$; Figure 6 and 7 visualize the hierarchical construction and the relationship between node and link degrees.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the higher-order network framework, introduces the dimension-wise preferential attachment growth algorithm, provides analytical derivations for degree evolution and scaling exponents, explores specific dimensional regimes and phase transitions, and concludes with numerical validation.

Abstract

Network science is a powerful framework allowing to model complex systems, it is capable to describe and take into account the intricate web of connections existing among the constituting basic element of the system. Recently scholars have brought to the fore the relevance of higher-order networks, namely structures allowing to encode for many-body interaction, differently from the pairwise case handled by networks. This novel research field opens new avenues of research with applications ranging from neurosciences to social sciences; there is thus a need for generative models of higher-order network capable to reproduce features present in empirical data. In this work we present a model for growing simplicial complex rooted on a preferential attachment process acting dimension-wise, i.e., returning a power law distribution for the generalized degree of simplexes of different dimension.

Module Lattice Security (Part III): Structured CVP Distance on the Log-Unit Lattice

Authors: Ming-Xing Luo
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17404v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of the security of module lattices used in post-quantum cryptography. It demonstrates that the structured nature of these lattices allows for much tighter error bounds than previously assumed, significantly reducing the estimated approximation factor for certain attacks on ML-KEM.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the security of module lattices in post-quantum cryptography, specifically determining the CVP distance on the log-unit lattice and its impact on the ML-KEM (Kyber) scheme.

Main result. Proves that the L2 CVP distance converges to a specific constant times sqrt(n) and reduces the Short Generator Problem approximation factor from exponential to sub-polynomial.

Method. Uses probabilistic methods including the Central Limit Theorem, sub-Gaussian concentration inequalities, and analytic tools like the Trigamma function and QR decomposition.

Model / system. The mathematical framework involves cyclotomic fields Q(zeta_{2^k}), the log-unit lattice, and module lattices of rank d over the ring of integers.

Key observables. L2 and L-infinity CVP distances, approximation factor gamma, and the variance of the log-determinant sigma_{g0}.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension n = 2^{k-1}, module rank d, and the coefficient variance sigma_c^2.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes ring elements have i.i.d. bounded coefficients and utilizes the Gaussian limit for large dimensions.

Figures summary. Table 4 summarizes the cumulative progress of the CDPR attack analysis across three parts of the paper, comparing prior states to the current work.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from analyzing L2 and L-infinity distances on the log-unit lattice to evaluating Babai's algorithm performance, then extends the analysis to module determinant ideals via the Trigamma Theorem, and concludes with security implications for ML-KEM.

Abstract

We prove that the $L^2$ CVP distance from a random short ring element to the log-unit lattice of $\Q(ζ_{2^k})$ converges to $\fracπ{2\sqrt{6}}\sqrt{n}$ as $n=2^{k-1}\to\infty$. We then show that this target lies inside the Voronoi cell of the origin for $k\ge 4$. For the $L^\infty$ norm, the maximum over $n$ sub-Gaussian coordinates yields $O(\sqrt{\log n})$ which translates into a sub-polynomial approximation factor for the Short Generator Problem. We show a Coarse Lattice Theorem that Babai's algorithm returns zero for all structured targets, yet exactly recovers unit perturbations of arbitrary size. For module determinant ideals, we further prove the Trigamma Theorem that proves an intrinsic imbalance $σ_{g_0}=O(1)$ independent of the modulus $q$. Finally, combined with Parts I and II, we reduce the CDPR factor for ML-KEM from $\exp(\tO(\sqrt{n}))$ to a sub-polynomial value.

Module Lattice Security (Part IV): Probabilistic Polynomial Quantum Attack on Module-LWE over 2-Power Cyclotomics

Authors: Ming-Xing Luo
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17412v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a quantum algorithm that breaks several post-quantum cryptographic standards, including ML-KEM and Falcon, by solving the Principal Ideal Problem in polynomial time. It achieves this by exploiting the algebraic structure of 2-power cyclotomic rings through a tower decomposition. The results suggest that the security of these lattice-based primitives is significantly lower than previously assumed under quantum attack.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a probabilistic polynomial-time quantum attack on Module-LWE (MLWE) schemes, specifically targeting ML-KEM, Falcon, Hawk, and NTRU over 2-power cyclotomic rings.

Main result. The paper presents a quantum algorithm that reduces the Principal Ideal Problem (PIP) complexity to polynomial time, effectively breaking the standardized parameter sets of ML-KEM, Falcon, Hawk, and NTRU.

Method. The attack uses an extended CDPR pipeline involving module-to-ideal reduction, a quantum tower decomposition of the Principal Ideal Problem via the Abelian Hidden Subgroup Problem, and classical log-unit CVP using Babai's algorithm.

Model / system. The mathematical framework involves module lattices over 2-power cyclotomic rings R = Z[zeta], utilizing the algebraic structure of cyclotomic towers and the log-unit lattice.

Key observables. The approximation factor (gamma), quantum gate complexity, qubit requirements, and the CVP residual.

Important parameters / regimes. ML-KEM-1024 parameters (n=256, q=3329), ring degree (n), module rank (d), and the approximation factor threshold (q/2).

Assumptions / limitations. The attack relies on the validity of the Hidden Subgroup Problem (HSP) solution, the use of the tower decomposition, and certain empirical/numerical results from previous parts of the series.

Figures summary. The notes mention a table summarizing quantum resource costs for ML-KEM-1024 and a table comparing theoretical vs. simulated approximation factors for Falcon.

Paper structure. The paper follows a logical progression from module-to-ideal reduction to a recursive quantum tower PIP algorithm, followed by complexity analysis, security evaluation of specific NIST standards, and a summary of cumulative improvements over previous works.

Abstract

We present a quantum attack on ML-KEM and related 2-power cyclotomic lattice schemes. Combining with Parts I-III, we provide an algorithm and verify the resulting approximation factor satisfies $γ\le 21 < q/2=1665$ for ML-KEM-1024, with a success probability $\ge 0.99$. We apply a tower decomposition of the Principal Ideal Problem (PIP) through the chain $\Q \subset \Q(ζ_8) \subset \cdots \subset \Q(ζ_{2^k})$ which yields a polynomial-time quantum algorithm costing $O(n^3 \log^2 n)$ gates, $O(n^2 \log n)$ qubits, and $\mathrm{poly}(n)$ classical bit operations. We extend the analysis to Falcon, Hawk, and NTRU over 2-power cyclotomic rings. This means that ML-KEM, Falcon, Hawk, NTRU-HPS, and NTRU-HRSS with all standardized parameter sets are broken under quantum attack.

Near-Optimal Quantum Time Evolution Circuits via Provably Convergent Compression

Authors: Erenay Karacan, Isabel Nha Minh Le, Matteo D'Anna, Juan Carasquilla, Christian B. Mendl, Ivan Rojkov
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17067v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a method for compressing quantum time-evolution circuits with guaranteed convergence to near-optimal gate complexity. By using a specific initialization strategy, the authors show that small-scale optimized circuits can be effectively scaled to simulate large, complex systems like the Kagome Heisenberg antiferromagnet. This approach significantly reduces the gate overhead required for digital quantum simulation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous mathematical bridge between variational quantum algorithms and efficient digital simulation, offering a provable way to scale quantum simulators toward the classical complexity limit.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of implementing efficient, large-scale quantum time evolution circuits that achieve near-optimal gate complexity while providing rigorous global convergence guarantees for variational compression.

Main result. The authors provide a recipe for choosing an initial optimization point that guarantees convergence to a near-optimal circuit with gate complexity O(N t polylog(N t/epsilon)) for local, translationally invariant Hamiltonians.

Method. A variational compression strategy (TICC) using a Trotterization-based 'warm start' and a bootstrapping approach to extend small-time-step optimized gates to larger evolution times.

Model / system. Local and translationally invariant Hamiltonians, specifically demonstrated on a Heisenberg antiferromagnet on a Kagome lattice, with proposed implementation on trapped-ion platforms using B-gates.

Key observables. Evolution infidelity, gate complexity (gate count), and local infidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (N), evolution time (t), error tolerance (epsilon), and the critical time threshold (t_crit) for convergence.

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonian is local and translationally invariant; the optimization manifold is real-analytic; the time step must be sufficiently small (below t_crit).

Figures summary. Visualizations of the optimization protocol, comparisons of error scaling between Trotterization and optimized circuits, transferability of gates to larger systems, and gate decomposition/performance in noisy ion-trap models.

Paper structure. Introduction of the scaling challenge, theoretical proof of convergence and convexity, demonstration of the TICC protocol on various lattices, verification of transferability to large systems, and hardware-specific gate implementation analysis.

Abstract

Variational compression can significantly lower implementation overheads for encoding the time evolution of Hamiltonians into quantum circuits. However, they usually lack global convergence guarantees and well-established scaling behavior. In this work, we provide a recipe for choosing the initial point of such variational optimizations that guarantees convergence to a quantum circuit with near-optimal gate complexity $\mathcal{O}\left( N \, t \, \text{polylog}(N \, t/ε) \right)$ for all local and translationally invariant Hamiltonians. We demonstrate our method by encoding the globally controlled time evolution of a Heisenberg antiferromagnet on a Kagome lattice. For $N = 48$ sites, evolution time $t=0.1$ and infidelity $ε\approx1\%$, the controlled time-evolution circuit requires 960 two-qubit B gates, for which we propose a straightforward implementation scheme for ion-trap setups. Thereby, our recipe extends digital quantum simulators toward system sizes and geometries that are challenging for classical computation.

Summary. This paper explores how the chiral magnetic effect behaves in a highly nonlinear regime using holographic duality. It reveals that the relationship between current and magnetic field can be multi-valued and shows how external fields can stabilize certain phases of the system.

Abstract

We investigate the nonlinear response of the chiral magnetic current to an external magnetic field in a holographic setup. Using the D3/D7 brane system, where the chiral magnetic effect (CME) can be realized by considering rotating probe D7-branes, corresponding to introducing an axial chemical potential, we analyze the current-magnetic field relation beyond the linear regime. Focusing on the vicinity of the phase boundary between the insulating phase and the CME phase, we find that the chiral magnetic current exhibits a multi-valued dependence on the magnetic field, indicating a highly nonlinear response characteristic of this model. We further study the dynamical stability of the insulating phase near the transition point, and show that the presence of both an axial chemical potential and an external magnetic field cooperatively stabilize the system. Our results clarify the interplay between the axial chemical potential and the magnetic field in determining the phase structure and stability of the system, and reveal new nonlinear aspects of chiral transport in holographic gauge theories.

Ordering, correlation functions and phase transitions in molecular systems

Authors: Yashwant Singh
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18326v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a comprehensive review of advanced Density Functional Theory methods for describing phase transitions in molecular systems. It focuses on overcoming the limitations of previous approximations by accurately capturing how correlation functions change when a system breaks symmetry. The work demonstrates high accuracy in predicting the stability of complex phases like liquid crystals and crystalline solids.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of accurately applying classical Density Functional Theory (DFT) to broken symmetry phases in molecular systems, specifically regarding the determination of pair correlation functions (PCFs) and the relative stability of phases.

Main result. The development of an 'exact DFT' and an Effective DFT (EDFT) approach that accounts for new contributions to correlation functions during symmetry breaking, providing accurate predictions for phase boundaries (e.g., fluid-solid, bcc-fcc) and thermodynamic stability.

Method. The use of functional calculus, Legendre transformations, and integral equation theories (such as RY and ZH closures) to relate the grand potential and intrinsic free energy to density distributions and direct correlation functions.

Model / system. Classical many-body systems of rigid or asymmetric molecules (including isotropic fluids, liquid crystals, and crystalline solids) interacting via pairwise additive potentials, such as Lennard-Jones or power-law potentials.

Key observables. One-particle density, pair correlation functions (PCFs), direct correlation functions (DCFs), order parameters (positional and orientational), and the structure factor S(q).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature, pressure, chemical potential, molecular anisotropy, softness parameter (1/n), and the degree of particle localization (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated in the classical limit with pairwise additive intermolecular potentials; specific models are limited to rigid linear molecules or specific symmetries.

Figures summary. The figures show the variation of free energy difference as a function of density and localization, phase diagrams in the softness vs. density plane, and density-temperature phase diagrams.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the basics of statistical mechanics, moves to the derivation of the grand potential and intrinsic free energy in terms of correlation functions, reviews recent developments in calculating PCFs for broken symmetry phases, and concludes with applications to various phase transitions and comparisons with simulation data.

Abstract

Although the classical density functional theory (DFT) of inhomogeneous fluids was formulated more than four decades ago, its application to broken symmetry phases of molecular systems remained a challenge. Approximate free energy functionals proposed in the past failed to give accurate description of relative stability of phases, phase transitions, and of properties arising due to broken symmetry. In a DFT pair correlation functions (PCFs) play a fundamental role. While in the case of homogeneous fluids, PCFs are routinely determined using experimental, theoretical or simulation methods, determination of PCFs of broken symmetry phases remained a problem. Breaking of symmetry at the transition point gives rise a new contribution to correlation functions which may differ significantly from that of the coexisting higher symmetry phase. We review methods which have been developed in the last few years to calculate PCFs of broken symmetry phases and their inclusion in the expressions of the grand potential and the intrinsic free-energy. This leads to formulation of an exact DFT. We describe application of the theory to freezing of variety of fluids into ordered phases and transition from an ordered phase of higher symmetry to a phase of lower symmetry. Comparison of results found from different versions of DFT and simulations reveal their accuracy. A brief description of basics of statistical mechanics is included to make the article self-contained.

Parameterized 4-Qubit EWL Quantum Game Circuits with Dirac-Solow-Swan Hamiltonian Integration for Quadruple Helix Disruptive Innovation Recommender Systems

Summary. This paper proposes a quantum computing framework for predicting innovation trends by mapping real-world economic funding data onto a 4-qubit quantum game circuit. Abstract

We present a novel parameterized 4-qubit Eisert-Wilkens-Lewenstein (EWL) quantum game circuit for recommender systems in quadruple helix innovation ecosystems (academia, industry, government, and civil society). The local strategy operators $U_{i} = R_y(θ_{i})$ for each helix actor are directly tuned by normalized dominance weights extracted from real participant funding data (\texit{ecContribution}) in the European Commission CORDIS Horizon Europe database (project COVend, ID 101045956). The circuit employs a multi-qubit EWL entangler followed by parameterized local rotations, inverse entangler, and full measurement, achieving only 22 gates and circuit depth 11 while scaling as $O(n)$ for $n$-round helix communications. Measurement probabilities after the quantum game serve as recommender scores for disruptive versus sustaining innovation trends. We formulate and assess a real-space-decomposed density-functional plus reduced-density-matrix-functional (DF+RDMF) scheme in which the Coulomb interaction is partitioned locally in real space and the RDMF correction is evaluated only for the strongly correlated part of the interaction. The resulting local density-matrix functionals are further compressed using the adaptive cluster approximation (ACA), which performs a unitary rotation of the bath subspace before truncation and therefore preserves the local interaction while reducing the number of explicitly correlated bath states. As a molecular test case, we consider the bending potential of carbon suboxide, C$_3$O$_2$. While semilocal PBE favors a linear molecule, the DF+RDMF/ACA correction stabilizes a bent configuration in qualitative agreement with the quasilinear behavior inferred from spectroscopy. The approach provides a systematic embedding hierarchy for combining density functionals with explicitly correlated density-matrix corrections in extended or spatially inhomogeneous systems.
