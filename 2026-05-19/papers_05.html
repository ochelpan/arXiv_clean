Limit theorems for random walks with spatio-temporal drift

Authors: Ngo P. N. Ngoc, Tuan-Minh Nguyen
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17725v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a complete classification of the long-term behavior of random walks with complex, time-dependent, and position-dependent drifts. It identifies how varying the strength of the drift relative to noise leads to transitions between diffusive, SDE-driven, and localized states.

Why it may be interesting. The study of phase transitions and localization in stochastic processes with memory and spatio-temporal dependence is highly relevant to understanding non-equilibrium dynamics and many-body localization in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the asymptotic behavior and limit theorems for a class of discrete-time random walks in R^d characterized by spatio-temporal drift.

Main result. The authors establish a phase transition in the convergence of the normalized process, identifying three distinct regimes in the nonlinear case: Gaussian convergence, convergence to an SDE-based stationary distribution, and almost sure localization on a hypersphere.

Method. The study employs martingale theory, multivariate martingale central limit theorems, Lyapunov functions, and diffusion approximations to analyze the process.

Model / system. A discrete-time random walk in R^d where the conditional drift decays polynomially in time and grows polynomially with the distance from the origin, related to models like the Elephant Random Walk and self-interacting processes.

Key observables. The asymptotic distribution of the scaled position (normalized process), the scaling exponent gamma, and the asymptotic covariance matrix.

Important parameters / regimes. Spatial scaling exponent (alpha), temporal scaling exponent (beta), and the eigenvalues of the drift matrix (A).

Assumptions / limitations. Increments must have moments of order p > 2; the drift field may be non-Lipschitz at the origin; the covariance matrix Sigma is assumed to be positive definite.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the RWSD model and its regimes, followed by proofs for the nonlinear case (Theorem 1.1) and the linear case (Theorem 1.2/1.3), and concludes with an appendix on non-convergence and martingale CLT tools.

Abstract

We study a class of discrete-time random walks in $\mathbb{R}^d$ whose conditional drift decays polynomially in time and grows polynomially with the distance from the origin to the current position. This class is related to several models of self-interacting random processes. We determine the asymptotic behavior of the walk under the assumption that its increments have moments of order $p$ for some $p>2$. In the linear case, where the drift depends linearly on the current position, we establish a phase transition in the convergence in distribution of the normalized process to Gaussian limits. In the nonlinear case, we identify three distinct regimes separated by a critical line and show that the normalized process exhibits qualitatively different behaviors in each regime, including convergence in distribution to a Gaussian law, convergence to a non-Gaussian limit given by the stationary distribution of a stochastic differential equation, and almost sure localization on a hypersphere.

Quantum Error Correction Assisted Axion Search in CMOS Spin Qubit Arrays

Authors: Xiangjun Tan, Zhanning Wang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17457v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. The paper proposes using quantum error correction to enhance the detection of axion dark matter using silicon spin qubit arrays. By using a repetition code to suppress local dephasing, the authors show that entanglement-enhanced sensing can be restored, significantly improving the sensitivity to axion-electron coupling.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum metrology, as it demonstrates how active error correction can be used to preserve quantum coherence for sensing tasks in the presence of non-Markovian and correlated noise.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Searching for axion dark matter using semiconductor spin qubits is limited by strong longitudinal dephasing, which suppresses the sensitivity gains of entanglement.

Main result. Quantum error correction (QEC) using a repetition code can mitigate local dephasing, restoring entanglement-enhanced sensing and providing up to an order-of-magnitude improvement in sensitivity to axion-electron coupling.

Method. The authors derive closed-form expressions for Quantum Fisher Information by integrating an optimal repetition code with logical GHZ block entanglement and optimizing the logical block size.

Model / system. The system consists of CMOS-compatible silicon spin qubit arrays (quantum dots) interacting with a classical, spatially coherent axion field that acts as an effective oscillating pseudo-magnetic field.

Key observables. Axion-electron coupling (gae), Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), and spin polarization sidebands.

Important parameters / regimes. Axion mass (10^-6 to 10^-3 eV), dephasing rates (local and correlated), QEC cycle frequency, and the Standard Quantum Limit (SQL).

Assumptions / limitations. The axion field is treated as a classical, spatially coherent field with a wavelength much larger than the qubit array; the repetition code specifically targets local phase-flip errors.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of a planar silicon quantum dot array with gates and repetition code blocks; Figure 2 shows the sensitivity improvement gain as a function of logical size; Figure 3 illustrates QEC benefit regimes; Figure 5 shows the scaling of sensitivity with the total number of physical qubits.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the dark matter search problem, defines the spin-qubit-axion interaction Hamiltonian, implements a QEC-protected GHZ protocol, analyzes the resulting Fisher information and sensitivity gains, and concludes with a scaling analysis and projection onto realistic CMOS hardware parameters.

Abstract

Searches for axion and axionlike dark matter based on solid-state spin qubits are fundamentally limited by strong longitudinal dephasing, which rapidly suppresses the sensitivity gains offered by entanglement. Here we show that quantum error correction (QEC) can substantially enhance axion search sensitivity in realistic semiconductor spin qubit platforms by mitigating this dominant noise source. By integrating an optimally chosen repetition code QEC with logical GHZ block entanglement, we derive closed-form expressions for the quantum Fisher information that explicitly incorporate the finite coherence time of the axion field. Our analysis demonstrates that modest QEC cycle frequencies are sufficient to significantly reduce the effective dephasing rate, thereby restoring a broad parameter regime in which entanglement-enhanced sensing surpasses the standard quantum limit. Projecting these results onto CMOS-compatible device parameters, we find that QEC-protected entangled sensing can revive otherwise inaccessible quantum advantages, yielding up to order-of-magnitude improvements in sensitivity to the axion-electron coupling $g_{ae}$. These results establish a practical and theoretically controlled pathway for using QEC to improve qubit array searches for physics beyond the Standard Model.

Quantum-Battery-Powered Geometric Landau-Zener Interferometry

Authors: Borhan Ahmadi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18108v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Tavis-Cummings & cavity-many-emitter 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper examines the performance of a geometric Landau-Zener interferometer powered by a finite quantum battery instead of a classical drive. It demonstrates that maintaining a coherent phase reference is more critical for interference contrast than reducing photon-number fluctuations. Consequently, standard coherent states outperform squeezed states for high-fidelity geometric control.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics as it explores the fundamental limits of using non-classical energy reservoirs for coherent control and quantifies the trade-off between energy fluctuations and phase coherence.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether a finite quantum battery can replace classical phase-coherent microwave drives for superconducting qubit control and determining if energy alone or phase-coherent energy is the critical resource for geometric control.

Main result. The study finds that reducing photon-number fluctuations via squeezing is insufficient for improving interference contrast because the loss of the coherent phase-reference strength outweighs the benefits of reduced gap broadening; coherent states provide the optimal balance.

Method. Full density-matrix evolution using a Lindblad master equation and an adaptive eighth-order Runge-Kutta solver to account for qubit-battery dynamics and back-action.

Model / system. A two-level system (qubit) coupled to a single quantized bosonic mode (the quantum battery) via a Jaynes-Cummings Hamiltonian, undergoing a Landau-Zener-Stueckelberg interferometry protocol with an echo pulse.

Key observables. Final excited-state population (interferogram contrast), fringe contrast (C), photon-number change (battery back-action), and the coherent phase-reference fraction (eta_coh).

Important parameters / regimes. Mean photon number (n_bar), photon-number variance, coupling strength (g), and the coherent fraction (eta_coh); regimes include the macroscopic coherent-state limit and the few-quanta quantum regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The echo pulse is treated as a qubit-only operation that does not act on the battery; the rotating-wave approximation is applied.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of photon-number-resolved Landau-Zener passages and dressed adiabatic branches; Figure 3 compares performance across different battery states.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of replacing classical drives with quantum batteries, defines the Jaynes-Cummings model and the GLZI protocol, analyzes the impact of photon-number fluctuations and gap broadening, compares various battery states (coherent vs. squeezed), and concludes with the necessity of a phase reference.

Abstract

Classical microwave drives are usually treated as ideal phase-coherent work sources for superconducting-qubit control. What if such a drive is replaced by a finite quantum battery. As a demanding benchmark, we consider echo-refocused geometric Landau--Zener interferometry powered by a single quantized bosonic mode. The qubit--battery dynamics are described by a Jaynes--Cummings Hamiltonian, while the echo pulse is retained as a qubit-only refocusing operation that cancels the dynamical phase. In the macroscopic coherent-state limit, the usual classical geometric interferometer is recovered. At finite mean photon number, however, the Jaynes--Cummings coupling generates photon-number-resolved avoided crossings with gaps $Ω_n=2g\sqrt{n}$. The qubit-only echo redistributes amplitudes between neighboring excitation sectors, so the finite-battery protocol is not a single classical interferometer but a coherent sector-resolved quantum evolution. This produces contrast loss, interferogram distortions, and measurable battery back-action. We further show that reducing photon-number fluctuations alone is not sufficient: geometric control requires a first-order phase reference. Geometric Landau--Zener interferometry therefore provides a practical benchmark for certifying phase-coherent quantum-battery energy.

Summary. This paper investigates whether machine learning for cold-atom experiments can be both accurate and explainable. By comparing CNNs to Explainable Boosting Machines, the authors show that physics-informed, interpretable models can effectively classify quantum gas excitations like solitons and vortices.

Why it may be interesting. It addresses a critical bottleneck in many-body dynamics experiments: how to use powerful ML tools to extract physical signals from noisy, high-volume data without losing the ability to verify the underlying physics.

Model / system. The physical platform consists of neutral-atom quantum simulators, specifically Bose-Einstein Condensates (BECs), using bright-field imaging (absorption or phase-contrast) to detect solitons and vortices.

Important parameters / regimes. Probe wavelength, resonant absorption cross-section, and photon shot noise levels.

Assumptions / limitations. The signal of interest is assumed to be concentrated within a specific interior region of the imaging window.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the imaging challenges, compares linear algebra vs. ML for reference reconstruction, evaluates CNNs vs. EBMs for excitation classification, and concludes on the feasibility of explainable scientific ML.

Abstract

Virtually all aspects of many-body atomic physics are challenging: experiments are technically demanding, datasets have become enormous, and the memory and CPU requirements for classical simulation of generic quantum systems often scale exponentially with system size. Machine learning (ML) methods are already assisting in each of these areas and are poised to become transformative. Here, we focus on two specific applications of ML to cold-atom-based quantum simulators. These devices generally generate data in the form of images; we first showcase denoising of raw images and then identify solitonic waves in Bose-Einstein condensates. In both of these examples, we comment on the interplay between performance, model complexity, and interpretability.

Summary. This paper develops a Central Limit Theorem for bosonic quantum channels, showing that repeated symmetric convolutions of a channel converge to a Gaussian channel. This result unifies classical and quantum limit theorems and allows for the derivation of new uncertainty relations and tight lower bounds on the quantum capacity of linear bosonic channels.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems as it establishes Gaussian channels as the fundamental limit of repeated channel applications and provides new tools for bounding the capacity of continuous-variable quantum communication.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to extend the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) from classical probability and quantum states to the setting of bosonic quantum channels, establishing Gaussian channels as extremal objects.

Main result. The authors prove that the n-fold symmetric convolution of a bosonic channel converges to a Gaussian bosonic channel, providing a unified framework that recovers both the classical CLT and the CLT for bosonic states.

Method. The study uses the Heisenberg picture, Weyl representation, and n-beam splitter unitaries to define a symmetric convolution of channels, analyzing convergence via the pointwise convergence of characteristic functions.

Model / system. The system consists of single-mode and multi-mode bosonic quantum systems (continuous-variable systems) described by quadrature operators and canonical commutation relations, specifically focusing on linear bosonic channels.

Key observables. First-order moments (displacement), second-order moments (covariance matrix), characteristic functions, and energy-constrained quantum capacity.

Important parameters / regimes. The n-fold convolution limit (n to infinity), energy-constrained regime (E >= 0), and the scaling function of the channel.

Assumptions / limitations. The results assume finite second-order moments for the channel, finite first-order moments, and specifically rely on the linearity of the channel for the capacity lower bound.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic diagram illustrating the construction of the symmetric convolution of a channel using an n-beam splitter unitary.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of extending the CLT to channels, defines the symmetric convolution mechanism, proves the convergence to Gaussian channels (the qCLT for channels), demonstrates the recovery of classical and state-based CLTs, and applies the result to derive uncertainty relations and capacity lower bounds.

Abstract

In this paper, we develop an extension of the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) to the setting of bosonic quantum channels. This extension provides a deeper understanding of Gaussian bosonic channels as extremal objects. Using our CLT for bosonic quantum channels, we recover both the classical CLT and the CLT for bosonic quantum states, thereby offering a unified perspective that connects classical probability theory with continuous-variable quantum systems. Moreover, using our result, we can provide necessary uncertainty relations that every physical (possibly non-Gaussian) bosonic quantum channel must satisfy. As another application of our limit theorems, we derive tight lower bounds on the energy-constrained quantum capacity of linear bosonic channels by relating it to the capacity of their associated Gaussian bosonic channels, further reinforcing the role of Gaussian channels as extremal.

Summary. This paper develops a theoretical framework using the Quantum Expectation Identity to describe the electronic properties of a molecular domain. It establishes a direct link between the change in quantum purity and the statistical covariance of the particle number. The results provide analytical tools to study how open quantum systems respond to changes in their chemical environment.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous statistical mechanical framework for understanding how fluctuations and correlations in open quantum systems (like molecular domains) relate to fundamental properties like purity and chemical potential.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Establishing analytical relationships between quantum observables, such as electronic population and chemical potential, and their derivatives within a molecular domain treated as an open quantum system.

Main result. The derivation of the Quantum Expectation Identity (QEI) applied to a three-state model, specifically finding that the derivative of quantum purity with respect to chemical potential is proportional to the negative covariance between the density matrix and the number operator.

Method. The study utilizes the Quantum Expectation Identity (QII) theorem and a Jaynesian inferential approach (maximizing von Neumann entropy) under constraints of particle number fluctuation.

Model / system. A three-state model of a molecular domain (or basin) treated as an open quantum system. The density matrix is expanded using a neutral state, a cationic state, and an anionic state, using a canonical-like ensemble where the number operator replaces the Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Electronic population, chemical potential, maximum charge capacity (q), quantum purity, and particle number variance.

Important parameters / regimes. Chemical potential (gamma), fractional charge (nu), and charge capacity (q); regimes include the pure state limit (gamma to infinity) and maximum disorder (gamma = 0).

Assumptions / limitations. The temperature effects are excluded; the test operator must be compatible with the density matrix; the density matrix must be non-singular.

Figures summary. Figure 1a shows the transferred charge fraction as a function of chemical potential for various q values; Figure 2a shows quantum purity transitioning from 1/3 to 1; Figure 2b shows the covariance between the density matrix and particle number.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the three-state model and the QEI theorem, progresses to deriving analytical expressions for particle number and variance, explores the dynamics of quantum purity and its correlation with the number operator, and concludes with an analysis of specific physical regimes.

Abstract

The electronic distribution of a molecular domain is examined in this study. A theoretical formulation of quantum molecular properties is presented using the Quantum Expectation Identity theorem (QEI), with a focus on the three-state model of the density matrix for the quantum state of a molecular domain as an open system. The report examines the relationship between ab initio statistical fluctuation-correlation theorems for quantum observables and their derivatives. We focus on three main quantities of a domain: the electronic population, its chemical potential, and its maximum capacity for accepting or donating charge with the neighbors. The analytical expressions for the quantities are presented and discussed in detail. At the end, we explore the concept of quantum purity and its proper application in the molecular domain.

Summary. This paper investigates how to optimize quantum conference key agreement in asymmetric star networks by using cutoff times to discard decohered qubits. Main problem. Optimizing strategies for Quantum Conference Key Agreement (CKA) in asymmetric star networks to mitigate the impact of memory dephasing and decoherence.

Paper structure. Abstract

The distribution of entangled states is a core task for quantum networks facilitating quantum communication, and the use of multipartite entangled states comes with its own set of considerations. In this work, we analyze a quantum conference agreement protocol based on GHZ states in a network with a central station to which multiple clients are connected. Using comprehensive numerical simulations, we investigate how minor variations in the scenario-such as the number of parties, the number of memories, and asymmetric distances from the central station-can drastically influence the performance of the protocol. In particular, we demonstrate that it is crucial to adjust the strategy by optimizing cutoff times. From a broader perspective, we argue that numerical simulations are an indispensable tool for protocol design for devising realistic schemes for quantum communication.

Finite-frequency anomaly-induced electromechanical response of Dirac fermions in deformed graphene

Authors: Ara Sedrakyan
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17632v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that mechanical deformations in graphene can generate transverse electrical currents through a parity-odd anomaly. It provides specific experimental signatures, such as frequency-doubled signals and gate-dependent decay, which allow for the direct detection of emergent gauge fields in deformed 2D materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the electromechanical response of Dirac fermions in deformed graphene induced by a finite-frequency anomaly and identifying experimental signatures to distinguish this from ordinary deformation-potential backgrounds.

Main result. The paper derives a mixed Chern-Simons relation where mechanical deformations generate a transverse electric current, predicting specific frequency harmonics (2w) and gate-voltage dependencies (1/|mu| decay) as clean experimental fingerprints.

Method. The author uses a continuum Dirac theory framework, evaluating the one-loop parity-odd current-current correlator and performing spin and valley summation to determine the observable charge response.

Model / system. The system consists of deformed, sublattice-asymmetric graphene (e.g., on hBN) modeled by low-energy Dirac fermions on a curved manifold with an emergent phonon gauge field.

Key observables. Transverse electric current, second-harmonic current (2w), charge modulation, and the effective phonon gauge field flux.

Important parameters / regimes. Dirac mass (m), chemical potential (mu), deformation gauge coupling (lambda_A), and frequency (omega).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the weak-deformation limit, the continuum limit of the tight-binding model, and the one-loop approximation for the fermion-phonon interaction.

Figures summary. Fig 1 illustrates local geometry and geometric gauge fields; Fig 2 shows the Feynman diagram for the anomaly-induced signal and compares it to other vertices.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the geometric origin of the phonon gauge field, derives the mixed Chern-Simons response via one-loop calculations, analyzes the response in different doping regimes, and concludes with specific experimental selection rules and symmetry-based detection protocols.

Abstract

A deformation of a graphene sheet changes more than the positions of the atoms. In the low-energy Dirac theory it also produces geometric electron-phonon vertices. One of these vertices acts as an emergent phonon gauge field, $\calA_μ$, which couples to the same Dirac current as the electromagnetic vector potential. This shared current vertex gives a direct route from mechanics to electronics: a moving deformation can generate a transverse electric current, and a deformation pattern with emergent phonon flux can bind electric charge. We show that the coefficient of this mixed electromechanical response is the parity-odd current-current correlator of a massive Dirac cone. For an insulating cone the coefficient is the one-cone Chern-Simons value, while for a doped cone in the local regime it is reduced by the Berry curvature factor $m/|μ|$. We apply the response to explicit deformations. A traveling flexural wave generates a transverse second-harmonic current; a static ripple mixed with a dynamic phonon generates a transverse current at the drive frequency; and two non-collinear modes can generate charge modulation through the emergent phonon flux. We keep the spin and valley sum explicit, so the paper shows when the one-cone anomaly becomes a charge current in graphene and when it instead appears in a valley, spin, or spin-valley channel. For sublattice-gapped graphene with a valley-odd deformation gauge coupling, the two valleys add rather than cancel. The experimentally sharp signature is a transverse electrical signal at twice the flexural-wave frequency, with a phase fixed by the sign of the sublattice gap and a gate dependence that crosses over from a gap plateau to a $1/|μ|$ decay. These direction, phase, frequency, and gate-voltage selection rules give clean tests of the anomaly-induced electromechanical channel in deformed graphene.

Summary. This paper explores how the curvature and topology of a spherical surface influence the formation and stability of solitary waves in a Bose-Einstein condensate. It demonstrates that these waves evolve from vortex dipoles to vortex-free solitons as rotation speed increases, with a maximum speed dictated by the Landau critical velocity. The findings provide a bridge between classical Jones-Roberts solitons in flat planes and excitations in curved, topologically non-trivial geometries.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and AMO physics as it explores how topological constraints and curved geometries modify fundamental nonlinear phenomena like solitons and the Landau criterion for superfluidity.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the nonlinear excitations (solitary waves) that drive the onset of rotation in a Bose-Einstein condensate confined to a thin spherical shell, specifically how curvature and topology affect these waves.

Main result. The authors identify a continuous family of excitations ranging from vortex dipoles to vortex-free Jones-Roberts solitons, and determine that the Landau critical velocity sets the upper limit for the angular speed of these waves.

Method. The study uses the mean-field Gross-Pitaevskii equation solved via an iterative Newton-Raphson scheme with pseudo-spectral accuracy and branch-following continuation.

Model / system. A dilute, quasi-two-dimensional Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) confined to a thin spherical shell of radius R, modeled by the Gross-Pitaevskii equation on a spherical surface.

Key observables. Density profile, phase angle, angular momentum (Lz), energy (E), and Bogoliubov excitation spectrum.

Important parameters / regimes. Sphere radius (R) in units of healing length, angular speed (Omega), chemical potential (mu), and the large-radius (Euclidean) limit.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be a dilute gas in the mean-field regime, confined to a thin shell with strong radial confinement, and the ground state density is assumed to be uniform.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the BEC on a spherical shell and visual representations of the density and phase angle for a specific vortex dipole configuration.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical system and GP model, describes the numerical Newton-Raphson and continuation methods, analyzes the different regimes of solitary waves (vortex dipoles to solitons), derives the energy-momentum relation, and establishes the stability condition via the Landau critical velocity.

Abstract

The nonlinear excitations underlying the onset of rotation in a dilute Bose-Einstein condensate confined to a thin spherical shell are studied. These excitations correspond to solitary waves rotating about the sphere at constant angular speed: at low speeds they appear as dipoles of singly quantized vortices with opposite circulation, while at higher speeds they evolve into vortex-free Jones-Roberts solitons. With further increase of the angular speed, these excitations hybridize with equatorially confined modes whose azimuthal wave number is set by the sphere radius measured in units of the healing length. The propagation speed of these modes is shown to play the role of a Landau critical velocity, thereby setting the upper limiting angular speed of the entire Jones-Roberts family.

Localization of a quantum particle in a classical one-component plasma. Fluctuation-induced random potential and the Coulomb logarithm

Summary. This paper provides a first-principles theoretical framework for understanding how quantum particles undergo localization due to the long-range Coulombic fluctuations in a classical plasma. It successfully links quantum interference effects to classical plasma kinetic theory via the Coulomb logarithm. The results are particularly relevant for understanding transport in dense astrophysical environments like the solar corona.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the localization of a quantum particle moving through a random potential generated by thermal density fluctuations in a classical one-component plasma.

Main result. The author derives closed-form expressions for the localization length in both weak and strong disorder regimes, showing that the length scale explicitly depends on the classical Coulomb logarithm.

Method. The study employs Efimov's path-integral formalism, the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) for density fluctuations, and the Eikonal (straight-line) approximation to evaluate the averaged Green's function.

Model / system. A quantum test particle with charge q0 moving in a fully ionized classical one-component plasma (OCP) consisting of mobile ions and a static neutralizing background.

Key observables. Localization length l(k) and the potential correlation function K(r).

Important parameters / regimes. Debye screening length (kappa inverse), Coulomb logarithm (ln(kappaL)), momentum (k), and temperature (T).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory uses a static-fluctuation approximation (ignoring dynamic screening) and treats the random potential as a Gaussian random field.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of disorder-induced localization in plasmas, develops the microscopic theory using path integrals and RPA, derives scaling laws for high-energy and low-energy regimes, and discusses the implications for astrophysical plasmas and the limitations of the static approximation.

Abstract

We develop a microscopic theory of disorder-induced localization for a quantum particle moving in a fully ionized classical one-component plasma, within the static-fluctuation approximation. The random potential acting on the particle originates from equilibrium thermal fluctuations of the ionic charge density, described within the random phase approximation (RPA). Method. The study utilizes 3- and 4-input relative-phase Toffoli gates combined with dynamic circuit techniques, such as intermediate measurements, qubit resets, and measurement-conditioned feed-forward operations to replace costly uncomputation.

Model / system. The work focuses on fault-tolerant quantum computing architectures using the Clifford+T universal gate set, specifically targeting the implementation of multi-controlled X gates ($C_n X$).

Key observables. T-count, T-depth, and CX-count (CNOT gate count).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of control qubits ($n$), availability of a single clean ancilla, and the use of conditionally clean ancillas.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the availability of at least one clean ancilla and the ability to perform intermediate measurements and classical feed-forward (dynamic circuits).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of multi-controlled Toffoli decomposition, presents new decomposition techniques using 3- and 4-input relative-phase gates, details the implementation of dynamic circuit techniques for uncomputation, provides a comparative analysis of resource bounds, and validates results through experimental/numerical evaluation.

Abstract

Multi-controlled Toffoli gates are fundamental building blocks in quantum computation, with applications in quantum arithmetic, simulation, and search algorithms. In fault-tolerant architectures, their realization is constrained by the high cost of non-Clifford resources, particularly in terms of T-count and T-depth. Recent advances have demonstrated that the use of ancillary qubits, relative-phase Toffoli gates, and dynamic circuit techniques can substantially reduce this overhead. In this work, we investigate the decomposition of large Toffoli gates using 3- and 4-input relative-phase Toffoli gates in the presence of a single clean ancilla and conditionally clean ancillas. We derive explicit resource bounds for Clifford+T implementations incorporating dynamic-circuit-based uncomputation and measurement-conditioned corrections. Our analysis emphasizes T-depth reduction under fixed CX and T-count overhead, ensuring relevance for near-term devices. We show that introducing 4-input relative-phase Toffoli gates enables significant T-depth reductions through enhanced parallelism while maintaining favorable ancilla requirements. We further validate our theoretical results through experimental evaluation and comparative analysis with existing approaches.
