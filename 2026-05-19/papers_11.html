Field Theory Models for a Holographic Superconductor in Two Dimensions

Authors: Salvatore Santoro, Roberto Auzzi, Stefano Bolognesi
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18628v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a field-theoretic description of a 2D holographic superconductor, showing that the complex phase diagram of the bulk gravity model can be recovered from a deformed 2D CFT. It specifically explains the emergence of fractional magnetic flux and the Little-Parks effect through a 1+1D gauge theory toy model. The work demonstrates the power of using boundary-side modularity and effective actions to understand holographic phase transitions.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a bridge between holographic gravity and effective field theories, demonstrating how universal features of phase transitions and topological effects like fractional flux can be captured by boundary-side statistical mechanics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to reconstruct the phase diagram and condensate behavior of a 2D holographic superconductor using only the boundary field theory description.

Main result. The authors successfully map the four-phase holographic structure (including Hawking-Page and superconducting transitions) using a deformed 2D CFT and provide a field-theoretic mechanism for fractional magnetic flux and the Little-Parks effect.

Method. The study employs large-c factorization, modular invariance, Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations, and an effective Ginzburg-Landau expansion to relate the bulk gravity solutions to the boundary CFT.

Model / system. The system consists of a 2D Conformal Field Theory (CFT) on a torus, subject to a relevant double-trace deformation, which is dual to Einstein gravity in AdS3 coupled to a U(1) gauge field and a charged scalar field.

Key observables. Scalar condensate, magnetic flux (integer and fractional), temperature, entropy, and zero-momentum susceptibility.

Important parameters / regimes. Central charge (c), scaling dimension (Delta), double-trace coupling (kappa or f), temperature (T), and winding number (n).

Assumptions / limitations. The large-c limit is assumed to suppress fluctuations and allow a mean-field description; the scalar mass is restricted to the range -1 < m^2 < -3/4.

Figures summary. Figures show the relationship between coupling and condensate, the reconstructed phase diagram in the T-kappa plane, and the suppression of the condensate as the winding number increases.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the holographic and dual field theory models, proceeds to reconstruct the phase diagram using modular invariance and effective action approaches, matches the near-critical behavior with Ginzburg-Landau theory, and concludes with a toy model explaining fractional flux and the Little-Parks effect.

Abstract

We investigate field theory models of holographic superconductors in which the condensation of the order parameter is induced by a Robin boundary condition. Assuming large-$c$ factorization, we study the phase diagram of a two-dimensional CFT deformed by a relevant double-trace perturbation. Using modular invariance, we relate the high- and low-temperature phases, reproducing analytically the results for the zero-winding sector of the holographic model. Moreover, we match the near-critical behaviour of the condensate with an effective Ginzburg--Landau field theory description. Another important feature of the holographic superconductor is the presence of vortices that carry fractional magnetic flux. We investigate a field theory toy model with similar properties and interpret it as a fractional Little--Parks effect.

Multi-rotational switching in a noncollinear antiferromagnet by spin-orbit torque

Authors: Yuma Sato, Yutaro Takeuchi, Yuta Yamane, Shun Kanai, Shunsuke Fukami
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18009v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers discovered that in noncollinear antiferromagnets, spin-orbit torque can drive the magnetic order through multiple full rotations rather than a single flip. This 'multi-rotational switching' explains why the current required to switch the device back is unexpectedly independent of the pulse duration, providing a new way to control antiferromagnetic states in nano-devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic dynamics of spin-orbit torque (SOT) driven switching in noncollinear antiferromagnets, specifically explaining the unconventional insensitivity of the switching-back threshold current density to pulse duration.

Main result. Discovery of a 'multi-rotational switching' mechanism where the Néel vector undergoes multiple rotations (3pi, 5pi, etc.) before reversal, and the establishment of a phase diagram containing pi-switching, multi-rotational, and coherent rotation regimes.

Method. Experimental measurement of anomalous Hall resistance and switching probabilities in Mn3Sn nanodots combined with numerical simulations using coupled Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert (LLG) equations with thermal fluctuations.

Model / system. A noncollinear antiferromagnetic Mn3Sn nanodot (Kagome lattice) in a W/Ta/Mn3Sn/MgO/Ru heterostructure, modeled using a three-sublattice LLG approach and a reduced equation of motion for the Néel vector in a tilted-washboard potential.

Key observables. Anomalous Hall resistance (Rxy), switching probability (P), and critical current densities (Js for switching and Jb for switching-back).

Important parameters / regimes. Pulse duration (5 ns to 5 ms), current density (up to 55 MA/cm2), in-plane magnetic field (Hx), and nanodot diameter (120-200 nm).

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics can be represented by a single Néel vector; the SOT is modeled via a Slonczewski-like torque; the system is treated within a three-sublattice model with equal saturation magnetization.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the device schematic, SEM image, and Hall resistance curves; supplementary figures show pulse waveforms, switching probability measurements, and the effect of nanodot diameter on critical currents.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the experimental platform and the observed switching anomaly, presents the experimental characterization of switching regimes, provides a theoretical/numerical explanation via multi-rotational dynamics, and concludes with the implications for spintronics.

Abstract

Spintronics has advanced through discoveries of various electrically-driven spin dynamics in nanomagnets. Here, we report a novel switching dynamics of spin systems driven by spin-orbit torque, using a noncollinear antiferromagnetic nanodot. With electric pulses spanning a wide range of durations and amplitudes, we find an unconventional insensitivity of a threshold current density to pulse duration in switch-back events. This observation is attributed to a previously unrecognized process, in which the noncollinear antiferromagnetic order undergoes multiple rotations before completing reversal, a phenomenon we term multi-rotational switching. Our theoretical analysis reveals that multi-rotational switching arises from the interplay of three key factors: current-driven coherent rotation of the noncollinear antiferromagnetic order, field-induced reorientation of the uncompensated net magnetization, and thermal fluctuations. These findings establish a microscopic mechanism governing current-induced switching in noncollinear antiferromagnets, a topic of growing interest for next-generation spintronics technologies, opening a new route to controlling antiferromagnetic order in nanodevices.

Nonreciprocal impurity scattering as a probe for pairing symmetries in kagome superconductors

Authors: Hong-Min Jiang, Hao Du, Shun-Li Yu
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16943v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a method to distinguish between s-wave and TRSB d-wave pairing in kagome superconductors by using the interference patterns of two magnetic impurities. It demonstrates that nonreciprocal impurity scattering provides a unique spectroscopic signature resolvable by STM, bypassing the ambiguities of conventional techniques.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Resolving the ambiguity in superconducting pairing symmetry and its link to time-reversal symmetry breaking (TRSB) in vanadium-based kagome superconductors.

Main result. The study shows that while single magnetic impurities cannot distinguish between s-wave and d-wave symmetries, two impurities reveal distinct LDOS patterns due to nonreciprocal scattering in the TRSB case.

Method. The authors use the Bogoliamb-de Gennes (BdG) framework, T-matrix formalism, and the Bethe-Salpeter equation to calculate the electronic Local Density of States (LDOS).

Model / system. A tight-binding kagome model representing the AV3Sb5 family, including chemical potential, nearest-neighbor hopping, and two superconducting order parameters: conventional on-site s-wave and TRSB d+id-wave.

Key observables. Local Density of States (LDOS) patterns, specifically the energy evolution and interference of Yu-Shiba-Rusinov (YSR) states.

Important parameters / regimes. Impurity positions, impurity strength (U, V), superconducting gap magnitude (delta), and the van Hove filling regime.

Assumptions / limitations. Magnetic impurities are treated as classical, static variables; the study focuses on a specific model of two representative pairing symmetries.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the kagome lattice and impurity configurations; Fig 3 shows LDOS interference at the midpoint of two impurities; Fig 4 compares s-wave and d-wave LDOS on interstitial sites; Fig 5 shows s-wave LDOS outside the impurity boundary.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of pairing symmetry ambiguity, defines the kagome model and impurity setup, analyzes single-impurity vs. two-impurity scattering, demonstrates the nonreciprocal signatures of TRSB, and concludes with experimental implications for STM.

Abstract

The superconducting (SC) pairing symmetry and its link to time-reversal symmetry breaking (TRSB) in the vanadium-based kagome superconductors remain unresolved, with ambiguities stemming from sublattice interference and charge-density-wave (CDW) entanglement with superconductivity. Using two representative SC pairings, i.e., the conventional on-site $s$-wave and the TRSB $d_{x^2-y^2}+id_{xy}$-wave, as a model study, we theoretically show that while single magnetic impurity yield qualitatively identical spectral behavior of local density of states (LDOS) for these two symmetries, two magnetic impurities give rise to distinct LDOS patterns. For the conventional on-site $s$-wave pairing, time-reversal symmetry (TRS) enforces equivalent forward and backward scattering between two impurities across all impurity configurations, leading to near disappearance of a Yu-Shiba-Rusinov (YSR) state pair along the line connecting the two impurities. However, for the TRSB $d_{x^2-y^2}+id_{xy}$-wave pairing, this scattering equivalence holds only for inversion-symmetric impurity configurations, with a pair of YSR disappearance restricted to this case. These distinct spectral features are resolvable in scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) experiments, providing a direct avenue to discriminate TRSB and non-TRSB SC pairing symmetries in kagome superconductors and an alternative method to probe SC nonreciprocity that circumvents the ambiguities of conventional critical current-based techniques.

Reprogrammable magnonic logic in a multiferroic heterostructure via magnetoelectric coupling

Authors: Ping Che, Amr Abdelsamie, Ádám Papp, Sali Salama, André Thiaville, Romain Lebrun, Stéphane Fusil, Vincent Garcia, Aymeric Vecchiola, Karim Bouzehouane, Manuel Bibes, Agnès Barthélémy, Jean-Paul Adam, Vladislav Demidov, Paolo Bortolotti, Abdelmadjid Anane, Isabella Boventer

Summary. This paper demonstrates a way to use electric fields to permanently and reversibly program the path of spin waves in a multiferroic material. By switching ferroelectric domains, the researchers created programmable magnonic waveguides and even simulated a frequency-splitting device. This provides a low-power, scalable platform for future magnonic-based logic and neuromorphic computing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for energy-efficient, non-volatile, and scalable on-chip magnonic devices for signal processing and neuromorphic computing, overcoming the limitations of current-driven or strain-based tuning methods.

Main result. Demonstrated a non-volatile, voltage-controlled method to tune magnon dispersion in a multiferroic heterostructure, enabling programmable waveguides and frequency demultiplexing via ferroelectric domain engineering.

Method. The researchers used pulsed laser deposition to grow BFO/LSMO heterostructures, Piezoresponse Force Microscopy (PFM) for domain writing, and Brillouin Light Scattering (BLS) for imaging spin-wave propagation and dispersion.

Model / system. A multiferroic thin-film heterostructure consisting of a BiFeO3 (BFO) ferroelectric layer and a La0.67Sr0.33MnO3 (LSMO) ferromagnetic layer grown on a NdGaO3 (NGO) substrate.

Key observables. Magnon dispersion relations, frequency shifts (up to 150 MHz), effective magnetization, Gilbert damping, and ferroelectric domain configurations.

Important parameters / regimes. Frequency shifts of ~150 MHz, effective magnetization M_eff = 336 mT, Gilbert damping alpha = 5.9 x 10^-3, and BFO layer thickness of 34 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of an effective equivalent magnetization pattern in simulations to represent the magnetoelectric influence of ferroelectric domains.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of the heterostructure and BLS process. Fig 2: PFM images showing reproducible ferroelectric domain writing. Fig 3: 2D BLS scans, magnon dispersion, and phase maps of the waveguide. Fig 4: Results of the inverse design for a frequency demultiplexer, including simulated propagation and output intensity.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of magnonic control, describes the BFO/LSMO heterostructure and growth process, details the experimental characterization of domain writing and magnon dispersion, presents the results of the magnetoelectric tuning, and concludes with an inverse-design demonstration of a functional device.

Abstract

The realization of fully reconfigurable, voltage-controlled, and programmable on-chip magnonic devices is essential to fully harness the potential of spin waves for signal processing, logic and neuromorphic computing. Yet, existing demonstrations of electrical tuning of magnonic responses are either volatile, current-driven and thus energy-inefficient, or rely on local strain modification limiting their scalability for wafer-scale integration. Here, we address this challenge using a BiFeO3/La0.67Sr0.33MnO3 multiferroic thin film heterostructure. We show that ferroelectric domain engineering in BiFeO3 enables deterministic tuning of the magnon dispersion of La0.67Sr0.33MnO3, producing frequency shifts up to $\sim 150 MHz$ and allowing reconfigurable waveguiding. Micro-focused Brillouin light scattering directly images these effects, revealing electrically defined magnonic waveguides and spatially programmable dispersion. Compared to conventional approaches, this method provides non-volatile and reversible control. Furthermore, using an inverse-design simulation code, we demonstrate the capability of our platform to perform advanced magnonic functions such as frequency demultiplexing. Our results open a new avenue for using magnetoelectric heterostructures for magnonic logic, with further applicability to reservoir and neuromorphic computing and AI driven magnonic devices.

Spin-caloritronic signatures of soft magnons in bilayer CrSBr

Authors: Rob den Teuling, Ping Tang, Gerrit E. W. Bauer, Yaroslav M. Blanter
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18478v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that in CrSBr, the spin angular momentum of magnons is not fixed at h-bar but diverges when magnon modes soften under an external field. This divergence creates a clear, measurable signature in the Spin Seebeck effect, providing a way to detect soft magnons via spin-caloritronic measurements.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how non-universal magnon spin angular momentum (SAM) and field-induced magnon softening in 2D van der Waals magnets like CrSBr lead to distinct spin-caloritronic signatures.

Main result. The study finds that triaxial anisotropy and dipolar interactions cause magnon SAM to diverge during field-induced softening, resulting in a pronounced peak in the thermal and diffusive Spin Seebeck response.

Method. Theoretical approach using Holstein-Primakoff transformation, Bogoliulyov transformation to diagonalize the Hamiltonian, and the linearized Boltzmann equation to calculate spin transport.

Model / system. Monolayer and bilayer CrSBr, a van der Waals magnetic insulator with tetragonal crystal lattice, featuring intralayer ferromagnetic and interlayer antiferromagnetic coupling.

Key observables. Magnon spin angular momentum (SAM), thermal Spin Seebeck coefficient (SSC), diffusive SSC, and magnon thermal conductivity.

Important parameters / regimes. Applied magnetic field (B0), temperature (1-5 K), magnetic phases (AFM, FM, Canted, Saturated), and anisotropy axes (easy axis b, intermediate axis a).

Assumptions / limitations. Non-interacting magnon approximation, lowest-order Holstein-Primakoff expansion, constant relaxation time approximation, and small-amplitude assumption (which breaks down near saturation).

Figures summary. Figures show the dependence of SAM, SSC, and thermal conductivity on the magnetic field for both monolayer and bilayer CrSBr, highlighting peaks and discontinuities at phase transitions and softening points.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-universal SAM, describes the CrSBr model and Hamiltonian, presents calculations for monolayer SAM and SSC, extends the analysis to bilayer systems, and concludes with temperature-dependent transport properties.

Abstract

Spin transport in magnetic insulators is often treated by assuming that magnons carry a fixed spin angular momentum of $\hbar$, which does not hold in general, however. Here we calculate the magnon spin angular momentum of a layered antiferromagnet as a function of applied magnetic field and wave vector. We show that the triaxial anisotropy and intralayer dipolar interactions in bilayer CrSBr renormalize the magnon spin angular momentum, which diverges upon field-induced magnon softening. This divergence gives rise to a pronounced peak in the thermal spin Seebeck response and provides a clear spin-caloritronic signature of soft magnons.

Supercollimating photonic crystal scintillators

Authors: Seou Choi, Sachin Vaidya, Charles Roques-Carmes, Marin Soljačić
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17006v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: interference shaping light 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes using 3D photonic crystals to engineer the direction of light emission in scintillators through supercollimation. By suppressing the lateral spreading of light, these structures allow for thicker, more efficient X-ray detectors without sacrificing image resolution. This breakthrough could enable high-quality medical imaging at significantly lower radiation doses.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The fundamental trade-off in X-ray scintillators where increasing thickness improves X-ray absorption but degrades spatial resolution due to diffraction-induced lateral light spreading.

Main result. 3D supercollimating photonic crystal scintillators can improve spatial resolution by up to an order of magnitude and reduce the required X-ray dose by approximately 10-fold while maintaining image quality.

Method. A multiscale modeling framework integrating nanophotonic band-structure simulations (MPB) with Monte Carlo particle transport (Geant4) to evaluate light propagation and X-ray imaging performance.

Model / system. A 3D single gyroid photonic crystal made of scintillating materials such as Lu2O3(Eu) or ZnSe(Te), designed with flat isofrequency contours to achieve supercollimation of emitted light.

Key observables. Spatial resolution (via Point Spread Function and Modulation Transfer Function), Detector Quantum Efficiency (DQE), Feature Similarity Index (FSIM), and X-ray dose.

Important parameters / regimes. Isofrequency contour (IFC) flatness, supercollimation bandwidth, X-ray energy (e.g., 30-33.5 keV), and refractive index contrast.

Assumptions / limitations. Light emitted outside the supercollimation bandwidth is assumed to be emitted isotropically; the detector is assumed to be an ideal silicon detector with 100% quantum efficiency.

Figures summary. Fig 1 illustrates the concept of supercollimation vs. bulk spreading; Fig 2 shows band structures, IFCs, and MIT logo imaging; Fig 3 compares emission spectra and DQE for different materials; Fig 4 demonstrates kidney vasculature imaging and dose reduction via FSIM scores.

Paper structure. Introduction of the resolution-efficiency trade-off, description of the 3D photonic crystal design and supercollimation mechanism, presentation of the multiscale simulation framework, results comparing performance to bulk scintillators, and demonstration of dose reduction in medical imaging simulations.

Abstract

Scintillators convert X-ray energy into visible or near-visible photons, enabling applications in high-energy particle detection and X-ray imaging. Increasing scintillator thickness improves X-ray absorption but degrades spatial resolution due to diffraction-induced lateral spreading of emitted light, resulting in a fundamental trade-off between detection efficiency and image resolution. Here, we propose a class of three-dimensional photonic crystal scintillators that overcomes this limitation through supercollimation, in which light propagates with suppressed diffraction. We develop a multiscale modeling framework that integrates nanophotonic band-structure simulations with Monte Carlo particle transport to quantitatively evaluate the performance of such scintillators. Our results show that supercollimating photonic crystal scintillators can enhance spatial resolution by up to an order of magnitude relative to conventional bulk scintillators of equal thickness. This improvement leads to a substantial increase in detector quantum efficiency (DQE), particularly at high spatial frequencies, enabling fine features to be preserved at reduced X-ray dose. We further demonstrate that comparable image quality can be achieved with approximately an order-of-magnitude lower X-ray dose. By directly engineering light transport within the bulk of the scintillator, this work establishes a nanophotonic route to simultaneously improving resolution and dose efficiency in X-ray imaging.

Effect of electric current on optical response of viscous electron-hole plasma

Authors: Yu. A. Pusep, M. A. T. Patricio, G. M. Jacobsen, M. D. Teodoro, G. M. Gusev, A. K. Bakarov
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18682v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

Summary. This paper explores how electrical currents can be used to manipulate the population of excitons and trions in a dense electron-hole plasma via Coulomb drag. By using Hall currents to drive charge accumulation, the researchers demonstrate a method for electrical control of optical responses in hydrodynamic systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how electric currents and Hall voltages affect the optical response and charge distribution of a dense, hydrodynamic electron-hole plasma.

Main result. The study demonstrates that Hall currents induce Coulomb drag, which causes light hole accumulation and the emergence of a double photoluminescence line (excitons and trions), distinguishing this from simple electric field-induced energy shifts.

Method. Scanning magneto-photoluminescence microscopy was performed at 4 K on a GaAs channel under intense laser excitation and applied magnetic fields (up to 9T) and electric currents.

Model / system. A mesoscopic n-doped GaAs channel containing a 14 nm thick quantum well, operating in a hydrodynamic electron-hole plasma regime where interparticle momentum-conserving collisions dominate.

Key observables. Photoluminescence (PL) energy, Landau fan patterns, and the appearance of specific spectral lines for excitons and trions.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic field up to 9T, Hall voltage, Hall current, electron drift velocity, and hole diffusion length (~5-10 micrometers).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is in the hydrodynamic regime where electron-hole scattering is intense enough to suppress the quantum Hall regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows PL contour plots for unbiased and parallel current configurations showing Landau fans and double PL lines; Figure 2 provides a microscopic image and a schematic of hole injection and diffusion; Figure 3 shows PL contour plots for perpendicular current configurations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the experimental setup and the hydrodynamic regime, describes the measurement of PL under different current configurations, compares the effects of Hall voltage versus Hall current, and concludes with the implications for controlling exciton populations.

Abstract

The influence of the Hall voltage on the photoluminescence of a dense hydrodynamic electron-hole plasma laser generated in a mesoscopic n-doped GaAs channel under intense laser excitation is studied. Laser excitation induces an interband current determined by the recombination of photogenerated electron-hole pairs. As a result, background electrons drifting under the influence of the Hall voltage form an effective Hall current. The Coulomb drag caused by the Hall current causes the accumulation of light holes, leading to the appearance of a double photoluminescence line formed by the recombination of excitons and trions. In contrast, in the absence of a Hall current, the shift in the photoluminescence energy associated with heavy holes occurs due to the electric field created by the Hall potential difference.

Summary. The paper uses machine learning-based interatomic potentials and Monte Carlo simulations to show that mechanical strain stabilizes Ag-Cu alloys in solar cells. Abstract

Alloying Ag into Cu(In,Ga)Se$_2$ has enabled record solar-cell efficiencies ($\sim23.6\%$), yet their long-term stability remains in question because initio calculations predict a Ag-Cu miscibility gap near ambient temperature. By off-lattice Monte-Carlo simulations using a newly developed machine learning (ML) interatomic potential we show that the presence of coherency strain is resolving the controversy between experimental observations and the predicted phase stability. The developed ML-MC framework provides a novel approach for resolving discrepancies in thermodynamic stability for systems where mechanical and chemical effects compete.

Competition and coexistence of superconducting symmetries in $p$-wave magnets

Authors: J. E. C. Carmelo, I. R. Pimentel, P. D. Sacramento
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17649v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that p-wave magnetic textures do more than just suppress conventional superconductivity; they can actually stabilize and promote spin-triplet p-wave pairing. By analyzing different magnetic helix orientations, the authors identify specific regimes where different superconducting symmetries coexist or compete, suggesting that unconventional magnets are promising platforms for realizing robust triplet superconductivity.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the interplay, competition, and coexistence between unconventional p-wave magnetism and different superconducting symmetries (spin-singlet s-wave and spin-triplet p-wave).

Main result. The researchers found that p-wave magnetic order can actively promote and stabilize spin-triplet p-wave pairing, with specific magnetic helix orientations driving quantum phase transitions between different pairing symmetries.

Method. The study uses a self-consistent Bogoliubov-de-Gennes (BdG) approach to numerically solve the microscopic equations on a lattice.

Model / system. A p-wave magnet on a square lattice featuring a helical magnetic structure along the x-direction, including electron hopping, Zeeman-like magnetic coupling, and superconducting pairing terms.

Key observables. Pairing amplitudes for s-wave and p-wave channels (mixed-spin and equal-spin), total spin projection (Sz), and critical temperatures (Tc).

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic coupling strength (J), magnetic helix orientation (pMzy vs. pMzx), temperature (T), and superconducting coupling strengths (Vs, Vp).

Assumptions / limitations. Mean-field approximation for superconductivity; periodic boundary conditions; square lattice geometry.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the two different magnetic helix configurations (pMzy and pMzx) on the square lattice.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the magnetic helix model, details the BdG methodology, analyzes the competition/coexistence of pairing symmetries under varying magnetic coupling, and discusses the implications for proximity effects and material realization.

Abstract

We investigate the interplay between unconventional magnetism and superconductivity in a model of a $p$-wave magnet on a square lattice. Using a self-consistent Bogoliubov-de-Gennes approach, we analyze the pairing amplitudes, competition, and coexistence of spin-singlet $s$-wave and spin-triplet $p$-wave pairings in the presence of a magnetic texture with a helical structure along the $x$ direction that is repeated in the $y$ direction. We find that the magnetic helix selectively stabilizes different pairing symmetries depending on its orientation and strength. In particular, mixed-spin $p_x$-wave pairing is enhanced at intermediate magnetic couplings and equal-spin $p_y$-wave pairing is robust and insensitive to all coupling intensities. When the multiple order parameters are simultaneously considered, we find regimes of coexistence and competition. Increasing the magnetic coupling drives two quantum phase transitions. The first from dominant spin-singlet $s$-wave to mixed-spin triplet $p_x$-wave pairings in a regime of coexistence. The second from spin-singlet $s$-wave and mixed-spin triplet $p$-wave pairings with total spin projection $S_z=0$ to dominant equal-spin triplet $p_y$-wave pairings with $S_z=\pm1$ in a regime of mutually exclusive superconducting phases. Our results demonstrate that $p$-wave magnetic order does not merely diminish spin-singlet $s$-wave superconductivity but can actively promote and stabilize spin-triplet $p$-wave pairing, both intrinsically and in proximity to spin-singlet $s$-wave superconductors. These findings highlight unconventional magnets as promising materials for realizing robust triplet superconductivity.

Abstract

Excitons in two-dimensional semiconductors are directly exposed to the environment and are sensitive to the dielectric properties of their surrounding. Here, we show that the Rydberg series of excited states of excitons in a monolayer WSe$_2$ encapsulated in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) can be used to probe the pressure-induced modifications of the surrounding dielectric properties. We propose a model based on the pressure induced evolution of the interlayer distances in this van der Waals heterostructure and on the bulk dielectric properties of hBN. This approach allows a direct measurement of the dielectric constant of pressurized hBN and establishes a new methodology for dielectric sensing.

Abstract

While modern Large Language Models (LLMs) and agentic artificial intelligence (AI) have demonstrated transformative capabilities in digital domains, the realization of embodied AI capable of real-world scientific discovery remains a difficult frontier. The advancements are hindered by the inherent complexity of integrating high-level reasoning, multimodal information processing and real-time physical execution. Here we introduce Qumus, the first AI quantum materials experimentalist. Physically embodied within a robotic mini-laboratory, Qumus is an intelligent, multimodal, and multi-agent system designed for the creation and nano-processing of atomically thin two-dimensional (2D) materials and stacked van der Waals (vdW) structures. Qumus autonomously navigates the full scientific cycle, from hypothesis generation and protocol planning to multi-step experimental execution, result analysis and reporting, acting as an experimentalist. Markedly, the system has achieved, for the first time, the AI-creation of graphene, as well as the first AI-fabrication of complex nanodevices including atomically thin field-effect transistors via vdW stacking. Qumus excels at these tasks by demonstrating autonomous error correction and closed-loop experimentation. Our results establish a generalizable framework for self-improving embodied AI systems that learn directly from the quantum world, opening a pathway toward accelerated discovery in quantum materials, electronics and beyond.
