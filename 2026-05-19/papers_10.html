Revealing Hund superdispersion with tunneling spectroscopy

Authors: Luke C. Rhodes, Fabian B. Kugler, Olivier Gingras, Carolina Marques, Edgar Abarca Morales, Phil D. C. King, Antoine Georges, Peter Wahl
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16580v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that Hund coupling in Sr2RuO4 creates a unique 'superdispersion' feature where band velocity reverses direction. By using tunneling spectroscopy to probe unoccupied states, the authors successfully match experimental conductance dips with theoretical predictions of non-monotonic self-energy. This provides a new experimental route to identifying Hund physics in multi-orbital materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of experimentally identifying and verifying the unique spectroscopic signature of Hund superdispersion in multi-orbital Hund metals, which is difficult to observe via ARPES due to its presence in unoccupied states.

Main result. The authors provide direct experimental evidence for Hund-induced superdispersion in Sr2RuO4 using tunneling spectroscopy, showing that Hund coupling induces a non-monotonic self-energy that causes band velocity to reverse direction.

Method. The study combines experimental STM/STS and ARPES with a theoretical framework involving DFT, DMFT (using NRG solver), and continuum local density of states (cLDOS) calculations.

Model / system. The physical system is the Hund metal Sr2RuO4, modeled using a multi-orbital (t2g) tight-binding Hamiltonian that incorporates Coulomb repulsion (U), Hund coupling (J), and spin-orbit coupling (SOC).

Key observables. Differential conductance (dI/dV), tunneling spectra (Feenstra function L(V)), quasiparticle interference (QPI) patterns, and the real and imaginary parts of the self-energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Hund coupling (J = 0.4 eV), Hubbard repulsion (U = 2.3 eV), spin-orbit coupling (lambda approx 200 meV), and energy scales of superdispersion (approx 160 meV).

Assumptions / limitations. The bulk self-energy is assumed to be applicable to the surface electronic structure despite surface reconstruction (octahedral rotations).

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares waterfall vs. superdispersion schematics and self-energy components; Figure 2 shows surface vs. bulk structures and DFT band structures; Figure 4 presents spectral functions and simulated tunneling spectra (Feenstra function) showing the 160 meV dip.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the distinction between Mott and Hund physics, describes the experimental STM/ARPES setup and theoretical DFT+DMFT framework, presents the identification of superdispersion in the unoccupied states, and validates the model through comparison of simulated and experimental tunneling spectra.

Abstract

In cuprate superconductors, electron-electron repulsion results in characteristic spectroscopic features known as waterfalls', where the sharp quasiparticle dispersion transitions into broad Hubbard bands. However, in multi-orbital systems, the additional Hund coupling results in behavior that defies the conventional Mott--Hubbard paradigm, creating qualitatively distinctsuperdispersive' features in the spectral function. Here, we use tunneling spectroscopy to reveal this signature of Hund physics in Sr$_2$RuO$_4$. By combining density functional theory, dynamical mean-field theory, and continuum local density of states calculations, we show that the experimental features are in excellent agreement with theoretical predictions and intimately linked to the non-monotonous energy dependence of the real part of the self-energy in a Hund metal. Our results provide direct experimental evidence for Hund-induced spectroscopic features and open a new route to probing correlation effects in quantum materials.

Scaling Quantum Optimization for Unit Commitment via Pauli Correlation Encoding

Authors: Kien X. Nguyen, Ilya Safro, Xiaoyuan Liu
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17145v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a way to solve large-scale power grid scheduling problems using much fewer qubits than previously possible. By using Pauli Correlation Encoding, the authors demonstrate that a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm can handle hundreds of decision variables, providing near-optimal solutions for complex energy management tasks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Scaling the quantum optimization of the Unit Commitment (UC) problem, which is NP-hard, by addressing the prohibitive qubit requirements of traditional encoding methods for multi-period power system scheduling.

Main result. The proposed method scales to 312 binary variables using only 15 qubits and produces feasible schedules with operating costs within 1-7% of the classical CPLEX optimum for small scales and 13-16% for large scales.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical leader-follower architecture using Pauli Correlation Encoding (PCE) to compress variables into fewer qubits, combined with a differentiable bilevel optimization approach using a classical Quadratic Program (QP) solver.

Model / system. A mixed-integer quadratic bilevel optimization model for power system unit commitment, involving binary on/off decisions and continuous power dispatch subject to load balance, ramping, and reserve constraints.

Key observables. Operating cost gap, feasibility rate, constraint violation percentage, and optimization stability (standard deviation).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (down to 4-6 for small and 15 for large scales), number of optimization steps, ansatz depth (layers), and PCE encoding parameters.

Assumptions / limitations. The cost function is assumed to be quadratic, and the optimization uses a smooth surrogate/relaxation of constraints to ensure differentiability.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the hybrid optimization architecture; Figure 2 and 3 show ablation studies on ansatz depth and optimization steps; Figure 4 and 5 show performance and scalability on large-scale instances.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the UC problem and scaling challenges, details the PCE encoding and hybrid bilevel architecture, describes the gradient-based optimization strategy, presents experimental results on small and large-scale power systems, and concludes with ablation studies and limitations.

Abstract

Unit commitment is an important optimization problem in power system operations, classified as NP-hard. This paper presents a hybrid quantum-classical method for the unit commitment problem with time-dependent constraints, where decisions must be made about which generators to turn on/off and how much power they should produce over a planning horizon. We use a hybrid quantum-classical optimization procedure to determine the on/off schedules of the generating units and the corresponding power dispatch that satisfies operational constraints such as load balance, generator limits, ramping, and reserve requirements. We frame the optimization loop as a leader-follower structure, where the quantum optimizer leads to give the on/off decisions, and the classical optimizer follows to produce the power level schedule. Leveraging Pauli-Correlation Encoding, our method scales to horizon-wide unit commitment schedules by encoding the binary variables with far fewer qubits. By combining these components, the method can handle multi-period settings while using far fewer qubits than straightforward quantum encodings that allocate one qubit per decision variable as in prior approaches. We evaluate the approach on both small- and large-scale instances, up to 312 binary variables, and show that it reliably produces feasible schedules with competitive operating costs.

Spatial statistics for screening molecular structures

Authors: Pranoy Ray, Surya R. Kalidindi
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17147v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper proposes replacing heavy, data-hungry deep learning architectures with a physics-informed approach using spatial statistics and FFTs. This method enables highly accurate material property prediction even with extremely small datasets and provides a convex latent space suitable for efficient molecular design.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Traditional deep learning models for materials discovery require massive DFT-labeled datasets and struggle with chemically disordered configurations, chiral geometries, and non-convex latent representations that hinder inverse design.

Main result. A physics-informed feature engineering framework using spatial statistics achieves sub-2% prediction error with as few as 10 training samples and provides strictly convex representations for efficient optimization.

Method. The method uses voxelized 3D scalar fields to compute two-point auto- and cross-correlations via Fast Fourier Transforms, followed by PCA for dimensionality reduction into low-dimensional manifolds.

Model / system. The framework is scale-agnostic and applicable to periodic crystals (e.g., Silicon), chemically disordered high-entropy alloys, and non-periodic organic molecules (e.g., PFAS).

Key observables. Two-point spatial correlations, pseudo-charge densities, and structural fingerprints like helicity and short-range order.

Important parameters / regimes. Data-scarce regimes (10-30 training samples), lean neural networks (<100k parameters), and computational complexity scaling as O(S log S).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of non-interacting pseudo-densities neglects explicit charge transfer and electron correlation effects; 2-point statistics may smooth over rare, localized topological anomalies.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows structural complexity across different regimes; Figure 2 illustrates the voxelization pipeline; Figure 3 shows voxelized data for a high-entropy alloy; Figure 4 depicts the end-to-end workflow; Figure 6 shows a closed-loop autonomous discovery pipeline.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the limitations of current GNNs, proposes a spatial statistics-based feature engineering workflow (voxelization, FFT-based correlations, PCA), demonstrates performance across various material classes (crystals, alloys, molecules), and discusses the utility for inverse design and active learning.

Abstract

The dominant paradigm in computational materials discovery relies on heavily parameterized deep architectures, including message-passing graph networks and equivariant models, that require millions of DFT-labeled training structures and produce non-convex latent representations that complicate continuous optimization for inverse design. These architectures are impractical in data-scarce regimes, which is the typical case in molecular screening, and exhibit well-documented limitations in capturing chemically disordered configurations and chiral geometries. This review presents feature engineering based on spatial statistics as a physically rigorous and immediately deployable alternative. Molecular structures are encoded as voxelized scalar fields, and two-point auto- and cross-correlations are evaluated deterministically via Fast Fourier Transforms, explicitly transferring the burden of spatial pattern recognition from the learning algorithm to a closed-form, physics-informed operation. Principal component analysis of the resulting correlation maps yields low-dimensional, strictly convex representations that support lean neural networks (<100k trainable parameters) and non-parametric surrogate models, achieving sub-2% prediction error with as few as 10 training samples. Demonstrated across periodic crystals, chemically disordered high-entropy alloys, and non-periodic organic molecules, this framework enables Bayesian active learning and zero-shot extrapolation on commodity hardware, which current large-scale architectures cannot replicate at equivalent data budgets.

Specific heat and susceptibility of S=1/2 antiferromagnets on square, triangular, and kagome lattices

Authors: Chisa Hotta
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17420v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a numerical framework using Sine-Square Deformation to study the thermodynamic properties of frustrated quantum magnets. It successfully captures complex features like double-peak specific heat in kagome lattices, providing a way to mimic the thermodynamic limit using small-scale clusters.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of accurately calculating the low-temperature thermodynamic properties of frustrated 2D quantum magnets in the thermodynamic limit due to finite-size effects and the sign problem in traditional numerical methods.

Main result. The study identifies a double-peak structure in the specific heat of kagome and triangular lattices and shows that the kagome lattice exhibits a strong enhancement of magnetic susceptibility at low temperatures due to magnetic excited states.

Method. The Sine-Square Deformation (SSD) technique is applied to the Hamiltonian to create fictitious bulk states, combined with the Thermal Pure Quantum (TPQ) method for finite-temperature calculations.

Model / system. S=1/2 antiferromagnetic Heisenberg models on 1D chains, square, triangular, and kagome lattices.

Key observables. Energy density, specific heat (Cv), magnetic susceptibility (chi), and thermal entropy density.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T/J), exchange interaction (J), and system size (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The SSD method assumes that the center of the deformed system effectively mimics the thermodynamic limit; kernel regression assumes a smooth temperature dependence for extrapolating data.

Figures summary. Figure 1 defines the SSD function and its spatial distribution; Figure 2 compares specific heat and susceptibility across 1D, square, triangular, and kagome lattices; Figure 3 shows the single-particle density of states; Figure 4 demonstrates convergence of energy density; Figure 6 highlights the reduction of the finite-size gap in 1D chains.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SSD method, validates it using free fermion and 1D chain benchmarks, applies it to 2D square lattices to compare with QMC, and finally investigates the thermodynamic properties of frustrated triangular and kagome lattices.

Abstract

We study the temperature dependence of the thermodynamic properties of spin-1/2 antiferromagnets on two-dimensional lattices. Our analysis employs the sine-square deformation (SSD), in which a real-space envelope function is applied to the Hamiltonian so that the local energy scale is smoothly reduced to zero at the system boundaries. The quantum eigenstates of the SSD Hamiltonian exhibit bulk-like behavior near the system center, effectively mimicking the thermodynamic limit even in small finite-size calculations. Using these fictitious bulk states, we compute the energy density, specific heat, and magnetic susceptibility as functions of temperature. We find that both the triangular- and kagome-lattice antiferromagnets show either a shoulder or a pronounced double-peak structure in the low-temperature specific heat, whereas the kagome case particularly shows a strong enhancement of magnetic susceptibility down to the lowest temperature range. These direct comparisons, together with the square-lattice and one-dimensional cases, reveal that although both frustrated systems retain a substantial amount of entropy, the low-energy excitations below ~ 0.5J of the kagome lattice are predominantly governed by the magnetic excited states, whereas not much for the triangular lattice.

Stable minimum principles for scattering states

Authors: Scott Lawrence, Yukari Yamauchi
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17139v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a new family of stable minimum principles for approximating quantum scattering states using the L1 norm of the Schrodinger equation violation. It proves that this metric allows for rigorous error bounds on the S-matrix and cross sections, making it a robust foundation for numerical scattering algorithms. The method is shown to be applicable to complex systems, including momentum-dependent and many-body potentials.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and numerical physics as it provides a rigorous error-bounding framework for using variational/machine-learning-based wavefunctions to compute scattering observables.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing stable variational minimum principles for approximating quantum-mechanical scattering states and bounding the error in the resulting S-matrix.

Main result. The authors establish that the L1 norm of the Schrodinger violation provides a stable metric for bounding errors in scattering amplitudes, phase shifts, and cross sections, unlike the L2 norm which can be unstable.

Method. Introduction of a family of stable minimum principles using the L1 norm of the Schrodinger violation, designed to be compatible with modern computational algorithms like Neural Quantum States and Variational Monte Carlo.

Model / system. Nonrelativistic quantum-mechanical scattering involving single-particle, many-body, and momentum-dependent potentials, including Coulomb interactions and scattering of bound states.

Key observables. S-matrix elements, scattering amplitudes, phase shifts, total cross section, and probability flux.

Important parameters / regimes. Energy (E), particle mass (M), potential strength and range, and the L1 norm of the Schrodinger violation.

Assumptions / limitations. The approximate wavefunction is assumed to have the correct ingoing plane wave; potentials are generally assumed to be self-adjoint and decay sufficiently rapidly at large distances.

Figures summary. A schematic of the problem setup.

Paper structure. The paper presents the minimum principle, establishes stability estimates, extends results to general and momentum-dependent potentials, discusses many-body scattering, and provides counterexamples for unstable metrics.

Abstract

Quantum-mechanical scattering states are energy eigenstates obeying particular boundary conditions, whose behavior at infinity encodes the S-matrix which defines the outcoming of scattering experiments. With an eye toward numerical algorithms for computing nonrelativistic S-matrices, we present a family of stable minimum principles for scattering states. States that approximately satisfy these minimum principles are shown to have a bounded difference with the true scattering states. These minimum principles and stability estimates can be used to obtain rigorous bounds on scattering amplitudes. We show that these minimum principles are applicable to momentum-dependent potentials, long-range (Coulomb) interactions, and elastic or inelastic scattering of bound states.

The QuaST Decision Tree: Achieving Automation With Data-Based Recommendations

Summary. This paper introduces the QuaST Decision Tree, a modular software framework designed to automate the selection of quantum algorithms and parameters for combinatorial optimization. By using data-driven recommendations and scalability analysis, it reduces the need for manual trial-and-error and helps identify when quantum approaches are practically feasible compared to classical methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The quantum software stack lacks a high-level abstraction layer to automate the complex decision-making process involved in selecting optimal algorithms, encodings, and hyperparameters for hybrid quantum-classical optimization.

Main result. The authors present the QuaST Decision Tree (QDT), a modular framework that automates algorithm selection and provides a scalability analysis module to predict the feasibility of Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs).

Method. The QDT uses a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) architecture with modular nodes and a data-driven automation module that utilizes a database of precomputed benchmarks to estimate required measurement shots.

Model / system. The framework targets quantum-enhanced combinatorial optimization (CO) using models such as QUBO, Ising, and MaxCut, applicable to gate-based NISQ devices, quantum annealers, and future error-corrected systems.

Key observables. Finite sampling error, optimizer tolerance threshold, approximation ratios, and the quantum disadvantage boundary.

Important parameters / regimes. Problem size (number of variables n), number of measurement shots (n_shots), QUBO density, and optimizer convergence thresholds.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Python package hierarchy; Figure 2 illustrates the decision tree branching logic for algorithm selection; Table I and II detail the class methods for the QDT core; Table III and IV quantify execution overhead and integration effort.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of software stack gaps, describes the modular architecture and design principles of the QDT, presents a specific automation module for VQA feasibility, validates the framework's efficiency and integration ease, and discusses future directions.

Abstract

Quantum computers are increasingly powerful. Software tools for the development of quantum-enhanced algorithms are maturing. However, the software stack still lacks the connection to applications that would enable hybrid algorithms combining classical and quantum computing steps. End users need to be assisted in choosing the best combination of preprocessing, postprocessing, classical and quantum algorithms options. The application-facing software stack is therefore required to cover problem modeling, encoding, algorithm selection and hyperparameter tuning. A variety of tools exist for specific recommendations. The QuaST Decision Tree reflects the complexity in combining individual decisions in its modular network structure, consisting of flexible computation nodes with modular recommendations. It can easily be configured to serve in an industrial solver, an HPC software stack, or for rapid prototyping in development. The key ingredient, automation, is delivered by modules. We present one such module judging the feasibility of variational algorithms based on a robust scalability analysis and classification of problem instances. The automation improves the performance of end-to-end solutions, highlights the benefit to be gained from the hybrid quantum solution, reduces expensive trial-and-error testing, and leads to an improved utilization of quantum devices for a practical benefit.

Theory of melting lines with a variable enthalpy of fusion

Authors: Anthony N. Papathanassiou
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17631v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new theoretical framework for calculating melting lines by accounting for the variable enthalpy of fusion caused by solid-state anharmonicity. The resulting model predicts a universal parabolic trajectory for phase boundaries that is consistent with both recent phonon-based theories and experimental observations across diverse materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty in defining a universal analytical equation for melting lines in P-T phase diagrams due to the inadequacy of the constant latent heat approximation in the Clausius-Clapeyron relation.

Main result. The derivation of a universal parabolic scaling law for melting lines, which aligns with the Phonon Theory of Liquids and matches experimental data for various substances.

Method. A two-phase analytical model that modifies the Clausius-Clapeyron equation to include a variable enthalpy of fusion correlated to the specific volumes of coexisting phases.

Model / system. A thermodynamic model of the solid-liquid phase transition (melting) applicable to a wide range of condensed matter, including noble gases, molecular systems, metals, and semiconductors.

Key observables. Melting lines (P-T phase boundaries), enthalpy of fusion, and the slope of the melting curve.

Important parameters / regimes. Enthalpy of fusion (delta H), scaling parameter epsilon (Cp/beta), bulk moduli, thermal expansion coefficients, and specific volumes.

Assumptions / limitations. The dominance of the anharmonic, volume-dependent component of heat capacity near the melting point and the assumption that the scaling parameter epsilon remains constant over narrow pressure ranges.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of melting line derivation, develops a modified Clausius-Clapeyron relation based on variable enthalpy, derives a second-order differential equation for the melting line, provides analytical parabolic solutions, and validates the results against existing theories and experimental data.

Abstract

Conventional derivations of phase boundaries from the Clausius-Clapeyron (CC) relation often employ the constant latent heat approximation to maintain analytical functions of the sublimation and boiling curves. To address the complex thermodynamics of the solid-liquid transition, we develop a two-phase analytical model by modifying the CC equation to account for a variable enthalpy of fusion along the melting line (ML). Our methodology utilizes recent theoretical and experimental progress demonstrating that the isobaric heat capacity of crystalline solids near the melting point features a dominant anharmonic, volume-dependent component. Consequently, the latent heat is correlated to the specific volumes of the coexisting phases. Differentiation of this modified CC relation yields a second-order differential equation governing ML. By imposing appropriate e boundary conditions, physically acceptable approximate parabolic solutions are derived. The parameters of these analytic functions are defined exclusively by fundamental thermophysical properties, including the bulk moduli, thermal expansion coefficients, and specific volumes of the coexisting phases, as well as the isobaric heat capacity of the solid. Our derivation, rooted in solid-state anharmonicity, yields approximate parabolic scaling laws that corroborate with a recent universal model derived from the Phonon Theory of Liquids [K. Trachenko, Phys. Rev. E 109, 034122 (2024)], supporting the universal parabolic nature of melting curves from a completely distinct theoretical foundation.

Truncated-Binary Encoding: Spectral Degree Reduction of Combinatorial Optimization Problems for Quantum Hardware

Authors: Tristan Zaborniak
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17143v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new method called Truncated-Binary Encoding to reduce the complexity of optimization problems mapped to quantum hardware. By strategically dropping high-degree interactions in the Ising basis, it minimizes hardware overhead while providing mathematical guarantees that the essential features of the optimization landscape remain intact.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. High-order interactions in the encoding of discrete cost functions into Ising models create high-degree monomials that are incompatible with the limited native locality of current quantum annealing and gate-based hardware.

Main result. The paper introduces Truncated-Binary Encoding (TBE), proving that discarding high-degree Ising monomials preserves the global minimum and local minimum structure under specific energy gap and basin barrier conditions.

Method. The author uses Walsh-Hadamard spectral analysis to treat the encoding as a low-pass filter and employs the Lindeberg-Feller Central Limit Theorem to characterize the truncation error as a Gaussian noise floor.

Model / system. The model consists of Cost Function Networks (CFNs) mapped to Ising-basis Higher-Order Unconstrained Binary Optimization (HUBO) problems, specifically targeting quantum annealing and gate-based architectures like QAOA.

Key observables. Truncation error (L-infinity norm), spectral power (P_k), energy gap (Delta E*), and the single bit-flip basin barrier (delta f).

Important parameters / regimes. The truncation cutoff degree (k_max), the maximum monomial degree (k_full), and the smoothness of cost tables (Lipschitz constants).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes an Ising-basis formulation and relies on a random-coupling ensemble where omitted couplings are treated as independent zero-mean random variables.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of high-order interaction overhead, introduces the TBE method, establishes error bounds and preservation conditions for minima, performs a spectral analysis using Walsh transforms, and concludes with a diagnostic workflow for selecting the truncation cutoff.

Abstract

Exact-binary encoding compiles a discrete cost function network (CFN) into a higher-order unconstrained binary optimization (HUBO) problem whose maximum monomial degree grows with the cardinalities of the underlying CFN variables. Given that quantum optimization hardware generally favours quadratic unconstrained binary optimization or low-degree HUBO Hamiltonians, high-cardinality CFNs therefore incur substantial overhead in the form of circuit depth, or ancilla qubits when degree-reduction techniques are employed. To ameliorate these issues, we propose \textit{truncated-binary encoding} (TBE): a modification of exact-binary encoding in which Ising-basis monomials exceeding a chosen cutoff $k_\text{max}$ are dropped from the encoded cost. We establish a tight $L^\infty$ bound on the truncation error in terms of the omitted couplings, derive sufficient conditions on the energy gap and on the single bit-flip basin barrier under which TBE preserves the global minimum and its local-minimum structure, and characterize a noise floor condition on the spectral profile under which the truncation residual acts as a perturbative correction to the underlying landscape. We then express the encoded coefficients directly as Walsh transforms of the underlying CFN cost tables, and prove a bound under which smoothness of each cost table implies rapid decay of its high-degree Walsh mass. Together these results yield a principled \textit{a priori} criterion for selecting $k_\text{max}$ and for judging whether a given CFN admits a small-$k_\text{max}$ TBE.

When Symmetries Twist: Anomaly Inflow on Monodromy Defects

Summary. This paper explores how bulk anomalies dictate the physics of monodromy defects, demonstrating that these defects host protected edge modes and undergo spectral pumping. It provides a unified framework for understanding these defects as domain walls in both gapped SPT phases and gapless anomalous theories.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the properties of monodromy defects, which are codimension-2 defects that terminate topological symmetry operators, specifically focusing on how bulk 't Hooft anomalies impact their physics.

Main result. The author shows that in anomalous gapless theories, monodromy defects are well-defined as domain walls between a symmetry defect and a topological decoration, leading to protected chiral edge modes and adiabatic spectral pumping of gapless degrees of freedom.

Method. The study utilizes the anomaly inflow mechanism, transgression/slant product in group cohomology, radial quantization, and the Symmetry TFT (SymTFT) framework.

Model / system. The paper considers gapped Symmetry Protected Topological (SPT) phases and gapless theories with anomalous global symmetries, including (3+1)d Dirac fermions, (1+1)d compact bosons, and (2+1)d scalar QED.

Important parameters / regimes. The twist parameter (magnetic flux) $ u$, the symmetry group $G$ and its centralizer $C_G(g)$, and the $ heta$ angle periodicity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the monodromy defect as a dynamical termination of a symmetry operator or as being sourced by magnetic flux; Figure 2 shows the defect as a domain wall between a symmetry defect and a topological decoration.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining monodromy defects, moves to using topological defects as probes of SPTs, extends these concepts to anomalous gapless theories, and concludes with specific applications to free Dirac fermions and lattice models.

Abstract

Monodromy defects describe a dynamical termination of topological symmetry operators, and are sourced by a localized background magnetic flux. We study their properties in gapped SPT phases and, by inflow, in gapless theories with an anomalous symmetry. The background flux acts as a source for the anomaly, impacting the definition of the monodromy defect, which can only be defined as a domain wall between the symmetry generator and a topological order induced by the anomaly. The topological dressing has several consequences, such as the presence of protected chiral edge modes on the defect's worldvolume, and the adiabatic pumping of gapless degrees of freedom bound to the localized flux. We verify our predictions in several examples, focusing on monodromy defects for anomalous chiral symmetries, both in the continuum and on the lattice.

Abstract

We investigate tunnel ferromagnetic Josephson junctions based on Superconductor-Insulator-thin superconductor-Ferromagnet-Superconductor multilayers. Important parameters / regimes. AFM exchange coupling (omega_E), spin-transfer torque (STT) coefficients (u, beta), pinning potential frequency (omega_pin), and Gilbert damping (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes spatial uniformity in the yz plane (1D walls), small Gilbert damping, and the limit where AFM exchange is dominant (|n| approx 1 and |m| much less than 1).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the model system with a driven domain wall; Figure 2 illustrates the two coupled DW modes (translational and canting).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of emergent impedance, develops a theoretical formalism using LLG equations and perturbation theory, identifies the two competing dynamical mechanisms, analyzes the frequency-dependent impedance regimes, and concludes with implications for spintronics.

Abstract

We theoretically investigate emergent impedance induced by domain-wall dynamics in antiferromagnets. Emergent impedance, arising from a combined action of spin-transfer torque and spinmotive force, was previously predicted and observed in spiral magnets. Here we develop a formalism for the electrical response of an antiferromagnetic domain wall under ac currents, and obtain analytical expressions for the resulting emergent impedance. We find that two dynamical modes play separate roles in the emergent impedance: Translational motion of the domain-wall center generates a contribution proportional to its velocity, analogous to that arising from the corresponding motion of a spiral magnet. Another contribution, unique to antiferromagnetic domain walls, originates from the time-dependent canting of the sublattice magnetizations localized within the moving domain wall, whose magnitude is inversely proportional to the antiferromagnetic exchange coupling constant. The competition between these two distinct contributions determines the sign and magnitude of the imaginary part of the emergent impedance at sub-resonant frequencies. Our results provide a fundamental insight into electron transport in antiferromagnets, and open avenues for novel antiferromagnet-based spintronics devices.
