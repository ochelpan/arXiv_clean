Summary. This paper demonstrates the realization of many-body quantum optics by coupling multiple quantum dots within a nanophotonic waveguide. By tuning the number of emitters, the researchers show that higher-order photon correlations can be engineered, specifically observing genuine three-photon correlations. This work provides a scalable foundation for creating many-body entangled states and quantum simulators.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates the experimental onset of many-body quantum optics in a waveguide-QED setting, providing a platform for quantum simulators, many-body entanglement, and exploring novel quantum phase transitions through engineered photon-photon interactions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving deterministic, scalable control of light-matter interactions to extend quantum optics from single-photon/single-atom regimes to many-body quantum optics.

Main result. The researchers realized many-body quantum optics in a waveguide by demonstrating that adding quantum emitters generates higher-order photon correlations, specifically observing genuine three-photon correlations from a pair of coupled emitters.

Method. The experiment uses pulsed excitation of semiconductor quantum dots in a nanophotonic waveguide, measuring n-photon correlation functions via three detection channels and comparing results to theoretical simulations.

Model / system. A nanophotonic photonic crystal waveguide (PCW) coupled to multiple InAs quantum dots (QDs) in a GaAs p-i-n diode heterostructure, where emitters are independently tunable via electrical (Stark) and magnetic (Zeeman) effects.

Key observables. n-photon correlation functions (g(2), g(3), g(4)), connected (genuine) and disconnected components of third-order correlations (Gc(3), Gd(3)), and transmission/reflection spectra.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling phase (phi = 0.8pi), decay rate (Gamma), dephuring rate (gamma_d), beta factor, and the scaling law where the nth-order joint cumulant peaks at n = m + 1.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the impact of spectral diffusion and detector jitter can be modeled; simulations exclude blinking effects.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the waveguide schematic and simulations of photon correlations; Figure 2 displays experimental g(3) measurements for single and coupled QDs; Figure 4 shows connected vs. disconnected three-photon components; Figure 5 illustrates the scalability to three emitters.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of many-body quantum optics, describes the experimental platform and tuning mechanisms, presents results for single and coupled emitters (including higher-order correlations), demonstrates scalability to three emitters, and concludes with a discussion on limitations and future directions.

Abstract

Controlling light photon-by-photon is central to quantum optics. At a fundamental level, photon interactions are mediated by their coupling to atoms, and ultimate control requires deterministic light-matter interfacing of single photons to single atoms. Extending this paradigm to radiatively couple multiple individual atoms in a deterministic and scalable manner opens the arena of many-body quantum optics. Here, we realize such a setting by coherently coupling solid-state artificial atoms to a nanophotonic waveguide and demonstrate higher-order photon correlations that are controlled by the number of quantum emitters. We study the scaling of nonlinear photonic transport induced by emitter-photon scattering and demonstrate that adding a quantum emitter generates higher-order photon correlations. Specifically, we experimentally observe genuine three-photon correlations from a pair of collectively coupled emitters, while contributions from lower photon numbers are suppressed. In addition, we scale to three resonant quantum emitters coupled to the waveguide. These advancements demonstrate the onset of many-body quantum optics in waveguide quantum electrodynamics, enabling new photonic quantum simulators, the creation of many-body entangled states, and the exploration of novel quantum phase transitions.

Summary. The researchers have demonstrated a new way to identify the spatial patterns (Schmidt modes) of entangled photon pairs without knowing them beforehand. By measuring the coherence of a single photon, they successfully mapped out over 3000 modes with high accuracy. This breakthrough enables much easier use of high-dimensional light for complex quantum communication and computing tasks.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and photonic quantum information as it provides a way to characterize and potentially manipulate extremely high-dimensional qudits without needing prior knowledge of their spatial structure.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing techniques for characterizing high-dimensional entangled two-photon states can only reconstruct the Schmidt spectrum if the Schmidt basis is known a priori, and often suffer from low efficiency or long data collection times.

Main result. The authors developed a technique to measure the complete set of spatial Schmidt modes without prior knowledge, achieving measurement of states with over 3000 Schmidt modes with up to 98% fidelity.

Method. The method uses a Sagnac-type interferometer with a 4f lens configuration to measure the cross-spectral density function (CSDF) of a single photon, which is then numerically diagonalized to extract the Schmidt modes and spectrum.

Model / system. The system consists of high-dimensional entangled two-photon states generated via type-I, collinear, degenerate Spontaneous Parampectrum Down-Conversion (SPDC) using a UV laser and a BBO crystal.

Key observables. Detection probability, cross-spectral density function (CSDF), degree of spatial coherence, Schmidt spectrum (eigenvalues), and Schmidt modes (eigenfunctions).

Important parameters / regimes. Pump beam waist (507 um), crystal thickness (1 mm), magnification factor (m=2 and 1/2), and Schmidt number (approx. 3413).

Assumptions / limitations. The signal photon field is assumed to be quasi-homogeneous, meaning the degree of coherence depends only on the difference in coordinates.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the conceptual measurement process; Figure 2 shows the experimental setup; Figure 3 displays recorded interferograms and reconstructed coherence/CSDF; Figure 4 shows the reconstructed Schmidt spectrum; Figure 5 displays reconstructed spatial Schmidt modes up to order 50.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of basis-dependent measurement, describes the experimental setup and interferometer-based reconstruction method, presents the results for high-dimensional mode measurement and fidelity, and discusses limitations related to camera resolution.

Abstract

Spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC) is the most widely-used source of high-dimensional entangled two-photon states, and the entanglement in the spatial degree of freedom is considered best suited for harnessing high-dimensional advantages. Although the Schmidt basis provides a natural choice for state characterisation of entangled two-photon states in any degree of freedom, there is currently no technique that can measure the Schmidt basis of an entangled two-photon field. The existing techniques can only reconstruct the Schmidt spectrum when the Schmidt basis is known a priori. In contrast, we present a technique that measures the complete set of spatial Schmidt modes without any prior knowledge. Using this technique, we report measurement of states with over 3000 Schmidt modes -- highest reported yet -- with up to 98$\%$ fidelity. We expect our work to significantly advance the harnessing of high-dimensional advantages in SPDC-based systems.

Summary. The paper presents a method to engineer exact many-body scars in a quantum cellular automaton by decorating the Rule-54 rule with local projectors. It proves that this construction creates a system where a sparse, non-thermal sector of solitons coexists with a thermalizing background, providing a platform for studying weak ergodicity breaking and digital quantum simulation.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and quantum computing as it provides a blueprint for digital quantum simulations of non-ergodic dynamics and explores the controlled coexistence of thermal and non-thermal phases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the engineering of exact quantum many-body scars (QMBS) within a quantum cellular automaton (QCA) framework, specifically addressing how non-thermal eigenstates can coexist with a thermalizing many-body spectrum.

Main result. The authors demonstrate a method to engineer exact Floquet scars using the intrinsic soliton structure of the Rule-54 QCA, where the number of scars grows with Fibonacci combinatorics while their fraction in the Hilbert space vanishes exponentially.

Method. The study uses analytical construction via orbit-Fourier modes, transfer matrix methods for combinatorics, and exact diagonalization (ED) for numerical verification of entanglement and spectral statistics.

Model / system. A decorated Rule-54 quantum cellular automaton consisting of a 1D chain of qubits with periodic boundary conditions, where local 'decorations' are added to the Rule-54 dynamics to create a thermalizing background while leaving a protected soliton sector invariant.

Key observables. Entanglement entropy (von Neumann and Renyi), fidelity decay, return probability, spectral statistics (CUE), and protected-sector weight.

Important parameters / regimes. System size N (or L), Fibonacci-scaled dimension of the scar sector, and the scaling of the scar fraction relative to the total Hilbert space.

Assumptions / limitations. The construction assumes exact projector-controlled decorations that are identity operators on the protected trajectory; generic perturbations may induce leakage from the scar sector.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the architecture of bare and decorated Rule-54 QCA; Figure 2 illustrates the scaling of scars and entanglement; Figure 3 displays numerical signatures like entanglement growth and fidelity for vacuum and soliton states; Figure 5 details the digital circuit implementation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of engineering scars, defines the Rule-54 QCA and the decoration method, provides analytical proofs for the existence and entanglement bounds of the scars, presents numerical simulations of the dual dynamics, and concludes with a digital circuit implementation strategy.

Abstract

Quantum many-body scars provide a controlled form of weak ergodicity breaking, in which structured nonthermal eigenstates coexist with a thermalizing many-body spectrum. We introduce a qubit-level route to exact scars based on the intrinsic soliton structure of the Rule-54 quantum cellular automaton. A hard-core dimer sector of Rule 54 supplies an exactly translatable protected skeleton, while local projector-controlled decorations are invisible on this skeleton and nontrivial outside it. The protected dynamics is therefore reducible to finite translation orbits, whose Fourier modes form exact Floquet eigenstates with sub-volume-law entanglement. The number of exact scars grows with Fibonacci combinatorics, whereas their fraction in the full qubit Hilbert space remains exponentially small. Finite-size simulations show Page-like eigenstate entanglement, rapid entanglement growth, fidelity decay, and circular unitary ensemble quasienergy statistics in the decorated complement. This construction demonstrates that exact many-body scars can be engineered from native finite-orbit structures of an interacting reversible automaton, and provides a direct starting point for digital quantum simulation of scarred cellular-automaton dynamics.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that a 1D Bose-Hubbard chain with dipole conservation undergoes weak Hilbert-space fragmentation. It shows that despite the presence of many small disconnected sectors, a large dominant sector allows for a transition from a nonergodic regime to a thermalizing, chaotic regime as interaction strength decreases.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a minimal platform to study the interplay between kinetic constraints (dipole conservation), fragmentation, and the breakdown of ergodicity, which is central to understanding many-body dynamics and thermalization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how Hilbert-space fragmentation, caused by dipole conservation, affects the transition between thermalizing (ETH) and nonergodic regimes in a 1D Bose-Hubbard chain.

Main result. The system exhibits weak Hilbert-space fragmentation where a single dominant Krylov sector coexists with many small ones, and this weak fragmentation does not prevent the emergence of quantum chaos or thermalization at large J/U.

Method. The author uses Exact Diagonalization (ED), graph theory to identify Krylov sectors, and dynamic programming to count frozen states, alongside statistical analyses of level spacing and entanglement entropy.

Model / system. A one-dimensional dipole-conserving Bose-Hublamd chain with open boundary conditions, featuring a three-site hopping term that conserves both total particle number and total dipole moment.

Key observables. Half-chain entanglement entropy, level spacing ratio statistics (r-parameter), density relaxation dynamics (autocorrelation function), and density of states (DOS).

Important parameters / regimes. The ratio of hopping amplitude to on-site interaction strength (J/U), system size (L), and the particle number/dipole moment sector (N=2L, P=0).

Assumptions / limitations. The transition between thermal and nonergodic regimes is treated as a crossover/transition that may be difficult to distinguish as a sharp phase transition due to finite-size limitations.

Paper structure. Abstract

We study Hilbert-space fragmentation and thermalization in a one-dimensional dipole-conserving Bose-Hubbard chain. By analyzing the structure of the Hamiltonian matrix in the Fock basis, we show that the system exhibits weak Hilbert-space fragmentation. We further construct an exponentially large family of frozen product states and derive analytical upper and lower bounds on their number. Using exact diagonalization, we examine the consequences of weak fragmentation for eigenstate half-chain entanglement, density relaxation dynamics, and level statistics. All these quantities reveal a transition from a weak eigenstate thermalization regime to a nonergodic regime with increasing on-site interaction strength. These results show that weak Hilbert-space fragmentation \textit{does not} preclude quantum chaos or thermalization, and provides a minimal platform for studying the interplay of dipole conservation, weak fragmentation, and ergodicity breaking.

Summary. This paper introduces a graph-theoretic framework to detect Hilbert space fragmentation and nearly fragmented regimes in quantum many-body systems. By treating the Hamiltonian as an adjacency matrix, the authors use spectral graph theory to identify disconnected or weakly coupled sectors. The method is shown to be physically accurate, as the identified graph communities correspond to real dynamical timescales in the t-J and Hubbard models.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a general, scalable tool to diagnose ergodicity breaking and prethermal-like hierarchical timescales in complex quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an unbiased method to detect Hilbert space fragmentation and 'nearly fragmented' regimes in quantum many-body systems without requiring prior knowledge of specific conservation laws.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that spectral graph theory (using Laplacian and modularity matrices) can reliably identify both exact and approximate fragmentation, capturing the hierarchical dynamical timescales in the t-J and Hubbard models.

Method. The many-body Hilbert space is mapped onto a graph where basis states are vertices and Hamiltonian matrix elements are edges, then analyzed using the Laplacian spectrum, Fiedler vectors, and modularity maximization.

Model / system. The study focuses on the one-dimensional t-J model (including t-Jz and t-models) and the Hubbard chain, specifically examining regimes near half-filling with varying hole concentrations.

Key observables. Laplacian and modularity eigenvalues, Fiedler vectors, modularity (Q), and the subspace-projected Loschmidt echo (D(tau)).

Important parameters / regimes. Spin exchange strength (J/t), number of holes (Nh), system size (L), and the number of rungs in ladder geometries.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach assumes the ability to represent the Hamiltonian in a chosen basis to construct the graph and focuses on the ability to compute matrix elements.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Laplacian and modularity spectra for different J strengths, the identification of subspaces via eigenvectors, and the time evolution of the projected Loschmidt echo to verify dynamical consistency.

Paper structure. The paper defines the models (Sec II), introduces graph-theoretic tools (Sec III), applies the framework to t-J-like models (Sec IV-V), extends the analysis to the Hubbard chain (Sec VI), and provides mathematical proofs in the Appendices.

Abstract

Hilbert-space fragmentation provides a mechanism for ergodicity breaking in quantum many-body systems even in the absence of disorder, leading to dynamically disconnected sectors and strong memory of initial conditions. However, identifying such structures is often challenging and typically relies on prior knowledge of conservation laws or model-specific analytical insight. Here we introduce an unbiased approach based on spectral graph theory and, within this framework, formulate the concept of nearly fragmented systems, in which perturbative processes couple otherwise fragmented sectors while preserving their dynamical imprint. By representing basis states as vertices and Hamiltonian matrix elements as edges, we map the connectivity structure of the many-body Hilbert space onto a graph and analyze it using tools such as the Laplacian spectrum, Fiedler vectors, and modularity. Exact fragmentation corresponds to disconnected graph components, while nearly fragmented systems manifest as weakly connected communities whose structure can still be resolved spectrally. Applying this framework to the one-dimensional $t$-$J$ model and its perturbations, we demonstrate that graph-theoretic diagnostics reliably identify both fragmented and nearly fragmented Hilbert-space structures and capture the hierarchy of dynamical time scales that governs the system's evolution. We further show that the method extends beyond kinetically constrained models by applying it directly to the Hubbard chain, where it reveals the emergence of nearly decoupled subspaces associated with doublon dynamics and spin configurations. Our results establish the spectral graph analysis as a general and scalable tool for diagnosing fragmentation and approximate dynamical constraints in complex quantum many-body systems.

Abstract

Fractional Chern insulators (FCIs) are lattice analogs of fractional quantum Hall systems, where the interplay of strong interactions with a frustrated tunnelling kinetics leads to the emergence of a gapped ground state with long-range entanglement and anyonic excitations. The highly correlated nature of such systems makes their adiabatic preparation challenging already beyond the minimal system size of two particles. Considering Floquet implementations of the bosonic Harper-Hofstadter-Hubbard model of few photons in superconducting circuits, we design protocols for the driven-dissipative stabilization of its FCI ground state at half filling via quantum bath engineering. Dissipation control is achieved through the coupling to driven leaky cavity modes, which realize a tuneable artificial environment having the Floquet-FCI as its approximate fixed point. For systems of two, three and six particles, we show numerically how the flexibility of the control scheme further allows for the detection of fractional quantum Hall signatures in the stabilized steady states, including bulk incompressibility, Hall response and the trapping of fractional charges. Our results provide a concrete pathway to dissipation-assisted preparation of strongly correlated states in quantum simulators.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that a single-particle dark state in a dissipative spin chain induces universal many-body dynamics characterized by logarithmic decay and specific scaling laws. Abstract

Open quantum systems can host dark or subradiant states whose decay is highly suppressed. While these states have been extensively studied in the few-excitation regime, their impact on the many-body dynamics remains largely unexplored. Here, we study a spin chain subject to correlated dissipation on neighboring sites, which admits a single-particle dark state at zero momentum. We show that the single-particle dark state qualitatively alters the many-body dynamics at long times, and identify its distinct universal behavior. While the zero-momentum mode is dark at the single-particle level, it decays slowly as $1/\log t$ as it becomes dressed by other modes through a dissipation-induced nonlinearity. We demonstrate that the momentum distribution takes a universal scaling form in $k\sqrt{t}$, and the total density decays as $1/\sqrt{t}\log t$. Our results further elucidate the origin of the conflicting results in recent works. Finally, we corroborate the analytics with matrix product state simulations and show that the same universal behavior persists for soft-core interactions, underscoring the universality of the emergent dynamics.
