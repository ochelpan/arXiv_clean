Chern classes of Laughlin bundles on the quasihole moduli space

Authors: Florent Dupont, Semyon Klevtsov
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18089v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous topological description of Laughlin quasihole states on arbitrary Riemann surfaces. By computing the Chern character of the quasihole bundle, the authors mathematically link the topological invariants of the bundle to the physical fractional statistics and Aharonov-Bohm phases observed in the fractional quantum Hall effect.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to compute the Chern character and Chern classes of the Laughlin quasihole bundle over the quasihole moduli space on Riemann surfaces of arbitrary genus.

Main result. The authors derive a general formula for the Chern character of the quasihole bundle and show that in the completely filled state, the bundle is compatible with projective flatness, with results matching the predicted Berry phase decomposition.

Method. The study employs the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem, the Abel-Jacobi map, and the Chern-Weil theory, alongside explicit verification in genus 0 and 1 using the Chern connection and theta functions.

Model / system. The system consists of single-layer and multi-layer Laughlin fractional quantum Hall states on Riemann surfaces of genus g, featuring particles and quasiholes with specific vanishing orders (b and c) and interaction matrices (K and C).

Key observables. Chern character, Chern classes, Berry phase (Aharonov-Bohm and fractional statistical contributions), and Hall conductance.

Important parameters / regimes. Genus (g), number of particles (n), number of quasiholes (m), particle-particle vanishing order (b), particle-quasihole vanishing order (c), and magnetic line bundle degree (d).

Assumptions / limitations. The calculation assumes the wave-functions can be analytically continued and that the chosen frame of sections is holomorphic in the quasihole positions.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the geometric construction of the quasihole bundle, proceeds to derive the Chern character using the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem, provides explicit low-genus verifications, and concludes with a generalization to multi-layer systems.

Abstract

We study fractional quantum Hall states with quasihole excitations, on Riemann surfaces of arbitrary genus. For configurations with $m$ quasiholes we construct a vector bundle above the $m$-th symmetric power of the curve so that the fiber at a point $\lbrace w_1,\dots,w_m \rbrace$ corresponds to the state with quasiholes localized at these positions. We determine the Chern character of this bundle via the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem and show that in the completely filled state, i.e. when the number of particles is maximal, the vector bundle is compatible with the condition of projective flatness. Furthermore, we obtain a generalization of this result to the case of multiple layers and multiple quasihole types. In genus zero and one, we construct explicit wave-functions and verify that the curvature of the associated Chern connection reproduces the predicted Chern classes. The Chern classes obtained match, term by term, the predicted decomposition of the Berry phase under quasihole exchange, into an extensive Aharonov--Bohm contribution and a fractional statistical contribution.

Coalescence of Polymer Droplets Moving on a Surface with Stiffness Gradient

Authors: Divyansh Tripathi, Vimal Kishore, Panagiotis E. Theodorakis, Swarn Lata Singh
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18279v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper studies how polymer droplets merge when moving across a surface with varying stiffness. It reveals that the merging process transitions between different physical regimes (capillarity vs. viscoelasticity) and is highly sensitive to the chemical attractions between the droplets and the surface.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the coalescence dynamics of two viscoelastic polymer droplets moving on a soft substrate with a stiffness gradient (durotaxis).

Main result. The study identifies a crossover from a capillarity-dominated to a viscoelasticity-dominated regime in bridge growth and shows that coalescence morphology and mixing depend strongly on inter- and intra-droplet attraction strengths.

Method. Coarse-grained molecular dynamics simulations using the LAMMPS package with a Langevin thermostat and Velocity-Verlet integration.

Model / system. Two viscoelastic polymer droplets (modeable via FENE potential and LJ interactions) moving on a deformable substrate with a stiffness gradient implemented via a harmonic potential.

Key observables. Temporal growth of bridge height (h) and radius (r), power-law exponents (alpha and beta), contact angle, and degree of droplet mixing.

Important parameters / regimes. Substrate stiffness gradient, velocity ratios of droplets, droplet-surface attraction strength, and droplet-droplet/intra-droplet interaction strengths.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is modeled using coarse-grained particles; the effect of droplet bending rigidity is not considered.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup of durotaxis motion; Figure 8 shows the distance between droplet centers of mass; Figure 9 shows time-sequenced snapshots of the 'enveloping' morphology.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of durotactic coalescence, describes the molecular dynamics model and simulation setup, compares stationary vs. moving droplets, analyzes the effect of various interaction parameters, and discusses biological implications.

Abstract

Here, we study the coalescence of two droplets that are moving in the same direction on a soft surface; the motion of the droplets is caused by a gradient in the surface stiffness. As reference, stationary coalescence of the same droplets is also studied on the corresponding uniform surfaces for different stiffness values. To describe the coalescence phenomenon on a surface with stiffness gradient, a relevant range of velocity ratios of the leading and the trailing droplet was considered to elucidate the effect of this parameter on coalescence. Moreover, to analyze the dynamics of the process, the temporal growth of the bridge height $(h)$ was investigated, which follows a power law $(h \sim t^α)$, before eventually attaining a constant value. The obtained values of $α$ show a transition from a higher to a lower value as a function of time, pointing to the presence of two distinct power-law growth regimes, where the transition signifies the crossover from the capillarity-dominated regime to the viscoelasticty-dominated regime of coalescence. In addition, varying attractive strengths for droplet--droplet and intra-droplet interactions were considered. The results indicate that both the dynamics and the degree of the coalescence strongly depend on these interaction parameters. Thus, we anticipate that our results will shed more light on the durotaxis-driven coalescence of polymeric droplets for various relevant system parameters, which will have practical implications for applications ranging from microfluidics to ink-jet printing, where substrate properties may vary. In addition, results may add to the fundamental understanding of the interactions among multicellular aggregates moving on biological surfaces.

Confinement-controlled pattern selection in a finite population-imbalanced dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate

Authors: Zhenhao Wang, Weijing Bao, Jia-Rui Luo, Gentaro Watanabe, Kui-Tian Xi
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18139v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how axial confinement and population imbalance control the formation of complex density patterns in a two-component dipolar BEC. It demonstrates that these quantum patterns mimic microphase-separated structures in polymers and that their characteristic length scales are tunable via the confinement geometry.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a controllable quantum fluid platform to study microphase separation and pattern formation, drawing a direct structural correspondence between quantum gases and soft-matter systems like diblock copolymers.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating ground-state density pattern selection and morphological transitions in a population-imbalanced, two-component dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate confined in a circular quasi-2D box.

Main result. The study identifies a sequence of morphologies (pancake, droplet arrays, concentric rings) where the population ratio acts as an effective volume fraction, and discovers that the pattern spacing scales linearly with the axial confinement length.

Method. A mean-field approach using coupled Gross-Pita_Pitaevskii equations (GPEs) minimized via the preconditioned conjugate-gradient method and evaluated using Fast Fourier Transforms.

Model / system. A binary (two-component) dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate of 52Cr atoms in a quasi-2D circular box with tight axial harmonic confinement.

Key observables. 2D column density profiles, density contrast (C2), structure factors (radial and 2D Fourier power spectrum), and characteristic wave vectors.

Important parameters / regimes. Population ratio (N2/N), axial confinement strength (omega_z), interaction asymmetry (a22/a11), and box radius (R0).

Assumptions / limitations. Zero temperature, mean-field approximation (neglecting Lee-Huang-Yang fluctuations), and rigid/hard-wall-like boundary conditions for the box potential.

Figures summary. Figures show real-space density patterns for different phases, density contrast (C2) as a function of population ratio and axial confinement, and scaling analysis of pattern spacing.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the system and its analogy to block copolymers, describes the mean-field model and numerical methods, presents phase diagrams and morphological transitions, analyzes scaling laws and geometric frustration, and concludes with implications for quantum fluid pattern selection.

Abstract

We study the ground-state density patterns of a population-imbalanced two-component dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate confined in a circular quasi-two-dimensional box. Using a mean-field model, we map out phase diagrams as functions of the axial confinement, interaction imbalance, and population ratio. The system supports a rich sequence of stationary morphologies, including a nearly uniform pancake state, pancake-droplet and ring-droplet coexistence states, droplet arrays, and concentric rings. These patterns show a close structural correspondence to microphase-separated morphologies in diblock-copolymer systems, with the population imbalance acting as an effective volume fraction that selects the pattern topology. Analysis of the density profiles and structure factors reveals that the modulated states possess an intrinsic nonzero characteristic wave vector, which remains essentially unchanged when the box size is varied. We also find that the characteristic pattern spacing scales linearly with the axial confinement length, indicating that the transverse thickness of the condensate controls the effective in-plane length scale. In a finite circular box, this smooth scaling is interrupted by discrete steps, reflecting geometric frustration and the integer locking of the number of rings or droplets. Our results show that box-trapped dipolar mixtures provide a controllable platform for studying finite-size pattern selection and nonlocal microphase formation in quantum fluids.

Correlation-Driven Spin Reorientation via Competing Anisotropy Channels in CrPS4

Authors: Raju Baral, Harald O. Jeschke, Igor I. Mazin, Jue Liu, David Mandrus, Stuart Calder
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16541v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the spin reorientation in CrPS4 is caused by the temperature-dependent decay of magnetic correlations, which affects exchange anisotropy differently than single-ion anisotropy. By combining neutron scattering with ab initio calculations, the authors provide a quantitative microscopic description of how low-dimensional correlations control magnetic anisotropy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Identifying the mechanism behind the temperature-induced spin reorientation transition (SRT) in the quasi-1D van der Waals antiferromagnet CrPS4.

Main result. The SRT is driven by a differential renormalization where decaying intersite correlations suppress exchange-anisotropy channels while single-ion anisotropy remains local, causing the effective easy axis to rotate.

Method. The study combines magnetic pair distribution function (mPDF) analysis of neutron total scattering with DFT+U calculations for energy mapping and classical Monte Carlo simulations.

Model / system. The system is CrPS4, a quasi-1D van der Waals antiferromagnetic semiconductor with A-type magnetic order. The model uses a microscopic spin Hamiltonian including isotropic exchange, exchange-anisotropy, and single-ion anisotropy terms.

Key observables. Magnetic pair distribution function (mPDF), local magnetic order parameter (m), spin polar angle (theta), correlation length (xi), and Cr-Cr bond distances (d1, d2).

Important parameters / regimes. Neeel temperature (TN = 37 K), Hubbard U (2 eV), exchange couplings (J1, J2), and spin-spin correlation functions.

Assumptions / limitations. The model uses a dominant-chain approximation focusing on n=1 and n=2 bonds and neglects Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya interactions due to inversion symmetry.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the system; Figure 3 compares the correlation-renormalization factor and predicted spin angles against experimental data, and shows structural anomalies in Cr-Cr bond distances at TN.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the spin reorientation problem, describes the mPDF and DFT+U methodology, presents the results of local correlation measurements, develops the anisotropy renormalization model, and discusses the connection to structural anomalies.

Abstract

We identify a correlation-driven mechanism for the temperature-induced spin reorientation in the quasi-one-dimensional van der Waals antiferromagnet CrPS4. Magnetic pair distribution function (mPDF) analysis resolves the local spin direction and shows that ferromagnetic intrachain correlations persist far above TN. Combining these correlations with a DFT-derived spin Hamiltonian reveals competing single-ion and exchange-anisotropy channels, with single-ion anisotropy remaining local while exchange anisotropy is renormalized as intersite correlations decay. This differential renormalization rotates the effective easy axis and captures the ordered-state canting. Above TN, the continued rotation beyond the model prediction delineates the limits of the dominant-chain approximation. These results establish mPDF-derived correlations as direct inputs to microscopic Hamiltonians and show how low-dimensional correlations can control magnetic anisotropy.

Direct On-Wafer Measurements of Noise Parameters in C- and X-bands at $T=4$ K

Authors: Daniil Frolov, Jean-Olivier Plouchart, Utku Soylu
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17073v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new method for measuring the noise properties of semiconductor transistors directly on a wafer at 4 K. By using a cryogenic preamplifier and a specialized calibration technique, the researchers can measure low-gain devices that would otherwise be hidden by room-temperature noise. This is crucial for developing the readout electronics needed for quantum computing and radio astronomy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of performing accurate, direct on-wafer noise parameter measurements of low-gain transistors at cryogenic temperatures (4 K) because their output noise power is often below the room-temperature instrumentation noise floor.

Main result. The development of a vector-corrected measurement framework that enables simultaneous S-parameter and noise parameter extraction for 14nm FinFETs and LNAs at 4 K, successfully overcoming the room-temperature noise floor using a cryogenic preamplifier.

Method. A combination of the 'source-pull' and 'cold-source' methods using a four-state cryogenic tuner, integrated with a TRL (thru-reflect-line) calibration scheme and a cryogenic preamplification chain.

Model / system. A cryogenic measurement platform consisting of a 4 K cryostat with wafer probes, a four-port VNA, a cryogenic impedance generator (tuner), and a preamplification chain, used to characterize 14nm FinFETs and LNA dies.

Key observables. S-parameters (S11, S21, S12, S22), noise parameters (minimum noise temperature Te, minimum noise factor Fmin, optimal reflection coefficient Gamma_opt, and Lange invariant 4N), and I-V curves.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature of 4 K; frequency range of 5-12 GHz (C- and X-bands); 14nm FinFET technology.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes S21 = S12 reciprocity for the probe; assumes high circulator isolation and good matching to simplify the mismatch coefficient.

Figures summary. Schematics of the measurement stand and electrical signal paths; physical layout of cryogenic hardware; signal flow graphs and calibration planes; and plots of FET/LNA S-parameters, noise temperatures, and optimal impedances.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific challenge, describes the hardware architecture and signal path, details the operational modes (S-parameter and noise parameter), explains the TRL calibration and de-embedding procedure, presents the characterization of the FETs and LNAs, and concludes with validation and future improvements.

Abstract

This paper describes the setup and the results of the direct on-wafer measurements of a FET noise parameters obtained with a source-pull method at temperatures down to T=4K and in the 5-12 GHz frequency range. The setup consists of a cryostat with wafer probes, two reflectometers, a programmable impedance generator, wideband isolators and bias tees and low noise preamplifier, all cooled to cryogenic temperatures, allowing to perform a full vector error-corrected wafer-level measurements of the discrete transistors and amplifier dies. The setup and its calibration procedure are designed in a such way that allows simultaneous calibration, S-parameters, noise parameters and I-V curve measurements of several FETs all in one cooldown. Using the described setup we perform first measurements of 14nm FinFETs and also measure noise parameters of an LNA based on these FETs. Resulting noise temperature values are compared against those obtained using independent and alternative measurement techniques.

Direction-selective triplet pairing and spin-edge locking in altermagnetic metals

Authors: Lie Yuan, Junkang Huang, Yu-Xuan Li, Tao Zhou
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18429v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that altermagnetic spin splitting acts as an intrinsic mechanism to select anisotropic triplet pairing in superconductors. It reveals that this symmetry leads to a unique spin-edge locking effect, where the spin of Majorana boundary states is determined by the crystal edge orientation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how altermagnetic spin splitting selects specific superconducting pairing symmetries and how this anisotropy influences the topological properties of boundary states.

Main result. Altermagnetic spin splitting suppresses singlet pairing and stabilizes anisotropic equal-spin triplet order, leading to spin-edge locking where boundary spin polarization is tied to edge orientation.

Method. The authors use a self-consistent mean-field Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) framework, Ginzburg-Landau theory, and Green's function approach for spectral analysis.

Model / system. A two-dimensional d-wave altermagnetic metal model featuring momentum-dependent spin splitting, Rashba spin-orbit coupling, and short-range attractive interactions.

Key observables. Pairing amplitudes (singlet and triplet), Majorana boundary states, spin-resolved boundary spectral functions, and momentum-space phase winding numbers.

Important parameters / regimes. Altermagnetic exchange splitting (J0), Rashba spin-orbit coupling strength (lambda), hopping parameter (t0), and interaction strength (V).

Assumptions / limitations. The model is a minimal effective two-band projection of a more complex system and assumes short-range attractive interactions on nearest-neighbor bonds.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows order parameter magnitudes and Fermi surfaces; Figure 2/3 illustrates the evolution of Majorana modes from flat to dispersive; Figure 4 displays spin-resolved boundary spectral weights.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the altermagnetic model, performs self-consistent calculations of pairing symmetry, analyzes the topological phase winding, investigates the effect of Rashba coupling on edge states, and concludes with the discovery of spin-edge locking.

Abstract

We investigate self-consistent unconventional superconductivity in a two-dimensional $d$-wave altermagnetic metal. We find that momentum-dependent altermagnetic spin splitting suppresses opposite-spin singlet pairing and stabilizes highly anisotropic equal-spin triplet order. In the spin-conserving limit, this directional triplet pairing gives rise to nearly dispersionless Majorana boundary states associated with effective one-dimensional topological channels. Rashba spin-orbit coupling mixes spin sectors, activates additional pairing components, and drives the system into a mixed-parity superconducting state with dispersive Majorana boundary states. The spin-resolved boundary spectra further reveal a characteristic locking between boundary orientation and spin polarization, reflecting the underlying altermagnetic symmetry. These results identify altermagnetic spin splitting as an intrinsic mechanism for selecting unconventional pairing and generating spin-resolved Majorana boundary states without external magnetic fields.

Disorder effect on the superfluid density and the origin of the pseudogap end point in the cuprate superconductors

Authors: Rong Cheng, Tao Li, Jianhua Yang
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17227v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper investigates how disorder affects the superfluid density in cuprate superconductors using a variational RVB approach to the t-J model. It demonstrates that strong correlations protect superconductivity from disorder in the underdoped regime, providing evidence for a Mott transition at the pseudogap end point. This explains the non-monotonic doping dependence of the superfluid density.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the origin of the pseudogap end point in cuprate superconductors and explaining why the superfluid density reaches a maximum at this critical doping despite a monotonic increase in Drude weight.

Main result. The study finds that d-wave superconductivity in the underdoped regime is remarkably robust against disorder due to intrinsic electron incoherence from strong correlations, whereas the overdoped regime is fragile, supporting a Mott transition at the pseudogap end point.

Method. A two-step variational theory using an unrestricted optimization of a Resonating Valence Bond (RVB) ground state and a construction of a variational state for the paramagnetic current response.

Model / system. The 2D disordered t-J model, which incorporates strong electron correlations and a no-double-occupancy constraint, compared against a BCS mean-field model.

Key observables. Zero-temperature superfluid density (rho_s(0)), Off-Diagonal Long-Range Order (ODLRO), total optical weight (K), and the ratio of superfluid density to optical weight (r).

Important parameters / regimes. Doping concentration (x), disorder strength (V/t), Heisenberg exchange coupling (J/t), and next-nearest-neighbor hopping (t'/t).

Assumptions / limitations. The variational approach is semi-quantitative; the ground state is assumed to be superconducting; the BCS comparison ignores the no-double-occupancy constraint.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows ODLRO evolution with disorder; Figure 2 maps ODLRO across doping and disorder; Figure 3 shows superfluid density evolution; Figure 6 compares normalized superfluid density between RVB and BCS theories.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of the pseudogap end point, defines the disordered t-J model and the RVB variational framework, details the numerical optimization and current response calculation, compares results with BCS theory, and concludes with implications for the Mott transition.

Abstract

A major puzzle in the study of the cuprate superconductivity is the origin of the pseudogap end point. Intriguingly, such a critical doping is also where the superfluid density of the system reaches its maximum. A non-monotonic doping dependence of the superfluid density is rather unusual since the Drude weight of the cuprate system is found to increase monotonically with the doping concentration. It is generally believed that such a peculiar behavior should be attributed to both the strongly correlated nature of the cuprate system and the disorder effect. In this work, we develop a variational theory for the zero temperature superfluid density of the disordered $t-J$ model. This is achieved in two steps. First, we perform an unrestricted variational optimization of an RVB variational ground state for the disordered $t-J$ model. Second, we construct the variational state that describes the paramagnetic current response on such an RVB state. The zero temperature superfluid density $ρ_{s}(0)$ is then extracted from the curvature of the variational ground state energy of the system as a function of the external electromagnetic field. We find that $ρ_{s}(0)$ computed in this way is remarkably robust against the disorder effect. More specifically, we find that $ρ_{s}(0)$ is a monotonically increasing function of doping concentration $x$ and scales linearly with the total optical weight. This is consistent with the observation in the underdoped cuprates but is strongly at odd with the behavior in the overdoped cuprates. The strong contrast between the disorder effect in the underdoped and the overdoped regime lends strong support to our previous proposal that there exist a Mott transition between a doped-Mott-insulating metal in the underdoped regime and a fermi-liquid-like metal in the overdoped regime around the pseudogap end point.

Emergent supersymmetry in a time-space inverted quantum mechanics

Authors: Marcus W Beims, Arlans JS de Lara
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17507v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper shows that supersymmetry is an inherent feature of a quantum mechanical framework where space and time roles are inverted. By factorizing a specific 'Momentunian' operator, the authors derive a new class of SUSY that naturally incorporates memory effects through fractional calculus. This suggests that SUSY can be a structural consequence of space-time symmetry rather than an added assumption.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether supersymmetry (SUSY) can emerge as an intrinsic structural property of a space-time symmetric framework rather than being imposed phenomenologically.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that supersymmetry emerges naturally from the factorization of the 'Momentunian' operator within the Time-Space Inverted (TSI) quantum mechanics framework, leading to momentum doublets and memory effects via fractional derivatives.

Method. The study employs Dirac's factorization procedure on the Momentunian operator, uses separation of variables for wavefunctions, and applies fractional calculus (Caputo sense) to handle 1/2-fractional time derivatives.

Model / system. The framework is Time-Space Inverted (TSI) quantum mechanics, which treats time as an operator and position as a parameter, encompassing both non-relativistic and relativistic (Dirac-like) regimes.

Key observables. Momentum doublets (p and -p), zero-mode states (zero energy or zero momentum), and the Witten index.

Important parameters / regimes. Kinetic energy of time (Kt), rest mass (m0), speed of light (c), and the 1/2-fractional time derivative.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes a space-time symmetric (STS) structure where the roles of space and time are inverted.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the TSI QM framework, derives the Momentunian operator, demonstrates the factorization leading to emergent SUSY, explores both non-relativistic and relativistic cases, and provides a case study using the Harmonic Oscillator.

Abstract

This Letter shows that a supersymmetric structure is inherent to the time space inverted (TSI) quantum mechanics (QM) framework, where the spatial evolution of states is generated by the operator $\hat{\mathcal{P}}^{\pm}(\hat{\mathcal{H}},\hat t;q)=\pm\sqrt{2m[\hat{\mathcal{H}}-\mathcal{\hat V}(q)]}$ [\href{https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevA.95.032133}{Phys. Rev. A. {\bf 95}, 032133 (2017)}], named here Momentunian, whose square-root structure that can be factorized. Such factorization leads directly to a supersymmetric algebra with supercharges and partner Hamiltonians. For the relativistic Momentunian the zero mode states are shown to be evanescent states, \textit{independent} of the physical potential. Furthermore, the existence of non-relativistic and relativistic Momentunian \textit{partners} is demonstrated, whose zero-mode states are no longer necessarily zero energies, but vanishing momenta states. The natural emergence of the $1/2$-fractional time derivatives in the TSI QM, leads to supercharges which incorporate memory effects into the supersymmetric wave functions. Results indicate that supersymmetry emerges as a structural property of the TSI QM rather than being imposed phenomenologically.

End-to-End Formalization of Quantum Error Correction

Authors: Mattias Ehatamm, Yi Lee, Xiaodi Wu, Runzhou Tao
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16523v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces Lean-QEC, a formal verification framework in Lean 4 that automates the proof of distance bounds for large-scale quantum error-correcting codes. By bridging high-level quantum theory with efficient SAT-based computation, it enables machine-checked certificates for codes that were previously too large for manual or formal verification. This provides a critical layer of trust for the development of fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Certifying the distance of large-scale quantum error-correcting codes (QECCs) is NP-hard, creating a 'trust gap' between unverified symbolic solvers and unscalable manual proofs.

Main result. The authors developed Lean-QEC, a Lean 4 library that provides end-to-end, machine-checked distance certificates for industrial-scale stabilizer codes, including Bivariate Bicycle codes up to 144 qubits.

Method. The framework uses a verified reduction to translate the distance condition into a Boolean satisfiability (SAT) problem, utilizing BitVec-flattened encoding and error-location encoding to ensure scalability within the Lean kernel.

Model / system. The paper focuses on stabilizer codes, specifically CSS and Bivariate Bicycle (BB) families, modeled using the Pauli group and binary symplectic representation.

Key observables. Code distance (d), Pauli weight, and stabilizer generators.

Important parameters / regimes. Scalability up to 144 qubits; specific verified codes include [[90, 8, 10]] and [[70, 6, 9]] BB codes.

Assumptions / limitations. The binary symplectic representation assumes phase information loss is irrelevant for distance arguments; the solver (CaDiCaL) is not trusted, but its output is verified via LRAT certificates.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a workflow diagram of the quantum verification hierarchy; Figure 2 maps the formalized QEC stack; Figure 3 illustrates the equivalence of tensor products and unfolded Pauli operators.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the trust gap in QEC, presents the Lean-QEC library architecture, details the mathematical formalization of the Pauli group and stabilizer codes, describes the SAT-based reduction pipeline and optimization techniques, and validates the framework with case studies ranging from the Shor code to large-scale BB codes.

Abstract

Quantum error-correcting codes (QECCs) sit between noisy quantum hardware and reliable computation, so the code parameters used in practice must be trustworthy. The single number that summarizes a code's strength is its distance, yet certifying a distance lower bound is NP-hard in general, placing it beyond the reach of pen-and-paper proofs as well as direct proof-assistant scripting. As a result, distance values in the literature come either from non-scaling hand proofs, or from unverified solvers that leave a trust gap exactly where the code is supposed to provide a guarantee. We present Lean-QEC, the first Lean 4 formalization of stabilizer-code theory that delivers end-to-end, machine-checked distance certificates at industrial code sizes. Lean-QEC formalizes the linear algebra of qubit states, the Pauli group, stabilizer codes, the binary symplectic representation, classical coding theory, and the CSS and Bivariate Bicycle families. To break the combinatorial barrier, Lean-QEC translates the distance condition into a Boolean satisfiability formula through a verified reduction. The pipeline scales through a BitVec-flattened encoding that replaces Lean's Matrix representation, and an error-location encoding that reduces the variable count from $n$ to $k\lceil \log_2 n\rceil$. With these, we obtain automatically-generated Lean-checked distance proofs for a large range of industrially viable qLDPC codes within the Bivariate Bicycle and Generalized Bicycle families, including [[90, 8, 10]] and [[70, 6, 9]] BB codes, with the formulation scaling up to 144 qubits when performed outside the Lean kernel. The resulting library is reusable and is designed to plug into broader Lean-based efforts toward end-to-end verification of fault-tolerant quantum computation.

Enhanced $s^\pm$-wave superconductivity in electron-doped La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$

Authors: Xun Liu, Chao Deng, Wenfeng Wu, Liang Si, Mi Jiang
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17520v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper provides the first theoretical prediction that electron doping enhances superconductivity in Ruddlesden-Popper nickelates. It identifies a cooperative mechanism between d_{x2-y2} and d_{z2} orbitals and suggests that heterostructures could achieve higher transition temperatures. This work offers a new direction for experimentalists seeking to control superconductivity in nickelate-based materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the effect of electron doping on the $s^\pm$-wave superconducting pairing in Ruddlesden-Popper nickelates, specifically addressing the lack of research on the electron-doped regime compared to the hole-doped regime.

By treating primes as states in a dynamical system, the author shows that the logarithmic integral function emerges as a solution to the governing evolution equation. This provides a new way to link number theory to the statistical mechanics of complex, correlated systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to understand the underlying mechanisms governing the irregularities in the sequence of prime numbers and determine if their distribution exhibits a deterministic structure linked to physical systems.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that prime gaps follow an iterative map and that the fluctuations around this trend are governed by a well-defined cohomological structure, with the logarithmic integral function serving as a solution.

Method. The study uses statistical analysis of tau-gaps, residual analysis (subtractive and relative), and fitting prime distributions to Gamma distributions, alongside asymptotic analysis of a proposed cohomological equation.

Model / system. The prime number sequence is modeled as a dynamical system exhibiting long-range correlations, where primes are treated as states of a system governed by a non-stationary, parametrically driven process and a cohomological equation.

Key observables. tau-gaps (d_tau), relative residuals (r), subtractive residuals (s), and the error term (epsilon).

Important parameters / regimes. gap distance (tau), prime index (n), shape parameter (alpha), and the decay rate (lambda).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the prime sequence can be treated as a system with a smooth backbone and that the asymptotic behavior of the error term can be modeled via the Mean Value Theorem and Stirling's formula.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of prime irregularities, presents a model using an iterative map and cohomological equation, performs statistical validation using gap analysis and residual distributions, and concludes by connecting the framework to the logarithmic integral function.

Abstract

Prime numbers appeared in contexts spanning statistical mechanics, quantum mechanics and dynamical systems. However, the mechanisms governing the irregularities observed in their sequence and linking them to physical systems remained unclear. Here, it is shown that prime gaps at different separation distances follow a function depending on that distance and can be described by an iterative map which predicts the primary growth of successive primes. On the other hand, the analysis of remaining fluctuations reveals the existence of a well-defined cohomological structure, where the deterministic functional relation holds for primes up to small decaying fluctuations. In consequence, the long-range correlations as well as local jumps in primes encode the underlying cohomological structure where prime numbers are states of a given system that becomes deterministic asymptotically. Remarkably, the solution to this cohomological equation turns out to be the logarithmic integral function.
