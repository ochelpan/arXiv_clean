Demonstration of transport in an ion trap design for two-dimensional lattices

Authors: Michael Pfeifer, Marco Valentini, Matthias Dietl, Fabian Anmasser, Simon Schey, Jakob Wahl, Philip C. Holz, Clemens Rössler, Yves Colombe, Philipp Schindler
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16543v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · analog quantum simulation 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper presents a new ion trap chip design that allows for the tuning of radial coupling in 2D ion lattices using only DC voltages. By optimizing electrode geometry, the authors successfully demonstrated reliable ion transport through transition zones with low heating rates. This approach provides a scalable path toward large-scale quantum computing using complex 2D architectures.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a scalable hardware solution for 2D ion trap architectures, which is crucial for implementing large-scale quantum simulations and many-body dynamics in multidimensional ion crystals.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of controlling radial separation between ion chains in Quantum Spring Array (QSA) architectures without using RF voltage modulation, which causes high heating rates.

Main result. Demonstration of a new ion trap design that enables radial transport and tuning of ion separation using only DC voltages, significantly reducing pseudo-potential barriers and heating.

Method. The researchers used an optimization strategy (Nelder-Mead solver) to design curved RF electrodes and transition zones, then fabricated a prototype on a fused silica substrate and characterized it using 40Ca+ ions.

Model / system. A microfabricated surface ion trap chip featuring a Quantum Spring Array (QSA) architecture, where ion chains are arranged in a 2D lattice and interact via the Coulomb force.

Key observables. Radial transport fidelity, ion heating rates, stray electric fields, micromotion modulation index, and radial secular frequencies.

Important parameters / regimes. RF drive frequency of 31.91 MHz, RF amplitude of 194.5 V, ion-surface separation of ~90 micrometers, and heating rates of 18 +/- 3 ph/s.

Assumptions / limitations. The design uses the gapless plane approximation, neglecting the physical gaps between RF and DC electrodes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the trap geometry and coupling modes; Figure 2 compares linear vs. optimized electrode transition zones; Figure 3 illustrates the reduction in pseudo-potential barriers and frequency variation; Figure 8 and 9 show microscopy of the multi-layer trap and its vias.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QSA architecture and the limitations of RF-based tuning, presents the optimized curved electrode design and its simulation, demonstrates experimental transport and characterization of a single-layer prototype, and concludes with a scaling path using a multi-layer fabrication approach.

Abstract

Microfabricated ion trap chips are at the core of some of the most advanced quantum computers. How a large number of ions is arranged and controlled on an ion trap chip depends on the chosen trap architecture. One such architecture is the quantum spring array (QSA). In the QSA architecture, ion chains are arranged in a two-dimensional lattice and interact with ion chains in neighboring sites in the radial and axial directions of the respective chain. This interaction, or coupling, is mediated by the Coulomb force while keeping ions in separate trapping sites, and scales inversely with the third power of the separation. The capability to control the distance between ions in the lattice is thus essential. In previous works, the radial separation between ions was tuned by controlling the rf pseudo-potential, which revealed to be experimentally challenging to realize while maintaining low heating rates. In this work, we present an ion trap chip design that allows tuning of the radial distance between ions using only dc voltages. The radial transport is executed between different interaction zones, designated for quantum operations, through specifically designed transition zones. A prototype of this type of ion trap chip was microfabricated on fused silica substrate. Its functionality is characterized by demonstrating dc-controlled radial transport of a single ion through a transition zone and measuring stray fields and ion heating rates in the center of the trap. Moreover, the fabrication of a multi-metal layer version of such a trap is presented as a scaling path for the presented chip design.

Closed-form Bayesian quantum estimation of Gaussian states

Authors: Edward Gandar, Jesús Rubio
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16978v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a closed-form method for Bayesian quantum estimation of Gaussian states by approximating the optimal operator within a finite-dimensional polynomial subspace. It transforms a complex, infinite-dimensional optimization problem into a tractable linear algebra problem. The framework allows for the design of near-optimal, experimentally feasible measurement strategies for tasks like displacement and squeezing estimation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical, analytical tool for optimizing continuous-variable quantum metrology, bridging the gap between complex numerical Bayesian methods and experimentally implementable Gaussian operations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The computational intractability of Bayesian quantum estimation for continuous-variable Gaussian states due to infinite-dimensional optimization and complex parameter integrals.

Main result. A variational framework that reduces the optimization of measurements and estimators to a finite-dimensional linear problem with closed-form solutions using polynomial subspaces.

Method. The authors use a variational approach restricting operators to a polynomial subspace of canonical quadratures, transforming the Mean Square Loss minimization into a solvable linear system involving a Gram matrix.

Model / system. Continuous-variable (CV) quantum systems consisting of N bosonic modes, specifically focusing on Gaussian states characterized by mean vectors and covariance matrices.

Key observables. Canonical quadratures (q and p), Symmetric Posterior Mean (SPM) operator, and Weyl symbols.

Important parameters / regimes. Displacement parameter, squeezing parameter, prior variance (Gaussian and uniform), and thermal photon number.

Assumptions / limitations. The input states are Gaussian; the optimization is restricted to a polynomial subspace; the Gaussian state is assumed to be faithful.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares relative Mean Squared Loss for different polynomial estimators (linear, cubic) and the posterior mean in displacement estimation. Figure 2 shows the convergence of constrained and posterior mean estimators toward the global minimum for squeezing estimation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational bottleneck in Bayesian QET, proposes a variational framework using polynomial subspaces, provides a geometric interpretation of the solution as an orthogonal projection, derives optimality conditions, and validates the method with single-shot examples of displacement and squeezing estimation.

Abstract

Bayesian quantum estimation provides a robust framework for quantum technologies, especially in scenarios with limited data and minimal prior information. Yet, its application to continuous-variable Gaussian systems has remained limited and largely numerical due to the complexity of the underlying parameter integrals. Here, we introduce a variational framework reducing the optimisation over measurements and estimators to a finite-dimensional linear problem and admitting closed-form solutions. This is achieved by restricting the analysis to operators polynomial in the canonical quadratures, leading to solutions with a geometric interpretation as orthogonal projections of the global optimum. We further derive a necessary and sufficient condition for global optimality. Through single-shot examples, we show that the framework yields experimentally feasible strategies based on Gaussian operations and quadrature measurements that are either optimal or near-optimal, and that replacing the induced estimator with the posterior mean further improves performance towards the global optimum.

Exact Entanglement-Depth Speed Frontier for Complete Quantum Charging

Authors: Wenlong Sun, Gang Lu, Yuanfeng Jin
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16935v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proves that the speed of charging a quantum battery is fundamentally limited by the amount of multipartite entanglement present in the system. It establishes an exact 'staircase' relationship where faster charging rates directly certify higher levels of entanglement depth, including genuine N-partite entanglement.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a model-independent, orbit-level diagnostic for quantifying many-body entanglement resources based solely on the observed speed of a dynamical process.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the fundamental relationship between the speed of a complete quantum charging process and the amount of multipartite entanglement depth required to achieve that speed.

Main result. The authors establish an exact integer staircase frontier relating the QSL-normalized rate to entanglement depth, proving that crossing the threshold eta > 1/sqrt(2) certifies genuine N-partite entanglement.

Method. The study uses Quantum Speed Limit (QSL) geometry, specifically the Mandelstam-Tamm bound, combined with Minkowski's integral inequality and a Baire category argument to relate path speed to block orthogonalization.

Model / system. A closed N-qubit quantum battery undergoing unitary evolution from an all-down state to an all-up state under a time-independent Hamiltonian.

Key observables. QSL-normalized rate (eta), entanglement depth (Ent[U]), and the number of independent blocks (m).

Important parameters / regimes. N-qubit system size, entanglement depth constraint (k), and the threshold eta = 1/sqrt(2) for N-partite entanglement.

Assumptions / limitations. The results apply to finite-dimensional pure-state complete charging under a closed, time-independent Hamiltonian with exact endpoint conditions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 displays the exact integer staircase frontier for N=20, plotting the charging rate against the lower bound on entanglement depth.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of quantum charging speed, defines the QSL-normalized rate, provides the mathematical derivation of the speed-depth frontier using block orthogonalization, constructs a saturated model to prove sharpness, and concludes with entanglement certification thresholds.

Abstract

Complete quantum charging provides a sharp setting in which to ask how much multipartite entanglement is forced by speed itself. For a closed \(N\)-qubit battery evolving from \(\ket{\downarrow}^{\otimes N}\) to \(\ket{\uparrow}^{\otimes N}\) under a time-independent Hamiltonian, we exactly solve the pure-state depth-constrained speed problem. If the realized trajectory has entanglement depth at most \(k\), then the largest possible QSL-normalized rate \(η=τ_{\rm QSL}/T\) is \(η_{\max}(k)=\lceil N/k\rceil^{-1/2}\). Conversely, an observed rate \(η\) certifies trajectory entanglement depth at least \(\bigl\lceil N/\lfloor η^{-2}\rfloor\bigr\rceil\). The mechanism is block orthogonalization: under a fixed product partition, complete charging forces all blocks to orthogonalize simultaneously, and the quantum speed limit converts this counting constraint into the speed bound. Balanced cluster-flip evolutions saturate the bound, establishing an exact integer staircase frontier. Thus fast complete charging cannot be explained by many small independently charging blocks; in particular, crossing the threshold \(η>1/\sqrt2\) certifies, for \(N>1\), the generation of genuine \(N\)-partite entanglement.

Krylov complexity and fidelity susceptibility in two-band Hamiltonians

Authors: Rishav Chaudhuri, Ayush Raj, Soham Ray, Sai Satyam Samal
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18594v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the derivative of Krylov spread complexity can detect any gap-closing event in two-band models, not just topological transitions. By establishing a mathematical bound related to fidelity susceptibility, the authors show that complexity captures the non-analyticity of the system's energy spectrum. They also uncover a universal scaling ratio and a duality between topological and trivial phases in the SSH model.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a new geometric tool to detect criticality and explores the fundamental relationship between quantum complexity and the sensitivity of the ground state to parameter changes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether Krylov spread complexity can serve as a diagnostic for phase transitions and gap-closing events in two-band Hamiltonians.

Main result. The authors prove that the derivative of Krylov spread complexity is bounded by fidelity susceptibility, meaning it is sensitive to both topological and trivial gap closings, and they identify a universal ratio between these two quantities.

Method. The study uses a geometric formulation based on Bloch sphere data to calculate complexity without constructing a circuit Hamiltonian, alongside analytical integration using elliptic functions.

Model / system. The analysis focuses on two-band Hamiltonians, specifically the Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) model for topological transitions and the massive Dirac Hamiltonian for trivial gap closings.

Key observables. Krylov spread complexity, fidelity susceptibility, winding number (Zak phase), and the ratio between the complexity derivative and the square root of fidelity susceptibility.

Important parameters / regimes. Hopping amplitudes (t1, t2), mass parameter (mu), Bloch sphere angles (theta, phi), and the duality parameter (r).

Assumptions / limitations. The derived inequality assumes a momentum-independent reference state, and the scope is primarily limited to two-band Hamiltonian systems.

Figures summary. Figures show the SSH model lattice structure, plots of Krylov complexity and its derivative exhibiting logarithmic divergence and plateaus, and the symmetry/saturation of the ratio R between complexity and fidelity susceptibility.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of complexity as a phase probe, develops a geometric formulation for two-band models, applies this to the SSH and Dirac models to compare topological vs. trivial transitions, establishes mathematical bounds and duality, and extends the analysis to non-Hermitian systems.

Abstract

We investigate Krylov spread complexity for the ground state of two-band Hamiltonians, where the reference state is a generic state on the Bloch sphere. The spread complexity is obtained by using a purely geometric formulation in terms of Bloch sphere data without constructing the circuit Hamiltonian. For generic reference states, the derivative of the spread complexity is logarithmically divergent at the topological phase transition in the Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) model. We demonstrate that the derivative of the spread complexity is bounded by fidelity susceptibility for general two-band models, indicating the sensitivity of the spread complexity to any gap closing (topological or trivial). This is illustrated in the massive Dirac Hamiltonian with a trivial gap closing. Finally, we introduce a non-unitary duality in the SSH model between the topological and trivial phases, which manifests itself in the spread complexity and fidelity susceptibility.

Non-Gaussian Entanglement Hierarchy Based on the Schmidt Number

Authors: Jiajie Guo, Shuheng Liu, Matteo Fadel, Qiongyi He
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18605v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes a new hierarchy for non-Gaussian entanglement in bosonic systems using a quantitative witness based on the Schmidt number. It connects the level of non-Gaussianity to the complexity of quantum state learning and proves that such entanglement can be distilled using only Gaussian operations. This provides a practical tool for identifying and utilizing non-Gaussian resources in quantum optical and circuit-QED platforms.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous framework for classifying non-Gaussian resources in continuous-variable systems, directly linking entanglement dimensionality to the practical difficulty of quantum state tomography and the utility of Gaussian-based distillation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of quantifying and characterizing non-Gaussian entanglement in bipartite bosonic systems and establishing a hierarchy that connects entanglement dimension to state-learning complexity.

Main result. The authors introduce a quantitative witness E_NG that defines a natural hierarchy of non-Gaussian entanglement based on the Schmidt number, and demonstrate that this entanglement can serve as a resource for distillation using only Gaussian operations.

Method. The study uses the trace norm of the partial transpose minimized over Gaussian unitaries to define the witness, alongside complexity analysis for state reconstruction and a two-copy distillation protocol.

Model / system. Bipartite bosonic systems, specifically focusing on continuous-variable states such as NOON states, squeezed Kerr states, and photon-subtracted Gaussian states, applicable to platforms like circuit-QED and quantum acoustics.

Key observables. The non-Gaussian entanglement witness E_NG, the Schmidt number hierarchy d, and the expectation value of a NOON-type witness operator F.

Important parameters / regimes. Squeezing strength, nonlinearity (Kerr parameter), loss rates, and the Gaussian-entanglable threshold f_G.

Assumptions / limitations. The optimization of the witness is performed over an effective set of Gaussian unitaries, and the distillation protocol assumes the availability of local homodyne measurements and classical communication.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the hierarchy of GE and non-Gaussian entangled states, linking the integer d to entanglement levels and state-learning complexity; Figure 5 shows the scaling of reconstruction complexity as a function of the entanglement integer d.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-Gaussian entanglement, defines the E_NG witness and its hierarchy, provides mathematical proofs for its properties, benchmarks the framework with specific states (NOON, Kerr), analyzes robustness to loss, proposes an experimental NOON-type witness, and demonstrates entanglement distillation protocols.

Abstract

Non-Gaussian entanglement is a promising resource in various quantum tasks. A recently defined class identifies entanglement that cannot be generated by applying Gaussian operations to separable inputs. To further explore the entanglement in this context, we introduce a quantitative witness $E_{\rm NG}$ in bipartite bosonic systems, which satisfies $E_{\rm NG}=1$ for all Gaussian-entanglable states, while $E_{\rm NG}>1$ certifies non-Gaussian entanglement. Its ceiling $d=\lceil E_{\rm NG}\rceil$ provides a lower bound on the Schmidt number irreducible by Gaussian transformations, thereby defining a natural hierarchy of non-Gaussian entanglement. For pure states, the condition is sharp and the hierarchy reflects the complexity of state learning. We benchmark the framework with some paradigmatic non-Gaussian states, such as NOON states and squeezed Kerr states, and analyze its robustness against loss. Moreover, we construct an experimentally economical NOON-type witness requiring only four projective measurements, where an analytical Gaussian-entanglable threshold is derived. These results establish an operationally meaningful and experimentally accessible framework for identifying non-Gaussian entanglement resources in continuous-variable quantum platforms.

Quantum randomness beyond projective measurements

Authors: Fionnuala Curran
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18291v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how much randomness can be extracted from non-projective quantum measurements. It proves that a new class of 'skewed SIC' measurements can achieve the theoretical maximum of randomness and identifies the SIC measurement as the least random among certain qubit measurement classes.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for quantum optics and quantum information as it provides fundamental bounds on randomness extraction and characterizes the security of measurement-based protocols against eavesdroppers.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Quantifying the amount of intrinsic quantum randomness generated by extremal measurements beyond standard projective measurements, specifically determining which measurements maximize or minimize the guessing probability of an eavesdropper.

Main result. The paper identifies that SIC measurements are the least random among unbiased qubit measurements and introduces 'skewed SIC' measurements that can generate the maximal $2 \log d$ bits of randomness in dimensions where SICs exist.

Method. The author uses semidefinite programming (SDP) dualities, geometric analysis of polytopes in the Bloch ball, and KKT optimality conditions to characterize guessing probabilities and randomness.

Model / system. The study focuses on finite-dimensional quantum systems (qubits, qutrits, and ququarts) using Positive Operator-Valued Measures (POVMs), specifically rank-one extremal and Symmetric Informationally Complete (SIC) measurements.

Key observables. Conditional min-entropy $H_{\min}( ho, M)$, optimal guessing probability $P_{ ext{guess}}$, and quantum fidelity $F( ho, \sigma)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension $d$, scaling parameter $\gamma$ for skewed SICs, noise parameter $\epsilon$, and angular parameters $\delta$ and $\alpha$ for qubit/qutrit measurement families.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis primarily focuses on rank-one extremal POVMs and assumes a device-dependent or source-device-independent framework.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate geometric regions in the Bloch ball, the comparison between SIC and 'scissors' POVMs, and the space of average post-measurement states.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of intrinsic randomness, moves to characterizing unbiased extremal qubit measurements, introduces the skewed SIC family and its randomness-generating properties, and concludes with higher-dimensional constructions for qutrits and ququarts.

Abstract

The unpredictability of quantum physics gives rise to intrinsic randomness. In an adversarial scenario, any additional degrees of freedom must be attributed to an eavesdropper with correlations to the measurement set-up. The true randomness is then quantified by the probability that she correctly guesses the measurement outcomes, optimised over all possible strategies. Extremal measurements are appealing here, since they do not allow information to leak to such an eavesdropper. Beyond projective measurements, however, a simple question remains open: how much intrinsic randomness can be generated by a given extremal measurement? In a step towards solving it, we characterise the randomness generated by any unbiased extremal rank-one measurement acting on any state, solving the problem explicitly in dimension two. Four-outcome qubit measurements of this type are tomographic, so these results hold for fully source-device-dependent randomness too. The tetrahedral symmetric informationally complete (SIC) measurement, we find, has the least intrinsic randomness within this class. We also present the skewed SIC family of measurements, and use them to partially solve an open problem: we prove that $2 \log d$ bits of randomness, the maximal amount, can be generated device-dependently (or source-device-independently) in any dimension in which there exists a SIC measurement.

Schedule-dependent basin occupation in a programmable quantum annealer

Authors: Luis Lozano
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17604v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper investigates how different annealing schedules affect the distribution of energy basins in D-Wave quantum annealers. It demonstrates that while schedules can redistribute probability between local minima, they do not provide a universal quantum advantage over classical parallel tempering, and it corrects previous claims regarding the effectiveness of multi-pause schedules.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the shape of reverse-anneal schedules in a quantum annealer controls the final sampling distribution of energy basins beyond simple temperature rescaling, and determining if these effects are genuine quantum signatures or reproducible by classical Monte Carlo processes.

Main result. The study finds that for certain instances, the QPU dynamics sit strictly between localized parallel tempering and delocalified path-integral annealing, and that schedule engineering can redistribute probability among basins but does not provide a universal quantum advantage, refuting previous claims of a 'two-pause enhancement'.

Method. The authors used a cycled reverse-anneal protocol on D-Wave Advantage2 and Advantage system6.4 hardware, comparing results against classical parallel tempering (PT) and path-integral simulated quantum annealing (SQA) using a bootstrap-based statistical framework.

Model / system. 12-qubit Ising instances with mixed frustration on random 3-regular logical graphs, implemented on D-Wave Advantage2 (Zephyr) and Advantage system6.4 (Pegasus) quantum annealers.

Key observables. Subsystem configuration autocorrelation C(k), memory order parameter M, endpoint distances to classical/quantum Gibbs targets (D_cl, D_q), and basin occupation/weight redistribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Effective temperature (beta_eff approx 7.22 and 4.29), annealing schedules (single-pause, two-pause, quench-and-hold), and transverse-field scale (A approx 1.38 GHz).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the observed 'unmatched' signals are not artifacts of minor-embedding, which is supported by native-graph control experiments.

Figures summary. Figure 5(a) compares QPU autocorrelation against PT benchmarks across various schedules; Figure 6 shows native-graph control experiments on induced subgraphs of the Zephyr hardware graph.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of schedule-dependent dynamics, describes the hardware and Ising models used, details the reverse-anneal and classical comparison protocols, presents results on basin occupation and the 'bracketing' effect, provides a native-graph control to rule out embedding artifacts, and concludes by refuting previous claims of universal quantum enhancement via two-pause schedules.

Abstract

On a mixed-frustration 12-qubit Ising instance (seed 14029) run on two D-Wave generations, Advantage2 Zephyr and Advantage_system6.4 Pegasus, the late-time subsystem autocorrelation under cycled reverse annealing sits strictly between two equilibrium reference processes at the device-calibrated effective temperature: localized parallel tempering, and delocalized equilibrated path-integral simulated quantum annealing at a fixed Advantage2 pause-point transverse-field scale. The bracket holds on all three tested schedules and at both hardware calibrations. We obtain this result through two ingredients: a cycled reverse-anneal protocol (reinitialize_state=False, 50 cycles per submission) used as a Markov-chain probe of the device's pause-point dynamics, and a parallel-tempering falsification framework with bias-corrected and accelerated bootstrap 95% confidence intervals. Of eighteen tested (seed, schedule) combinations on Advantage2, three are PT-unmatched and correspond to two distinct Ising instances; an independent native-graph control with no minor embedding on a third mixed-frustration instance reproduces the same direction of mismatch. Among twenty random training instances, schedule shape modulates basin occupation on six of the thirteen multi-basin-in-readout instances, with dominant-configuration shifts of up to 38 percentage points including changes of the dominant configuration. A pre-registered linear predictor of schedule sensitivity from exhaustively computable landscape features fails on ten held-out instances, indicating that schedule sensitivity is not captured by simple linear functions of the tested landscape moments. The bracketing result revises an earlier two-pause-enhancement claim and reframes reverse-anneal schedules as instance-specific basin-occupation probes rather than universal quantum-enhancement knobs.

Cryogenic Time-Division-Multiplexed Voltage Control for Scalable Trapped-Ion Quantum Processors

Authors: Ryutaro Ohira, Shinichi Morisaka, Yoshinori Kurimoto, Toshiaki Inada, Ippei Nakamura, Takefumi Miyoshi, Atsushi Noguchi
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.17824v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates a cryogenic voltage control system using time-division multiplexing to reduce the number of wires needed for large-scale trapped-ion quantum processors. By successfully operating at 14 K and 27 K, the authors show that both static and dynamic electrode requirements can be met with significantly fewer vacuum feedthroughs. This approach provides a scalable path for managing the massive electrode counts required by QCCD architectures.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a practical engineering solution to the scalability limits of trapped-ion processors, which is crucial for the realization of large-scale quantum computing architectures and complex many-body simulations in ion traps.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The scaling of trapped-ion quantum computers using the QCCD architecture is limited by a 'wiring bottleneck,' where the massive number of electrodes required leads to an unsustainable number of vacuum feedthroughs and room-temperature DAC channels.

Main result. The researchers successfully demonstrated a cryogenic Time-Division-Multiplexed (TDM) voltage control system that achieves both static and dynamic electrode control at 14 K and 27 K, significantly reducing the required physical connections.

Method. A TDM-based architecture was implemented using a room-temperature FPGA-driven DAC and a cryogenic demultiplexer (DEMUX) board, utilizing a holding capacitor at each output to retain voltage between update cycles.

Model / system. The experimental platform is a cryogenic ion-trap system (utilizing 40-K and 4-K stages) designed for the Quantum Charge-Coupled Device (QCCD) architecture, featuring both static electrodes for potential compensation and dynamic electrodes for ion shuttling.

Key observables. Effective voltage update rates, voltage drop (leakage) over time, crosstalk between channels, and output voltage ranges.

Important parameters / regimes. Update rates of 37.5 kHz (static) and 1 MHz (dynamic); voltage range of +/- 10 V; operating temperatures of 14 K and 27 K; crosstalk levels around -12 to -35 dB.

Assumptions / limitations. The RC time constant of the output nodes must allow for DAC output settling and capacitor charging within the allocated time slot.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the TDM system schematic and architecture for electrode classes; Figure 2 details the physical cryogenic setup and hardware components; Figure 3 displays measured DC output voltages for 32 channels; Figure 4 shows the dynamic electrode setup and measured sinusoidal waveforms.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the wiring bottleneck, describes the TDM architecture and hardware implementation, presents experimental validation for both static and dynamic electrode classes at cryogenic temperatures, and discusses scaling implications and hardware limitations.

Abstract

Trapped-ion quantum computers based on the quantum charge-coupled device architecture require on the order of ten trap electrodes per qubit, making the number of vacuum feedthroughs a bottleneck at the system scale. Time-division multiplexed (TDM)-based voltage control for trap electrodes provides a natural route to alleviate this constraint. However, previous studies have been limited to architectural proposals for static trap-potential compensation and room-temperature demonstrations of dynamic-electrode control, leaving cryogenic operation of TDM-based voltage control for static and dynamic electrodes experimentally unexplored. In this study, we develop and cryogenically validate TDM-based voltage control schemes for two distinct electrode classes. For static electrodes used in trap-potential compensation, we implement a 32-channel demultiplexed system operating at approximately 27~K, achieving an effective voltage update rate of 37.5~kHz with an output range of $\pm10~\mathrm{V}$ per channel. For dynamic electrodes used in ion operations, such as shuttling, we implement a four-channel demultiplexed system operating at approximately 14~K, achieving an effective voltage update rate of 1~MHz with a comparable output range. These results establish TDM-based voltage control as a practical approach for both electrode classes, providing a path for mitigating the vacuum feedthrough bottleneck in scalable trapped-ion quantum processors.

Exact Organization of Density Matrices and Entanglement Structure in the Kitaev Spin Liquid

Authors: Chen-Chih Wang, Yi-Ping Huang, Sungkit Yip
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.18002v1

Summary. This paper provides an exact derivation of the density matrix for the Kitaev spin liquid, revealing how the entanglement entropy splits into distinct gauge and matter components. It establishes the microscopic link between spin and Majorana fermion representations and explains the high degeneracy in the entanglement spectrum through the lens of emergent gauge symmetries.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to derive an explicit, physically transparent form of the density matrix for the Kitaev spin liquid in the spin representation to understand the microscopic origin of its entanglement structure.

Main result. The authors derive an exact form of the density matrix and prove that the reduced density matrix is block-diagonal, leading to the separability of entanglement entropy into gauge and matter parts and explaining the extensive degeneracy in the entanglement spectrum.

Method. The study employs an algebraic approach using Majorana fermion representations, Gutzwiller projection, and the study of string operator equivalence classes, alongside symmetry analysis of the Gauss law and 1-form Wilson symmetry.

Model / system. The Kitaev honeycomb model, an exactly solvable quantum spin system featuring a spin-1/2 liquid ground state with fractionalized Majorana fermion excitations and an emergent Z2 gauge field.

Key observables. Reduced density matrix (RDM), entanglement entropy (EE), entanglement spectrum (ES), spin correlation functions, and fermion parity.

Important parameters / regimes. Subregion size (even vs. odd number of lattice sites), boundary length, and the flux-free sector (fixed Z2 fluxes).

Assumptions / limitations. The subregion is assumed to be simply-connected, and the Z2 fluxes are assumed to be static (flux-free sector).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows equivalent edge configurations and Wilson loops; Figure 2 illustrates the linking of Majorana fermions via gauge-field strings; Figure 6 shows 't Hooft loop operators; Figure 8 shows the duality between triangular and honeycomb lattices.

Paper structure. The paper begins by deriving the exact density matrix in the spin representation, moves to analyzing the block-diagonal structure of the RDM using gauge symmetries, extends the formalism to odd-sized subsystems, and concludes with a geometric derivation of the entanglement entropy components using lattice duality.

Abstract

We give an exact form of the density matrix of the spin-1/2 Kitaev spin liquid represented in terms of spin operators and study the entanglement structures of the Kitaev honeycomb model within the spin framework. We show that the density matrix is naturally organized by equivalence classes of string operators associated with the underlying gauge structure of the model. With the explicit form of the density matrix, plus the exact Gauss law of the emergent gauge theory and the exact 1-form Wilson symmetry in the Kitaev model, we demonstrate the existence of the underlying symmetry-resolved block-diagonal structure of the reduced density matrix, which gives rise to the extensive degeneracy in the entanglement spectrum. The block-diagonal structure is then proven to be responsible for the separability of the entanglement entropy into the gauge and matter parts. Summary. This paper uses effective field theory to study the transition between dimer and trimer states in a three-component Fermi gas. It reveals that external driving can control the crossover between these states and that many-body effects can create trimer states that do not exist in a vacuum. This work is significant for understanding the stability and manipulation of complex clusters in ultracold gases.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical framework for controlling many-body bound states using external drives, offering new insights into how the transition between different types of many-body clusters (like polarons and molecules) can be manipulated in ultracold atomic systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the crossover between dimer and trimer states in a three-component Fermi gas, specifically how external drives and many-body effects influence the stability and binding energies of these states.

Main result. The study demonstrates a guaranteed crossover from a dimer to a trimer ground state as the atom-dimer interaction is tuned, and shows that many-body effects can induce trimer solutions (Cooper pairs) even in regimes where they are absent in vacuum.

Method. The authors use an Effective Field Theory (EFT) and a variational approach, employing Feynman rules, the T-matrix formalism, and renormalization to solve self-consistent energy equations.

Model / system. A three-component Fermi gas consisting of three distinguishable atomic species (a, b, and c). Species a and b interact via an external drive to form a non-universal closed-channel dimer, which then interacts with a Fermi sea of the third species (c) via a contact interaction.

Key observables. Two-body and three-body binding energies, atom-dimer scattering length, and the energy difference between the dimer and trimer branches.

Abstract

We develop an Effective Field Theory (EFT) for a system with three distinguishable atomic species and present a variational calculation of the two and three-body binding energies in vacuum and in the presence of a single Fermi sea. Specifically, we consider the case where the interaction between first two atomic species is externally driven so as to produce a (non-universal) closed-channel dimer whose coupling can be controlled independently of all other interactions. We then model the remaining interactions as a contact interaction between the dimer and a third atomic species which forms the medium. We derive analytical expressions for the dimer and trimer binding energies in vacuum and in medium, and in the latter case we predict a crossing between the dimer and trimer branches as a function of the atom-dimer scattering length, analogous to the usual polaron and molecule problem. Furthermore, we show that the position of this crossing can be controlled by varying the atom-atom coupling that results from the external drive and we discuss the implications of these findings.

Summary. This paper explores how a shared stochastic resetting protocol induces correlations between independent Brownian particles. It develops a mathematical framework using nested boundary value problems to show that these particles no longer behave independently. The study establishes a direct link between multi-particle exit statistics and the moments of a stochastic diffusion equation.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a mathematical framework for understanding how shared environmental noise (common-mode noise) generates entanglement-like statistical correlations in otherwise independent constituents.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how global stochastic resetting induces statistical correlations between non-interacting Brownian particles and determining the resulting joint exit (splitting) probabilities.

Main result. The paper proves that global resetting creates emergent correlations such that the joint splitting probability cannot be decomposed into independent single-particle probabilities, and establishes a mapping between these probabilities and the moments of a stochastic diffusion equation.

Method. The author uses generalized Ito's lemma, backward Kolmogorov equations, and Green's function methods to derive a hierarchy of nested boundary value problems (BVPs).

Model / system. A population of M non-interacting Brownian particles in a 1D interval [0, L] with absorbing boundaries, subject to a global Poissonian resetting process to a common position.

Key observables. Splitting probability (probability of exiting through a specific boundary), joint splitting probability, and survival probability.

Important parameters / regimes. Resetting rate (r), diffusion coefficient (D), interval length (L), and the characteristic scale xi = sqrt(r/D).

Assumptions / limitations. Particles are non-interacting (no physical collisions); resetting is global (all particles reset simultaneously); boundaries are strictly absorbing.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the global resetting mechanism; Figure 2 plots joint splitting probability vs. reset rate; Figure 4 compares global vs. local gating; Figure 5 compares joint splitting probabilities for global and local gating scenarios.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of emergent correlations, derives the BVP hierarchy using Ito's lemma, provides an analytical solution for the two-particle case, establishes a mapping to SPDE moment equations, and concludes with a comparison between global and local gating dynamics.

Abstract

There is considerable current interest in the emergence of statistical correlations within a population of otherwise non-interacting Brownian particles subject to a common fluctuating environment or drive. Examples include global stochastic resetting, switching confining potentials, fluctuating diffusivities, and stochastically gated boundaries. Most studies have focused on the analytical structure of the stationary joint probability density (assuming it exists). In this paper, we extend previous work on the exit statistics of multiple particles in stochastically gated domains to the case of global resetting in an interval with absorbing boundaries at both ends. First, we use a generalised Itô's lemma to derive a hierarchy of boundary value problems (BVPs) for the joint splitting probability that all particles exit from the same end of the interval. The BVPs form a nested sequence with respect to the initial number of particles $M$. We explicitly solve the BVP for a pair of particles ($M=2$) and use this to illustrate the emergence of pairwise correlations. Second, we show how the BVP for the splitting probability of $M$ Brownian particles can be mapped onto the $M$th order moment equation of a stochastic diffusion equation with resetting. We thus establish a general mathematical framework to study exit problems for globally-driven particle systems.
