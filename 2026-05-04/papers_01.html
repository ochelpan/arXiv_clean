Experimental Evidence of Fractional Entropy in Critical Kondo Systems

Authors: C. Piquard, A. Veillon, Y. Sato, F. Zanichelli, A. Aassime, A. Cavanna, U. Gennser, A. K. Mitchell, A. Anthore, F. Pierre
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00669v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 Kondo & dissipative impurity 5/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. The researchers successfully measured fractional impurity entropy in two-channel and three-channel Kondo systems using a charge-sensing technique. These findings provide a direct thermodynamic fingerprint of non-Abelian anyons, specifically Majorana zero modes and Fibonacci anyons. This approach establishes entropy measurements as a powerful, model-independent tool for identifying exotic quantum states.

Why it may be interesting. The paper demonstrates a direct thermodynamic method to identify non-Abelian statistics through fractional entropy, which is highly relevant to the study of topological phases and many-body dynamics in correlated systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of unambiguously characterizing non-Abelian anyons, such as Majorana zero modes and Fibonacci anyons, using traditional transport measurements.

Main result. Experimental evidence of fractional entropy in two-channel and three-channel Kondo critical states, with scaling dimensions consistent with Majorana zero modes and Fibonacci anyons.

Method. Using a thermodynamic Maxwell relation to extract impurity entropy by measuring the temperature derivative of the island charge in a metal-semiconductor quantum circuit.

Model / system. A charge-Kondo circuit consisting of a micrometer-scale metallic island coupled to two or three electronic leads via quantum point contacts, implementing the multi-channel Kondo model.

Key observables. Island charge, differential conductance, impurity entropy change (Delta S), and Kondo temperature (TK).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), Kondo temperature (TK), transmission probability (tau), charging energy (EC), and gate-voltage detuning (delta Vpl).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a 'frozen' impurity regime in the Coulomb valley to ensure residual entropy is negligible; the validity of the thermodynamic Maxwell relation for entropy extraction.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the quantum phase transition, charge-Kondo mapping, and circuit schematic. Figure 2 displays conductance vs. gate voltage. Figure 3 illustrates the entropy measurement process. Figure 5 shows the universal crossover flow of conductance and entropy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of anyon characterization, describes the experimental platform and charge-Kondo mapping, details the entropy extraction methodology, validates the device via conductance and scaling, and presents the final entropy measurements and comparison with NRG theory.

Abstract

Unconventional quantum states defying the ubiquitous Fermi-liquid paradigm can emerge in the presence of strong electronic correlations. Among these, non-Abelian anyons - such as Majorana zero modes and Fibonacci anyons - are of particular interest for topological quantum computing due to their non-integer quantum dimensions d>1, which allows for protected non-local encoding and processing of quantum information. However, despite considerable efforts, the unambiguous characterisation of such anyons via transport measurements has proved challenging. Instead, here we provide experimental evidence for the low-temperature fractional entropy Delta S associated with a single anyon, which directly implies its non-Abelian character through the relation Delta S = kB ln(d). This thermodynamic signature is measured in metal-semiconductor quantum circuits engineered to realize quantum-critical states from frustrated interactions. Using a micrometre-scale metallic island coupled to two or three electronic leads, we tune the system to two-channel and three-channel Kondo critical points. By measuring the island charge and exploiting a thermodynamic Maxwell relation, we estimate the entropy associated with the anyons that emerge in these critical states. Our observations reveal fractional values, exposing non-Abelian anyons. The corresponding scaling dimensions are consistent with theoretical predictions for a Majorana zero mode Delta S = kB ln(sqrt(2)) and a Fibonacci anyon Delta S = kB ln(1 +sqrt(5))/2 for two and three channels. These findings establish entropy measurements as a powerful tool for characterizing exotic quantum states.

Nonreciprocity-enriched steady phases in open quantum systems

Summary. This paper demonstrates that nonreciprocity can create entirely new steady-state phases in open quantum systems, such as a traveling-wave phase of symmetry defects. It shows that while periodic systems exhibit standard symmetry breaking, open boundaries allow for chiral dynamics driven by the non-Hermitian skin effect. This work expands the understanding of how non-Hermitian physics can fundamentally alter the phase diagram of driven-dissipative matter.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new mechanism for phase formation where spatial boundaries and nonreciprocity interact to create phases that are topologically or dynamically distinct from their periodic counterparts, directly impacting the study of non-equilibrium many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how nonreciprocity and the non-Hermitian skin effect (NHSE) can stabilize new non-equilibrium steady-state phases of matter beyond the standard spontaneous-symmetry-breaking paradigm.

Main result. The discovery of a boundary-induced 'traveling-wave phase' where nonreciprocity drives domain walls to form a persistent chiral wave, as well as a 'chiral damping phase' where local fluctuations are amplified by the NHSE.

Method. The study employs mean-field theory, linear stability analysis, and truncated Wigner simulations to characterize phase boundaries and fluctuation dynamics.

Model / system. A driven-dissipative bosonic model with Z2 symmetry, featuring a Lindblad master equation with nonreciprocal hopping (iJ), single-particle gain, and two-particle loss.

Key observables. Order parameter (expectation value of the bosonic field), density, and the autocorrelation function for coherence time.

Important parameters / regimes. Nonreciprocity strength (J), gain/loss parameters (kappa1, kappa2), and spatial boundary conditions (PBC vs OBC).

Assumptions / limitations. The continuum limit approximation neglects spatial variations of the time-derivative of the mean-field solution near domain walls; the Wigner method relies on truncating third-order derivative terms.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the model lattice and representative examples of the three OBC phases (uniform, traveling-wave, and damping) alongside the effective Hamiltonian spectrum. Figure S1 shows the time evolution and exponential decay of the autocorrelation function amplitude.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model and the problem of nonreciprocity in steady states, presents the discovery of the traveling-wave and chiral damping phases under OBC, analyzes the role of the non-Hermitian skin effect, and concludes with a stability analysis of Goldstone-mode fluctuations using numerical simulations.

Abstract

Nonreciprocity can profoundly alter the spectra and dynamics of open quantum systems, yet its impact on the long-time steady-state phases of matter has remained largely unexplored. Here we show that the interplay of nonreciprocity, symmetry defects, and spatial boundaries can generate phases beyond the standard spontaneous-symmetry-breaking paradigm. We demonstrate this mechanism by showing that sufficiently strong nonreciprocity turns boundaries into sources and drains of symmetry defects, while simultaneously endowing these defects with chiral dynamics in the bulk. As a result, the conventional uniform symmetry-broken state gives way to a domain-wall traveling-wave phase, in which symmetry defects form a persistent chiral wave. We showcase this mechanism in a bosonic model with \(Z_{2}\) symmetry, where periodic boundary conditions support only the conventional symmetric and symmetry-broken phases, whereas open boundary conditions allow the traveling-wave phase. We further show that even in the absence of symmetry breaking, the steady state can exhibit anomalous chiral relaxation: owing to the non-Hermitian skin effect in the stability matrix, local fluctuations are chirally amplified as they approach a boundary, where they eventually decay. Combining mean-field theory with truncated Wigner simulations, we characterize these phases, analyze the order parameter and Goldstone-mode fluctuations of the traveling-wave phase, and confirm its existence in three spatial dimensions.

Summary. This paper explores how to generate scalable entanglement in three-level Rydberg atom arrays using dipole-dipole interactions. It shows that symmetric interactions lead to one-axis twisting-like squeezing, while antisymmetric interactions can produce more efficient two-axis countertwisting. These findings provide a theoretical foundation for using multi-level atoms in advanced quantum sensing applications.

Why it may be interesting. It extends well-known qubit-based squeezing protocols to qutrit systems and demonstrates how spatial interaction geometry (all-to-all vs. 2D dipolar) impacts the scalability of entanglement for quantum metrology.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the generation of scalable spin-nematic (quadrupolar) squeezing and entanglement in multi-level (qutrit) Rydberg atom arrays.

Main result. The study identifies that symmetric interactions enable scalable squeezing via One-Axis Twisting (OAT) dynamics, while antisymmetric interactions with a microwave drive enable Two-Axis Countertwisting (TAT) mechanisms.

Method. The authors use Exact Diagonalization for all-to-all interaction models and the Discrete Truncated Wigner Approximation (dTWA) for 2D dipolar interaction simulations.

Model / system. A three-level (spin-1) system realized in arrays of Rydberg atoms in optical tweezers, featuring dipole-dipole interactions and microwave driving.

Key observables. Squeezing parameter (xi^2) and Quantum Fisher Information (FQ).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction ratio (Jr), interaction range/decay (alpha), and system size (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The dTWA is used as a semiclassical approximation for large-scale 2D dynamics and is benchmarked against exact results.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a model overview of the Rydberg system; Figure 3 and 4 show squeezing and QFI scaling for the bright manifold under all-to-all interactions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the three-level Rydberg model, analyzes the Hamiltonian decomposition into SU(2) subspaces for symmetric and antisymmetric regimes, presents scaling results for different interaction ranges, and validates the semiclassical approximation.

Abstract

We study the generation of metrologically useful entanglement in a three-level (spin-1) system naturally realized in arrays of dipole-interacting Rydberg atoms confined in optical tweezers. In the spin-quadrupolar operator basis, the interaction Hamiltonian decomposes into effective SU(2) subspaces, within which quench dynamics from product initial states generate scalable spin-nematic squeezing. For symmetric interactions, we identify a mapping to effective one-axis twisting within bright and dark manifolds and demonstrate that the squeezing parameter scales as $ξ^{2}\propto N^{-2/3}$ ($ξ^{2}\propto N^{-0.5}$) with system size for all-to-all (two-dimensional dipolar) couplings. In both cases the quantum Fisher information reaches $F_Q\propto N^{2}$. For antisymmetric interactions supplemented by a microwave drive we find a distinct two-axis countertwisting mechanism. This results in squeezing $ξ^{2}\propto N^{-0.7}$ for all-to-all interactions and moderate squeezing for dipolar interactions in 2D. Our results constitute a first theoretical step beyond the well-studied qubit setting toward scalable entanglement generation in qudit systems with dipolar interactions, directly relevant to current Rydberg tweezer experiments.

A Unified Framework for Locally Stable Phases

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes a new unified framework for characterizing phases of matter by defining 'locally stable states' through the lens of local recoverability. It proves that this operational definition is equivalent to the decay of correlations, effectively bridging the gap between pure and mixed state phase descriptions. The work also introduces a new family of nonlinear observables to diagnose non-equilibrium phases.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous, unified language for researchers studying open quantum systems and many-body dynamics by linking operational stability (recoverability) to structural properties (correlation decay) across different physical regimes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a unified framework to characterize phases of matter across equilibrium, non-equilibrium, and metastable regimes, specifically bridging the gap between pure and mixed state descriptions.

Main result. The authors establish that 'local stability' (the ability to reverse local operations via a local channel) is equivalent to 'short-range correlations' (decay of operator correlations and conditional mutual information), providing a unified definition for locally stable phases.

Method. The framework utilizes quantum information-theoretic tools including Petz recovery maps, Rényi divergences, and the analysis of quantum Markov chains and Lindbladian dynamics.

Model / system. Quantum many-body systems on D-dimensional lattices with local Hilbert spaces, including equilibrium Gibbs states, non-equilibrium steady states of local Lindbladians, and systems subject to local decoherence.

Key observables. Operator correlations, conditional mutual information (CMI), fidelity correlators, and a two-parameter family of nonlinear correlators (C_{p,q}).

Important parameters / regimes. Correlation length, Markov length, spectral gap of the Lindbladian, and the parameters (p, q) of the nonlinear observables.

Assumptions / limitations. The equivalence between local stability and short-range correlations is subject to technical constraints on the buffer region size and the dimensionality of the lattice.

Figures summary. Figure 1 maps different phase types to diagnostic measures; Figure 2 illustrates lattice partitioning for proofs; Figure 3 shows the schematic for phase invariance; Figure 5 illustrates lattice partitioning for nonlinear observables; Figure 6 shows the light-cone structure of recovery.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of local stability, proves its equivalence to short-range correlations, demonstrates phase invariance under reversible circuits, develops a theory for nonlinear correlators, and concludes with applications to Lindbladian dynamics and counterexamples.

Abstract

We propose a unifying framework for characterizing pure and mixed state phases of matter across equilibrium, non equilibrium, and metastable regimes. We introduce the concept of locally stable states, defined by the operational property that any local operation (including post selection) can be reversed by a local channel. We prove that local stability is equivalent to a state being short range correlated, defined by the decay of both correlations and conditional mutual information. We demonstrate that these properties are invariant under locally reversible channels, thus defining locally stable phases. Furthermore, we prove that local stability implies both the decay of a family of nonlinear correlators, including the fidelity correlator, and the decay of correlations in the canonical purification, thus bridging the gap between mixed and pure states. Along the way, we establish two results which may be of independent interest: we show that post-selection on locally stable (short range correlated) states can be implemented via local channels and that quantum Markov chains can be characterized by the local computability of nonlinear observables.

Asymptotic Replacement for Quantum Channel Products with Applications to Inhomogeneous Matrix Product States

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding how sequences of different quantum channels forget their initial states. It proves that the decay of a specific contraction coefficient determines whether channels converge to a replacement family, and shows how these dynamics translate to the stability and correlation decay in random Matrix Product States.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides rigorous quantitative bounds on how environmental noise and time-dependent dynamics lead to the loss of initial state memory and the emergence of unique steady-state behaviors in many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the asymptotic behavior and memory loss of products of non-identical (inhomogeneous) and random quantum channels, and applying these results to the stability and correlation decay of Matrix Product States (MPS).

Main result. The paper establishes that the decay of the trace-Dobrushin coefficient is equivalent to the asymptotic replacement of channels by a moving replacement family, and proves that a negative Lyapunov exponent leads to exponential convergence and spatial clustering in random MPS.

Method. The authors develop a product-level trace-Dobrushin theory using contraction coefficients, ergodic theory (Lyapunov exponents), and transfer map formalism to analyze both deterministic and random CPTP cocycles.

Model / system. The study focuses on finite-dimensional Completely Positive Trace-Preserving (CPTP) quantum channels, specifically sequences of inhomogeneous channels and stationary random quantum cocycles, with applications to inhomogeneous Matrix Product States (MPS) in the left-canonical gauge.

Key observables. Trace-norm distance, trace-Dobrushin coefficient, trace-Dobrushin Lyapunov exponent, and connected correlation functions of local observables.

Important parameters / regimes. Trace-Dobrushin coefficient (kappa_tr), Trace-Dobrushin Lyapunov exponent (lambda_tr), and the rho-mixing profile of the channel environment.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, CPTP maps, and specifically for the MPS application, the left-canonical gauge; random results require a negative Lyapunov exponent almost surely.

Paper structure. The paper begins by developing a product-level trace-Dobrushin theory for deterministic channels, extends these results to two-sided products and pullback convergence, then moves to random quantum cocycles using ergodic theory, and concludes by transferring these estimates to the study of inhomogeneous and random Matrix Product States.

Abstract

We develop a product-level trace-Dobrushin theory for finite-dimensional quantum channel products and apply it to deterministic and stationary random inhomogeneous matrix product states in left-canonical CPTP gauge. For a product of channels, the centered trace-Dobrushin coefficient quantifies the residual dependence on the input state, and its decay is the criterion for trace-norm forgetting. In the deterministic setting, this decay is equivalent to asymptotic replacement by a moving replacement channel. For two-sided products, pullback forgetting produces a unique boundary state, which determines the canonical replacement family. For stationary random CPTP cocycles, submultiplicativity of the product coefficient yields a trace-Dobrushin Lyapunov exponent. We prove that the almost sure negativity of this exponent is equivalent to quenched trace-norm memory loss and gives exponential forward and pullback convergence to a unique dynamically stationary random replacement channel. When the \(\varrho\)-mixing profile of the channel environment tends to zero, we obtain annealed super-polynomial estimates, while independence gives annealed exponential estimates. Finally, we transfer these estimates to inhomogeneous matrix product states whose auxiliary transfer maps are CPTP. These channel estimates transfer to deterministic and stationary random inhomogeneous MPS, giving infinite-volume limits of trace-closed finite-volume states, quantitative boundary stability, and correlation bounds governed by the same auxiliary product coefficients.

Summary. The researchers developed a technique to optically detect the NMR signal of massive 13C nuclear spin ensembles in bulk diamond using NV centers. By using microwave frequency sweeps to transfer polarization, they achieved high-contrast readout at low magnetic fields. This work provides a scalable platform for high-sensitivity quantum sensing and precision measurements.

Why it may be interesting. The paper explores the dynamics of a large-scale spin bath coupled to a quantum sensor, involving non-equilibrium polarization transfer, diabatic transitions, and the interplay between electron and nuclear spin relaxation in a disordered solid-state environment.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving high-sensitivity, low-field, and room-temperature Optically Detected Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (ODNMR) of large ensembles of 13C nuclear spins in bulk diamond for applications in fundamental physics and inertial sensing.

Main result. Demonstrated successful 13C Ramsey spectroscopy in bulk diamond with a nuclear-spin-dependent fluorescence contrast exceeding 0.5% and identified the dominant dephasing source as the longitudinal relaxation of nearby NV centers.

Method. Uses microwave frequency sweeps to induce state-selective Landau-Zener transitions that bidirectionally transfer polarization between NV electron spins and 13C nuclear spins, followed by RF pulses for NMR encoding and optical readout of the NV state.

Model / system. Bulk diamond with natural abundance (1.1%) 13C and low nitrogen density (0.5-10 ppm), utilizing Nitrogen-Vacancy (NV) centers as sensors interacting with 13C nuclear spin ensembles.

Key observables. 13C NMR frequency, fluorescence contrast (delta F/F), nuclear spin dephasing time (T2*), and polarization buildup rate.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic fields of 8-20 mT, 13C gyromagnetic ratio of 10.7 MHz/T, NV zero-field splitting of 2.87 GHz, and nuclear spin dephasing times of ~1.74 ms.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that the 13C nuclear spin diffusion and the longitudinal relaxation of NV centers are the primary drivers of the observed signal dynamics.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the ODNMR apparatus schematic; Figure 4 displays Ramsey interferograms, Hahn-echo, and WAHUHA decay curves; supplementary figures show polarization buildup and laser-induced depolarization effects.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the need for low-field NMR, describes the experimental setup and the polarization transfer mechanism via Landau-Zener transitions, presents results for Ramsey spectroscopy and dephasing, analyzes the impact of laser power and magnetic field, and concludes with sensitivity estimates.

Abstract

Precision measurements based on optically detected nuclear magnetic resonance offer exquisite sensitivity to absolute shifts in spin transition frequencies, with potential applications in fundamental physics experiments and inertial sensing. We investigate 13C nuclear spins in diamond as a candidate system for solid-state implementations, which hold the promise for high-fidelity readout of large numbers of coherent nuclear spins in millitesla or lower magnetic fields. We demonstrate a technique that allows for both optical polarization and readout of large ensembles of ~10^{16} polarized nuclear spins. Our method takes advantage of state-selective Landau-Zener transitions under microwave frequency sweeping, which bidirectionally transfer spin polarization between Nitrogen-Vacancy (NV) electron spins and remote 13C nuclear spins. Using natural isotopic abundance diamonds with nitrogen densities of ~0.5-10 ppm, we perform optically-detected 13C Ramsey spectroscopy and realize a nuclear-spin-dependent fluorescence contrast exceeding 0.5% peak-to-peak. We observe nuclear spin dephasing times T2*~2 ms that only modestly improve with homonuclear dipolar decoupling, indicating that they are limited by the longitudinal spin relaxation of nearby NV electron spins. We study the magnetic field dependence of the optical readout and find comparable contrast and dephasing times for magnetic fields in the range 8-20 mT. Our method can be interpreted as a type of repetitive readout, where each NV center optically reads out the spin state of ~100 nuclei before nuclear spins depolarize.

Summary. The paper demonstrates that the localization transition in East-type models is determined by the connectivity structure in Fock space rather than real-space locality. By scrambling the real-space connections while keeping the magnetization-sector structure intact, the authors show that the phase transition remains qualitatively unchanged. This implies that the hierarchical organization of the Fock-space graph is the primary driver of localization in these constrained systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it identifies that the topology of the Hilbert space graph, rather than physical distance, can be the fundamental driver of many-body localization and slow dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether the localization transition in East-type models is driven by real-space geometric locality or by the underlying connectivity structure in Fock space.

Main result. The study finds that the localization-delocalization transition persists even when real-space locality is removed via random permutations, suggesting that the hierarchical structure of the Fock-space graph is the essential ingredient for localization.

Method. The authors use a graph-theoretic framework to analyze the Hamiltonian and perform numerical simulations including finite-size scaling analysis of Shannon entropy and dynamical participation ratios.

Model / system. The study focuses on the Quantum East Model and a newly introduced 'Permuted East Model', which preserves magnetization-sector organization but randomizes connectivity in Fock space to remove real-space locality.

Key observables. Dynamical participation ratio, Eigenstate Shannon entropy, Shannon-entropy fluctuations, and Local spectral variance.

Important parameters / regimes. The hopping rate parameter (s) which controls the transition, system size (L), and the disorder parameter (w).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that the connectivity in Fock space can be effectively modeled by random permutations while preserving the magnetization sector structure.

Figures summary. Abstract

Local kinetic constraints in quantum many-body systems can generate slow dynamics or complete many-body localisation. Here we focus on a modification of the quantum East model: Inspired by random matrix theory, we randomise the connectivity in Fock space (rendering it nonlocal in real space) while preserving its organisation into neighbouring magnetisation sectors. We find that there is still a transition between two distinct phases, one delocalised and the other localised. We conclude that, for East-type constrained models, the essential ingredient is the structure of the graph in Fock space rather than geometric locality of spin flips.

Summary. This paper explores how entanglement is transformed into thermodynamic entropy during objective quantum state reduction. It demonstrates that this conversion is not one-to-one and proposes that the non-monotonic evolution of local entropy can serve as a signature to distinguish state reduction from environmental dephasing.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a way to experimentally distinguish fundamental wave-function collapse models from standard decoherence by looking at thermodynamic signatures in the presence of correlated noise.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the relationship between quantum entanglement and thermodynamic entropy during the process of objective quantum state reduction, specifically whether entanglement can be converted into thermodynamic entropy in a one-to-one fashion.

Main result. Method. The study uses ensemble averaging over stochastic trajectories of a non-linear, stochastic master equation to calculate the evolution of von Neumann and thermodynamic entropies.

Model / system. A bipartite entangled pure state (two degrees of freedom) evolving under an objective quantum state reduction model described by a stochastic, non-linear equation with a temporally correlated noise parameter.

Important parameters / regimes. Noise correlation time (tau), strength of non-linear term (J), amplitude of stochastic term (G), and the white-noise limit (tau -> 0).

Abstract

Discrete-time quantum walks provide a natural framework for quantum transport on complex networks. On regular structures, coin-walker entanglement has been widely used to characterize quantum transport and to support quantum algorithmic protocols. However, this notion relies on a fixed Hilbert space factorization separating coin and position and is therefore not directly applicable to more complex, irregular structures. Here we introduce an entanglement measure for general networks based on a bipartition that assigns each node two roles, acting as both a source and a target. The resulting bipartition defines the source-target entanglement, a measure for general networks, motivated by coin-walker entanglement. We show that the connectivity of the network imposes an upper bound on this entanglement and identify graph matchings as the underlying structure governing entanglement generation. We further illustrate that in random graphs improving graph connectivity reduces the attainable entanglement, establishing a structure-dependent constraint on quantum correlations.

Summary. This paper provides an analytical framework to calculate the entanglement distance in variational quantum circuits. Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to quantify the 'entanglement distance' and characterize the spreading of quantum correlations in variational quantum circuits as circuit depth increases.

Main result. The authors derived analytical recurrence relations for Pauli expectation values and demonstrated that increasing circuit depth expands the neighborhood of qubits that influence a target qubit's local observables.

Method. The researchers used the Heisenberg picture to evolve Pauli operators backward through the circuit and applied the Clifford property of CZ gates to derive exact analytical expressions, which were then verified via Qiskit numerical simulations.

Abstract

We study the entanglement distance of variational quantum states for two-qubit and multi-qubit systems. These states are constructed using variational quantum circuits with $R_Y$ rotations and entangling $CZ$ gates.For the two-qubit case, we analytically derive recurrence relations for expectation values of Pauli observables using. This approach allows us to analytically calculate quantum correlators and evaluate the entanglement distance depending on the circuit parameters and depth. The analysis were extended to a closed one-dimensional chain of $N$ qubits. It is shown that with increasing circuit depth, more qubits influence a given qubit, which reflects the spreading of quantum correlations in the system. For a closed one-dimensional chain of $N$ qubits, explicit analytical expressions are derived for the case of two layers. The results are compared with numerical simulations performed using quantum programming tools. The results agree with the theoretical predictions.
