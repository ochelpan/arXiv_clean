Exploring the Geometric and Dynamical Properties of Spin Systems and Their Interplay with Quantum Entanglement

Authors: Jamal Elfakir
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00241v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This thesis investigates how the geometry of quantum state space, characterized by metrics and curvature, relates to the dynamical evolution and entanglement of spin systems. It demonstrates that quantum correlations effectively 'compress' the state space by reducing its curvature and geometric phase. The work bridges classical symplectic mechanics with quantum information geometry.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified geometric framework for understanding how entanglement modifies the topology and curvature of state space, which is fundamental for developing robust geometric quantum gates and understanding many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The research explores the interplay between the geometric/topological properties of quantum state space and the dynamical evolution of spin systems, specifically regarding entanglement and evolution speed. **Main result.** The study establishes that increasing entanglement decreases the curvature of the state space and reduces the accumulated geometric phase, with higher spin values enhancing these variations. **Method.** The author utilizes the mathematical framework of projective Hilbert space, applying the Fubini-Study metric, symplectic geometry, and the Gauss-Bonnet theorem to analyze quantum states. **Model / system.** The study focuses on spin systems, including two-body anisotropic Heisenberg (XXZ) models and many-body all-range Ising models for both spin-1/2 and general spin-s particles. **Key observables.** Quantum entanglement (concurrence, I-concurrence), geometric phase (Berry, Aharonov-Anandan), evolution speed, and G-curvature. **Important parameters / regimes.** Interaction anisotropy, coupling intensity, spin value (s), number of particles (N), and the thermodynamic/large-spin limits. **Assumptions / limitations.** The analysis is primarily restricted to unitary evolution in closed quantum systems. **Figures summary.** Plots illustrating the relationship between evolution speed, entanglement, and Fubini-Study distance, as well as curvature and geometric phase as functions of I-concurrence. **Paper structure.** The thesis progresses from classical Hamiltonian mechanics and symplectic structures to the geometric description of quantum states, concluding with the application of these tools to analyze the dynamics and topology of Heisenberg and Ising spin models.
Abstract This thesis, explores the quantum entanglement and evolution through both a geometric and dynamical perspective. The first part focuses on classical phase space and its central role in Hamiltonian mechanics, emphasizing the importance of symplectic structures in describing mechanical states. The study highlights the formal analogy between classical phase space and the Hilbert space used in quantum mechanics. The second part is devoted to the geometric description of quantum states through the projective structure of Hilbert space. Emphasis is placed on the geometric interpretation of quantum evolution, particularly via the Fubini-Study metric, associated symplectic structures, and the geometric phase acquired during unitary evolutions. The final two parts are dedicated to the study of spin systems (both two-body and many-body) under different interaction models (XXZ Heisenberg and all-range Ising). Both the dynamical aspects (evolution speed, entanglement, and the quantum brachistochrone problem) and the geometric and topological structures of the corresponding quantum states are analyzed.

Sequential Measurements as a Resource for Quantum Metrology

Authors: Koray Mentesoglu, Rahul Trivedi, Sara Mouradian

Summary. This paper proposes a protocol using sequential weak measurements to simultaneously estimate both quadratures of a quantum displacement. It demonstrates that this approach can achieve enhanced scaling of information, increase dynamic range, and remain robust against environmental decoherence.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel way to use measurement backaction as a resource and demonstrates how sequentiality can fundamentally change the scaling of precision and robustness to noise in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Developing a quantum metrology protocol for the simultaneous estimation of both quadratures of a displacement channel while overcoming limitations like decoherence and phase wrapping. **Main result.** Sequential weak measurements act as a resource that can increase information gain via measurement backaction, achieve N^3 scaling of Quantum Fisher Information in certain regimes, and provide robustness to decoherence. **Method.** The authors use a sequential measurement protocol on an ancilla qubit coupled to a harmonic oscillator, employing a Dicke basis to reduce computational complexity and analyzing information via Quantum and Classical Fisher Information. **Model / system.** A Quantum Harmonic Oscillator (QHO) coupled to an ancilla qubit, subject to a displacement channel and subject to environmental decoherence (loss and dephasing) modeled by a Lindblad master equation. **Key observables.** Measurement outcomes of the ancilla qubit (N-bit string), Hamming weights, and Quantum/Classical Fisher Information. **Important parameters / regimes.** Displacement parameters (beta_1, beta_2), measurement strength (alpha), number of measurement rounds (N), and effective decay rate (Gamma). **Assumptions / limitations.** The thermal occupancy of the oscillator is assumed to be negligible (n_th approx 0), and the protocol assumes the QHO cannot be measured directly. **Figures summary.** Fig 1 illustrates the single vs. sequential measurement protocols; Fig 2 shows QFI superiority and scaling exponents; Fig 3 compares single vs. two-parameter performance; Fig 4 shows CFI dips; Fig 7 shows the monotonic decrease of CRB in the presence of decoherence; Fig 8 compares joint-system QFI. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the sequential protocol, establishes the mathematical framework using the Dicke basis, compares scaling regimes (weak vs. strong measurement), extends the protocol to two-parameter estimation, analyzes robustness against decoherence, and discusses connections to grid states.
Abstract We present a protocol in which sequential weak measurements of a quantum harmonic oscillator enable simultaneous estimation of both quadratures of a displacement channel. Calculations of the quantum Fisher information show that the measurement backaction can increase the information gained for a range of measurement strengths. The protocol distributes information over a $N$-bit string after $N$ weak measurements. Thus we find that post-processing can be used to avoid information loss due to phase wrapping, increasing the effective dynamic range. Finally, the periodic information extraction makes the protocol robust to decoherence. Our results establish mid-sensing measurement as a resource for single- and multi-parameter quantum metrology.

Criticality on Rényi defects at (2+1)$d$ O(3) quantum critical points

Authors: Yanzhang Zhu, Zhe Wang, Meng Cheng, Zheng Yan
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00104v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the universality of Renyi entanglement entropy is determined by the specific geometry of the entanglement cut, which can lead to different defect universality classes. It identifies a phase transition on the 'extraordinary' defect line driven by the Renyi index. This provides a framework to understand why entanglement scaling can appear sensitive to microscopic lattice details.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The study investigates why the universal scaling of Renyi entanglement entropy depends on the microscopic details of the entanglement cut and explores the universality classes of codimension-two Renyi defects at (2+1)d O(3) quantum critical points. **Main result.** The authors find that different entanglement cuts realize distinct Renyi defect universality classes (ordinary, special, and extraordinary) and provide evidence for a phase transition on the extraordinary defect as a function of the Renyi index n. **Method.** Large-scale quantum Monte Carlo simulations on n-sheeted replica manifolds are used to measure local observables and extract scaling dimensions via finite-size scaling analysis. **Model / system.** The study uses (2+1)d quantum spin models at the O(3) Wilson-Fisher quantum critical point, specifically the 1/2 columnar dimerized Heisenberg model and the spin-1/2 bilayer Heisenberg model. **Key observables.** Renyi-n expectation values, defect spin-spin correlation functions, and the defect Binder cumulant. **Important parameters / regimes.** Renyi index n, system size L, and the coupling ratio J'/J used to reach the critical point. **Assumptions / limitations.** Numerical access is restricted to positive integer n > 1 via the replica approach; the behavior of the special defect remains somewhat inconclusive due to subtle numerical signals. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows schematic entanglement cuts (ordinary, special, extraordinary); Figure 2 shows finite-size scaling for ordinary/special cuts; Figure 3 shows scaling for the extraordinary defect; Figure 4 shows the Binder cumulant crossing for the extraordinary cut. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of entanglement cut sensitivity, describes the quantum spin models and replica QMC method, presents results for different defect types (ordinary, special, extraordinary), discusses the phase transition in the extraordinary case, and concludes with implications for entanglement entropy scaling.
Abstract At a quantum critical point, the universal scaling behavior of Rényi entanglement entropy is controlled by the universality class of the codimension-two Rényi (or conical) defects in the infrared theory. In this work we perform a systematic study of critical correlations along Rényi defect lines in (2+1)d quantum spin models realizing quantum phase transitions described by the O(3) Wilson-Fisher universality class, using large-scale quantum Monte Carlo simulations. We present numerical evidence that, for a fixed Rényi index $n$, there exist multiple Rényi defect universality classes, with distinct critical exponents for the O(3) order parameter on the defect. These universality classes are realized by choosing microscopically different entanglement cuts in lattice models, which we classify as ordinary, special and extraordinary according to their relation to surface criticality. For the extraordinary entanglement cut, we further find evidence for a phase transition on the defect as a function of the Rényi index. Our results highlight the key role of defect universality classes in determining the universal scaling of Rényi entropy, and provide a framework for understanding the previously observed dependence of Rényi entropy scaling on microscopic lattice details.

Induced discommensurations in the lock-in transition of charge-density waves

Authors: Katsuhiko Inagaki, Satoshi Tanda
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00521v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 Frenkel-Kontorova 4/5

Summary. This paper uses simulated annealing to study the lock-in transition in charge-density waves. It finds that discommensurations are topologically protected and can drive a commensurate system into an incommensurate state, providing a theoretical basis for observed current-induced incommensuration in materials like o-TaS3.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Understanding the mechanism behind the emergence of discommensurations during the lock-in transition of charge-density waves (CDWs) and explaining current-induced incommensuration. **Main result.** The study demonstrates that discommensurations are topologically protected and can be excited in the commensurate state, driving the system toward an incommensurate state, which is consistent with experimental observations in o-TaS3. **Method.** Numerical optimization using simulated annealing based on the Metropolis algorithm to minimize the McMillan free energy functional. **Model / system.** The model uses McMillan's free energy functional for charge-density waves in quasi-one-dimensional conductors like o-TaS3 and K0.3MoO3. **Key observables.** CDW intensity, wave profiles, phase jumps, and free energy relative to the commensurate state. **Important parameters / regimes.** The parameter Y (representing the third-order Umklapp process), the critical value Yc, and the phase theta. **Assumptions / limitations.** The model assumes a second-order transition via the McMillan free energy, though the authors note that the Umklapp term might induce a first-order transition. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows the evolution of wave profiles during annealing; Figure 2 shows wave profiles for various Y values; Figure 3 shows profiles for different initial slopes; Figure 4 compares free energies of discommensurations and soliton-antisoliton pairs. **Paper structure.** The paper begins with a validation of the numerical method, followed by an analysis of wave profiles near the critical value, an investigation of discommensurations in the commensurate state, a discussion of topological protection and energy asymmetry, and finally a comparison with experimental results.
Abstract We studied McMillan's free energy of the lock-in transition in charge-density waves. The wave profiles near the critical value were obtained by numerical annealing. First, we demonstrated that our method reproduces the previous studies. The obtained wave profiles include discommensurations near the critical value. Then, we calculated possible wave profiles in the commensurate state. We found that discommensurations are able to be excited in the commensurate state, leading the system to turn out to an incommensurate state. We proposed that these wave profiles result from topological invariants. Moreover, excitation of the discommensurations is favorable for the direction to the original wavelength of the incommensurate state. This is attributed to the nature of McMillan's free energy. The current-induced incommensurations, which we discovered with the diffraction study of $o$-TaS$_3$ [Inagaki \textit{et al.}, J. Phys. Soc. Jpn. 77, 093708 (2008)], is consistent with this study.

Summary. This paper investigates the Cascaded Variational Quantum Eigensolver (CVQE), an algorithm designed to reduce communication between quantum and classical units. By analyzing probability distributions, the authors show how to optimize guiding-state parameters to maintain chemical accuracy and maximize signal-to-noise ratios on noisy quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. It provides practical insights into error mitigation and parameter tuning for VQE-like algorithms on NISQ devices, specifically showing how to balance adiabaticity and noise susceptibility.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Optimizing the selection of guiding states in the Cascaded Variational Quantum Eigensolver (CVQE) to achieve accurate many-electron ground-state solutions with minimal resource consumption and robustness against hardware noise. **Main result.** The study demonstrates that CVQE can achieve chemical accuracy even with single-step adiabatic preparation and that tuning the adiabatic energy scale (h-Omega) can maximize the signal-to-noise ratio on real NISQ hardware. **Method.** The authors use a trapezoidal-state preparation method for the CVQE algorithm, comparing noiseless simulations with IBM quantum hardware (ibm_aachen) and utilizing classical benchmarks like CCSD and FCI. **Model / system.** A prototypical bimolecular reaction (H2 + H2+ -> H3+ + H) modeled using a many-electron Hamiltonian and a 256-dimensional Fock space, mapped to 8 qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation. **Key observables.** Ground-state energies (Eg), reaction energies (Delta E), and probability distributions of the quantum state at various algorithm stages. **Important parameters / regimes.** Discretization steps (K), adiabatic energy scale (h-Omega), sampling scale (shot counts), and the adiabaticity condition (h-Omega K^-1 << h-omega_0 << h-Omega). **Assumptions / limitations.** The use of a minimal STO-6G basis set and the assumption that the classical optimization can compensate for distortions in the guiding state if the sampled Fock state set is representative. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows energy evolution via discretization; Figure 2 plots the minimal-energy path; Figure 3 shows molecular structures; Figure 8 shows noiseless probability distributions; Figure 9 and 10 illustrate how the guiding-state distribution and sampling counts change with the adiabatic energy scale on real hardware. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the CVQE algorithm, defines the trapezoidal-state preparation method, analyzes different parameter regimes (adiabaticity and sampling) on simulators, evaluates performance on real IBM hardware, and provides a symmetry-based justification for the Fock state distributions in the appendix.
Abstract The cascaded variational quantum eigensolver (CVQE) circumvents the need for iterative communication between the quantum and classical processing units that is necessary in the conventional VQE algorithm. While CVQE offers complete freedom to choose the guiding state as input, not all guiding states suffice for solution accuracy, as well as resource efficiency. Our work presents a process based on trapezoidal-state preparation for selecting guiding states that yield accurate many-electron ground-state solutions with minimal resource consumption. By analyzing the state probability distributions at different stages of the CVQE calculations, we determine the optimal guiding-state parameters for given resource constraints. We demonstrate the process by comparing electronic energies along the minimal-energy path for a prototypical bimolecular reaction, $\mathrm{H}_2 + \mathrm{H}_2^+ \rightarrow \mathrm{H}_3^+ + \mathrm{H}$, using Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) computing.

Toward Secure Multitenant Quantum Computing: Circuit Affinity, Crosstalk Patterns, and Grouping Strategies

Authors: Andrew Woods, Chi-Ren Shyu
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00118v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how running multiple quantum circuits simultaneously on the same hardware causes crosstalk that can degrade performance and compromise security. By characterizing which circuits are 'aggressive' or 'sensitive,' the authors propose a framework for a scheduler to pair compatible jobs to maximize system utility and stability.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The study addresses the security and performance risks introduced by inter-circuit crosstalk during multitenant execution in cloud-based quantum computing, specifically focusing on how concurrent jobs can lead to fault-injection or information leakage. **Main result.** The authors identify three classes of circuits (universally aggressive, universally sensitive, and cotenant-dependent) and demonstrate that while crosstalk patterns are transferable within hardware revisions, they are fundamentally different across different topologies like Heavy-Hex and Square lattices. **Method.** The researchers used a 'Sandwiched Baseline-Cotenant Calibration' strategy to isolate crosstalk from temporal drift and applied the Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) to quantify the consistency of interference patterns across seven IBM superconducting processors. **Model / system.** The experimental platform consists of seven IBM superconducting quantum processors, including various generations of the Heron (Heavy-Hex lattice) and Nighthawk (Square lattice) architectures. **Key observables.** Crosstalk Aggression (N), Crosstalk Sensitivity (S), Structural Similarity Index (SSIM), t-statistic of interference, and Bhattacharyya Coefficient for fidelity. **Important parameters / regimes.** Padding depth (p) for spatial isolation, circuit depth, 2-qubit gate ratio, and architectural topology (Heavy-Hex vs. Square). **Assumptions / limitations.** The study uses a pairwise approximation for circuit interactions, which may not fully capture the complexity of much higher-load multitenant scenarios. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows the experimental methodology and mapping flow; Figure 2 illustrates cumulative stress-tests with increasing circuit load; Figure 3 displays interaction scores across different machines; Figure 4 and 5 show SSIM scores indicating hardware transferability and architectural differences. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of multitenancy security, describes the hardware and circuit selection, details the experimental protocols for isolation and mapping, presents the classification of circuits and architectural similarity results, and concludes with implications for hardware-aware scheduling.
Abstract Multitenancy increases throughput and reduces costs in cloud-based quantum computing, but concurrent job execution introduces security risks through inter-circuit crosstalk. We characterize the structural predictability of these interference patterns across seven IBM superconducting processors, spanning Heron (r1-r3) and Nighthawk (r1) architectures and five different circuit types. We evaluate pairwise interactions, by applying the Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) and a structural $t$-statistic to the concurrent execution of five foundational quantum circuits (QAOA, Grover's, QPE, QFT, and ZZFeatureMap), we quantify behavioral consistency across disparate hardware. Our results identify three types of circuits: universally aggressive, universally sensitive, and cotenant-dependent circuits. Aggressive circuits, such as Grover's Algorithm, exhibit a statistically significant interference pattern, yielding a $t$-statistic range of $[1.37,2.61]$ relative to the standalone baselines across all tested pairings. Conversely, sensitive circuits, such as the Quantum Fourier Transform, demonstrate a disproportionate susceptibility to multitenant execution, showing high deviations from single-tenant computational behavior. We demonstrate that crosstalk signatures are highly consistent within architectural revisions--with intra-revision similarity reaching $0.77$ (Hr3) and $0.68$ (Hr2)--while inter-revision similarity drops to $0.43$. Furthermore, we identify a ``topological decoupling" between Heavy-Hex and square lattice systems, where structural similarity falls to $0.01$ between Heron r1 and Nighthawk r1. These findings provide an empirical foundation for hardware-aware schedulers to strategically pair jobs, maximizing system utilization while preserving computational integrity.

Atomic Interferometry with Spin-Orbit-Coupled Spin-1 Condensates

Authors: Renfei Zheng, Junying Wu, Josep Cabedo, Alessio Celi, Zhihao Lan, Weiping Zhang, Lu Zhou

Summary. This paper proposes a new type of atomic interferometry using spin-orbit-coupled spin-1 Bose gases. By tuning the Raman coupling, researchers can independently control spin interactions to create entanglement and use spatial density patterns as a new way to read out phase information. This approach allows for precision measurements that can surpass the standard quantum limit.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a highly controllable platform for nonlinear atom interferometry, offering a unique way to implement time-reversal (echo) protocols and exploit spin-momentum locking for new types of quantum-enhanced sensing.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Developing a flexible quantum interferometry scheme that overcomes the limitations of conventional spinor BECs, specifically the difficulty in independently controlling spin-mixing interactions and measurement strategies. **Main result.** The authors demonstrate that Raman-dressed spin-1 gases allow for enhanced metrological sensitivity beyond the standard quantum limit using both ground-state and excited-state quantum phase transitions, and provide a novel readout via spatial density modulations. **Method.** The study uses a three-mode truncation of the effective Hamiltonian, exact diagonalization, the Truncated Wigner Approximation (TWA) for quantum dynamics, and Gross-Pitaevskii Equation (GPE) simulations. **Model / system.** A Raman-dressed spin-1 Bose-Einstein condensate with synthetic spin-orbit coupling, described by an effective spinor Hamiltonian where the spin-mixing interaction strength can be tuned via Raman coupling. **Key observables.** Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), estimation precision, magnetization, quadrupolar operator, and spatial density modulations (density stripes). **Important parameters / regimes.** Raman coupling (Omega), linear and quadratic detuning (delta, epsilon), spin-mixing interaction strength (lambda), and the P, TF, and BA ground-state phases. **Assumptions / limitations.** The low-energy dynamics are assumed to be localized around the three minima of the dispersion branches (three-mode truncation), and TWA accuracy is noted to be limited to short time scales. **Figures summary.** Fig 1 shows the Raman coupling scheme, dispersion bands, and ground-state phase diagram; Fig 2 illustrates the sequential protocol; Fig 3 displays energy spectra, ESQPT lines, and QFI dynamics; Fig 4 demonstrates phase extraction via density stripe shifts. **Paper structure.** The paper begins by introducing the Raman-dressed platform and its effective Hamiltonian, moves to describing specific interferometric protocols (sequential and echo-type), analyzes metrological enhancement via ground-state and excited-state criticality, and concludes with a novel spatial readout strategy.
Abstract We propose and analyze a quantum interferometry scheme based on a Raman-dressed Bose gas with spin-orbit coupling. In this system, the atom-light coupling mixes spin and momentum degrees of freedom, giving rise, in the low-energy regime, to an effective spinor condensate whose spin-mixing interaction can be tuned independently of the atomic density. This controllability enables a separation between state preparation and phase imprinting, and provides a natural route to echo-type protocols based on effective time reversal. Within this framework, critical regimes of the effective spinor Hamiltonian can be used to generate entanglement and enhance interferometric sensitivity beyond the standard quantum limit. In addition, the spin-momentum locking of the dressed modes gives access to spatial density modulations that provide an alternative readout of the interferometric phase. In particular, phase information can be extracted from the displacement of spin-orbit-induced density stripes even when conventional spin observables are insensitive within the effective spinor description. Our results identify Raman-dressed spinor gases as a flexible platform for nonlinear atomic interferometry, combining controllable spin-mixing dynamics with spatially resolved phase readout.

Summary. The paper proposes a new all-optical saddle trap for levitated nanoparticles using structured light to reduce decoherence. This platform allows for advanced quantum tasks such as controlled wavefunction expansion and highly sensitive force detection in the zepto-Newton range.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel platform for studying open quantum systems by demonstrating how structured light can be engineered to suppress specific decoherence channels like photon recoil.

As an application, we show that this saddle-trap architecture enables force detection with sensitivity in the zepto-Newton regime.

Topological Charge of Causality at a PT-Symmetric Exceptional Point

Authors: Kejun Liu
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00117v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that causality in PT-symmetric systems is not just a binary property but carries a topological charge that changes at an exceptional point. It shows that while standard Kramers-Kronig relations fail in the broken phase, a corrected version can be derived. The study also reveals that the violation of standard KK relations is most intense at the phase transition threshold.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel topological interpretation of causality in non-Hermitian systems and introduces a model-independent diagnostic for identifying non-Hermitian gain through the breakdown of standard dispersion relations.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Investigating whether the failure of standard Kramers-Kronig relations in non-Hermitian systems can be described as a topological transition where causality carries a topological charge. **Main result.** The authors show that causality in a PT-symmetric dimer carries a topological charge (Blaschke winding number) that jumps from 0 to 1 at the exceptional point, and that the violation of standard KK relations scales with a negative exponent of approximately -1.08. **Method.** Analytical derivation using Heisenberg-Langevin equations, input-output relations, and the Blaschke winding number framework, supplemented by numerical verification of the residue-corrected dispersion relation. **Model / system.** A PT-symmetric open dimer coupled to a one-port waveguide, modeled by an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian with gain-loss parameter gamma and coupling kappa. **Key observables.** Blaschke winding number (Nb), KK residual (Delta_KK), and the scaling exponent (nu). **Important parameters / regimes.** Gain-loss strength (gamma), coupling strength (kappa), and external coupling/decay (gamma_ex). **Assumptions / limitations.** The system is treated within the Markov approximation using the Gardiner-Collett interaction; the scaling results are derived for a single-port geometry. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows the trajectory of the reflection coefficient pole in the complex plane and a phase diagram of the exceptional point; Figure 2 compares phase diagrams for different coupling geometries. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of KK violation, defines the PT-symmetric dimer model, derives the topological charge via the Blaschke winding number, presents the residue-corrected KK relation, analyzes the scaling of the KK residual, and proposes an experimental protocol.
Abstract Causality in linear response is conventionally treated as a binary property: a response function is either analytic in the upper half-plane or it is not. We show that in a PT-symmetric open dimer it instead carries a topological charge. As the gain-loss parameter crosses the exceptional point, a single pole of the reflection coefficient migrates into the upper half-plane, the Blaschke winding number jumps from 0 to 1, and standard Kramers-Kronig (KK) reconstruction acquires a Lorentzian residual fixed by the pole residue. The transition is sharp, protected by the codimension-one structure of the exceptional point, and directly measurable in a one-port reflection experiment. Most strikingly, the violation magnitude scales as Delta_KK ~ |gamma - gamma_c|^nu with nu ~ -1.08 in the single-port geometry: the breakdown of standard KK is strongest at threshold and weakens deeper in the broken phase. We derive the exact reflection coefficient, verify the residue-corrected dispersion relation, and propose a THz time-domain spectroscopy protocol that detects the topological charge through the residual itself.

Summary. The paper identifies that standard entropy production is not minimized by conservative forces in jump processes. By proposing a 'renormalized' version of entropy production, the authors restore the principle that conservative forces are the optimal drivers for a given time evolution. Finally, we demonstrate accurate long-range extrapolation, phase-diagram learning in many-body systems, and sample-efficient Bayesian optimization in a quantum sensing task. Our results identify quantum Gaussian processes as a promising route toward simpler and more structured forms of quantum learning.

Summary. This paper provides a complete structural characterization of quantum operations and semigroups that preserve gauge-invariant Gaussian states in finite Fermion systems. It proves that one-to-one operations are determined by specific symbol maps and shows that such operations can be naturally extended to the full CAR algebra. The results establish a precise correspondence between GIG semigroups and pairs of operators acting on the single-particle space.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a rigorous classification of noise processes (quantum operations) that preserve the Gaussian structure of Fermionic systems, which is a cornerstone of many-body physics and quantum information processing.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper aims to characterize the structure of quantum operations (CPTP maps) and semigroups on finite-dimensional Fermion systems that preserve the set of gauge-invariant Gaussian (GIG) states. **Main result.** The author provides a structure theorem for one-to-one quantum operations and semigroups, showing they are parameterized by pairs (G, A) where G is a contraction semigroup generator and 0 <= A <= -G - G*, and demonstrates how these operations extend to the full CAR algebra. **Method.** The study utilizes the algebraic framework of the Canonical Anti-commutation Relations (CAR), second quantization, symbol maps for Gaussian states, and tools from operator theory such as the Evans-Hugenholtz-Kadison construction and modular theory. **Model / system.** The system consists of finite-dimensional Fermion systems defined by a single-particle Hilbert space H1 and a corresponding Fock space K, governed by the CAR algebra and a gauge automorphism group. **Key observables.** The number operator (N), the symbol (Q) representing the covariance/two-point function, and the particle-hole automorphism. **Important parameters / regimes.** The dimension of the single-particle space, the contraction semigroup generator G, and the operator A satisfying the stability condition. **Assumptions / limitations.** The primary structure theorem assumes the quantum operations are one-to-one; the study is restricted to finite-dimensional Fermion systems. **Paper structure.** The paper begins by defining the algebraic framework of the CAR field and gauge structure, proceeds to classify GIG states via symbols, establishes structure theorems for one-to-one quantum operations and semigroups, and concludes with results on the extension of these maps to the full CAR algebra and the properties of conditional expectations.
Abstract Let ${\mathcal H}_1$ be a finite dimensional complex Hilbert space. Let $ψ\mapsto Z(ψ)$ be a canonical anti-commutation relations (CAR) field over ${\mathcal H}_1$ acting irreducibly on a Hilbert space ${\mathord{\mathscr K}}$. The $*$-algebra ${\mathscr A}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$ generated by the $Z(ψ)$, $ψ\in {\mathcal H}_1$, is simply all operators on ${\mathscr K}$. However, the CAR field endows ${\mathscr A}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$ with additional structure, and we are concerned with quantum operations whose acting in harmony with this structure. In particular, there is a gauge automorphism group generated by ``second quantizing'' $ψ\mapsto e^{it}ψ$. The fixed point algebra of the gauge group, ${\mathscr G}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$, is a sub-algebra of ${\mathscr A}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$ studied by Araki and Wyss. It contains the density matrices of an important class of states, the gauge invariant Gaussian states, ${\mathfrak S}_{GIG}$. Our focus is on semigroups $\{e^{t{\mathscr L}}\}_{t\geq 0}$ of quantum operations on ${\mathscr A}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$ that map ${\mathfrak S}_{GIG}$ into itself. Each $e^{t{\mathscr L}}$ is one-to-one, and our first main result is a structure theorem forsuch quantum operations on ${\mathscr G}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$ that map ${\mathfrak S}_{GIG}$ into itself. We apply this to study semigroups of quantum operations on ${\mathscr G}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$ that map ${\mathfrak S}_{GIG}$ into itself. Our second main result is a structure theorem showing that they are parameterized by pairs $(G,A)$ where $G$ is a contraction semigroup generator on ${\mathcal H}_1$, and $0 \leq A \leq -G -G^*$. We then show that each of these semigroups has a natural extension to the full CAR algebra ${\mathscr A}_{{\mathcal H}_1}$. Further results are obtained under further assumptions on the pair $(G,A)$.
