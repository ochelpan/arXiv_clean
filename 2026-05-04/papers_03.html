Comment on "Controlling the dynamical evolution of quantum coherence and quantum correlations in $e^{+} e^{-} \rightarrow Λ\barΛ$ processes at BESIII''

Authors: Saeed Haddadi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00127v1

Summary. This paper critiques a recent study on quantum correlations in hyperon-antihyperon production, arguing that the use of open quantum system theory is physically unjustified. The author shows that because the particles do not share an environment and cannot be subjected to controlled measurements, the reported dynamical evolution and steering are conceptually flawed. The work emphasizes the need to distinguish between mathematical quantifiers and physically realizable quantum information protocols.

Why it may be interesting. This paper provides a critical cautionary tale for researchers applying open quantum system formalisms (like non-Markovianity and steering) to high-energy physics systems where the necessary environmental coupling and measurement control are absent.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The author critiques the physical validity of applying open quantum system frameworks, such as correlated quantum channels and non-Markovian dynamics, to the hyperon-antihyperon (Lambda-antiLambda) system. **Main result.** The author demonstrates that the analyzed work is physically inconsistent because the produced particles are free, unstable, and do not interact with a shared environment, making the reported quantum correlations and steering operationally meaningless. **Method.** The author uses a theoretical critique of the underlying physical assumptions, specifically addressing the lack of a shared bath, the absence of information backflow, and the impossibility of real-time measurement-induced state updates in relativistic particle decays. **Model / system.** The system consists of a Lambda-antiLambda (Lambda-antiLambda) pair produced via the electron-positron annihilation process e+e- -> Lambda-antiLambda, treated as a bipartite spin system. **Key observables.** spin correlations, quantum coherence, entanglement, and quantum steering **Assumptions / limitations.** The author assumes that the particles are free, unstable, relativistic particles that do not interact with a common environment after production. **Paper structure.** The paper is structured as a series of four remarks and a main point, each addressing a specific physical inconsistency regarding correlated channels, Markovianity, quantum steering, and the hierarchy of correlations.
Abstract We critically examine recent claims [Phys. Rev. D 113, 016024 (2026)] regarding quantum coherence, steering, and non-Markovian dynamics in the hyperon-antihyperon system produced in the process $e^{+} e^{-} \rightarrow Λ\barΛ$. We argue that the theoretical framework employed in the analyzed work suffers from fundamental physical inconsistencies. In particular, the treatment of the $Λ\barΛ$ pair as a bipartite system evolving under correlated quantum channels is not physically justified, since the produced hyperons are free, unstable particles that do not interact with a common environment after production. Consequently, the application of open quantum system techniques, including Markovian and non-Markovian quantum channels, lacks a clear physical basis. Moreover, we show that the computation and interpretation of quantum steering for this system is operationally and conceptually meaningless, as no well-defined measurement-induced state update or controllable local measurement scenario exists for unstable relativistic particles. These issues call into question the physical relevance of the reported quantum correlations, their hierarchy, and their dynamical behavior. Our analysis highlights the necessity of carefully distinguishing between formal mathematical quantifiers of quantumness and physically realizable quantum information protocols in high-energy particle systems.

Graph theoretic derivation of mutual linearity for transient probabilities and hitting time distributions in Markov networks

Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00135v1

Summary. This paper provides a new graph-theoretic proof for the mutual linearity of transient probabilities and hitting time distributions in Markov networks. By using the all-minorable matrix-tree theorem, the authors derive explicit combinatorial formulas for how these distributions respond to changes in transition rates. The results bridge the gap between stationary and non-stationary dynamics and show that short-time responses are determined by graph geometry.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides fundamental mathematical constraints on the dynamics of stochastic networks, which is highly relevant for understanding the relaxation and response of open quantum systems and biochemical networks.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper seeks to establish a graph-theoretic derivation for the 'mutual linearity' property in Markov networks, extending it from the stationary regime to the non-stationary (transient) regime in Laplace space. **Main result.** The authors prove that mutual linearity applies to both transient occupation probabilities and hitting time distributions, providing explicit combinatorial expressions for non-stationary response ratios and susceptibilities. **Method.** The study employs the all-minors matrix-tree theorem, Laplace transforms, and combinatorial tools like spanning forests and trees on augmented graphs to derive the results. **Model / system.** The system consists of irreducible, time-homogeneous Markov networks defined by a continuous-time stochastic process on a directed, edge-weighted graph where dynamics are governed by the Kolmogorov forward equation. **Key observables.** Occupation probabilities, network currents, response ratios, susceptibility, and hitting time distributions. **Important parameters / regimes.** Transition rates (k_ij), Laplace variable (s), stationary regime (long-time limit), and short-time asymptotics (governed by minimal path distances). **Assumptions / limitations.** The Markov networks are assumed to be irreducible and time-homogeneous. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 visualizes graph theoretic concepts like directed graphs and spanning forests; Figure 5 illustrates an auxiliary map for edge exchange; Figure 6 shows short-time scaling of response ratios in an example network. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of mutual linearity, develops a graph-theoretic framework using the all-minors matrix-tree theorem, extends the derivation to the non-stationary regime in Laplace space, proves the extension to hitting time densities, and analyzes short-time and long-time limits.
Abstract For irreducible, time-homogeneous Markov networks, mutual linearity has recently been established for both occupation probabilities and network currents in the stationary regime as well as in the non-stationary regime in Laplace space. The derivation of this property for the stationary distribution utilized the Markov-chain tree theorem, which also allows for an explicit combinatorial expression of the response ratios under variation of a single transition rate. The extension of this result was proven at the trajectory level by employing the Doob-Meyer decomposition. By employing the all-minors matrix-tree theorem, we show that this property also follows from a graph theoretic formulation and derive explicit combinatorial expressions for the non-stationary response ratios. The stationary result follows as the long-time limit and we also show that the small-time asymptotics are entirely determined by minimal path distances in the underlying graph. Finally we use the graph theoretic approach to prove that mutual linearity also extends to hitting time densities.

Summary. This review provides a structured framework to evaluate the evidence for quantum effects in biological processes and the potential for biological structures to advance quantum technology. It categorizes research into three directions: quantum in biology, quantum for biology, and biology for quantum. The paper highlights the need for decisive experiments to distinguish true quantum functionality from classical or semiclassical alternatives.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a high-level synthesis of open quantum system dynamics, specifically how environmental noise, dissipation, and vibronic coupling interact with biological structures, which is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum biology.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper seeks to map the intersection of quantum science and biology across three dimensions: quantum in biology, quantum for biology, and biology for quantum, specifically evaluating whether quantum resources are functionally necessary for biological processes. **Main result.** The authors establish a structured evidence map and a qualitative 'evidence ladder' that categorizes biological phenomena from established quantum-chemical contributions (e.g., enzyme tunneling, radical-pair magnetoreception) to speculative proposals (e.g., microtubule quantum computing). **Method.** The review employs a structured narrative evidence map using a five-criteria analytical framework: central claim, invoked quantum resource, strength of evidence, competing classical alternatives, and required decisive benchmarks. **Model / system.** The paper covers a diverse range of biological and bio-inspired systems, including enzymatic hydrogen transfer, radical-pair spin chemistry in cryptochromes, photosynthetic excitonic dynamics, DNA replication, and biohybrid quantum platforms like DNA origami. **Key observables.** Kinetic isotope effects (KIE), temperature dependence, hyperfine/Zeeman interactions, vibrational wavepackets, spin-dependent dynamics, and quantum yield. **Important parameters / regimes.** Dephasing rates, vibronic coupling, tunneling probabilities, redox landscapes, and physiological conditions (temperature, pH, and noise). **Assumptions / limitations.** The review assumes that distinguishing true quantum coherence from vibrational or ensemble effects is a primary challenge and that the utility of quantum tools must be measured against strong classical baselines. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 presents a conceptual framework for evaluating the three review directions; Abstract Quantum science and biology now intersect in three complementary directions: quantum in biology, quantum for biology, and biology for quantum. This review provides a structured narrative evidence map of that interface rather than an exhaustive catalogue or formal systematic review. For each topic, we ask what the mechanistic or technological claim is, which quantum resource is invoked, what the strongest experiments and models establish, which classical alternatives or engineering confounds remain competitive, and what decisive tests or benchmarks would most strongly change confidence. The most mature quantum-in-biology cases remain mechanistically constrained tunneling in some enzymatic hydrogen-transfer reactions and radical-pair spin chemistry as a viable framework for magnetoreception, whereas several higher-visibility topics remain suggestive but unresolved under physiological conditions. In quantum for biology, the central issue is whether quantum-enabled tools improve biological inference relative to strong classical baselines under realistic calibration, dose, throughput, and uncertainty constraints. In biology for quantum, the strongest claims arise when biomolecular structure or self-assembly measurably improves fabrication, integration, or robustness in quantum devices. Summary tables in the Appendix provide a compact cross-map view of the current evidence, major confounds, and the experiments or benchmarks most likely to discriminate between competing explanations.

Suppressing spin qubit decoherence during shuttling via confinement modulation

Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00611v1

Summary. This paper proposes using confinement modulation to implement continuous dynamical decoupling for spin qubits during transport. By periodically changing the qubit's potential, the researchers show they can suppress low-frequency noise in germanium hole-spin systems. This provides a practical method for creating noise-resilient quantum links in large-scale quantum computers.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to open quantum systems researchers as it explores the control of decoherence in a driven-dissipative system using continuous dynamical decoupling and examines the interplay between control protocols and the spatial/temporal scales of environmental noise.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The goal is to suppress spin qubit decoherence caused by low-frequency magnetic and electric noise during the long-range shuttling of qubits in scalable quantum architectures. **Main result.** The study demonstrates that continuous dynamical decoupling (CDD) via confinement modulation (temporal or spatial breathing) can significantly enhance coherence by rotating the spin to mitigate noise, with the effectiveness depending on the noise correlation length. **Method.** The authors use the filter function formalism, Schrieffer-Wolff transformations, and the Magnus expansion to analyze the susceptibility of the driven two-level system to various noise sources. **Model / system.** The system consists of germanium (Ge) hole-spin qubits in a conveyor-mode shuttling architecture, modeled as a driven two-level system with strong spin-orbit interaction and a position-dependent g-tensor. **Key observables.** Average gate infidelity (I), transition probabilities, filter functions (F), and the ratio of infidelity without driving to infidelity with driving (I*/I). **Important parameters / regimes.** Rabi frequency (Omega), Larmor frequency (omega_q), driving frequency (omega_d), driving amplitude (gamma), shuttling velocity (v), and noise correlation length (l_c). **Assumptions / limitations.** The analysis assumes the orbital adiabatic limit, the weak spin-orbit interaction limit, and that the driving frequency is much smaller than the orbital splitting; it also assumes 1/f noise power spectral density. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 sketches the shuttling architecture and breathing protocols; Figure 2 shows heatmaps of infidelity ratios and examples of filter functions; Figure 6 illustrates the infidelity ratio as a function of shuttling velocity and driving amplitude. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the shuttling problem, defines the physical model of Ge hole-spin qubits, develops the effective Hamiltonian via unitary transformations, analyzes noise suppression using filter functions for both global and local noise, and concludes with numerical simulations of the protocols.
Abstract Reliable long-range qubit shuttling is a powerful tool for scalable quantum computing architectures. We investigate strategies to improve the coherence of moving spin qubits by performing continuous dynamical decoupling by modulating their confinement potential. Specifically, we introduce temporal and spatial breathing shuttling protocols that leverage spin-orbit interactions in hole-spin systems to electrically drive the qubit while moving. This enables efficient dressed-state shuttling, where the spin is continuously rotated during transport, suppressing the effect of low-frequency noise. Using the filter function formalism, we identify driving regimes that efficiently mitigate both global and local magnetic and electric noise sources. We find that confinement-modulated shuttling can significantly enhance coherence during transport, while revealing distinct limitations depending on the correlation length of the noise. Applying our framework to germanium hole-spin qubits, we show that these protocols provide a practical route toward noise-resilient long-range coherent quantum links.

Bell Correlations and Selection Bias

Authors: Huw Price
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00406v1

Summary. This paper proposes that the apparent nonlocality in quantum mechanics is actually a statistical artifact caused by selection bias. By treating Bell correlations as 'correlator bias,' the author suggests that local realism and special relativity can be preserved without needing to invoke spooky action at a distance.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper addresses the tension between Bell correlations and the principles of local realism and special relativity, questioning if these correlations necessitate nonlocality. **Main result.** The author proposes that Bell correlations are not evidence of fundamental nonlocality but are instead 'selection artefacts' or 'correlator bias' arising from conditioning on a specific variable. **Method.** The author uses a statistical framework of selection bias (specifically collider bias) and directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to re-interpret Bell's theorem. **Model / system.** The paper analyzes standard Bell-type experiments, including V-shaped (entangled pair preparation) and W-shaped (entanglement swapping) geometries, using classical and quantum toy models of selection bias. **Key observables.** Bell correlations (violations of Bell inequalities) and the factorizability of joint probabilities. **Important parameters / regimes.** The distinction between a 'super-ensemble' (full population) and a 'sub-ensemble' (selected subset) defined by a Correlator. **Assumptions / limitations.** The proposal does not provide a physical mechanism for the underlying correlations and relies on the assumption that a randomizer can model the unconstrained super-ensemble. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows WWII bomber survivorship bias; Figures 2 and 3 show DAGs for colliders and common causes; Figure 4 shows a Bell experiment setup; Figure 5 shows Bell states; Figure 6 illustrates entanglement swapping (W-shaped) geometry. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces selection bias, applies it to Bell's theorem via 'correlator bias,' develops classical and quantum toy models, extends the argument to entanglement swapping (W-shaped experiments), and discusses implications for time-symmetric causality.
Abstract Selection artefacts are common in science. A method of selecting samples from a larger population may produce bias, in either direction. It may induce correlations between variables independent in the full population, or mask correlations between variables dependent in the full population. Here we propose a surprising application of these familiar ideas. We argue that they are relevant to puzzling correlations uncovered in quantum theory by John Stewart Bell (Bell 1964). In the light of Bell's work and subsequent experiments it is widely believed that the quantum world is 'nonlocal', in apparent tension with relativity. Many hold that the only alternative is to abandon 'realism', the view that there is an objective world independent of measurement. We propose instead that Bell correlations are selection artefacts, in tension neither with relativity nor realism.

Escaping Mode Collapse in LLM Generation via Geometric Regulation

Authors: Xin Du, Kumiko Tanaka-Ishii
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00435v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · scars & prethermalization 2/5

Summary. This paper reinterprets LLM mode collapse as a geometric collapse of internal representation trajectories into low-dimensional attractors. It introduces RMR, a method to suppress persistent directions in the Transformer cache, effectively preventing repetitive generation. The approach maintains high text quality while enabling stable generation at much lower entropy rates.

Why it may be interesting. The paper uses tools from dynamical systems and statistical mechanics, such as Iterated Function Systems, phase transitions, and fractal dimensions, to analyze the stability and ergodicity of a high-dimensional stochastic process.

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Addressing mode collapse in Large Language Models (LLMs), where autoregressive generation enters repetitive, low-entropy loops or loses semantic diversity. **Main result.** The proposed Reinforced Mode Regulation (RMR) significantly increases non-collapse rates in LLMs (e.g., from 8% to 56% at temperature 0.7) without degrading text quality, by preventing the geometric collapse of internal trajectories. **Method.** A lightweight, online intervention called Reinforced Mode Regulation (RMR) that uses low-rank damping to attenuate dominant, self-reinforcing directions in the Transformer value cache identified via a generalized eigenvalue problem. **Model / system.** The system is modeled as an autoregressive Transformer-based LLM (e.g., Qwen, Llama) where the generation process is viewed as a stochastic trajectory in a high-dimensional state space (the KV cache), specifically using an Iterated Function System (IFS) framework to represent the dynamics. **Key observables.** Correlation dimension (fractal dimension of the trajectory), next-token entropy, Distinct-n, and generalized eigenvalues of the value-cache subspace. **Important parameters / regimes.** Temperature (T), damping strength (eta), eigenvalue threshold (lambda_min = 0.8), and entropy-locked/temperature-locked decoding regimes. **Assumptions / limitations.** The method assumes access to the internal Transformer KV cache and relies on the approximation that the high-dimensional vocabulary can be binned for efficient computation. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 visualizes trajectory transitions in an IFS; Figure 2 shows explicit looping examples; Figure 4 shows the widening spectral gap signaling collapse; Figure 6 illustrates non-looping degeneration (template/conceptual looping); Figure 8 and 9 show the stability of correlation dimension under RMR. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of mode collapse, proposes a dynamical-systems reinterpretation as geometric collapse, presents the RMR method and its efficient implementation, validates the method using correlation dimension as a diagnostic, and demonstrates performance gains across various LLMs and temperatures.
Abstract Mode collapse is a persistent challenge in generative modeling and appears in autoregressive text generation as behaviors ranging from explicit looping to gradual loss of diversity and premature trajectory convergence. We take a dynamical-systems view and reinterpret mode collapse as reduced state-space accessibility caused by *geometric collapse*: during generation, the model's internal trajectory becomes confined to a low-dimensional region of its representation space. This implies mode collapse is not purely a token-level phenomenon and cannot be reliably solved by symbolic constraints or probability-only decoding heuristics. Guided by this perspective, we propose *Reinforced Mode Regulation* (RMR), a lightweight, online state-space intervention that regulates dominant self-reinforcing directions in the Transformer value cache (implemented as low-rank damping). Across multiple large language models, RMR substantially reduces mode collapse and enables stable, high-quality generation at extremely low entropy rates (down to 0.8 nats/step), whereas standard decoding typically collapses near 2.0 nats/step.

Summary. This paper bridges the gap between tensor networks and knowledge compilation by proving they are mathematically equivalent representations of the same class of functions. This allows physicists to use advanced algorithmic tools from formal verification, such as variable reordering and canonical forms, to optimize tensor network computations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Establishing formal mathematical equivalences between the Tensor Network community (used in physics/ML) and the Tractable Circuits community (used in formal verification) to enable the transfer of algorithms and structural properties. **Main result.** The paper proves that Matrix Product States (Tensor Trains) are equivalent to non-deterministic edge-valued decision diagrams, and Tree Tensor Networks correspond to structured-decomposable circuits, allowing for bidirectional transfer of complexity results and heuristics. **Method.** The authors use the language of semi-rings to unify weights and develop constructive, polynomial-time transformation procedures between the two frameworks. **Model / system.** The paper focuses on mathematical data structures for representing pseudo-Boolean functions, specifically Matrix Product States (MPS), Tree Tensor Networks (TTN), and Edge-Valued Decision Diagrams (EVDD). **Assumptions / limitations.** The transformation from EVDD to TT assumes EVDDs are complete (all variables are read on every path), though this can be enforced efficiently. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 illustrates the structural mapping between a structured-decomposable circuit and a tree tensor network; Figure 2 demonstrates the functional equivalence between an nd-EVDD and a Tensor Train. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of isolated frameworks, defines the mathematical models for both sides using semi-rings, proves specific structural equivalences (TT to EVDD and TTN to circuits), provides complexity analysis for transformations, and discusses the implications for cross-domain utility like variable ordering and lower bounds.
Abstract Tensor networks and circuits are widely used data structures to represent pseudo-Boolean functions. These two formalisms have been studied primarily in separate communities, and this paper aims to establish equivalences between them. We show that some classes of tensor networks that are appealing in practice correspond to classes of circuits with specific properties that have been studied in knowledge compilation as \emph{tractable circuits}. In particular, we prove that matrix product states (tensor trains) coincide with nondeterministic edge-valued decision diagrams and that tree tensor networks exactly correspond to structured-decomposable circuits. These correspondences enable direct transfer of structural and algorithmic results; for example, canonicity and tractability guarantees known for circuits yield analogous guarantees for the associated tensor networks, and vice versa.

Comment on "Quantum teleportation, entanglement, LQU and LQFI in $e^{+} e^{-} \rightarrow \mathrm{Y} \overline{\mathrm{Y}}$ processes at BESIII through noisy channels''

Authors: Saeed Haddadi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00129v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a critique of applying quantum information theory to high-energy physics, arguing that metrics like teleportation fidelity and decoherence models lack physical meaning when applied to hyperon pairs. The author contends that these quantities should be viewed as descriptions of static production correlations rather than dynamical quantum resources or environmental effects.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** The paper critiques the physical validity of applying standard quantum information metrics and decoherence models to high-energy particle production processes. **Main result.** The author argues that using noise channels and teleportation fidelity to describe hyperon-antihyperon pairs is physically unjustified because there is no identifiable environment-system interaction and the particles cannot be used for operational quantum protocols. **Method.** A critical theoretical analysis of the physical correspondence between standard CPTP noise models (like amplitude damping) and the dynamics of relativistic, unstable particles produced in scattering events. **Model / system.** Hyperon-antihyperon (Y anti-Y) pairs produced via e+ e- annihilation at the BESIII experiment, characterized by a reconstructed spin density matrix. **Key observables.** Logarithmic negativity, Local Quantum Uncertainty (LQU), Local Quantum Fisher Information (LQFI), and teleportation fidelity. **Important parameters / regimes.** Abstract noise parameters used in the criticized study to model decoherence. **Assumptions / limitations.** The author assumes that the validity of quantum information measures depends on the existence of a physical environment and the ability to perform operational control, which is absent in this high-energy system. **Paper structure.** The paper presents a critique of a recent study by first identifying the mismatch between standard decoherence models and the physics of free-propagating unstable particles, then arguing against the operational interpretation of teleportation fidelity, and finally proposing a re-interpretation of information metrics as static production correlations.
Abstract We provide a critical assessment of a recent study applying quantum information concepts, including noisy channels and teleportation fidelity, to hyperon-antihyperon pairs produced in $e^{+}e^{-} \to Y\bar Y$ reactions at BESIII. While the spin density matrix reconstructed from experimental data provides a physically meaningful description of production correlations, we argue that its subsequent interpretation in terms of standard decoherence models-such as amplitude damping, phase damping, and phase flip-lacks a clear physical correspondence for these systems. The produced particles emerge from a single scattering event and propagate as free, unstable relativistic states, without a well-defined system-environment interaction acting on their spin degrees of freedom. As a result, the variation of quantum correlations with an abstract noise parameter does not describe a genuine physical evolution. We further contend that the reported teleportation fidelity should not be interpreted as evidence for operational quantum communication, since hyperon states cannot be prepared, controlled, or measured in a way that would enable a realizable teleportation protocol. More generally, quantities such as logarithmic negativity, local quantum uncertainty, and local quantum Fisher information primarily characterize static production correlations rather than directly usable quantum resources. Our analysis highlights the importance of distinguishing between formal quantum-information measures and their physical interpretation in high-energy particle systems.

Dynamical magnetotropic susceptibility as a new probe of Kitaev materials and beyond

Authors: João C. Inácio, J. Schwab, G. Rakhmanova, S. Safari, V. Zambra, H. Nasir, S. Paschen, K. A. Modic, Fakher F. Assaad, Toshihiro Sato

Summary. This paper introduces the dynamical magnetotropic susceptibility as a novel experimental probe for ultra-low-frequency fluctuations in quantum materials. It demonstrates how this probe can uniquely identify Kitaev physics and study charge fluctuations in metals. The work combines analytical derivation with advanced machine-learning-enhanced Monte Carlo simulations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure **Main problem.** Developing a theoretical and numerical framework to use dynamical magnetotropic susceptibility as a new probe for the low-energy, uniform (q=0) spin and charge fluctuations in Kitaev materials and other correlated systems. **Main result.** The study demonstrates that the magnetotropic susceptibility can identify Kitaev exchange dominance via a specific scaling law (k(0)/T = f(B/T)) and can distinguish between different metallic states and spin-orbit coupling effects. **Method.** The authors use linear response theory to derive the susceptibility and employ auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFQMC) with machine-learning-based optimization to mitigate the sign problem. **Model / system.** The study focuses on Kitaev materials like alpha-RuCl3, insulating spin systems, and 2D metallic systems (e.g., PdCoO2), using a generic correlated-electron Hamiltonian that includes spin and charge degrees of freedom. **Key observables.** Dynamical magnetotropic susceptibility k(omega), specifically its real part (frequency shift) and imaginary part (damping/energy absorption). **Important parameters / regimes.** Magnetic field (B), temperature (T), magnetic anisotropy, and exchange couplings (Kitaev, Heisenberg, and Gamma terms). **Assumptions / limitations.** The numerical approach is limited by the fermion sign problem at very low temperatures; the study assumes the weak-field limit for certain metallic models. **Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows the experimental cantilever setup; Figure 3 compares static susceptibility scaling for different parameter sets; Figure 4 shows the dynamical spectrum k''(omega); Figure 6 illustrates cantilever voltage suppression and a phase diagram in UTe2; Figure 7 and 8 show correlation ratios and spin susceptibility for the XXZ model. **Paper structure.** The paper introduces the magnetotropic susceptibility, derives its properties via linear response theory, applies it to insulating spin systems and metallic systems, performs numerical simulations on Kitaev models using ML-optimized AFQMC, and discusses broader applications to quantum criticality.
Abstract The magnetotropic susceptibility $k(ω)$ probes ultra-low-frequency uniform fluctuations. For a crystal mounted on an oscillating cantilever in a magnetic field, it is defined as the ratio of torque to angular-displacement amplitude. Its real and imaginary parts determine the oscillation-frequency shift and crystal-induced damping. It is a low-energy probe of uniform $q=0$ spin and charge degrees of freedom. We demonstrate this by deriving $k(ω)$ within linear response theory for a generic correlated-electron Hamiltonian with charge and spin degrees of freedom. Although it covers metallic and insulating magnets, correlated paramagnets, and exotic quantum critical points, we focus on limiting cases. For insulating spin systems $k(0)$ is sensitive to magnetic anisotropy whereas its finite-frequency imaginary part probes uniform dynamical spin susceptibility even in spin-symmetric models. For metallic systems we identify when eddy currents cause low-frequency damping. Our numerical results focus on Kitaev-material magnetotropic response. Using auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo with machine-learning-based sign-problem optimization we compute $k(ω)$ for several models proposed for $α$-RuCl$_3$. The observed low-temperature scaling of $k(0)/T$ with $B/T$ results from dominant Kitaev couplings: parameter sets without dominant Kitaev coupling do not exhibit this scaling. It remains robust upon inclusion of optical phonons. Beyond the static response, $k''(ω)$ for the $α$-RuCl$_3$ parameter set reproducing the experimental $k(0)$ data shows local-moment features at high and low $T$, with a single peak at the Larmor frequency. Beyond Kitaev systems we highlight broader applications. Probing ultra-low-energy uniform charge fluctuations is pertinent to Kondo destruction quantum criticality, of broad interest in strange metallicity and unconventional superconductivity.

The observed MR anomalies at the spin-flip transition are attributed to strain effects via magnetostriction upon the magnetic-structure change of the Co nets in NCTO, because similar MR jumps are observed in both Pt/NCTO and Cu/NCTO. Interestingly, we found that the high-field slopes of the MR spectra show opposite signs between Pt/NCTO and Cu/NCTO at low temperatures. Because the opposite signs of the high-field MR are prominent below the antiferromagnetic transition temperature of NCTO, the interaction between the interface spin accumulation and magnetization is likely to contribute to the MR effect in the induced ferromagnetic state.

Abstract Detailed numerical results obtained with state-of-the-art Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) simulations are presented for the superfluid phase of $^4$He at saturated vapor pressure. The aim of this contribution is that of providing reliable, up-to-date estimates for this archetypal superfluid, reflecting the methodological progress that has taken place over the past two decades.
