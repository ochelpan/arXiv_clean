Quantized Collective Fluctuations in Correlated Fermion Systems

Authors: S. S. Onuchin, Ya. S. Lyakhova, L. D. Silakov, A. N. Rubtsov
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00211v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces the Q-FLF method, an extension of the Fluctuating Local Field approach that incorporates quantum Matsubara modes to better describe collective excitations. It demonstrates that while low-frequency modes are sufficient for global thermodynamic quantities, higher-frequency modes are necessary for accurate single-particle spectra. This provides a selective tool for studying the impact of specific fluctuation channels in correlated systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient theoretical approach to quantify the specific contributions of individual bosonic Matsubara modes (quantum fluctuations) to the observable properties of correlated fermion systems.

Main result. The proposed Q-FLF method shows that low-frequency bosonic modes quantitatively impact global observables like total energy and susceptibility, whereas an accurate description of single-particle Green's functions requires higher-frequency modes.

Method. The Quantum Fluctuating Local Field (Q-FLF) method, which extends the classical FLF approach by incorporating selected bosonic Matsubara modes into a Gaussian ensemble of fluctuating external fields.

Model / system. A one-dimensional (1D) Hubbard chain at half-filling with hopping constant t and on-site Coulomb interaction U, studied in finite sizes (N = 4, 6, 8, 10, 12).

Key observables. Matsubara Green's function, total energy per site, and antiferromagnetic susceptibility (specifically the Curie constant).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (U/t = 1), inverse temperature (beta), and system size (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The bosonic spectrum is truncated to include only the zeroth and +/- 1 Matsubara modes; the system is modeled using a minimal approximation of small-size chains to neglect spatial inhomogeneities.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the dispersion law and Fermi level for N=4 and N=6; Figure 2 compares the Green's function for Q-FLF, FLF0, and ED; Figure 3 compares total energy per site; Figure 4 shows the temperature dependence of the Curie constant.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Q-FLF method, applies it to the 1D Hubbard model, evaluates its performance on global and single-particle observables, provides a diagrammatic interpretation of the expansion, and compares results against exact numerical benchmarks.

Abstract

Collective excitations in fermionic systems play a crucial role in determining their physical properties. An important challenge is to develop efficient theoretical approaches for describing these excitations and their coupling to fermionic degrees of freedom. In this work, we revisit the problem of quantifying the contributions of individual bosonic modes of collective fluctuations to observable properties of correlated fermion systems within the framework of the Fluctuating Local Field (FLF) method. Whereas the auxiliary field in this method was previously considered only classically, we formulate its systematic extension termed Quantum FLF (Q-FLF) that incorporates selected bosonic Matsubara modes, thus tailoring it to description of quantum collective fluctuations. As a testbed, we apply the approach to a half-filled one-dimensional Hubbard chain and compute the Green's function, the total energy, and the antiferromagnetic susceptibility. Our results demonstrate that the proposed scheme enables an efficient and selective characterization of the contributions of individual bosonic modes. In particular, low Matsubara frequencies are found to have a quantitative impact on integrated observables such as total energy and antiferromagnetic susceptibility. At the same time, an accurate description of single-particle properties requires inclusion of higher-frequency bosonic modes.

Quantum Data Loading for Carleman Linearized Systems: Application to the Lattice-Boltzmann Equation

Authors: Reuben Demirdjian, Thomas Hogancamp, Abeynaya Gnanasekaran, Amit Surana, Daniel Gunlycke
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00302v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new method for loading nonlinear fluid dynamics data onto a quantum computer by using Carleman linearization and a novel unitary embedding technique. It achieves polylogarithmic scaling with respect to the simulation grid size, making it a significant step toward efficient quantum simulation of complex classical fluids.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient quantum data loading strategy for simulating nonlinear dynamical systems, specifically addressing the high cost of decomposing Carleman-linearized matrices into a Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU).

Main result. The authors introduce an LCNU-to-LCU strategy that embeds non-unitary terms into unitaries without increasing the number of terms, providing a framework that scales polylogarithmically with spatial and temporal discretization points for the 3D Lattice-Boltzmann Equation.

Method. The method uses Carleman linearization to transform nonlinear PDEs into linear systems, a 'judicious zero padding' technique to handle non-uniform submatrix sizes, and a unitary completion strategy to implement the resulting operators via quantum circuits.

Model / system. The primary application is the 3-dimensional Lattice-Boltzmann Equation (LBE), a discretized model for fluid dynamics involving streaming and collision operators (linear, quadratic, and cubic) on a periodic domain.

Key observables. T gate cost, number of LCU terms (Ns), condition number scaling, and Reynolds/Mach numbers.

Important parameters / regimes. Carleman truncation order (alpha), number of discrete velocities (Q), spatial grid points (n), and the Reynolds and Mach numbers.

Assumptions / limitations. The number of discrete velocities is a power of two; the density is approximately one; and the accuracy of the simulation depends on the truncation order alpha.

Figures summary. Figure 4.1 and 4.2 show quantum circuit embeddings for linearized terms; Figure 4.3 shows the most expensive circuit for the linear collision term; Table 1 summarizes resource costs for different lattice types (D1Q3, D2Q9, D3Q15).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the LCNU-to-LCU strategy, develops a generalized framework for Carleman linearized systems, applies this to the 3D LBE, provides explicit circuit constructions and resource estimations, and concludes with a condition number stability analysis.

Abstract

Herein, we introduce a strategy to decompose an arbitrary square matrix into a linear combination of non-unitaries (LCNU) where each non-unitary term is embedded into a unitary matrix. The result is a linear combination of unitaries (LCU) with an equal number of terms as the LCNU. Using this approach, we construct a generalized LCU framework for any Carleman linearized autonomous dynamical system with a polynomial nonlinearity. This framework is then used to construct an LCU for the 3-dimensional Carleman linearized lattice Boltzmann equation (LBE) in which the number of terms scales like $N_s \sim \mathcal{O}(α^2 Q^2)$, where $α$ is the Carleman truncation order and $Q$ is the number of discrete velocities from the LBE. Importantly, $N_s$ is completely independent of both the number of temporal and spatial discretization points. Lastly, we provide an estimate of our LCNU strategy's T gate cost in conjunction with (1) PREP and SELECT block encoding oracles, and (2) the variational quantum linear solver. In the former, the T cost scales like $\mathcal{O}(α^3 Q^2 (\log_2 n)^2)$, where $n$ is the total number of spatial grid points across all dimensions. Next, the latter requires $N_s^2(\log_2 (2n_tn^α)+1)$ circuits per iteration, with a worst case T gate cost of $\mathcal{O}(α(\log_2 Qn)^2)$ among them. We, therefore, provide an efficient decomposition strategy useful for both fault-tolerant and variational approaches.

Quantum Decoding Algorithms: Quantum Speedups in Optimization

Authors: Jan Ljubas, Tim Byrnes
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00312v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces and reviews the Decoded Quantum Interferometry (DQI) algorithm, which leverages classical coding theory to solve optimization problems. It demonstrates a potential superpolynomial quantum speedup for specific polynomial intersection problems, offering a new paradigm for quantum advantage beyond standard Grover-like approaches.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving a scalable quantum advantage in solving combinatorial optimization problems, specifically the max-LINSAT and Optimal Polynomial Intersection (OPI) classes.

Main result. The paper demonstrates that the Decoded Quantum Interferometry (DQI) algorithm provides a superpolynomial quantum speedup for the OPI problem and achieves a higher expected constraint satisfaction rate compared to classical benchmarks like Prange's algorithm.

Method. The DQI algorithm uses a 7-step quantum circuit involving polynomial amplification, Dicke state preparation, and quantum Fourier transforms to transform optimization into a quantum decoding problem using coding theory.

Model / system. The framework utilizes finite fields (Galois fields) and linear codes (such as Reed-Solable and Reed-Muller) to model optimization constraints, specifically focusing on the max-LINSAT and OPI problem structures.

Key observables. Expected constraint satisfaction rate (s-bar), Hamming weight, and the performance gap between quantum and classical algorithms.

Important parameters / regimes. Field size (p), number of constraints (m), number of variables (n), polynomial degree (u), and the minimum distance (d) of the underlying codes.

Assumptions / limitations. The efficiency and orthonormality of the algorithm depend on the existence of efficient classical decoders and a sufficiently large minimum distance (d_perp) in the dual code.

Figures summary. Figure 1 visualizes the algorithm's foundation; Figure 2 shows a t-SNE visualization of decoding; Figure 3 compares classical vs. quantum optimization approaches; Figure 4 details the 7-step DQI circuit; Figure 5 plots satisfaction rate vs. problem scale; Figure 6 illustrates a bivariate mOPI instance.

Paper structure. The paper provides a self-contained review covering the mathematical foundations of Galois fields and coding theory, defines the max-LINSAT and OPI optimization problems, details the step-by-step DQI algorithm architecture, and concludes with performance comparisons and extensions to multivariate and non-diagonal Hamiltonians.

Abstract

Attaining a quantum speedup in solving practically useful optimization problems has been one of the holy grails in the field of quantum computing. While prior approaches have demonstrated speedups for certain structured problem classes, establishing a clear and scalable advantage on broadly useful practical optimization problems remains challenging. Recently, a new approach to solving the max-LINSAT class of optimization problems has emerged, called Decoded Quantum Interferometry (DQI). In DQI, a combination of techniques rooted in (classical) coding theory and interferometry are used to obtain the solution of max-LINSAT. In the special problem instance of the optimal polynomial intersection (OPI) problem, strong evidence exists to show that an superpolynomial speedup exists over the best classical methods in obtaining an approximate solution. In this review, we give a self-contained description of DQI and the necessary background to understand the algorithm. Specifically, we give the essentials of Galois fields, optimization problems such as max-LINSAT and OPI, and coding theory, followed by a step-by-step walkthrough of the quantum algorithm and its operating principle.

Quantum Interval Bound Propagation for Certified Training of Quantum Neural Networks

Authors: Emma Andrews, Nahyeon Kim, Prabhat Mishra
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00747v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new certified training routine called Quantum Interval Bound Propagation (QIBP) to make quantum neural networks robust against adversarial attacks. By propagating mathematical bounds through the quantum circuit, the method provides a formal guarantee that predictions will remain correct within a specified noise range. The study highlights that affine arithmetic is more effective than standard interval arithmetic for maintaining tight bounds in complex quantum models.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQCs) are vulnerable to adversarial attacks where small input perturbations cause incorrect predictions, and there is a lack of certified training methods to guarantee robustness in the quantum domain.

Main result. The authors propose Quantum Interval Bound Propagation (QIBP), demonstrating that using affine arithmetic provides tighter bounds and higher certified accuracy than interval arithmetic, and that margin loss improves decision boundary robustness.

Method. The method propagates upper and lower bounds of input intervals through the quantum circuit layers (feature map, ansatz, and measurement) using interval and affine arithmetic to establish worst-case logits.

Model / system. The study uses Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) consisting of $n$ qubits, featuring an amplitude or angle embedding feature map, parameterized rotation gates ($R_Y, R_X$), and entangling gates (CNOT).

Key observables. Logits (expectation values of the Pauli-Z operator), test accuracy, certified accuracy, and PGD (Projected Gradient Descent) accuracy.

Important parameters / regimes. Perturbation magnitude (epsilon), weighting hyperparameter (kappa), number of qubits, number of layers, and number of classes.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes model layers can be treated via interval or affine arithmetic (linearity/monotonicity of gates) and uses Chebyshev approximation for nonlinear transformations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows an adversarial attack on MNIST; Figure 2 shows the QML architecture; Figure 3 shows a specific two-layer ansatz; Figure 4 illustrates interval propagation; Figure 5 shows the QIBP framework; Figure 6 plots accuracy trends vs. qubit count.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of adversarial vulnerability in QML, presents the QIBP framework using interval and affine arithmetic, evaluates the method using various loss functions and architectures on MNIST-like datasets, and analyzes the impact of model complexity and hyperparameters.

Abstract

Quantum machine learning is a promising field for efficiently learning features of a dataset to perform a specified task, such as classification. Interval bound propagation (IBP) is a popular certified training method in classical machine learning, where the lower and upper bounds are tracked throughout the model. These bounds are used during training to ensure that the model is certified to predict the correct label even under adversarial perturbations. While IBP is successful in classical domain, there are limited certified training efforts in quantum domain. In this paper, we present quantum interval bound propagation (QIBP) to establish a certified training routine for quantum machine learning, certifying the accuracy of models under adversarial perturbations. We implement QIBP using both interval and affine arithmetic to explore the tradeoffs between the two implementations in terms of accuracy and other design considerations. Extensive evaluation demonstrates that the resulting certified trained models have robust decision boundaries, guaranteed to predict the correct class for the samples within the trained adversarial robustness bounds.

Quantum Simulation of Differential-Algebraic Equations with Applications to Unsteady Stokes Flow

Authors: Hsuan-Cheng Wu, Xiantao Li
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00794v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new quantum algorithm for simulating constrained dynamical systems like the Stokes equations by embedding them into a larger, unitary-compatible Hilbert space. It shows that by using quantum Zeno dynamics and low-energy subspace techniques, one can potentially achieve better scaling with respect to mesh refinement than classical projection methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient quantum algorithmic framework for simulating constrained linear Differential-Algebraic Equations (DAEs), specifically addressing the non-Hermitian nature and the mesh-dependent scaling of incompressibility constraints in fluid dynamics.

Main result. The authors establish a dilation framework that maps DAEs to a projected Schrödinger-type dynamics, demonstrating a potential asymptotic advantage in the time-evolution scaling (h^{-5/2} vs h^{-3} in 2D) for the unsteady Stokes equations.

Method. The framework utilizes unitary dilation, Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT) for projector construction, and block-encoding, while leveraging the Quantum Zeno effect and low-energy subspace simulation.

Model / system. The primary application is the unsteady incompressible Stokes flow, modeled as a constrained DAE where the velocity field evolves via a differential equation and pressure acts as a Lagrange multiplier to enforce the divergence-free constraint.

Key observables. Velocity and pressure fields of the fluid flow.

Important parameters / regimes. Spatial mesh size (h), simulation time (t), precision (epsilon), and the spectral gap of the constraint operator.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the constraint operator has full row rank, the existence of a spectral gap in the constraint matrix, and the availability of sparse block-encodings for the differential operators.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a commutative diagram for dilation/reduction equivalence; Figure 2 compares error between dilation and ancilla refreshment; Figure 3 and 4 display the reconstructed velocity and pressure fields on a 32x32 grid.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the DAE problem, develops a mathematical dilation framework using Quantum Zeno dynamics, applies this to the discretized Stokes equations, provides a complexity analysis for the simulation, and concludes with a comparison to classical methods.

Abstract

Differential-algebraic equations (DAEs) arise naturally in constrained dynamical systems, but their algebraic constraints and hidden compatibility conditions make them more subtle than standard ordinary differential equations. This paper initiates a quantum-algorithmic study of constrained linear DAEs. We introduce a dilation framework that embeds the generally non-Hermitian constrained evolution into a projected Schrödinger-type dynamics on an enlarged Hilbert space, \[ i\frac{d}{dt}Ψ(t)=P\tH PΨ(t), \] where $\tH$ is Hermitian and $P$ is the orthogonal projector onto the lifted constraint subspace. This identifies the DAE evolution with a quantum Zeno-type dynamics and enables the use of block encodings, QSVT-based projector construction, and Hamiltonian simulation. We apply the framework to structure-preserving discretizations of the unsteady Stokes equations, where the pressure enforces the discrete incompressibility constraint. We derive the corresponding projected Hamiltonian formulation, identify low-energy spectral cutoffs motivated by solution smoothness, and discuss the resulting quantum simulation cost in comparison with classical projection-type methods. The results provide a first step toward understanding the potential intersection of quantum algorithms, DAEs, and constrained PDE dynamics.

Quantum simulation of nanographenes and Trotter error cancellation

Authors: Andreas Juul Bay-Smidt, Nina Glaser, Marcel D. Fabian, Earl T. Campbell, Nick S. Blunt, Gemma C. Solomon
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00745v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that for quantum simulations of nanographenes, the Trotter error in energy gaps is much smaller than the error in absolute energies. This error cancellation allows for significantly shallower quantum circuits when calculating chemically relevant excitation energies. The work provides a roadmap for using scalable, intermediate-scale problems to bridge the gap toward large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a critical insight into how application-specific error analysis (focusing on energy gaps rather than absolute energies) can drastically lower the hardware threshold for practical quantum chemistry simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The massive resource requirements for fault-tolerant quantum simulation of molecules and materials, specifically the overestimation of Trotter errors when using worst-case analysis for chemical applications.

Main result. Discovery of a Trotter error cancellation phenomenon where errors in energy gaps are significantly smaller than absolute energy errors, allowing for an order-of-magnitude reduction in circuit depth for Quantum Phase Estimation.

Method. A combination of Trotterized quantum simulation analysis, tensor-network-based spectral analysis, and Time-Dependent Density Matrix Renormalization Group (TD-DMRG) to evaluate various error types.

Model / system. The Pariser-Parr-Pople (PPP) model applied to the pi-systems of nanographenes, including acenes (1D), rhombenes (2D), and triangulenes (2D).

Key observables. Energy gaps (vertical excitation energies), absolute energy eigenvalues, and Trotter error types (worst-case, average-case, energy, and gap errors).

Important parameters / regimes. Chemical accuracy (approx. 0.04354 eV), Trotter time step (t), and system size up to 140 spin orbitals.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the second-order split-operator Trotter scheme and the semi-empirical PPP model; it assumes high overlap between true and effective eigenstates.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate energy diagrams and nanographene structures, plots of various Trotter error types and their scaling with system size, and comparisons between exact and effective energies/gaps.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalability problem, proposes nanographenes as a bridge to large-scale FTQC, performs a rigorous multi-type error analysis, demonstrates error cancellation in energy gaps, and concludes with resource estimation for QPE.

Abstract

Fault-tolerant quantum computing is a promising tool for simulating molecules and materials, but frequently-considered applications require substantial resources, and the gap between hardware capabilities and requirements remains significant. We propose quantum simulation of nanographene $π$-systems as relevant and scalable problems to span the gap between early and large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computing. We examine the efficiency of Trotterized quantum simulation, present a detailed analysis of worst-case, average-case and energy eigenvalue Trotter errors, and show that these Trotter error estimates vary by orders of magnitude. Trotter eigenvalue errors are obtained from a novel tensor-network-based approach which allows spectral analysis of product formulas for systems beyond brute-force calculation. Notably, we observe a Trotter error cancellation phenomenon whereby the Trotter error for energy differences between low-lying eigenstates is significantly smaller than the Trotter error for absolute energies, resulting in approximately an order of magnitude circuit depth reduction for quantum phase estimation calculation of energy gaps. This is a significant result because for most chemical applications, only energy differences are of practical relevance. We estimate that calculation of energy gaps to chemical accuracy between the ground- and excited-states within the Pariser--Parr--Pople model for large 2D nanographenes (up to 140 spin orbitals) requires circuits with $< 3.2 \times 10^7$ Toffoli gates. This work shows that considering details of chemically-relevant applications and exploiting error cancellation can lead to substantial reductions in resource requirements.

Reconstruction of spin structures from topological charge distributions via generative neural network systems

Authors: Kyra H. M. Klos, Jan Disselhoff, Michael Wand, Karin Everschor-Sitte, Friederike Schmid
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00732v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a generative neural network approach to 'back-map' macroscopic topological defect patterns to their underlying microscopic spin configurations. By incorporating physical constraints and Fourier-space information, the model accurately captures essential thermodynamic properties and long-range correlations in the 2D XY model. It demonstrates how machine learning can bridge the gap between multiscale topological features and microscopic physics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of reconstructing microscopic spin configurations from macroscopic topological charge distributions (vortices) using generative models.

Main result. A physics-informed conditional Wasserstein GAN (cWGAN) successfully reconstructs spin configurations that accurately reproduce magnetization, susceptibility, and spin-spin correlations, though it struggles with higher-order energy fluctuations like specific heat.

Method. An extended WGAN architecture using a U-Net generator with AdaIN for temperature embedding and a dual-critic system (real-space and Fourier-space) to enforce physical and spectral constraints.

Model / system. The two-dimensional (2D) XY model on a square lattice, characterized by the Berezinskii-Kosterlitz-Thouless (BKT) phase transition and topological defects (vortex-antivortex pairs).

Key observables. Magnetization, magnetic susceptibility, helicity modulus (spin stiffness), specific heat, spin-spin correlation functions, and topological descriptors (Minkowski functionals, Betti numbers, and persistence diagrams).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), defect-pair distance (d_nu), winding number distribution (y), and lattice size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The continuum approximation for energy is valid only outside defect cores; the model is tested primarily in the regime below the BKT transition.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the vortex-antivortex structure, the construction of cubical complexes and persistence images, the U-Net and critic architectures, training progress of thermodynamic observables, and comparisons of generated vs. simulated physical and topological metrics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the multiscale reconstruction problem, describes the 2D XY model and the cWGAN architecture (including Fourier-space critics), details the topological data analysis (TDA) pipeline, presents validation results via thermodynamic and geometric observables, and discusses limitations regarding energy fluctuations and critical behavior.

Abstract

Localized topological defects inherently possess a multiscale character. While their microstructure configuration depends on the specific physical system, their topological features and mutual interactions can be described on the macroscale in terms of a particle representation. However, determining the physical properties associated with a given defect pattern often requires knowledge of the underlying microscopic structure. In this work, we extend a Wasserstein generative adversarial neural network by incorporating physical constraints and Fourier-space information to generate microscopic spin configurations consistent with prescribed macroscopic patterns and thermodynamic parameters. Using the two-dimensional XY model as a test case, where vortex-antivortex pairs act as long-range interacting defects, we show that the model generates spin configurations that accurately reproduce magnetization, susceptibility, helicity modulus, and spin-spin correlations over a wide range of temperatures below the Kosterlitz-Thouless transition. At the same time, deviations in the specific heat reveal limitations in reproducing higher order energy fluctuations. A complementary analysis based on topological data analysis uncovers subtle differences in global spin-correlation structures at near critical temperatures that are not apparent from conventional correlation functions alone. These results demonstrate both the promise and current limitations of generative approaches for multiscale studies of defect-dominated spin systems and at the same time highlight topological methods as valuable tools for characterizing critical behavior.

Signatures of time-reversal-symmetry breaking in multiband 2H-TaS2 revealed by zero-field Josephson nonreciprocity

Authors: Daniel Margineda, David Caldevilla-Asenjo, Yuriy Yerin, Covadonga Álvarez-García, Andrei Mazanik, Maxim Ilyn, Celia Rogero, Luis E. Hueso, F. Sebastian Bergeret, Marco Gobbi

Summary. The researchers demonstrate a zero-field Josephson diode effect in TaS2/NbSe2 van der Waals junctions, providing evidence for intrinsic time-reversal-symmetry breaking. By ruling out extrinsic factors through intercalation and homojunction controls, they attribute the nonreciprocity to a multiband superconducting state with an anomalous phase difference. This work highlights how interband scattering can drive complex, non-sinusoidal current-phase relations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether the observed zero-field Josephson diode effect in 2H-TaS2/2H-NbSe2 heterostructures arises from intrinsic time-reversal-symmetry breaking (TRSB) or extrinsic mechanisms like strain or interface effects.

Main result. The study identifies the zero-field Josephson nonreciprocity as an intrinsic signature of a multiband superconducting state in TaS2, where interband scattering stabilizes a TRSB s+is' state.

Method. Experimental measurements of switching-current statistics and Hall transport in van der Waals junctions are combined with a theoretical framework using the quasiclassical Usadel equations.

Model / system. The system consists of 2H-TaS2/2H-NbSe2 van der Waals Josephson junctions. The theoretical model describes a multiband superconductor with an anomalous interband phase difference driven by interband scattering.

Key observables. Diode efficiency (eta), switching-current probability distributions (SCPD), critical currents (Ic+ and Ic-), and nonlinear Hall transport.

Important parameters / regimes. Interband scattering amplitude (W_GammaK), anomalous phase difference (phi), and the Stewart-McCumber parameter (beta_c).

Assumptions / limitations. The theoretical model assumes a minimal framework in the dirty limit (Usadel equations) and the short-junction limit, treating the junction as quasi-one-dimensional.

Figures summary. Figures show the temperature evolution of diode efficiency, switching-current statistics, evidence of reciprocal transport in homojunctions, tests ruling out extrinsic magnetic/vortex effects, and theoretical calculations of efficiency vs. interband scattering and phase.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of TRSB in spin-singlet superconductors, presents experimental evidence of the Josephson diode effect in TaS2/NbSe2 junctions, uses control experiments (homojunctions and intercalation) to rule out extrinsic causes, provides a theoretical model based on interband scattering, and concludes with the identification of an intrinsic multiband TRSB state.

Abstract

Superconductors that spontaneously break time-reversal symmetry host complex order parameters and are widely regarded as a hallmark of unconventional superconductivity. Whether such symmetry breaking can also arise in superconductors with nominally isotropic spin-singlet pairing remains an open question. Here we report a zero-field Josephson diode effect in noncentrosymmetric 2H-TaS2/2H-NbSe2 van der Waals junctions. The diode efficiency shows no systematic correlation with supercurrent amplitude, TaS2 thickness, or normal-state resistance, arguing against simple extrinsic, purely interfacial, or transparency-driven mechanisms. Time-reversal-symmetric scenarios are further tested using symmetry-controlled and molecule-intercalated control devices, in which the nonreciprocal response is absent or strongly reduced. Normal-state Hall transport in TaS2 exhibits a nonlinear response consistent with multiband correlated electronic states. Within a Josephson framework, our modelling shows that interband scattering acts as a phase-locking mechanism generating an intrinsic anomalous phase difference and a nonsinusoidal asymmetric current-phase relation, leading to finite zero-field rectification. Together, zero-field Josephson nonreciprocity and nonlinear Hall transport provide complementary evidence for a multiband superconducting phase structure in 2H-TaS2, consistent with intrinsic time-reversal-symmetry breaking.

Statistical mechanics for Scrabble predicts strategy, entropy and language

Authors: Olivier Witteveen, Marianne Bauer
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.00813v1

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper applies the maximum entropy principle and Ising-like models to analyze the structural patterns of Scrabble boards. It demonstrates that a simple pairwise model can capture complex geometric features and even identify the underlying language of a game based solely on tile placement patterns.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining if a statistical mechanics framework can describe the patterns, strategies, and linguistic features of Scrabble board configurations and distinguish between different languages.

Main result. A pairwise maximum entropy model effectively captures Scrabble board statistics, including higher-order correlations and geometric features, and can correctly identify the source language of a board configuration without knowing the specific letters.

Method. The authors use a maximum entropy approach with a pseudolikelihood method for parameter estimation and thermodynamic integration to calculate entropy differences.

Model / system. The system is a Scrabble graph represented as a 15x15 square lattice of spins (sigma_i in {-1, +1}). The model is an Ising-like Hamiltonian: E(sigma) = -sum J_ij * sigma_i * sigma_j - sum h_i * sigma_i.

Key observables. Mean square occupation, pairwise correlations, word-length statistics, geometric features (radius of gyration, perimeter-to-area ratio, number of holes), and Jensen-Shannon divergence.

Important parameters / regimes. External fields (h_i) representing spatial bias and pairwise interactions (J_ij) representing word placement constraints.

Assumptions / limitations. The configuration space is restricted to connected graphs with a permissible number of tiles; the posterior calculation assumes all languages are initially equiprobable.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Scrabble board and spin representation; Figure 2 compares predicted vs. observed occupation and correlations; Figure 3 displays geometric observables and covariance matrices across languages; Figure 4 illustrates the language decoding framework and confusion matrix.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Scrabble graph system, defines the pairwise maximum entropy model, demonstrates model accuracy using occupation and correlation data, extends the analysis to geometric and linguistic features, and concludes with a Bayesian framework for language identification.

Abstract

The crossword-like patterns of tiles in Scrabble form connected graphs of occupied sites on a square lattice. We find the most structureless description that reproduces means and covariances observed in real Scrabble games by adapting a maximum entropy approach to connected graphs. This pairwise model captures the data well, and predicts word-length statistics and geometric features of the Scrabble graphs correctly; in addition, the parameters of this model are interpretable and allow us to understand Scrabble playing strategies. Using this pairwise model, we calculate entropy differences and distinguishability of Scrabble graphs across languages, without having access to the letters on the tiles. Notably, we find that the entropy is predicted better by strategic gameplay -- such as word length on the board -- than lexicon size. Finally, we find that we can use the pairwise model to correctly assign Scrabble graphs to languages, avoiding explicit feature selection and at relatively low computational cost.

Abstract

While vertical GaN-on-silicon architectures promise a transformative leap in cost-effective power electronics and high-resolution micro-LEDs, their deployment remains bottlenecked by the high electrical resistance of conventional epitaxial buffer layers. Here, a universal and straightforward sputtering-based strategy is presented to realize high quality GaN epitaxial films on Si(111) substrates characterized by exceptionally low vertical resistance, ohmic behavior, and robust thermal stability. Abstract

Fabrication process-induced performance variability remains a formidable barrier in the high-volume manufacturing of semiconductor chips. With skyrocketing Artificial Intelligence (AI) workload, demand for non-volatile and computational memories is growing exponentially. As embedded non-volatile memory, ferroelectric Hf0.5Zr0.5O2 emerged as a strong candidate due to their CMOS back-end-of-line (BEOL) compatibility, scalability and high performance. However, their sensitive crystallization kinetics leads to significant device-to-device (D2D) non-uniformity leading to unpredictability of performance over wafer scale. In this work, we demonstrate unsupervised machine learning can analyze intra-die D2D variations and predict performance of "unseen" dies efficiently. We present a framework utilizing Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and K-Means clustering to analyze D2D performance variations in HZO capacitors and building on data from multiple dies, we move beyond traditional descriptive statistics to a predictive "Virtual Metrology" approach that separates performance categories, defined by key parameters like remanent polarization (Pr) and coercive voltage (Vc). The analysis further extends to comparing uniformity across different dies across the wafer showing the proposed methodology can accurately predict device performance on untested dies with a low Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) in the range of 5-10%, suggesting a robust path for accelerated yield improvement and reduced metrology overhead.
