Charge density waves and enhanced superconductivity in the electron-hole gas: a plausible, simple, physically intuitive model

Summary. This theoretical work models an electron-hole gas to propose a unified mechanism explaining high-$T_c$ superconductivity, CDWs, and anomalous resistivity. By analyzing the system's response function $\Delta$, the authors show that instabilities inherent to the two-component plasma simultaneously enhance pairing attraction and drive charge ordering. The model provides quantitative estimates for $T_c$ approaching room temperature under specific mass ratio conditions.

Why it may be interesting. Although focused on condensed matter correlations, the methodology of linking multiple emergent phenomena (superconductivity, charge order) through a single mathematical instability parameter $\Delta$ is highly relevant for understanding complex many-body phase diagrams in quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates a potential common origin for high-temperature superconductivity, charge density waves (CDWs), and anomalous $T^2$ electrical resistivity in an electron-hole gas.

Main result. A single underlying instability mechanism within the two-component Coulomb gas simultaneously enhances all three phenomena, leading to crude estimates of room-temperature superconductivity ($T_c \approx 275 ext{ K}$ at $M/m=9$).

Method. The analysis uses linear response theory applied to a simple, physically motivated model involving electron and hole interactions via Coulomb potentials.

Model / system. The system is a neutral degenerate plasma of equal numbers of electrons and positively charged fermions (holes), interacting purely via the Coulomb potential. The behavior is governed by a single diagnostic function $\Delta(q)$, which signals instabilities.

Key observables. Superconducting transition temperature ($T_c$), charge density wave instability wave vector ($q$), compressibility instability ($q=0$), and effective electron-electron interaction strength ($\lambda$).

Important parameters / regimes. Mass ratio ($M/m$) and reduced Wigner-Seitz radius ($r_s$); the critical value $M/m = 4.97$ marks a triple point of instabilities.

Assumptions / limitations. Key assumptions include approximating electron-hole correlation energy independence from density, assuming local field factors are those of independent uniform gases, and setting certain off-diagonal correlation terms to zero ($G_{12}=0$).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the coexistence of instabilities (triple point at $M/m=4.97$), the strong enhancement of attractive interactions near instability points, and plots showing $T_c$ dependence on $M/m$.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by establishing the model using linear response theory, identifying the key diagnostic function $\Delta$, demonstrating how its zeros trigger CDW or compressibility instabilities, and finally applying these insights to estimate superconducting parameters ($\lambda, T_c$).

Abstract

A neutral degenerate plasma of equal numbers of electrons and positively charged fermions (holes) -- the electron-hole gas -- is studied using a simple, physically motivated two-parameter model (density r_s and mass ratio M/m). Two simplifying assumptions are made but not proven: the electron-hole correlation energy is approximately independent of density and does not affect the pressure or bulk modulus, and the electron-hole contribution to the local field factors is zero, allowing use of the known electron-gas local field factors. With these assumptions the phase diagram, instabilities, and effective interactions can be calculated for all mass ratios and densities using simple formulae, reproducible on a laptop. The key new physics is the additional screening provided by the mobile holes. A single function Delta appears in the denominator of all effective interactions and response functions. Near the zeros of Delta at q = 0 (compressibility instability) and at finite q, three phenomena are simultaneously enhanced: charge density waves (for M/m >= 4.97), a large T^2 electrical resistivity from electron-hole scattering, and an attractive electron-electron interaction that is the purely electronic analog of BCS electron-phonon coupling. Crudely estimated superconducting transition temperatures approach room temperature, reaching approximately 275 K at M/m = 9. No claim is made that this model applies to any specific material. The aim is to provoke scrutiny of the assumptions, calculation of the unknown local field factors, and investigation of whether this minimal two-carrier framework maps onto real systems.

Summary. This paper theoretically analyzes how nonlinearity influences the transition between regular and chaotic motion in optomechanical oscillators driven by modulated light. By mapping out phase space using Lyapunov exponents and Poincaré sections, the authors demonstrate that nonlinearity does not monotonically enhance chaos. The findings suggest controlling these systems near the edge of chaos for applications like sensing or random number generation.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant as it applies nonlinear dynamics tools to a quantum optics platform (optomechanics), providing insights into chaos control and the engineering of non-classical states or noise sources using engineered potentials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the transition between regular and chaotic dynamics in nonlinear optomechanical systems subjected to modulated driving fields.

Main result. The system exhibits a non-monotonic dependence of chaos on nonlinearity order, and specific configurations (like membranes) show clear transitions from quasi-periodic motion to chaos, suggesting potential applications for sensing or random number generation.

Method. The analysis employs advanced tools from dynamical systems theory, including constructing bifurcation diagrams, calculating the largest Lyapunov exponent, and analyzing Poincaré sections.

Model / system. The model is an optomechanical system involving a mechanical resonator coupled to an optical field. The dynamics are governed by nonlinear interactions (linear, quadratic, cubic) between photons and mechanical displacements.

Key observables. Bifurcation diagrams ($\epsilon$ vs. dynamic regime), Largest Lyapunov exponent ($\lambda_{max}$), Power spectra, Phase portraits, and Poincaré sections.

Important parameters / regimes. Modulation amplitude/parameter ($\epsilon$), modulation frequency ($\Omega$), and the coupling strengths (linear $g$, quadratic $g'$, cubic $g''$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis often relies on the adiabatic elimination of the optical field variables, assuming the cavity decay rate is much larger than the mechanical resonance frequency.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate transitions between regimes: quasi-periodic motion (structured Poincaré points/discrete spectrum) vs. chaotic motion (randomly distributed points/continuous spectrum), particularly in membrane systems showing interwell chaos.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the dynamics by varying nonlinearity orders and control parameters ($\epsilon$), comparing results from different coupling terms, and detailing specific physical realizations like the membrane-in-the-middle configuration.

Abstract

The nonlinear dynamics of a mechanical resonator in an optomechanical system with linear, quadratic and cubic photon-vibration interactions (with respect to mechanical displacements) in a modulated driving field under conditions of adiabatic elimination of the optical field is studied. Based on the constructed bifurcation diagrams of the mechanical coordinate and the largest Lyapunov exponent as a function of the modulation amplitude, as well as power spectra, phase portraits and Poincare sections, regions of regular and chaotic dynamics of the optomechanical system are identified. It is also shown that for a certain modulation amplitude in the presence of all three types of interactions, chaotic dynamics of the mechanical resonator (oscillator) is realized, which is replaced by quasi-periodic oscillations in the absence of cubic interaction, and the system returns to chaotic behavior if only linear interaction remains. This non-monotonic dependence of chaotic dynamics on the order of nonlinearity originates from the interplay between parametric driving and effective potential reshaping and manifests that nonlinearity does not always enhance chaos. For an optomechanical system in a membrane-in-the-middle configuration, where only quadratic photon-vibration interaction is present, it is demonstrated that at small modulation amplitudes the mechanical oscillator exhibits quasi-periodic motion in each of the wells of a symmetric two-minimum potential, whereas large modulation amplitudes lead to chaotic motion, involving interwell transitions.

Summary. This paper introduces Constructor Theory, a foundational framework proposing that fundamental laws should be expressed as constraints on the possible transformations (tasks) achievable by physical systems. It generalizes concepts like non-cloning and irreversibility to abstract information media, offering potential tests applicable even in regimes where standard quantum mechanics might break down.

Why it may be interesting. It offers a meta-theoretical framework that attempts to unify disparate physical concepts—information, computation, thermodynamics, and biology—under a single set of possibility constraints, which is highly relevant for foundational physics beyond current quantum field theories.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a foundational theory of physical principles that constrains what tasks are possible or impossible, extending beyond current quantum theories by focusing on task feasibility rather than specific dynamics.

Main result. Constructor Theory provides a framework where fundamental laws (like irreversibility and non-cloning) emerge from general constraints on information processing capabilities. It suggests that physical laws should be described as necessary conditions for the possibility of tasks.

Method. The theory uses an abstract concept of 'tasks' (input/output transformations) defined over generalized 'information media,' allowing statements to be made independent of specific dynamical formalisms like Hamiltonians or Hilbert spaces.

Model / system. The core model is the 'universal constructor,' a hypothetical machine capable of performing all physically possible tasks. Testing involves analyzing hybrid systems (e.g., quantum probes interacting with unknown media) and biological self-reproduction.

Key observables. Possibility/impossibility of transformations (tasks), non-classicality witnesses, fidelity of self-reproduction, and correlations in hybrid quantum-classical measurements.

Important parameters / regimes. Scale independence, information capacity, and the degree of correlation between local interactions and global entanglement.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes principles like locality and interoperability of information; it aims to supplement existing laws rather than replace them entirely.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the replicator-vehicle logic for self-reproduction, the structure of composite information media, and entanglement witnesses derived from local interactions with an unknown medium.

Paper structure. The paper reviews constructor theory by first establishing its foundational principles (tasks, possibility), then applying it to specific domains like thermodynamics, quantum information (non-cloning/entanglement witnesses), and the emergence of life. It contrasts this approach with standard axiomatic reconstructions.

Abstract

Constructor theory is a proposal to extend quantum information theory beyond both quantum theory and computation, to cover more general machines than programmable computers -- called constructors. It consists of newly conjectured physical principles that can be expressed as constraints on what tasks are possible, what are impossible, and why. These principles also determine the repertoire of the universal constructor, which is a programmable machine that can perform all physically possible tasks. The principles of constructor theory have novel physical content that supplements current dynamical laws, leading to new predictions for experimental tests. In this paper, we review the main experimental proposals to test the principles of constructor theory and discuss their implications for existing theories of physics and for their successors.

Summary. This paper proposes a novel machine learning framework using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to pinpoint the critical temperature in anisotropic spin systems. By training on correlation matrices from Monte Carlo simulations, the method significantly reduces computational overhead while maintaining high accuracy compared to traditional thermodynamic calculations. This demonstrates a powerful synergy between deep learning and condensed matter physics for studying phase transitions.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on spin systems, the core methodology—using deep learning to extract complex physical parameters from high-dimensional simulation data—is highly relevant for analyzing quantum many-body dynamics or phase transitions in simulated quantum materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to evaluate the critical scaling behavior and identify the critical point ($T_c$) in anisotropic spin systems.

Main result. The machine learning approach provides estimates for $T_c$ that show very good agreement with reference Monte Carlo simulations while achieving a computational cost reduction of about 10x compared to classical methods.

Method. A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is trained on correlation matrices derived from Monte Carlo simulations. The critical temperature is then estimated using multiple inference techniques applied to the CNN's probabilistic output.

Model / system. The system modeled is a three-dimensional Heisenberg spin system exhibiting easy-axis anisotropy, described by a specific Hamiltonian involving exchange interactions ($J$) and anisotropy ($\Delta$).

Key observables. Correlation function $C_i(T)$, averaged output probabilities $P(T, L)$, deviation functions $E_r(T, L)$, and correlation-length-like quantities $\xi$.

Important parameters / regimes. The critical exponent ($ u$) is determined to be approximately 0.58(1). The analysis depends on the system size ($L$) and temperature discretization.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate correlation matrix changes across temperatures, the CNN architecture, and how varying the temperature sampling grid affects the sharpness of the transition observed in probability curves.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical problem, details the ML/CNN methodology using correlation matrices as input, presents multiple estimators for $T_c$ (crossing points, peak positions), validates results against classical Monte Carlo simulations, and concludes with finite-size scaling analysis to determine critical exponents.

Abstract

Computational models adequately representing phase transitions and evaluating the critical system parameters are essential for the understanding of the properties of a wide range of materials. Here we propose a machine learning (ML)-based approach to the identification of the critical point in anisotropic spin systems. Our approach implies training of a convolutional neural network (CNN) model from the correlation matrices obtained by Monte Carlo simulations. Next, the pretrained model is employed as a fast estimator of the critical temperature, which can be extracted in several complementary ways from the CNN model inference, this way improving the robustness of the analysis. The ML-based estimates obtained in this study are in very good agreement with the reference Monte Carlo simulation results, while computational costs are about 10x lower compared to the classical thermodynamic approach.

Summary. This work develops an algebraic framework to precisely define objectivity by linking it to the local recoverability properties within quantum error-correcting codes. By analyzing the structure of logical Pauli algebras on system fragments, the authors unify existing measures and provide quantitative criteria (SAO, LAO) for how classical information emerges from quantum entanglement.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep mathematical link between fundamental concepts of quantum foundations (objectivity/classicality) and the highly structured tools of Quantum Error Correction Codes, which is crucial for understanding decoherence dynamics in complex systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to precisely characterize the emergence of 'objectivity' and 'classicality' from quantum mechanics, moving beyond partial descriptions like Quantum Darwinism.

Main result. Objectivity is rigorously identified with the algebraic local recoverability of quantum codes, providing a unified and computable classification scheme for classicality and redundancy.

Method. The authors connect Quantum Darwinism to Operator Algebra Quantum Error Correction (QECC), using tools like analyzing the center of recoverable logical algebras ($Z(M_f)$) on stabilizer codes.

Model / system. The analysis is performed within the framework of quantum error-correcting codes, specifically utilizing stabilizer codes defined by Pauli subgroups. The dynamics are simulated via 'brickwork circuits' modeling system-environment interaction.

Key observables. Objectivity/Classicality Measure ($Z(M_f)$ non-triviality), Quantum Mutual Information (QMI), Classical bits ($c$), and Quantum qubits ($q$).

Important parameters / regimes. Strong Algebraic Objectivity (SAO), Localized Algebraic Objectivity (LAO), Quantum Doped Objectivity (QDO); plateau widths related to code distances.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis heavily relies on the mathematical structure of stabilizer codes and uses Poisson approximations for calculating probabilities in random subsystem fragments.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate QMI plateaus vs. fragment size, depict different algebraic regimes (SAO, LAO, QDO), and show heatmaps mapping classical ($c$) versus quantum ($q$) information content over simulated time steps.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining the problem using QD to establishing the connection with QECC algebra. It then develops theorems quantifying recoverability ($Z(M_f)$) and mutual information, culminating in proving equivalences between physical conditions (sQD) and algebraic structures (SAO).

Abstract

Quantum Darwinism extends the decoherence formalism to explain how classicality and objectivity emerge from quantum mechanics. However, existing approaches often capture only partial aspects of objectivity, leading to its mischaracterization and making it difficult to pin down precisely. By connecting quantum Darwinism to operator algebra quantum error correction, we show that the emergence of objectivity can be identified with the algebraic local recoverability of quantum codes. Applying this algebraic framework to stabilizer codes, we show that it yields a far more precise characterization of classicality and redundancy, unifies the traditional measures of objectivity, enables efficient classification via coding-theoretic tools, and supports large-scale Clifford simulations of decoherence dynamics.

Summary. This theoretical work demonstrates that applying light to Berry dipole semimetals allows for active, tunable control over their nonlinear Hall response. Main problem. The study investigates how light influences the intrinsic nonlinear Hall conductivity (NLHC) mediated by quantum metrics within Berry dipole semimetals.

Abstract

We investigate the effect of light on quantum metric-mediated intrinsic nonlinear Hall conductivity in Berry dipole semimetals. We show that the nonlinear response can be tuned directly by the light amplitude. In particular, we note that the direction of the nonlinear Hall signal changes when the light amplitude is increased beyond a threshold value. Light thus emerges as a promising stimulus to control the quantum geometric response in topological semimetals.

Summary. This work analyzes the noise accumulation when distributing multipartite entanglement over star network topologies. Main problem. The paper characterizes the noise incurred during multipartite entanglement distribution across star networks, focusing on how memory decoherence affects the final state fidelity.

Main result. It derives closed-form expressions showing that the piecemaker protocol significantly outperforms the factory protocol in terms of achievable users and key rates for a given level of noise.

Method. The analysis uses analytical derivations, including generating functions and Pauli basis expansions, to calculate the average noise parameter E[Lambda] and its full probability distribution.

Model / system. The system models quantum networking using star topologies where entangled states (like GHZ states) are distributed via a central node. The primary physical effect modeled is memory decoherence (dephasing or depolarizing noise) accumulated during stochastic waiting times.

Abstract

Multipartite entanglement forms the core of many networking applications. In the near-term future, it is expected that multipartite distribution will be achieved first through star topologies, making it important to understand the noise incurred during the distribution process. We derive analytical expressions for both the average noise and its distribution, when distributing GHZ states under memory dephasing in star networks. Furthermore, we find expressions for the case of a global cut-off (allowing fast optimization of the cut-off without requiring Monte Carlo simulations) and extend the analysis for the factory protocol to depolarizing noise for arbitrary states.

Summary. This paper establishes a general theoretical framework for performing quantum state tomography when the unknown state has a low-complexity decomposition over a structured model class. By boosting weak agnostic learners, the authors derive efficient algorithms that estimate the state with bounded error, providing significant advances over previous methods that required full access to the state preparation unitary.

Why it may be interesting. The work provides powerful tools to characterize and learn complex quantum states based on their underlying structure, which is crucial for understanding resource limitations in quantum computation protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a general framework for quantum state tomography when the unknown state $|\psi angle$ is assumed to have a 'bounded extent' with respect to a structured class of states $C$. This addresses efficiently learning an unknown quantum state that admits a low-complexity decomposition over $C$.

Main result. The authors provide algorithmic protocols for tomography achieving $\epsilon$-error estimation, showing that the weak agnostic learner can be boosted into a strong algorithm. For stabilizer states, this is achieved in quasipolynomial time.

Method. The core method involves leveraging the existence of a weak agnostic learner for $C$ and applying an algorithmic decomposition theorem to reconstruct $|\psi angle$. This process uses techniques like ridge projection and boosting from computational learning theory.

Model / system. The system is general quantum states, specifically focusing on $n$-qubit systems. The structure is formalized by a class of states $C$ (e.g., stabilizer states, MPS states), and the complexity is measured by the 'bounded extent' $\xi$.

Key observables. Fidelity ($F_C(|\psi angle)$), trace distance ($\epsilon$), Stabilizer extent ($\xi$).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits ($n$), Extent bound ($\xi$), Error tolerance ($\epsilon$), Sample complexity, Time complexity.

Assumptions / limitations. The results rely on $|\psi angle$ admitting a low-complexity decomposition over $C$, and the best polynomial bounds assume the algorithmic PFR conjecture.

Paper structure. The paper builds from general framework development (Chunk 1) to specific algorithms for structure learning and parameter estimation using ridge projectors (Chunk 2). It details protocols for various structured classes like stabilizer states and MPS (Chunks 3, 5, 6), while also providing rigorous mathematical analysis of the projection process and condition number bounds (Chunks 7, 8).

Abstract

We give a general framework for tomography of states that have bounded-extent with respect to a structured class of states. Let $\textsf{C}$ be a family of $n$-qubit states such that: $(i)$ $\textsf{C}$ is succinctly representable and $(ii)$ there is a weak agnostic learner of $\textsf{C}$. We give a tomography protocol for an unknown state $|ψ\rangle$ that is promised to admit a decomposition of the form $|ψ\rangle = \sum_i c_i |φ_i\rangle$, where $|φ_i\rangle \in \textsf{C}$ with bounded $\ell_1$-norm of the coefficients (which we call extent). Our main contribution is to show that a weak agnostic learner for $\textsf{C}$ can be boosted into a tomography algorithm for states with bounded extent with respect to $\textsf{C}$. Our reduction is black-box and applies broadly across model classes. As an application, when $\textsf{C}$ is the class of stabilizer states, we obtain tomography algorithms for states with stabilizer extent $ξ$ up to trace distance $\varepsilon$, in time $\textsf{poly}(n,(ξ/\varepsilon)^{\log(ξ/\varepsilon)})$, which is improvable to $ \textsf{poly}(n,ξ,1/\varepsilon)$ assuming the algorithmic polynomial Freiman-Ruzsa conjecture in the high-doubling regime. When the unknown state $|ψ\rangle$ is arbitrary, we give an algorithmic decomposition result in the spirit of a weak regularity lemma for quantum states with respect to $\textsf{C}$ and show that the structure in $|ψ\rangle$ that is explainable by $\textsf{C}$ can be efficiently learned. Our main conceptual message is that agnostic learning of a structured base class automatically yields learnability of its low-complexity linear span.

Abstract

We investigate the emergence of chaotic Bohmian trajectories in a three-mode superposition of the ground and first excited states of a two-dimensional anisotropic harmonic oscillator. The analysis focuses on the interference-induced phase structure of the wavefunction, which determines the Bohmian velocity field through its phase gradient. We show that the spatial extent of chaotic motion is controlled by the temporal coherence of the interference pattern, set by the detuning between oscillator modes. Near resonance, slow beating generates long-lived phase-gradient structures that repeatedly stretch and fold nearby trajectories, leading to more spatially extended chaotic regions. In contrast, strong detuning produces rapid temporal decorrelation of the phase field and confines chaotic dynamics to localized regions of configuration space. To quantify this behavior, we use a dimensionless coherence parameter comparing the beating time scale with a characteristic transport time. The results identify temporal coherence of the interference-induced phase field as a useful diagnostic for chaotic transport in low-dimensional Bohmian systems.

Abstract

QBism recasts quantum theory as a normative framework for an agent's probability assignments, with the Born rule taking the form of a consistency condition known as the Urgleichung. Motivated by this perspective, qplex theories provide a broader class of probabilistic models in which the sets of valid states and measurements are constrained by QBist-inspired geometric conditions. While qplexes have been extensively studied for single systems, their implications for bipartite correlations remain largely unexplored. In this work, we investigate bipartite correlations in qplex theories by expressing joint expectation values as inner products between suitably defined $C$-vectors. This geometric formulation allows Bell-type inequalities to be studied as optimization problems over qplex-compatible probability assignments. We first analyze the CHSH scenario and show that the shared inner-product structure of the $C$-vectors restricts the maximal value to the Tsirelson bound $2\sqrt{2}$. We then turn to the three-outcome CGLMP inequality $I_{2233}$ and find that the same qplex-derived norm and inner-product constraints allow the algebraic maximum of 4, thereby exhibiting superquantum correlations. These results show that qplex geometry captures enough structure to reproduce an important quantum bound in the two-outcome case, but not enough to recover the full set of quantum correlation constraints. The analysis therefore suggests that additional principles are needed to complete the QBist reconstruction of quantum theory.

Abstract

We introduce and study a unified framework for density-functional theory and its variants for quantum systems on finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces. These theories seek to reduce the complexity inherent in the many-body quantum problem by describing ground states through reduced variables. The central ingredients of our unified framework are a generalized choice of basic observables, whose expectation values define precisely those reduced variables, and a fixed part of the Hamiltonian characterizing the class of quantum systems under consideration. It is this minimal structure, which we call the scope of a functional theory, that is necessary and sufficient for the formulation of a functional theory. In particular, it allows one to define the universal functionals, establish their convexity and differentiability properties, address representability questions, and prove a Hohenberg-Kohn-type uniqueness result. A purification construction also relates ensemble and weighted-ensemble functionals to the pure-state variant. Particular emphasis is placed on functional theories with Lie-algebra observable structures, connecting the variational framework to symplectic geometry. The result of this work is a systematic mathematical formulation in which structural results can be proved once and applied across a broad class of finite-dimensional functional theories.
