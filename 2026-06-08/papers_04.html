Summary. This paper advances the many-body bootstrap method by introducing non-local 'string' operators to improve bounds on ground-state observables in quantum spin systems. By applying this technique to models like the TFIM and ANNNI, the authors demonstrate that these extended constraints can accurately map out phase boundaries and resolve issues related to domain-wall excitations in symmetry-broken phases.

Why it may be interesting. The development of methods that rigorously constrain ground state properties using extended, non-local operators is highly valuable for understanding quantum phase transitions in strongly correlated magnetic materials where simple local descriptions fail.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is obtaining rigorous, tight bounds on ground-state observables for quantum spin systems in the thermodynamic limit.

Main result. By incorporating non-local, string-like operators into the bootstrap method, the authors significantly tighten existing bounds, especially in spontaneously symmetry-breaking (SSB) phases.

Method. They employ the many-body bootstrap technique, formulating it as a Semidefinite Program (SDP), and crucially extend the operator set with non-local 'string' operators to handle domain-wall excitations.

Model / system. The work applies to 1D quantum spin systems, including the transverse-field Ising model (TFIM), the axial next-nearest-neighbor Ising model (ANNNI), and the Z3 chiral clock model. The Hamiltonians describe various magnetic ordering phases.

Key observables. Ground-state observables, energy density ($\Delta E$), magnetization density, and correlation functions ($\langle Z_0 Z_r angle$).

Important parameters / regimes. The transverse field strength ($g$), the next-nearest-neighbor coupling ($\kappa$), and the symmetry parameter ($\phi$) are key tuning parameters used to map out phase diagrams.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that incorporating string operators allows for a formulation directly in the thermodynamic limit, overcoming limitations of local operator sets in SSB phases.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the tightening of bootstrap bounds (e.g., energy density vs. parameters) when comparing results from local versus string-operator constrained SDPs across different models like TFIM and ANNNI.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the limitations of local operators in SSB phases, then introduces non-local string operators to formulate the bootstrap in the thermodynamic limit. It applies this construction sequentially to 1D TFIM, then ANNNI, and finally generalizes it to the Z3 chiral clock model.

Abstract

Bootstrap is a numerical many-body method that provides rigorous bounds on ground-state observables by imposing a set of necessary constraints on the expectation values of operators. The quality of the resulting bounds is sensitive to the choice of operators entering the constraints. In particular, bounds on ground-state correlations are often loose in spontaneous symmetry-breaking (SSB) phases, since local operator sets cannot exclude domain-wall excitations. In this work, we introduce non-local, string-like operators into the bootstrap and show that the program can be formulated directly in thermodynamic limit. We then apply our construction to several 1D spin models. First, we obtain a significant tightening of the bounds in the SSB phase of the 1D transverse-field Ising model. Using the 1D axial next-nearest-neighbor Ising model, we further show that this tightening allows for a quantitative estimate of the locations of phase boundaries. Finally, we generalize the string operators to the 1D $\mathbb{Z}_3$ chiral clock model and track the behavior of the bounds across the phase diagram. Our results broaden the class of constraints available to the bootstrap and open a route toward bootstrapping more general symmetry-broken and topological phases, where the relevant constraints may involve non-local or extended operators.

Summary. The paper presents scalable quantum algorithms to prepare the Gutzwiller-projected BCS state, which serves as an essential input for simulating strongly correlated electron models like the $t$-$J$ model. By employing Amplitude Amplification (AAGP), the required computational resources scale quadratically better than previous measurement-based techniques. This breakthrough moves projected state preparation from a theoretical hurdle to a potentially deployable protocol for quantum simulation.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant as it develops a concrete quantum computational primitive—scalable state preparation—essential for simulating complex many-body phenomena in condensed matter physics that are intractable classically.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is preparing physically accurate input states for quantum simulation of strongly correlated lattice models, specifically due to the non-trivial Gutzwiller projection.

Main result. The proposed Amplitude Amplification for Gutzwiller Projection (AAGP) algorithm achieves a quadratic speedup in required projection queries compared to measurement-based methods. This makes state preparation practically feasible at relevant finite system sizes.

Method. The authors develop and analyze the AAGP quantum circuit, which uses amplitude amplification techniques derived from Grover's search structure to estimate the overlap between the desired Resonating Valence Bond (RVB) state and the initial BCS ansatz. The full BCS state itself is constructed using Givens rotations applied after diagonalizing a Bogoliubov-de Gennes Hamiltonian.

Model / system. The work focuses on strongly correlated lattice models, particularly the $t$-$J$ model derived from the Hubbard model, relevant to high-temperature superconductivity. The target state is the Gutzwiller-projected BCS state $|\psi_{ ext{RVB}} angle = \mathcal{P}_G |\psi_{ ext{BCS}} angle$.

Key observables. The probability weight $W = |\langle\psi_{RVB}|\psi_{BCS} angle|^2$ is the key observable, and the required number of projection queries ($O(1/\sqrt{W})$ vs. $O(1/W)$) quantifies the algorithmic improvement.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (e.g., 100 sites), pairing gap ($\Delta$), hole doping concentration ($x$), and the desired success tolerance ($\delta$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that while the weight $W$ decreases exponentially with system size, the quadratic speedup of AAGP is large enough to provide a decisive practical advantage at finite sizes. The BCS state construction relies on mapping fermionic operators via Bloch-Messiah decomposition.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the workflow for Gutzwiller projection using amplitude amplification (AAGP), show the structure of the quantum circuit involving Givens rotations, and demonstrate the convergence of success probability versus iteration number.

Paper structure. The paper systematically builds the necessary components: first, defining the physical problem and the BCS state construction; second, detailing the inefficient measurement-based projection method; third, introducing and analyzing the AAGP algorithm using amplitude amplification theory (including recursion relations solved via Chebyshev polynomials); and finally, calculating the overlap weight $W$ for specific models like the $t$-$J$ model.

Abstract

Quantum simulation requires highly accurate input states. Gutzwiller-projected Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) states provide physically motivated input states for solving strongly correlated lattice models, but their preparation on a quantum computer is hindered by the non-trivial nature of the Gutzwiller projection. We construct scalable quantum algorithms for this task by combining a circuit construction for arbitrary BCS states with the amplitude amplification for Gutzwiller projection (AAGP) procedure. AAGP yields a quadratic reduction in the number of projection queries compared with measurement-based postselection and leads to substantially improved fault-tolerant resource scaling. For projected BCS states optimized for the square-lattice $t$-$J$ model, we find that the projected-state weight decreases exponentially with system size, but the quadratic improvement is still large enough at physically relevant finite sizes to make a decisive practical difference. In particular, for a 100-site benchmark, AAGP reduces the required number of projection queries by about seven orders of magnitude. These results establish AAGP as an enabling input-state preparation protocol for projected BCS states in quantum simulation.

Abstract

In this paper, the class of randomised mixed labyrinth fractals is introduced. It is a class of finitely ramified Sierpinski carpets that generalize mixed labyrinth fractals. The structures are generated by randomly selected labyrinth patterns with fixed selection probabilities at each iteration level, offering a flexible framework to study fractal topology, arc dimensions, and shortest path properties. Here, the focus lies on analysing how the randomised mixing of patterns - specifically their shape, symmetry, and path geometry - effects arc dimensions, path lengths, and isotropy restoration. The study reveals that isotropy, previously shown for self-similar fractals, extends to the randomised mixed class. Various scaling behaviours of shortest path dimensions with respect to the mixing probability are identified, including linear and nonlinear monotonic trends, as well as transitions with maxima. The approximated path matrix is proposed as an efficient alternative to extensive iterative simulations, reliably reproducing statistical results. The findings highlight the relevance of pattern properties in determining fractal structures and dynamics and suggest applications in physical systems such as diffusion, signal processing, and antenna design.

Abstract

In conventional antiferromagnets a long-range ordered 3D ground state transitions to a disordered paramagnetic state on warming, often via lower dimensional spin correlations within the critical regime. Abstract
