Saddle-node bifurcation in interfacial morphology selects battery degradation phase

Authors: Changdeuck Bae
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.10252v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. The paper introduces a simple mathematical model to describe how battery interfaces transition from stable to unstable morphologies. By applying this model to different battery types, it reveals that anode-free lithium technology operates very close to a critical point of failure, suggesting a very narrow window for safe operation.

Why it may be interesting. The paper applies the concept of a saddle-node bifurcation and mean-field critical slowing down to a non-equilibrium morphological transition, which is relevant to researchers studying phase transitions and stability in driven-dissipative systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to model the morphological stability of battery interfaces and predict the transition between smooth passivating phases and morphologically unstable phases during degradation.

Main result. The authors identify a saddle-node bifurcation in a minimal nonlinear ODE that governs the active-area factor, predicting that anode-free Li/Cu configurations sit dangerously close to a critical threshold of instability.

Method. The study uses a minimal nonlinear closure ODE approach and maps experimental steady-state data from various anode configurations onto the theoretical bifurcation framework.

Model / system. A nonlinear closure ODE for the dimensionless excess active area (u), representing the dynamic active-area factor of battery interfaces including graphite, silicon composite, lithium metal, and anode-free Li/Cu.

Key observables. Dimensionless excess active area (u), dimensionless drive (K), and the ratio K/K_c.

Important parameters / regimes. Saturation parameter (alpha), critical drive (K_c), current density, temperature, and electrolyte composition.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes the interface dynamics can be captured by a minimal nonlinear closure ODE and that the bifurcation is driven by the saturation of the smoothing rate with surface roughness.

Paper structure. The paper proposes a nonlinear ODE model, demonstrates the existence of a saddle-node bifurcation, applies the model to various battery anode configurations using public data, and tests falsifiable predictions regarding critical current density and temperature.

Abstract

We propose a minimal nonlinear closure ODE for the dynamic active-area factor of a battery interface and show that it exhibits a saddle-node bifurcation when the smoothing rate saturates with surface roughness. The closure is the simplest physically motivated extension of a recently introduced single-fixed-point closure [C. Bae, in preparation (2026)]: u = K - u/(1 + alphau^2), where u = xi - 1 is the dimensionless excess active area, K the dimensionless drive, and alpha a single saturation parameter. The bifurcation occurs at K_c = 1/(2sqrt(alpha)), separating a smooth passivating phase from a morphologically unstable phase. Mapping four canonical anode configurations -- graphite, silicon composite, lithium metal, and anode-free Li/Cu -- onto the closure via end-of-cycling steady-state xi extracted from publicly available long-cycle data populates the stable branch with monotonically increasing K/K_c ratios: graphite (~0.01), silicon composite (~0.24), lithium metal (~0.73), and anode-free (~0.95). The anode-free configuration sits within 5% of the saddle-node threshold, predicting a vanishingly small operational stability window in current density, temperature, and electrolyte composition. We test three falsifiable predictions of the framework -- a critical current density, a critical temperature shift, and a mean-field critical-slowing-down exponent -- and find them broadly consistent with publicly available data. We argue that this near-critical position is universal to nucleation-controlled deposition on non-passivating substrates.

A Topological Soliton Model for Ball Lightning: Theory and Numerical Verification with the 3D Gross-Pitaevskii Equation

Authors: Zhe Li

Summary. This paper proposes a new theoretical model for ball lightning, treating it as a 3D topological soliton described by the Gross-Pitaevskii equation. Numerical simulations verify that these solitons are stable, can pass through barriers via wavefunction orthogonality, and match observed energy and lifetime scales. The work provides a unified physical explanation for a long-standing atmospheric mystery using the framework of ultracold gas dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. It applies the physics of topological protection and Gross-Pitaevskii dynamics—central to BEC and many-body studies—to an atmospheric phenomenon, suggesting that 3D topological solitons can be experimentally prepared in BEC or nonlinear optical systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Explaining the physical nature of ball lightning, specifically its long lifetime, stability, and ability to penetrate obstacles.

Main result. The paper proposes that ball lightning is a projection of a high-dimensional topological soliton, demonstrating through simulations that such structures possess the required stability, penetrability, and physical scales.

Method. 3D numerical simulations of the Gross-Pitaevskii equation using a Fourier Spectral Method and a fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm.

Model / system. A 3D Gross-Pitaevskii equation (GPE) with attractive interactions (g < 0), modeling the system as a Bose-Einstein condensate-like structure with a non-zero topological charge.

Key observables. Topological charge (Q), lifetime (tau), transmission coefficient (T), soliton width (sigma), and total energy (E).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (g approx -2.0), topological winding number (n=1), trap frequency (omega = 0.3), and dissipation coefficient (gamma = 0.02).

Assumptions / limitations. The phenomenon can be modeled as a projection of a high-dimensional topological soliton into 3D space; the stability is governed by the decoherence rate.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows initial wavefunction density and phase winding; Figure 2 visualizes the 3D soliton isosurface; Figure 3 plots the time evolution of topological charge and energy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the ball lightning problem, proposes a topological soliton model based on the GPE, details the numerical simulation methods, presents results regarding stability and penetrability, compares predictions to observations, and concludes by comparing the model to existing atmospheric physics theories.

Abstract

Ball lightning is one of the most mysterious atmospheric phenomena, whose long lifetime, penetrative ability, and stability are difficult to explain with traditional physical models. This paper proposes a novel theoretical framework, interpreting ball lightning as a projection of a high-dimensional topological soliton into three-dimensional space. Its essence is described by a nonlinear Schrödinger equation with attractive interaction, protected by a non-zero topological charge. Through numerical simulation of the three-dimensional Gross-Pitaevskii equation, we verify the core predictions of this model: in a Bose-Einstein condensate with attractive interactions, solitons carrying topological charge exhibit: (1)long-lived stability (topological charge conserved under perturbations); (2)low transmission probability (due to minimal overlap integral resulting from orthogonality with the ground state wavefunction); (3)energy and size scales consistent with actual observations. Theoretical analysis indicates that the soliton lifetime is governed by the system's decoherence rate, providing a natural explanation for the observed second-scale lifetimes. This work not only offers a self-consistent physical explanation for ball lightning but also provides a concrete scheme for the experimental preparation and observation of three-dimensional topological solitons.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that compensated ferrimagnets can act as efficient, stray-field-free sources of spin current via the Spin Seebeck effect. By focusing on exchange-coupling asymmetry, the authors show that these materials can match the performance of traditional ferromagnets. This provides a theoretical foundation for developing new, low-interference spintronic devices.

Main result. The study shows that isotropic magnon splitting due to exchange-coupling asymmetry generates a robust spin current comparable to standard ferromagnets, while the effect vanishes in altermagnets.

Method. The authors utilize the nonequilibrium Green's function (NEGF) framework to model spin transport.

Model / system. A junction consisting of a compensated ferrimagnet and a normal metal, modeled using a four-sublattice system where sublattice inequivalence arises from differences in exchange couplings.

Key observables. Spin current magnitude and the Spin Seebeck effect.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange-coupling asymmetry and magnon splitting.

Assumptions / limitations. The sublattice inequivalence is driven by exchange-coupling differences rather than anisotropy-based mechanisms.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of compensated ferrimagnets, presents a four-sublattice model based on exchange-coupling asymmetry, applies the NEGF framework to calculate spin currents, compares the results to altermagnets, and discusses implications for spintronic applications.

Abstract

Compensated ferrimagnets enable ferromagnet-like spin transport without net magnetization. We study the spin Seebeck effect in a compensated ferrimagnet/normal-metal junction using a four-sublattice model in which sublattice inequivalence arises from differences in exchange couplings, in contrast to the previously studied anisotropy-based mechanism. Within the nonequilibrium Green's function framework, we show that isotropic magnon splitting generates a robust spin current with a magnitude comparable to that in standard ferromagnetic junctions. We also demonstrate that the spin Seebeck effect vanishes in altermagnet junctions under identical conditions, thereby establishing compensated ferrimagnets as uniquely suited for thermal spin-current generation among magnetically compensated systems. These results provide a theoretical basis for the applications of compensated ferrimagnets with exchange-coupling asymmetry as stray-field-free spin-current sources in spintronic devices.

Chiral Porphyrin Monolayers on Ferromagnetic Thin Films: Ultrafast Spectroscopy of Hybrid Interfaces

Summary. The study examines the ultrafast dynamics of chiral porphyrin molecules when placed on a ferromagnetic metal surface. While the metal surface accelerates the decay of the excited state, the researchers found no evidence of spin-dependent dynamics or long-lived charge transfer in this specific interface.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Main problem. Investigating how the proximity of ferromagnetic Co/Ni multilayers affects the spectroscopic properties and photoinduced electron dynamics of chiral porphyrin-based self-assembled monolayers.

Main result. Adsorption on the metal surface significantly reduces the excited-state lifetime of the porphyrin, but no long-lived charge-transfer products or dependence on magnetization or chirality were detected.

Method. Femtosecond pump-probe transient absorption spectroscopy.

Model / system. A self-assembled monolayer of chiral oligopeptides bearing a porphyrin chromophore, covalently attached to a gold-capped Co/Ni ferromagnetic multilayer.

Key observables. Transient absorption signal of the porphyrin singlet excited state, excited-state lifetime, and magnetization-dependent dynamics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of spinterfaces, describes the fabrication of the chiral porphyrin monolayer on Co/Ni multilayers, presents the transient absorption spectroscopy results, and discusses the observed excited-state dynamics and lack of magnetic/chiral dependence.

Abstract

Hybrid ferromagnetic metal/organic interfaces (spinterfaces) exhibit unique properties, including spin filtering. In parallel, chiral organic molecules can themselves induce efficient spin filtering, leading to unexpectedly high spin polarizations. Here, we investigate how the proximity of gold-capped Co/Ni ferromagnetic multilayers influences the spectroscopic properties and photoinduced electron dynamics of chiral oligopeptides bearing a porphyrin chromophore. The molecules are covalently attached to the gold cap via a chiral linker, forming a self-assembled monolayer. The porphyrin macrocycles adopt an orientation parallel to the surface, resulting in the formation of J-like aggregates. Photoinduced dynamics are probed using femtosecond pump-probe transient absorption spectroscopy. Despite excitation of only a single molecular layer, a clear transient absorption signal of the porphyrin singlet excited state is observed. Adsorption on the metal surface leads to a pronounced reduction of the excited-state lifetime. However, no signatures of long-lived photoinduced charge-transfer products are detected. Furthermore, no dependence of the excited-state dynamics on either the magnetization direction of the ferromagnetic layer or the molecular chirality is observed.

Antiferro-Chiral Phonons in $\mathcal{P}\mathcal{T}$-Symmetric Antiferromagnets

Authors: Sanjib Kumar Das, Randy Yeh, Yafei Ren
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.08490v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. The paper proposes a new type of phonon, called antiferro-chiral phonons, that can couple to the staggered magnetization of antiferromagnets. These phonons carry no net angular momentum but have opposite rotation on different sublattices. This discovery provides a new way to probe and potentially control antiferromagnetic order using light or coherent phonons.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to couple lattice motion to the compensated Néel order in antiferromagnets, given that conventional chiral phonons carry net angular momentum that couples only to net magnetization.

Main result. The discovery of antiferro-chiral phonons (AFCPs) in PT-symmetric antiferromagnets, which possess vanishing total angular momentum but finite sublattice-staggered angular momentum.

Method. Theoretical analysis of phonon-magnon coupling using symmetry arguments (PT-symmetry) and a microscopic derivation via molecular Berry curvature in a prototype lattice model.

Model / system. PT-symmetric antiferromagnets where Raman and infrared-active phonons are coupled to the Néel vector.

Key observables. Sublattice-staggered phonon angular momentum, Néel vector, and phonon chirality.

Important parameters / regimes. PT-symmetry, inversion-related sublattices, and the coupling between Raman and infrared-active phonons.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is governed by PT-symmetry, which forbids net phonon angular momentum while allowing counter-rotating motion on sublattices.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of AFCPs, explains the symmetry requirements for staggered angular momentum in PT-symmetric systems, describes the mechanism of phonon hybridization via Néel-vector-locked coupling, and demonstrates the microscopic origin through a lattice model.

Abstract

Chiral phonons provide a route to couple lattice motion to magnetic order, but conventional chiral phonons carry a net angular momentum and thus couple naturally to net magnetization rather than to compensated Néel order. Here we show that $\mathcal{P}\mathcal{T}$-symmetric antiferromagnets can host \emph{antiferro-chiral phonons} (AFCPs): phonon modes with vanishing total angular momentum but finite sublattice-staggered angular momentum. Symmetry enforces this distinction because $\mathcal{P}\mathcal{T}$ forbids a net phonon angular momentum while allowing counter-rotating local motion on inversion-related sublattices. AFCPs arise from a Néel-vector-locked coupling between Raman and infrared-active phonons. The coupling is odd under both $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{T}$ while preserving their product. Through this hybridization, the normal modes acquire both Raman and infrared character and carry a sublattice-staggered phonon angular momentum that acts as a conjugate field to the Néel vector. This coupling is microscopically generated by the molecular Berry curvature, which is demonstrated in a prototype lattice model. Reversing the Néel vector reverses the staggered phonon chirality. These results indicate AFCPs as probes of antiferromagnetic order and suggest coherent phonon excitation as a route to its dynamical control.

Abstract

We consider a model of 1D relativistic hydrogen-like atom, formed by a Coulomb impurity in graphene nanoribbon. Describing the electron motion in terms of the one-dimensional Dirac equation for Coulomb potential taking into account the finite-size of the atomic nucleus, we compute the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of the atomic electron. Experimentally measurable characteristics, local density of states is also plotted for both cases. Existence of strong localization for atom-in-box system is shown.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that Rashba superconductors can mediate long-range ferromagnetic interactions between magnetic insulators via power-law decaying supercurrents. Paper structure. Abstract

We study a Rashba superconductor thin film with ferromagnetic insulators (FIs) placed on top of it. We show that the ferromagnetic insulators generate circular super-currents, enabling long-range magnetic interactions (LRMI), decaying in power laws. In the static case, the long-range magnetic interaction can be ferromagnetic, in contrast to previous studies showing that superconductor mediates anti-ferromagnetic interactions decaying exponentially. Surprisingly, we find that in the dynamic case, the LRMI has a different distance dependence. Our results have potential applications in superconducting spintronics.

Abstract

Lithium electrolytes are commonly described using separate conceptual frameworks for local coordination chemistry, electrostatic screening, and ionic transport. This separation is effective in dilute conditions but breaks down at higher concentration, where coordination, ion pairing, clustering, and collective dynamics become intrinsically coupled. In this Perspective, we develop a unified multiscale framework that links local coordination motifs, mesoscopic ionic organization, and macroscopic transport within a single physical picture. This perspective implies that rational electrolyte design must simultaneously control short-range coordination, mesoscale organization, and collective electrostatic response.

Abstract

The rapid progress of electron spin resonance scanning tunneling microscopy experiments has enabled the manipulation of individual adsorbate spin states physisorbed on ultrathin oxide layers supported on metal substrates. Electron resonance requires unpaired spin density on the adsorbate, which can be achieved, for instance, through charge transfer from the supporting substrate. This requires the correct energy-level alignment between the energy levels of the adsorbate and the Fermi energy of the substrate. Experiments on molecules and single atoms adsorbed on metal-insulator systems have revealed complex phenomena, including electronic bandgap narrowing, charge transfer, Fermi-level pinning, and the re-ordering of adsorbate orbitals after charge transfer. Despite these advances, a predictive first-principles approach based on accurate methods such as quasiparticle GW, capable of capturing these effects without the prohibitive cost of full adsorbate/oxide/metal simulations, remains an open challenge. In this work, we present a theoretical approach to determine the energy-level alignment of adsorbates on oxide/metal substrates. Our method transparently exposes all physical processes and strikes a balance between computational cost and accuracy. Ionization potentials and electron affinities of the isolated adsorbates are obtained using GW calculations, electronic bandgap polarization is quantified through the quasiparticle renormalization caused by the substrate, Fermi-level pinning is evaluated within the integer charge transfer model, and work function shifts arising from Pauli pushback or from the adsorbate-metal dipole are determined from the local variations of the electrostatic potential. This computationally efficient framework paves the way for highthroughput screening of molecular qubits and organic electronic interfaces.

Abstract
