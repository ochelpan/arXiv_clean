Quantum Sensing and Quantum Error Correction: Two Sides of the Same Coin

Authors: Zhuoran Bao, Daniel F. V. James
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24120v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper demonstrates that quantum sensing and quantum error correction are two sides of the same coin, as the states most sensitive to a parameter are mathematically identical to the states most susceptible to errors. By linking Fisher Information to error metrics, the authors provide a method to construct optimal sensor states using principles from error-correcting codes.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a unified theoretical framework that allows researchers to use the well-established tools of quantum error correction to design more robust and sensitive quantum sensors, which is highly relevant for quantum metrology and precision spectroscopy.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Establishing a formal mathematical link between the sensitivity of a quantum state in parameter estimation (quantum sensing) and its vulnerability to unitary errors (quantum error correction).

Main result. The authors prove that the most sensitive states for rotation sensing and the worst-case codewords for error correction are governed by the same mathematical criteria, specifically maximizing the expectation value of the generator squared.

Method. The study uses statistical distance (Wootters' distance) and Fisher Information to relate state distinguishability to fidelity and error metrics, utilizing the Wigner-Eckart theorem and irreducible tensor operators for angular momentum calculations.

Model / system. The paper focuses on angular momentum (spin) systems undergoing unitary rotations, specifically exploring the construction of second-order anti-coherent states and the use of Absorption-Emission (AE) codes.

Key observables. Fisher Information, fidelity, Bures metric, angular momentum components (Jx, Jy, Jz), and the error of the state.

Important parameters / regimes. Rotation angle (theta), total angular momentum (J), and the generator of the unitary transformation (G).

Assumptions / limitations. The derivations primarily focus on pure quantum states and rely on a small-angle approximation (theta approaching zero) for the error-correction analysis.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing a mathematical connection between distinguishability and statistical distance, moves to a sensitivity analysis of unitary errors, demonstrates the symmetry between optimal sensing and worst-case error, and concludes with the construction of specific optimal sensor states using angular momentum algebra.

Abstract

Quantum metrology has been making amazing progress in the past decades. It is always in researchers' interest to search for new optimal states that improve parameter estimation. In this paper, we point out a connection between the code's error correcting capacity and its ability to act as a sensor. We backed our claim by providing an example that relates the Absorption emission code to the sensor state for arbitrary state rotation. It is hoped that, in building such a unified theory, one can draw inspiration from error correction to develop promising quantum sensors.

Response of a dipolar BEC to Laguerre-Gaussian beam driven STIRAP

Authors: Deepu Singh, Hari Sadhan Ghosh, Arpana Saboo, Soumyadeep Halder, Sonjoy Majumder
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26094v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · interference shaping light 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the orbital angular momentum of laser beams can be transferred to a dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate using the STIRAP technique. It demonstrates that the stability of the resulting quantized vortices is highly sensitive to whether the condensate is in a superfluid, droplet, or supersolid phase. The findings offer a versatile method for engineering topological states in quantum gases.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical framework for using coherent light-matter coupling to manipulate topological defects and probe the stability of exotic phases like supersolids and droplets in dipolar gases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the efficiency of transferring orbital angular momentum (OAM) from Laguerre-Gaussian beams to a dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate via the STIRAP protocol and how this transfer is affected by different interaction-driven phases.

Main result. The study finds that vortex nucleation and angular momentum retention depend on the BEC phase: stable vortices form in the superfluid phase, while the supersolid phase can lead to vortex expulsion depending on the magnetic field orientation.

Method. Numerical simulations using a set of Raman-coupled extended Gross-Pitaevskii equations (eGPE) including the Lee-Huang-Yang correction to account for quantum fluctuations.

Model / system. A quasi-two-dimensional trapped dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate consisting of two hyperfine states coupled via an intermediate state in a Lambda-configuration, subject to long-range dipole-dipole interactions and short-range contact interactions.

Key observables. Population transfer efficiency, angular momentum transfer, vortex topological charge, density profiles, and collective modes (scissors and quadrupole modes).

Important parameters / regimes. Superfluid, droplet, and supersolid phases; magnetic field orientation (parallel vs. perpendicular to beam propagation); pulse sequences (G-LG and LG-G); and trap anisotropy.

Assumptions / limitations. Adiabatic elimination of the intermediate excited state due to large detuning; use of a quasi-2D approximation for the LHY correction; and a mean-field framework.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental schematic of the LG beam and the three-level system; Figure 2 and 4 illustrate time-resolved density distributions and vortex generation; Figure 5 tracks the evolution of collective modes and energy components.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the STIRAP-driven OAM transfer problem, describes the eGPE model and LHY correction, analyzes the dynamics across superfluid, droplet, and supersolid phases, and concludes with the implications for engineering vortex states.

Abstract

Coherent light-matter coupling via STIRAP can offer a versatile route to nucleate quantized vortices in Bose-Einstein condensates, yet its efficacy in dipolar condensates remains an open question. Can the orbital angular momentum of a Laguerre-Gaussian beam be coherently transferred to a dipolar BEC via STIRAP? We investigate this for a quasi-two-dimensional trapped dipolar condensate using co-propagating Gaussian and Laguerre-Gaussian laser beams. The interplay between long-range dipole-dipole interactions and short-range contact interactions enables access to three interaction-driven phases: superfluid, droplet, and supersolid. We find that the amount of angular momentum transferred from the optical field to the dipolar condensate, along with the nucleation and persistence of vortices, depends strongly on the underlying phases of the dipolar BEC. In the superfluid, STIRAP achieves near-complete population transfer and nucleates a stable, long-lived quantized vortex. In the droplet phase, although the vortex remains pinned within the density profile, the angular momentum is partially retained and oscillatory, accompanied by droplet fragmentation and recombination. In the supersolid phase, when the external magnetic field is oriented perpendicular to the LG beam's propagation direction, the emergence of a modulated density distribution along with a slight reduction in inter-droplet coherence leads to vortex delocalization and eventually expels it along the field direction, yielding a vanishing average angular momentum. However, reorienting the magnetic polarization along the beam propagation direction restores efficient angular momentum transfer and stabilizes the vortex within the supersolid phase. Overall, our results demonstrate a versatile route to engineer vortex states and probe collective excitations in dipolar Bose gases via coherent light-matter coupling.

Strategic Non-Shareability of Quantum Correlations

Authors: Fumin Wang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25516v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a framework to quantify how quantum correlations can prevent third-party collusion. It demonstrates that quantum entanglement provides a unique 'anti-collusion power' that classical shared randomness lacks, specifically identifying the threshold where Bell non-locality enables certified security.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a quantitative resource theory for entanglement monogamy, framing it as a measurable 'shareability deficit' which is highly relevant for securing quantum networks and device-independent protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Quantifying the 'strategic non-shareability' of quantum correlations, specifically measuring the degree to which a mediator's coordination can be protected from an outside colluder in adversarial settings.

Main result. The paper proves that anti-collusion capacity equals the total-variation distance to the collusive shadow and identifies the CHSH Bell local bound as the sharp onset of positive certified anti-collusion power.

Method. The study uses a geometric approach based on total-variation distance, the NPA hierarchy (level-2 SDP relaxation) for bounding vulnerability, and Hoeffding-based protocols for finite-data certification.

Model / system. A tripartite setup involving an authorized pair (Players 1 and 2) and a potential colluder (Player 3), utilizing both classical hidden-variable models and quantum models (including Werner states and tilted-CHSH inequalities).

Key observables. CHSH score (S12), collusive leakage score (C13), anti-collusion capacity (CAC), and collusive vulnerability.

Important parameters / regimes. CHSH score (s), visibility parameter (eta) for Werner states, and the tilt parameter (alpha) for tilted-CHSH inequalities.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes no-signalling consistency and finite alphabets; results for the CHSH slice rely on the Toner-Verstraete monogamy inequality.

Figures summary. Figure 1 visualizes the three-player game and classical vs. quantum collusion; Figure 2 shows the transition of anti-collusion power with noise; Figure 3 displays the Hoeffding-based certification and visibility thresholds; Figure 4 presents NPA upper bounds for tilted-CHSH inequalities.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of strategic non-shareability, defines the mathematical framework of the collusive shadow, provides analytical proofs for the CHSH case, extends the analysis to tilted-CHSH via NPA hierarchy, and concludes with finite-data certification protocols.

Abstract

Correlations distributed by a mediator are usually valued for the coordination they enable between authorized agents, but in adversarial settings a more decisive property is whether the same coordination can be inherited by an outside colluder without disturbing the authorized marginal. Classical shared randomness is freely copyable, so a hidden seed coordinating two agents can be duplicated for a third; entanglement is constrained by monogamy, which can forbid such lossless extensions in strongly nonlocal regimes. We turn this asymmetry into an operational resource for private-information games. For a fixed authorized behavior $P_{12}$, we define its \emph{collusive shadow} as the set of relabelled behaviors a colluder can reproduce in any admissible tripartite extension preserving $P_{12}$, and we identify \emph{strategic non-shareability} with the distance from this shadow. We prove that, on finite alphabets, the game-optimized anti-collusion capacity equals the total-variation distance to the shadow; a fixed game provides a task-specific separating witness, while optimization over relabelled games recovers the full distance. In the CHSH score slice, Toner--Verstraete monogamy yields the exact certified frontier, so the Bell local bound $S_{12}=2$ is the sharp onset of positive certified anti-collusion power, saturating at $1/(2\sqrt{2})$ for the maximally entangled CHSH strategy. Classical hidden-variable mediators have zero capacity in this slice. We complement these results with two operational tools: a Hoeffding-based finite-data certification protocol that turns observed Bell scores into confidence-bounded anti-collusion certificates, and a level-2 NPA semidefinite relaxation that extends certified upper envelopes to tilted Bell inequalities. These results recast entanglement monogamy as a measurable shareability deficit for quantum-mediated strategic networks.

Toward General Quantum Control with Physics-Informed Large Language Models

Authors: Yusheng Zhao, Han Wang, Xin Liu, Xinjie Song, Jixi He, Lingwei Song, Yuanhe Ji, Ken Deng, Runqing Zhang, Zhiguo Huang, Ling Qian, Jize Han, Di Luo

Summary. This paper introduces VF-QCTRL, a framework that uses physics-informed large language models to propose interpretable, analytic quantum control pulses. By combining symbolic reasoning with numerical optimization, it outperforms or matches classical methods in both noiseless and noisy environments. The approach is training-free and highly scalable across various quantum platforms.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a novel way to bridge symbolic physical reasoning with numerical optimization, potentially automating the discovery of robust control protocols for complex open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Designing high-fidelity quantum control protocols is difficult due to complex optimization landscapes, hardware noise, and the uninterpretability of traditional numerical solvers.

Main result. The VF-QCTRL framework achieves performance competitive with or exceeding state-of-the-art classical solvers, providing interpretable analytic control ansatze that are robust to calibration noise.

Method. A physics-informed LLM framework that uses a Proposal Agent to generate symbolic control ansatze and a Reflection Agent to provide feedback, followed by SPSA-based numerical refinement of coefficients.

Model / system. A general framework applicable to diverse systems including single- and multi-qubit systems, closed and open quantum dynamics, Transmon qubits, and NMR spin systems.

Key observables. Fidelity (state and gate overlap), query efficiency, robustness to calibration noise, and pulse-resolution scaling.

Important parameters / regimes. Calibration noise levels (sigma = 0.02), pulse duration (T), time discretization (Nt), and coupling strengths (J).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes that control pulses can be represented by a constrained symbolic grammar and that the LLM can reason about physical principles like counter-diabatic driving.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the VF-QCTRL architecture and benchmark composition; Figure 2 compares fidelity across tasks in noiseless and noisy regimes; Figure 3 shows fidelity distributions under noise; Figure 5 shows alignment with analytical counter-diabatic protocols; Figure 6 shows convergence trajectories; Figure 7 compares token efficiency.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the limitations of current methods, presents the VF-QCTRL architecture (Proposal, Reflection, and Refinement agents), introduces the QCTRL-Bench, evaluates performance across various tasks and noise regimes, and discusses scaling and interpretability.

Abstract

Quantum control is essential for quantum information science and technology, yet designing high-fidelity control protocols remains challenging due to complex optimization landscapes, hardware noise, and long pulse sequences. Existing numerical solvers often require problem-specific engineering and produce opaque control amplitudes, while naive large language models (LLMs) lack the physical consistency and long-horizon precision for reliable quantum control synthesis. Here we introduce VF-QCTRL, a physics-informed large language model framework for general quantum control that combines symbolic reasoning with optimization to propose analytic control ansätze and coherently refine their parameters through feedback. To systematically evaluate LLM-driven quantum control, we develop QCTRL-BENCH, a benchmark spanning sixteen tasks across single- and multi-qubit systems, closed and open quantum dynamics, noiseless and noisy settings, and both analytic and numerical protocols. Across the benchmark, VF-QCTRL demonstrates strong universality, accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability: it applies to generic quantum control systems without task-specific training, achieves performance competitive with or exceeding state-of-the-art conventional solvers in both noiseless and noisy regimes with query efficiency, exhibits favorable inference-time scaling and pulse resolution scaling, and derives physically interpretable analytical protocols directly from prompts. Our results establish physics-informed LLM-based quantum control as a promising paradigm for accurate, efficient, interpretable, and training-free quantum control protocol design across a broad range of quantum systems.

Hybrid Quantum-Classical Machine Learning Algorithms for Multi-Output Time-Series Forecasting at Utility Scale

Authors: Mackenson Polché, Varun Puram, Aditi Lal, Weronika Golletz, Joan Étude Arrow, Vardaan Sahgal, Kumar Ghosh, Giorgio Cortiana, Corey O'Meara
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24252v1

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates the application of hybrid quantum machine learning to large-scale energy demand forecasting. It shows that while quantum kernels can significantly outperform classical ones in simulation, real-world hardware noise currently limits the practical advantage on NISQ devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the feasibility and performance of hybrid quantum-classical machine learning algorithms for multi-output time-series forecasting of energy consumption at a utility scale.

Main result. The QGP model achieved a 62.01% MAE reduction on simulator and 40.37% on hardware compared to classical GP, while KQRC-RM showed significant improvement on simulator but performance degradation on hardware due to device noise.

Method. Two hybrid frameworks were tested: Kernelized Quantum Reservoir Computing with Repeated Measurement (KQRC-RM) and Projected Quantum Kernel Gaussian Process (QGP) using local reduced-state statistics.

Model / system. The experiments were performed on the IBM marrakesh superconducting quantum processor and an MPS simulator, using a Smart Meter dataset of 103 residential electricity consumption time-series.

Key observables. Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared Error (MSE), geometric difference (gCQ), and model complexity (kappa).

Important parameters / regimes. 100+ qubit scales (114 qubits for KQRC-RM, 100 qubits for QGP), number of system qubits per customer, and hardware noise parameters (T2 dephasing and CZ gate error).

Assumptions / limitations. The QGP model assumes conditional independence across outputs; hardware performance is limited by NISQ-era noise, decoherence, and readout errors.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the KQRC-RM circuit; Figure 2 shows the QGP feature map; Figure 3 displays geometric difference and model complexity; Figure 4 compares simulator vs hardware trajectories; Figure 5 shows per-horizon MAE; Figure 6 shows MSE for 5-customer groups; Figure 7 benchmarks against classical baselines; Figure 8 maps hardware noise; Figure 9 shows energy profiles and customer grouping; Figure 10 shows error vs qubit scaling; Figure 11 shows training loss and forecasting results; Figures 12-13 map the hardware noise landscape.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the forecasting problem, describes the two hybrid quantum architectures (KQRC-RM and QGP), details the experimental setup on IBM hardware and simulators, presents performance benchmarks against classical models, analyzes the impact of hardware noise, and concludes with scalability and utility-scale findings.

Abstract

Multi-output time-series forecasting in energy systems is challenging because of nonlinear dynamics, multi-scale seasonality, and strong dependencies across correlated series. In this work, we investigate two hybrid quantum-classical frameworks for multi-stream time-series forecasting on a real Smart Meter dataset comprising 103 household electricity consumption time-series, with experiments executed on the $ibm\_marrakesh$ superconducting quantum processor. The first model, Kernelized Quantum Reservoir Computing with Repeated Measurement (KQRC-RM), combines coupled quantum reservoirs, ancilla-assisted repeated measurement, and kernelized readouts to model temporal dynamics and cross-stream correlations jointly. For a 3-stream time-series input and output, the KQRC-RM model using 114 qubits achieves an MAE of 0.0811 on MPS simulator (36.92\% improvement over its classical analog) whereas performance degrades to an MAE of 0.1524 on hardware. The second, a Projected Quantum Kernel Gaussian Process (QGP), replaces fidelity-based kernels with projected kernels constructed from local reduced-state statistics. Using a topology-aware 100-qubit QGP model to predict 100 multi-output time-series values, we observe 49\% of time-series outputs achieve high-accuracy predictions (MAE $<0.15$), with an average MAE of $0.082$ for this low-error group. The medium-error regime (MAE $0.15$-$0.35$) has an average MAE of $0.229$, while the high-error regime (MAE $>0.35$) has an average MAE of $0.664$. Overall, this reduces the average MAE relative to the classical GP baseline by 62.01\% on MPS simulator and 40.37\% on hardware. Together, these results demonstrate the feasibility of hybrid quantum machine learning for multi-input, multi-output time-series forecasting at the 100+ qubit scale on NISQ devices.

Geometric phases of reduced states in the transverse-field Ising chain

Authors: Chiragkumar R. Vasani, Erik Sjöqvist
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24645v1

Summary. This paper compares the interferometric and Uhlmann geometric phases as indicators of quantum phase transitions in the transverse-field Ising model. It demonstrates that the interferometric phase is a superior order parameter because it correctly reflects the absence of long-range correlations in the paramagnetic phase. The work highlights how different geometric definitions respond uniquely to many-body critical fluctuations.

Why it may be interesting. This is interesting for many-body dynamics and open quantum systems because it explores how geometric structures in mixed-state density matrices encode information about quantum criticality and long-range correlations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates whether two types of geometric phases for mixed quantum states—the interferometric geometric phase and the Uhlmann phase—can serve as effective order parameters to identify quantum phase transitions.

Main result. The interferometric geometric phase deviation is found to be a more reliable indicator of the quantum phase transition in the transverse-field Ising model than the Uhlmann phase, as it vanishes for large spin separation in the paramagnetic phase.

Method. The authors use Jordan-Wigner, Fourier, and Bogoliubov transformations to diagonalize the Hamiltonian and calculate two-site reduced density matrices via Toeplitz determinants, then compute the geometric phases using local unitary rotations and the purification method.

Model / system. The transverse-field Ising chain, an exactly solvable model with a Hamiltonian consisting of nearest-neighbor Ising interactions and a transverse magnetic field.

Key observables. Interferometric geometric phase deviation (delta gamma), Uhlmann phase deviation (delta gamma_U), magnetization, and spin-spin correlation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Transverse field strength (lambda), inter-site distance (r), and rotation angle (theta).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on the zero-temperature ground state in the thermodynamic limit (N to infinity).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the interferometric phase deviation as a function of lambda for various distances; Figure 2 shows the Uhlmann phase deviation dependence on lambda, distance, and angle; Figure 3 compares the sensitivity of both phases near the critical point.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of geometric phases as order parameters, describes the Ising model and the mathematical framework for density matrices, presents the calculation of both geometric phases, compares their behavior across the phase transition, and concludes with a discussion on their inequivalent structures.

Abstract

Geometric phases have been extensively investigated in a wide range of quantum systems, often revealing deep connections to the underlying topology of many-body states. In this work, we examine two geometric phases defined for mixed quantum states$-$the interferometric geometric phase and the Uhlmann phase$-$extracted from two-site reduced density matrices of the transverse-field Ising model with nearest-neighbor interacting spins. By applying coordinated local unitary rotations to the spins, we compute the geometric phases associated with the two-site states across the critical point. We find that the interferometric phase is a more reliable indicator of the quantum phase transition in this model than the Uhlmann phase.

Global quantum phase estimation via hybrid quantum--classical learning

Summary. This paper presents a hybrid quantum-classical neural network approach to achieve global quantum phase estimation without the typical loss of precision or phase ambiguity. By jointly training a shallow quantum circuit and a classical decoder, the method creates a well-conditioned, globally invertible mapping across the full 2pi phase range. The study highlights that the symmetry of the classical activation functions is crucial for maintaining stable and accurate estimation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel way to use machine learning to overcome the precision-dynamic range trade-off in quantum sensing, specifically by optimizing the geometric structure of the quantum-classical interface.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The fundamental trade-off in quantum metrology between high local precision and a large unambiguous dynamic range, which often leads to phase ambiguity or 'branch folding' in standard variational interferometers.

Main result. The introduction of a hybrid quantum-classical neural network interferometer (VQ-CNNI) that achieves global phase estimation over the full [-pi, pi) range with high precision by jointly optimizing quantum encoding and classical decoding.

Method. End-to-end joint optimization of a shallow parameterized quantum circuit and a classical neural network using a circular loss function and analyzing the geometric conditioning of the learned representation.

Model / system. A hybrid variational quantum-classical neural network interferometer (VQ-CNNI) utilizing a shallow Ramsey-type quantum circuit with unitary evolution U = exp(-i * phi * Jz) and projective measurements of population imbalance.

Key observables. Squared Wrapped Phase Error (SWPE), Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), Decoding Jacobian (J), and measurement statistics p(m|phi).

Important parameters / regimes. Phase range [-pi, pi), quantum circuit parameters (theta, vartheta), neural network weights and biases, and activation functions (e.g., Softsign, Tanh).

Assumptions / limitations. The performance advantage is attributed to the joint optimization of the hybrid architecture rather than increased quantum circuit depth.

Figures summary. Fig 1: VQ-CNNI pipeline; Fig 2: Comparison of predicted vs. ground-truth phase and SWPE for VQI vs. VQ-CNNI; Fig 3: Visualization of quantum feature and latent manifolds; Fig 4: Evolution of QFI and SWPE during training; Fig 5: Impact of activation function symmetry on SWPE and Jacobian statistics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the VQ-CNNI architecture, details the joint optimization method and circular loss function, compares performance against a VQI baseline, analyzes the geometric conditioning of the learned features, investigates the role of activation function symmetry, and concludes with the implications for programmable quantum metrology.

Abstract

Achieving both high precision and large dynamic range remains a central challenge in quantum metrology, as improving local sensitivity typically reduces the unambiguous estimation range. Variational quantum interferometers enhance precision but are generally limited to narrow operating regimes. Here we introduce a hybrid variational quantum--classical neural network interferometer (VQ-CNNI), where a shallow quantum circuit encodes phase-dependent measurement statistics and a neural network performs nonlinear phase reconstruction. Joint optimization enables accurate and unambiguous phase estimation over $[-π,π)$ without loss of precision. We show that this performance requires co-optimization of quantum encoding and classical decoding. Visualization of the learned representation geometry links global estimation to well-conditioned measurement statistics across the full phase range, enabling stable inversion. Odd-symmetric activations further improve robustness by promoting global consistency. These results suggest that global quantum metrology can be understood through the learnability of the quantum--classical representation, providing a practical route to programmable interferometers with both high precision and large dynamic range.

Summary. This paper develops a theory showing that the sideband peaks in quantum wave mixing can act as a fingerprint for antibunched light. It demonstrates that by tuning the linewidth ratio of a source and probe qubit, one can observe the parametric suppression of specific multiphoton pathways. This allows for the frequency-domain identification of the photon statistics of microwave radiation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel spectroscopic method to characterize the quantum nature of itinerant light using nonlinear wave mixing, which is highly relevant for characterizing photon statistics in circuit QED and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an analytical theory to use cascaded quantum wave mixing (QWM) as a spectroscopic tool to identify the photon statistics (specifically antibunching) of incident microwave radiation.

Main result. The study establishes that the sideband hierarchy in QWM provides a frequency-domain fingerprint of antibunched light, where higher-order sidebands are parametrically suppressed based on the linewidth ratio of the source and probe.

Method. The authors use a weak-drive Taylor expansion and a Neumann series expansion of the stationary cascaded master equation, validated by numerical solutions of the full dynamics.

Model / system. A cascaded waveguide-QED system consisting of a source qubit and a probe qubit coupled unidirectionally to a one-dimensional microwave waveguide, modeled using a cascaded master equation.

Key observables. Probe coherence, probe inversion, and the amplitudes/frequencies of the QWM sideband hierarchy.

Important parameters / regimes. The ratio of decay rates (gamma_s/gamma_pr), the dimensionless cascaded coupling (alpha), and the drive amplitudes (Omega_s, Omega_pr).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes a weak-drive regime, a stationary (steady-state) approximation, and negligible back-action from the probe to the source.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of multiphoton processes; Figure 2 compares analytical and numerical QWM spectra in the antibunching and coherent-filtering regimes; Figure 3 illustrates the scaling of side-peak amplitudes as a function of the source linewidth.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of using QWM for photon statistics, presents the analytical framework using a master equation and Taylor expansion, discusses the two physical limits (coherent-filtering vs. antibunching), provides numerical validation, and concludes with the diagnostic utility of the sideband hierarchy.

Abstract

Quantum wave mixing on a single superconducting qubit produces a hierarchy of coherent side peaks associated with elastic multiphoton scattering pathways. In a cascaded source--probe geometry these pathways become sensitive to the photon statistics of the radiation emitted by the source qubit. We develop an analytical theory of this effect starting from the cascaded master equation in the weak-driving regime. In the coherent-filtering limit $γ_{\rm s}\ggγ_{\rm pr}$, the standard coherent--coherent wave-mixing hierarchy is recovered. In the opposite limit $γ_{\rm pr}\ggγ_{\rm s}$, side peaks associated with multiphoton absorption from the antibunched source field are parametrically suppressed. Numerical solutions confirm the analytical scaling laws. The resulting sideband hierarchy provides a frequency-domain fingerprint of antibunched itinerant microwave light.

Authors: Chen-Hao Zhao, Jia-Rui Li, Yuping Tian, Wei-Jiang Gong
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25386v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper extends the theory of the Non-Hermitian Skin Effect to nonperiodic and disordered systems using a new site-resolved 'twisting' framework. It introduces the Zahlen-Brillouin Zone and discovers a multiple-channel skin effect arising from competing nonreciprocal sites. This provides a universal way to diagnose non-Hermitian physics in both crystalline and amorphous media.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a universal framework for understanding non-Hermitian localization in the absence of translational symmetry, which is highly relevant for studying open quantum systems and non-equilibrium dynamics in disordered environments.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The Generalized Brillouin Zone (GBZ) description of the Non-Hermitian Skin Effect (NHSE) is limited to translationally invariant systems, failing to account for localization in disordered, quasiperiodic, or amorphous lattices.

Main result. The authors develop a site-resolved theory that introduces the Zahlen-Brillouin Zone (ZBZ) and identifies a Multiple-Channel Skin Effect (MCSE) where competing nonreciprocal sites lead to complex localization patterns.

Method. A Local Scaling Transformation (LST) is used to absorb nonreciprocity into coordinates, alongside a new metric-state correspondence (MSC) principle and a local lattice twisting parameter (Tn).

Model / system. Generic nonreciprocal lattices, including periodic, nonperiodic, and quasi-periodic (modulated) systems, described by a Hamiltonian with asymmetric intracell hoppings.

Key observables. Local lattice twisting (Tn), Global skin index (Gamma), and the Maximal Normalized Cumulative Twisting Weight.

Important parameters / regimes. Unidirectional Twisting (UT) regime, Competing Twisting (CT) regime, and the Antagonistic vs. Complete phases.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes the existence of a metric operator and a local scaling transformation that can map the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian to a Hermitian counterpart.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the LST and ZBZ discretization; Figure 2 shows the localization profiles; Figure 3 displays the skin index and ZBZ distribution for non-periodic and quasi-periodic models.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the limitation of GBZ, develops the LST and ZBZ framework, defines the local twisting parameter, classifies NHSE phases (UT vs CT), and establishes the metric-state correspondence.

Abstract

The non-Hermitian skin effect (NHSE) is a hallmark of non-Hermitian system, yet its generalized Brillouin zone (GBZ) description is restricted to periodic systems. We develop a site-resolved theory via a local scaling transformation (LST), introducing local twisting $T_n$ to quantify metric operator $ξ$ nontriviality. This elucidates the NHSE's origin and uncovers the generalized multiple-channel skin effect (MCSE). Exploiting $T_n$'s translational independence, we define the Zahlen-Brillouin Zone (ZBZ), extending non-Hermitian band theory to nonperiodic and disordered lattices. Furthermore, we unify the $ξ$ with GBZ Riemannian geometry, establishing the metric and state correspondence (MSC) as the principle for real-space localization. With a global skin index $\mathbfΓ$ for phase transitions, our results provide a universal paradigm for non-Hermitian physics in both crystalline and amorphous media.

QCommE2E: An Open-Source Simulation of End-to-End Quantum Communication Systems

Authors: Omar Alnaseri
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24723v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces QCommE2E, an open-source simulation framework designed for modeling end-to-end quantum communication systems. It provides a unified way to simulate various quantum channels and evaluate performance metrics like BER and SER. The framework is particularly useful for designing and benchmarking novel quantum communication architectures.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified software tool for simulating open quantum systems and noise models (CPTP maps) in the context of quantum communication, which is highly relevant to quantum optics and network design.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a comprehensive, open-source, and tutorial-oriented simulation framework capable of integrating heterogeneous quantum channels (qubit-based and bosonic surrogates) for end-to-end quantum communication system analysis.

Main result. The development of QCommE2E, a modular Python-based simulation framework that successfully demonstrates a unified pipeline for comparing various quantum channel degradation mechanisms using 16-QAM modulation.

Method. A modular end-to-end pipeline (Modulator -> Transmitter -> Channel -> Receiver -> Metrics) using a density-matrix interface and a Pretty Good Measurement (PGM) detector.

Model / system. A quantum communication link utilizing density-matrix representations of quantum states, incorporating channels such as Depolarizing, Dephasing, Erasure, Bosonic (truncated), Turbulence, and PMD surrogates.

Key observables. Bit Error Rate (BER), Symbol Error Rate (SER), constellation diagrams, and Bloch sphere representations.

Important parameters / regimes. 16-QAM embedding, 4000 symbol benchmark, 3dB bosonic loss, and various channel-specific noise parameters.

Assumptions / limitations. The 16-QAM embedding is an engineering approximation rather than a full physical model; the bosonic channel is restricted to a low-dimensional (dim=2) subspace; the system is assumed to be memoryless.

Figures summary. Figures 1 and 2 illustrate the impact of weak turbulence on 16-QAM constellations and Bloch spheres, while Figures 3 and 4 show the effects of 3dB loss in a bosonic channel.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the software motivation, details the mathematical interface and channel implementations, demonstrates a benchmark channel-comparison workflow, and concludes with future work directions.

Abstract

This paper presents QCommE2E as an open-source simulation framework for end-to-end quantum communication systems, with explicit tutorial emphasis. The primary objective is to develop a comprehensive framework that includes transmitters, receivers, communication channels, performance metrics, and visualization tools, to facilitate the systematic design, configuration, and analysis of experimental simulations for novel quantum communication architectures. As the primary use case, we walk through the current quantum channel comparison, which maps textbook quantum-information channels and reduced optical-fiber/free-space surrogates into a single executable benchmark. We describe the common density-matrix interface, the matched modulation and detection chain, and the exact role of the channel classes Depolarizing Channel, Dephasing Channel, Erasure Channel, Bosonic Channel, Turbulence Channel, and PMD Channel. We also explain the current visualization layer, which projects received states onto constellation and Bloch representations for qualitative inspection. To keep the implementation-faithful, we provide a summary of the baseline execution, which uses a square 16-QAM embedding, a pretty-good-measurement detector constructed from the same reference-state codebook, and BER/SER. Finally, we position the channel-comparison as an entry point for broader future work, including equalization, quantum autoencoder, learning-based, and system-level algorithm integration.
