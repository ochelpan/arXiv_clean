Tuning quantum tunneling in WSe$_2$ via strain engineering

Authors: Rachid El Aitouni, Hasna Chnafa, Clarence Cortes, David Laroze, Ahmed Jellal
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24851v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical study demonstrates that mechanical strain can be used as a 'control knob' to manipulate the spin and valley degrees of freedom in WSe2. By combining strain with electrostatic engineering, researchers can create highly efficient, reconfigurable filters for quantum transport. This provides a promising pathway for designing next-generation valleytronic and spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how uniaxial strain and electrostatic potentials can be used to tune quantum transport, specifically spin and valley polarizations, in monolayer WSe2.

Main result. Strain acts as a powerful tuning parameter that modifies electronic dispersion and induces oscillations in conductance and transmission, enabling highly controllable spin and valley filtering.

Method. Analytical solution of the Dirac equation using a transfer matrix approach and the Landauer-Buttiker formalism to calculate conductance and polarization.

Model / system. A monolayer of tungsten diselenide (WSe2) featuring a rectangular electrostatic potential barrier and subject to uniaxial strain, modeled via a low-energy Dirac framework with spin-orbit coupling and a strain-induced gauge field.

Key observables. Transmission probability, conductance, spin polarization, and valley polarization.

Important parameters / regimes. Strain (u_xx), barrier height (V0), barrier width (D), incident energy (E), and incident angle (phi).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a low-energy Dirac-like band structure, ballistic transport, and uniaxial strain along the transport direction with uniform deformation.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of the strained WSe2 system; Fig 2: Transmission probability vs. incident angle; Fig 3: Spin-up transmission vs. strain; Fig 4: Spin-up/down transmission vs. barrier height; Fig 5: Conductance vs. strain; Fig 6: Polarization vs. barrier width; Fig 7: Spin and valley polarization vs. strain.

Paper structure. Introduction of the physical system and problem, description of the Dirac Hamiltonian and strain-induced gauge field, derivation of transmission/reflection coefficients via boundary conditions, numerical results for transport observables, and discussion of applications in spintronics/valleytronics.

Abstract

We present a comprehensive theoretical study of strain-engineered quantum transport in monolayer tungsten diselenide (WSe$_2$) in the presence of an electrostatic scalar potential. By incorporating strain effects within a low-energy Dirac framework, we analyze their impact on spin- and valley-resolved transmission, conductance, and polarization. The applied potential barrier partitions the system into three distinct regions, allowing for an analytical derivation of the wave functions in each domain. Enforcing continuity conditions at the interfaces yields exact expressions for the transmission and reflection amplitudes. The transmission probability is evaluated from the corresponding current densities, while the conductance is obtained using the Landauer-Büttiker formalism, enabling a quantitative determination of spin and valley polarizations. Our numerical analysis reveals that strain acts as a powerful tuning parameter that reshapes the electronic dispersion and strongly modifies transport characteristics. In particular, the transmission and conductance exhibit pronounced oscillatory behavior driven by quantum interference and resonant tunneling mechanisms. More importantly, both spin and valley polarizations display substantial and highly controllable variations as functions of strain, barrier height, and incident energy. These results demonstrate that strain and electrostatic engineering provide an efficient and versatile platform for manipulating spin-valley degrees of freedom in WSe$_2$. The ability to tailor polarization and interference effects suggests promising opportunities for the design of next-generation spintronic, valleytronic, and optoelectronic devices based on two-dimensional transition-metal dichalcogenides.

Emergence of Triplet Superconductivity from Cavity Vacuum Fluctuations

Authors: Xin-Xin Yang, Shuai Zhang, Kun Ding, Xiaopeng Li
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24439v1

Summary. This paper shows that the vacuum fluctuations of a cavity can be used to engineer new superconducting phases in organic materials. By anisotropically renormalizing the electronic bands, the cavity can trigger a transition from singlet to triplet pairing. This establishes cavity vacuum engineering as a tool for generating unconventional and potentially topological superconducting states.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a novel way to use vacuum-mediated light-matter interactions to control many-body phase transitions and manipulate the symmetry of superconducting order parameters.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether cavity vacuum fluctuations can drive a symmetry transition from singlet to triplet superconductivity by reshaping the electronic band structure and Fermi surface.

Main result. Above a critical light-matter coupling, the cavity vacuum fluctuations anisotropically renormalize hopping amplitudes, causing the leading superconducting instability to switch from a singlet d-wave to a triplet p-wave state.

Method. The authors use a quantum-Floquet expansion in the high-frequency regime to derive an effective Hamiltonian, combined with linearized BCS theory and symmetry analysis of the C2v group.

Model / system. A tight-binding extended Hubbard model representing the quasi-2D organic superconductor kappa-(ET)2X, coupled to a quantized electromagnetic field in a dark cavity.

Key observables. Superconducting transition temperature (Tc), gap structure, Fermi surface contours, and the zero-energy spectral function.

Important parameters / regimes. Effective light-matter coupling (geff), cavity polarization directions (ex, ey, e13), and the cavity compression factor (C).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a high-frequency regime where the Hamiltonian is projected onto the zero-photon sector and neglects cavity-induced corrections to the interaction vertex.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the singlet-to-triplet pairing switch, the crystal structure of kappa-(ET)2X in a cavity, and the underlying four-site lattice structure.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of cavity engineering, defines the effective Hamiltonian via Floquet projection, performs symmetry analysis of pairing channels, demonstrates the mechanism of band renormalization, and concludes with the emergence of the triplet phase.

Abstract

Engineering quantum materials with cavity fields has emerged as a powerful route to manipulate phases of quantum matter in solids. Here we demonstrate that cavity vacuum fluctuations alone can drive the emergence of triplet superconductivity in an otherwise singlet superconductor. The vacuum field renormalizes the electronic band structure in a polarization dependent manner, reshaping the Fermi surface and altering the competition among symmetry allowed pairing channels. As a result, multiple superconducting phases arise from the cavity vacuum fluctuations. Above a critical light matter coupling, the leading instability switches from singlet to triplet pairing, yielding a superconducting state absent in the bare material. This vacuum induced symmetry transition produces distinct modifications of the gap structure and low energy quasiparticle spectrum. Our results establish cavity vacuum engineering as a mechanism for generating unconventional superconducting phases and stabilizing triplet states of potential relevance for topological superconductivity.

Summary. This paper proposes a 'quantum printing' technique where the spatial structure of Laguerre-Gaussian light pulses is used to programmatically create topological magnetic textures in ferromagnets. By tuning the spin and orbital angular momentum of the light, researchers can deterministically imprint complex patterns like skyrmions without needing specific material properties. This approach offers a versatile, ultrafast, and material-agnostic method for controlling magnetic topology.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a unique way to use structured light (quantum optics) to control and program the topology of a many-body magnetic system, bridging the gap between optical angular momentum and emergent magnetic textures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a material-agnostic, non-contact, and ultrafast method to create and control complex, spatially programmable topological magnetic textures like skyrmions without relying on intrinsic material properties like Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya interactions.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that Laguerre-Gaussian (LG) pulses can 'print' various magnetic textures (skyrmions, domain walls, multi-ring structures) by using optical quantum numbers (SAM, OAM, and radial order) to act as a symmetry-selected torque during an optical quench.

Method. Micromagnetic simulations using the Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation with a fourth-order Runge-Kutta integration scheme on a uniform Cartesian mesh.

Model / system. A collinear ferromagnetic thin film modeled with a Heisenberg spin Hamiltonian featuring only intrinsic isotropic exchange coupling and an external time-dependent magnetic field induced by THz Laguerre-Gaussian pulses.

Key observables. Magnetization field (m_z, m_x, m_y), topological charge density, skyrmion number (Q), and spin angular momentum/orbital angular momentum transfer.

Important parameters / regimes. Optical quantum numbers (s, l, p), exchange coupling (J = 5 meV), Gilbert damping (alpha = 0.1), and THz pulse electric field strength (E0 = 1.5e10 V/m).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a simplified system with only isotropic exchange, deliberately omitting Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya interactions, anisotropy, and dipolar fields to prove the fundamental printing principle.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of light-driven spin dynamics; Figure 2 illustrates the temporal evolution of the optical pulse and the resulting spin response; Figure 3 displays snapshots of magnetization (m_z) for different beam configurations; Figure 7 shows complex multi-ring spin textures and in-plane magnetization maps.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of quantum printing, describes the physical mechanism of optical quenching and angular momentum transfer, presents micromagnetic results for various LG mode indices, and discusses the hierarchy of resulting magnetic textures and potential experimental implementations.

Abstract

Structured light has become a practical tool for controlling matter by applying tailored, space- and time-dependent electromagnetic fields. We show that Laguerre-Gaussian pulses imprint non-collinear magnetic textures via the spatial structure of optical magnetic field. Our route offers a direct spatial selectivity determined by the optical features without relying on material anisotropic interactions. The proposed printing approach does not require interfacial anisotropy or bulk chirality, current-driven torques, or thermal quenching. We use micromagnetic simulations to demonstrate the potential to create topological charge density emerging during the pulse and reveal control through the optical topological properties and polarization. These results suggest structured-light quantum printing as a viable approach for magnonics and motivate studies toward reconfigurable topological textures enabled by ultrafast THz optics and non-thermal control.

Summary. This paper demonstrates a new method for generating broadband, phase-coherent microwave frequency combs using the natural nonlinearity of Strontium Titanate substrates. By using two microwave pump tones, the researchers created a comb with over 1000 lines that is highly tunable and stable. This technology could significantly reduce the complexity of control electronics for large-scale superconducting quantum computers.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum control as it presents a new material platform for generating large-scale, phase-locked microwave photon states, potentially solving the 'cabling bottleneck' in quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for scalable, on-chip, and tunable cryogenic microwave frequency combs to replace bulky electronics for tasks like multiplexed control and readout in large-scale quantum processors.

Main result. Demonstrated the generation of a phase-coherent, broadband microwave frequency comb (>1000 lines) using the intrinsic Kerr nonlinearity of a Strontium Titanate (STO) substrate via cascaded four-wave mixing.

Method. The researchers used dual-tone microwave pumping on a superconducting aluminum coplanar waveguide resonator at millikelvin temperatures and analyzed the nonlinear response via microwave reflection (S11) and time-domain pulse analysis.

Model / system. A superconducting aluminum coplanar waveguide (CPW) resonator fabricated on a Strontium Titanate (STO) quantum paraelectric substrate, modeled as a nonlinear oscillator with a Kerr-type Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Resonance frequency shifts, self-Kerr coefficient, frequency comb bandwidth, Free Spectral Range (FSR), spectral flatness, and long-term phase stability.

Important parameters / regimes. Operating temperature ~600 mK, Kerr coefficient of -10.20e-11 Hz per photon, comb bandwidth >150 MHz, and tunable FSR from 10 kHz to 1 MHz.

Assumptions / limitations. The resonator is assumed to be in an over-coupled regime, and the nonlinearity is primarily attributed to the third-order Kerr effect from soft transverse phonon modes in the STO.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the device schematic, the dual-tone pumping scheme, and the power-dependent resonance evolution; Figure 2 displays the broadband comb spectrum and its high spectral flatness; Figure 4 demonstrates the long-term phase stability of the comb lines.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the motivation for on-chip combs, describes the STO-based experimental platform, quantifies the intrinsic Kerr nonlinearity, demonstrates the generation of the broadband frequency comb, and concludes with stability and tunability analysis.

Abstract

Here, we demonstrate an on-chip cryogenic microwave frequency-comb on a planar superconducting resonator fabricated on Strontium titanate(STO), a quantum paraelectric material. The material's Kerr-type nonlinearity arising from the quantum paraelectric phase, an underexplored state, enables pronounced resonance shifts, Duffing-like bifurcations and comb generation via cascaded foure-wave mixing. Our results establish STO resonators as a low power, cryogenically compatible platforms for nonlinear microwave photonics, with applications in scalable quantum control and on-chip frequency synthesis.

Summary. This paper analytically derives the conditions for the onset of chaos in the motion of phi^4 kinks under external driving and spatial modulation. It demonstrates that the kink's internal shape mode can significantly lower the threshold for chaos through a resonant back-reaction. The analytical predictions from the reduced-order models are shown to be consistent with the full field-theory dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. The study of how internal degrees of freedom (shape modes) can resonantly enhance chaos in a driven system is highly relevant to the study of non-equilibrium dynamics and driven-dissipative many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the emergence of chaotic dynamics and the mechanism of separatrix splitting in the reduced-order models of driven and spatially modulated phi^4 kinks.

Main result. The study provides an explicit analytical characterization of chaos via Melnikov theory, identifying that internal-mode excitation acts as a resonant amplifier that lowers the chaos threshold.

Method. The author uses Melnikov theory applied to translation-only and two-collective-coordinate (2-CC) reductions, supplemented by numerical simulations including Lyapunov exponents and Poincaré sections.

Model / system. A driven-damped phi^4 field theory on a real line featuring spatial modulation (pinning), traveling-wave forcing, and linear dissipation.

Key observables. Separatrix splitting amplitudes, Lyapunov exponents, Poincaré sections, and the back-reaction coefficient alpha.

Important parameters / regimes. Dissipation (eta), external bias (Gamma), modulation amplitudes (epsilon_1, epsilon_2), and the relationship between driving frequency (omega) and internal-mode frequency.

Assumptions / limitations. Smallness of modulation and dissipation parameters, and the assumption of sufficient spatial localization of the kink to ensure well-defined projection integrals.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares critical forcing thresholds and splitting amplitudes for different modes; Figure 2 and 3 show Poincaré sections, chaotic layers, and the time evolution of the kink center.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the modulated phi^4 model to deriving collective-coordinate reductions, applying Melnikov theory to find chaos criteria, and validating these via numerical simulations of both reduced and full PDE dynamics.

Abstract

We investigate the emergence of chaotic dynamics in collective-coordinate reductions of a driven and spatially modulated $φ^4$ field describing the motion of topological kinks. Focusing on finite-dimensional effective models, we consider both translation-only and constraint-consistent two--collective--coordinate reductions in the presence of spatial pinning, dissipation, and traveling-wave forcing. Using Melnikov theory, we obtain an explicit analytical characterization of separatrix splitting and derive closed-form criteria for the onset of chaotic dynamics in the reduced phase space. In the two--collective--coordinate framework the Melnikov analysis is formulated in an extended phase space, allowing the distinct roles of translational motion and internal-mode excitation to be identified. Numerical simulations of the reduced systems, including stroboscopic Poincaré sections and Lyapunov exponent computations, confirm the analytical predictions and reveal chaotic layers organized around the unperturbed separatrix.

Summary. This paper provides a quantitative theoretical framework for simulating the electrically detected magnetic resonance of silicon vacancy spin pairs in silicon carbide. It identifies the role of a shallow-level nitrogen-related complex in the observed EDMR spectra and predicts new methods for enhanced magnetic field sensing using double-quantum transitions.

Why it may be interesting. The use of Lindblad master equations to model spin-dependent recombination and the prediction of double-quantum transitions for enhanced sensing are highly relevant to researchers working on open quantum systems and quantum sensing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a quantitative theory to simulate Electrically Detected Magnetic Resonance (EDMR) of silicon vacancy-related spin pairs in silicon carbide to optimize power electronics and magnetometer technology.

Main result. The theory successfully describes room-temperature EDMR measurements and identifies that spectral features arise from the hyperfine splitting of a shallow-level spin (an incomplete K-center) rather than the vacancy itself.

Method. The authors use steady-state Lindblad master equations to model the dynamics of different charge states and the occupancy of the shallow level, incorporating a weighted sum over the nuclear spin bath.

Model / system. The system consists of silicon vacancy-related spin pairs (V1a and V2a) in 4H-SiC interacting with a nearby shallow level defect (the incomplete K-center) within 10 nm. The Hamiltonian includes Zeeman interaction, zero-field splitting, and hyperfine interactions with 29Si, 13C, and 14N.

Key observables. EDMR spectrum (change in spin-dependent recombination current dI/dB0) and integrated EDMR spectrum I(B0).

Important parameters / regimes. Spin coherence times (T1, T2), electrical transport rates, g-factors, zero-field splitting (D), and hyperfine coupling constants (A).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a three-manifold model for charge state dynamics and uses the rotating wave approximation in the rotating frame.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows EDMR diagrams for shallow donor and acceptor models illustrating recombination processes; Figure 2 compares simulated and experimental EDMR spectra for V1a vacancies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of SiC defect recombination, presents a Lindblad-based theoretical framework, applies the model to room-temperature V1a data, predicts low-temperature hyperfine resolution, and discusses potential for V2a double-quantum transitions.

Abstract

We present a quantitative theory for simulating the electrically detected magnetic resonance (EDMR) of silicon vacancy-related spin pairs in silicon carbide using steady-state Lindblad master equations. In our theory, we consider V1a and V2a deep level silicon vacancies near the (0/-) charge state transition level in proximity to a previously identified nitrogen-related complex, the incomplete K-center, due to the hyperfine, spin structure, and Landé g factor of the shallow state. Our theory describes recent room temperature measurements attributed to V1a silicon vacancies, with reasonable extracted parameters for defect spin coherence times and electrical transport rates. At lower temperatures we predict that the shallow level hyperfine structure may be spectrally resolvable. Finally, we predict the EDMR spectrum of V2a silicon vacancy-related spin pairs and predict that two-photon, double quantum transitions of the silicon vacancy's negative charge state can be electrically read-out for enhanced magnetic field sensing.

Pattern Formation with Two Length Scales: Spatiotemporal Chaos

Authors: Laura Pinkney, Alastair M. Rucklidge, Cedric Beaume
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25849v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how nonlinear interactions between two different length scales drive the transition from stable patterns to spatiotemporal chaos. By combining amplitude equation theory with direct PDE simulations, the authors show that chaos emerges from the competition between wave triads and the expansion of modes beyond the initial subspace. The work provides a new framework for classifying complex, time-dependent patterns in multi-scale systems.

Why it may be interesting. The study of how competing scales and nonlinear mode-coupling drive transitions to spatiotemporal chaos is highly relevant to understanding non-equilibrium dynamics and pattern formation in many-body systems and driven-dissipative quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the mechanisms by which nonlinear wave interactions between two distinct length scales lead to the emergence of spatiotemporal chaos (STC).

Main result. The study identifies that STC arises from the growth of modes outside a predefined subspace, driven by time-dependent competition between wave triads and, specifically for certain wavenumber ratios, four-wave interactions.

Method. The authors use weakly nonlinear theory to derive a system of eighteen-mode amplitude equations (ODEs) and compare their stability predictions with direct numerical simulations of a model partial differential equation (PDE).

Model / system. A model partial differential equation (PDE) featuring two critical wavenumbers (k=1 and k=q) and nonlinearities that break variational structure, relevant to Faraday wave experiments and reaction-diffusion systems.

Key observables. Fourier power spectrum (peak counting and fuzziness), time-derivative metrics (C1, C2, C3) for pattern classification, and growth rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Wavenumber ratio q (specifically q = 1/sqrt(7)), linear growth rates (mu, nu), and quadratic/cubic interaction coefficients.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the dynamics can be captured by a truncated expansion of amplitude equations near the onset of instability.

Figures summary. Schematics of wavevector interactions, growth rate plots, Fourier spectra showing pattern types, and demonstrations of the pattern classification method using local Fourier analysis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of two-scale pattern formation, derives amplitude equations via weakly nonlinear theory, performs stability analysis of various equilibrium patterns, presents large-scale PDE simulations, and details a robust classification method for complex chaotic states.

Abstract

Three-wave interactions (or resonant triads) are the lowest-order nonlinear interaction in pattern formation and arise between waves with different orientations when the sum of two wavevectors equals a third one. When a pattern has only one length scale, stripe patterns are possible but three-wave interactions are responsible for the prevalence of hexagons close to onset. In problems with two length scales, there is a much wider range of possible three-wave interactions, leading to more complex structures such as superhexagons, stars, quasipatterns and even spatiotemporal chaos (STC). We investigate the role that nonlinear wave interactions play in the formation of STC in a model partial differential equation (PDE) in the case that the length scale ratio is $\sqrt{7}$, relevant to superlattice patterns in the Faraday wave experiment. The simpler aspects of the dynamics can be represented by a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) derived from the PDE using weakly nonlinear theory. We analyze the equilibrium patterns in these ODEs and evaluate their stability, comparing the results with direct numerical simulations of the model PDE. The ODEs predict parameter regimes where there are no stable simple equilibria, which is where we typically find complex behavior in the PDE. We have conducted a careful study of the transition from simple patterns to patterns that include modes beyond the finite-dimensional subspace imposed in the reduction to the ODEs, to time-dependent competition between different triads, ending up with fully developed STC. For our choice of length scale ratio, we show that four-wave interactions also play an important role. Our analysis is relevant to any pattern-forming system with three-wave interactions involving two length scales, such as the Faraday wave experiment, coupled reaction-diffusion systems, and pattern formation in dryland vegetation.

Patterned fronts in the wake of a parameter ramp in the complex Ginzburg Landau equation

Authors: Ryan Goh, Benjamin Krewson, Nilay Patel, Kiersten Ratcliff
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24209v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper analyzes how a moving, slowly-varying parameter ramp dictates the properties of pattern-forming fronts in the complex Ginzburg-Landau equation. It shows that the transition from convective to absolute instability governs the leading-order dynamics, while higher-order corrections follow a specific scaling law derived from an Airy-type inner solution. The findings are validated through both analytical asymptotic expansions and numerical simulations.

Why it may be interesting. The study of pattern formation driven by parameter ramps and the transition between convective and absolute instabilities is highly relevant to non-equilibrium dynamics and the stability of driven-dissipative systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how a slowly-varying, propagating parameter ramp influences the formation, location, and wavenumber selection of pattern-forming fronts.

Main result. The transition between convective and absolute instability (AC instability) determines the leading-order front location and temporal frequency, with higher-order delays scaling as epsilon^(2/3) via an Airy quotient inner solution.

Method. Geometric Singular Perturbation Theory (GSPT), multiple scales analysis, and numerical continuation of heteroclinics and spectral stability.

Model / system. The supercritical complex Ginzburg-Landau equation (CGLE) with a spatially and temporally varying parameter ramp, and the Swift-Hohenberg equation for validation.

Key observables. Front interface location (delay), selected spatial wavenumber, temporal oscillation frequency, and spectral stability (eigenvalue accumulation).

Important parameters / regimes. Ramp speed (c), ramp slope (epsilon), dispersion/nonlinear coefficients (alpha, gamma), and the transition between convective and absolute instability.

Assumptions / limitations. The ramp is slowly-varying (epsilon << 1); the system is in the Benjamin-Feir stable regime (1 + alpha*gamma > 0).

Figures summary. Numerical simulations of pattern formation; plots of the complex Airy quotient function (zeros, poles, and contours); and comparisons of numerical wavenumber selection and front position against analytical AC instability predictions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the ramped CGLE, develops a traveling wave reduction, applies GSPT and multiple scales analysis to derive an Airy-based inner solution, performs numerical continuation to validate the manifold structure, and concludes with spectral stability analysis and validation using the Swift-Hohenberg equation.

Abstract

We study of the formation of pattern-forming fronts in the presence of a rigidly-propagating parameter ramp which is slowly-varying in space. In the context of the prototypical supercritical complex Ginzburg-Landau equation, we show that not only the leading order front interface, but also the selected spatial wave number is governed by the transition of the ramp between absolute and convective instability. The slow ramp then induces a further delay of the front interface and perturbation of the selected wave number, controlled by the slow passage near a complex fold of strong- and weak-stable eigenspaces. To analyze the behavior near this fold, we perform a multiple scales analysis to predict the higher-order front interface delay in terms of zeros and poles of a complex Airy quotient inner solution. We confirm these predictions with numerical continuation of heteroclinics in the associated traveling wave equation. We also numerically characterize their spectral stability, finding accumulation of eigenvalues consistent with previous results on slow absolute spectrum. We then show the leading-order absolute/convective instability heuristic accurately describes selected wave numbers in an analogous slowly-ramped Swift-Hohenberg equation.

We find that all dopants induce finite localized magnetic moments, primarily originating from $d$-orbitals of the impurity atoms. We show that these localized magnetic states give rise to nearly dispersionless bands in the vicinity of the Fermi energy (taking Co doped SnO as an example). In addition, we investigate dimensional effects by constructing nanoribbon geometries of SnO monolayer. The ribbons exhibit intrinsic edge-localized states that are largely independent of ribbon width. For chiral nanoribbons oriented along a low-symmetry direction of the square lattice, we find that oxygen-rich edges are thermodynamically most stable and remain semiconducting, whereas Sn-terminated edges host metallic one-dimensional conduction channels. Our results demonstrate that transition-metal doping and edge engineering provide effective routes to tailor the electronic properties of SnO monolayer, making it a promising candidate for future spintronic and nanoelectronic applications.

Electrostatically stabilized surface flat bands in rhombohedral graphite at zero displacement field

Summary. This paper demonstrates that even without an external displacement field, the self-consistent electrostatic screening in thick rhombohedral graphite can flatten surface bands. This discovery establishes a new, low-field regime for exploring interaction-driven phenomena like superconductivity and magnetism in large-N graphene systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether the surface flat-band regime, typically observed in few-layer rhombohedral graphene at large displacement fields, can be accessed in thick rhombohedral graphite where strong screening usually suppresses internal electric fields.

Main result. Self-consistent, nonlinear electrostatics provides a mechanism to flatten surface bands even at zero displacement field by creating a near-surface potential that counteracts quadratic dispersion. This effect is enhanced by hole doping and stronger interactions, providing a robust regime for symmetry-broken phases in large-N samples.

Method. The authors use a self-consistent iterative numerical approach to solve Gauss' Law and the tight-binding Hamiltonian simultaneously, comparing the results against analytical limits and a non-self-consistent 'frozen-charge' model.

Model / system. The system is rhombohedral (ABC-stacked) multilayer graphene/graphite with a realistic N-layer tight-binding Hamiltonian including interlayer hopping (t1), remote tunneling (v3, v4), and layer-specific electrostatic potentials (Ul) determined by screening.

Key observables. Surface-band dispersion (flatness), Density of States (DOS), layer-resolved probability density, and layer-specific electrostatic potentials.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of layers (N), displacement field (D), carrier density (n), out-of-plane dielectric constant (epsilon_perp), and remote hopping parameters (v3, v4).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a realistic tight-binding parameter set and uses the out-of-plane dielectric constant as a proxy for interaction strength; it also assumes monotonic potential profiles for certain analytical derivations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a phase diagram in the n-D plane and compares idealized vs. realistic band structures. Figure 2 illustrates the evolution of the potential profile and DOS. Supplemental figures compare self-consistent results to linear-potential and frozen-charge approximations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of screening in thick rhombohedral graphene, presents a self-consistent electrostatic model, develops the mechanism for electrostatic band flattening, analyzes the effects of doping and interaction strength, and concludes with implications for future experimental observations of symmetry-broken phases.

Abstract

Rhombohedral (ABC-stacked) multilayer graphene hosts interaction-driven phases enabled by surface flat bands at large displacement fields. In thick flakes, however, strong screening suppresses internal electric fields, raising the question of whether a flat-band regime is accessible within the same experimental paradigm. Here, we show that self-consistent, nonlinear electrostatics provides a robust alternative mechanism: even in the absence of a displacement field, a nonuniform near-surface potential flattens the surface-band dispersion and enhances the density of states. In the strong-coupling limit, electrostatics drives the system toward uniform half-filling at each momentum, yielding an asymptotically flat surface band without any gating. At realistic interaction strengths, surface-band flatness is tuned by the proximal gate, with maximal flatness achieved at hole doping when the band is empty. Combining analytic arguments with fully self-consistent calculations in a realistic model, we map the resulting low-field regime and connect to finite $N\!\sim\! 6-15$ layered samples, providing a framework for analyzing the symmetry-broken phases observed in these systems. Our results motivate future experiments in large-$N$ devices and establish a low-field regime for exploring electrostatically induced flat-band physics.
