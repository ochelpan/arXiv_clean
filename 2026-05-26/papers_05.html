Resonant interactions in the $α$-FPUT lattice with site-dependent coefficients

Authors: Lorenzo Migliorelli, Giovanni Dematteis, Sergio Chibbaro, Miguel Onorato
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24268v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · scars & prethermalization 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how spatial variations in the properties of a nonlinear 1D chain can accelerate the process of thermalization. By using wave turbulence theory, the authors demonstrate that site-dependent coefficients create new resonance channels that allow energy to redistribute much faster than in a uniform lattice.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how spatial modulation of spring stiffness and nonlinear coefficients in an alpha-FPUT lattice affects energy transfer, resonant manifolds, and the thermalization timescale.

Main result. The derivation of a new wave kinetic equation shows that spatial modulation creates a non-trivial resonant manifold, leading to significantly faster thermalization (1/epsilon^2) compared to the standard case (1/epsilon^4) and introducing a Bragg-scattering mechanism.

Method. Wave Turbulence theory using a perturbative treatment, normal form expansion (near-identity transformation), and Wick decomposition in the large-box limit.

Model / system. A one-dimensional alpha-Fermi-Pasta-Ulam-Tsingou (alpha-FPUT) lattice with site-dependent spring stiffness and nonlinear coefficients.

Key observables. Wave-action spectral density function, spectral energy density evolution, and thermalization timescales.

Important parameters / regimes. Small nonlinearity parameter epsilon, site-dependent stiffness chi_j, and nonlinear coefficient alpha_j.

Assumptions / limitations. Weak nonlinearity (epsilon << 1), weak spatial modulation of coefficients (order epsilon), and the large-box limit (L -> infinity).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the site-dependent alpha-FPUT model, applies Wave Turbulence theory and normal form expansion to derive a modified wave kinetic equation, analyzes the resulting resonant manifolds (including Bragg scattering), and discusses the implications for thermalization rates and the approach to the Rayleigh-Jeans equilibrium.

Abstract

The wave turbulence framework has proven to be an effective tool for analyzing certain features of nonlinear energy transfer in one-dimensional nonlinear chains. In this work, we extend this approach to the $α$-FPUT problem when the spring stiffness $χ$ and the nonlinear coefficient $α$ are site-dependent. Although three-wave interactions are non-resonant for constant coefficients, their spatial modulation gives rise to a non-trivial resonant manifold. In this framework, we derive a new kinetic equation that suggests the possibility of substantially faster thermalization with respect to the constant coefficient case. The new kinetic equation includes also an extra term that can be associated to the Bragg-scattering mechanism, which promotes the isotropization of the wave-action spectral density function.

Scaling features for the stress fluctuations in the OFC model

Authors: Naveen Kumar, Rahul Chhimpa, Avinash Chand Yadav
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25884v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores new scaling properties in the OFC model of earthquakes, finding that a power-law distribution exists for the difference between avalanche size and area only in the conservative limit. It also demonstrates how local dissipation fundamentally changes the dynamic exponent of stress fluctuations from ballistic to diffusive behavior.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates new scaling features in the Olami-Feder-Christensen (OFC) model, specifically the probability distribution of the difference between avalanche size and area, and the power spectra of stress fluctuations.

Main result. In the conservative OFC model, the difference between avalanche size and area follows a power-law distribution, a feature that vanishes in the dissipative regime. Additionally, the dynamic exponent shifts from nearly ballistic to diffusive behavior as dissipation increases, with local stress exhibiting 1/f noise.

Method. Numerical simulations using an efficient algorithm with active site tracking and 1D array conversion, combined with finite-size scaling (FSS) analysis and power spectra analysis via Fast Fourier Transform (FFT).

Model / system. The Olami-Feder-Christensen (OFC) model, a cellular automata model of seismic activity on a 2D square lattice with open boundary conditions, which exhibits self-organized criticality.

Key observables. Avalanche size (S), area (A), difference (X = S - A), duration (T), local and total stress fluctuations, and power spectra.

Important parameters / regimes. Dissipation parameter (alpha), where alpha = 0.25 is locally conservative and alpha < 0.25 is locally dissipative.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes uniform driving and specific redistribution rules that distinguish it from standard sandpile models.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows critical exponents as a function of alpha; Figure 2 shows the probability distribution and data collapse of X; Figure 3 shows the impact of decreasing alpha on P(X, L); Figure 4 compares X scaling in BTW and Manna models; Figures 5 and 6 show stress signals and their power spectra.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the OFC model and its parameters, presents the analysis of the size-area difference (X), compares these findings to BTW and Manna models, analyzes the power spectra of stress fluctuations, and concludes with the implications of dissipation on dynamics.

Abstract

We consider the Olami-Feder-Christensen (OFC) model on a square two-dimensional lattice with open boundary conditions. The model exhibits self-organized criticality and explains the Gutenberg-Richter law observed for earthquakes. A parameter $α$ controls the level of local dissipation: $α< 0.25$ corresponds to locally dissipative and $α= 0.25$ marks locally conservative dynamics. The avalanche size distribution follows a decaying power-law, with a non-universal critical exponent. Here, we examine the probability distribution of the difference between avalanche size and area. This quantity remains unexplored despite being of significant interest in earthquakes. We find a power-law with a scaling exponent close to one in the conservative OFC model. The scaling feature vanishes even for the physically relevant case $α= 0.21$. To examine the robustness of such features, we also examine the same quantity in the BTW and Manna sandpile models on a square lattice. We find that the power-law behavior survives for these systems due to locally conservative dynamics. We further examine the local and total stress fluctuations in the OFC model for both locally conservative and dissipative dynamics. The finite-size scaling analysis of the power spectra for the stress fluctuations reveals qualitatively the same but quantitatively significantly different behavior. The dynamic exponent describing the divergence of the correlation time with system size changes from nearly ballistic in the conservative to diffusive behavior in the locally dissipative dynamics with $α= 0.21$. The local stress also exhibits a signature of nearly canonical $1/f$ noise in the intermediate regime, and $1/f^2$-type scaling dominates the high-frequency regime.

Towards a nuclear isomer quantum battery

Authors: Ying-Bo Gao, Fu-Quan Dou
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24935v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. The paper proposes a new class of quantum batteries using nuclear isomers to achieve unprecedented energy density and power. By utilizing X-ray Free Electron Lasers to drive transitions in nuclear systems, these batteries can store much higher energy levels than atomic versions and maintain that energy for much longer periods.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a novel application of nuclear physics to quantum thermodynamics, demonstrating how extremely long-lived metastable states can overcome the decoherence and energy-density limits typical of atomic-scale quantum batteries.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing quantum batteries based on atomic or molecular systems suffer from low energy density and rapid self-discharging due to short excited-state lifetimes.

Main result. Nuclear Isomer Quantum Batteries (NIQBs) can achieve energy storage and charging power enhancements of up to 10^6 and 10^11, respectively, compared to atomic systems, with lifetimes extending up to 10^5 years.

Method. The study uses a density matrix approach with a master equation to model coherent population transfer (NCPT) and STIRAP driven by X-ray Free Electron Laser (XFEL) pulses.

Model / system. The system consists of nuclear ensembles in two-level, Lambda-type, and ladder-type configurations using various isotopes (e.g., 193Ir, 117Sn, 129Xe). The Hamiltonian includes nuclear energy levels and interaction with an external XFEL field.

Key observables. Stored energy (E), average charging power (P), ergotropy (W), energy extraction ratio (R), and system purity (P).

Important parameters / regimes. Energy scales of 10^5 eV to hundreds of keV; charging power in the Watt scale; interaction times on the femtosecond scale; lifetimes from microseconds to 10^5 years.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes the use of XFEL as a charger and focuses on regimes where spontaneous emission is negligible for most isotopes due to long isomeric lifetimes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows schematic setups for two-level, Lambda, and ladder-type NIQBs. Supplemental figures include contour plots of energy and extraction ratio versus laser intensity and energy extraction plots.

Paper structure. Introduction of the NIQB concept, description of the nuclear energy level configurations, mathematical framework for charging dynamics and energy metrics, comparative analysis between nuclear and atomic systems, and discussion of optimization and limitations.

Abstract

Quantum batteries (QBs) -- quantum devices governed by the principles of quantum mechanics -- hold great promise for next-generation energy storage. However, most existing research efforts focus on atomic or molecular systems featuring low-energy and short-lived energy levels, leaving the exploitation of high-energy, ultra-stable nuclear energy levels for energy storage as a critical unaddressed challenge. Here, we propose an innovative design of referred to as nuclear isomer quantum batteries (NIQBs), whose energy storage unit is typically composed of two-level and three-level nuclei incorporating nuclear isomers. The charging dynamics is driven by the interaction between the nuclear system and x-ray free electron laser (XFEL). Compared to QBs based on atomic systems, NIQBs deliver substantial performance enhancements, with stored energy and average charging power enhanced by factors of $10^{1}$--$10^{6}$ and $10^{6}$--$10^{11}$, respectively, and a markedly extended lifetime range spanning from microseconds to $10^5$ years. Notably, the vast majority of NIQBs enable complete energy extraction as their excited-state lifetimes exceed the laser-nuclear interaction time, rendering spontaneous emission negligible. The proposed NIQBs framework is compatible with diverse nuclear systems, enabling tailored nucleus selection for varied operating conditions. Our results provide a feasible pathway toward realizing high-performance QBs with superior energy-storage efficiency.

Wideband Balanced Photodetectors for Classical and Quantum Light Detection from Optical, EUV, to X-rays

Authors: Ivan Ryger, Terry Brown, Dina S. Eissa, Chen-Ting Liao
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24199v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5 · quantum optics experiment 2/5

Summary. This paper presents a new electronic architecture for photodetectors that allows for high-speed, low-noise measurements in the EUV and soft X-ray regimes. By using a JFET-based bootstrapping technique, the researchers overcame the bandwidth limitations typically caused by high detector capacitance. This enables more sensitive quantum-noise-limited measurements for applications in X-ray spectroscopy and EUV lithography.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The high junction capacitance of large-area silicon photodiodes in the EUV and soft X-ray regimes creates a bandwidth-noise trade-off that limits high-speed, low-noise detection.

Main result. The authors developed a wideband bootstrapped transimpedance amplifier architecture that achieves a six-fold extension in signal-to-noise limited bandwidth and a noise floor of 13 fA/sqrt(Hz).

Method. The design uses a JFET-based bootstrapping network to isolate photodiode capacitance and a grounded field plate to tune parasitic capacitance for high common-mode rejection.

Model / system. The experimental platform consists of a balanced photodetector system using silicon-based AXUV and SXUV photodiodes, featuring a bootstrapped transimpedance amplifier (TIA) with a JFET interface and an active load.

Key observables. Input-referred noise floor, signal-to-noise limited bandwidth, Common-Mode Rejection Ratio (CMRR), and spectral responsivity.

Important parameters / regimes. Spectral range from 1 nm to 1,100 nm, noise floor of 13 fA/sqrt(Hz), CMRR exceeding 30 dB up to 100 kHz, and junction capacitance reduction via reverse biasing.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes performance in the EUV-SXR range is comparable to optical results if readout conditions are maintained; notes that thermal management is required for long-term stability.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the concept of balanced detection and spectral responsivity; Fig 2 compares detector architectures and shows the circuit diagram; Fig 10/11 illustrate the field plate mechanism and the resulting CMRR performance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the detection gap in short-wavelength regimes, details the electronic architecture of the bootstrapped TIA, describes the capacitance management techniques (reverse bias and field plate), presents experimental noise and bandwidth characterization, and discusses thermal and practical implementation challenges.

Abstract

The rapid development of coherent short-wavelength light sources in the extreme ultraviolet (EUV) and soft X-ray (SXR) regimes has created a growing need for advanced optoelectronic detection capabilities, particularly for quantum-noise-limited measurements, microelectronics and semiconductor metrology, and emerging quantum information applications. However, extending balanced photodetection to these wavelength regimes is severely hindered by a fundamental bandwidth-noise trade-off imposed by the exceptionally large junction capacitance of EUV-SXR silicon photodiodes. Here, we report a novel wideband photoreceiver architecture that overcomes this bottleneck via a bootstrapped transimpedance amplifier design. By leveraging a low-noise junction field-effect transistor interface, we effectively isolate the photodiode capacitance and suppress the apparent input capacitance seen by the core amplifier. Combined with active compensation of parasitic feedback reactance, this architecture mitigates the conventional trade-off between detector active area and signal bandwidth. Experimentally, we achieved a system-level input-referred noise floor of 13 $fA/\sqrt{Hz}$, closely approaching theoretical thermal limits. Furthermore, we achieved a six-fold extension in signal-to-noise limited bandwidth and, through the implementation of a novel grounded field plate, demonstrated a common-mode rejection ratio (CMRR) exceeding 30 dB up to 100 kHz. This highly scalable, silicon-based architecture effectively bridges the short-wavelength detection gap, establishing a robust experimental platform for next-generation quantum-noise-limited and quantum-enhanced X-ray measurement, as well as ultra-sensitive inspection and metrology applications in high-numerical-aperture EUV lithography.

Characterizing emergent multi-scale dynamics in colloidal nanoparticle gels

Authors: William D. Brackett, Zachary M. Sherman, Felix Lehmkühler, Thomas M. Truskett, Delia J. Milliron
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26083v1

Summary. This perspective paper outlines how to bridge the gap between nanoscopic particle interactions and macroscopic gel properties. It emphasizes the integration of X-ray Photon Correlation Spectroscopy with rheology and simulations to characterize the multi-scale dynamics of nanoparticle networks. The goal is to transition from observing gelation to the intentional design of adaptive, programmable soft materials.

Why it may be interesting. The study of structural arrest, anomalous diffusion, and the emergence of heterogeneous dynamics in these classical systems provides a macroscopic analog to many-body localization and non-equilibrium phase transitions in quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a unified understanding of how nanoscopic interactions and hierarchical processes across multiple length and time scales translate into the macroscopic mechanical and structural properties of colloidal nanoparticle gels.

Main result. The paper proposes a framework for moving from descriptive studies to the programmable design of nanoparticle gels by integrating multi-scale dynamics (particle, cluster, and network scales) using advanced X-ray techniques and complementary probes.

Method. The authors discuss the use of X-ray Photon Correlation Spectroscopy (XPCS) and in situ rheo-XPCS to probe nanoscopic motion, alongside complementary techniques like DLS, microscopy, and coarse-grained simulations.

Model / system. The physical system consists of colloidal nanoparticle (NP) gels, including metallic, semiconductor, or insulating NPs, which can form fractal networks (DLCA/RLCA), attractive/repulsive glasses, or depletion gels through tunable interparticle interactions.

Key observables. Intensity correlation functions (g2, g1), intermediate scattering function (F(q, delta t)), relaxation time (tau), stretching/compression exponent (gamma), and nonergodicity factor (f).

Important parameters / regimes. Nanoparticle size, ligand grafting density, surface charge, volume fraction, bond strength, and spatiotemporal scales ranging from 5 nm to 500 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes Gaussian electric field distributions for the Siegert relation and assumes ergodicity in certain regimes, while explicitly addressing nonergodic/aging dynamics.

Figure 2 shows calculated field correlation functions for different dynamics (anomalous, localized) and the scaling of relaxation time with the wavevector.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the scientific problem of multi-scale dynamics in NP gels, describes the physical hierarchy of the system, details the experimental and computational platforms (specifically XPCS), establishes the mathematical framework for analyzing scattering data, and concludes with a perspective on the future of programmable soft matter design.

Abstract

Colloidal gels assembled from nanoparticles (NPs) are a versatile class of soft network-based materials capable of rich dynamic, mechanical, and even optical or magnetic responses to stimuli. Understanding how their hierarchically organized processes relate to macroscopic network properties remains a broad and unresolved problem in soft matter physics. The mechanisms of gel formation can depend sensitively on the pathway and the nature of NP interactions, thus far preventing a unified theoretical bridge between nanoscopic interactions and structural evolution and network dynamics. Indirect measurement of dynamics using light-scattering techniques provides an experimental means to quantify underlying particle and network motion. X-ray photon correlation spectroscopy (XPCS) has emerged as a powerful tool for probing nanoscopic motion in nanoparticle gels, but alone cannot resolve the full spatiotemporal spectrum of dynamics that drive gelation, aging, and network mechanical properties. While in situ rheo-XPCS enables simultaneous probing of nanoscale and bulk mechanical responses, complementary light scattering, microscopy, or simulations can extend spatiotemporal characterization and, consequently, understanding of NP gel network physics. Implementing a modular model platform with tunable primary nanoparticle features allows systematic variation of nanoscopic characteristics that drive emergent gel responses and inform the development of theoretical models for a wide range of soft, dynamic, nanostructured materials. The rapid expansion of XPCS capabilities at fourth-generation light sources, combined with complementary tools and robust model systems, positions the field to move beyond descriptive fundamental studies toward the design of nanoparticle gels with adaptive and programmable behaviors.

Detectability Limits for Intra-Block Temporal Drift in Finite-Key Entanglement-Based QKD

Authors: Rafael Duarte Marcelino, Julio Smanioto Garcia, Matheus Rufino
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24230v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes the fundamental statistical limits for detecting temporal fluctuations in QKD error rates that do not change the overall average error. It proves that the minimum detectable drift amplitude scales with the square root of the block size and shows that a CUSUM-based monitoring approach is optimal for this task.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the statistical limits for detecting intra-block temporal drift in entanglement-based Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) where the drift is mean-preserving and thus invisible to global-average error rate tests.

Main result. The authors derive a minimax optimal scaling for the minimum detectable drift amplitude, finding that delta_min = Theta(n^{-1/2}), and demonstrate that a calibrated CUSUM statistic achieves this rate.

Method. The study uses a minimax hypothesis-testing framework, applying the functional Central Limit Theorem and Donsker's theorem for the upper bound and Kullback-Leibler divergence for the lower bound, verified via Monte Carlo simulations.

Model / system. The system is an entanglement-based QKD protocol (E91-type) where the error rate is modeled as a sequence of Bernoulli trials subject to mean-preserving temporal perturbations (linear, sinusoidal, or step profiles).

Key observables. Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER), Bell-violation statistics, and the partial-sum process (S_k).

Important parameters / regimes. Block size (n), baseline error rate (e_0), significance level (alpha), target power (1-beta), and the signal functional A(g).

Assumptions / limitations. The drift is assumed to be mean-preserving (the integral of the perturbation over the block is zero) and constrained by a Lipschitz bound for physical regularity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows drift trajectories; Figure 2 visualizes the partial-sum process; Figure 6 illustrates the statistical trade-off between estimation variance and drift resolution.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of intra-block drift, defines the mathematical model and drift classes, provides theoretical proofs for the minimax bounds, presents numerical simulations for different drift profiles, and concludes with a discussion on the fundamental resolution limits.

Abstract

We study the statistical detectability of intra-block temporal drift in finite-key entanglement-based quantum key distribution, with particular relevance to E91-type parameter estimation and monitoring. Drift is modeled as a mean-preserving Lipschitz perturbation of Bernoulli observables, capturing structured temporal variation that is invisible to global-average tests. For a block of size $n$ and confidence levels $(α,β)$, we formulate a minimax hypothesis-testing problem and define the minimal detectable amplitude. We derive matching lower and upper bounds yielding $δ_{\min}(n,α,β)=Θ(n^{-1/2})$: if $nδ^2 \to 0$, no level-$α$ procedure can guarantee nontrivial uniform power over the admissible drift class, whereas a calibrated CUSUM statistic detects drift at the matching scale. Explicit constants for linear, sinusoidal, and step profiles, together with simulations, confirm the predicted scaling collapse. The result quantifies a finite-block monitoring-resolution limit and is distinct from composable security certification.

Summary. This paper proposes a generalized semiclassical propagator that uses an additional real exponent to represent quantumness and entropy. It demonstrates that this formalism can unify quantum dynamics, field theory, and the thermodynamic interpretation of gravity in cosmological models.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating a generalized semiclassical ansatz for the quantum propagator where an additive exponent R is introduced to serve as a measure of quantumness and entropy.

Main result. The framework successfully recovers classical dynamics (Hamilton-Jacobi, Friedmann, and Klein-Gordon equations) in the limit h -> 0 and provides a link between the imaginary part of the action and gravitational entropy.

Method. A semiclassical expansion of a generalized exponential ansatz K = exp(R + iS/h) applied to Schrödinger, Klein-Gordon, and Dirac equations, as well as minisuperspace models.

Model / system. The study covers non-relativistic quantum mechanics (harmonic oscillators), quantum field theory (scalar and Dirac fields), and quantum cosmology (Robertson-Walker minisuperspace).

Key observables. The quantumness function R, the classical action S, and the functional momentum density.

Important parameters / regimes. The reduced Planck constant h, the scale factor a, the cosmological constant Lambda, and the semiclassical limit h -> 0.

Assumptions / limitations. The classical action S is assumed to satisfy the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, and the expansion is truncated at the leading order in h.

Paper structure. The paper begins with non-relativistic particle models, extends the formalism to field theories (scalar and spinor), applies the ansatz to constrained cosmological systems, and concludes with a conceptual discussion on the thermodynamic connection to gravity.

Abstract

This work explores the non-relativistic quantum propagator $K(x,t)$ as a solution of the Schrödinger equation. We suppose that the propagator takes the form ${\rm exp}\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}S+R\right)$, generalizing the usual WKB ansatz by allowing an additive exponent $R$ whose role as a measure of quantumness is investigated. Since $S$ is subject to the assumption that it is a solution of the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, here we are further interested in the role of $R$ and its interpretation as a measure of quantumness. Several systems are studied as concrete examples to illustrate this approach. Furthermore, a proposal to generalize it for fields is put forward. This is tested for some simple systems. Finally, the possibilities to use this approach for the case of systems whose dynamics are controlled by the Hamiltonian constraint are analyzed.

Summary. This paper explores how the density gradients at the surface of nuclear pasta in neutron stars create a Rashba-type spin-orbit coupling. It shows that temperature gradients can drive spin polarization via a mechanism analogous to the Rashba-Edelstein effect in spintronics. This provides a new way to understand spin dynamics and angular momentum in extreme astrophysical environments.

Main problem. Investigating how density gradients at the surface of nuclear pasta in neutron stars generate spin polarization via nuclear spin-orbit coupling, specifically through a thermal Rashba-Edelstein effect.

Abstract

We discuss anomalous spin polarization on the surface of nuclear pasta in a neutron star, driven by a nuclear spin-orbit interaction. We present an effective two-band model of surface-localized neutrons near the nuclear pasta. The central point is the emergence of a Rashba-type spin-orbit hybridization generated by the neutron--nucleus spin-orbit force in the presence of the strong density gradient normal to the pasta surface. Starting from a single-particle Hamiltonian with a central potential and a standard nuclear spin-orbit interaction, we show that the surface spin polarization occurs due to the thermal inhomogeneity even in the absence of a magnetic field. Our study links neutron-star physics and solid-state spintronics and would contribute to understanding the interplay between spin dynamics and strong magnetic fields.

A particle-resolved rheological study of chirality transfer and odd transport

Authors: Rémi Goerlich, Alexander P. Antonov, Kristian Stølevik Olsen, Lorenzo Caprini, Christian Scholz, Hartmut Löwen, Yael Roichman
Type: both · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25136v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates how chirality can be transferred from an active environment to a passive object, leading to a spontaneous Hall-like transverse motion. It reveals that nonlinear friction is the key mechanism that rectifies chiral fluctuations into macroscopic transport. The findings suggest potential applications in the selective sorting of particles based on their size.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic mechanisms behind chirality transfer and the emergence of 'odd transport' (a Hall-like transverse drift) in a passive tracer embedded in a chiral active bath.

Main result. The study identifies that local collisions transfer chiral dynamics to the tracer and that nonlinear (Coulomb) friction rectifies these fluctuations into a macroscopic transverse response, which is non-monotonic with respect to the chirality radius.

Method. A combination of table-top experiments using active 'bristle-bots', many-body numerical simulations, and a reduced coarse-grained chiral active Ornstein-Uhlenbeck particle (AOUP) model.

Model / system. A 2D active granular bath of self-propelled 'bristle-bots' (active particles) containing a passive, symmetric 3D-printed tracer, subject to an external driving force and nonlinear substrate friction.

Key observables. Hall angle (phi_Hall), odd mobility (mu_odd), tracer trajectory autocorrelation functions, and local velocity/density fields around the tracer.

Important parameters / regimes. Ratio of chirality radius to tracer radius (r_c/R_T), chirality (omega), driving force (f_x), and the type of friction (Coulomb vs. Stokes).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated as a 2D active bath; the collision-induced reorientation can be modeled via a single-collision experiment; the friction is primarily governed by nonlinear substrate effects.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the experimental setup and bot/tracer trajectories; Fig 2 illustrates local density and velocity flows around the tracer; Fig 4 shows the Hall angle as a function of the r_c/R_T ratio; Fig 6 characterizes individual bot dynamics and chirality.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the phenomenon of chirality transfer, describes the experimental and simulation platforms, analyzes the mechanism of collision-induced transport, explores the role of nonlinear friction, and concludes with implications for particle sorting.

Abstract

Chirality, or the breaking of mirror symmetry, appears across all scales in nature, from molecular conformations to the dynamics of bacterial collectives. Environments composed of such symmetry-breaking constituents can give rise to emergent physical phenomena, particularly in the transport and response of embedded tracers. Yet it remains unclear how chiral environments influence such tracers and through which microscopic mechanisms anomalous responses emerge. Here, we present a particle-resolved study of these systems, demonstrating chirality transfer and odd transport of an object embedded in a chiral active bath. In a rheological experiment, a symmetric passive tracer is driven through collisions with the particles of a non-equilibrium chiral bath. Combining table-top experiments, many-body simulations, and a reduced coarse-grained theory, we demonstrate that local collisions transfer chiral active dynamics to the tracer, which displays circular trajectories. We show that the same mechanism gives rise to a systematic transverse drift under a constant pulling force. Crucially, we identify nonlinear friction as an essential factor that rectifies these transferred chiral active fluctuations into a macroscopic odd response. Our results reveal a microscopic mechanism for odd transport in chiral active matter and provide general insights into transverse transport in driven non-equilibrium systems.

By using asymptotic distributions instead of full trajectory tracking, it achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups for complex scenarios like temporal injection and multi-target competition. Classical solvers quickly reach complexity limits, so we propose Q-LEAK, a quantum-based verification approach using Grover's algorithm, compiling each CNF into an oracle and applying amplitude amplification to search in O(sqrt(N)) oracle calls, with oracles that encode the two-trace leakage predicate and the CNF constraints. Benchmarking against classical SAT shows both potential gains and practical resource limits. In noiseless tests on 5-7 variable benchmarks, Q-LEAK consistently recovered a satisfying assignment within 1-4 tries, with marked bitstrings amplified clearly above the background distribution, exceeding 20 percent probability. The evaluation of Q-LEAK on real quantum hardware revealed at least one classically verified SAT assignment, despite the presence of noise. These results point to a potential path toward quantum-assisted verification of side-channel protections.

Summary. This paper explores how the deformation of a Pöschl-Teller potential can be used to tune the performance of a quantum Otto engine and refrigerator. It shows that by adjusting the deformation parameter and potential depth, one can switch between optimal heat engine and refrigerator regimes. The findings suggest that q-deformed potentials provide a versatile platform for designing tunable quantum thermal machines.

Why it may be interesting. This work is relevant for researchers in quantum thermodynamics and open quantum systems as it demonstrates how tuning the underlying algebraic deformation and potential geometry can control the operational phases and efficiency of quantum thermal machines.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the parameters of a q-deformed modified Pöschl-Teller potential, specifically the deformation parameter q and the potential depth and width, affect the thermodynamic performance of a quantum Otto cycle.

Main result. The study identifies distinct performance regions where low-depth and high-deformation parameters favor heat engine efficiency, while high-depth and low-deformation parameters optimize refrigerator performance.

Abstract

We study the impact of the potential parameters of the q-deformed modified Pöschl-Teller potential on the thermodynamic performance of a quantum Otto cycle, where the $q$-deformed modified Pöschl-Teller potential serves as the working substance. Analytical expressions for the energy spectrum and wave functions are derived, enabling a systematic investigation of heat exchange, work output, efficiency, and coefficient of performance. We show that $q$-deformation modifies the energy spectrum and creates distinct performance regions in the ($q$, $Δ$) parameter space. Low ($Δ$) and high ($q$) favour optimal heat engine efficiency, whereas high ($Δ$) and low ($q$) improve refrigerator performance. The heat engine efficiency peaks in the low-($Δ$), high-($q$) regime. These results highlight the q-deformed modified Pöschl-Teller potential as a versatile and tunable platform for exploring potential parameter-driven effects in quantum thermal machines.
