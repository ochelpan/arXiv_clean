Probing the limits of the semiclassical Einstein equation

Authors: Gustavo Schranck Habermann, Daniel A. Turolla Vanzella
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24103v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a method to test the limits of the semiclassical Einstein equation by using a superposition of boosted masses. It shows that the nonlinear nature of gravity can cause the semiclassical average to predict a strong-gravity regime that is absent in the individual quantum branches. This provides a way to potentially distinguish between quantum and semiclassical gravity using branch-degenerate observables.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the validity and limits of the semiclassical Einstein equation, specifically whether the approximation holds when quantum fluctuations of the stress-energy tensor are large or when nonlinear gravity is involved.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the semiclassical approximation can predict a strong-gravity, nonlinear regime even when individual branches of a quantum superposition are in a weak-field regime, due to the amplification of energy-momentum in the averaged stress-energy tensor.

Method. The study uses an analytical approach with the exact Levi-Civita metric in Weyl canonical form, applying Lorentz boosts to compare the predictions of a quantum superposition of states versus a semiclassical average.

Model / system. A toy model consisting of a cylinder with low proper linear density in a coherent superposition of two opposite relativistic boost velocities.

Key observables. The rate of change of the proper circumference with respect to the proper radius (dC/dr).

Important parameters / regimes. Dimensionless mass parameter (sigma), Lorentz factor (gamma), and the Tolman gravitational mass per unit length (Sigma).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a 'flat-interior' approximation for the quantum scenario, which may break down in the semiclassical regime as the gravitational field becomes non-perturbative.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the difference in gravitational content between the two scenarios.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of semiclassical gravity validity, defines a toy model of a boosted cylinder, performs analytical calculations for both quantum and semiclassical scenarios, and compares observable predictions to demonstrate the breakdown of the approximation.

Abstract

In the context of semiclassical gravity, the semiclassical Einstein equation is often invoked when backreaction of quantum matter/fields on the spacetime is at stake. It is expected to hold when quantum fluctuations are small. Yet, it is routinely used to justify the central role of the expectation value of the stress-energy tensor of quantum fields, whose fluctuations formally diverge. Here we propose a new way to probe the limits of this approximation by exploiting peculiar nonlinearities of gravity. As a proof of principle, we construct a controlled, analytically tractable setting where the incoherent mixture of weak-gravity states drives the system into a strong-gravity regime. By selecting a branch-degenerate observable, one can compare predictions of quantum and semiclassical gravity, potentially delimiting the validity of the latter.

Q-RAIL: A Reliability-Aware Framework for Quantum Federated Learning on Heterogeneous Noisy Hardware

Authors: Walid El Maouaki, Muhammad Shafique
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25783v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces Q-RAIL, a framework designed to make Quantum Federated Learning more robust to the noise and hardware differences found in NISQ devices. By weighting client updates based on the reliability of their specific hardware and circuit depth, it prevents noisy backends from degrading the global model. This approach significantly improves learning accuracy and stability in heterogeneous quantum networks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing hardware heterogeneity in Quantum Federated Learning (QFL), where varying noise levels and transpilation-induced circuit changes across different NISQ backends cause unreliable client updates.

Main result. The Q-RAIL framework significantly improves QFL accuracy (e.g., a 10.0-point gain on MNIST) and robustness against 'bad clients' by using a reliability-aware aggregation method.

Method. Q-RAIL computes a client-specific effective noise budget using backend calibration metadata and transpiled circuit statistics, then applies a stabilized, temperature-scaled softmax weighting for aggregation.

Model / system. The study uses Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQCs) with angle-encoding feature maps and various ansatz topologies (linear, ring, full) implemented on simulated heterogeneous NISQ hardware (Qiskit FakeProvider).

Key observables. Test accuracy, test loss, Area Under the Curve (AUC), and effective noise budget.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature scaling (tau), uniform mixing (beta), minimum weight floor (w_min), qubit counts (4, 10, 15), and CX-fold noise amplification.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the server has access to backend calibration metadata and transpilation statistics; focuses on the NISQ era where noise-induced device shift is a primary driver of degradation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Q-RAIL workflow; Figure 2 ranks backends by error; Figure 3 details the methodology flowchart; Figures 4-6 show learning curves, robustness to bad clients, and scalability with client count; Figures 7-8 show heatmaps of noise budgets and accuracy relative to circuit complexity.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of hardware heterogeneity in QFL, proposes the Q-RAIL framework, details the reliability-scoring and aggregation mechanism, presents benchmarking results across various datasets and noise regimes, and discusses limitations and future directions.

Abstract

Quantum federated learning (QFL) on NISQ hardware is highly sensitive to backend heterogeneity: some clients contribute informative updates, while others contribute noise-dominated drift that uniform averaging cannot distinguish. We propose Q-RAIL (Quantum Reliability-Aware Federated Inference and Learning), a circuit- and calibration-aware aggregation method for hardware-heterogeneous QFL. Q-RAIL computes a client-specific effective noise budget from backend calibration metadata together with transpiled circuit statistics. This budget is converted into stabilized aggregation weights using temperature scaling, uniform mixing, and a minimum-weight floor. Q-RAIL was evaluated across multiple experimental settings, including an ablation study, and benchmarked against state-of-the-art methods on three datasets: MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, and OrganAMNIST. On the primary MNIST benchmark under strong hardware skew, Q-RAIL improves final test accuracy from FedAvg's 0.777 to 0.877, a +10.0-point gain corresponding to about 44.8% relative error reduction, while also exceeding the strongest wpQFL baseline (0.833). At the same time, test loss drops from 0.722 to 0.585, and test AUC rises from 0.920 to 0.973. Under non-IID MNIST, Q-RAIL reaches 0.813 vs 0.722 for FedAvg. It also outperforms FedAvg in 12/12 ansatz/CX-fold stress configurations and remains stronger at 4, 10, and 15 qubit setups. Overall, the results support calibration-driven, circuit-aware aggregation as a practical path toward robust QFL on heterogeneous quantum hardware.

QGCL: Quantum-Guided Clause Learning for Cryptanalytic SAT

Authors: Walid El Maouaki, Alberto Marchisio, Muhammad Shafique
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25756v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a hybrid algorithm called QGCL that integrates Grover search into classical SAT solvers to accelerate AES cryptanalysis. By using quantum search to provide local heuristic hints rather than attempting a global search, it significantly reduces the computational effort required to solve massive, structured search problems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of recovering AES-128 secret keys from noisy power side-channel leakage, which is a massive and highly structured combinatorial search problem impractical for full-scale Grover search.

Main result. The QGCL framework achieves up to an 86% reduction in solver conflicts and improves wall-time in ideal simulations by using Grover search to provide heuristic guidance to a classical CDCL solver.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical approach (QGCL) where Grover search is invoked on small, dynamically extracted subformulas (conflict cores) to provide branching hints (variable activities and polarities) to a classical CDCL solver.

Model / system. The problem is modeled as a Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) problem in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) derived from AES-128 power side-channel leakage, simulated using the PennyLane framework on an NVIDIA GPU.

Key observables. Solver-internal statistics including conflicts, decisions, propagations, restarts, and total wall-time.

Important parameters / regimes. Grover budget (subproblem size), maximum number of Grover calls (C_max), mixing parameter (eta_t), and the depth of the cryptanalytic problem (T).

Assumptions / limitations. The effectiveness relies on a useful operating regime where quantum subproblems are small enough for near-term hardware; the framework assumes the quantum output only influences heuristics and cannot alter the logical SAT/UNSAT result.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows asymptotic complexity scaling; Figure 2 shows the AES side-channel workflow; Figure 3 illustrates the QGCL control flow; Figure 5 shows subformula extraction; Figure 6 compares ideal vs. noisy Grover outputs; Figure 7 compares CDCL vs. QGCL performance metrics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the cryptanalytic challenge, defines the QGCL hybrid framework and circuit construction, details the subformula extraction and heuristic update methods, presents experimental results on various scales and noise levels, and concludes with future work.

Abstract

Power side-channel attacks on AES exploit data-dependent physical leakage to recover secret keys, but turning noisy leakage observations into a verified AES-128 key remains a hard combinational search problem. SAT-assisted power side-channel cryptanalysis addresses this challenge by encoding AES semantics, key constraints, plaintext/ciphertext consistency, and leakage predicates as CNF, so that candidate keys must satisfy the exact cryptographic specification. These cryptanalytic SAT formulas are large and highly structured; our largest controlled AES-oriented power-SCA instances contain up to 39,389 variables and 137,712 clauses, making a full-formula Grover search well beyond the scale studied here and beyond currently practical near-term implementations. We propose QGCL, a Quantum-Guided Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL) framework in which Grover search is invoked only on small subformulas extracted dynamically around CDCL conflict cores. The quantum subsolver returns candidate assignments and violation scores that bias branching heuristics, while final SAT/UNSAT decisions and key verification remain classical. We evaluate QGCL on AES-oriented cryptanalytic SAT instances derived from power side-channel CNFs with leakage-derived hint configurations, measuring conflicts, restarts, decisions, and propagations. The experiments show consistent reductions in these solver-internal statistics on harder instances, with up to an 86% reduction in conflicts compared with the classical conflict-learning baseline. Parameter sweeps over the number of Grover oracle calls and the subproblem size identify a regime in which a modest quantum resource allocation captures most of the observed improvement.

Quantum Domain Decomposition for Preconditioning the Finite Element Method

Authors: Elise Fressart, Michel Nowak, Nicole Spillane
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26090v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a quantum-compatible domain decomposition framework to precondition large-scale linear systems. By reducing the condition number of FEM-discretized PDEs, it enables more efficient execution of quantum linear solvers, potentially overcoming the scaling limits of current quantum algorithms.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the scalability bottleneck in Quantum Linear System Solvers (QLSS) by reducing the condition number of large-scale matrices arising from the Finite Element Method (FEM).

Main result. The authors prove the feasibility of quantum domain decomposition and provide upper bounds for the block-encoding parameters and computational complexity of the preconditioned system.

Method. The study utilizes a two-level Additive Schwarz preconditioner with BPX local solvers, implemented via block-encoding and Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT) for the Poisson equation.

Model / system. The mathematical model is the Poisson equation (a diffusion-type PDE) on a d-dimensional hyperrectangle, discretized using the Finite Element Method (FEM) on a Cartesian mesh.

Key observables. The condition number of the preconditioned operator, the subnormalization factor (alpha), and the complexity of computing inner products (quantities of interest).

Important parameters / regimes. Mesh size (h or L), overlap parameter (delta), number of subdomains (N), and the diffusion tensor (rho).

Assumptions / limitations. The domain is assumed to be a hyperrectangle, the diffusion coefficient is constant over each mesh element, and the local solvers are implemented via block-encoding.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the spatial arrangement of overlapping subdomains in a 2D setup; numerical tables compare condition numbers and eigenvalue ratios across different preconditioners and subdomain counts.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from the FEM discretization setting to domain decomposition and spectral bounds, followed by block-encoding construction, cost analysis, and a numerical proof-of-concept.

Abstract

Even in cases where quantum linear solvers provide significant speedup compared to their classical counterparts, their performance depends on some of the same parameters. In particular, the condition number of the matrix which is to be inverted is a decisive parameter. A well known classical, and now quantum, remedy is to precondition the linear system $A x = b$ by premultiplying it by a matrix $H$ in such a way that the condition number of $HA$ is significantly smaller than the condition number of $A$. In this work, we focus on a family of preconditioners called domain decomposition. First, we prove that it is feasible to apply quantum domain decomposition. We provide upper bounds for the block-encoding parameters of the Poisson problem discretized by the finite element method and preconditioned by the two-level Additive Schwarz preconditioner (one of the most fundamental domain decomposition techniques). From these bounds, we deduce the complexity of the quantum linear system solver. Second, we focus on a particular choice of local solver within the domain decomposition preconditioner by applying recent work by [Deiml and Peterseim, \textit{Math. Comput.}, 2025] on the Bramble--Pasciak--Xu (BPX) preconditioner. Finally, we provide details on how the operators are implemented.

Quantum Parameterized Self-Attention Network for Image Classification

Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25365v1

Summary. This paper introduces a hybrid quantum-classical neural network that replaces the classical dot-product attention mechanism with a parameterized quantum circuit. By leveraging quantum features like entanglement and non-monotonicity, the model achieves higher accuracy in image classification tasks compared to standard Vision Transformers.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the limitations of classical self-attention, such as linear operations and unbounded output ranges, by exploring whether a quantum-enhanced scoring function can provide superior structural inductive biases like non-separability and non-monotonicity.

Main result. The proposed QPSAN architecture significantly outperforms the Vision Transformer (ViT) baseline on several image classification datasets, with the quantum advantage increasing alongside data complexity.

Method. The authors use a hybrid quantum-classical Variational Quantum Algorithm (VQA) framework, implementing a two-qubit Parameterized Quantum Circuit (PQC) as the attention scoring module and optimizing it via classical backpropagation.

Model / system. A hybrid architecture consisting of a classical Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone integrated with a Quantum Parameterized Self-Attention (QPA) module, which utilizes a two-qubit circuit with an encode-entangle-measure design.

Key observables. The attention score $\mu_d(q, k)$, calculated as the joint measurement probability $P(|00\rangle) + P(|11\rangle)$ in the computational basis.

Important parameters / regimes. Five trainable quantum parameters: $\theta_s$ (initial encoding), $\gamma_d$ (difference encoding), $\gamma_s$ (sum encoding), $\alpha$ (entanglement), and $\beta$ (mixer).

Assumptions / limitations. The proofs for non-monotonicity and asymmetry assume non-zero values for encoding and entanglement parameters; the study also assumes the ability to classically simulate the circuit for validation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the hybrid VQA architecture; Figure 2 illustrates the QPSAN architecture replacing classical dot-product; Figure 3 shows the circuit structure; Figure 8 shows the impact of various quantum noise channels on accuracy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of classical attention limitations, proposes the QPSAN architecture, provides a mathematical framework proving the scoring function's properties (non-separability, asymmetry, non-monotonicity), presents experimental results on vision datasets, and conducts ablation and noise robustness studies.

Abstract

Transformer now underpins modern AI as its core infrastructure. Its defining capability-dynamically focusing on the most relevant information in complex inputs-is bounded above by the self-attention scoring function. Quantum computing, with its superposition, entanglement, and probabilistic outputs, offers a fundamentally distinct computational framework for exploring beyond the design constraints of classical scoring functions. While quantum attention mechanisms have shown initial promise, existing works remain largely confined to redefining feature similarity measures, leaving the systematic use of parameterized quantum circuits (PQCs) as scoring functions largely unexplored; a substantial portion of existing schemes further rely on purely quantum architectures, precluding effective encoding of high-dimensional image inputs in the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum era. We propose the Quantum Parameterized Self-Attention Network (QPSAN), implementing the self-attention scoring function via PQCs with only 5 trainable quantum parameters per layer. QPSAN computes query-key attention scores through quantum state encoding and joint measurement, yielding naturally bounded outputs without the explicit scaling of classical dot-product attention. We further establish a theoretical framework of the mathematical properties of this scoring function, demonstrating its potential to capture complex nonlinear query-key interactions, and quantifying the structural constraints of the encoding layer via effective degrees of freedom analysis. Experiments on four vision datasets show that QPSAN significantly outperforms the Vision Transformer (ViT) baseline, with the quantum representational advantage amplifying as data complexity increases. Ablation studies indicate that the performance gains may stem from the structural inductive bias of the quantum circuit rather than from parameter scale.

Quantum-Adaptive KS($\varphi$): A Parameterized Three-Qubit Gate Family Embedding Toffoli with Measurement-Free Phase Kickback and Intrinsic Error Non-Amplification

Authors: Kripa Sankaranarayanan, Marek Perkowski
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24182v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper proposes a new class of three-qubit gates that act like a Toffoli gate but include extra features like measurement-free phase kickback and built-in error resistance. This could allow for more efficient quantum computing by reducing the need for complex error correction and ancilla qubits.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The high resource cost of Toffoli (CCX) gates in fault-tolerant architectures and the lack of intrinsic, gate-level error suppression mechanisms that do not require measurement or ancilla overhead.

Main result. The introduction of the QA-KS(phi) gate family, which embeds the Toffoli gate while providing measurement-free phase kickback and intrinsic error non-amplification on specific subspaces.

Method. The authors use a mathematical framework of Hadamard conjugation and controlled-phase gates, verified via unitarity checks and Qiskit depolarizing-noise simulations.

Model / system. A parameterized three-qubit gate family (QA-KS) consisting of a Hadamard sandwich around a CCX gate and a controlled-phase gate, designed for linear connectivity in trapped-ion or surface-code architectures.

Key observables. Average gate fidelity, phase kickback amplitude, trace-distance weight of error propagation, and computational divergence (orthogonality) from standard CCX gates.

Important parameters / regimes. The compile-time parameter phi (controlling kickback amplitude), noise rate p (tested up to 10^-1), and the error types (X, Y, Z).

Assumptions / limitations. The gate does not self-modify at runtime (parameters are set at compile-time); the analysis assumes a standard depolarizing noise model.

Figures summary. Fig. 1 shows the Qiskit circuit diagram of the gate sequence involving H, CCX, and P gates.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the gate family, defines its mathematical structure and unitary matrix, demonstrates its unique computational signatures via kickback chains, evaluates its error-suppression properties, and performs noise simulations.

Abstract

We introduce Quantum-Adaptive KS($\varphi$) ($K$ = kickback, $S$ = sandwich), a parameterized three-qubit gate family that structurally embeds the Toffoli (CCX) gate within two additional components: (1)a palindromic Hadamard sandwich on the first control qubit $q_0$ that conjugates $Z$-type errors to $X$-type in the CCX frame, providing simultaneous sensitivity to both error types without ancilla overhead; and (2)a controlled-phase (CP) gate whose quantum phase kickback propagates post-CCX target-state information into the control-qubit phase without measurement. The term Quantum- Adaptive refers to amplitude steering conditioned by the compile-time parameter $\varphi$ via a Quantum Neural Cellular Automaton (QNCA) majority-inspired bias rule; the gate does not self-modify at runtime. Two QA-KS($π$) gates chained on a shared control qubit $q_0$ produce outputs completely orthogonal to two sequential CCX gates on $q_0$=1 inputs (output fidelity F=0.000), while agreeing exactly on $q_0$=0 inputs (F=1.000). This subspace-dependent divergence is the direct computational signature of coherent phase retention across gate boundaries -- impossible for CCX-only circuits. On the $q_1$ = 0 subspace the gate acts deterministically (up to a relative phase), providing intrinsic error non-amplification. On the $q_1$ = 1 subspace it produces four-component entangled superpositions, making it a strictly distinct quantum-native primitive from CCX. We present the complete $8 \times 8$ unitary matrix, confirmed exact to $||U^{\dagger}U-I||_{\infty} < 10^{-15}$, and define two canonical variants: QA-KS$_{π/2}$ ($\varphi = π/2$, $S$ gate) and QA-KS$_π$ ($\varphi = π$, $Z$ gate). Qiskit depolarizing-noise simulation demonstrates near-unit fidelity at $p \leq 10^{-2}$ with an honest depth cost at higher error rates. The gate preserves the three-qubit footprint of CCX with no qubit overhead.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the integration of classical layers in hybrid quantum neural networks fundamentally changes the optimization landscape, breaking the traditional trade-off between circuit expressibility and trainability. By using a multi-objective neural architecture search, the authors show that classical components can mitigate barren plateaus, suggesting that hybrid design principles differ significantly from standalone quantum circuit design.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the well-known expressibility-trainability trade-off in standalone parameterized quantum circuits (PQCs) persists in hybrid quantum-classical neural networks (HQNNs).

Main result. The study finds that classical layers in hybrid architectures decouple trainability from expressibility, effectively eliminating the trade-off observed in pure PQCs under full end-to-end training.

Method. A two-stage approach involving a systematic empirical analysis of gradient variance and KL divergence across various architectures, followed by a multi-objective Neural Architecture Search (NAS) using the NSGA-II algorithm.

Model / system. Hybrid Quantum-Classical Neural Networks (HQNNs) consisting of classical convolutional/fully connected layers integrated with Parameterized Quantum Circuits (PQCs) using various entanglement topologies and rotation gates.

Key observables. Expressibility (via KL divergence), Trainability (via gradient variance), and Task Performance (via MNIST validation accuracy).

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (L), qubit count (n), entanglement topologies (linear, star, all-to-all, etc.), and training configurations (pure PQC, quantum-only, and full end-to-end).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that the properties of standalone PQCs might be used as design heuristics for hybrid models, a premise the paper seeks to challenge.

Paper structure. Abstract

Hybrid quantum neural networks (HQNNs) integrate parameterized quantum circuits (PQCs) within classical networks, where the behavior of the underlying PQCs is often the primary focus of analysis. In this context, expressibility and trainability are widely used to characterize PQC's performance and are commonly assumed to exhibit a trade-off, where highly expressive circuits are more susceptible to barren plateaus. However, the validity of this relationship in HQNNs remains unclear. In this paper, we systematically analyze the expressibility--trainability relationship in HQNNs across varying circuit depths, qubit counts, entanglement topologies. We consider different training configurations, including pure PQCs, quantum-only training in hybrid setting, and full end-to-end training of hybrid models. Our results show that pure PQCs exhibit only a weak and regime-dependent trade-off, while hybrid architectures increasingly disrupt and can eliminate this relationship under full hybrid training. This indicates that classical components reshape the optimization landscape, decoupling trainability from PQC expressibility. We further propose a multi-objective neural architecture search (NAS) framework that jointly optimizes expressibility, trainability, and task performance over a combined classical--quantum design space, revealing different Pareto-optimal solutions under full end-to-end and quantum only training in hybrid setting. different trainability definitions. Our results suggest that hybridization is not just an implementation detail, but a defining factor in the performance of quantum machine learning models.

Revisiting spin Hamiltonian parameters in a Kitaev material via Bayesian optimization of magnetization curves

Authors: Takahiro Misawa, Ryo Tamura, Kazuyoshi Yoshimi, Youhei Yamaji
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24857v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper presents a systematic, data-driven method to determine spin Hamiltonian parameters in quantum magnets using Bayesian optimization of experimental magnetization data. It successfully constrains the exchange interactions in alpha-RuCl3 but highlights that magnetization alone cannot resolve the absolute energy scale of the Kitaev interaction.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the precise spin Hamiltonian parameters for the Kitaev material alpha-RuCl3 is challenging due to the high sensitivity of ab initio calculations to methodological details.

Main result. The authors identified an optimized parameter set (K=-6.0, Gamma=7.5, Gamma'=-0.3, J=-1.75, gc=2.3 meV) and demonstrated that while magnetization constrains parameter ratios, it cannot determine the absolute energy scale of the Kitaev coupling K.

Method. A data-driven approach using Bayesian optimization (PHYSBO) of experimental magnetization curves combined with exact diagonalization (ED) and the H-Phi method.

Model / system. The physical system is the candidate Kitaev material alpha-RuCl3, modeled by a spin-1/2 Hamiltonian on a honeycomb lattice including Kitaev (K), Heisenberg (J), and off-diagonal (Gamma, Gamma') interactions.

Key observables. Ground-state magnetization curves (along b and c axes), static spin structure factor, magnetic susceptibility, and specific heat.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange couplings K, J, Gamma, Gamma', and the c-axis g-factor gc.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes R-bar-3 trigonal symmetry instead of monoclinic C2/m and assumes fixed values for the a and b-axis g-factors.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the honeycomb structure of Ru atoms; Figure 2 depicts the Bayesian optimization workflow and cluster geometry; Figure 3 shows magnetization curves; Figure 4 shows the cost function landscape; Figure 5 shows the ground-state spin structure factor.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of parameter ambiguity, describes the Bayesian optimization framework and numerical solvers, presents the optimized parameter results, performs a sensitivity analysis of the cost function, and validates the results using secondary observables like spin structure factor and susceptibility.

Abstract

Determining the spin Hamiltonian of a magnetic compound is crucial for understanding its magnetic properties. A standard approach is to derive model parameters from $ab$ $initio$ calculations based on the crystal structure. However, the resulting Hamiltonian can depend sensitively on methodological details of the $ab$ $initio$ procedure. This issue is particularly evident in $α$-RuCl$_3$, a candidate Kitaev material. Here, we present an alternative, data-driven approach to determine the spin Hamiltonian parameters of $α$-RuCl$_3$ by Bayesian optimization of experimental magnetization curves along the $b$- and $c$-axis directions. We optimize five parameters, namely the Kitaev interaction $K$, off-diagonal interactions $Γ$ and $Γ'$, the Heisenberg interaction $J$, and the $c$-axis $g$-factor $g_c$. The parameter set that minimizes the cost function is $(K,Γ,Γ',J,g_c)=(-6.0,\,7.5,\,-0.3,\,-1.75,\,2.3)$, where the exchange couplings are in meV. We find that the cost function is insensitive to the absolute value of the Kitaev coupling $K$. Thus, the magnetization data alone do not determine its energy scale. The cost function also depends only weakly on $Γ'$ and $J$, while the optimization favors a large positive $Γ$. By computing the static spin structure factor, magnetic susceptibility, and specific heat, we show that these quantities favor the large-$Γ$ scenario over the small-$g_c$ scenario and that the parameter set that minimizes the cost function yields good agreement with experiment. The combination of Bayesian optimization and accurate low-energy solvers provides an effective approach for determining parameters of spin Hamiltonians. This methodology opens a systematic route to determining spin Hamiltonians in quantum magnets from experimental data.

Summary. The authors present a refined force-matching workflow that uses a separable nonlinear least-squares method to derive efficient water potentials from expensive hybrid-functional DFT data. This approach avoids numerical instabilities and allows for a more accurate description of liquid water, including nuclear quantum effects, by effectively decoupling potential parameters.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of accurately and efficiently parameterizing flexible water models from high-level hybrid-functional DFT forces without encountering numerical instability or parameter compensation.

Main result. A new 'separable' force-matching protocol reduces the nonlinear optimization dimensionality and enables a more accurate, stable, and reproducible water potential that captures PBE0-level structural features.

Method. A variable projection (separable nonlinear least-squares) approach that analytically solves for linear force-field amplitudes, combined with PBE0 hybrid-functional reference data from CP2K.

Figures summary. Figure 4 shows site-site RDFs for the final force-matched potential; Figure 5 illustrates the stability and structural broadening of the model during path-integral simulations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific challenge of water modeling, describes the new separable optimization protocol, details the PBE0 reference generation, compares Lennard-Jones vs. Buckingham forms, and validates the resulting model via classical and path-integral MD.

Abstract

Force-matched water models provide a practical route from first-principles reference data to long classical and path-integral molecular simulations. Previous flexible four-site potentials in the spirit of q-TIP4P/F showed that fitting analytic models to density-functional-theory forces can reproduce key structural features of liquid water while retaining the efficiency required for quantum-nuclear sampling. Here we introduce two refinements aimed at making this strategy more accurate and more reproducible for molecular simulation. Abstract

We propose a training-free quantum generative paradigm, which is fundamentally different from current generative models, which demand substantial computational power, face practical scalability limits, and often function as opaque black boxes, despite their remarkable success. We enable image and text generation without parameter training, by constructing a local parent Hamiltonian whose ground state encodes the target distribution and then solving the global Hamiltonian. Rooted directly in quantum mechanical principles, this approach establishes a new pathway for generative modeling that leverages superposition and entanglement to maintain global consistency.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a unified theoretical framework to describe both low-energy spinon excitations and higher-energy excitation bands in trimerized quantum spin chains.

Main result. The development of a self-consistent Jordan-Wigner mean-field theory that successfully connects low-energy spinon physics to higher-energy bands and thermodynamic responses like the 1/3 magnetization plateau.

Method. The authors use a combination of Bethe Ansatz for low-energy mapping and a self-consistent Jordan-Wigner mean-field theory in the Hartree-Fock channel for high-energy dynamics.

Model / system. A spin-1/2 antiferromagnetic chain with trimerized exchange couplings (a J1-J1-J2 model) relevant to the compound Na2Cu3Ge4O12.

Key observables. Dynamical spin structure factor, magnetization (specifically the 1/3 plateau), magnetic susceptibility, and specific heat.

Important parameters / regimes. The modulation parameter gamma (J2/J1), the exchange coupling J1, and the external magnetic field h.

Assumptions / limitations. The mean-field approximation neglects the BCS channel and non-zero momentum mean-field contributions, and intra-trimer second-nearest-neighbor interactions are neglected.

Abstract

We investigate a spin-1/2 antiferromagnetic chain with trimerized exchange couplings relevant to Na$_2$Cu$_3$Ge$_4$O$_{12}$. We show that the low-energy Hilbert space maps onto an effective Heisenberg chain of composite spin-1/2 trimer degrees of freedom with positive coupling, enabling an exact spinon excitations via the Bethe ansatz. To access higher-energy dynamics, we develop a self-consistent Jordan-Wigner mean-field theory that yields three fermionic bands reflecting the underlying trimer structure. Remarkably, this approach reproduces the exact low-energy spinon continuum while predicting two additional higher-energy excitation bands consistent with the experimental observations and previous numerical simulations. The theory further captures the 1/3 magnetization plateau under an applied magnetic field, and provides testable predictions in magnetic susceptibility and specific heat. Our results establish a unified framework connecting low- and high-energy excitations in trimerized quantum spin chains.
