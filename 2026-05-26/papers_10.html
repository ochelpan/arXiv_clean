Exact Single-Scale Outer Solution of the Abrikosov Vortex in the Extreme Type-II Limit

Authors: Eugene B. Kolomeisky
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25938v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proves that in the limit of extremely high Ginzburg-Landau parameters, the Abrikosov vortex loses its two-scale structure (coherence length and penetration depth) and instead follows a single-scale pattern. By showing that the superconducting density is tied to the velocity field, the author provides an exact analytical solution for the vortex exterior. This challenges the standard assumption of two distinct length scales in extreme type-II superconductors.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the structure of the Abrikosov vortex in the extreme type-II limit (where the Ginzburg-Landau parameter kappa approaches infinity) to determine if the conventional two-length-scale picture holds.

Main result. The study demonstrates that in the extreme type-II limit, the vortex outer structure is governed by a single length scale (the London penetration depth), as the superconducting density becomes algebraically slaved to the velocity field.

Method. The author uses analytical derivation within the Ginzburg-Landau framework, employing an amplitude-phase representation and solving nonlinear differential equations using modified Bessel functions, supplemented by numerical verification.

Model / system. The system is an Abrikosov vortex in a type-II superconductor described by the Ginzburg-Landau theory, which is mathematically equivalent to the static limit of the Abelian Higgs model.

Key observables. Superfluid velocity field, magnetic induction, superconducting density, and vortex line energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Ginzburg-Landau parameter kappa (specifically the limit kappa to infinity), coherence length, and London penetration depth.

Assumptions / limitations. The solution is valid for the outer region of the vortex (r > 1/kappa) and assumes the extreme type-II limit where the vortex core shrinks to a point.

Figures summary. Figure 2 compares numerical Ginzburg-Landau solutions for various kappa values against the exact analytical Bessel-function profiles for magnetic induction and density deficit, showing uniform convergence.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of the extreme type-II limit, defines the Ginzburg-Landau model and the mathematical framework, derives the exact outer solution via a slaving condition, compares the results to the London model and numerical simulations, and discusses the implications of the single-scale structure.

Abstract

We determine the exact outer structure of the Abrikosov vortex in the extreme type-II limit, which occurs when the Ginzburg-Landau parameter $κ$ diverges. In this limit, Ginzburg-Landau theory simplifies, outside a shrinking core, to a closed nonlinear theory for the superfluid velocity subject to an algebraic density constraint. The resulting solution is asymptotically exact everywhere outside the vanishing vortex core, demonstrating that both magnetic field and superconducting density vary on the length scale of the London penetration depth. This establishes that the conventional two-length-scale picture of the vortex does not hold in the $κ\gg 1$ limit.

Intrinsic Topological Control of the Orbital Hall Effect in Buckled Dirac Materials

Authors: Madiha Zia, Muzamil Shah, Kashif Sabeeh, Gao Xianlong, Reza Asgari
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25121v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper demonstrates how the Orbital Hall Effect in buckled 2D Dirac materials can be precisely controlled by tuning external electric and magnetic fields. It shows that topological phase transitions, such as the transition between Quantum Spin Hall and Quantum Anomalous Hall phases, lead to significant enhancements in orbital Hall conductivity. These findings suggest that such materials are highly promising platforms for developing tunable orbitronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the intrinsic topological control of the Orbital Hall Effect (OHE) in buckled 2D Dirac materials using external electric and magnetic fields.

Main result. The orbital Hall conductivity is significantly boosted near band-inversion points and can be tuned across various topological regimes (QSH, QVH, QAH) by adjusting external fields.

Method. Berry-curvature-based linear response theory using a low-energy massive Dirac Hamiltonian and Kubo formula calculations.

Model / system. Buckled two-dimensional Dirac materials (such as the silicene family) modeled by a low-energy massive Dirac Hamiltonian including intrinsic spin-orbit coupling, a perpendicular electric field, and an antiferromagnetic exchange field.

Key observables. Orbital Hall conductivity, Berry curvature, orbital magnetic moment, and orbital angular momentum.

Important parameters / regimes. Intrinsic spin-orbit coupling (lambda_SO), perpendicular electric field (Ez), and antiferromagnetic exchange field (lambda_AF).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is in the intrinsic (disorder-free) limit, electrons are treated as independent (neglecting electron-electron interactions), and Rashba spin-orbit coupling is neglected.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of silicene; Figure 2 illustrates band structures for different topological regimes; Figure 3 shows OHC as a function of Fermi energy and temperature; Figure 5 shows OHC vs. tuning parameters; Figure 6 is a phase diagram heatmap; Figure 7 and 8 show the momentum dependence of Berry curvature and orbital magnetic moment.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical system and model, develops the theoretical framework for orbital transport, analyzes the redistribution of Berry curvature across different topological phases, examines the impact of temperature, and concludes with a phase diagram of the orbital response.

Abstract

We study the orbital Hall response in buckled two-dimensional Dirac materials using a unified framework that includes an antiferromagnetic exchange field, a perpendicular electric field, and intrinsic spin-orbit coupling. We show that the orbital Hall conductivity is considerably boosted around band-inversion points and shows different signatures across multiple electronic phases using a low-energy massive Dirac model in conjunction with Berry-curvature-based linear response theory. We find a series of quantum spin Hall, valley Hall, and anomalous Hall regimes by methodically adjusting external fields, and demonstrate how the evolution of the orbital response is controlled by the redistribution of Berry curvature between spin and valley sectors. We examine the impacts of finite temperature in more detail and find that although the response s size is suppressed by thermal broadening, the distinctive phase-dependent features remain robust. Our findings demonstrate that orbital Hall conductivity offers a sensitive band topology probe in Dirac systems and emphasize buckled two-dimensional materials as a flexible platform for engineering tunable orbital currents for orbitronic applications.

Summary. This paper proposes a way to move liquid metal in sealed microchannels using spin torque from the Einstein-de Haas effect. It shows that the flow can be controlled by tuning spin-current symmetry and provides a method to use frequency sweeps to measure fluid and spin properties.

Detailed structure

Important parameters / regimes. Spin-diffusion length (lambda), channel width (L), kinematic viscosity (nu), and the dimensionless ratio L/lambda.

Assumptions / limitations. Low-Reynolds-number, low-frequency, and incompressible flow; assumes an isotropic liquid and neglects micro-inertia and convective terms in the steady-state limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the device schematic; Figure 2 illustrates the time evolution of velocity profiles for odd and even modes; Figure 3 presents universal scaling of DC response and the frequency-dependent AC admittance and phase.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the EdH-driven microfluidic concept, develops the micropolar hydrodynamic model, derives steady-state scaling laws and symmetry-resolved modes, extends the theory to AC admittance spectroscopy, and discusses parameter extraction.

Abstract

We propose spin-current microfluidic actuation of a sealed liquid metal. Spin angular momentum injected from Pt contacts enters the liquid as an Einstein-de Haas torque and is converted through micropolar angular-momentum balance into viscous flow without pressure drive, moving walls, magnetic fields, Lorentz forces, or charge flow through the liquid. The dc velocity obeys universal spin-diffusion scaling, and the finite-frequency spin-mechanical admittance resolves viscous momentum diffusion, spin transport, microrotation relaxation, and interface transparency of the liquid-metal channel.

Abstract

The altermagnetic materials have emerged as model systems for studying spin split electronic structures, yet controlled epitaxial growth on technologically relevant substrates remains challenging. Among the known candidates, MnTe stands out as a prominent altermagnetic material owing to its layered structure and high Neel temperature. Here, we report the molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) growth of high quality alpha MnTe thin films on GaAs(111)B substrates and provide a comprehensive analysis of the growth evolution and structural properties. Combined with first principles phonon calculations, these features are identified as the Raman-active phonons of the hexagonal NiAs type lattice. Our results show that the high crystalline quality of MBE grown alpha MnTe enables the complete experimental resolution of all symmetry allowed Raman active phonon modes, highlighting epitaxial alpha MnTe as a robust thin film platform for investigating altermagnetism and its lattice coupled excitations.

Summary. The paper theoretically predicts a new family of 1:1 kagome metals that integrate superconductivity and topological order without external doping. By identifying specific stable candidates like MoSn, HfSn, and NbSn, it provides a roadmap for discovering new superconducting topological metals.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of 1:1 type kagome materials that exhibit the intrinsic coexistence of superconductivity and nontrivial band topology, as existing candidates like FeSn and CoSn are often suppressed by magnetism.

Main result. The identification of a new class of 1:1 kagome metals (MSn, where M = Mo, Hf, Nb, Ta, W, Ti) that are stable and include superconducting topological metals (MoSn, HfSn, and NbSn) with nontrivial Z2 topology.

Method. First-principles calculations using Density Functional Theory (DFT) with VASP and Quantum Espresso, including phonon spectra for stability, Wannier90/WannierTools for topological surface states, and the McMillan/Allen-Dynes formula for superconductivity.

Model / system. A 1:1 kagome lattice of transition metal (M) and Sn atoms in the P6/mmm space group, specifically focusing on candidates like MoSn, HfSn, and NbSn.

Key observables. Superconducting transition temperature (Tc), electron-phonon coupling constant (lambda), Z2 topological invariant, Dirac points, and van Hove singularities.

Important parameters / regimes. Predicted Tc range of 0.71 K to 2.31 K; EPC constants (lambda) of 0.49 for MoSn and 0.57 for HfSn; Z2 = 1 for specific candidates.

Assumptions / limitations. The superconductivity is assumed to be conventional and phonon-mediated; the stability is assessed via DFT-based phonon and formation energy calculations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the crystal structure and the screening flowchart for stable candidates; Figure 2 displays electronic structures, band topology, and orbital-projected DOS for MoSn and HfSn; Figure 3 shows phonon dispersion and the Eliashberg spectral function.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a screening of 27 candidates for stability, followed by an analysis of the electronic and topological properties of the stable non-magnetic candidates, and concludes with an investigation of the superconducting mechanism and phonon contributions.

Abstract

Kagome superconductors featuring topologically nontrivial band structures have attracted extensive research interest. FeSn and CoSn is a new kind of kagome material with intrinsic magnetism, which suppresses the emergence of superconductivity. Here, we theoretically predict a new kind of 1:1 kagome MSn (M=transition metal), which exhibit intrinsic superconductivity and nontrivial band topology by first-principles calculations. Among twenty-seven candidates, MSn (M= Mo, Hf, Nb, Ta, W, Ti) are theoretically identified as both dynamically and thermodynamical stable. And, five non-magnetic MSn (M= Mo, Hf, Nb, Ta, W) exhibit phonon-mediated superconductivity. Especially, the d orbitals bands display Dirac points and van Hove singularities near the Fermi level, which contribute to the emergence of topology and the electron-phonon coupling (EPC). More interestingly, MoSn, HfSn and NbSn show nontrivial topological band structure at the Fermi level. Thus, the predicted MSn establish a platform integrating superconductivity and topological order.

Proton-electron coupled catalyst for ionomer-free electrochemical energy conversion

Authors: Ao Zhang, Ran Wang, Mohammed O. Bazaid, Shiyi Wang, Ting-Jung Hsiao, Yibo Wang, Antonio Sorrentino, Yang Liu, Yu-Han Joseph Tsai, Boxuan Zhou, Bosi Peng, Zeyan Liu, Xiangfeng Duan, Adam Z. Weber, William A. Goddard, Seung Soon Jang, Yu Huang

Summary. This paper introduces a new 'ionomer-free' catalyst architecture that integrates proton and electron transport within the catalyst itself. By eliminating the ionomer barrier, the design significantly improves oxygen access and power density in fuel cells. This approach offers a path toward much cheaper and more efficient electrochemical energy conversion devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Conventional PEMFC catalyst layers suffer from a trade-off between ionic conductivity and reactant accessibility due to the ionomer layer blocking oxygen access to the catalyst surface.

Main result. The development of a proton-electron coupled catalyst (PECC) architecture enables an ionomer-free cathode, reducing oxygen transport resistance by 95% and boosting power density by up to 85% at low platinum loadings.

Method. The study combines experimental synthesis of PtNi-grafted CNTs, electrochemical testing (EIS, polarization curves, AST), and multi-scale modeling including 2D multi-physics MEA models and all-atom molecular dynamics simulations.

Model / system. The physical system is a proton-electron coupled catalyst (PECC) consisting of PtNi nanoparticles on chemically functionalized carbon nanotubes (IonoSkin) used in PEMFCs and electrochemical hydrogen pumps.

Key observables. Power density, mass activity, proton conductivity, oxygen transport resistance (Fickian and non-Fickian components), and platinum loading.

Important parameters / regimes. Platinum loading (0.037 to 0.090 mg/cm2), proton conductivity (S/m), hydration level (lambda ~ 14-15), and oxygen transport resistance.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a 2D continuum for the MEA and uses the DREIDNG and F3C force fields for molecular dynamics simulations of the water-hydronium network.

Figures summary. Figures likely include TEM/STEM images of catalyst structure, XPS spectra for chemical verification, electrochemical polarization and power density curves, and MD-derived density profiles and diffusivity plots.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the PECC design, details the synthesis and characterization of the catalyst, presents electrochemical performance in PEMFCs and EHPs, provides a theoretical framework for transport resistance via multi-physics modeling, and uses MD simulations to explain the proton transport mechanism.

Abstract

Efficient electrochemical energy devices are vital to renewable energy technology, yet coordinating the effective flow of electrons, ions, and chemical species continues to be a major challenge. In conventional proton-exchange membrane fuel cell (PEMFC) catalyst layers, proton and electron transport are supplied separately through percolating carbon networks and ionomer binders, rendering the catalyst largely passive and imposing fundamental trade-offs between reactant accessibility, ionic conductivity, and catalyst activity. Here, we introduce a one-dimensional proton-electron coupled catalyst (PECC) design, a transport-integrated electrocatalyst architecture in which the catalyst itself simultaneously supplies electronic and protonic transport to catalyst active sites. Using this PECC, PEMFCs can have an ionomer-free cathode catalyst layer (CCL), resulting in a dramatic 95% reduction in non-Fickian oxygen transport and boosting power density by 34% and 85% compared to traditional CCLs, with cathode Pt loadings of approximately 0.090 mg/cm^2 and 0.037 mg/cm^2, respectively. Meanwhile, PECC retains 65% of its mass activity and exhibits 32% higher power density than its ionomer-based CCL counterpart after 30k accelerated stressed test. Similar mass transport improvements have been observed in the electrochemical hydrogen pump (EHP) using PECC in the catalyst layers. Molecular dynamics simulations show the PECC's proton conductivity is 249% higher than Nafion. This PECC catalyst structure addresses core transport problems in PEMFCs, leading to almost 20% improvement in fuel efficiency and opens up new possibilities for designing high-performance, cost-effective electrochemical devices.

Abstract

This study explores the emergence of superconductivity in high-quality ZrTe$_{2-x}$Se$_x$ crystals, grown via the isothermal chemical vapor transport (ICVT) technique. Resistive, structural, and thermal measurements reveal that substituting Te with Se in the ZrTe$_2$ matrix induces a superconducting state at low temperatures. The critical temperature ($T_c$) exhibits a clear dependence on the selenium concentration, peaking at $x=0.15$ with a $T_c$ of $4.8$ K. Calorimetric data indicates that even a low Se substitution range is capable of modifying both the electronic contribution and the vibrational modes of the crystal lattice. Combined with ab initio calculations and Wannier Hamiltonian interpolation between ZrTe$_2$/ZrSe$_2$, we established an extensive phase diagram mapping the transition from charge density wave (CDW) to the state with coexistence between the Dirac semimetal and superconductivity (SC), up to the semiconductor phase. This coexistence suggests that ZrTe$_{1.85}$Se$_{0.15}$ could be a candidate platform for topological superconductivity, as it hosts a nontrivial $\mathbb Z_2$ invariant, with nonvanishing surface states in its $(001)$ planes.

Abstract

ZrTe$_2$ is an outstanding layered semimetal due to the topologically nontrivial electronic structure. In this work, we present an investigation of the electronic evolution of ZrTe$_2$ in the presence of Fe intercalation, namely Fe$_{x}$ZrTe$_2$ ($x= 0 - 0.25$), scrutinized by both experimental measurements and \textit{ab} initio calculations. Finally, our results reveal that the new compound with inclusion of Fe intercalation preserves the enforced semimetal classification.

Abstract

Chiral helimagnets based on intercalated transition-metal dichalcogenides, characterized by nano-scale spin ordering, provide a powerful route to engineer chiral spin textures (e.g. the topologically protected magnetic solitons) and emergent electronic functionality at reduced dimensions, where surface and interface states often dominate device operation. Abstract

Molecular Dynamics (MD) is performed on supercells of 216 atoms of bismuth, going from 300 K to 573 K in 100 steps and maintaining it in the liquid state, at 573 K, during 500 steps using the Materials Studio (MS) suite of codes. The Pair Distribution Functions (PDFs) and the Plane Angle Distributions (PADs) of the last 1, 10, 25 and 100 steps of the MD have been obtained. Averaging the last 100 steps, as representative of the liquid, Reverse Monte Carlo (RMC) was applied to obtain 4 atomic structures, one for each set of initial random velocities. Then, a detailed structural study of liquid bismuth at 573 K was undertaken; PDFs and PADs are calculated and reported. Two noticeable peaks appear for the PDFs, at 3.25 and 6.55 Å, along with a pseudo peak (shoulder) at 4.6 Å. This shoulder (after the first peak) of the PDFs is found to be related to the third and fourth neighbor peaks of the crystalline Wyckoff structure and also to the diagonal distances in deformed squares in the liquid structure. For completeness J(r)s are also reported. Two prominent peaks in the MS PADs are observed: 53° and 85°; and two for the RMC PADs: 58° and 90°, suggesting the existence of deformed triangles and squares. Less abundant are higher-order geometrical structures.
