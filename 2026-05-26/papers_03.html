Structural constraint on delayed-choice quantum eraser architectures

Authors: Chakir Fikri
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24994v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the seemingly paradoxical correlations in delayed-choice quantum eraser experiments are actually a result of incompatible structural assumptions. It proves that any realistic implementation must necessarily involve losses, non-deterministic routing, or coarse-graining to remain consistent with the independence of the measurement choice.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental structural explanation for the 'paradoxical' nature of DCQE, showing that losses are not just experimental flaws but a structural necessity to maintain causality and independence.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the apparent paradox in Delayed-Choice Quantum Eraser (DCQE) experiments, where measurement choices at a later time seem to correlate with earlier detection events.

Main result. The author proves a no-go theorem stating that four intuitive properties of idealized DCQE architectures—independence of choice, absence of losses, deterministic routing, and distinct conditional distributions—are mutually incompatible.

Method. A purely probabilistic analysis using elementary probability constraints and a framework of minimal compatibility relations between random variables, independent of specific quantum dynamics.

Model / system. A generic bipartite DCQE scenario involving a signal system (S) and an idler system (I), modeled via random variables for detection outcomes, experimental choices, and density operators.

Key observables. Detection positions (X), experimental settings (C), coincidence counts, and conditional detection distributions P(X|D).

Important parameters / regimes. Loss rate (p), probability of erasure choice (q), and the minimum loss rate threshold p_min = q^2/2.

Assumptions / limitations. The core derivation relies on the assumption of statistical independence between the choice and the signal detection (X is independent of C).

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates Architecture I (coarse-graining leading to interference cancellation); Figure 2 illustrates Architecture II (non-deterministic routing in a Mach-Zehnder setup).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the DCQE paradox, defines a probabilistic model for the system, presents a no-go theorem for idealized architectures, classifies DCQE schemes into three types based on which property they violate, and provides an analytical derivation of loss rate constraints.

Abstract

Delayed-choice quantum eraser (DCQE) experiments are often presented as challenging classical causal intuitions by correlating detection events with choices implemented at later times. While it is well understood that post-selection plays a crucial role in producing the observed interference patterns, the structural features underlying such correlations are rarely analyzed within a unified framework. In this work, we introduce a simple probabilistic constraint applicable to idealized DCQE architectures. We show that four intuitive properties -- statistical independence of the choice, absence of losses, deterministic routing conditioned on the choice, and distinct conditional detection distributions -- cannot be simultaneously satisfied. This incompatibility provides a transparent classification of DCQE schemes and clarifies how conditional interference patterns arise without invoking exotic mechanisms.

Summary. This paper presents a hybrid quantum-classical framework that uses a quantum processor to sample important electronic configurations and a Transformer neural network to refine the resulting wavefunction. It successfully demonstrates high-accuracy simulations of large, strongly correlated biological clusters that are challenging for classical computers.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a powerful synergy between quantum hardware-native sampling and classical machine learning to overcome the limitations of NISQ noise and the exponential scaling of many-body wavefunctions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Accurately simulating strongly correlated electronic structures on NISQ-era quantum hardware, which is hindered by noise, high measurement costs, and the exponential growth of the Hilbert space.

Main result. The QiankunNet-QSCI framework achieves chemical accuracy for a 40-qubit ferredoxin cluster and reaches 12-18 milli-Hartree agreement with DMRG for a massive 146-spin-orbital nitrogenase P-cluster.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical approach using a Unitary Selected Configuration Interaction (USCI) ansatz for importance sampling on a quantum processor, followed by a Transformer-based neural network (QiankunNet) for denoising and wavefunction refinement.

Model / system. The study utilizes the Zuchongzhi 3.1 superconducting quantum processor to simulate various systems, including the H10 ring, the [2Fe-2S] ferredoxin active center, and the large-scale nitrogenase P-cluster (CAS(114e, 73o) active space).

Key observables. Ground-state energy, absolute energy error, excitation rank, single-orbital entropy, mutual information, and sampling enrichment factor.

Important parameters / regimes. Gate error rates (single-qubit ~0.1%, two-qubit ~0.6%), readout error (~0.6%), and the number of shots (up to 10^8).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes a global depolarizing noise model and focuses on the importance of the chemically significant determinant sector.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the workflow stages (Quantum, Data, AI); Figure 2 validates the H10 ring; Figure 4c compares energy errors against classical methods and DMRG; supplementary figures show hardware metrics, circuit depths, and sampling distributions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific challenge, details the USCI quantum sampling and QiankunNet Transformer architecture, presents experimental implementation on Zuchongzhi 3.1, demonstrates scalability through various molecular benchmarks, and concludes with error analysis and future outlook.

Abstract

Although quantum computing offers a promising solution for strongly correlated system simulation, existing algorithms face significant bottlenecks on current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. Here, we introduce QiankunNet-QSCI, a hybrid quantum-classical framework that addresses this challenge by combining efficient quantum-sampling with a transformer neural network. An efficient unitary selected configuration Interaction (USCI) ansatz especially designed for quantum sampling is proposed to identify the most chemically significant electronic configurations on the Zuchongzhi 3.1 quantum processor. Subsequently, the transformer model QiankunNet learns from these sparse yet critical quantum data to infer and reconstruct the complete electronic wavefunction with high fidelity. Simulation of the challenging 40-qubit [2Fe-2S] ferredoxin active center achieves chemical accuracy. Simulation of the nitrogenase P-cluster in a 114-electron 73-orbital active space also reaches 12 milli-Hartree-level agreement with the best density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) result. QiankunNet-QSCI thus offers a practical route to accurate quantum-assisted electronic structure calculations on current devices.

QML-PipeGuard: Drift-Aware Behavioral Fingerprinting for Quantum Machine Learning Pipeline Integrity

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

Summary. This paper introduces a framework to verify that a quantum machine learning pipeline is executing the intended circuit rather than a substituted or degraded version. It provides a unified way to monitor for both malicious attacks and natural hardware noise using a lightweight measurement protocol. This is crucial for the secure deployment of QML in regulated cloud-based quantum services.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Ensuring the integrity of Quantum Machine Learning (QML) pipelines against both adversarial channel substitution and natural hardware calibration drift.

Main result. The QML-PipeGuard framework successfully detects 'sneaky' channel substitutions on IBM Heron hardware while distinguishing them from benign hardware drift within a calibrated tolerance.

Method. A contract-based framework using behavioral fingerprinting, where a vector of observable expectation values is compared against a declared specification using a predefined tolerance.

Model / system. The study focuses on QML pipelines (VQC, QSVM, QNN) implemented on the IBM Heron r2 processor (ibm_fez), modeling the quantum stage as a sequence of CPTP channels.

Key observables. A vector of observable expectation values, specifically using Pauli observable families (local Pauli, diagonal correlations, and full Pauli strings).

Important parameters / regimes. Calibrated tolerance (epsilon_A), adversarial separation (delta), frame-bound constant (C), and measurement shot budget (N).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the adversary controls the execution environment, the QML model is already trained, and the verifier possesses a trusted reference implementation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the system model of trust between owner, environment, and verifier; Figure 4 visualizes the relationship between shot budget and error rates (TPR/FPR).

Paper structure. The paper introduces a threat model for QML integrity, proposes the QML-PipeGuard framework, provides mathematical theorems for detection and sample complexity, and validates the approach via hardware experiments on IBM processors.

Abstract

Quantum machine learning (QML) is moving from research prototypes to deployed cloud services. As QML enters regulated industries, the integrity of the quantum stage becomes a practical concern on two fronts: noisy hardware drifts at the channel level between recalibrations, and an adversary with control over the execution environment can substitute the declared quantum channel with a behaviorally similar but mathematically distinct one. Neither concern is covered by existing QML verification work on pulse-level noise, input drift, input-perturbation robustness, or device identity. We introduce QML-PipeGuard, a contract-based framework addressing both concerns under a single mathematical machinery. It characterizes a QML pipeline at runtime by its behavioral fingerprint, the vector of observable expectation values under a tomographically structured measurement family, and operates in two modes: drift-aware monitoring that absorbs benign calibration changes within a calibrated tolerance, and adversarial detection that catches channel substitution as a violation of an informationally complete observable contract. The framework contributes a pipeline-composition treatment of the encoder-ansatz-measurement channel with a QML-specific threat model (tight frame-bound C=sqrt(3) for the single-qubit Pauli family), a finite-shot sample-complexity bound, and a tolerance decomposition separating adversarial and natural-drift contributions. We validate the framework end-to-end on a two-qubit QSVM pipeline on the IBM Heron r2 processor (ibm_fez), with a sample-complexity validation on a noise-matched simulator. The prescribed measurement budget (about 1.4e4 shots) fits in a single batched job, the sneaky channel is detected with a wide safety margin while evading the weak contract, and the typical hardware drift sits within tolerance.

Kernel Embedding for Operator-Valued Measures and Its Application to Quantum Tomography

Authors: Philipp Nikolas Mayer, Ho Yun
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25146v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5

Summary. This paper develops a new kernel-based framework for quantum state tomography that avoids the need for sparsity assumptions. By embedding measurements into a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space, the authors create a method that is statistically optimal and accounts for the physical geometry of the measurement apparatus.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous statistical foundation for quantum tomography that is robust to noise and does not rely on the potentially biased assumption of Pauli sparsity, making it highly relevant for characterizing realistic, noisy quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the limitations of existing Quantum State Tomography (QST) methods, which often rely on basis-dependent sparsity constraints that can introduce bias in the dense regime and fail to account for the spectral geometry of physical hardware.

Main result. The authors introduce the Quantum Covariance Embedding (QCE) and the QUARK estimator, proving they achieve minimax optimal rates for density estimation and establishing that Unitary Designs are statistically superior to Pauli observables.

Method. The framework utilizes Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) techniques, specifically a tensorized Bochner integral, to embed Positive Operator-Valued Measures (POVMs) into a feature space, combined with kernel regression.

Model / system. The study focuses on finite-dimensional quantum systems (qubits and qudits) described by density operators in a Hilbert space, using measurement models ranging from ideal PVMs to noisy POVMs.

Key observables. Positive Operator-Valued Measures (POVMs), Pauli matrices, and Unitary Designs.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension of the Hilbert space (q), number of measurement shots (r), error rate (eta) for bit-flip noise, and kernel bandwidth (c).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the use of characteristic kernels and focuses on finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces; it also assumes the existence of complete measurement designs for identifiability.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the QCE pipeline from microscopic states to macroscopic outcomes; Figure 7 shows spectral error comparisons between LSE and QUARK; Figure 8 plots the MSE decay of various estimators.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the mathematical framework for operator-valued kernel embeddings, moves to the construction of the Quantum Maximum Discrepancy metric, develops the QUARK estimator and its statistical properties (CLT, concentration inequalities), and concludes with numerical simulations comparing the new method to standard least-squares estimation.

Abstract

This paper introduces the Quantum Covariance Embedding, which embeds Positive Operator-Valued Measures into a tensor product of a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space and the quantum state space via a tensorized Bochner integral. This construction induces the Quantum Maximum Discrepancy that metrizes the space of quantum measurements. Applying this framework to Quantum State Tomography, we reformulate density estimation as a tensorized kernel regression, enabling optimal inference without the basis-dependent sparsity constraints that restrict existing methods. We develop a unified geometric design theory for quantum Gram superoperators, establishing that Unitary Designs are strictly E-optimal experimental designs and thus statistically superior to Pauli observables. For general structure-free estimation, we derive the exact minimax lower bound and prove that our tensorized estimators achieve this optimal rate. Furthermore, we introduce the QUAntum Regression with Kernels (QUARK) estimator to accommodate the spectral geometry of physical implementations, deriving central limit theorem and concentration inequalities. To facilitate practical estimation, we establish the exactness of trace-preserving projections and demonstrate efficient estimation under mutually unbiased bases via the fast Walsh-Hadamard transform.

Point-group symmetry analysis of many-electron wavefunctions on a quantum computer

Authors: Rei Sakuma, Kenji Sugisaki, Shu Kanno, Toshinari Itoko, Hajime Nakamura
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24824v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5

Summary. This paper presents a new quantum-classical hybrid method to analyze the point-group symmetries of many-electron wavefunctions without requiring ancilla qubits. By applying orbital rotations, the method efficiently calculates the weights of irreducible representations. The approach is successfully demonstrated on real IBM quantum hardware, showing that error mitigation can accurately recover symmetry properties in molecular simulations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical framework for using molecular symmetries to reduce computational overhead and verify the accuracy of quantum simulations on near-term hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of practical, ancilla-free quantum algorithms for performing detailed point-group symmetry analysis of realistic many-electron wavefunctions.

Main result. The authors propose an ancilla-free hybrid method to calculate irreducible representation weights and demonstrate its efficacy on IBM's ibm_kawasaki device, achieving results within a few percent error using QESEM error mitigation.

Method. A quantum-classical hybrid approach using orbital rotations derived from representation matrix eigenvectors, combined with tensor-network-based state preparation (DMRG-to-brick-wall) and error mitigation techniques like ZNE and QESEM.

Model / system. Many-electron molecular systems, specifically benzene (D6h) and ferrocene (D5d), simulated using electronic structure models and implemented on a superconducting quantum processor.

Key observables. Weights of irreducible representations (w_Gamma), energy expectation values, and wavefunction infidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (up to 32), number of circuit layers (L=6), regularization parameter (lambda = 0.005), and error mitigation strategies (ZNE, QESEM).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on the spin-restricted case and utilizes DMRG-compressed wavefunctions for the hardware demonstration.

Figures summary. Figures show the optimization process for brick-wall ansatz construction, infidelity vs. circuit layers, and a comparison of symmetry weight estimation accuracy under different error mitigation regimes (No mitigation, ZNE, and QESEM).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of symmetry analysis, proposes a new ancilla-free algorithm, validates it through numerical simulations of benzene and ferrocene, and concludes with a hardware demonstration on an IBM quantum device.

Abstract

A point group is a set of spatial symmetry operations in molecular systems and is an indispensable tool for analyzing molecular orbitals and spectroscopy experiments in chemistry. Several quantum algorithms to exploit this symmetry have been proposed, but practical implementations of point-group symmetry operations and the detailed symmetry analysis of realistic many-electron wavefunctions are still missing. In this work, we propose an ancilla-free hybrid method to analyze point-group symmetries of many-electron states, which works for both abelian and non-abelian groups. For a given wavefunction, our method calculates the projection weights of point-group irreducible representations by applying orbital rotations derived from the eigenvectors of the representation matrices, therefore it is applicable to arbitrary basis functions. The usefulness of our approach is demonstrated through numerical simulations of benzene and ferrocene molecules. Furthermore, we perform a hardware demonstration of the weight calculation of the ground state and the first excited state of benzene in $D_{2h}$ symmetry, using up to 32 qubits of IBM's \texttt{ibm_kawasaki} device. By combining a tensor-network based encoding scheme and error mitigation techniques, we find the weights of irreducible representations for both states are faithfully reproduced within a few percent error. Our results suggest that the proposed method serves as a practical tool for analyzing symmetry properties of many-electron wavefunctions in realistic material simulations on near-term and early fault-tolerant quantum computers.

Summary. This paper provides a new way to study rare trajectories in noisy systems by avoiding the complex math of Doob transforms through a post-selection approach. It allows for the calculation of fluctuations in quantities like heat dissipation using an optimal-control framework. The method is validated through analytical models and applied to complex biomolecular landscapes.

Why it may be interesting. The development of a variational principle for conditioned trajectories is highly relevant for studying rare fluctuations and non-equilibrium transitions in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Calculating the statistics and moment generating functions of path observables for Doob-conditioned processes in the small-noise, large-deviation regime.

Main result. The authors develop a framework to bypass the need for an explicit Doob h-function by treating the conditioned ensemble as a post-selected original process, yielding an optimal-control representation for the action.

Method. The study uses weak-noise large deviation theory, a WKBJ ansatz, and a Hamilton-Jacobi formulation to derive a variational principle for the leading exponential contribution to the generating function.

Model / system. The framework is applied to time-homogeneous diffusion processes (SDEs) with additive noise, specifically using Brownian bridges, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck processes, and a 2D biomolecular folding model.

Key observables. Moment generating functions, Scaled Cumulant Generating Function (SCGF), rate functions, dissipated heat, and accumulated area under the path.

Important parameters / regimes. Noise intensity (epsilon), terminal time (T), tilting parameter (s), and diffusion coefficient (D).

Assumptions / limitations. The noise is primarily assumed to be additive, the processes are Markovian and time-homogeneous, and the study focuses on the small-noise (epsilon -> 0) limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Doob conditioning concept; Figure 2 compares analytical and numerical optimal trajectories and area rates for Brownian bridges; Figure 3 shows OU process trajectories, heat dissipation, and SCGF; Figure 4 presents numerical results for a 2D folding model including instantons and heat dissipation.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of Doob conditioning and the difficulty of the h-transform, introduces a post-selection reinterpretation, develops the mathematical framework via Hamilton-Jacobi and optimal control theory, provides analytical benchmarks (Brownian bridge and OU process), and concludes with a numerical application to a biomolecular folding landscape.

Abstract

Doob fixed-time conditioning enables the sampling of rare trajectories of Markov processes by modifying the drift so that reaching a prescribed target at a given time is guaranteed. We study the statistics of this conditioned path ensemble through the moment generating function in the weak-noise large deviation regime. Since the Doob drift is rarely available in closed form, we reinterpret the conditioned ensemble as the original process post-selected on the terminal constraint, thereby avoiding explicit construction of the Doob transform. This viewpoint then yields an optimal-control representation for the leading exponential contribution to the generating function, expressed as a variational principle with terminal boundary conditions set by the Doob end-point constraint. We illustrate the framework with two analytical examples and with an application to heat dissipation of a minimal model of biomolecular folding.

Summary. This paper provides exact analytical solutions for how localized bottlenecks affect current fluctuations in the SSEP model. It establishes that while the system is robust to small defects, a critical scaling regime exists where the slow bonds fundamentally change the macroscopic transport statistics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how localized microscopic disorder, specifically 'slow bonds' with reduced hopping rates, modifies the large deviation statistics of particle current in the Symmetric Simple Exclusion Process (SSEP).

Main result. The authors derive exact expressions for the large deviation function of the current across three geometries (infinite, semi-infinite, and finite lattices) and identify a critical scaling regime where slow bonds act as a bottleneck.

Method. The study uses an extension of Macroscopic Flutuation Theory (MFT) and fluctuating hydrodynamics combined with a new coarse-graining scheme, validated by rare-event simulations using the cloning algorithm.

Model / system. The Symmetric Simple Exclusion Process (SSEP) on 1D lattices, where particles perform unbiased random walks with exclusion rules, featuring localized defect bonds with a reduced jump rate gamma.

Key observables. Large deviation function (LDF) and Scaled Cumulant Generating Function (SCGF) of the particle current (net particle flux).

Important parameters / regimes. Jump rate (gamma), number of slow bonds (l), system size (L), observation time (T), and the scaling parameter Gamma = gamma / sqrt(T) or gamma / L.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that the large-scale properties can be captured by coarse-grained density fields and utilizes the additivity principle for decomposing the system.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the SSEP lattice with defect bonds; Figures 2, 4, and 6 provide quantitative comparisons between theoretical SCGF results and numerical simulation data for different geometries.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of slow bonds, presents exact results for three different lattice geometries (infinite, semi-infinite, and finite), provides a new coarse-graining derivation, and validates the analytical findings with numerical cloning simulations.

Abstract

The symmetric simple exclusion process (SSEP) is a paradigmatic model of classical non-equilibrium dynamics. Exact results for large deviations of particle current in the SSEP have been obtained in various settings using integrability-based methods. In this Article, we discuss how these results are modified in the presence of localized slow bonds. We consider three conventional geometries: (a) a finite one-dimensional lattice weakly coupled to unequal reservoirs at its boundaries, (b) a semi-infinite one-dimensional lattice weakly coupled to a boundary reservoir, and (c) an infinite one-dimensional lattice with localized slow bonds near the origin. For each case, we present exact expressions for the large deviation function of current and validate them through rare-event simulations based on the cloning algorithm. In connection with our results, we present an elementary derivation of the exact large deviation function for the current in the semi-infinite SSEP, complementing recent results obtained through more elaborate techniques.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that nonreciprocal couplings in a double-quantum-dot setup can actively regulate information flow and enhance the performance of an information engine. Abstract

Quantum information engines leverage information as a thermodynamic resource to facilitate energy conversion. In the operation of such engines, the information flow between the working substance and the controller is pivotal, however, strategies for its efficient manipulation remain largely unexplored. Here, we investigate an autonomous information engine based on a double-quantum-dot setup, where a downstream dot coupled to two reservoirs acts as the working substance, and an upstream dot coupled to a single reservoir serves as the controller. By extending the second law of thermodynamics to incorporate the effects of nonreciprocal couplings between the dots and their electronic reservoirs, we develop a thermodynamic framework that allows us to demonstrate that nonreciprocity can significantly modulate the inter-dot information flow, thereby providing a robust control mechanism. We show that the influence of nonreciprocity can be equivalently understood through a mapping to an effective reciprocal system upon a reparameterization of chemical potentials and the electron-electron coupling strength. We further analyze the impact of nonreciprocity on the engine's performance and operation regime. Our findings establish nonreciprocal coupling as an effective control knob for designing and optimizing quantum dot information engines, surpassing the capabilities of conventional reciprocal configurations.

Summary. The paper explores how colored noise from a thermal bath influences nonreciprocal quantum tunneling. It demonstrates that while noise can break the symmetry of tunneling in certain regimes, the system remains robust against noise in the presence of strong nonreciprocity. This work is significant for understanding how environmental fluctuations affect non-Hermitian quantum dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. This paper provides deep insights into the interplay between non-Hermitian physics (exceptional points) and environmental decoherence, which is central to understanding the stability of non-reciprocal quantum devices and open quantum system dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how heat-bath-induced colored noise affects nonreciprocal Landau-Zener tunneling and the emergence of exceptional points.

Main result. Colored noise generally enhances tunneling under nonadiabatic sweeping and can break the reciprocity of tunneling in the adiabatic limit, though tunneling remains insensitive to noise in the high-nonreciprocity regime ($\delta > 1$).

Method. The study employs the stochastic Schrodinger equation, numerical Runge-Kutta and Heun schemes, and analytical techniques including the Wiener-Hermite expansion and Weber functions.

Model / system. A non-Hermitian two-level Landau-Zener model coupled to a heat bath modeled via a Drude spectral density, resulting in Ornstein-Uhlenbeck colored noise.

Key observables. Tunneling probability (forward and backward sweeps), ensemble-averaged tunneling probability, and instantaneous energy levels.

Important parameters / regimes. Sweep rate ($\alpha$), nonreciprocity parameter ($\delta$), noise amplitude ($D$), and noise correlation rate ($\gamma$).

Assumptions / limitations. Markovian approximation, classical limit ($\hbar o 0$), and the use of a steady-state approximation in the high-dissipation regime ($\Gamma \gg 1$).

Figures summary. Phase diagrams of tunneling probability as a function of sweep rate and nonreciprocity parameter for various noise amplitudes and correlation times; comparison of different numerical and analytical solution types.

Paper structure. Introduction of the nonreciprocal LZ model; derivation of the stochastic dynamics and noise model; numerical results for energy levels and tunneling; analytical derivation of tunneling probabilities in the white-noise and high-damping limits; discussion of physical implications.

Abstract

In this work, we investigate the effects of heat-bath-induced colored noise on nonreciprocal Landau-Zener tunneling. We perform numerical calculations of the instantaneous energy levels and find that noise induces stochastic fluctuations in these levels. Beyond such fluctuations, sufficiently large nonreciprocity parameters enable noise to further trigger the emergence of multiple exceptional points. The corresponding tunneling probability is significantly modified by colored noise. On the one hand, colored noise generally enhances the tunneling probability under nonadiabatic sweeping. On the other hand, it can break the reciprocity of Landau-Zener tunneling in the adiabatic limit. In particular, for large nonreciprocity parameters, the adiabatic tunneling probability is insensitive to noise and determined by exceptional points. We plot the phase diagram of tunneling probability as a function of the sweep rate and nonreciprocity parameter, for different amplitudes and correlation times of colored noise. Analytically, we derive the exact expression for the tunneling probability in the absence of noise and an approximate solution in the white-noise limit, employing Weber functions and the Wiener-Hermite expansion method. Some implications of our theory are discussed.

Abstract

Recent developments in the formalisation of quantum causal structures have made it possible to test and compare hypotheses about causal structure empirically, rather than being a-priori assumptions. Such differences in causal structure may be leveraged to distinguish between the processes they belong to, akin to distinguishing between quantum states known to belong to different eigenspaces of an observable. So how far this analogy can be pushed? Can causal order be interpreted as a kind of observable? Can it be measured? To this end we construct a completely operational definition of observables in terms of discrimination tasks'. This begins with some base set of classes (analogous to a quantum observable's eigenspaces) before we impose three conditions on how these classes relate under discrimination tasks. We find their analyses presume special circumstances equivalent to conditions (2) and (3), and so do not contradict our own findings.

Abstract

We generalise the quantum mechanical toy model for gravitationally induced decoherence presented in Xu, Blencowe (2022) and Domi et al. (2024). In contrast to earlier formulations, in which the Hamiltonian of the system of interest is linearly coupled to the position operators of the oscillators in the environment, we consider an interaction formulated in terms of Weyl elements of the environment's position operators. This extension is motivated by polymer quantum mechanics, in which Weyl elements are fundamental operators, as well as by the possibility of generating non-linear interactions through suitable truncations of the exponential Weyl elements. Here we focus on a sinus-like coupling that is still quantised using the Schrödinger representation and, in the limit of a small Weyl parameter, reproduces the conventional linear interaction. To derive the corresponding master equation, we developed two complementary methods for the analytical calculation of the environmental correlation functions. The first utilises Wick's theorem for thermal expectation values in conjunction with annihilation and creation operators, while the second is based on the short-time Fourier transform and completely avoids the use of annihilation and creation operators, making it more readily transferable and generalisable to a polymer quantisation. Both approaches yield identical results. We further generalise the spectral density required for the exponential coupling structure. A numerical analysis shows that the environmental correlation functions decay rapidly with time, which supports the validity of the Markov approximation. Using a Taylor expansion in the Weyl parameter, we show that the first-order term reproduces the decoherence model of Xu, Blencowe (2022) and Domi et al. (2024). Finally, we derive the solution to the renormalised master equation.

Abstract

We investigate the effectiveness of entanglement detection based on multiple fidelities via the geometry of the joint separable numerical range. When all reference states are product states, we derive a necessary and sufficient criterion for such detection: either some pair of reference states has nontrivial moduli of the local inner products on both subsystems, or the orthogonal complement of the span of the reference states is completely entangled. We further show that there exist sets of reference product states for which no proper subset is effective for entanglement detection, whereas the full set is. A typical example of this phenomenon is provided by unextendible product bases. Moreover, for a pair of reference product states on a bipartite system with arbitrary local dimensions, we characterize both the joint numerical range and the joint separable numerical range, showing that the joint separable numerical range is determined solely by their local fidelities, as illustrated by a representative two-qubit example. Our results offer a systematic approach to designing effective entanglement witnesses and lay the groundwork for extensions to higher-dimensional and multipartite scenarios.
