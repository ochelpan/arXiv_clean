Emergent Dispersive Multipolar Excitations in NaErSe$_{2}$

Authors: Zheng Zhang, Mingfang Shu, Mingtai Xie, Weizhen Zhuo, Yanzhen Cai, Christian Balz, Jianting Ji, Feng Jin, Jie Ma
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24527v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates how the interaction between local crystal field levels and collective magnons in NaErSe2 gives rise to new, dispersive multipolar excitations. It shows that these excitations can be tuned using an external magnetic field to create a multipolar ferromagnetic state. This provides a new platform for studying complex, high-rank multipolar physics in frustrated magnets.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to understand the coupling between local crystalline electric field (CEF) excitations and collective spin dynamics, specifically how this coupling leads to emergent dispersive multipolar excitations.

Main result. The researchers identified that the coupling between ground-state magnons and higher-lying dipole-octupole CEF states creates a distinct, symmetry-selected multipolar band, and demonstrated that magnetic fields can induce a transition to a multipolar ferromagnet.

Method. The work combines inelastic neutron scattering (INS) and Raman spectroscopy with theoretical modeling using the J = 15/2 manifold approach, Linear Spin Wave theory, and SU(N) coherent state simulations.

Model / system. The system is NaErSe2, a triangular-lattice antiferromagnet containing Er3+ ions with a 4f11 configuration. The model uses a total angular momentum J = 15/2 Hamiltonian including both CEF terms and anisotropic exchange interactions.

Key observables. Inelastic neutron scattering spectra, magnetic Bragg peaks, CEF energy levels, and magnetization/specific heat measurements.

Important parameters / regimes. CEF splittings (e.g., 1.2, 3.8, 5.3, and 6.0 meV), exchange parameters (Jzz, Jplus, Jplusplus, Jzplus), and magnetic field strengths up to 7.5 T.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the validity of the J = 15/2 manifold approach and uses the D3d symmetry of the Er3+ environment to classify the CEF levels.

Figures summary. The figures likely display neutron scattering intensity maps (constant-energy cuts), spin-wave dispersions, CEF energy levels, and the evolution of the spectra under applied magnetic fields.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of local-collective coupling, describes the experimental setup and material properties, presents the identification of CEF levels and magnetic order, details the discovery of dispersive multipolar modes, and concludes with the effects of high magnetic fields.

Abstract

In most condensed-matter systems, local and collective excitations remain decoupled due to their distinct energy scales. Here, we identify coupled local-collective excitations in the triangular antiferromagnet NaErSe$_2$ by combining neutron spectroscopy with total angular momentum modeling. The low-lying crystalline electric field (CEF) doublets include a dipolar $Γ_4$ ground state forming stripe-$x$ order and a $Γ_{5,6}$ excited state with dipole-octupole character. High-resolution spectra reveal emergent symmetry-selected dispersions, where magnon branches from the ground state are replicated on higher $Γ_4$ levels but couple with the $Γ_{5,6}$ levels to form a distinct multipolar band. An applied magnetic field reconstructs the CEF wavefunctions and polarizes the system into a multipolar ferromagnet, further reshaping the spectra. This study demonstrates the emergent coupling of local and collective excitations driven by strong spin-orbit coupling and establishes NaErSe$_2$ as a platform for field-tunable multipolar excitations in frustrated magnets.

Equivalent-neighbor $k$-core percolation in two dimensions

Authors: Qiyuan Shi, Ming Li, Youjin Deng
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25812v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the hybrid phase transition characteristic of infinite-dimensional k-core percolation does not exist in two dimensions. Instead, the system undergoes either a standard 2D percolation transition or a first-order transition driven by a nucleation mechanism. This reveals how geometric constraints in finite dimensions fundamentally alter the nature of phase transitions compared to mean-field predictions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the nature of the phase transition in 2D k-core percolation as the interaction range r varies, specifically determining if the hybrid transition seen in infinite dimensions persists in 2D.

Main result. The authors find that no hybrid transition exists in 2D; instead, the phase diagram contains only continuous (2D percolation universality) and first-order regimes, with the latter driven by a nucleation mechanism.

Method. Large-scale numerical Monte Carlo simulations on triangular lattices using recursive pruning and a fast sampling algorithm, combined with finite-size scaling analysis.

Model / system. An equivalent-neighbor k-core percolation model on a 2D triangular lattice where a site remains active if it has at least k neighbors within a Euclidean distance r.

Key observables. Order parameter (fraction of sites in the k-core), susceptibility, critical polynomial (wrapping probability difference), and fractal dimension.

Important parameters / regimes. Core index k, interaction range r, occupation probability p, and system size L.

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on 2D triangular lattices and assumes the behavior is representative of the 2D regime, noting that the results for k-core percolation are sensitive to spatial dimension.

Figures summary. Figures show the phase diagram in the (k, r) plane, the bimodal distribution of the order parameter in the first-order regime, and the logarithmic finite-size scaling of the pseudocritical point.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the k-core problem and the mean-field limit, describes the numerical simulation methodology and sampling algorithms, presents the constructed phase diagram, analyzes the nature of the transitions (continuous vs. first-order), and discusses the renormalization-flow implications.

Abstract

We perform large-scale numerical simulations to investigate the critical behavior of $k$-core percolation in two dimensions with an extended interaction range $r$. By systematically varying both the core index $k$ and the interaction range $r$, we construct a comprehensive phase diagram in the $(k,r)$ plane. In contrast to $k$-core percolation in infinite dimensions, no hybrid transition is observed in two dimensions: the phase diagram contains only a continuous transition regime and a strictly first-order regime, separated by a tricritical or critical-end point $(k_s,r_s)$. For $kk_s$ and finite $r>r_s$, the transition is discontinuous, with no hybrid features or critical singularities. In this first-order regime, the pseudocritical point approaches the critical point as $1/\ln L$, where $L$ is the linear system size, distinct from the $L^{-d}$ scaling typical of conventional thermodynamic first-order transitions in $d$ dimensions. This logarithmic finite-size drift is consistent with a nucleation-driven mechanism, in which rare voids trigger the collapse of the finite-range $k$-core. These results demonstrate that geometric constraints can fundamentally alter the nature of $k$-core percolation found in finite dimensions.

Everything at Every Scale: Scale-Invariant Diffusion with Continuous Super-Resolution

Authors: Zixin Jessie Chen, Zhuo Chen, Archer Wang, Jeff Gore, William T. Freeman, Congyue Deng, Marin Soljačić
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26032v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The authors introduce SKILD, a diffusion model that treats scale as an explicit coordinate by manipulating image content in frequency space. This allows a single model to perform both image generation and continuous super-resolution without task-specific architectures. The model's ability to preserve the statistical self-similarity of critical physical systems like the Ising model validates its physical foundation.

Why it may be interesting. The paper demonstrates a deep connection between generative modeling and renormalization group theory, showing that a diffusion process designed to respect scale-invariance can accurately capture the higher-order correlations of critical systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Unifying image generation and continuous super-resolution into a single framework by leveraging the scale-invariance inherent in both natural images and critical physical systems.

Main result. The SKILD model achieves high-quality unconditional generation on CIFAR-10 and continuous super-resolution on ImageNet, while successfully reconstructing the non-Gaussian four-point correlations of the critical 2D Ising model.

Method. A frequency-space diffusion process (SKILD) using Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) coefficients to attenuate high-frequency modes before low-frequency modes, using spectrum-matched Gaussian noise.

Model / system. The framework is applied to natural images (CIFAR-10, ImageNet) and the 2D ferromagnetic Ising model on a square lattice at the critical temperature.

Key observables. FID, Inception Score, LPIPS, SSIM, PSNR, and connected four-point correlations (fourth-order joint cumulants) in the Ising model.

Important parameters / regimes. Scale-invariant noise schedules (log-linear and linear), SNR threshold for effective resolution, and the power-law decay exponent of the radial power spectrum.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that natural images and the studied physical systems exhibit approximate scale-invariance and power-law spectral decay.

Figures summary. Illustrations of the scale-invariant forward process, radial power spectra showing k^-2 decay, comparison of super-resolution quality, and accuracy of reconstructed Ising four-point correlators.

Paper structure. Introduction of the unification problem, description of the SKILD frequency-space diffusion framework, mathematical derivation of the DDPM posterior and SDE formulation, empirical validation on image datasets (CIFAR/ImageNet), and scientific validation using the critical Ising model.

Abstract

Creating images from noise is image generation; reconstructing fine details from coarse inputs is super-resolution. Despite their practical differences, both can be understood as reversing information loss across scales. We introduce $\textbf{SKILD}$, a $\textbf{S}$cale-invariant $\textbf{K}$-Space $\textbf{I}$mage $\textbf{L}$earning $\textbf{D}$iffusion model that unifies generation and continuous super-resolution within a single unconditional framework. Both natural images and critical physical systems exhibit scale invariance, and we leverage it to design a forward process that attenuates image content from fine to coarse scales while injecting spectrum-matched Gaussian noise, making scale an explicit coordinate of the diffusion dynamics. The same trained reverse process performs generation and continuous super-resolution by varying only the starting timestep: $\textit{no task-specific architecture, no conditioning branch, no classifier-free guidance, no retraining per scale factor}$. Empirically, SKILD reaches FID $2.65$ and Inception Score $9.63$ on unconditional CIFAR-10, performs $2\times$--$8\times$ super-resolution on ImageNet from a single unconditional checkpoint while outperforming conditional models across perceptual metrics, and reconstructs critical Ising models whose connected four-point correlations closely track the ground truth.

Fermi-Dirac machines as quantizations of neurons

Authors: Alexander He, Nana Liu, Mark M. Wilde
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.24386v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a way to turn classical neural network architectures into quantum-native models by quantizing the neuron's activation function. It provides a rigorous mathematical framework and efficient hybrid algorithms for training these networks, proving that they possess a computational complexity that makes them hard to simulate classically.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to develop a framework for quantizing classical neurons into 'Fermi-Dirac machines' and providing efficient hybrid quantum-classical algorithms for their training and evaluation.

Main result. The authors prove that a decision problem based on these neurons is BQP-complete, providing evidence against efficient classical simulation, and demonstrate that these neurons can learn functions that classical neurons cannot when processing quantum data.

Method. The approach uses canonical quantization to promote classical Hamiltonians to quantum operators, employing functional calculus, Hamiltonian simulation, and the Hadamard test to develop hybrid algorithms for gradient estimation and objective function evaluation.

Model / system. The model consists of parameterized quantum Hamiltonians (including second-order interactions like the Heisenberg or transverse-field Ising models) where activation functions (e.g., Fermi-Dirac, SiLU, GeLU) are applied to the Hamiltonian operator.

Key observables. The activation observable phi(H_Q(theta)) and its expectation value with respect to an input quantum state rho.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T) controlling activation sharpness, weights (w), bias (b), and interaction coefficients (Omega, omega).

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonians are assumed to be k-local, and the advantage is specifically tied to the presence of quantum input data (entangled or non-orthogonal states).

Figures summary. Figure 1 depicts the conceptual transition from classical to quantized neurons; Figure 2 shows quantum circuits for gradient and objective function estimation; Figure 3 illustrates a general quantum convolution algorithm.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the quantization framework, defines the physical model and activation functions, develops hybrid algorithms for estimation and gradients, provides complexity-theoretic proofs of BQP-completeness, and concludes with numerical experiments and generalizations to continuous variables.

Abstract

Fermi-Dirac machines were proposed recently as an approach to solving semidefinite optimization problems on quantum computers. Here, we reinterpret them as canonical quantizations of classical neurons. By viewing a classical neuron as an activation function applied to a parameterized classical Hamiltonian, we quantize this model by replacing classical variables with operators whose eigenvalues encode their possible values. This follows the standard approach to canonical quantization in quantum mechanics. Crucially, when the Hamiltonian consists of commuting operators, our construction reduces exactly to a classical neuron. More generally, our approach yields an activation observable, defined as an activation function applied to a parameterized quantum Hamiltonian. The output of this quantized neuron is a random variable with expectation value equal to that of the activation observable with respect to an input state. We develop efficient hybrid quantum-classical algorithms for evaluating outputs and gradients of our quantized neurons, enabling evaluation and training. These algorithms rely on basic primitives that include random sampling, Hamiltonian simulation, and the Hadamard test. We also quantize a whole host of other activation functions, including the smooth rectified linear unit (ReLU), sigmoid linear unit, Gaussian-smoothed ReLU, and Gaussian error linear unit (GeLU), which are known to be useful for deep learning applications. Numerical experiments indicate that neurons based on quantum Hamiltonians can learn functions that classical neurons cannot. We further define a computational decision problem based on Fermi-Dirac neurons and prove that it is BQP-complete, providing complexity-theoretic evidence against efficient classical simulation. Finally, we generalize our approach to continuous quantum variables and sketch two different ways of composing these neurons into networks.

It demonstrates that such backgrounds induce a fermion condensate and a mass shift without altering the fundamental ultraviolet divergence structure of the theory.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how a classical, periodic Yang-Mills (non-Abelian) plane-wave background field modifies the fermion-gluon vertex, the fermion effective mass, and the fermion condensate.

Main result. The study derives a gauge-consistent pole mass shift and shows that the background field induces a non-zero fermion condensate, while the UV divergence structure of the theory remains unchanged by the background.

Important parameters / regimes. Gauge coupling constant (g), color factors (CF, CA), field intensity parameter (alpha), and the light-like vector (k).

Abstract

We investigate the renormalized fermion-gluon vertex, the fermion effective mass, and the fermion condensate when the fermion propagates in an external Yang-Mills gauge field. We use an exact Green's function for the Dirac operator in a non-Abelian plane-wave gauge field to construct the renormalized vertex function, calculate the on-shell fermion self-energy, and the background-dependent condensate. We consider both the background and operator fields in the axial gauge $k^{μ} \mathcal{A}_{μ}^{a}=0$, thereby preserving the gauge. Its applications to strong-field QCD and non-Abelian Schwinger physics are discussed.

Freezing of the tetrahedral amorphous network in supercooled water triggers crystallization towards LDA ice

Authors: Ashutosh Srivastava, Pankaj A. Apte
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25926v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This study demonstrates that the crystallization of supercooled water into LDA ice is triggered by the 'freezing' of its tetrahedral hydrogen-bonded network. When the network becomes rigid, the system shifts from a network-driven relaxation to a crystallization-driven relaxation. This mechanism provides a specific pathway for structural ordering in the absence of traditional crystal nucleation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Identifying the specific mechanism by which crystallization into Low-Density Amorphous (LDA) ice is initiated in deeply supercooled water across its limit of stability.

Main result. The freezing of the tetrahedral amorphous network, characterized by a loss of structural relaxation, triggers a change in the relaxation mechanism that drives crystallization via cubic and hexagonal motifs.

Method. Molecular dynamics simulations using the TIP4P/2005 water potential in an NPT ensemble with linear cooling rates.

Model / system. Supercooled water modeled using the TIP4P/2005 potential, focusing on the transition from a liquid state to LDA ice.

Key observables. Tetrahedrality (f4c), Average Angular Deviation (AAD), density, potential energy, and presence of cubic (D10) and hexagonal (W12) structural motifs.

Important parameters / regimes. Cooling rates of 5 to 10 K/ns, temperature of maximum density (T0 = 277 K), and the limit of stability (Ts approx 235 K).

Assumptions / limitations. The mechanism may be general to tetrahedral liquids, but the specific extent of crystallization may depend on the choice of water potential.

Figures summary. Figure 1 and 2 show potential energy and density vs. temperature with markers for crossover and freezing points; Figure 3 plots AAD and its difference; Figure 4 tracks 4-coordinated molecules and bond length; Figure 5 shows the fraction of cubic and hexagonal motifs.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of crystallization in supercooled water, describes the MD simulation setup, details the dynamical crossover and network freezing process, analyzes the resulting crystallization into cubic/hexagonal motifs, and discusses the potential universality of this mechanism.

Abstract

In this work, we provide mechanistic insight into the initial stages of formation of ice across the limit of stability of supercooled water. Such an analysis is particularly important since crystal nucleation is not a relevant mechanism under these conditions. Using molecular dynamics simulation with the TIP4P/2005 potential, water is cooled at a constant pressure with cooling rates of 5 to 10 K per nanosecond. As the liquid is cooled across the temperature of maximum density (T_0 = 277 K), we find that there is a continuous increase in the tetrahedrality of the system. As the cooling continues across the limit of stability of water (T_s $\approx$ 235 K), large scale thermal fluctuations dissipate while the thermal equilibration is achieved through small scale fluctuations. This phenomenon, known as the dynamical crossover [Goutam et. al. in J. Stat. Phys., 168: 1302--1318 (2017)], ends the existence of the liquid state. Subsequently, we find that the tetrahedral network drives the decrease of energy and density. This process terminates when the network undergoes `freezing' (i.e., the bonds of the network acquire sufficient rigidity), due to which the network evolution, as a whole, stops. This triggers a qualitative change in the relaxation mechanism: subsequent relaxation occurs through crystallization, i.e., an increase in the structural order. In particular, we find that the cubic and hexagonal crystalline motifs, which possess medium range order, increase rapidly across the freezing point. In the resulting LDA ice states, cubic ice is found to have a significant contribution in the overall extent of crystallization, which is consistent with the experimental findings. Overall, our work provides the specific mechanism by which crystallization (leading to LDA ice) is initiated across the limit of stability of supercooled water.

Generalized field mixing for endpoint criticality with marginal flow: resolving the four-state Potts endpoint in the square-lattice $J_1$--$J_2$ Ising model

Authors: Yihua Sun, Yuchen Fan
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25946v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a generalized field-mixing framework to accurately identify critical endpoints in systems where marginal scaling fields cause deceptive pseudo-first-order behavior. By applying this to the $J_1$-$J_2$ Ising model, the authors prove the stripe-ordering endpoint follows four-state Potts universality. This resolves a decades-old controversy regarding the phase transition nature in this frustrated system.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Resolving the long-standing controversy regarding the nature and location of the stripe-ordering endpoint in the $J_1$-$J_2$ Ising model, specifically distinguishing true first-order transitions from pseudo-first-order signatures caused by marginal flow.

Main result. The authors identify the endpoint as belonging to the four-state Potts universality class with logarithmic corrections, resolving the ambiguity caused by marginally irrelevant scaling fields.

Method. A new generalized field-mixing framework that separates relevant detuning from marginal drift, combined with Monte Carlo simulations and finite-size scaling analysis.

Model / system. The frustrated square-lattice $J_1$-$J_2$ Ising model with nearest-neighbor ($J_1$) and next-nearest-neighbor ($J_2$) antiferromagnetic couplings.

Key observables. Magnetic Binder cumulant ($U_M$), nematic Binder cumulant ($U_N$), magnetic order parameter ($m$), and centered fluctuations of the mixed scaling operator ($ ilde{Q}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Frustration ratio $g = J_2/J_1$, temperature $T$, and the scaling variables involving logarithmic corrections $(\ln L)^q$.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes the endpoint criticality is governed by one relevant and one marginally irrelevant scaling field.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the schematic of the field-mixing framework; Figure 2 illustrates the application to the $J_1$-$J_2$ model; Figure 3 shows histogram scaling; Figure 4 displays Binder cumulant crossings and data collapse.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a new field-mixing framework, applies it to the $J_1$-$J_2$ Ising model to locate the stripe-ordering endpoint, validates the results via universality class identification and comparison with the Ashkin-Teller model, and concludes by demonstrating the resolution of historical numerical ambiguities.

Abstract

Identifying the asymptotic criticality of a critical endpoint is challenging, as pseudo-first-order signatures persist over accessible system sizes and mask its underlying critical nature. This ambiguity is amplified at endpoints controlled by a marginally irrelevant scaling field, where logarithmic flow delays the onset of asymptotic scaling. Here we develop a generalized field-mixing framework for endpoint criticality governed by one relevant scaling field together with a marginally irrelevant one, a setting that lies outside the conventional two-relevant-field formulation. By constructing a finite-size pseudocritical manifold, the framework removes the normal relevant detuning and exposes the residual marginal drift, enabling controlled histogram- and Binder-based finite-size analyses. We apply this approach to the frustrated square-lattice $J_1$--$J_2$ Ising model, where the location and even the nature of the stripe-ordering endpoint have remained controversial for decades. The endpoint is isolated directly as a distinct singular point, rather than inferred from where the phase boundary appears most Potts-like, and its asymptotic criticality is shown to follow four-state Potts universality with logarithmic corrections. This identification is independently supported by direct comparison with the Potts point of the Ashkin--Teller model and by consistent Binder scaling in both the magnetic and nematic sectors. Our results resolve a longstanding numerical ambiguity in a paradigmatic frustrated Ising system and establish a general framework for extracting asymptotic endpoint criticality in the presence of marginal flow.

Homomorphic Quantum Error Correction

Authors: Kornikar Sen, Miguel A. Martin-Delgado
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25692v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how to combine quantum error correction with homomorphic encryption to allow secure, noise-resilient computation in the cloud. It provides the mathematical conditions required to keep encrypted data within a protected code space and offers strategies to overcome the difficulty of performing non-Clifford gates on encrypted data.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the algebraic compatibility between quantum homomorphic encryption (QHE) and quantum error correction (QEC) to ensure that encrypted states remain within the code space during cloud-based computation.

Main result. The authors establish a necessary and sufficient condition for stabilizer codes to be compatible with transversal Pauli masking and propose solutions for implementing non-Clifford gates, such as using triorthogonal codes or logical-gate masking.

Method. Algebraic analysis of stabilizer codes, verification using specific examples (Shor, Steane, and 3D color codes), and the development of gate teleportation protocols for non-Clifford gates.

Model / system. A client-server model for cloud quantum computing using an 'honest-but-curious' server, employing Pauli-based encryption (block-Pauli masking) and stabilizer error-correcting codes.

Key observables. Error syndromes, encryption keys (a, b), and measurement outcomes (ra, rb) from Bell measurements.

Important parameters / regimes. Small error rate regime (p << 1).

Assumptions / limitations. The server is honest-but-curious (not malicious); the existence of pre-shared Bell states; and the availability of physical implementations for logical gates.

Figures summary. Schematic of the gate teleportation process, visual representation of key-updating rules for Clifford gates, and a walkthrough of a two-qubit circuit involving H, T, T-dagger, and S gates.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of QHE and QEC compatibility, derives a general algebraic criterion for stabilizer codes, verifies this for specific codes (Shor, CSS, 3D color), addresses the non-Clifford gate obstruction, and presents solutions via triorthogonal codes and logical-gate masking.

Abstract

Homomorphic quantum error correction aims to protect quantum data against both unauthorized access and environmental noise during server-based processing. We investigate the algebraic compatibility between quantum homomorphic encryption and quantum error correction, determining precise conditions under which encrypted encoded states remain inside the relevant code space during storage and computation. Our work establishes a necessary and sufficient criterion for an $[[n,1,d]]$ stabilizer code to remain compatible with the restricted transversal block-Pauli masking $U_{\rm enc}(a,b)=(X^aZ^b)^{\otimes n}$, stated explicitly for $[[n,1,d]]$ codes and extending directly to code-space preservation for $[[n,k,d]]$ codes. We verify this condition for standard examples (bit-flip and Shor codes, with the phase-flip repetition code following analogously), derive a practical criterion for Calderbank-Shor-Steane codes, and extend the analysis to three-dimensional color codes. A critical challenge emerges for non-Clifford gate implementation: the Shor code lacks a naive transversal $T$-gate implementation of the desired logical operation on encrypted encoded data. We present two routes around this obstruction. First, suitable triorthogonal codes admit transversal $T$-type logical implementations, up to Clifford corrections. Second, logical-gate masking gives code-space compatibility for arbitrary stabilizer codes, provided that suitable unitary representatives of the required logical gates are available. These results separate code-space compatibility from a full cryptographic security proof and provide explicit criteria for combining error correction with homomorphic processing in cloud quantum computing.

Implicit Binarization via Complex Phase Dynamics in Combinatorial Optimization

Summary. This paper proposes a new way to solve hard optimization problems by treating discrete bits as complex phases. This approach creates a natural 'push' toward discrete values, allowing for much more accurate solutions to complex problems like QUBO and sparse coding even under high noise.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the computational intractability of NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems, such as QUBO and sparse coding, by overcoming the limitations of standard continuous convex relaxations.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that parameterizing binary variables as complex phases introduces an implicit regularization mechanism that promotes convergence to discrete states, achieving zero error in large-scale noisy QUBO tasks and outperforming LASSO in sparse coding.

Method. A physics-inspired continuous relaxation framework that maps discrete binary variables to continuous wave-like states on the complex unit circle, utilizing gradient descent and analyzing the resulting implicit regularization via random matrix theory.

Model / system. The framework utilizes an Ising Hamiltonian formulation where discrete spins are relaxed into higher-dimensional algebraic manifolds, specifically the complex unit circle, spherical, and quaternion spaces.

Key observables. Ground-state convergence rates, bit error rates, and recovery success of planted solutions.

Important parameters / regimes. The diagonal shift hyperparameter beta, noise levels sigma, sparsity cardinality C, and the spectral properties (singular values) of the measurement matrix A.

Assumptions / limitations. The effectiveness of the binarization depends on the spectral characteristics of the interaction matrix, and for certain QUBO formulations, the measurement matrix is assumed to be square with determinant 1.

Figures summary. Visualizations of the continuous relaxation process across different manifolds (real, complex, spherical) and histograms of estimated ground-state energies.

Paper structure. The decoding performance of high-distance triorthogonal codes obtained via the doubling construction is then evaluated over the dephasing channel. We compare bounded-distance decoding, belief propagation plus ordered-statistics post-processing, and a GRAND-based decoder adapted to the quantum setting, which turns out to be a promising option.

Abstract

Processing large Pauli sums is a significant bottleneck in quantum chemistry, Pauli propagation, and Pauli-based compilation. Existing frameworks often suffer from Python interpreter overhead or utilize hash-map data structures that hinder SIMD vectorization and complicate multi-threaded merging. We present PauLIB, a header-only C++20 library designed to eliminate these bottlenecks through three key architectural choices. A bit-packed binary symplectic representation that encodes each qubit in two bits, reducing Pauli multiplication to a bitwise XOR and a population count; a sorted array layout that replaces hash maps to enable branch-predictable SIMD bulk operations; and a struct-of-arrays (SoA) memory layout that exposes contiguous word arrays for explicit SIMD vectorization. Benchmarks at 500 qubits show that single Pauli string multiplication runs at 25ns per operation-14 times faster than PauliEngine and 660 times faster than Qiskit-flat across all pair counts tested. Hamiltonian outer-product multiplication is approximately 10 times faster than PauliEngine and 45 times faster than Qiskit at all tested sizes. Greedy commutation grouping, the dominant preprocessing cost in variational algorithms, achieves up to 21,000 times speedup over PennyLane, driven by the compact bit-packed representation. The compact layout reduces the memory footprint of a one-million-term Hamiltonian at 500 qubits from 1,036MB (Qiskit) to 142MB, a 7.3 times reduction that directly enables larger problem sizes within a fixed memory budget. PauLIB is open source and provides C++ and Python interfaces.
