Vector Magnonics: Electrical Injection and Control of Spin Flow in Altermagnets

Authors: Yanmeng Lei, Rui-Chun Xiao, Weiwei Lin, Tao Yu
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01225v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper predicts that altermagnets can support a multidirectional 'vector' magnon spin current when electrically injected. It demonstrates that the transverse component of this current is significantly larger in altermagnets than in antiferromagnets, providing a clear experimental fingerprint to identify altermagnetic materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to identify and manipulate distinctive transport signatures of altermagnets (ATMs) for information processing, specifically focusing on how to experimentally distinguish them from conventional antiferromagnets.

Main result. The authors predict a 'vector' magnon spin current with both longitudinal and sizable transverse components, where the transverse response is enhanced by two orders of magnitude in altermagnets due to broken parity-time symmetry.

Method. The researchers use microscopic quantum kinetic equations to model electrical spin injection, diffusion, and dephasing, combined with a symmetry-based expansion of the response tensor.

Model / system. A heavy-metal/altermagnet (NM/ATM) interface modeled using a minimal square-lattice two-sublattice Hamiltonian including inter-sublattice exchange, anisotropy, and an applied magnetic field.

Key observables. Longitudinal and transverse magnon spin current components, and the transverse-to-longitudinal spin-current ratio.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange coupling J1 = 1 meV, spin S = 3/2, lattice constant a = 1 nm, and relaxation time tau_m = 1 ns.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a relaxation time tau_m that is equal for both magnon modes and focuses on the diffusion-driven transport regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of diffusive magnon transport and a comparison of flow in AFMs vs ATMs; Figure 2 illustrates injected magnon populations in the Brillouin zone; Figure 3 shows the dependence of longitudinal and transverse spin currents on the Neel vector orientation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of altermagnet transport, describes the microscopic model and quantum kinetic framework, presents the symmetry analysis of the response tensor, details the results regarding vector spin currents and their enhancement, and concludes with the experimental implications for distinguishing altermagnets.

Abstract

Altermagnets host chirally split magnons that promise unique functionalities for information processing. However, their distinctive transport signatures, crucial for experimental identification and manipulation, remain elusive. Here, we predict that a spin accumulation electrically injects a ``vector" or multidirectional magnon spin current into an altermagnet, comprising both longitudinal and sizable transverse components. Notably, this transverse current exhibits a sign reversal away from the source and can be switched on or off by reorienting the Néel vector. While such a transverse current is found to be not forbidden even in conventional antiferromagnets, we demonstrate through quantum-kinetic calculations that in altermagnets, the transverse response is enhanced by two orders of magnitude due to broken parity-time symmetry. This giant enhancement provides a decisive transport fingerprint for detecting magnon spin splitting and Néel-vector orientation, offering a clear criterion to experimentally distinguish altermagnets from conventional antiferromagnets.

Abstract

In this report, a unified description of the loss of saturation magnetization in the presence of a distribution of finite-size effects is provided for weakly disordered spin-1/2 ferromagnets. This description allows us to derive a unified form of the Bloch equation for these systems. We further extend this approach to obtain a unified expression for spin transport in such systems.

Frequency locking in lasing ZnO nanowire pairs

Authors: Ann-Kathrin Kollak, Lukas R. Jäger, Hark Hoe Tan, Carsten Ronning

Summary. The researchers demonstrated that two ZnO nanowire lasers can be dynamically synchronized through frequency locking when placed extremely close together. By controlling where the laser pump hits the wires, they can switch between full, partial, and no locking. This provides a robust way to control light at the nanoscale for future integrated photonic circuits.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the implementation and control of frequency locking and optical coupling in ZnO nanowire lasers at the nanoscale, specifically distinguishing between dynamic nonlinear interaction and static spectral filtering.

Main result. Demonstrated that closely spaced ZnO nanowire lasers (gap < 10 nm) exhibit dynamic frequency locking, including full and partial locking regimes, which can be actively controlled via spatial excitation distribution.

Method. Micromanipulation of ZnO nanowires into pairs, followed by spatially resolved micro-photoluminescence spectroscopy using an inhomogeneous optical pump spot.

Model / system. A pair of ZnO (Zinc Oxide) nanowires assembled in a staggered geometry on a SiO2/Si substrate, operating as evanescently coupled Fabry-Perot resonators in the extreme near-field.

Key observables. Lasing photoluminescence spectra, Free Spectral Range (FSR), group index (ng), spatio-spectral intensity maps, and side-mode suppression ratio (SMSR).

Important parameters / regimes. Gap distance (< 10 nm), pump power, detuning (cavity length difference), and pumping asymmetry (relative excitation intensity).

Assumptions / limitations. The threshold for lasing is operationally defined by the emergence of Fabry-Perot modes; the group index is approximated as a linear function of wavelength.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the nanowire assembly schematic, individual spectra before assembly, SEM images of the pair, and spatially resolved spectra. Supplementary figures show pump spot intensity maps, single nanowire spectral stability, and the derivation of the group index.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of nanoscale coupling, describes the experimental assembly and spectroscopic setup, presents the demonstration of frequency locking and its tunability, and concludes with the implications for nanophotonic systems.

Abstract

Frequency locking between coupled laser systems provides a powerful mechanism for stabilizing and controlling coherent emission, yet its implementation and applicability down to the nanoscale remains unknown and unexplored. Here, we demonstrate optical coupling and frequency locking in closely spaced ZnO nanowire lasers operating in the extreme near field (gap < 10 nm). We observe both full and partial frequency locking, manifested as the alignment of all or a subset of the lasing modes, by spatially controlling the optical excitation. We also observe single-mode lasing in a coupled nanowire pair where the multi-mode lasing of individual nanowires is suppressed. In contrast to previously reported coupled-cavity nanowire lasers, where spectral control arises from static filtering mechanisms such as the Vernier effect, our results indicate a dynamically established relationship between actively lasing nanowires. These findings establish frequency locking as a robust and tunable mechanism in nanowire lasers, opening new routes toward stabilized and controllable nanoscale light sources for integrated nanophotonic systems.

Summary. This paper proves that the quantum metric cannot induce an intrinsic longitudinal nonlinear conductivity in solids. It resolves theoretical discrepancies by showing that previous claims of a finite response were due to using gauge-variant energy instead of gauge-invariant energy. This suggests that reported longitudinal signals in materials like MnBi2Te4 likely stem from extrinsic, disorder-induced mechanisms.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether the quantum metric can generate an intrinsic longitudinal nonlinear transport response in solids, aiming to resolve theoretical inconsistencies in existing literature.

Main result. The author demonstrates that the quantum-metric-induced intrinsic longitudinal response identically vanishes due to the dissipationless nature of intrinsic currents, even though an intrinsic Hall response is allowed.

Method. The study employs standard quantum-mechanical perturbation theory up to the second order in an external electric field, utilizing semiclassical wave-packet dynamics and the Heisenberg equation of motion.

Model / system. The system consists of electrons in a crystal (metallic system) described by an unperturbed Hamiltonian H0 and subject to a uniform static electric field E.

Key observables. Nonlinear Hall conductivity, nonlinear longitudinal/nonreciprocal conductivity, intrinsic current density, Berry curvature, and the quantum metric.

Important parameters / regimes. Electric field strength E, relaxation time tau, chemical potential mu, and the quantum metric G and Berry curvature Omega.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes a uniform static electric field and is truncated at the second order of the external electric field.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem and the Hamiltonian, proceeds to derive field-perturbed Bloch states and velocities using second-order perturbation theory, simplifies the resulting tensors using the quantum metric, and concludes by demonstrating the vanishing of the longitudinal response and discussing implications for experimental discrepancies.

Abstract

Nonlinear charge transport in solids has emerged as a powerful probe of the quantum geometric properties of Bloch electrons. While the Berry curvature underlies the intrinsic anomalous Hall effect, recent studies have suggested that the quantum metric may generate both \emph{intrinsic} nonlinear Hall and longitudinal transport. Here, using standard quantum-mechanical perturbation theory, we demonstrate that the quantum-metric-induced intrinsic longitudinal response identically vanishes, even though the corresponding intrinsic Hall response is allowed. This conclusion follows from the dissipationless nature of intrinsic currents and holds independently of band structure details and to all orders in the nonlinear response. Our work resolves existing inconsistencies in the theoretical formulation of quantum-metric-induced nonlinear transport and suggests a reexamination of recently reported intrinsic longitudinal responses attributed to the quantum metric.

Summary. The researchers show that in CsOH.H2O, water and hydroxyl molecules are not stable entities but constantly interconvert through proton exchange. This process drives the structural transition of the crystal and provides a unique mechanism for high ionic conductivity without long-range molecular diffusion.

Why it may be interesting. The study explores a 2D analogue of water and the dynamics of a chemical-reaction-driven phase transition, which relates to many-body dynamics and the stability of networked systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the atomic-level dynamics, lifetime of water molecules, and the mechanism of the order-disorder transition in the crystal CsOH.H2O.

Main result. The order-disorder transition proceeds via a chemical reaction (proton exchange) rather than molecular rotation, where water and hydroxyl units interconvert on a picosecond scale.

Method. Ab initio Molecular Dynamics (AIMD) using rSCAN and PBE functionals, combined with Density Functional Perturbation Theory (DFPT) and a 1D anharmonic Hamiltonian model for Raman spectroscopy.

Model / system. The system is caesium hydroxide monohydrate (CsOH.H2O), featuring a quasi-2D honeycomb lattice of water and hydroxide ions that acts as a 2D analogue of water.

Key observables. Raman spectra (stretching, bending, and librational modes), radial distribution functions (RDF), mean squared displacements (MSD), and proton exchange barriers.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature range (200K to 700K), proton exchange barrier (~0.2 eV), and picosecond-scale molecular lifetimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The hydrogen arrangement obeys 'ice rules'; Raman activity is modeled using a linear polarizability and a 1D harmonic well perturbed by a Gaussian term.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the crystal structure, describes the AIMD simulation setup, analyzes the symmetry-breaking and energetic stability of the lattice, investigates the dynamical network of proton exchange, and concludes with a theoretical model for Raman signatures.

Abstract

We describe the atomic-level motions in caesium hydroxide monohydrate (CsOH$\cdot$H$_2$O), which is a chemical compound containing layers of water and hydroxide ions. At this composition, each oxygen is involved in three hydrogen bonds which, in the hexagonal structure, form a quasi-2D honeycomb lattice. While oxygen and caesium atoms form a typical crystal lattice, the dynamics of the hydrogen atoms are more complex. Here we show that the covalent and hydrogen bonds are continually interconverting, meaning that the water and hydroxyl are interconverting by proton exchange. The order-disorder transition of the water and hydroxyl proceeds by chemical reaction rather than rotation or diffusion of the molecules. A hydrogen can rotate out of the layer, leaving a vacant site in the 2D layer. Such a hydrogen vacancy can diffuse rapidly by single molecule rotation, leading to fast-ionic conduction. The proton exchange leads to a novel type of Raman activity combining stretch and exchange processes, for which we develop a theoretical model. This would manifest in a broad single peak associated with both H$_2$O and OH stretches and a low frequency peak appearing at elevated temperature.

Abstract

Organic semiconductors (OSCs) are widely explored for flexible optoelectronic technologies, with performance governed not only by molecular design, but also by solid-state packing, which can give rise to polymorphism. Dinaphthothienothiophene (DNTT) is a benchmark OSC that has long been considered monomorphic. Here, we discover, isolate, and resolve the crystal structure of a previously unrecognised bulk polymorph of DNTT, termed blue DNTT owing to its characteristic blue emission. Coexisting with the well-known (green) DNTT in commercial powders, yet previously overlooked, blue DNTT represents the thermodynamically stable form. By combining X-ray diffraction, Raman, and THz spectroscopy with simulations, we demonstrate that polymorphism in DNTT reshapes the low-frequency phonon landscape and transfer-integral network, impacting charge transport. Electron mobility along the crystallographic a and b-axes in blue DNTT exceeds twice the hole mobility in the green phase. To our knowledge, this is the first reported acene-based semiconductor exhibiting three-dimensional charge transport. Polymorphism emerges as a key lever to tune charge transport dimensionality and carrier efficiency in organic semiconductors.

Summary. This paper uses first-principles calculations to show that a specific 3D carbon network, C136, is prone to magnetic and structural instabilities due to its flat-band electronic structure. It identifies these schwarzites as candidate platforms where magnetism, lattice distortions, and superconductivity compete.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the negative Gaussian curvature in 3D sp2-bonded carbon schwarzites (C136) generates electronic structures, such as flat-bands, strong enough to drive broken-symmetry instabilities like magnetism or structural reconstruction.

Main result. The study finds that C136 is not a stable metal but exhibits significant spin-polarized instability (lowering energy by ~0.50 eV) and lattice instability characterized by large imaginary-frequency phonon modes.

Method. First-principles Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations using the Quantum ESPRESSSE software with the PBE functional, combined with the finite-displacement method via Phonopy for phonon calculations.

Model / system. A 136-atom D-type carbon schwarzite (C136), which is a three-dimensional sp2-bonded carbon network with negative Gaussian curvature and non-trivial ring topology.

Key observables. Total magnetization (~11.01 Bohr magnetons), energy difference between spin-polarized and non-spin-polarized states, and phonon frequency (specifically the softest mode at -11.90 THz).

Important parameters / regimes. High density of states (DOS) near the Fermi level, flat-band features, and the wave vector q = [0.5, 0.5, 0.5] for the softest phonon mode.

Assumptions / limitations. The phonon calculations were performed on a 1x1x1 supercell and have not yet been fully converged with respect to supercell size; the structure has not undergone full variable-cell relaxation.

Figures summary. Table 1 summarizes SCF calculation results (energy and magnetization); Figure 1 shows the phonon density of states with negative spectral weight; Figure 2 shows the diagnostic phonon band structure highlighting imaginary branches.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the schwarzite system, presents DFT results for electronic and magnetic properties, details phonon calculations revealing structural instability, and concludes by discussing the competition between magnetism, lattice reconstruction, and potential superconductivity.

Abstract

Carbon schwarzites are three-dimensional sp2-bonded carbon networks with negative Gaussian curvature. Because negative curvature can reorganize the pi-electron manifold, these materials provide a natural platform for flat-band physics, enhanced density of states, and competing broken-symmetry phases. Here I report first-principles calculations for a 136-atom D-type carbon schwarzite cell using density-functional theory. Non-spin-polarized calculations show a metallic electronic structure with pronounced flat-band features and high density of states near the Fermi level. A spin-polarized calculation converges to a magnetic solution with total magnetization of approximately 11.01 Bohr magnetons per 136-atom cell and absolute magnetization of approximately 12.15 Bohr magnetons per cell. The spin-polarized state is lower in energy than the corresponding non-spin-polarized solution by approximately 0.03687 Ry, or about 0.50 eV per cell. Finite-displacement phonon calculations using Phonopy and Quantum ESPRESSO, with 78 symmetry-reduced displaced configurations, further reveal substantial imaginary-frequency modes in the ideal submitted structure. The softest mode in the calculated band path occurs near q = [0.5, 0.5, 0.5], with frequency approximately -11.90 THz. These results indicate that ideal C136 is not a simple harmonically stable metallic carbon phase. Instead, it appears to be a flat-band carbon network close to spin and lattice broken-symmetry instabilities. Possible interpretations include Stoner-like flat-band magnetism and Peierls- or charge-density-wave-like structural reconstruction. Superconductivity is not claimed here as an established ground state; rather, the results identify C136 and related D-type schwarzites as candidate platforms where magnetism, soft-mode reconstruction, and possible doped or structurally stabilized superconducting phases compete.

Analyticity and symmetry of band extrema in gapped solids: when does the effective mass approximation hold?

Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02576v1

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the effective mass approximation in semiconductors, proving that band dispersion is analytic at non-degenerate extrema for standard computational methods. It also establishes that any observed anisotropy in the effective mass tensor must strictly follow the symmetry of the crystal's point group, providing a way to identify numerical errors in first-principles calculations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the validity of the effective mass approximation (EMA) by determining whether band dispersion is real-analytic at non-degenerate extrema and establishing symmetry constraints on the effective mass tensor.

Main result. The authors prove that band dispersion is real-analytic at any non-degenerate extremum for standard ab initio Hamiltonians (DFT, hybrid DFT, and G0W0), implying that band warping is intrinsically tied to degeneracy. They also provide a symmetry-based classification of the effective mass tensor for all 32 crystallographic point groups.

Method. The study employs mathematical proofs using the Implicit Function Theorem, Weierstrass theorem, and Combes-Thomas estimates, alongside group theory for symmetry analysis and numerical finite-difference calculations.

Model / system. Gapped solids (semiconductors and insulators), including 2D materials like monolayer MoS2 and TiO2, modeled using DFT (LDA, GGA, meta-GGA), hybrid functionals, and G0W0 quasiparticle energies.

Key observables. Effective mass tensor, band dispersion E(k), band warping, and quasiparticle energies.

Important parameters / regimes. Non-degenerate vs. degenerate band extrema, little group symmetry (Gk0), and the analyticity strip (Omega) in the frequency domain.

Assumptions / limitations. The system must be gapped; for G0W0, the quasiparticle correction must be smaller than the Kohn-Sham gap; the proof assumes the non-local potential decays exponentially.

Figures summary. Figure 1 is a decision tree for the applicability of the analyticity theorem; Figure 2 shows numerical results for monolayer TiO2; Figure 4 illustrates the spectral structure and analyticity region of the correlation self-energy.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of EMA validity, proceeds to a mathematical proof of analyticity for local and non-local Hamiltonians, uses group theory to derive symmetry constraints on the mass tensor, and concludes with numerical applications to MoS2 and TiO2.

Abstract

The effective mass approximation is widely used across models of carrier transport, optical response, and excitons in semiconductors and insulators, but its validity hinges on the assumption that the band dispersion $E_n(\mathbf{k})$ at the relevant extremum is analytic. We prove that analyticity holds at any non-degenerate extremum for the standard ab initio Hamiltonians, including density functional theory with local or hybrid exchange-correlation functionals and for band-edge $G_0W_0$ quasiparticle energies in gapped systems. Band non-analyticity (or warping) in these settings is therefore intrinsically tied to degeneracy. We then use group theory to determine the symmetry-allowed form of the effective mass tensor for each of the 32 crystallographic point groups, providing a stringent consistency check on first-principles calculations. As a representative application, we show that the electron and hole effective masses at the $K$ point of monolayer MoS$_2$ must be strictly isotropic at the DFT and $G_0W_0$ levels.

Abstract

We present a first principles study of the ferrochiral phase transition in chiral BaTiOCu$_4$(PO$_4$)$_4$, which has been shown using X-ray diffraction to proceed via the antiferroaxial rotation of antipolar structural units. We analyze the atomic-site electric and magnetic multipole moments to identify connections between these multipoles and chirality and corroborate the previous experimental interpretation. We show that antiferroically ordered atomic-site electric toroidal dipole moments act as an order parameter for the antiferroaxial rotations, and that the overall chiral order is parameterized by the composite order of the antipolar electric dipole and electric toroidal dipole moments. Finally, we evaluate the suitability of various proposed order parameters for chirality, show that some can be excluded and suggest the most promising directions for future exploration.

Assumptions / limitations. The review assumes the relevance of the Drude and Drude-Smith models for conductivity and focuses on the intraband-dominated regime at THz frequencies.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the graphene band structure and light-matter interaction; Figure 3 illustrates responsivity maps and modulation depth improvements in graphene/Si and graphene/Ge; Figure 5 depicts the architectural evolution of graphene modulators from large-area to resonant metamaterials.

Paper structure. The paper begins with graphene physics (band structure and transport), moves to detection mechanisms (PTE, PB, and plasma-wave effects), discusses advanced quantum-limited detection and modulation, and concludes with the evolution of high-speed metamaterial-integrated architectures.

Abstract

Graphene has been one of the most investigated materials in the last decade. Its unique optoelectronic properties have indeed raised it to an ideal and revolutionary candidate for the development of entirely novel technologies across the whole electromagnetic spectrum, from the microwaves to the x-rays, even crossing domain of intense application relevance, as terahertz (THz) frequencies. Owing to its exceptionally high tensile strength, electrical conductivity, transparency, ultra-fast carrier dynamics, non-linear optical response to intense fields, electrical tunability and ease of integration with semiconductor materials, graphene is a key disruptor for the engineering of generation, manipulation, and detection technologies with ad-hoc properties, conceived from scratch. In this review, we elucidate the fundamental properties of graphene, with an emphasis on its transport, electronic, ultrafast and non-linear interactions, and explore its enormous technological potential of integration with a diverse array of material platforms. We start with a concise introduction to graphene physics, followed by the most remarkable technological developments of graphene-based photodetectors, modulators, and sources in the 1-10 THz frequency range. As such, this review aims to serve as a valuable resource for a broad audience, ranging from novices to experts, who are keen to explore graphene physics for conceiving and realizing micro- and nano-scale devices and systems in the far infrared. This would allow addressing the present challenging application needs in quantum science, wireless communications, ultrafast science, plasmonics and nanophotonics.
