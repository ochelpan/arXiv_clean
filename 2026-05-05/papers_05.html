Effective attraction by repulsion

Authors: Rosalba Garcia-Millan, Luca Cocconi, Ziluo Zhang, Marius Bothe, Letian Chen, Zigan Zhen, Gunnar Pruessner
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01421v1

Summary. This paper provides a microscopic theoretical explanation for how purely repulsive active particles can exhibit effective attraction, a key driver of phase separation in active matter. By using field-theoretic renormalization, the authors show that attraction emerges as a higher-order effect of the repulsion. This bridges the gap between individual particle dynamics and macroscopic phase separation phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the microscopic origin of effective attraction in Motility-Induced Phase Separation (MIPS) and clarifying how purely repulsive interactions between active particles lead to particle accumulation.

Main result. The study demonstrates that while the leading-order effect of increasing repulsion is effective repulsion, higher-order renormalization effects create an emergent effective attraction. This provides a microscopic basis for the equilibrium-like phase separation analogy in active matter.

Method. The authors use a bottom-up microscopic theory based on Doi-Peliti Field Theory (DPFT) and a path-integral approach, employing a perturbation expansion and Feynman diagrams to calculate loop corrections to the interaction vertices.

Model / system. A minimal model of two soft run-and-tumble particles (RTPs) in a one-dimensional periodic domain, interacting via a repulsive Yukawa potential and undergoing stochastic reorientation (tumbling).

Key observables. Two-point correlation function, compressibility factor (S1), joint particle density, effective potential (W_eff), and the distinction between jamming distance (x_J) and accumulation distance (x_A).

Important parameters / regimes. Normalized repulsion coupling (nu-bar), Péclet number (Pe) representing activity, normalized interaction length (xi-bar), and normalized reorientation rate (gamma-bar).

Assumptions / limitations. The model is restricted to a minimal 1D two-particle system and assumes a Yukawa potential for the soft interactions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the particles, a plot of compressibility vs. repulsion strength showing a crossover, and a phase diagram; Figure 2 displays joint particle density profiles for varying repulsion and activity levels.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MIPS and the equilibrium analogy, describes the 1D two-particle model and field-theoretic framework, presents the perturbative results showing the emergence of attraction, and concludes by discussing the implications for higher-dimensional MIPS.

Abstract

Repulsive self-propelled particles tend to cluster, leading to Motility-Induced Phase Separation (MIPS). By analogy with equilibrium phase separation, the onset of MIPS has been associated with a transition to effective attraction between particles. Using an exact microscopic theory, we quantify the emergence of effective attraction in a minimal model: two soft run-and-tumble particles in a periodic domain. We show that, as repulsion increases, the leading-order behaviour is that of effective repulsion, while effective attraction emerges as a higher-order contribution to the renormalisation of the pair potential.

Emergent population dynamics of random walkers with cooperative reproduction and spatial selection

Authors: Ohad Vilk, Baruch Meerson
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01770v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper explores how the complexity of reproduction rules changes the way populations spread through space. It identifies a phase transition in ternary reproduction where populations can either spread or collapse into localized 'bullets,' suggesting that the prevalence of binary division in nature may be due to fundamental dynamical constraints.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how higher-order asexual reproduction (beyond binary division) affects the population dynamics and existence of traveling wave solutions in invading species.

Main result. Increasing reproduction order $k$ qualitatively changes invasion dynamics: $k=2$ leads to $D$-independent wave speeds, $k=3$ exhibits a phase transition between diffusive spreading and population collapse (invasion bullets), and $k>3$ results in the disappearance of traveling waves.

Method. The authors use a combination of hydrodynamic (PDE) modeling, dimensional analysis, Monte Carlo simulations of a discrete lattice model, and a matching procedure for self-similar solutions.

Model / system. A 1D lattice model of $N$ random walkers with $k$-order cooperative reproduction ($kA o (k+1)A$) and spatial selection, where the leftmost particle is removed to maintain constant population size.

Key observables. Traveling wave speed ($c$), population density profiles ($u(x,t)$), characteristic width ($\sigma$), and the dimensionless parameter $\alpha = \Lambda/D$.

Important parameters / regimes. Reproduction order $k$, branching rate $\Lambda$, diffusion constant $D$, and the critical threshold $\alpha_c \simeq 7.089$ for the ternary case.

Assumptions / limitations. The continuum approximation assumes the density profile spans many lattice sites; the hydrodynamic limit assumes $N o \infty$.

Figures summary. The notes mention a comparison of discrete mean-field theory and continuum solvers, and a figure (S1) comparing models.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the $N$-BBM model extension, derives the hydrodynamic limit, analyzes different reproduction orders ($k=1, 2, 3, >3$), explores the critical behavior and self-similar collapse for $k=3$, and validates results via Monte Carlo simulations.

Abstract

We extend the $N$ branching Brownian motions model of population invasion to higher-order asexual reproduction. Increasing reproduction order leads to qualitative changes: invasion fronts generically cease to exist beyond binary reproduction; and in the binary case itself, their speed becomes diffusion-independent. Ternary reproduction shows critical behavior, with collapse into a strongly localized `invasion bullet' in the supercritical regime, diffusive spreading in the subcritical regime, and a continuous family of fronts at criticality. These results suggest that the dominance of division and binary reproduction in nature reflects fundamental constraints on invasion dynamics.

Stochastic first-passage modeling of single-event burnout in SiC power MOSFETs

Summary. This paper develops a statistical-physics framework to model the probabilistic failure of SiC power MOSFETs under radiation. It demonstrates that microscopic fluctuations transform a sharp deterministic burnout threshold into a broad transition band and can trigger failure even in nominally safe conditions. This provides a way to bridge the gap between deterministic device simulations and observed experimental variability.

Why it may be interesting. The paper utilizes a first-passage framework and stochastic differential equations to describe a phase transition between stable and unstable regimes, which is highly relevant to the study of non-equilibrium phase transitions and noise-induced phenomena in open systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the stochastic nature of Single-Event Burnout (SEB) in SiC power MOSFETs by moving beyond deterministic thresholds to account for threshold broadening and noise-induced subthreshold runaway.

Main result. Finite fluctuations broaden the deterministic burnout threshold into a probabilistic transition band and enable failure under nominally recoverable conditions via rare stochastic excursions.

Method. A reduced-order, stochastic electrothermal feedback-relaxation model formulated as a first-passage problem using stochastic differential equations (SDEs) and Euler-Maruyama integration.

Model / system. A coarse-grained electrothermal model of SiC power MOSFETs subject to heavy-ion-induced ionization, incorporating carrier multiplication, avalanche feedback, Joule heating, and thermal relaxation.

Key observables. Probability of Single-Event Burnout (P_SEB), first-passage-time distributions, survival probability, and threshold dispersion (transition width).

Important parameters / regimes. Ionization strength (l), feedback strength (F), thermal relaxation rate (r), noise amplitudes (sigma_n, sigma_Theta), and the thermal indicator (Lambda_th).

Assumptions / limitations. The model uses a coarse-grained approach that does not resolve specific device geometries or spatial profiles of electric fields and current density, and assumes independent noise for carriers and temperature.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows spatial overlap of ion tracks and high-field regions; Figure 2 shows the electrothermal framework; Figures 3-5 illustrate deterministic trajectories, stochastic broadening, and noise-induced subthreshold runaway; Figure 6 details first-passage-time statistics; Figure 7 presents a feedback-relaxation phase diagram.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from a deterministic reduced-order model to a stochastic extension, followed by Monte Carlo trajectory simulations, and concludes with the presentation of a phase diagram and statistical observables.

Abstract

Single-event burnout (SEB) in silicon carbide (SiC) power MOSFETs is often characterized by deterministic threshold quantities. Near the boundary between recovery and runaway, stochastic variability can make this threshold description probabilistic rather than sharp. This work introduces a first-passage perspective for stochastic threshold broadening in burnout. The process is described by a reduced electrothermal feedback-relaxation model with an absorbing boundary. The model combines carrier multiplication, avalanche feedback, localized heating, carrier loss, and thermal relaxation. Stochastic carrier and thermal terms represent unresolved event-level variability. The main finding is that finite fluctuations broaden the deterministic burnout threshold into a probabilistic transition band. Noise-induced subthreshold runaway also emerges, where nominally recoverable conditions can still fail through rare stochastic excursions. First-passage-time distributions resolve the time scale of burnout and survival probabilities further distinguish rapid feedback-dominated runaway from delayed stochastic failure. A feedback-relaxation phase diagram organizes recoverable, probabilistic, and rapidly unstable regimes. This framework provides a statistical-physics interpretation of threshold dispersion in single-event burnout of SiC power MOSFETs by linking coarse-grained electrothermal dynamics to probabilistic and time-resolved failure observables.

Emergent flocking dynamics in chemorepulsive active colloids: interplay of disorder and noise

Authors: Sagarika Adhikary, Rajesh Singh
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02854v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper studies how noise and quenched disorder compete to influence flocking in active matter with chemical interactions. It reveals that the nature of the phase transition and the formation of spatial patterns like density bands are highly sensitive to the range of the chemical interaction.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how collective flocking dynamics and spatial order emerge and are modified by the interplay of quenched disorder (pinned particles) and angular noise in chemorepulsive active colloids.

Main result. The noise-driven phase transition depends significantly on particle density, whereas the pinning-driven transition does not; additionally, short-range interactions lead to discontinuous transitions and density band formation.

Method. Particle-based numerical simulations in a 2D continuous space using an Euler-Maruyama integrator and finite-size scaling analysis.

Model / system. A 2D collection of active colloids consisting of mobile and pinned particles that interact via a self-generated, chemorepulsive chemical field with both translational and rotational components.

Key observables. Polar order parameter (m), hexatic order parameter (psi_6), susceptibility (chi), Binder cumulant (U), and order parameter distribution P(m).

Important parameters / regimes. Pinning fraction (np), angular noise strength (Lambda_n), interaction screening parameter (lambda), and particle area fraction (phi).

Assumptions / limitations. The chemical field is modeled in the steady-state limit (instantaneous interaction), and particles have a fixed radius and constant self-propulsion speed.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows model schematics; Figure 2 presents phase diagrams for order parameters; Figure 4 illustrates phase transitions and density bands; Figure 5 shows order parameter probability distributions; Figure 6 shows radial pair correlation functions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the chemorepulsive model, presents phase diagrams for noise-free/disorder-free systems, analyzes the impact of noise and pinning disorder on phase transitions, explores the effect of interaction range (long vs. short), and concludes with scaling and structural analysis.

Abstract

Recent studies of active colloidal matter have revealed that a global polar order can arise from chemorepulsive interactions among particles without any explicit alignment interaction between them. In this work, we investigate such chemically interacting active colloids in the presence of quenched disorder, where a fraction of particles are randomly pinned in space. These pinned particles are restricted to rotational motion while remaining chemically coupled to the mobile population. In addition, angular noise is incorporated into the rotational dynamics to capture stochastic effects. To elucidate the interplay of quenched disorder and noise, we construct phase diagrams based on polar order and its fluctuations, and systematically analyze the associated disorder- and noise-driven phase transitions. Surprisingly, we find that the phase transition driven by the noise is significantly dependent on the density of the particles, whereas such a density-dependence is not present when the control parameter is the pinning fraction. The finite-size effects on these transitions are also examined. An effective interaction range, governed by the coefficient related to screening of the chemorepulsive interaction, plays a crucial role in collective behavior. When the effective interaction range is much smaller than the system size, the system exhibits density band formation, a feature absent in the long-range interaction regime. Moreover, near the transition point, the order parameter distribution becomes bimodal for the case of short-range interaction.

From Characterization To Construction: Generative Quantum Circuit Synthesis from Gate Set Tomography Data

Authors: King Yiu Yu, Aritra Sarkar, Erbing Hua, Maximilian Rimbach-Russ, Ryoichi Ishihara, Sebastian Feld
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01367v1

Summary. This paper introduces a new machine learning framework that uses raw Gate Set Tomography data to directly generate quantum circuits tailored to a specific noisy device. By using a hierarchical diffusion model, it bypasses the need for idealized gate models, allowing for the synthesis of circuits that are inherently aware of hardware-specific errors like crosstalk and drift.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems as it proposes a way to implicitly learn and utilize complex, non-Markovian noise environments (like crosstalk) for practical control without requiring explicit Hamiltonian modeling.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The decoupling of quantum gate characterization and circuit compilation leads to a hardware-software gap where compilers fail to account for complex, correlated noise like crosstalk and drift in NISQ devices.

Main result. The authors propose a Quantum Machine Learning Control (QMLC) framework that bypasses traditional two-step pipelines by directly learning a hardware-native latent concept space from Gate Set Tomography (GST) data to synthesize noise-aware circuits.

Method. A hierarchical generative pipeline using a Set Vision Transformer (Set-ViT) encoder to extract device context from GST data, followed by a two-stage diffusion model (Global-Context and Conditional Circuit-Token Diffusion) for circuit synthesis.

Model / system. The framework targets NISQ-era quantum processors, specifically focusing on Clifford circuits on n-qubit registers, and is designed to handle hardware-specific noise such as crosstalk, decoherence, and calibration drift.

Key observables. GST circuit-measurement pairs, measurement probability distributions, and target conditional covariance matrices.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth, gate count, qubit number (n), and the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) schedule for the diffusion process.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be stationary over the characterization window, and the implementation assumes a diagonal covariance matrix for efficient computation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares the traditional GST-to-decomposition pipeline with the proposed QMLC pipeline; Figure 2 illustrates the architecture of the generative decoder; Figure 3 shows the data grouping strategy and curriculum learning progression.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the limitations of current compilation pipelines, introduces the QMLC framework, details the Set-ViT encoder and hierarchical diffusion decoder architectures, describes the training strategies (curriculum learning and label consistency), and discusses potential applications in quantum control and neuromorphic integration.

Abstract

High-fidelity circuit execution on noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices is bottlenecked by compilation pipelines that disregard complex, correlated noise. To address this, this methodology article proposes a quantum machine learning control (QMLC) framework for generative quantum circuit synthesis from gate-set tomography (GST) data that bypasses the traditional two-step pipeline of characterizing native quantum gates via GST followed by unitary decomposition algorithms. Instead, a generative concept space is directly learnt from GST data, enabling conditional synthesis of quantum circuits on a desired output distribution. Our approach tokenizes GST germ circuits and embeds them into a structured latent space using a curriculum-learning-motivated strategy, starting with short circuits and progressively incorporating longer ones with diverse output statistics. The embedded sequences are processed by a set-vision transformer with permutation-invariant pooling, producing k-seed vectors that represent the learned concept space of the quantum device. Aggregating data across multiple circuits makes this latent representation inherently context-aware, capturing the shared physical noise environment (e.g., crosstalk, drift) that isolated gate metrics miss. We propose an unconditional diffusion model to sample from the concept space. During inference, a user provides a target measurement distribution, and the model generates a corresponding circuit. To ensure fidelity and robustness, the output is denoised using a diffusion model that operates on the target conditional covariance matrix. This end-to-end framework is a step towards context-aware, hardware-native circuit synthesis directly from raw GST data, which offers a new paradigm for integrating quantum control and compilation. The QMLC framework is particularly suited for near-term quantum devices with complex calibration procedures.

Summary. This work quantitatively examines how confining a polymer film between two walls affects its internal structure and topology. Key findings show that knotting probability peaks at an intermediate thickness related to the chain's bulk size, and that wall proximity causes the polymer conformation to flatten. The study uses advanced simulation techniques and layer-by-layer analysis to build a comprehensive picture linking confinement, chain flexibility, and topological complexity.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on classical polymer physics, the study of topological constraints and confinement effects provides a rich analogy for understanding entanglement in quantum many-body systems or the topological stability of quantum states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how physical confinement, modeled by varying film thickness, alters the topological properties (knotting) and conformational statistics (entanglements) of polymer chains.

Main result. Knotting probability shows a non-monotonic dependence on film thickness, peaking near the bulk radius of gyration, while layer-resolved analysis confirms that wall effects dictate local structural changes.

Method. The research employs Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations and Monte Carlo methods, utilizing specialized codes for knot detection (Alexander polynomial) and entanglement analysis (Z1+ code).

Model / system. The system consists of polymer chains confined between two parallel walls (thin films). The model incorporates harmonic bonds, repulsive WCA interactions, and a Kratky-Porod potential to account for chain stiffness.

Key observables. Knotting probability ($P_{ ext{knot}}$), entanglement length ($ ext{N}_{ ext{entang}}$), and layer-resolved components of the squared radius of gyration ($\langle R_g^2 angle$).

Important parameters / regimes. Film thickness ($h$), stiffness parameter ($B$), and the bulk radius of gyration ($R_{g, ext{bulk}}$) serve as critical length scales.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on developing an integration scheme to extrapolate global film properties from layer-resolved data, though deviations are noted for very thin films.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the non-monotonic dependence of $P_{ ext{knot}}$ on $h$ for flexible chains, and show layer-resolved profiles of knotting and $\langle R_g^2 angle$ components near the confining walls.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the effect of confinement on topology, first establishing the general $h$-dependence, then using layer-resolved analysis to map local wall effects, and finally investigating the role of chain flexibility (stiffness $B$).

Abstract

We study how confinement affects topology and conformations in polymer films of varying thickness $h$. The knotting probability exhibits a maximum at intermediate thicknesses near the bulk radius of gyration $h \approx R_\mathrm{g,bulk}$, vanishes at small $h$ and approaches bulk values for large $h$. Close to walls, the entanglement length increases monotonically and conformations become flatter. A layer-resolved analysis of structural and topological properties allows us to reconstruct the explicit thickness dependencies by integrating layer-resolved properties of a thick film.

Quantum State Engineering Under Multiple Expectation-Value Constraints

Authors: Anjali Mahapatra, Gururaj Kadiri
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02367v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5

Summary. This paper presents QUEST, a new quantum algorithm for engineering pure states that satisfy multiple simultaneous expectation-value constraints. By using an adaptive, analytic approach to determine rotation angles, it avoids the vanishing gradient and local minima problems that plague standard variational quantum algorithms. The method is shown to be highly effective for preparing complex states, such as thermal states and Hubbard model configurations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a robust alternative to ADAPT-VQE for many-body dynamics and state engineering, specifically offering a way to synthesize complex states (like thermal or fermionic states) without the instabilities of classical gradient-based optimization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of preparing a pure quantum state that simultaneously satisfies multiple prescribed expectation-value constraints, a task that generalizes standard ground-state preparation but suffers from vanishing gradients and local minima in standard variational algorithms.

Main result. The introduction of QUEST, a framework that uses an adaptive sequence of Pauli rotations with analytically determined angles to bypass the optimization failures (like barren plateaus and stalling) common in gradient-based variational methods.

Method. An iterative, 'optimizer-free' algorithm that constructs a state via a depth-adaptive sequence of Pauli rotations, using either gradient-based selection or an exact analytic approach to determine rotation angles by reconstructing a trigonometric polynomial.

Model / system. The framework is general and applicable to any n-qubit system, with specific demonstrations using the Transverse Field Ising Model (TFIM) for pseudo-thermal state generation and the 1D Hubbard model for fermionic lattice simulations.

Key observables. A set of Hermitian operators (Pauli strings) and their target expectation values.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of qubits, the set of target values, the Pauli pool (e.g., weight-2 strings), and the weighting strategy for the cost function (e.g., spectral width or uncertainty-based).

Assumptions / limitations. The algorithm assumes the state is prepared via a Pauli path (a sequence of Pauli rotations) and that expectation values can be accessed via a quantum oracle.

Figures summary. Figures show the convergence of the RMS cost per constraint for different QUEST variants in noise-less and noisy scenarios, the evolution of expectation values during the algorithm, and the success of the method in capturing thermal and fermionic properties in the Hubbard model.

Paper structure. The paper formalizes the expectation-value targeting problem, critiques the failure modes of existing VQAs, introduces the QUEST framework and its various implementations (tG, bG, tE, bE), provides mathematical proofs for gradient vanishing, and demonstrates performance through TFIM and Hubbard model simulations.

Abstract

This work introduces a formulation of quantum state engineering termed expectation-value targeting: the task of preparing a pure state whose expectation values with respect to a prescribed set of observables attain specified targets. This formulation subsumes standard ground-state preparation problems in quantum chemistry and many-body physics, while extending beyond variational energy minimization to multi-constraint state synthesis. The problem amounts to solving a system of nonlinear constraints on an exponentially large state space, for which no general efficient classical approaches are known. Variational quantum algorithms tackle this problem by restricting the search to a low-dimensional parameter space, and relying on classical optimization techniques for solutions. However, these approaches can become extremely ineffective for the present problem, where competing constraints can induce rugged landscapes and vanishing gradients (barren plateaus). Adaptive variational methods, in which the ansatz is constructed iteratively from a pool of candidate operators rather than fixed in advance, have been developed primarily for ground-state preparation. However, we show that the present problem also admits a similar construction. We introduce QUEST (Quantum Unitary Engineering of States to Target), a framework purpose-built for expectation-value targeting, in which the engineered state is constructed as a depth-adaptive sequence of Pauli rotations, with each rotation chosen to descend a sum-of-squared-residuals cost. QUEST provides a constructive route to expectation-value targeting, building the engineered state one Pauli rotation at a time, and establishes adaptive synthesis as a primitive for state preparation under multiple, potentially inconsistent target constraints.

Summary. This paper develops an algebraic method to solve Hamiltonians with position-dependent mass and complex potentials. It demonstrates how pseudo-bosonic structures can be preserved in these systems and provides a complete construction of biorthogonal eigenstates and bi-coherent states.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a rigorous mathematical framework for handling non-Hermitian systems with spatially varying parameters, which is highly relevant for describing dissipation and effective models in open quantum systems and quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to find the eigenvalues, eigenvectors, and bi-coherent states for a class of one-dimensional Hamiltonians featuring position-dependent mass (PDM) and potentially non-self-adjoint potentials.

Main result. The authors successfully constructed biorthogonal eigenstates and proved the mathematical equivalence of two different approaches to defining bi-coherent states using pseudo-bosonic algebra.

Method. The study employs an algebraic approach using abstract ladder operators (ALOs), operator factorization, and variable transformations to map the PDM Hamiltonian to a quadratic form.

Model / system. A one-dimensional particle with a position-dependent mass m(x) and a complex potential V(x), where the Hamiltonian is factorizable and related to pseudo-bosonic operators.

Key observables. Energy eigenvalues (E_n), wavefunctions (eigenstates), and bi-coherent states.

Important parameters / regimes. The commutation parameter lambda, the mass function m(x), and the complex parameter gamma; specifically, the real part of lambda determines square-integrability.

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonian is not assumed to be self-adjoint, and the mass function m(x) is assumed to be smooth and positive.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the PDM Hamiltonian and the ALO strategy, proceeds to derive the explicit forms of the potential and operators via factorization, analyzes the square-integrability of states under different mass profiles, and concludes by constructing and proving the equivalence of bi-coherent state definitions.

Abstract

We consider the Hamiltonian $H$ of a particle in one dimension with a position dependent mass for which we apply the recent strategy of the so-called {\em abstract ladder operators}, in the attempt to find its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. We don't assume that $H$ is self-adjoint, while we focus on the case of a factorizable operator. We show then that pseudo-bosonic operators play a relevant role in this analysis, and we construct bi-coherent states attached to these operators. Explicit examples are discussed.

From Qubit to Qubit: A Graduate Course in Quantum Mechanics

Authors: Jeremy Levy

Summary. This is a graduate-level textbook that reinterprets the standard quantum mechanics curriculum as a series of controlled generalizations starting from a single qubit. It connects fundamental quantum mechanics to advanced topics like the Dirac equation and the Renormalization Group through the lens of discrete-to-continuous transitions.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep conceptual unification between quantum information (qubits/QEC) and many-body physics (RG/field theory), which is highly relevant for researchers studying the emergence of continuous physics from discrete quantum architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The text aims to provide a unified pedagogical framework for graduate quantum mechanics by connecting the simplest quantum system (the qubit) to complex phenomena like the Dirac equation and the Renormalization Group.

Main result. It demonstrates that the standard apparatus of graduate quantum mechanics can be understood as a sequence of controlled generalizations, moving from discrete to continuous systems across various scales.

Method. A 'looping' pedagogical approach that progressively relaxes constraints (dimensionality, space, time, and coupling) and uses the Renormalization Group to bridge microscopic and macroscopic physics.

Model / system. The foundational model is the single spin-1/2 qubit (Bloch cube), which is expanded into multi-qubit lattices, the hydrogen atom, the Dirac equation, and the Ising model.

Key observables. Expectation values, probability (Born rule), energy eigenvalues, critical exponents, and scaling dimensions.

Important parameters / regimes. Hilbert space dimension, real space lattice spacing, time steps, coupling constants, and the epsilon parameter (4-d).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a working command of undergraduate-level linear algebra, complex analysis, and multivariable calculus.

Figures summary. The Bloch cube for visualizing qubit states; the beta function for the phi^4 theory showing the Gaussian and Wilson-Fisher fixed points; and energy gaps in the transverse-field Ising model.

Paper structure. The text follows a circular logic: starting with the single qubit, moving through multi-qubit lattices and continuous wave mechanics, progressing to 3D angular momentum and the hydrogen atom, then to relativistic dynamics and the Renormalization Group, and finally returning to the protected logical qubit.

Abstract

This textbook is drawn from notes for a two-semester graduate course in quantum mechanics. It begins with the most constrained quantum system, and recovers the rest of the subject by relaxing those constraints one at a time. The starting point is a single qubit, the smallest nontrivial Hilbert space with the strongest possible restriction on its dynamics, made concrete by a Bloch cube whose six faces are the cardinal states of a spin-1/2 system. Tensor products admit many qubits; lattices give them a place to live; time evolution sets them in motion; the continuum limit produces wavefunctions; three-dimensional angular momentum, the hydrogen atom, and perturbation theory follow; Lorentz invariance promotes the lattice of spinors to the Dirac equation; and the renormalization group asks how theories at different scales relate. Each chapter loosens one feature of the qubit while keeping the others fixed, so that the standard apparatus of graduate quantum mechanics arrives as a sequence of controlled generalizations rather than as separate topics. Discrete-to-continuous transitions recur at four scales: in Hilbert-space dimension, in real space, in time, and in coupling. The book closes by reimposing one of the original constraints, returning to a two-level system that is now a logical qubit protected by quantum error correction, with the fault-tolerance threshold appearing as an unstable RG fixed point and supplying the reason a logical qubit, independent of its underlying hardware, can exist at all.

Summary. The paper introduces a new quantum generative model that uses classical noise re-uploading to generate quantum states in a single step. This approach avoids the exponential decay of success probability found in diffusion-based quantum models and demonstrates high efficiency in learning many-body properties like magnetization and entanglement.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing quantum generative models like QuDDPM and QFM suffer from extremely low post-selection probabilities due to multi-step processes and high training/preparation overhead.

Summary. This paper provides a general method to calculate diffusion coefficients in interacting systems by relating them to known ideal-system values through thermodynamic geometry. It offers a computationally efficient alternative to expensive molecular dynamics for predicting transport properties in solids and surfaces.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a general thermodynamic framework to determine the transport coefficients (Onsager matrix) for interacting particle diffusion on a lattice.

Main result. The authors derive a fundamental relationship where the Onsager matrix of an interacting system is the ideal-system matrix scaled by the ratio of the determinants of their covariance matrices. This framework successfully reproduces the Darken equation for solids and the Zhdanov model for surface diffusion.

Method. The study employs classical irreversible thermodynamics, Einstein's fluctuation theory, and information geometry (using Massieu functions and Hessian matrices). Results are validated using Monte Carlo simulations.

Model / system. A lattice-gas model where each site is treated as an open thermodynamic system. The system includes binary mixtures of species A and B with various interaction potentials, including soft-core and power-law chemical potentials.

Key observables. Onsager matrix (L), transport coefficients, diffusion currents (intrinsic, tracer, and collective), and thermodynamic factor (Gamma).

Important parameters / regimes. Coverage (theta), chemical potential (mu), temperature (T), and the degree of particle interaction (represented by the excess chemical potential).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes local thermal equilibrium, small thermodynamic gradients (linear response regime), and that the range of interactions is much smaller than the cell size. It also neglects higher-order terms in the entropy expansion.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of particle transfer between cells; Figure 2 displays steady-state coverage profiles on a 1D lattice; Figures 3 and 4 show diffusion currents as a function of coverage for various interaction models, demonstrating agreement between theory and simulation.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the thermodynamic framework and the relationship between transition rates and entropy; it then develops the scaling relation for the Onsager matrix using the covariance matrix determinant; finally, it applies this theory to specific models (Darken and Zhdanov) and validates it via Monte Carlo simulations.

Abstract

This work presents a general thermodynamic approach to describe particle diffusion on a lattice, a model used to study transport processes in solids and on surfaces. By treating each lattice site as an open thermodynamic system, the effects of microscopic particle interactions are represented through the chemical potential. A fundamental relationship between the Onsager matrix ($L$) and its ideal-system counterpart ($L_\text{id}$, where interactions are neglected) using the determinant of the covariance matrix is demonstrated. This framework allows for the calculation of transport coefficients using the combination of their ideal values and thermodynamic properties. The general result is successfully applied to reproduce the Darken equation for substitutional diffusion in solids and to derive the non-diagonal diffusion matrix of the Zhdanov model for surface diffusion of Langmuir particles. In the last case, analytical predictions are further validated through numerical simulations across various interaction potentials.
