Mpemba Effect in Parametrically Driven Coupled Oscillators under White and Colored Noise

Authors: Aref Pahlevani, Morteza Rafiee, Mehdi Ansari-rad
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01723v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how parametric driving and colored noise influence the Mpemba effect in coupled oscillators. It finds that while driving is the essential mechanism for anomalous relaxation, colored noise further accelerates this process and widens the parameter range where it can be observed.

Why it may be interesting. It provides new insights into how environmental memory (non-Markovianity) and external driving can be used to control anomalous relaxation dynamics in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the Mpemba effect (anomalous relaxation) in a pair of coupled harmonic oscillators subject to parametric driving and both Markovian (white) and non-Markovian (colored) noise.

Main result. Parametric driving is the primary driver of the Mpemba effect, while colored noise enhances the effect by accelerating relaxation and expanding the parameter space where the effect occurs.

Method. The study uses the covariance-matrix formalism, quantum Langevin equations, and the Lyapunov equation, employing spectral decomposition of the drift matrix and Markovian embedding for colored noise.

Model / system. A pair of linearly coupled harmonic oscillators where one oscillator is parametrically driven and coupled to independent thermal baths (Gaussian white or Lorentzian colored noise).

Key observables. Frobenius distance to the steady state, projection onto the slowest relaxation mode, and the Mpemba crossing time (t*).

Important parameters / regimes. Parametric driving amplitude (Lambda), coupling strength (g), noise correlation time, and damping rates.

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics are assumed to be Gaussian and bosonic, with noise modeled via Lorentzian spectral density.

Figures summary. Phase diagrams showing the Mpemba crossing time (t*) and the Mpemba strength (M) as functions of driving and coupling strength, illustrating the expansion of the effect under colored noise.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Mpemba effect in driven systems, defines the coupled oscillator model and noise regimes, details the covariance-matrix and spectral methods, presents results on the influence of driving and noise, and concludes with potential applications.

Abstract

We study the Mpemba effect in a pair of linearly coupled harmonic oscillators, one of which is parametrically driven and coupled to an independent thermal bath. Using the covariance-matrix formalism, we derive the relaxation dynamics under both Gaussian white noise and Lorentzian colored noise, including single-channel and dual-channel noise embedding. We characterize relaxation through the Frobenius distance to the steady state and through the projection onto the slowest mode of the dynamical generator. Our results show that parametric driving provides the primary control knob for anomalous relaxation: as the drive approaches the stability boundary, the Mpemba crossing time decreases systematically. Colored noise further enhances the effect, with dual-oscillator Lorentzian noise producing a stronger reduction in the crossing time than single-oscillator noise and enlarging the parameter region where the Mpemba effect occurs. Nevertheless, the slow-mode structure of the drift matrix remains the dominant mechanism, while the influence of colored noise is secondary and mainly quantitative. We show that the Mpemba crossing time decreases as the system approaches the parametric stability boundary and that Lorentzian colored noise enlarges the region in the parametric and coupling strength plane where the Mpemba effect is observed.

Quantum scars from holographic boson stars

Authors: Yan Liu, Ya-Wen Sun, Yuan-Tai Wang
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02446v1

Summary. This paper demonstrates that holographic boson stars act as a realization of quantum many-body scars. It shows that these horizonless objects support integrable spectral branches and exhibit non-thermal entanglement and complexity revivals despite being embedded in a chaotic gravitational background.

Why it may be interesting. It connects gravitational dynamics to many-body scar physics, providing a holographic window into how non-ergodic, integrable subsectors can coexist with chaos, which is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and quantum chaos.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether holographic boson stars provide a gravitational realization of quantum many-body scars, characterized by non-thermal, integrable-like states embedded within a chaotic spectrum.

Main result. The study finds that boson stars exhibit a 'scarred' structure where the spectrum shows random-matrix signatures of chaos alongside embedded integrable branches, accompanied by subthermal entanglement and periodic revivals in Krylov complexity.

Method. The authors use numerical shooting methods for background solutions, WKB approximation for asymptotic spectral analysis, and Random Matrix Theory (RMT) to analyze spectral statistics.

Model / system. Asymptotically AdS mini-boson stars in 3+1 dimensions, modeled by Einstein gravity minimally coupled to a complex scalar field.

Key observables. Average gap ratio , normal-mode frequencies, vacuum-subtracted holographic entanglement entropy, and Krylov complexity.

Important parameters / regimes. Cosmological constant, scalar mass, central scalar amplitude, and angular momentum l.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is limited to the 'mini' boson star regime, which prevents a definitive large-mass scaling analysis to distinguish between volume-law and sub-volume-law entanglement.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows mass and frequency vs. central amplitude; Figure 2 shows normal-mode spectra; Figure 3 shows the transition in gap ratios; Figure 4 compares entanglement entropy of boson stars to black holes; Figure 5 shows periodic revivals in Krylov complexity.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of quantum scars in gravity, describes the boson star model and numerical setup, analyzes spectral statistics and integrability, evaluates entanglement and complexity dynamics, and concludes with implications for holographic non-ergodicity.

Abstract

Quantum many-body scars are atypical nonthermal states embedded in the chaotic spectrum that evade conventional ergodicity. We show that asymptotically AdS mini-boson stars provide a holographic realization of scar-like states. Their spectrum exhibits random-matrix signatures of chaos while supporting embedded integrable spectral branches. The full holographic system, including black holes, is generically chaotic with most eigenstates satisfying the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis; in contrast, the boson star macrostate probes an approximately integrable subsector within this chaotic spectrum, signaling scarred spectral structures. Boson stars further display anomalously low entanglement relative to black holes at the same energy density, and also robust revivals in Krylov complexity, revealing nonergodic dynamics. These spectral, entanglement, and dynamical diagnostics provide unified evidence for holographic quantum scars in a self-gravitating system. Our work suggests a new connection between many-body scar physics, quantum chaos, and horizonless gravitational dynamics.

Entanglement dynamics after quenches with inhomogeneous Hamiltonians

Authors: Andrea Di Pasquale, Federico Rottoli, Vincenzo Alba
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01595v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper studies how entanglement spreads after a quantum quench in systems with an interface between two different Hamiltonians. It provides an analytical framework for free-fermion models and discovers that in interacting systems, entanglement can still grow even when the transport of physical quantities like magnetization is blocked by the interface.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep look into how spatial inhomogeneities and scattering at interfaces modify the standard quasiparticle picture of entanglement spreading, specifically revealing how interactions can decouple entanglement growth from particle transport.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating entanglement dynamics and entropy growth following a quantum quench in one-dimensional many-body systems with inhomogeneous Hamiltonians.

Main result. The authors derive analytical formulas for entanglement growth in free-fermion models (XX and Ising) based on a quasiparticle scattering picture, and find that in the interacting XXZ chain, entanglement can continue to grow even when magnetization transport is suppressed.

Method. Analytical derivation using a quasiparticle picture and scattering theory (solving the lattice Schrodinger equation) combined with numerical simulations using tDMRG and TEBD.

Model / system. One-dimensional bipartite spin chains (XX, Transverse-field Ising, and XXZ models) with an interface where the Hamiltonian parameters (like magnetic field) differ between the left and right regions.

Key observables. Von Neumann entanglement entropy, transmission and reflection coefficients, magnetization, and Yang-Yang entropy.

Important parameters / regimes. Hydrodynamic limit (long times), magnetic field imbalance (h_L vs h_R), anisotropy parameter (delta), and the transmission coefficient (T).

Assumptions / limitations. The proposed analytical formula is specifically derived for cases where the subsystem boundary coincides with the Hamiltonian interface.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the setup and scattering mechanism; Figure 2 plots transmission coefficients for Ising and XX chains; Figure 3 shows linear entanglement growth for free models; Figure 4 shows numerical slopes for the Ising model; Figure 5 shows the vanishing of growth in the XX chain; Figures 6 and 7 show entanglement growth and magnetization profiles for the interacting XXZ chain.

Paper structure. Introduction of the problem and setup, analytical derivation of the scattering-based quasiparticle picture for free fermions, numerical benchmarking for XX and Ising chains, extension to the interacting XXZ chain via tDMRG, and discussion of the discrepancy between entanglement and transport in the interacting regime.

Abstract

We investigate entanglement dynamics in bipartite systems governed by inhomogeneous Hamiltonians of the form $H = H_L + H_R$, where $H_{L/R}$ acts only on the left or right region and is homogeneous within each region. Focusing on the XX chain and the transverse-field Ising chain, we derive analytical formulas for the entanglement entropy between the two regions in the hydrodynamic limit of long times. In this regime, fermions incident on the interface undergo scattering, generating entanglement between reflected and transmitted modes. The resulting quasiparticle picture is controlled by the transmission coefficient, which we obtain analytically by solving the stationary lattice Schrödinger equation. Due to the bounded dispersion, strong inhomogeneity suppresses both transport and entanglement growth. We benchmark our analytical predictions against numerical simulations in paradigmatic setups. Finally, we extend the analysis to the interacting XXZ chain using tDMRG. The numerical data show qualitative agreement with the quadratic case: entanglement growth remains suppressed in the strongly inhomogeneous limit. Notably, however, entanglement continues to increase even when transport is suppressed, at least at intermediate times.

Entropic Reciprocity in Time-Reversed Young Interferometry

Authors: Jianming Wen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01052v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 interference shaping light 4/5 · entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 3/5

Summary. This paper shows that time-reversed Young interferometry acts as an information processor that redistributes rather than reverses entropy. It identifies mutual information as the fundamental invariant between standard and time-reversed optical readouts and demonstrates how operating near destructive interference can boost parameter sensitivity.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel information-theoretic perspective on optical interferometry, specifically how 'source-space programming' can be used to engineer measurement sensitivity, which is highly relevant to quantum optics and precision parameter estimation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether time-reversed Young interferometry (TRY) reverses or reorganizes optical entropy and seeks to identify the reciprocal invariant between standard and time-reversed geometries.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that TRY reorganizes rather than reverses entropy, identifying mutual information as the true reciprocal invariant, and show that sensitivity to perturbations can be enhanced near destructive interference at the cost of detection probability.

Method. The study uses information-theoretic tools including Shannon entropy, Mutual Information, and Fisher Information applied to a statistical framework of joint event distributions and Bayesian updates.

Model / system. A reciprocal linear optical system modeled using scalar paraxial diffraction theory (Fresnel approximation) comparing standard Young interference (detector-plane readout) to a time-reversed geometry (source-plane readout).

Key observables. Detector-space entropy, conditioned source-label entropy, Mutual Information, Fisher Information for parameter estimation, and detection probability.

Important parameters / regimes. Physical perturbations (phase, tilt, defocus, etc.), residual null-depth (epsilon-bar), background noise floor (b), and the dimensionless reciprocal tuning parameter (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes scalar paraxial approximation, optical reciprocity, and the absence of nonreciprocal media or polarization-dependent effects.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a conceptual comparison of geometries; Figure 2 shows the relationship between detector entropy, source entropy, and mutual information; Figure 3 illustrates the trade-off between sensitivity enhancement and photon-budget cost in a regularized null model.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the physical geometries and the statistical framework, moves to establishing the entropic reciprocity and mutual information invariance, analyzes the enhancement of Fisher information in the null-response regime, and concludes with the trade-offs involving background noise and detection probability.

Abstract

We show that time-reversed Young interferometry reorganizes, rather than reverses, optical entropy. A fixed detector conditions the reciprocal source--detector Green function and produces a source-label probability distribution. Marginal entropies in the standard and time-reversed geometries are generally unequal; the reciprocal invariant is instead the mutual information between source and detector coordinates. Near a destructive response, the conditioned source-label entropy can decrease while Fisher information for small phase, tilt, or defocus perturbations increases. The result identifies time-reversed Young interferometry as a source-space information processor with no analogue in ordinary detector-plane fringe readout.

Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02872v1

Summary. The paper shows that by tuning the on-site interactions of a Bose-Einstein Condensate in a vertical optical lattice to match a resonance condition with the gravitational gradient, one can significantly enhance the precision of gravimetry. This resonance-induced amplification allows for better gravitational acceleration measurements using a finite number of particles.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates how many-body interactions, typically seen as a source of complexity, can be precisely tuned to enhance quantum sensing metrology in ultracold atom systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how to enhance the precision of gravitational field (gradient field h) measurements using many-body physics in optical lattices.

Main result. On-site interactions can amplify the Quantum Fisher Information by a factor of approximately 4 when the resonance condition U = mh is met, improving gravitational sensitivity by a factor of approximately 2.

Method. Quantum Estimation Theory using the Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) and the Cramér-Rao bound to analyze the scaling of sensing precision.

Model / system. A Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC) of ultracold bosonic atoms confined in a vertical 1D optical lattice, modeled by the Stark Bose-Hubbard Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), gravitational sensitivity (delta g/g), site occupation, and the resonance coefficient Ar(U, m).

Important parameters / regimes. On-site interaction strength (U), tunneling rate (J), gradient field strength (h), lattice size (L), and particle number (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is analyzed in the long-time limit where QFI scales as t^2, and the analysis focuses on the unitary time evolution of the state.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the lattice schematic and particle tunneling at resonance; Fig 2 shows the time evolution of normalized QFI across different phases; Fig 7 plots normalized QFI and the resonance coefficient for different m and N.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of gravimetry, defines the Stark Bose-Hubbard model, analyzes QFI scaling in different phases (localized, transition, extended), quantifies the resonance enhancement via the coefficient Ar, and concludes with the implications for sensitivity.

Abstract

By confining a Bose-Einstein condensate in a vertical lattice subjected to a gravitational potential, we analyze the quantum Fisher information to determine its scaling with respect to time, system size and particle number. Our results reveal that in the localized phase, on-site interactions $U$ amplify the quantum Fisher information by a factor with respect to resonance condition $U=mh$ where $U$ is factor of gradient field amplitude $h$. This precision enhancement can be employed in gravitational acceleration measurements with a finite number of particles trapped in optical lattices.

Statistics of Marginal Wavefunctions as a Real-Space Diagnostic of Quantum Entanglement

Authors: Ivan P. Christov
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01728v1

Summary. This paper introduces a new method to map quantum entanglement in real space by analyzing the statistical diversity of one-body wavefunctions in a Monte Carlo ensemble. By treating the Gram matrix of these waves as a covariance operator, the authors show that entanglement entropy can be tracked via functional standard deviation. This approach avoids the heavy computational cost of full many-body wavefunction reconstruction.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel bridge between classical ensemble statistics and quantum entanglement, offering a way to map the spatial distribution of correlations in many-body dynamics using efficient Monte Carlo-based diagnostics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a computationally efficient, real-space diagnostic tool to extract spatially resolved quantum entanglement and coherence directly from an ensemble of marginal wavefunctions without reconstructing the full many-body wavefunction.

Main result. The paper demonstrates that the Gram matrix of a marginal-wave ensemble acts as a covariance operator whose spectrum matches the Schmidt spectrum, and its functional standard deviation accurately tracks the spatial profile of von Neumann entanglement entropy.

Method. The authors utilize Time-Dependent Quantum Monte Carlo (TDQCO) to generate an ensemble of one-body wavefunctions and particle trajectories, applying spatial partitioning and Gram matrix analysis to compute local entanglement metrics.

Model / system. The framework is applied to one-dimensional two-electron bosonic and fermionic systems, specifically including the 1D helium atom (parahelium and orthohelium) and 1D hydrogen-like molecules.

Key observables. Gram matrix spectrum, Schmidt spectrum, von Neumann entanglement entropy, functional standard deviation, reduced density matrix (RDM), and local linear entropy.

Important parameters / regimes. Hartree-Fock limit (weak entanglement), interaction-driven divergence (enhanced entanglement), and spatial domains (Omega_alpha) for partitioning.

Assumptions / limitations. The method relies on the specific dual particle-wave representation of TDQMC; it assumes localization occurs in walker configuration space and that spatial domains do not restrict the global support of marginal waves.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows spatial partitioning of a molecule; Figure 2 displays walker distributions and guide waves for helium and hydrogen-like systems; Figure 3 compares TDQMC and exact results for entropy, standard deviation, and Schmidt spectra; Figure 5 shows spatially resolved statistics for molecules.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the statistical framework for wave ensembles, develops the Gram matrix and functional statistics approach, applies the method to 1D helium atoms to validate against exact results, extends the analysis to 1D hydrogen-like molecules, and discusses implications for fermionic antisymmetry and future scalability.

Abstract

We present a statistical framework for extracting spatially resolved entanglement directly from an ensemble of marginal (one-body) wavefunctions in Time-Dependent Quantum Monte Carlo (TDQMC). Treating the guide waves as a statistical mixture in Hilbert space, we show that the Gram matrix acts as a covariance operator whose spectrum coincides with the Schmidt spectrum. The associated functional standard deviation closely tracks the von Neumann entanglement entropy both globally and locally via walker partitioning, providing a physically transparent real-space diagnostic of quantum correlations without requiring construction of the full many-body wavefunction. Applications to one-dimensional two-electron bosonic and fermionic systems (helium atom and hydrogen-like molecule) demonstrate excellent agreement with strict conditional-wave results for opposite-spin electrons. For same-spin fermions, TDQMC statistical treatment of exchange symmetry yields positive, physically consistent local entropies. The method establishes a direct bridge between classical ensemble statistics and quantum entanglement measures, offering a computationally efficient real-space diagnostic tool for mapping the spatial distribution of correlations.

Temporal State Tomography via Quantum Snapshotting the Temporal Quasiprobabilities

Authors: Zhian Jia
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02655v1

Summary. The paper introduces Temporal State Tomography, a new method to reconstruct quantum processes across multiple time steps using temporal quasiprobability distributions. It demonstrates that complex temporal dynamics can be recovered through simple sequential measurements and provides a theoretical guarantee on the number of experimental samples needed for accurate reconstruction.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics as it provides a rigorous method for characterizing the dynamics of time-dependent quantum processes and temporal correlations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a unified framework for Temporal State Tomography (TST) to reconstruct multi-time quantum processes, including both density operators and quantum channels, from experimental data.

Main result. The paper establishes that any temporal quasiprobability distribution (TQD) can be accessed via classical post-processing of quantum instruments and derives the sample complexity required for faithful reconstruction.

Method. The authors use 'quantum snapshotting' to transform TQDs into physically realizable sequential measurements and employ the Choi-Jamiołkowski isomorphism and Hoeffding inequalities to bound reconstruction error.

Model / system. The model considers a multi-time quantum process characterized by an initial state and a sequence of time-ordered, completely positive trace-preserving (CPTP) maps across multiple time instances.

Key observables. Temporal quasiprobability distributions (TQDs), specifically Kirkwood-Dirac and Margenau-Hill distributions, and temporal Pauli correlators.

Important parameters / regimes. Sample complexity (N), error tolerance (epsilon), failure probability (delta), and the dimension of the temporal state space (d).

Assumptions / limitations. The reconstruction assumes the use of informationally complete (IC) instruments and that the temporal state admits a specific linear expansion.

Figures summary. Figure 1(a) illustrates the generalization of multipartite density operators to include timelike correlations, while Figure 1(b) shows the construction of a temporal phase space for quantum trajectories.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of temporal states, defines the TQD-based reconstruction framework, proposes the quantum snapshotting protocol, and concludes with a formal mathematical proof of the sample complexity and error bounds.

Abstract

Quantum tomography is a cornerstone of quantum information science, enabling the reconstruction of states and channels from experimental data. Here we introduce a new paradigm, temporal state tomography (TST), for reconstructing quantum processes across multiple times. Our approach is based on temporal quasiprobability distributions (TQDs), which, in the informationally complete setting, provide a complete description of multi-time quantum processes and uniquely determine temporal states. We formulate TST as a unified framework for reconstructing both density operators and quantum channels within a single scheme. We show that any TQD can be obtained via classical post-processing of measurement outcomes generated by a fixed set of quantum instruments, thereby establishing a direct operational route to accessing TQDs experimentally. For informationally complete TQDs, the associated temporal state can be reconstructed via a temporal Bloch-type representation. Leveraging this correspondence, we derive the sample complexity of TST, thereby quantifying its statistical efficiency.

Validity and Limits of Low Order Hybridization Expansion Approaches for Multi-Orbital Systems

Summary. This paper identifies why low-order hybridization expansion methods fail in multi-orbital settings by showing that the least correlated orbital unphysically suppresses correlations in all other orbitals. It provides a theoretical and numerical guide for determining when single-orbital intuition remains valid in multi-orbital systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in many-body dynamics and open quantum systems as it identifies a fundamental failure mode in common impurity solvers used to model the dynamics of interacting subsystems coupled to environments.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the accuracy and fundamental limitations of low-order hybridization expansion methods, such as NCA and OCA, when applied to multi-orbital quantum impurity systems.

Main result. The authors find that the accuracy of these methods is governed by the least correlated orbital; a spurious coupling is generated by the truncated expansion that transfers the properties of the rapidly decaying orbital to all others, suppressing features like the Kondo resonance.

Method. The study uses an analytical approach based on a decoupled orbital limit as a reference point, combined with numerical verification using two-orbital model systems in the steady-state.

Model / system. A general multi-orbital Anderson impurity model where an interacting impurity region is coupled to infinite noninteracting fermionic baths, allowing for arbitrary intra- and inter-orbital electron-electron interactions.

Key observables. Single-particle spectral functions, real-time retarded Green's functions, and the Kondo resonance peak height.

Important parameters / regimes. Hybridization strength (Gamma), inverse temperature (beta), Coulomb interaction (U), and the hybridization ratio between different orbitals.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the truncated hybridization expansion (NCA/OCA) and uses the decoupled orbital limit as a controlled benchmark.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Feynman diagrams for NCA and OCA; Figure 2 compares diagrammatic structures of single-orbital vs multi-orbital NCA; Figures 6-9 show spectral functions, Kondo peak suppression, and the decay of retarded Green's functions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the multi-orbital framework, establishes the analytical connection between multi-orbital and single-orbital propagators in the decoupled limit, identifies the mechanism of spurious coupling, and provides numerical validation through two-orbital steady-state calculations.

Abstract

Low-order hybridization expansion methods such as the non-crossing approximation (NCA) and the one-crossing approximation (OCA) are widely used impurity solvers in the study of strongly correlated systems, yet their accuracy in genuine multi-orbital settings remains poorly understood. Using the decoupled orbital limit as a controlled reference point, we derive analytic results connecting multi-orbital restricted propagators and Green's functions to their single-orbital counterparts, identify the diagrammatic mechanisms responsible for the breakdown of low-order methods in multi-orbital settings, and determine their regimes of applicability. Our central finding is that the accuracy of these methods is governed by the least correlated orbital: i.e., the orbital with the most rapidly decaying retarded Green's function. That orbital's properties are transferred to all other orbitals through a spurious coupling generated by the truncated expansion, thereby suppressing correlation-induced features such as the Kondo resonance. This occurs even in orbitals that are themselves strongly correlated within single-orbital calculations using the same approximation scheme. We confirm this numerically across representative two-orbital model systems in the steady-state, systematically identifying the parameter regimes in which low-order methods succeed or fail. Our results provide a practical guide for assessing when insights from single-orbital calculations carry over to multi-orbital settings, and serve as a benchmark for the development and validation of higher-order multi-orbital impurity solvers.

Direct Time-Domain Observation of l-Doubling via Centrifugal-Distortion Pre-compensation

Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02512v1

Summary. This paper presents a method to use shaped femtosecond pulses to cancel out centrifugal distortion in rotating molecules. By pre-compensating for this distortion, the researchers were able to resolve fine rotational features, such as $l$-doubling, that are normally obscured in time-domain experiments.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and AMO physics as it demonstrates precise coherent control over rotational wavepackets, providing a method to resolve fine-scale quantum features that are otherwise hidden by dephasing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Centrifugal distortion in molecular rotational dynamics causes $J$-dependent dephasing, which leads to broadened, multi-cycle revival structures that obscure fine rotational features like $l$-doubling.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated that applying a cubic spectral phase to femtosecond pulses can pre-compensate for centrifugal distortion, compressing distorted revivals into near single-cycle events and enabling the direct time-domain observation of $l$-doubling in CO2.

Method. The study uses a 4-f pulse shaper with a Spatial Light Modulator to impose a tailored cubic spectral phase on an ultrashort laser pulse, based on an analytical approximation for the required phase coefficient.

Model / system. The study focuses on the rotational dynamics of linear molecules, specifically CH3I for validating centrifugal distortion compensation and CO2 for observing $l$-doubling. The molecules are modeled as rigid rotors with a $J^4$ centrifugal distortion correction.

Key observables. Time-dependent molecular alignment (induced birefringence) and rotational revival structures.

Important parameters / regimes. Rotational constants (B, D), cubic spectral phase coefficients (b-tilde), and gas temperature (up to 403 K for CO2).

Assumptions / limitations. The model neglects the linear term in J (time shift) and the J-independent global phase, focusing primarily on quadratic and cubic spectral phase terms.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup for transient birefringence; Figure 2 demonstrates the compression of distorted CH3I alignment signals using shaped pulses; Figure 3 shows the resolution of $l$-doubling in CO2 through distortion mitigation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of centrifugal distortion, presents an analytical framework for pulse design, validates the method using CH3I, and demonstrates the observation of $l$-doubling in CO2.

Abstract

We demonstrate direct time-domain observation of l-doubling contributions in molecular rotational dynamics using shaped femtosecond laser pulses. By imposing a tailored spectral phase on the excitation pulse, we pre-compensate centrifugal distortion, which otherwise leads to temporally broadened, multi-cycle revival structures that obscure fine rotational features. A cubic spectral phase [Phys. Rev. A 107, 053108 (2023)] compresses selected revivals into near single-cycle events, in agreement with an analytic expression derived from molecular rotational constants, enabling predictive pulse design beyond numerical optimization. The resulting distortion-free revivals reveal temporally separated l-doubling contributions that remain unresolved in conventional impulsive alignment experiments. The method proves robust against experimental imperfections, including spatial light modulator discretization. While selective control of individual l-doubling components becomes feasible, here we focus on their direct observation in the time domain.

Abstract

The exceptional nonlinearity of alkali-metal vapors enables highly efficient nonlinear optical processes even at relatively low optical intensities. However, such processes have traditionally relied on centimeter-scale vapor cells. Here, we utilize a versatile chip-scale Rb vapor platform to generate coherent blue and mid-IR light in continuous-wave mode by means of resonant four-wave mixing. Optimized optical overlap with the atomic medium enables blue light generation of $\sim$20 $μ$W over a very short interaction length, while maintaining a directly measured linewidth of $\sim$1 MHz, which is presently limited by the measurement apparatus. Comparison with a conventional glassblown vapor cell further shows that the micromachined platform can achieve higher coherent blue-light generation efficiency despite its substantially shorter interaction length. Moreover, an anodically bonded Si window enables to detect coherent mid-IR emission with collected powers of $\sim$50 nW. We further characterize the temperature dependence and input-power scaling of the blue emission, confirming efficient nonlinear conversion within these compact vapor cells. This chip-scale platform provides a versatile foundation for a range of nonlinear optical functions, from precise wavelength references and quantum light sources to next-generation quantum sensors.

Summary. This paper presents a new method to estimate multipartite entanglement using only observable quantities like state purities and correlations. It avoids the exponential cost of full state tomography and provides a scalable way to certify entanglement in large-scale quantum systems. The approach is particularly useful for characterizing GHZ, W, and Dicke states in the presence of noise.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for experimentalists in AMO and quantum optics as it provides a way to certify complex multipartite entanglement using low-depth measurements (purity and correlations) without the need for full state tomography.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Quantifying multipartite entanglement in laboratory settings is extremely difficult because it typically requires full state tomography or computationally intractable calculations for mixed states.

Main result. The authors developed a framework of experimentally accessible upper and lower bounds for multipartine entanglement (including entanglement of formation and squashed entanglement) using only local and global state purities and correlation functions.

Method. The approach uses the entropy strong subadditivity inequality and the Koashi-Winter monogamy relation to derive bounds, and employs a dynamic programming strategy to efficiently compute these bounds for large systems.

Model / system. The framework applies to arbitrary-sized multipartite quantum systems, with specific analytical and numerical tests performed on GHZ, W, and Dicke states, as well as random pure states.

Key observables. Local and global state purities, correlation functions of local observables, and operator norms.

Important parameters / regimes. System dimension (d), number of particles (N), entanglement degree (k), and noise/purity parameters (p).

Assumptions / limitations. The bounds are most effective when the global state is nearly pure; the method assumes that higher-order purities are measurable, though higher-order expansions increase measurement complexity.

The paper introduces the problem of entanglement quantification, derives bipartite bounds using information-theoretic inequalities, extends these to multipartite entanglement using a recursive method, and validates the results through analytical tests on symmetric states and numerical simulations of random states.

Abstract

Multipartite entanglement is the premier resource for quantum technologies. Yet, its exact quantification in the laboratory is notoriously challenging, typically requiring the full knowledge of high dimensional quantum states. Here, we construct observable bounds to multipartite entanglement for systems of arbitrary size, which are functions of the local and global state purities, and correlation functions. First, we derive experimentally accessible upper and lower limits to both the bipartite entanglement of formation and the squashed entanglement of bipartite systems, by leveraging cornerstone results of quantum information theory: the entropy strong subadditivity inequality and the Koashi-Winter monogamy relation. Then, we convert them into bounds to the entanglement up to degree k for arbitrary states, and to the genuine k-partite entanglement, by employing a recently proposed method. Finally, we analytically and numerically test these results, by bounding the multipartite entanglement of several relevant states and mixtures, including the important classes of GHZ, Dicke, W states, and random pure states.
