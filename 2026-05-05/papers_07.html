Collinear ferromagnetism with reduced moment length in kagome magnet Nd3Ru4Al12

Authors: Yuki Ishihara, Ryota Nakano, Rinsuke Yamada, Takuya Nomoto, Priya R. Baral, Moritz M. Hirschmann, Kamini Gautam, Kamil K. Kolincio, Akiko Kikkawa, Seno Aji, Hiraku Saitoh, Masaaki Matsuda, Yasujiro Taguchi, Taka-hisa Arima, Yoshinori Tokura, Taro Nakajima, Max Hirschberger

Summary. This paper uses neutron diffraction to prove that the kagome magnet Nd3Ru4Al12 has a collinear hexagonal ferromagnetic ground state rather than a previously proposed orthorhombic one. This finding provides a microscopic basis for the large Hall and Nernst responses observed near the transition temperature. The result resolves a long-standing discrepancy in the magnetic modeling of this material.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Resolving the discrepancy regarding the magnetic ground state of the kagome lattice magnet Nd3Ru4Al12, specifically whether it follows a previously reported orthorhombic 'ortho-FM' state or a simpler hexagonal state.

Main result. The study identifies the ground state as a collinear hexagonal ferromagnetic (hex-FM) state with uniform moment length, effectively refuting the existence of the previously proposed orthorhombic state.

Method. Single-crystal neutron diffraction (unpolarized and polarized) was used to analyze magnetic structure factors and flipping ratios, supplemented by DFT calculations.

Model / system. The physical system is the kagome lattice magnet Nd3Ru4Al12, characterized by a staggered Nd sublattice and a hexagonal ferromagnetic (hex-FM) magnetic space group.

Key observables. Magnetic structure factors, magnetic intensity at (100) and (200) reflections, flipping ratio (P_obs), and ordered magnetic moment (mc).

Important parameters / regimes. Critical temperature Tc approx 41 K, magnetic moment mc approx 2.1-2.3 uB/Nd, and ordering vector Q = 0.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the validity of the nuclear structure refinement to isolate the magnetic signal.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Kagome lattice and fluctuation mechanism; Fig 2: Unpolarized neutron scattering intensities; Fig 3: Comparison of observed vs calculated structure factors; Fig 4: Comparison of different magnetic models (hex-FM, AOAO, ortho-FM); Fig 5: Polarized neutron scattering geometry and flipping ratio results.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific conflict, describes the experimental neutron diffraction setup, presents the structural refinement and symmetry analysis, compares competing magnetic models, and concludes with the implications for understanding thermal spin fluctuations.

Abstract

We determine the magnetic ground state of the kagome lattice magnet Nd3Ru4Al12 by single-crystal neutron diffraction, supported by experiments with polarized neutrons. We identify this material as a collinear ferromagnet ("hex-FM") with uniform moment length mc = 2.1 μB/Nd and ordering vector Q = 0, in contrast to a previous, seminal report that proposed unequal moment lengths on two Nd sites, here called the "ortho-FM" state. Our analysis of the flipping ratio in polarized neutron scattering is consistent with the hex-FM state. The results provide a microscopic basis for understanding the large fluctuation-induced Hall and Nernst responses near TC = 41 K, as previously reported for Nd3Ru4Al12.

Colloidal layer deposition with a controllable number of layers and compositional order

Summary. The paper presents a method to control the thickness and composition of colloidal layers by engineering the kinetics of DNA strand-displacement reactions. By using reaction kinetics to suppress unwanted equilibrium interactions, the researchers achieved a system that assembles into predictable, alternating layers. This provides a new way to program the morphology of complex soft-matter aggregates.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Controlling the morphology, thickness, and compositional order of DNA-mediated colloidal aggregates by overcoming kinetic limitations and undesired equilibrium interactions.

Main result. Demonstrated that engineering reaction kinetics via toehold-mediated strand displacement can act as a digital filter to suppress undesired interactions, enabling the assembly of multi-layered crystals with alternating particle types.

Method. A hybrid simulation approach combining Brownian dynamics for particle positions and the Gillespie algorithm for reaction-diffusion dynamics, validated against theoretical free-energy calculations.

Model / system. A binary suspension of self-protected colloids (Types A and B) and a functionalized surface, where DNA oligomers facilitate reversible formation of two-strand and three-strand complexes.

Key observables. Number of layers in the aggregate, compositional order, number of DNA complexes (loops, bridges), and effective particle-surface/particle-particle interaction potentials.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of ligands per particle (N_L), chemical potential (mu), reaction timescale (tau_0), and hybridization free energies (delta G).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglects non-specific ligand-ligand interactions, assumes ligands are thin rods, and assumes lateral diffusion of tethered linkers is much faster than the self-assembly timescale.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the binary system and DNA design; Figure 2 compares kinetic vs. equilibrium interaction potentials; Figure 3 shows steady-state layer configurations and scaling with N_L and density; Figure 4 tracks complex dynamics over time.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of kinetic limitations in DNA-assembly, presents a design for controlled layer deposition, derives the mathematical forces and free energy of the system, and uses reaction-diffusion simulations to validate the kinetic control mechanism.

Abstract

We design a system with a binary suspension of colloids and a surface that triggers the self-assembly of crystallites with a finite thickness. The proposed design allows controlling the number of layers forming the aggregate and constrains the two types of particles to lie on different planes. These functionalities are achieved by decorating the colloids and the surface with multiple DNA oligomers featuring specific interactions. The surface triggers a chain of reactions between DNA oligomers, leading to localized self-assembly. Equilibrium principles control the thickness of the aggregates. Instead, compositional order is achieved by engineering the reaction kinetics between DNA oligomers in a way that limits interactions between colloids of the same type. We validate our design using theory and reaction-diffusion simulation algorithms, which capture the multibody nature of the interactions. This work demonstrates how engineering the kinetics provides a new avenue for controlling the morphology of aggregates assembled by DNA.

Combinatorial Analysis of Dyadic and Quasi-Dyadic Codes

Authors: Anthony Gómez-Fonseca, Gretchen L. Matthews, Kirsten D. Morris, Tefjol Pllaha
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01942v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a mathematical toolkit for constructing and optimizing QLDPC codes by using the algebraic properties of dyadic matrices to minimize structural defects like short cycles. By controlling these cycles, the authors show that decoding performance can be substantially improved even when the girth cannot be increased.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Improving the performance of Quantum Low-Density Parity-Check (QLDPC) codes under iterative decoding by controlling short-cycle structures and harmful subgraphs like absorbing sets.

Main result. The authors developed an algebraic framework using dyadic and quasi-dyadic matrices to efficiently enumerate and control 4-, 6-, and 8-cycles, demonstrating that reducing cycle multiplicity can significantly improve decoding gains.

Method. The paper utilizes an algebraic framework of dyadic matrices (forming a commutative ring) and TBC (Tanner-Based Code) walk analysis to relate cycles in lifted Tanner graphs to walks in a protograph, alongside PEG-style construction algorithms.

Model / system. The study focuses on the CSS (Calderable-Shor-Steane) framework for quantum error correction, using dyadic and quasi-dyadic matrices as the basis for constructing parity-check matrices $H_X$ and $H_Z$ that satisfy the commutation constraint.

Key observables. Girth, short-cycle multiplicity (4-, 6-, and 8-cycles), permutation shift, and absorbing sets.

Important parameters / regimes. Lifting factor $N=2^\ell$, signature weight $\omega$, and the girth of the protograph.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the order of the permutation shift $m$ is 1 and that the girth of the lifted graph is bounded by the properties of the underlying protograph.

Figures summary. Figures compare random versus optimized dyadic matrices to show decoding gains from 4-cycle reduction, and illustrate performance comparisons between different construction strategies and girth levels.

Paper structure. The paper introduces an algebraic framework for dyadic matrices, develops methods for cycle enumeration and construction algorithms (PEG-style), analyzes absorbing sets, and concludes with performance simulations and 6/8-cycle counting theorems.

Abstract

Quantum low-density parity-check (QLDPC) codes offer a promising route to scalable fault-tolerant quantum computation, but their performance under iterative decoding is strongly influenced by short-cycle structure and other harmful subgraphs in the associated Tanner graphs. This paper develops an algebraic framework for constructing and analyzing (Q)LDPC codes from dyadic and quasi-dyadic matrices-translation-invariant $2^\ell \times 2^\ell$ binary matrices specified compactly by a signature row and forming a commutative ring with recursive block structure. Leveraging this structure, we relate cycles in lifted Tanner graphs to tailless backtrackless closed walks in the protograph and derive efficient, implementable methods to enumerate and control short cycles (notably $4$-, $6$-, and $8$-cycles). We introduce dyadic-aware PEG-style construction algorithms that use forbidden sets of shifts to maximize attainable girth when possible and otherwise minimize the multiplicity of the shortest cycles at the target girth. Motivated by error-floor phenomena, we further characterize and explicitly enumerate absorbing sets in key dyadic layout boundary cases, identifying configurations that induce abundant $(a,0)$-absorbing sets. Finally, we propose CSS-oriented dyadic constructions that satisfy commutation constraints by design and demonstrate via belief-propagation simulations that reducing short-cycle multiplicity can yield substantial decoding gains even when girth cannot be increased.

Comment on `On computing quantum waves exactly from classical action'

Authors: Gabor Vattay
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02621v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper is a formal rebuttal to a claim of an exact equivalence between classical and quantum dynamics. The author proves that the proposed method is actually a semiclassical approximation because it fails to account for the quantum potential term.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper critiques a claim that the Schrödinger equation can be solved exactly using only classical action and density, arguing that the proposed method is actually a semiclassical approximation.

Main result. The author demonstrates that the original derivation neglects the spatial derivatives of the probability density, which omits the essential quantum potential term.

Method. The author uses mathematical derivation via the Madelung hydrodynamic formulation, applying the Laplacian to the wave function ansatz and comparing the resulting real and imaginary components to the standard Schrödinger equation.

Model / system. The discussion focuses on the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a scalar potential V(x) within the Madelung hydrodynamic framework.

Key observables. Quantum potential (Q), probability density (rho), and classical action (phi).

Important parameters / regimes. The semiclassical (WKB) limit where the quantum potential is neglected.

Assumptions / limitations. The critique targets the assumption in the original paper that the spatial variation of the density amplitude is negligible.

Paper structure. The paper begins by identifying the error in the cited work, provides a mathematical derivation showing the omission of the quantum potential, and then analyzes specific examples (particle in a box, double-slit, etc.) to show how the error was masked.

Abstract

A recent article by Lohmiller \& Slotine (Proc.\ R.\ Soc.\ A \textbf{482}: 20250413) claims that the Schrödinger equation can be solved exactly using only classical least action and classical fluid density, asserting that this formulation avoids semiclassical approximations. We show that their mathematical derivation contains a foundational error. By neglecting the spatial derivatives of the probability density amplitude, the authors inadvertently omit the quantum potential -- the term originally identified by Madelung and later emphasised by Bohm. Consequently, their proposed equivalence is not exact but rather constitutes the standard semiclassical approximation. We further demonstrate that each of the paper's illustrative examples either belongs to a class where the quantum potential vanishes identically due to the geometry of the problem, or recovers the correct quantum result by importing quantum eigenfunctions through the initial conditions, thereby concealing the error.

Constraint Preserving XY-Mixers under Trotterized Adiabatic Evolution

Authors: Abhishek Awasthi, Maximilian Hess, Salome Lomadze, Francesco Bär, Christian Biefel
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02465v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the success of using constraint-preserving XY-mixers in quantum adiabatic evolution depends heavily on whether the constraints are global or local. It shows that while global constraints cause high Trotter errors that negate the benefits of XY-mixers, local constraints allow these mixers to significantly outperform standard methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how Trotterization errors in XY-mixers affect the performance of Trotterized Adiabatic Evolution (TAE) for combinatorial optimization problems with equality constraints.

Main result. The effectiveness of XY-mixers depends on constraint locality: global constraints lead to significant Trotter errors that favor standard X-mixers, whereas local/disjoint constraints allow XY-mixers to outperform X-mixers by several orders of magnitude.

Method. Theoretical analysis of Trotter error scaling combined with numerical statevector simulations using Qiskit on various optimization problems (Portfolio, Paint Shop, Multi-Commodility Flow).

Model / system. Quantum optimization frameworks using Trotterized Adiabatic Evolution (TAE) with XY-mixers (which preserve Hamming weight) and standard Pauli-X mixers, applied to Ising Hamiltonians encoding $k$-hot constraints.

Key observables. Probability of measuring/sampling the optimal solution.

Important parameters / regimes. Trotter step size ($\delta t$), Trotter depth ($p$), constraint locality (global vs. local), and Hamming weight ($k$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes linear, unweighted equality constraints and uses a first-order Suzuki-Trotter expansion for the implementation.

Figures summary. Figures compare success probabilities of XY vs. X mixers across different problem types (Portfolio, MCPS, MCFP) and varying Trotter step sizes and depths, highlighting the performance degradation in global vs. local constraint regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of constraint handling, provides a theoretical analysis of Trotter error scaling in XY-mixers, presents numerical validation using three distinct optimization problems, and concludes with a discussion on constraint locality as a design principle.

Abstract

Constraint handling is a central challenge for quantum algorithms applied to combinatorial optimization. Standard penalty-based approaches increase problem size, distort energy landscapes, and often degrade performance. Constraint-preserving mixers, such as XY-mixers, restrict quantum evolution to feasible subspaces, but their implementation on gate-based hardware requires Trotterization, which introduces approximation errors. In this work, we systematically investigate the interplay between constraint-preserving XY-mixers and Trotterized Adiabatic Evolution (TAE). We present a theoretical analyses of the origin and scaling of Trotter errors in XY-mixers and show that the dominant contribution depends on the size and structure of individual constraints rather than on the total problem size. Our findings are validated through extensive numerical simulations on three representative problems: Portfolio Optimization, the Multi-Car Paint Shop problem, and a Multi-Commodity Flow problem. For problems with a single global equality constraint spanning all variables, Trotter errors significantly impair XY-mixer performance, making standard Pauli-X mixers more robust under realistic implementations. In contrast, for problems whose constraints decompose into multiple disjoint local blocks, XY-mixers outperform X-mixers by several orders of magnitude even under Trotterized evolution. These results identify constraint locality as the key criterion for the effective use of XY-mixers and demonstrate that TAE combined with structure-aware mixer design provides a robust and theoretically grounded alternative to variational quantum optimization methods. We further present a dedicated mixer Hamiltonian for TSP-like 2-way-1-hot constraints.

Construction of Quantum Rank-Metric Codes Using Hermitian Orthogonality

Authors: Ryota Nizuka, Ryutaroh Matsumoto
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02571v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new way to build error-correcting codes for stacked quantum memory architectures. By using Hermitian orthogonality instead of the traditional CSS construction, the authors remove previous geometric restrictions and significantly improve the error-correcting efficiency of the system.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing quantum Gabidulin codes for stacked quantum memories are restricted to square-shaped layouts with an odd number of cells due to algebraic constraints in the CSS construction.

Main result. The authors propose a new construction method using Hermitian orthogonality that eliminates the odd-length restriction and approximately doubles the ratio of minimum rank distance to the number of physical qubits.

Method. The method combines the Hermitian self-orthogonality of classical Gabidulin codes with the Matsumoto-Uyematsu mapping to transform classical codes into symplectic self-orthogonal quantum stabilizer groups.

Model / system. The paper models a stacked quantum memory architecture consisting of multiple layers of qubits arranged in an m x n configuration, where errors are modeled as 'stacked errors' occurring during Clifford circuit execution.

Key observables. Code rate, minimum rank distance, and relative rank distance.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of physical qubits (N), number of logical qubits (K), minimum rank distance (d_R), and the extension degree of the finite field.

Assumptions / limitations. The error model focuses on Pauli errors in Clifford circuits, and the construction assumes the existence of a self-dual basis for the finite field extension.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a conceptual diagram of a stacked quantum memory with a specific layer and cell configuration.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of existing code limitations, defines the stacked memory error model, proposes a new construction framework using Hermitian orthogonality, provides mathematical proofs for rank invariance and error bounds, and concludes with a comparative analysis and concrete construction example.

Abstract

Stacked quantum memory is an architecture in which multiple layers of qubits are stacked. Quantum rank-metric codes are effective for error correction in stacked quantum memories. However, the previously proposed quantum Gabidulin codes based on the CSS construction had a problem: due to algebraic constraints, the applicable memory layouts were strictly limited to square shapes of odd length. In this paper, we first propose a framework for constructing quantum rank-metric codes from classical linear codes with symplectic self-orthogonality. Building upon this, we propose a new construction method for quantum Gabidulin codes by combining the Hermitian self-orthogonality of classical Gabidulin codes--utilizing the self-dual basis that exists when the extension degree of the finite field is even--with the quantum code construction method using Hermitian orthogonality by Matsumoto and Uyematsu. The proposed method succeeds in approximately doubling the ratio of the minimum rank distance to the number of physical qubits while maintaining the code rate. Furthermore, it eliminates the restriction of the conventional method that requires the number of cells and layers of the stacked memory to be odd, realizing the construction of quantum rank-metric codes applicable to memories with an even number of cells and layers. This construction improves the relative error correction capability of the stacked quantum memory architecture and increases the degree of freedom in design while preserving the code rate.

Diffusion Operator Geometry of Feedforward Representations

Authors: Kanishka Reddy
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01107v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper replaces unstable, discrete graph metrics with a smooth diffusion operator to analyze the geometry of neural network representations. It proves that this operator-based approach is robust to noise and provides a closed-form mathematical link between feature-level distributions and class-level transport.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The instability of discrete, graph-based geometric diagnostics (like k-NN graphs) in analyzing the evolution of neural network representations under small perturbations.

Main result. The development of a smooth diffusion operator framework that provides stable, Lipschitz-continuous geometric observables, and a theoretical 'Gaussian bridge' that predicts class-level transport based on Mahalanobis separations.

Method. The authors use Bakry-Emery Gamma-calculus applied to a Gaussian-kernel diffusion Markov operator and validate the framework through both a solvable Gaussian snapshot model and experiments on trained MNIST MLPs.

Model / system. The system consists of feature clouds (sets of vectors) produced by layers of a feedforward neural network, analyzed as snapshots of a diffusion process.

Key observables. Cross-class leakage, label-boundary energy, spectral gap, soft diffusion radius, and regularized Mahalanobis separation.

Important parameters / regimes. Diffusion bandwidth (epsilon), network width, and regularized class separation (c_epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. The theoretical bridge assumes a balanced Gaussian snapshot model with shared class covariance; experimental bandwidth selection assumes a universal scale across different network widths.

Figures summary. Figure 1 validates the Gaussian bridge theory; Figure 2 shows the evolution of class geometry during training; Figure 3 demonstrates how increasing width affects class separation; Figure 4 compares the stability of operator-based metrics against discontinuous k-NN metrics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of graph instability, proposes the diffusion operator framework, derives analytical results using a Gaussian model, provides stability proofs, and concludes with empirical validation on MNIST and width-scaling experiments.

Abstract

Neural networks transform data through learned representations whose geometry affects separation, contraction, and generalization. Recent work studies this geometry using discrete curvature on neighborhood graphs, suggesting Ricci-flow-like behavior across layers. We develop a smooth operator-theoretic alternative for feedforward representation snapshots. Each feature cloud induces a Gaussian-kernel diffusion Markov operator, and transport, spectral, label-boundary, and local-scale observables are derived from this single object via Bakry-Emery $Γ$-calculus. In a balanced Gaussian class-conditional snapshot model with shared covariance, the population operator has closed-form class affinities, leakage, and coarse spectra, all controlled by pairwise regularized Mahalanobis separations $c_\varepsilon^{(a,b)}$. We also prove that the resulting operator observables vary smoothly under feature perturbations, while hard neighborhood-graph diagnostics can change discontinuously. Synthetic experiments validate the closed-form Gaussian bridge, while learned MNIST experiments show that the same operator observables track training, width, and perturbation stability. Together, these results give a stable operator-geometric framework for analyzing feedforward representation geometry.

Distributed Quantum Circuit Optimisation: Evaluating Global and Local encodings

Authors: Maria Gragera Garces, Majid Haghparast
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02727v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper evaluates how different ways of applying circuit optimization affect the performance of distributed quantum computers. It finds that the best strategy depends on whether the primary bottleneck is the number of gates, the amount of inter-QPU communication, or the time required to compile the circuit.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how different quantum circuit optimization strategies (global, local, and hybrid) impact the trade-offs between computational costs and communication costs in distributed quantum computing architectures.

Main result. No single optimization strategy is universally superior; while global optimization minimizes computational resources and overhead, local optimization can reduce communication costs, and hybrid strategies can reduce both at the expense of significantly higher compilation time.

Method. The authors compared three compilation strategies (global, local, and hybrid) using the MQT Bench suite of 1,943 circuits, employing KaHyPar for balanced hypergraph partitioning and Qiskit's transpiler for optimization.

Model / system. A distributed quantum computing (DQC) architecture consisting of multiple interconnected Quantum Processing Units (QPUs) using a fully interconnected network model where cross-partition operations are implemented via teleportation-based telegates.

Key observables. Circuit depth, gate counts (1Q, 2Q, 3Q), number of induced non-local gates (communication cost), and classical compilation overhead/runtime.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit size (ranging from 2 to 130 qubits), optimization levels, and the balance between computational, communication, and preprocessing costs.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a fully interconnected network topology, ignores path length effects, assumes all non-local gates have equal cost, and assumes no additional qubit overhead for communication.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares computational resources (gate counts and depth) across strategies as circuit size increases; Figure 2 shows the advantage of global/hybrid encodings in reducing communication costs at larger scales; Table I provides compilation time statistics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of distributed circuit optimization, describes the network and optimization models, details the benchmarking methodology, presents comparative results for computational and communication costs, and concludes with a discussion on the trade-offs between the studied strategies.

Abstract

As distributed quantum architectures begin to emerge, understanding the interaction between quantum circuit optimisation and circuit partitioning becomes increasingly important. In this work, we study how circuit optimisation influences distributed quantum workloads under system-level trade-offs. We compare three compilation strategies (global optimisation, local optimisation, and a hybrid approach) across a large benchmark suite of quantum algorithms. Using telegate-based partitioning, we evaluate the resulting distributed circuits in terms of gate counts, circuit depth, the number of induced non-local gates, and compilation overhead, thereby approximating computational, communication, and classical preprocessing costs. Our results show that circuit optimisation does not uniformly benefit distributed execution. Global optimisation minimises computational resources and achieves the lowest compilation overhead. Local optimisation can reduce communication cost even though it is not explicitly communication-aware. The hybrid strategy can simultaneously reduce both computational and communication overhead, but at the expense of significantly increased compilation time.

Exact Microcanonical Formulation and Thermodynamics of Equispaced Finite-Level Systems

Authors: J. Ricardo de Sousa
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02736v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a new, exact mathematical framework for the thermodynamics of any system with equally spaced energy levels. It shows how the existence of negative temperatures is a direct consequence of a bounded energy spectrum and how this feature vanishes as the number of levels becomes infinite.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a unified, exact microcanonical thermodynamic formulation for systems with a finite number of equally spaced energy levels for arbitrary p, and understanding the crossover between bounded and unbounded spectra.

Main result. The derivation of analytical expressions for entropy per particle and inverse temperature that recover known cases (p=2, 3, and infinity) and demonstrate that finite-p systems exhibit a negative-temperature branch that disappears as p approaches infinity.

Method. A generating function approach combined with Cauchy's formula and saddle-point analysis in the thermodynamic limit.

Model / system. A system of N noninteracting particles with p equally spaced energy levels {0, epsilon, ..., (p-1)epsilon}, applicable to spin-S Zeeman multiplets.

Key observables. Entropy per particle s(u, p), inverse temperature beta(u, p), and reduced energy u.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of levels p, reduced energy u, energy spacing epsilon, and the limit p -> infinity.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation is specifically for the thermodynamic limit (N -> infinity) and assumes the microcanonical multiplicity can be represented via a generating function.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the dimensionless entropy per particle and dimensionless inverse temperature as functions of reduced energy u for various values of p, illustrating the shift in entropy maximum and the presence of negative temperature.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of finite-level systems, presents the generating function and saddle-point derivation, provides the analytical results for entropy and temperature, validates the results against known p=2 and p=3 cases, and discusses the p -> infinity limit.

Abstract

We present an exact microcanonical formulation, in the thermodynamic limit, for a system of $N$ noninteracting particles with $p$ equally spaced energy levels $\{0,ε,2ε,\ldots,(p-1)ε\}$. Writing the microcanonical multiplicity $Ω_p(E,N)$ as the coefficient of a generating function and evaluating the resulting representation by saddle-point analysis, we derive analytical expressions for the entropy per particle $s(u,p)$ and inverse temperature $β(u,p)$, with $u=E/(Nε)$ in the interval $[0,p-1]$. The formulation applies to arbitrary $p$ and recovers the known cases $p=2$, $p=3$, and $p\to\infty$. For finite $p$, the bounded spectrum implies an entropy maximum at $u_c=(p-1)/2$, where $β$ vanishes and changes sign. In the limit $p\to\infty$, the upper spectral bound is lost, the finite-energy entropy maximum disappears, and no negative-temperature branch remains. To our knowledge, this is the first general thermodynamic-limit microcanonical solution for arbitrary $p$. It therefore provides a unified framework for the thermodynamics of equispaced finite-level systems and their bounded-spectrum crossover with increasing $p$.

Exotic magnetism and persistent short-range spin correlations in a frustrated honeycomb lattice antiferromagnet

Authors: M. Barik, Q. Faure, F. Damay, J. P. Embs, S. Petit, P. Khuntia

Summary. This paper investigates the magnetic properties of the frustrated honeycomb magnet CaZn2Fe(PO4)3. It demonstrates that competing exchange interactions place the system near a tricritical point and that an applied magnetic field induces an exotic spin-canted state with 2D correlations. This work provides a new platform for studying tunable magnetic phases in high-spin systems.

Why it may be interesting. The paper explores many-body dynamics and phase stability near a tricritical point, specifically looking at how external magnetic fields can tune the system between different magnetic regimes and dimensionality.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating exotic magnetism, field-induced phase transitions, and the interplay of competing exchange interactions and anisotropy in a high-spin (S=5/2) frustrated honeycomb antiferromagnet.

Method. The researchers used a combination of neutron scattering (INS), thermodynamic measurements (specific heat, magnetization, susceptibility), and theoretical modeling via Linear Spin Wave Theory (LSWT) and spin-wave calculations.

Abstract

Two-dimensional high-spin bipartite honeycomb networks, where anisotropy, competing exchange interactions, and spin fluctuations interplay, provide an alternative platform to test theoretical models that distinguish between classical and quantum magnetism in the context of emergent many-body phenomena and exotic excitations. Here, we report the crystal structure, magnetization, specific heat, and inelastic neutron scattering measurements of the $S = 5/2$ distorted honeycomb magnet $\mathrm{CaZn_2Fe(PO_4)_3}$. Magnetization measurements reveal dominant antiferromagnetic interactions between the $\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}$ ($S = 5/2$) moments. The development and field evolution of a dip in the magnetic susceptibility under an external magnetic field indicate an unconventional field-induced transition, further supported by anomalies observed in magnetization isotherms. Zero-field specific heat measurements show an antiferromagnetic transition at $T_N \approx 1.67 \mathrm{K}$, which evolves under applied magnetic field, suggesting stabilization of a field-induced spin-canted state. Thermodynamic measurements reveal short-range spin correlations above the transition temperature. Assumptions / limitations. The inter-QPU network is assumed to be fully connected, and the study assumes that qubit interaction topology is the primary driver of partitioning performance.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows total cut costs across partitioners; Figure 2 shows performance variance heatmaps; Figure 3 shows normalized cut cost vs. number of QPUs; Figure 4 shows normalized cut cost; Figure 5 uses violin plots to illustrate the distribution of total cut costs.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the DQC partitioning problem, defines the three circuit ensembles (Real, Random, Generated), details the partitioning algorithms and evaluation metrics, presents comparative results regarding cost and ranking stability, and concludes with implications for compiler design and future work.

Abstract

Hypergraph partitioning is a central component of distributed quantum computing (DQC) compilers. However, due to the limited size of available quantum benchmark suites, many partitioning studies rely on random quantum circuits as evaluation workloads. In this work, we investigate whether such benchmarking practices provide a faithful assessment of partitioner performance. We evaluate a diverse set of state-of-the-art hypergraph partitioning strategies across three circuit origins: real algorithmic circuits, structured generated circuits, and fully random circuits. Our results show that random circuits significantly distort partitioning evaluation. They inflate cut costs, alter scaling trends across QPU counts and circuit sizes, and change the relative ranking of partitioning strategies. In contrast, structured generated circuits exhibit substantially lower distortion, more closely approximating real workload behaviour in cost, scaling, and strategy rankings. These findings demonstrate that benchmark selection directly influences methodological conclusions in DQC research and that random circuits may provide misleading guidance for compiler design.
