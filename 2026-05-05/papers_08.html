Summary. This paper uses reinforcement learning to optimize the hardware parameters of a CV-QKD system. By jointly tuning filters and signal intensity, the researchers achieved much higher key rates and longer transmission distances despite realistic hardware limitations like low-resolution converters.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing the performance of Continuous-Variable Quantum Key Distribution (CV-QKD) under practical hardware constraints such as finite filter lengths, limited DAC/ADC resolution, and inter-symbol interference.

Main result. The joint optimization of transmitter/receiver filters and mean photon number significantly improves the Secure Key Rate (SKR) and extends the maximum transmission distance from 60 km to nearly 100 km.

Method. An end-to-end reinforcement learning approach using the REINCOFFE policy gradient algorithm to update filter weights and photon number without requiring gradient computation.

Model / system. A CV-QKD system over optical fiber networks using Gaussian-modulated symbols, coherent detection, and standard telecommunication components including DAC/ADC and FIR filters.

Key observables. Secure Key Rate (SKR)

Important parameters / regimes. Filter lengths (L_TX, L_RX), DAC/ADC bit resolution (b), transmission distance (L), channel excess noise (n_ch), and mean photon number (n*)

Assumptions / limitations. Quantization errors from DAC and ADC are modeled as equivalent Gaussian additive noise terms.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the system architecture; Fig 2 shows SKR vs resolution and filter amplitude response; Fig 3 shows the gap to maximum SKR relative to hardware resolution and filter length; Fig 4 shows SKR vs distance for different noise levels.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the hardware constraints in CV-QKD, describes the reinforcement learning optimization framework, presents results regarding performance gains and distance extension, and analyzes the efficiency of hardware requirements.

Abstract

Using reinforcement learning, we optimize for practical hardware constraints, including limited FIR filter taps at the transmitter and receiver, mean photon number and finite DAC/ADC resolution. Under these realistic conditions, the proposed approach achieves significant performance improvements.

Perfect state transfer in Grover walks on dihedral Cayley graphs

Authors: Koushik Bhakta, Bikash Bhattacharjya, Xiwang Cao
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02254v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a complete mathematical classification of when perfect state transfer can occur in Grover walks on dihedral Cayley graphs. By moving beyond abelian groups to the non-abelian dihedral group, it identifies specific graph structures that allow for high-fidelity state transport, which is a fundamental requirement for quantum information processing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to provide a complete characterization of the conditions required for Perfect State Transfer (PST) in Grover walks on Cayley graphs over the non-abelian dihedral group D_n.

Main result. The authors establish necessary and sufficient conditions for PST in both normal and non-normal Cayley graphs, proving that PST cannot occur in normal Cayley graphs when n is odd and constructing infinite families of graphs that do exhibit PST.

Method. The study employs the representation theory of the dihedral group, spectral analysis of the transition matrix, and Fourier analysis on finite groups, utilizing Chebyshev polynomials to evaluate the dynamics.

Model / system. The model is a discrete-time Grover walk on Cayley graphs of the dihedral group D_n, where the evolution is governed by a transition matrix derived from the graph's adjacency structure.

Key observables. Perfect State Transfer (PST) between specific vertices and the periodicity of the quantum walk.

Important parameters / regimes. The parity of n, the normality of the connection set S (whether it is a union of conjugacy classes), and the degree of the graph.

Assumptions / limitations. The graphs are assumed to be finite, simple, and symmetric (S = S^-1), and the study is specifically focused on the Grover walk model.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a classification flowchart or matrix summarizing the existence of PST based on the normality of the graph and the parity of n.

Paper structure. The paper begins with an introduction to Grover walks and Cayley graphs, followed by the application of representation theory to compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors, then proceeds to a case-by-case analysis of PST conditions for even and odd n, and concludes with specific examples and open questions.

Abstract

The paper investigates perfect state transfer (PST) in Grover walks on Cayley graphs over the dihedral group $D_n$. The Grover walk is a discrete-time quantum walk widely studied in quantum information processing. A Cayley graph $\operatorname{Cay}(Γ,S)$ is called normal if $S$ is the union of some conjugacy classes of the group $Γ$; otherwise, it is called non-normal. Most existing studies have been restricted to Cayley graphs over abelian groups. In contrast, we investigate both normal and non-normal cases for Cayley graphs over the non-abelian group $D_n$. By examining the parity of $n$ and the normality of the Cayley graph, we obtain a complete characterization of PST on $\operatorname{Cay}(D_n,S)$. In particular, we establish necessary and sufficient conditions for the occurrence of PST in all possible cases, and prove that PST does not occur for normal Cayley graphs when $n$ is odd. Furthermore, we construct several infinite families of normal and non-normal Cayley graphs $\operatorname{Cay}(D_n,S)$ that exhibit PST, illustrating the application of the main result. Our approach is based on the representation theory of the dihedral group.

Rethinking How to Act: Action-Space Engineering for Reinforcement Learning-Based Circuit Routing in Distributed Quantum Systems

Summary. This paper presents an improved reinforcement learning framework for compiling quantum circuits in distributed architectures. By re-engineering the RL action space to use routing chains rather than single edges, the authors significantly speed up both the training process and the resulting circuit execution time. This is a crucial step toward managing the complexity of scaling quantum computers via modular interconnects.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing quantum circuit routing and compilation in distributed quantum computing (DQC) architectures to minimize total execution time.

Main result. The proposed RL agent using action-space engineering achieves up to a 35% reduction in modeled execution time and a 64% reduction in training wall-clock time compared to existing baselines.

Method. A Reinforcement Learning approach using Deep Q-Networks (DQN) with a novel action-space formulation (routing via precomputed shortest paths), action-masking strategies, and a low-dimensional Q-value approximation.

Model / system. A distributed quantum computing architecture consisting of multiple interconnected modules (QPUs) with local intra-module connectivity and remote inter-module links for EPR pair generation and tele-operations.

Key observables. Total circuit execution time (measured in timesteps), cumulative reward, and training wall-clock time.

Important parameters / regimes. Entanglement generation probability (p_gen = 0.95), gate execution latencies (SWAP vs. remote operations), and circuit size (number of CNOT gates).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes classical channels are available for heralding, neglects single-qubit gate routing, and approximates stochastic entanglement generation as a deterministic process with fixed latency.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows remote entanglement generation; Figure 2 depicts a circuit DAG; Figure 3 visualizes qubit mapping and remote actions (tele-qubit, tele-gate); Figure 4 shows state vector encoding; Figures 5-6 show hardware topologies; Figures 11-12 show execution time distributions via boxplots.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the DQC challenge, describes the hardware and circuit models, details the RL agent's novel action-space and masking innovations, presents numerical comparisons against a baseline, and discusses scalability and training dynamics.

Abstract

As it becomes increasingly difficult to monolithically scale a quantum processor, distributed quantum computing (DQC) offers an alternative by distributing qubits across multiple smaller interconnected quantum processor modules. In such an architecture, the challenge of quantum circuit compilation shifts from placing and routing qubits within one module to placing, routing and using the qubits efficiently across modules. In order to optimize circuit execution time, the right state-dependent networking decisions must be found, such as when and where to generate shared remote quantum states to support remote operations. Reinforcement learning (RL) provides a natural framework for this problem, generating a compilation policy that can generalize across different circuits. Building on the framework of Promponas et al. (2024), we introduce an agent that combines a novel action-space formulation with effective action-masking strategies. A comprehensive numerical comparison of the two approaches under different coupling constraints shows that our agent achieves improved training and inference performance with a relative reduction in the modeled execution time of up to 35\%.

Abstract

Quantum conference key agreement (QCKA) protocols utilize GHZ states to establish shared group keys between multiple parties. While previous work has shown that standard Classical Advantage Distillation (CAD) protocols can sometimes benefit QCKA performance, it was unknown if past results were asymptotically tight. In this work, we design a new CAD protocol, "Selective Classical Advantage Distillation (S-CAD)", for QCKA, which generalizes prior QCKA+CAD work and allows the parties to selectively enable or disable CAD. Finally, we evaluate in a variety of simulated star network topologies, showing when S-CAD can help, and when it is best to disable CAD entirely.

Semantics-Based Verification of an Implemented Shor Oracle for ECDLP in Qrisp

Authors: Lei Zhang, Zhiyuan Chen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01008v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper proposes a method for verifying that quantum software implementations of ECDLP oracles correctly match their mathematical specifications. It demonstrates that even when individual components work correctly, the compilation process in frameworks like Qrisp can introduce errors in controlled operations. This highlights the need for semantic auditing in the development of trustworthy quantum algorithms.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The discrepancy between abstract mathematical oracles for the Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP) and their actual implementation in quantum programming frameworks like Qrisp, which can lead to invalid algorithmic conclusions.

Main result. The paper establishes a semantics-first verification framework and identifies a specific case where a compiled controlled-gate implementation fails to satisfy the expected control law, despite passing standalone tests.

Method. The authors use a refinement-style verification approach, defining correctness obligations across multiple layers of implementation and using a well-formedness predicate to handle exceptional inputs.

Model / system. The study uses the Qrisp quantum programming framework to implement a Shor-style oracle for ECDLP over a prime field Fp, specifically utilizing Short Weierstrass curves.

Key observables. Circuit complexity metrics including qubit count, circuit depth, T-count, and CX-count, as well as the verification of the control law and point-update accuracy.

Important parameters / regimes. The field size parameter m = ceil(log2 p) and the specific small-scale instance parameters p=5, a=3, b=3.

Assumptions / limitations. The complexity analysis assumes that underlying modular arithmetic and inversion primitives compile to uniform polynomial-size reversible circuits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the composition of the oracle, showing how the accumulator register is updated via sequential bitwise-controlled updates.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the semantic gap problem, proposes a refinement-based verification framework, provides a complexity argument for the oracle family, and concludes with a case study demonstrating a control-law violation.

Abstract

Shor-style quantum algorithms for the elliptic-curve discrete logarithm problem (ECDLP) are highly sensitive to the exact semantics of their group-operation oracles. Consequently, minor implementation choices can invalidate the intended mathematical model and lead to misleading conclusions. This paper introduces a semantics-first verification perspective for an end-to-end, compilable ECDLP implementation built on Qrisp. We specify the implemented oracle at the level of program semantics, derive refinement-style verification obligations for its key components, and provide a high-level complexity argument for the resulting oracle family. A small case study highlights that (i) the core point-update primitive agrees with a classical reference on well-formed inputs, yet (ii) controlled execution may violate the expected control law under the evaluated toolchain, despite a passing trivial control sanity check. These results position semantic auditing as a practical prerequisite for trustworthy ECDLP-oriented quantum software.

Sequential Learning and Catastrophic Forgetting in Differentiable Resistor Networks

Authors: Maniru Ibrahim
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01383v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper studies how learning new tasks causes the erasure of previously learned behaviors in electrical resistor networks. It reveals that forgetting is a physical reconfiguration of the network's primary current pathways and demonstrates a fundamental trade-off between retaining old knowledge and adapting to new inputs.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the mechanisms of catastrophic forgetting and the stability-plasticity trade-off in differentiable physical networks during sequential learning tasks.

Main result. Forgetting is identified as a localized reconfiguration of high-current transport pathways and is strongly driven by task conflict; regularization strategies like anchoring can reduce forgetting but at the cost of reduced adaptation to new tasks.

Method. Gradient-based optimization of edge conductances using automatic differentiation, combined with regularization techniques such as uniform and gradient-weighted anchoring.

Model / system. Differentiable resistor networks governed by Kirchhoff's laws, where edge conductances are trainable parameters in various graph topologies (Erdos-Renyi, small-world, scale-free, and random-geometric).

Key observables. Forgetting (loss increase on Task A), Task B loss (adaptation), gradient cosine similarity, task target contrast, and conductance update magnitudes.

Important parameters / regimes. Regularization strength (lambda), training budget (number of gradient steps), network size (N), and graph topology.

Assumptions / limitations. The network is assumed to reach physical equilibrium; conductances are constrained to be positive via softplus transformation; the study uses a minimal benchmark setup.

Figures summary. Figures visualize the forgetting-adaptation trade-off, the impact of shortest-path distance, and the localization of conductance updates on high-current edges.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the differentiable resistor network framework, defines the sequential learning protocol and regularization methods, presents results on task conflict and physical mechanisms of forgetting, analyzes the impact of network topology, and concludes with implications for tunable matter.

Abstract

Differentiable physical networks provide a simple setting in which learning can be studied through the interaction between trainable parameters and physical equilibrium constraints. We investigate sequential learning in differentiable resistor networks governed by Kirchhoff's laws. Although individual input--output mappings can be learned by gradient-based adjustment of edge conductances, sequential training on conflicting tasks produces catastrophic forgetting. We show that forgetting is controlled by task conflict and by the degree of adaptation to the new task. Uniform anchoring and normalised gradient-weighted anchoring reduce forgetting only by increasing the final loss on the new task, giving a clear forgetting--adaptation trade-off. We also show that forgetting is associated with localised conductance changes on high-current edges, giving a physical interpretation as reconfiguration of dominant transport pathways. Broader random-task ensembles show that the strongest forgetting occurs when the second task reverses the output ordering imposed by the first task. Finally, comparisons across Erdős--Rényi, small-world, scale-free, and random-geometric graph ensembles show that topology changes the forgetting--adaptation balance. These results position differentiable resistor networks as compact, physically interpretable testbeds for studying continual learning in tunable matter.

Topological Ising superconductivity in two-dimensional p-wave magnet

Authors: Kyoung-Min Kim, Gibaik Sim, Moon Jip Park
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01686v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that 2D p-wave magnets can host topological superconductivity through an Ising-type s+p wave mixing mechanism. By tuning the ratio of interactions or applying an external magnetic field, one can realize robust Majorana edge modes without needing strong spin-orbit coupling.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to find a platform for topological superconductivity that does not rely on weak, relativistic spin-orbit coupling, specifically investigating the emergence of Majorana modes in 2D p-wave magnets.

Main result. The researchers find that an odd-parity exchange field allows for a coupled s+p Ising superconducting state, which can transition into a nodal topological phase with Majorana edge modes when the triplet component dominates.

Method. The study uses a Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) formulation, self-consistent linearized gap equations, and topological invariant calculations (winding numbers and Pfaffian invariants).

Model / system. A two-dimensional p-wave magnet on a square lattice described by an extended Hubbard model with on-site and nearest-neighbor attractive interactions and an odd-parity exchange field.

Key observables. Pairing amplitudes (singlet and triplet), superconducting phase diagrams, momentum-resolved winding numbers, and Majorana edge mode spectral density.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange field strength (J), on-site attraction (U), nearest-neighbor attraction (V), chemical potential (mu), and transverse Zeeman field (hx).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a square lattice with an effective two-band tight-binding structure derived from a larger four-band model via Schrieffer-Wolff transformation.

Figures summary. Figure 1a shows the pairing mechanism and d-vector orientation; Figure 1b illustrates the real-space lattice model; Figure 1c presents superconducting phase diagrams.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical problem of spin-orbit coupling-independent TSC, defines the p-wave magnetic model and Hamiltonian, derives the gap equations and symmetry classification, analyzes the topological phases and Majorana modes, and discusses the effects of external Zeeman fields.

Abstract

Fermi-surface spin splitting generated by non-relativistic exchange fields provides a new route to topological superconductivity without relying on strong spin-orbit coupling. Here, we study superconducting instabilities of a square-lattice $p$-wave magnet with onsite and nearest-neighbour attractive interactions. The odd-parity exchange field removes inversion symmetry in the spin-split electronic structure, allowing singlet and triplet order parameters to mix within a single $A_1$ symmetry channel. The leading instability is a coupled $s+p_x$ Ising state, whose singlet-triplet balance is continuously tunable by the relative strength of the nearest-neighbour attraction. When the triplet gap amplitude exceeds the singlet one, this Ising state undergoes a transition into a nodal topological superconducting phase with Majorana edge modes protected by momentum-resolved winding numbers. These modes extend over finite momentum intervals bounded by the surface projections of bulk point nodes. We further show that a Zeeman field perpendicular to the exchange field can induce a $Z_2$ topological superconducting phase, even in the regime where a single gap dominates.

When Independent Gaussian Models Break Down: Characterizing Regime-Dependent Modeling Failures in $φ^4$ Theory

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the failure of Gaussian models in interacting field theories is primarily due to complex dependencies between modes rather than the non-Gaussian nature of individual modes. By identifying specific regimes of coupling, it provides a diagnostic tool to determine when more expressive, nonlinear models are necessary.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates why Gaussian and independent Fourier-mode models fail to capture the complexity of self-interacting physical systems, specifically distinguishing between failures due to marginal non-Gaussianity versus structured inter-mode dependencies.

Main result. The authors find a separation between marginal simplicity and joint complexity: while individual Fourier modes become approximately Gaussian as system size increases, the inter-mode coupling grows, driving the failure of Gaussian models.

Method. The researchers used Metropolis-Hastings MCMC to sample the 1D phi-4 theory and employed various diagnostics including a 4th-order coupling metric, spectral error, and KL divergence to compare Gaussian baselines against the true distribution.

Model / system. A one-dimensional phi-4 theory on a lattice with periodic boundary conditions, defined by a Hamiltonian containing kinetic, mass, and quartic interaction terms.

Key observables. Two-point correlation function, power spectrum, normalized coupling metric (C), spectral error (epsilon), excess kurtosis, and Kullback-Leibler divergence.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (lambda), system size (N), and bare mass (m^2).

Assumptions / limitations. The study is restricted to 1D lattice systems with periodic boundary conditions and focuses on 4th-order statistics, potentially overlooking higher-order cumulants.

Figures summary. The notes suggest figures demonstrating the scaling of Fourier mode Gaussianity (decreasing kurtosis) alongside the simultaneous growth of the coupling metric (C) and spectral error.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of modeling failures, describes the 1D phi-4 system and MCMC methodology, presents the diagnostic metrics, details the discovery of the separation between marginal and joint complexity, identifies three distinct coupling regimes, and concludes with implications for future nonlinear model design.

Abstract

In practical physical systems, modeling assumptions of Gaussianity and basis independence break down due to self-interactions. We study a specific instance of one-dimensional $φ^4$ theory on a lattice, analyzing how the interaction strength and system size jointly affect the marginal and joint distributions of frequency-based representation of the field (i.e., Fourier modes). We find that models relying on Gaussian and independent Fourier modes fail primarily from structured dependencies rather than marginal non-Gaussianity, since individual modes become approximately Gaussian despite mode coupling growing with size. Based on this, we identify three distinct regimes that delineate where traditional methods remain effective and where more expressive models are needed. Our results provide a computationally simple diagnostic to establish when Gaussian models are insufficient, and establish a concrete design criterion that future nonlinear models must satisfy.

Coherent exciton spin dynamics and three-dimensional quantum state tomography in a single InAlAs quantum dot

Authors: J. Njala, Y. Yamamoto, R. Kaji, S. Adachi, H. Sasakura
Type: experiment · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01801v1

Summary. The study uses advanced tomography to map the 3D spin precession of an exciton in an InAlAs quantum dot. It reveals that spin coherence persists longer than the exciton lifetime itself, providing a robust platform for spin initialization and control in semiconductor nanostructures.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum optics as it demonstrates high-fidelity 3D reconstruction of a single-qubit-like state (exciton spin) and provides a quantitative benchmark for decoupling intrinsic dephasing from recombination-induced decay.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving a complete, three-dimensional understanding of the spin trajectory of a neutral exciton in a single quantum dot and disentangling anisotropic exchange interaction from other relaxation processes.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated 3D quantum state tomography of exciton spin, revealing a long-lived spin coherence (1.1 ns) that exceeds the exciton lifetime and a spin trajectory that follows a spiral motion on the Bloch sphere.

Method. Time-resolved quantum state tomography using micro-photoluminescence in three orthogonal polarization bases (circular, diagonal, and rectilinear) combined with a global fitting procedure.

Model / system. A single InAlAs/AlGaAs quantum dot under two-LO-phonon quasi-resonant excitation, modeled by a Hamiltonian dominated by the anisotropic exchange interaction (AEI) and a Lindblad master equation.

Key observables. Fine-structure splitting (FSS), exciton lifetime, spin coherence time, initial degree of circular polarization, and the time-resolved spin vector magnitude.

Important parameters / regimes. Fine-structure splitting of ~19.6 ueV, exciton lifetime of ~767 ps, spin coherence time of 1.1 ns, and an instrument response function of ~30 ps.

Assumptions / limitations. The spin evolution is dominated by the anisotropic exchange interaction with negligible influence from the local nuclear environment (no Overhauser shift).

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the micro-PL spectrum and FSS determination; Fig 2 displays differential polarization signals showing quantum beats; Fig 3 illustrates the 3D spiral spin trajectory and the decay of the spin vector magnitude.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the experimental setup and spectral characterization, presents the time-resolved polarization measurements in three bases, details the 3D tomographic reconstruction and Hamiltonian fitting, and concludes with the analysis of spin relaxation and coherence.

Abstract

We investigate the coherent exciton spin dynamics in a single InAlAs/AlGaAs quantum dot using time-resolved quantum state tomography. Under two-LO-phonon quasi-resonant excitation of neutral exciton, we observe pronounced quantum beats in the circular and diagonal polarization components, reflecting the fine-structure splitting ($Δ\approx 19.6 \ μ\text{eV}$). By employing a global fitting procedure across three orthogonal polarization bases, we demonstrate that the spin evolution is consistently described by a unified Hamiltonian dominated by the anisotropic exchange interaction. While the initial degree of circular polarization is limited to $\approx 0.28$ due to fast relaxation processes during carrier cooling, the subsequent dynamics reveal a long-lived spin coherence ($1.1 \pm 0.2$ ns) that exceeds the exciton lifetime ($\sim 767$ ps). Our analysis reveals that the spin-formation time is significantly shorter than the instrument response function, and the absence of a discernible Overhauser shift confirms a negligible influence from the local nuclear environment under the present conditions. These results provide a quantitative benchmark for the three-dimensional reconstruction of spin trajectories using differential polarization signals, demonstrating the feasibility of using quasi-resonant excitation for stable spin initialization in semiconductor nanostructures.

Summary. This paper simplifies the complex problem of calculating far-field thermal radiation by replacing an infinite volume of 'environmental dust' with a simple, local photonic bath at infinity. The authors derive analytical expressions for this bath in both 3D and 2D geometries, demonstrating that it correctly recovers black-body radiation properties. This new formalism provides a much more efficient way to perform both analytical and numerical calculations in radiative heat transfer.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful new computational framework for calculating far-field thermal radiation and Casimir forces, which is highly relevant for researchers working on open quantum systems and quantum optics in the presence of thermal environments.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of modeling far-field thermal radiation due to the computational complexity of integrating electromagnetic wave propagation over infinite space using the 'environmental dust' model.

Main result. The authors successfully map the distributed environmental dust to a simplified 'photonic bath at infinity' represented by a local self-energy, proving it behaves as a black body and providing analytical expressions for 3D and 2D systems.

Method. The study utilizes the nonequilibrium Green's function (NEGF) formalism, specifically the Keldysh Green's function, and employs a surface Green's function framework for verification.

Model / system. A material object (either a 3D body or a 2D planar system) at temperature T1 surrounded by an environment at temperature T0, modeled as a medium with infinitesimal absorption (dust).

Key observables. Radiative energy and momentum transfer, Casimir force, far-field heat, momentum, and angular-momentum radiation, and energy density.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperatures T1 and T0, frequency omega, infinitesimal absorption parameter eta, and the vacuum permittivity and permeability.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes infinitesimal absorption (eta to 0+), time-translational invariance, and a linear-response regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic comparison between the traditional distributed dust model and the proposed effective photonic bath at infinity model for both 3D and 2D systems; Figure 2 shows a discretized lattice model used for the surface Green's function derivation.

Paper structure. Abstract

In far-field thermal radiation, electromagnetic waves emitted by an object propagate to infinity, requiring the far region to be modeled as an effective thermal bath. This bath was proposed as infinitesimal environmental "dust", but explicit calculations with such distributed dust involve integrals over infinite space that are difficult to evaluate. In this work, we map this environmental dust to a photonic bath at infinity within the nonequilibrium photonic Green's function formalism. By explicitly evaluating the spatial integral over the dust, we show that its contribution reduces to a simple local self-energy, for which we derive analytical expressions for both three-dimensional objects and planar systems. We further demonstrate that the bath behaves as a black body and clarify its role in far-field thermal radiation. An alternative derivation based on the surface Green's function framework is also provided in Appendix B, demonstrating the theoretical consistency of the results without invoking the dust model. The photonic bath at infinity provides a convenient framework for both analytical and numerical calculations in far-field thermal radiation.

Summary. This paper develops a theoretical framework to study how electrical bias affects the van der Waals-like forces between nanostructures. It reveals that applied voltage can significantly strengthen attractive forces or even turn them repulsive through population inversion. This offers a mechanism for active, voltage-controlled control of nanoscale forces and self-assembly.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental generalization of the London picture to nonequilibrium settings, showing how breaking detailed balance via voltage can fundamentally change the nature of long-range forces.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a theory for dispersion (van der Waals) interactions between two nanostructures driven into a nonequilibrium steady state by an applied bias voltage, and determining how voltage can tune the magnitude and sign of these forces.

Abstract

Electrochemical metallization (ECM) memristors have potential applications in future neuromorphic computing hardware. The set, reset, and variable-resistance features of these devices originate in the formation and breakup of metal filaments in a solid-state electrolyte. While the performance characteristics of these devices are widely investigated, the driving principles behind the morphology of the filament formation process remain unclear. In this study, we propose an approach motivated by the extremal principles found in non-equilibrium thermodynamics and observe an entropy production and energy dissipation rate minimization during the filament-forming process in kinetic Monte Carlo simulations.
