Opportunities and challenges in scaling quantum error detection on hardware

Authors: Yanis Le Fur, Ethan Egger, Hong-Ye Hu, Vincent Russo, William J. Zeng, Ryan LaRose
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02861v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper evaluates how effectively quantum error detection can mitigate noise on real IBM quantum hardware. It shows that while larger codes can exponentially improve accuracy, the practical benefits are limited by a pseudothreshold where hardware overheads and circuit depth begin to outweigh error suppression.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the scalability and performance of quantum error detection (QED) on real hardware, specifically addressing the trade-offs between error suppression and the overheads of sampling and classical processing.

Main result. The study demonstrates that QED can exponentially improve expectation value accuracy as code distance increases, though it identifies a pseudothreshold where increased circuit depth and SWAP gate overhead can eventually degrade performance.

Method. The authors perform a detailed benchmarking study using both real IBM quantum computers and noisy simulations, employing repetition and triangular color codes for memory and logical Bell state experiments.

Model / system. The experiments were conducted on IBM quantum hardware (including IBM Kyiv, Fez, and Boston) using the heavy-hex lattice topology and a global depolarizing noise model.

Key observables. Error-mitigated expectation values (e.g., and ), logical Bell state fidelity, and the fraction of measured codewords (post-selection rate).

Important parameters / regimes. Code distance (d), number of physical qubits (n), circuit depth (D), physical noise rate (p), and classical processing time.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that all occurring errors are correctable and that the operator commutes with the code projector; assumes a depolarizing noise model for certain derivations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the exponential scaling of classical processing time, the convergence of expectation values with increasing code distance, the crossover effect where larger codes perform worse at high depths, and the estimation of the pseudothreshold decay.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the benefits and scaling bottlenecks of QED, moves to experimental benchmarks on repetition and color codes, analyzes the impact of hardware topology and circuit depth, and concludes with a mapping of the error detection frontier (pseudothresholds).

Abstract

Quantum error detection can produce unbiased expectation values that exponentially converge to noiseless results as the code distance is increased. Despite this, its performance as an error mitigation technique is relatively understudied on quantum hardware because of its two main drawbacks: (i) the number of samples increases exponentially in the circuit depth/noise level, and (ii) the classical processing generally grows exponentially in the code distance, though exceptions exist. Additionally, the constant (but often large) overhead of embedding the code and logical operations on hardware can make accuracy worse instead of better. In this work, we seek to provide a clear picture of these opportunities and challenges for scaling quantum error detection on hardware. We do so by performing a detailed benchmarking study on real and simulated noisy quantum computers, using the repetition code and triangular color code for memory experiments and logical computations with up to $74$ physical qubits. In addition to these benchmarks, we estimate the pseudothreshold of codes to map the frontier of error detection on current and future quantum computers. Despite the challenges, our results show strong promise for scaling quantum error detection on hardware.

A Quantum Approach to Stochastic Optimization in Insurance Underwriting

Summary. This paper presents a novel quantum-classical hybrid algorithm designed to solve stochastic optimization problems used in insurance underwriting. By combining a specialized QAOA-based circuit with a robust classical post-processing repair scheme, the authors demonstrate that quantum hardware can outperform traditional classical heuristics on large-scale, high-constraint problems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Solving the Chance-Constrained Knapsack Problem (CCKP), a stochastic combinatorial optimization problem where item weights are random variables and constraints must be met within a specific risk tolerance.

Main result. The proposed quantum-classical hybrid scheme achieves solution quality comparable to classical heuristics for small instances and outperforms them for larger, more complex instances (e.g., n=75, M=5).

Method. A hybrid framework using a QAOA-based variational circuit (LD-DA) on IBM Heron processors, combined with a parameter transfer strategy to mitigate barren plateaus and a self-consistent classical recovery scheme for post-processing noisy samples.

Model / system. A variational quantum circuit using a phase separator Hamiltonian (encoding values and mean weights) and a mixer Hamiltonian (using X and XX rotations) acting on a specific Hamming-weight sector of qubits.

Key observables. Measurement probability P(T) of target subsets, solution quality metric (closeness kappa to Gurobi), and constraint residual.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of items (n), number of constraints (M), weight variance (sigma^2), risk tolerance (epsilon), and circuit depth (p).

Assumptions / limitations. The weight distributions are assumed to be closed under convolution (specifically focusing on Normal distributions) to allow for deterministic reformulation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the QAOA circuit architecture; Figure 2 compares Gurobi runtime and solution quality against heuristics; Figure 3 shows the CDF of loss values and the impact of the recovery algorithm; Figure 4 illustrates the evolution of profit distributions during fine-tuning.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the CCKP problem, proposes a deterministic reformulation via SOCP, details the quantum-classical hybrid architecture (ansatz, mixer, and parameter transfer), describes the classical post-processing recovery algorithm, presents experimental results on IBM hardware and MPS simulations, and compares performance against classical benchmarks.

Abstract

The presence of stochastic elements in combinatorial optimization problems makes them particularly challenging, as such problems quickly become intractable for classical computers even at relatively small sizes. In this work, we propose a novel quantum-classical hybrid scheme for solving a class of stochastic optimization problems known as chance-constrained knapsack problems, in which item weights follow probability distributions and constraints may be violated within a specified risk tolerance. Our method employs knapsack-specific QAOA-based circuits to generate samples which, when combined with a self-consistent classical recovery scheme introduced in this work, produce high-quality solutions. Experiments carried out on IBM Heron processors, using circuits with depths up to 177 and comprising 3443 gates acting on as many as 150 qubits, yield solutions that indicate improvement over classical optimization schemes. The proposed quantum-classical scheme paves the way to tackling such problems, with the potential to outperform approaches that rely solely on classical computation.

Entanglement signature of fully and partially dimerized phases in frustrated spin chains

Authors: Wuttichai Pankeaw, Teparksorn Pengpan, Pruet Kalasuwan
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02581v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that entanglement entropy can act as a quantitative fingerprint to resolve the internal bond structure of frustrated spin chains. By analyzing different bipartitions, the authors show that the partially dimerized phase exhibits a unique multi-band signature in pairwise entropy that distinguishes it from the fully dimerized phase.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether von Neumann entanglement entropy can serve as a robust diagnostic tool to distinguish between different dimerized bond architectures (fully vs. partially dimerized) in frustrated spin chains.

Main result. The study establishes that entanglement entropy provides a clear fingerprint for bond patterns, specifically identifying a unique multi-band structure in pairwise entropy for the partially dimerized phase and specific saturation values for the fully dimerized phase.

Method. The authors use exact Matrix Product State (MPS) representations of valence-bond ground states and perform finite-size scaling analysis to extract thermodynamic limits.

Model / system. The study focuses on the spin-1/2 Majumdar-Ghosh model and the spin-3/2 J1-J2-J3 chain, which supports both fully dimerized (FD) and partially dimerized (PD) phases.

Key observables. von Neumann entanglement entropy (half-chain, single-site, and pairwise), entanglement spectrum, and correlation length.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin magnitude (S=1/2 and S=3/2), bond multiplicity, dimerization patterns, and boundary conditions (Periodic vs. Open).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is based on exact valence-bond ground states, which are specific types of gapped, non-critical states.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate dimer patterns and boundary configurations, plots of half-chain entropy convergence for MG, FD, and PD phases, single-site entropy showing edge effects, and pairwise entropy showing multi-band structures and distance-dependent correlations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of identifying bond architecture, describes the MPS construction for the studied spin models, presents entanglement entropy results for various bipartitions and boundary conditions, and concludes with a diagnostic comparison of scaling behaviors.

Abstract

The von Neumann entanglement entropy of exact valence-bond ground states is studied in two frustrated one-dimensional spin chains: the spin-1/2 Majumdar-Ghosh (MG) model and the spin-3/2 J1-J2-J3 chain in its fully dimerized (FD) and partially dimerized (PD) phases. Using matrix-product-state representations, the entropy is computed as a function of system size for three complementary bipartitions - half-chain, single-site, and pairwise - under both open and periodic boundary conditions. In all cases, the entropy saturates to a finite constant in the thermodynamic limit, confirming area-law behavior. The saturation values, extracted via finite-size scaling, are directly related to the underlying virtual-spin bond structure. The MG model and FD phase exhibit similar entanglement behavior, differing primarily in saturation magnitude determined by spin value and bond multiplicity, and both display even-odd oscillations and exponential convergence with system size. In contrast, the PD phase shows qualitatively distinct signatures, including multiple half-chain saturation values depending on the bond type at the cut, asymmetric edge contributions in the single-site entropy, and a multi-band structure in the pairwise entropy reflecting the coexistence of single- and double-singlet bonds. These results establish entanglement entropy as a robust signature of frustrated bond architecture, enabling clear distinction among dimerized phases with different spin magnitude, bond multiplicity, and dimerization patterns.

Measuring Accuracy and Energy-to-Solution of Quantum Fine-Tuning of Foundational AI Models

Summary. This paper presents an experimental study measuring the actual power consumption of a trapped-ion quantum processor during AI fine-tuning tasks. It demonstrates that while classical simulations scale exponentially in energy, quantum processors show linear scaling, suggesting a clear energy advantage for quantum computing at around 34 qubits. Furthermore, it shows that quantum-enhanced models can outperform classical counterparts in text classification accuracy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Evaluating the energy-to-solution (ETS) and classification accuracy of hybrid quantum-classical pipelines for fine-tuning foundational AI models, specifically comparing QPU efficiency against GPU-based classical simulations.

Main result. The study identifies a break-even point for energy-to-solution at approximately 34 qubits, where QPUs become more efficient than GPUs, and demonstrates that quantum fine-tuned models can achieve a 24% improvement in classification error over classical baselines.

Method. The researchers used direct electrical instrumentation of an IonQ Forte Enterprise processor to measure power draw and applied a hybrid pipeline involving a Sentence Transformer and a parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) with error mitigation techniques like debiasing and non-linear aggregation.

Model / system. The experimental platform is an IonQ Forte Enterprise trapped-ion quantum processor. The computational model is a hybrid QML architecture using a BERT-based encoder (SetFit) followed by a PQC with angle encoding and data re-uploading.

Key observables. Classification accuracy (error rate), Energy-to-Solution (ETS), power draw (kW), and Z-basis expectation value .

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit counts (10 to 28), circuit depth, number of re-uploads (R=4), and power draw of the QPU (~5 kW) vs GPU (~0.072 kW).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that the energy-to-solution metric is a scalable metric for future applications; assumes gate counts scale linearly with qubit number; assumes training on classical simulators is a valid precursor to hardware execution.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the hybrid architecture; Figure 2 is a semilog plot of energy scaling (linear QPU vs exponential GPU); Figure 3 shows QPU power draw stability; Figure 4 compares classification accuracy across different qubit counts and noise regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of energy efficiency in QML, describes the hybrid architecture and error mitigation pipeline, presents the experimental setup for energy and accuracy measurements, compares results against classical baselines (SVM, LR) and simulations (MPS, statevector), and concludes with scaling analysis.

Abstract

We present an experimental study of energy-to-solution (ETS) of hybrid quantum-classical applications, enabled by direct instrumentation of power consumption of a Forte Enterprise trapped-ion quantum processor. We apply this methodology to a hybrid quantum-classical pipeline for quantum fine-tuning of foundational AI models, and validate the approach end-to-end on quantum hardware. Despite noise and limited qubit counts, the resulting models achieve accuracy competitive with and exceeding classical baselines such as logistic regression and support vector classifiers. Our results show that QPU energy consumption scales approximately linearly with qubit number for shallow circuits, while classical simulation exhibits exponential scaling, indicating a break-even for ETS around 34 qubits. The classification error improvement of the best quantum fine-tuned model over the best classical fine-tuned model considered in this study is around 24%. We further contextualize these findings with comparisons to tensor network methods. This work establishes energy-to-solution as a measurable and scalable metric for evaluating quantum applications and provides experimental evidence of favorable energy-accuracy trade-offs.

Permutation Routing on Ramanujan Hypergraphs with Applications to Neutral Atom Quantum Architectures

Authors: Joshua M. Courtney
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02498v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 Rydberg arrays 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper demonstrates that using Ramanujan hypergraphs as connectivity overlays can reduce the time required to rearrange atoms in a quantum processor from polynomial to logarithmic scaling. It introduces several advanced routing protocols, including hybrid greedy-Valiant and entanglement-assisted methods, to optimize large-scale neutral-atom architectures.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous mathematical blueprint for designing the connectivity of next-generation quantum processors, specifically showing how expander-like hypergraph topologies can overcome the scaling bottlenecks of 2D/3D grids.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing the permutation routing of atoms in reconfigurable neutral-atom quantum architectures to minimize routing depth and latency.

Main result. Proves that routing on Ramanujan (d,r)-regular hypergraphs achieves a logarithmic depth of Theta(log N), providing a significant speedup over standard grid-based routing.

Method. Uses hypergraph theory, spectral analysis of the clique expansion graph, and Valiant's two-phase randomized routing algorithm.

Model / system. Reconfigurable neutral-atom arrays (e.g., optical tweezers, AOD, or AOL architectures) where qubits are encoded in atoms and entangling gates are performed via Rydberg blockade.

Key observables. Routing number (rt(H)), routing depth (T), spectral ratio (beta), and displacement energy (Phi_t).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of atoms (N), hypergraph degree (d) and uniformity (r), capacity (k), and the crossover threshold for entanglement-assisted routing (R_break approx 4).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the effectiveness of covering towers relies on a uniform spectral bound; assumes a matching-model where edge-disjoint swaps can be performed simultaneously.

Figures summary. Tables comparing routing regimes, routing bounds for various hypergraphs, and trade-offs between AOL capacity and routing depth.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the routing problem in neutral atom arrays, develops a mathematical framework using Ramanujan hypergraphs, analyzes specific architectural models (2D AOD and 3D AOL), explores algebraic constructions and their limits (Abelian Alon-Boppana barrier), and evaluates advanced strategies like hierarchical and entanglement-assisted routing.

Abstract

We consider the routing of neutral atoms on a reconfigurable lattice in terms of hypergraph transformations. We prove the routing number of a Ramanujan $(d,r)$-regular hypergraph on $N$ vertices satisfies $\mathrm{rt}(H) = Θ(\log N)$, where routing is via matchings in the clique expansion graph $G_{\mathrm{cl}}(H)$. Hypergraphs reframe the qubit routing problem by replacing Nenadov's two-sided spectral gap hypothesis with a one-sided condition based on eigenvalue centering. Song--Fan--Miao (SFM) coverings scale for Ramanujan families of every uniformity. A virtual overlay theorem establishes a capacity--depth tradeoff for 3D acousto-optic lens (AOL) architectures, with multi-layer stacking achieving $Θ(\log N)$ routing with $L = O(\log N)$ independent overlay layers. An abelian Alon--Boppana barrier shows that fixed-degree Cayley graphs on $\mathbb{Z}_n^2$ cannot be Ramanujan and affine derandomization on such graphs achieves 15--30% congestion reduction. Towers of $k$-fold Ramanujan coverings yield $\mathrm(H_L) = O(\log N)$ by recursive routing lift. Entanglement-assisted routing by pre-distributed Bell pairs achieves $O(\log N)$ teleportation depth with a stable crossover at $\sim\!4$ routing rounds. Displacement energy analyzes greedy adaptive routing, identifying stalling and a hybrid greedy--Valiant protocol achieving $\sim\!3\times$ speedup at practical scales. Hierarchical multi-scale routing achieves $O(\log^2 N / \log b)$ depth with boundary-only transfers at capacity $k = O(\sqrt{N} \log N)$, and $O(\log N)$ depth with optimal block size $b = Θ(\sqrt{n})$.

Summary. This paper establishes new, computable criteria for determining the full separability of multipartite quantum states using third-order negativity. It provides a complete classification of entanglement measures for four-qubit systems and demonstrates how these algebraic invariants can be applied to study entanglement in Conformal Field Theory.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides fundamental tools for characterizing multipartite entanglement, which is essential for understanding complex correlations in many-body dynamics and quantum information processing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the difficulty of classifying multipartite entanglement and establishing necessary and sufficient criteria for full separability in multi-qubit and multi-qudit systems.

Main result. The authors prove that the third-order negativity (I5) is a necessary and sufficient criterion for the full separability of tripartite pure states and develop a complete set of 18 independent entanglement measures for four-qubit pure states.

Method. The study employs invariant theory, the replica trick, convex-roof extensions for mixed states, and algebraic decomposition of hyperdeterminants.

Model / system. The research focuses on multipartite quantum systems, specifically 3-qubit and 4-qubit pure and mixed states, as well as general q-partite qudit systems and applications to 2D Conformal Field Theory.

Key observables. Third-order negativity (I5), three-tangle, two-tangle, four-tangle, and the hyperdeterminant.

Important parameters / regimes. Central charge (c) in CFT, number of qubits/qudits (q), and the large-c limit.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the use of local unitary transformations for classification and relies on the compact nature of the parameter simplex for finding extrema.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides graphical notation for tripartite wavefunctions and the construction of the 2nd Renyi multi-entropy.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing separability criteria for tripartite states, moves to the classification of 4-qubit entanglement measures, generalizes these findings to q-partite qudit systems, and concludes with applications to Conformal Field Theory.

Abstract

We show that the third-order negativity provides a necessary and sufficient criterion for full separability of tripartite pure states, and extend this to mixed states beyond bipartite diagnostics such as negativity. As a minimal nontrivial example, a four-qubit pure state has three-qubit mixed reductions; its complete characterization requires six bipartite, eight tripartite, and four quadripartite measures, with the third-order negativity serving as a key separability criterion. We further generalize these separability criteria to multipartite qudit systems and discuss an application to conformal field theory.

Sub-Cubic Quantum Gate Synthesis via Stochastic Commutator Decomposition

Authors: Yevgen Kotukh
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02051v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces Stochastic Commutator Synthesis, a new method for quantum gate compilation that combines efficient sub-cubic scaling with randomized compilation. By using Gibbs sampling to decompose commutators, it transforms harmful coherent errors into manageable incoherent noise, significantly improving circuit fidelity on trapped-ion hardware.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of efficiently approximating arbitrary unitary operators using a discrete gate set while minimizing word length and preventing the accumulation of coherent approximation errors that degrade fault-tolerant thresholds.

Main result. The SCS framework achieves sub-cubic word-length scaling, reduces T-count by 10-25%, and demonstrates up to a 35% fidelity improvement on Forrelation circuits by converting coherent errors into incoherent noise.

Method. A hybrid framework integrating Kuperberg's sub-cubic algorithm with Randomized Compilation (RC) using Gibbs-sampled commutator decomposition and RL-guided pre-synthesis (Q-PreSyn).

Model / system. The framework is implemented and tested on the Sandia QSCOUT trapped-ion hardware using multi-fold Forrelation circuits and Clifford+T gate sets.

Key observables. Process fidelity, T-count, word-length exponent, trace distance, and operator/Frobenius norms.

Important parameters / regimes. Sub-cubic exponent c approx 1.44042, ensemble size K in [8, 32], temperature schedule with cooling factor beta in (0.5, 0.9), and precision epsilon between 2^-8 and 2^-12.

Assumptions / limitations. Operates within the classical inverse-closed gate set setting.

Figures summary. The paper includes a comparison table of SCS, classical SKA, and GridSynth across various performance criteria.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of gate synthesis, presents the SCS framework (ensemble generation, Gibbs sampling, and recursive lifting), integrates it with RC and Q-PreSyn, demonstrates experimental results on Sandia QSCOUT, and situates the work within the complexity-theoretic context of the Raz-Tal oracle separation.

Abstract

We present Stochastic Commutator Synthesis, a hybrid quantum gate compilation framework that integrates Kuperberg's sub-cubic Solovay-Kitaev exponent c near 1.44042 with the error-tailoring machinery of randomized compilation. Classical Solovay-Kitaev implementations produce known word lengths and accumulate coherent approximation errors that degrade fault-tolerant threshold estimates. Kuperberg's 2023-2025 result reduces this via doubly exponential convergence and higher-order commutator decompositions. SCS augments this geometric backbone with a Gibbs-sampled stochastic choice of commutator factors at each recursion level, converting coherent synthesis residuals into incoherent, Pauli-twirl-compatible noise -- a property exploited by RC. Combined with RL-guided pre-synthesis, SCS achieves consistent T-count reductions of 10-25 percent and demonstrates fidelity gains of up to 35 percent on multi-fold Forrelation circuits on trapped-ion hardware such as Sandia QSCOUT. We situate SCS within the complexity-theoretic landscape established by the Raz-Tal oracle separation, arguing that low-error, noise-robust compilation of Forrelation-type circuits constitutes a practical pathway toward demonstrating this separation on physical hardware.

Quantum-enhanced sensing from the interplay of long-range interactions and non-Hermiticity

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the combination of long-range interactions and non-Hermiticity can significantly boost the sensitivity of quantum sensors during the transient evolution of a spin system. While it provides a clear advantage for dynamical sensing, the authors establish that this benefit does not extend to the estimation of parameters in stationary states.

Why it may be interesting. It reveals a genuine metrological advantage arising from the synergy of non-unitary dynamics and long-range connectivity, providing new insights into how non-Hermitian many-body dynamics can be leveraged for enhanced quantum sensing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the interplay between long-range interactions and non-Hermiticity can enhance quantum sensing performance, specifically for parameter estimation of magnetic fields and anisotropy.

Main result. The study finds that long-range interactions and non-Hermiticity synergistically enhance the dynamical Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) in the transient regime, though no genuine advantage is found for stationary-state sensing.

Method. The authors use a mathematical framework involving Jordan-Wigner and Fourier transformations to analyze the spectrum of a non-Hermitian Hamiltonian and calculate the dynamical and stationary Quantum Fisher Information (QFI).

Model / system. A one-dimensional, long-range, RT-symmetric non-Hermitian iXY spin model with power-law decaying interactions, effectively modeled as a spin-1/2 lattice coupled to a reservoir.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) for the transverse magnetic field (h) and anisotropy parameter (gamma), and scaling exponents (mu and beta).

Important parameters / regimes. Decay exponent (alpha), coordination number (Z), non-Hermiticity parameter (gamma), magnetic field (h), and the broken/unbroken phase regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. Periodic boundary conditions are assumed, and the analysis focuses on a system initialized in a fully polarized state.

Figures summary. Figures show the ratio of non-Hermitian to Hermitian QFI versus alpha, the scaling of QFI with system size near exceptional points, and the lack of improvement in stationary scaling with increased coordination number.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the non-Hermitian long-range model, provides an analytical framework for its momentum-space decomposition, analyzes the metrological performance in both dynamical and stationary regimes, and concludes with a no-go result for stationary sensing.

Abstract

Long-range (LR) quantum spin systems offer promising advantages for quantum information processing and sensing. Here, we investigate parameter estimation in an long-range XX spin model coupled to a reservoir, which gives rise to an effective long-range RT-symmetric non-Hermitian iXY Hamiltonian. The interactions extend up to a tunable coordination range and decay algebraically with distance, enabling a direct comparison between long-range and short-range (SR) regimes. Focusing on the estimation of the transverse magnetic field and anisotropy parameter, we initialize the system in a fully polarized state and analyze the resulting dynamical quantum Fisher information (QFI). We show that, with suitable tuning of the system parameters, both the time and system-size scaling of the QFI are enhanced in the LR regime relative to their SR counterparts. Moreover, the non-Hermitian LR model can exhibit superior dynamical QFI compared with the corresponding Hermitian model, demonstrating a genuine metrological advantage induced by the interplay of long-range interactions and non-Hermitian effects. In contrast, we establish a no-go result at the critical magnetic field: when the probe is prepared in the lowest-energy eigenstate, the QFI scaling remains identical for the Hermitian and non-Hermitian cases.

Main result. Demonstration of a planar reflective multipass vapor cell that achieves a differential gradiometer sensitivity of approximately 28 fT/sqrt(Hz) in the geomagnetic field range.

Method. The researchers used a paraffin-coated borosilicate glass cell with integrated dielectric mirrors to create a 20-pass optical trajectory, employing Free Induction Decay (FID) magnetometry with a two-cell differential configuration.

Model / system. An experimental platform consisting of a $^{87}$Rb vapor cell (12 x 12 x 8 mm$^3$) with an anti-relaxation paraffin coating and integrated $ ext{SiO}_2/ ext{Ta}_2 ext{O}_5$ dielectric mirrors, operated in a magnetic gradiometer setup within a $\mu$-metal shield.

Abstract

We demonstrate a paraffin-coated planar reflective multipass vapor cell for compact optical atomic magnetometry. The cell has an internal volume of $12 \times 12 \times 8~\mathrm{mm}^3$ and supports 20 optical passes with a total transmittance exceeding $65\%$, while maintaining a longitudinal spin-relaxation time of $^{87}\mathrm{Rb}$ longer than $1~\mathrm{s}$. The planar geometry provides spatially separated input and output beams, enabling compact optical integration. A single-cell free-induction-decay (FID) magnetometer reaches $10~\mathrm{pT}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ in the geomagnetic-field range, presently limited by current-source noise in the field coils. A two-cell differential configuration achieves a sensitivity of $\sim 28~\mathrm{fT}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ over the $1$--$15~\mathrm{Hz}$ band for bias fields of $0.3$--$0.7~\mathrm{G}$. These results establish that paraffin-coated planar multipass cells offer high optical depth, long coherence times, and an integration-friendly platform for ultrasensitive magnetometry.

Aging Record Statistics in Saturating Self-Interacting Random Walks

Authors: J. Brémont, R. Voituriez, O. Bénichou
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02433v1

Summary. This paper provides an exact analytical solution for the record-age statistics in a class of non-Markovian random walks. It demonstrates how memory effects localized at the boundaries of a visited region lead to the emergence of aging phenomena. The work bridges the gap between scaling theory and microscopic dynamics by providing a complete description of the crossover between different asymptotic regimes.

Why it may be interesting. The derivation of exact scaling functions for non-Markovian processes with long-range memory is highly relevant to understanding the dynamics of open quantum systems and many-body systems where environmental memory effects induce non-equilibrium aging.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how memory in non-Markovian processes breaks the translation invariance of record ages, leading to aging phenomena where the distribution of time intervals between records depends on the record index.

Main result. The authors derive the exact asymptotic distribution of the record age for saturating self-interacting random walks, uncovering two distinct regimes: a short-time regime governed by the geometry of the explored region and a long-time regime where memory effects act as subdominant prefactor corrections.

Method. The study employs the Ray-Knight theorem and its aged extension to map the dynamics to a sum of independent squared Bessel processes (BESQ), using Laplace transforms, path integrals, and hypergeometric functions to obtain exact analytical solutions.

Model / system. Saturating Self-Interacting Random Walks (SIRWs), specifically the SATW model, where the walker's future motion depends on its history, but memory effects are localized at the boundaries of the visited territory.

Key observables. Record age (tau_k), aged First-Passage Times (FPTs), and the scaling function psi(x).

Important parameters / regimes. Parameters alpha and beta (interaction weights), the record index k, and the scaling variable x = k^(-dw) * tau.

Assumptions / limitations. The results rely on the 'saturation' property of the self-interaction, which ensures the process is Markovian in the bulk; the derivation also assumes a continuum scaling limit for large visited territories.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates a SATW trajectory showing the visited region and how transition probabilities change at the boundaries; Figure 3 shows the decomposition of the total area into three independent BESQ processes.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of aging in record statistics, introduces the SATW model, develops a mathematical framework using the Ray-Knight representation, derives the exact scaling function through Laplace-space analysis, and concludes by discussing asymptotic regimes and universality.

Abstract

The record age tau_k, defined as the time between the k-th and k+1-st record-breaking events, is a central observable of extreme-value statistics. In Markovian processes, the absence of memory makes tau_k independent of k. How memory breaks this invariance and induces aging, meaning a dependence of tau_k on k, remains a fundamental question, closely connected to widely observed aging phenomena in non-Markovian dynamics. In this Letter, we derive the exact asymptotic distribution of tau_k for saturating self-interacting random walks, a broad class of non-Markovian processes. We uncover two asymptotic regimes, in agreement with recent scaling predictions: at short times (tau much smaller than k squared), record statistics are governed by the geometry of the explored region, while at long times (tau much larger than k squared), memory effects become subdominant and reduce to nontrivial prefactor corrections. Our exact result provides a rare analytic window beyond scaling theory and extends to a framework that fully quantifies aging dynamics in the presence of saturating self-interaction.
