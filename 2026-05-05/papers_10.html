Evidence for altermagnetic order in Cr-doped FeSb2

Authors: A K M Ashiquzzaman Shawon, Eoghan Downey, Shane Smolenski, Thomas J. Hicken, Amir Henderson, Mingyu Xu, Trisha Musall, Rafael Lopes Sabainsk, Yuan Zhu, Weiwei Xie, Elena Gati, Lu Li, Zurab Guguchia, Na Hyun Jo
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01088v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides experimental evidence for altermagnetism in Cr-doped FeSb2 by demonstrating a spin-compensated magnetic state that breaks time-reversal symmetry. The observation of a spontaneous anomalous Hall effect and bulk magnetic order supports the theoretical prediction of a d-wave altermagnetic ground state.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. To investigate the magnetic ground state of Cr-doped FeSb2 and provide experimental evidence for the existence of altermagnetic order.

Main result. The study identifies a spin-compensated magnetic ground state with broken time-reversal symmetry, evidenced by a spontaneous anomalous Hall effect and bulk magnetic order below 3.5 K.

Method. Single crystals of Fe1-xCrxSb2 were grown via the Sb-flux method and characterized using X-ray diffraction, magnetometry, electrical transport measurements, and Muon Spin Relaxation (uSR).

Model / system. The physical system is Cr-doped FeSb2 (specifically Fe0.85Cr0.15Sb2), which possesses an orthorhombic marcasite-type crystal structure and is a candidate for d-wave altermagnetism.

Key observables. Magnetic susceptibility, magnetization, longitudinal resistivity, Hall resistivity, magnetoresistance, and muon spin relaxation decay rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Néel temperature (TN) of approximately 3.5 K, Cr-doping concentration (x), and magnetic anisotropy along the b-axis.

Assumptions / limitations. The magnetic order is assumed to be intrinsic and bulk-like rather than due to impurity phases, a claim supported by uSR.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the crystal structure, XRD patterns, and lattice parameters; Fig 2 displays temperature-dependent resistivity, magnetoresistance, Hall resistivity, and magnetization; Fig 3 presents uSR spectra under zero and longitudinal fields.

Paper structure. The paper begins with crystal growth and structural characterization, followed by magnetic and transport property measurements, then presents muon spin relaxation data to confirm bulk order, and concludes with a discussion of the altermagnetic state.

Abstract

Altermagnets are a class of materials with compensated magnetic moments, in which spin sublattices are related by specific symmetries other than inversion or translation. This allows time-reversal symmetry to be broken without a net magnetization. Here, we synthesize single crystals of Fe1-xCrxSb2 and investigate their electrical transport and magnetic properties, with a focus on Fe0.85Cr0.15Sb2. Magnetization measurements suggest spin-compensated ordering below ~ 3.5 K, where magnetic moments align along the crystallographic b-direction. Transport measurements reveal a crossover from large positive to negative magnetoresistance, while an anomalous Hall response emerges below 3.5 K, indicating time-reversal symmetry breaking below TN. Muon spin relaxation measurements demonstrate bulk magnetic order below 3.5 K, confirming that the low temperature ordering is intrinsic rather than due to an impurity phase. These results support a potential altermagnetic ground state in Cr-doped FeSb2 with time-reversal symmetry breaking without net magnetization.

Excited states engineering maximizes singlet generation by triplet fusion in conjugated systems

Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01998v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers developed a way to maximize the production of high-energy photons from low-energy light by engineering the electronic structure of organic molecules. By tuning the energy levels to favor singlet formation during triplet collisions, they achieved high upconversion yields compatible with solar energy technologies.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The efficiency of sensitized triplet-triplet annihilation upconversion (sTTA-UC) is limited by a low statistical factor (f) for singlet formation, often due to energy loss through accessible triplet states.

Main result. By engineering the excited-state energy landscape of a naphthalene-derived annihilator, the authors achieved a singlet generation yield (f) of 0.83 and an absolute upconversion yield of 0.19.

Method. The study combines molecular engineering via selective substitution, DFT/TD-DFT calculations to model the energy landscape, and experimental spectroscopy (steady-state and time-resolved) to validate the TTA process.

Model / system. A dual-dye solution consisting of an iridium complex sensitizer, Ir(ppy)2(acac), and a naphthalene-derived annihilator, TIPS-Ph-Naph, operating via Dexter energy transfer and triplet-triplet annihilation.

Key observables. Photon conversion quantum yield (phi_uc), statistical factor (f), upconversion peak wavelength, excitation intensity threshold (I_th), and triplet lifetime.

Important parameters / regimes. The transition from quadratic to linear power dependency, the activation threshold of 0.71 mW/cm2 under broadband excitation, and the resonance between the T1T1 complex and high-energy Sn states.

Assumptions / limitations. The design assumes that tuning the energy landscape to favor resonances with high-energy singlets is more effective than the traditional approach of avoiding all resonances.

Figures summary. Fig 1a shows the sTTA-UC photophysical process; Fig 4a compares absorption and excitation spectra; Fig 4b shows the upconversion intensity vs. excitation intensity, illustrating the quadratic-to-linear transition and solar compatibility.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the sTTA-UC efficiency bottleneck, describes the molecular design strategy and synthesis of the engineered annihilator, presents computational modeling of the energy landscape, provides experimental validation of the high singlet yield and upconversion efficiency, and concludes with the system's performance under solar-simulated conditions.

Abstract

Photon upconverters are anti Stokes emitters capable of generating photons with higher energy than those absorbed. This behavior can be achieved through different mechanisms, which are extensively studied for applications in imaging, anticounterfeiting, information encryption and most importantly, solar energy technologies. Among these mechanisms, photon upconversion based on sensitized triplet-triplet annihilation (sTTA-UC) is particularly attractive because it operates under low intensity, incoherent light. In sTTA UC, two optically dark triplet states of a conjugated annihilator fuse upon collision to populate a higher energy fluorescent singlet, with the triplets initially generated via energy transfer from a lower energy sensitizer. Here we introduce a general molecular design strategy to maximize singlet generation through TTA. By selective substitution, we engineered a naphthalene derived annihilator with an excited state energy landscape that strongly favors singlet formation, achieving yields up to 0.83. When combined with an appropriate triplet sensitizer, the system delivers stable UV/visible upconversion peaking at 390 nm, with an absolute upconversion yield about 0.19 and an activation excitation intensity threshold lower than 0.1 suns under non-coherent broadband excitation fully compatible with the requirements of solar powered technologies.

Detailed structure

Main result. Key observables. Optical reflectivity, equation of state, and ionic configurations.

Important parameters / regimes. 11% helium atomic fraction, shock-compressed regime, warm dense matter regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the validity of the thermal Tr2SCANL and RS-KDT0 exchange-correlation functionals for predicting properties in the warm dense matter regime.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of H-He demixing in Jupiter, describes the ab initio molecular dynamics and Kubo-Greenwood methodology, presents the calculated reflectivity and equation of state, and concludes by comparing results to experimental laser-shock data to evaluate the diagnostic utility of reflectivity.

Abstract

Recent laser-shock experiments on an H--He mixture containing 11~$\%$ helium (atomic fraction) have suggested the presence of an immiscibility region inside Jupiter. Reflectivity measurements were used as the primary diagnostic of H--He demixing, with discontinuities in the optical reflectivity proposed as a signature of phase separation under conditions relevant to Jupiter's interior. Here, we investigate shock-compressed H--He using \textit{ab initio} molecular dynamics simulations with optical properties evaluated within the Kubo--Greenwood formalism. The equation of state and ionic configurations were obtained using the thermal Tr$^2$SCANL meta-GGA exchange--correlation (XC) functional, while optical properties were computed using the recently developed RS-KDT0 range-separated thermal hybrid XC, which provides state-of-the-art accuracy for band-gap predictions in the warm dense matter regime. The calculated reflectivity shows overall good agreement with experimental measurements; however, no discontinuity is observed at elevated temperatures. Moreover, the reflectivity predictions for the mixed system are consistent with the experimental measurements in the temperature range where the mixture is inferred to be demixed. These results suggest that reflectivity alone may not provide a unique or sensitive diagnostic of H-He demixing at low helium concentrations under these conditions.

Generalized continuum theory of phonon angular momentum in crystals

Authors: Mamoru Matsuo, Naoki Nishimura, Ai Yamakage, Takeo Kato
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01678v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes a new continuum theory for phonon angular momentum that bridges the gap between acoustic and optical phonons. By introducing a local rotation frame, it provides a consistent way to calculate angular momentum density and explains how structural chirality leads to the splitting of phonon modes.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a unified continuum theory that can simultaneously describe both acoustic displacement modes and optical rotational modes within a single conservation law for phonon angular momentum.

Main result. The development of a generalized continuum theory using a local SO(3) material frame that identifies a definite phonon angular-momentum density and explains chiral phonon splitting.

Method. The authors use a local-frame formulation of micropolar elasticity, applying Noether's theorem to a Lagrangian density to derive conservation laws and analyzing the linearized limit and symmetry-breaking terms.

Model / system. A generalized continuum model of crystals/solids, specifically focusing on microstructures with rotational optical degrees of freedom represented by a local SO(3) material frame.

Key observables. Phonon angular momentum density (comprising orbital, displacement-polarization, and intrinsic microrotation parts), phonon dispersion relations, and chiral splitting of modes.

Important parameters / regimes. Mass density, moment of inertia, Lamé parameters, Cosserat/wryness moduli, and the chiral coupling constant.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory is applied in the long-wavelength (continuum) limit and assumes a regime where torsion and curvature terms are not required; the electron-spin coupling is deferred to future work.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a 2D schematic of microrotation and displacement fields in a lattice; Figure 2 shows phonon dispersion relations; Figure 3 compares transverse phonon dispersions in achiral versus chiral media.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of unified phonon angular momentum, defines the local SO(3) material frame and co-rotated deformation gradient, applies Noether's theorem to derive angular momentum density, analyzes the linearized limit (micropolar elasticity), explores the locking limit and chiral splitting, and concludes with technical remarks on the hierarchy of continua.

Abstract

We formulate a generalized continuum theory of phonon angular momentum in crystals by introducing a local SO(3) material frame in addition to the macroscopic displacement field. The local frame represents rotational optical degrees of freedom of the unit cell and brings acoustic displacement modes and optical rotational modes into a common long-wavelength continuum description. In the linearized limit, the co-rotated deformation gradient and the rotational gradient associated with the local material frame recover the Eringen microdeformation and wryness tensors; isotropic micropolar elasticity then appears as a special case. Rotational symmetry and Noether's theorem determine the continuum phonon angular-momentum density, including both the displacement-polarization contribution and the intrinsic microrotation contribution. The theory further identifies the locking limit in which microrotation reduces to lattice vorticity and the improper-symmetry-breaking terms responsible for chiral phonon splitting.

Geometric Percolation Threshold Defines Half-Metallic Window in Vacancy-Doped Titanium disulfides

Authors: Shrestha Dutta, Rudra Banerjee
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01754v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the emergence of half-metallicity in sulfur-deficient TiS2 is a geometric percolation event rather than a simple concentration-dependent effect. It identifies a specific concentration window for stable spin-polarized transport and establishes a design principle for 2D spintronics based on defect connectivity.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Resolving why defect engineering in 2D materials often produces local magnetic moments without achieving the desired itinerant half-metallic ferromagnetism.

Main result. The transition to half-metallicity in vacancy-doped TiS2 is driven by the geometric percolation of the vacancy network, occurring at a critical concentration of approximately 12.5%.

Method. The study combines Density Functional Theory (DFT) with large-scale Tight-Binding (TB) simulations and finite-size scaling analysis to study percolation universality.

Model / system. Monolayer 1T-phase titanium disulfide (TiS2) with sulfur vacancies, modeled using a 2D percolation framework and a large-scale tight-binding Hamiltonian on a triangular lattice.

Key observables. Spin polarization, giant cluster fraction (P_infinity), Fisher exponent (tau), Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR), and vacancy concentration (x).

Important parameters / regimes. Critical concentration x_c approx 12.5%, functional window 11% < x < 15%, and geometric jamming limit at x > 20%.

Assumptions / limitations. The mechanism is applicable to isostructural 1T-type TMDs with extended defect wavefunctions; Hubbard-U correction is used to model electron correlation.

Figures summary. Figure 5 shows thermodynamic limits and defect formation energy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the paradox of defect engineering, details the electronic and magnetic mechanism via symmetry breaking, applies percolation theory to identify the critical threshold, discusses the functional window and jamming limits, and concludes with experimental signatures.

Abstract

Defect engineering of two-dimensional materials routinely produces local magnetic moments, yet itinerant half-metallic ferromagnetism remains elusive -- experiments frequently yield paramagnetic insulators. We resolve this paradox for vacancy-doped monolayer $1T$-\ptis~by demonstrating that the insulator-to-half-metal transition is governed by universal geometric percolation of the defect network, extending the percolation framework established for three-dimensional diluted magnetic semiconductors into the 2D vacancy-doped regime. Half-metallicity emerges via a two-step mechanism: crystal-field symmetry breaking ($O_h \to C_{4v}$) selectively stabilizes the Ti $3d_{z^2}$ orbital, generating robust local moments ($0.94~μ_B$), but spin-polarized transport requires these moments to form a spanning cluster. At critical vacancy concentration $x_c \approx 12.5\%$, a percolation transition drives the majority-spin impurity band from flat, localized levels ($W < 0.1$~eV) to a dispersive 1.5~eV-wide band with 100\% spin polarization and a minority-spin gap of 1.0~eV. The percolation mechanism is independently corroborated by a striking supercell-size effect: at identical concentration, $2\times2$ cells yield antiferromagnetic order while $4\times4$ cells mandate ferromagnetism, reflecting the presence or absence of a spanning cluster. We estimate a Curie temperature exceeding 300~K from the exchange coupling, and identify a geometric jamming instability at $x > 20\%$ that fragments the network. These results define a narrow functional window ($11\% < x < 15\%$) for half-metallic operation and establish geometric connectivity as a quantitative design principle for defect-engineered 2D spintronics.

Grain boundary segregation of light elements and their effects on cohesion in ferritic steels

Authors: Han Lin Mai, Xiang-Yuan Cui, Tilmann Hickel, Simon P. Ringer, Jörg Neugebauer
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01181v1

Summary. This paper provides a comprehensive DFT-based dataset and analysis of how light elements segregate to grain boundaries in iron. It demonstrates that the ability of a site to relax and accommodate strain is a better predictor of segregation than initial void volume, and classifies elements as either strengtheners or embrittlers.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how the segregation of light elements (H, He, B, C, N, O, P, S) to grain boundaries in ferritic steels affects interfacial cohesion and macroscale properties.

Main result. The study identifies B and C as cohesion enhancers, while He, O, and S are potent decohesion agents; it also reveals that segregation is driven by a site's ability to accommodate strain rather than initial Voronoi volume.

Method. Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations using the VASP package with the PBE functional, combined with bond-order (ANSBO) and Rice-Wang frameworks for cohesion analysis.

Model / system. Six specific Coincident Site Lattice (CSL) grain boundaries in bcc-Fe (including Sigma3, Sigma5, Sigma9, and Sigma11) with various light element solutes in interstitial and substitutional sites.

Key observables. Segregation energies (Eseg), grain boundary energy (gamma_GB), work of separation (WRGS), and bond orders (ANSBO).

Important parameters / regimes. Solute types (H, He, B, C, N, O, P, S), site volumes, and nearest-neighbor distances after relaxation.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the PBE functional and the assumption that the Rice-Wang framework might be unphysical for certain elements like Helium due to rigid-grain separation assumptions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows GB atomic structures; Figure 3 shows the lack of correlation between GB energy and segregation strength; Figure 5 shows segregation energy distributions; Figure 7 shows the correlation between nearest-neighbor distance and segregation energy; Figures 8 and 9 show elemental cohesive effects.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of light element segregation, describes the DFT methodology and site sampling strategy, presents results on segregation energy trends and cohesion modifiers, analyzes the structural drivers of segregation (volume vs. strain), and concludes with data availability.

Abstract

Light elements play an important role in influencing the macroscale properties of engineering alloys through grain boundary (GB) segregation phenomena. However, the scarcity and scattered nature of ab initio datasets for light elements in steels makes reproduction and extraction of general trends from the literature difficult. Here, we present a comprehensive ab initio evaluation of the segregation energies and cohesive effects for H, He, B, C, N, O, P, S, extensively sampling both substitutional and interstitial sites in six model coincident site lattice (CSL) ferritic iron GBs using density functional theory (DFT). Cohesive effects are evaluated in both a quantum-chemistry bond-order and rigid Rice-Wang interfacial cohesive strength framework. Our calculations indicate that, compared at the same concentration, B and C enhance GB cohesion, N, P, H are mildly detrimental, and He, O, S as powerful decohesive agents/embrittlers. Sampling both interstitial and substitutional starting positions is necessary to accurately capture segregation spectra. Commonly utilised sampling criteria such as site volumes prove insufficient for identifying deepest GB binding sites. Solutes placed in either kind of site can induce large relaxations to the same final configuration, resulting in site classification ambiguity. The nearest neighbour distance of a solute to its neighbours after relaxation is shown to be a controlling factor for the lower threshold of segregation energies at sites. The freely available DFT dataset and analysis repositories are expected to advance understanding of GB segregation behaviours of light elements in steels and serve as a resource for developing machine learning interatomic potentials.

Impurity-Scattering Assisted Umklapp Scattering as the Origin of Low-Temperature Resistivity in the Normal-State of Cuprate Superconductors

Authors: Xingyu Ma, Minghuan Zeng, Huaiming Guo, Shiping Feng
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01316v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes that the change in resistivity power-law from T-linear to T-quadratic in cuprates is caused by the suppression of spin-excitation-mediated Umklapp scattering due to the opening of a spin pseudogap. It provides a microscopic mechanism that links the transport properties directly to the evolution of the antinodal spin dynamics with doping.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Explaining the origin of the crossover in low-temperature resistivity between the T-linear behavior in the overdoped strange-metal phase and the T-quadratic behavior in the underdoped pseudogap phase of cuprate superconductors.

Main result. The resistivity behavior is driven by impurity-scattering assisted electron Umklapp scattering from spin excitations; the opening of the antinodal spin pseudogap in the underdoped regime suppresses the spin excitation density of states, leading to T-quadratic resistivity.

Method. The authors use the Boltzmann transport equation within a microscopic pseudogap theory framework, specifically employing a fermion-spin transformation and the t-J model to derive the electron propagator and resistivity.

Model / system. The system is the normal-state of cuprate superconductors, modeled using a low-energy effective t-J model on a square lattice including nearest and next-nearest-neighbor hopping, chemical potential, and magnetic coupling.

Key observables. Low-temperature resistivity (rho), dc conductivity, antinodal spin pseudogap, and the pseudogap crossover temperature (T*)

Important parameters / regimes. Doping level (delta), temperature (T), impurity scattering rate (gamma_0), and the antinodal spin pseudogap magnitude

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the presence of natural impurities due to doping and focuses specifically on the low-temperature regime rather than high-temperature transport.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the electron spectral function intensity map; Figure 2 displays the antinodal spin pseudogap and its crossover temperature as a function of doping; Figure 3 and 4 show the T-quadratic and T-linear resistivity fits for underdoped and overdoped regimes respectively; Figure 5 plots the temperature scale as a function of doping.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the microscopic electronic structure and the t-J model, develops the pseudogap theory via fermion-spin transformation, applies the Boltzmann transport equation to investigate momentum relaxation via impurity-assisted Umklapp scattering, and concludes by comparing the results to different doping regimes.

Abstract

The transport experiments reveal that the low-temperature resistivity in the normal-state of cuprate superconductors is quadratic in temperature (T-quadratic) in the underdoped pseudogap phase, while it is linear in temperature (T-linear) in the overdoped strange-metal phase, however, the full understanding of these different behaviours is still a challenging issue. Here starting from the microscopic electronic structure of cuprate superconductors, the low-temperature resistivity in the normal-state is investigated from the underdoped pseudogap phase to the overdoped strange-metal phase. It is shown that the mechanism requires both the impurity scattering and the umklapp scattering: the impurity scattering is needed to restrict the modification of the distribution function to at around the antinodal region,while the impurity-scattering assisted umklapp scattering from a spin excitation is at the heart of the behaviour in the low-temperature resistivity, where the doping dependence of the temperature scale exists, and presents a similar behavior of the antinodal spin pseudogap crossover temperature. In the low-temperature region above the temperature scale in the overdoped strange-metal phase, the resistivity is T-linear, however, in the low-temperature region below the temperature scale in the underdoped pseudogap phase, the opening of the spin pseudogap lowers the spin excitation density of states at around the antinodal region, which reduces the strength of the electron umklapp scattering from a spin excitation associated with the antinode, and thus leads to a T-quadratic behaviour of the resistivity.

Influence of Coherent Elastic Strain on Phase Separation in BCC Nb-V Alloys

Authors: Siya Zhu, Raymundo Arróyave

Summary. This paper develops a thermodynamic model to show that coherent elastic strain is a critical factor in suppressing phase separation in lattice-mismatched alloys. By incorporating elastic compatibility into phase-diagram calculations, the authors successfully align theoretical predictions for the Nb-V system with experimental observations. The work demonstrates that elastic constraints fundamentally change the nature of phase equilibria by making coexistence compositions dependent on the total alloy composition.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how coherent elastic strain influences phase separation thermodynamics and the width of the miscibility gap in alloys with significant lattice mismatch.

Main result. Coherent elasticity substantially suppresses phase separation, narrows the miscibility gap, and lowers the critical temperature, while making equilibrium compositions dependent on the overall alloy composition.

Method. A thermodynamic framework combining DFT-derived chemical free energies with explicit coherent elastic contributions using both isotropic and biaxial strain models.

Model / system. The BCC Niobium-Vanadium (Nb-V) binary alloy system, modeled using a combination of CALPHAD-style chemical free energies and mechanical compatibility constraints (isotropic and biaxial).

Key observables. Coherent binodal and spinodal boundaries, critical temperature (Tc), miscibility gap width, and equilibrium phase compositions (x1, x2).

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice mismatch, temperature (T), overall alloy composition (X), and elastic parameters (bulk modulus, pressure derivative, and biaxial expansion coefficients).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglect of PV terms and electronic entropy; approximation of vibrational entropy as linear in composition; assumption of an ideal random-solution configurational entropy.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows composition dependence of properties; Figure 2 shows the energy surface as a function of biaxial strain; Figure 4 compares decomposition compositions in chemical-only vs. coherent-elastic models; Figure 5 analyzes the elastic origin of the suppression mechanism.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of lattice mismatch in phase separation, presents a thermodynamic framework integrating chemical and elastic energies, compares isotropic and biaxial models, validates the model against DFT and experimental data, and discusses the qualitative shift in phase boundary interpretation.

Abstract

Coherent elastic strain is an important but often neglected contribution to phase-separation thermodynamics in alloy systems where decomposed phases have appreciable lattice mismatch. We develop a thermodynamic framework that incorporates coherent elastic compatibility directly into phase-diagram calculations alongside conventional CALPHAD chemical free energies. Applied to the BCC Nb-V system, the framework shows that coherent elasticity substantially suppresses phase separation, narrows the miscibility gap, and lowers the critical temperature toward experimentally observed values. Beyond these quantitative effects, the coherent constraint qualitatively alters the interpretation of phase equilibria: the equilibrium decomposition compositions become functions of both temperature and overall alloy composition, so the two-phase boundary no longer represents unique coexistence compositions. These results establish coherent elasticity as a key thermodynamic factor in lattice-mismatched systems and provide a general framework for coherent phase-diagram modeling.

Injection of orbital angular momentum into transition metals from first-principles

Authors: Max Rang, Paul J. Kelly
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02548v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The study uses first-principles calculations to show that injected orbital angular momentum currents in transition metals decay much more rapidly (within a few atomic layers) than previously thought. It reveals that spin-orbit coupling plays a crucial role in converting these short-lived orbital currents into longer-lived spin currents.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the decay length scale of injected orbital angular momentum (OAM) currents in transition metals and addressing the discrepancy between theoretical predictions and experimental interpretations.

Main result. Orbital currents decay rapidly within a few atomic layers upon injection, unlike spin currents which have longer diffusion lengths; however, in the presence of spin-orbit coupling, injected orbital current is partially converted into a spin current.

Method. First-principles quantum mechanical scattering calculations using a tight-binding muffin-tin orbital (TB-MTO) basis and Ando's Wave Function Matching scheme.

Model / system. Transition metals (including Pt, Ti, V, Cr, and Cu) modeled as a scattering region sandwiched between two semi-infinite leads, incorporating spin and orbital Zeeman terms to create polarized leads.

Key observables. Charge, spin, and orbital currents (j_c, j_s, j_l); orbital Hall conductivity (OHC); spin Hall conductivity (SHC); spin-flip and orbital diffusion lengths.

Important parameters / regimes. Spin-orbit coupling (SOC) strength, thermal lattice disorder (temperature), and spin-flip diffusion length (l_sf).

Assumptions / limitations. The orbital diffusion length is assumed to be driven by electronic hopping; SOC is sometimes switched off in the scattering region to isolate orbital effects; the orbital Zeeman term is used as a proxy for orbital polarization.

Figures summary. Schematic of the L|S|R geometry for current generation; plots showing the decay of injected orbital polarization in Pt, Cr, and V; and visualization of current density in the leads.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of OAM decay, describes the TB-MTO scattering formalism, presents results for various transition metals (Pt, 3d metals), discusses the role of SOC and disorder, and concludes with implications for orbitronics.

Abstract

We use quantum mechanical scattering calculations implemented in a basis of tight-binding muffin-tin orbitals to calculate nonequilibrium spin and orbital currents in transition metals with a view to understanding the length scale on which they decay. In the case of spin currents, the relaxation length, called the spin-flip diffusion length, is reasonably well understood. We apply our experience with spin currents to study orbitally-polarized currents and find that they behave qualitatively differently. Upon injection from a lead, orbital currents decay within a few atomic layers contradicting the current interpretation of experimental results which appear to show exponential decay on the length scale of the spin-flip diffusion length and longer. When spin-orbit coupling is included, the injected orbital current is partially converted into a spin current within a few atomic layers. This insight provides a new perspective on the physics of the orbital Hall effect.

Abstract

Cathode particle fracture is widely recognised as a major degradation mechanism in lithium-ion batteries, yet cracking also permits electrolyte wetting of newly exposed internal surfaces, modifying interfacial reaction pathways. The mechanistic role of electrolyte wetting in redistributing reactions within cracked particles remains unclear. Here, we isolate this effect through a controlled comparison between (i) a fully coupled electro-chemo-mechanical model resolving lithium concentration, electrostatic potential, and stress fields in both the active material and the electrolyte inside and outside cracks, and (ii) a single-particle chemo-mechanical model employing the conventional uniform flux assumption. The coupled model predicts strong spatial heterogeneity in interfacial reaction rates, with flux amplification approximately 8x relative to the imposed uniform flux at the crack tip. Reaction redistribution, and thus lithium flux, is governed predominantly by local solid-state lithium concentration and stress variations, while electrolyte potential gradients inside cracks remain secondary under the conditions considered. Uniform flux models can underpredict delivered capacity by 25% at 1C-rate; this discrepancy increases at higher rates. They also underestimate tensile stresses throughout the delithiation process by 10%, directly affecting crack driving conditions. These results demonstrate that neglecting crack-electrolyte coupling leads to systematic underestimation of both utilisation limits and fatigue-relevant stress histories.

Abstract

In collinear altermagnets, the real-space rotational symmetry of opposite spin sublattices generates a large nonrelativistic spin-splitter current. Orbital transport in this setting has remained largely unexplored. Here, we introduce the orbital-splitter current (OSC), an orbital analogue of the spin-splitter current, and derive its Drude and orbital Berry curvature contributions using a density-matrix framework. We show that the $d$-wave altermagnet $\mathrm{FeSb}_2$ realizes a purely intrinsic OSC because mirror symmetries suppress the Drude channel by forcing the orbital magnetic moment to vanish. The OSC response is strongly anisotropic and, for selected field orientations, exceeds the spin-splitter current by nearly a factor of four. We further show that the OSC generates a damping-like torque in an altermagnet-ferromagnet heterostructure and, when combined with the spin-splitter current, significantly reduces the magnetization switching time.
