Persistent Spin Texture and Spin-Orbital Hall Responses on the AgI (110) Surface

Authors: Manish Kumar Mohanta
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02368v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the AgI (110) surface can host a robust, symmetry-protected persistent spin texture, expanding the known materials platform for spintronics from chalcogenides to halides. It shows that these spin textures are highly tunable via electric fields and strain, offering a new way to control spin and orbital transport.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether halide semiconductors can host symmetry-protected persistent spin textures (PST) and exploring the tunability of spin-orbital Hall responses via strain and electric fields.

Main result. The AgI (110) surface is found to host a robust PST that is resistant to strain and structural distortion, but can be transitioned to a Rashba-type texture by a vertical electric field.

Method. First-principles DFT calculations using Quantum Espresso and Wannier90, combined with analytical modeling using effective spin-orbit coupled Hamiltonians and the Kubo formula.

Model / system. The (110) surface of gamma-phase Silver Iodide (AgI), a non-centrosymmetric and nonsymmorphic zinc-blende structure, modeled via an effective Hamiltonian capturing anisotropic spin splitting.

Key observables. Spin Hall conductivity (SHC), orbital Hall conductivity (OHC), spin-projected band structure, spin-projected Fermi surface, and momentum offset (Q).

Important parameters / regimes. Bandgap of 1.85 eV, spin-orbit coupling constants (0.3-0.48 eV-Angstrom), biaxial strain (up to 4%), and vertical electric field strength.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the stability of the surface structure and relies on the accuracy of the pseudoDojo library pseudopotentials and the density functional approximation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows surface geometry, phonon stability, and electronic band structures; Figure 2 displays spin-projected bands and Fermi surfaces; Figure 6-8 illustrate the modulation of bands and Hall conductivities under strain and electric fields; Figure 9 details multilayer AgI properties.

Paper structure. The paper begins with structural and stability analysis, moves to the identification of the PST and its electronic properties, develops analytical models for the spin-orbit interaction, investigates the effects of external perturbations (strain and electric fields), and concludes with the study of multilayer configurations.

Abstract

A systematic investigation of the structural, electronic, and spin-orbital transport properties of the AgI (110) surface is presented using first-principles calculations combined with analytical modelling. The non-centrosymmetric and nonsymmorphic nature of the system gives rise to a robust persistent spin texture (PST), characterized by a unidirectional spin configuration and suppressed spin relaxation, enabling an effectively infinite spin lifetime. Unlike previously reported PST materials, which are predominantly based on chalcogen compounds, this work demonstrates that a halide semiconductor can host PST, thereby significantly expanding the materials platform for spintronic applications. The underlying mechanism is captured using an effective spin-orbit coupled Hamiltonian, which reproduces the anisotropic spin splitting and momentum shift observed in the band structure. This work introduces two new analytical models describing PST and compares them with existing models, offering new perspectives on PST arising from spin-orbit interaction. In addition, the system exhibits sizable intrinsic spin Hall conductivity (SHC) and orbital Hall conductivity (OHC), highlighting its potential for efficient charge-to-spin and charge-to-orbital conversion. The PST is found to be robust against biaxial strain, structural distortion, and multilayer formation, while a vertical electric field breaks the symmetry protection and drives a transition to a Rashba-type spin texture. These findings establish AgI (110) as a promising platform for realizing long-lived spin transport and tunable spin-orbit functionalities in the low-dimensional halide systems.

Photovoltaic creation of charged domain walls in barium titanate

Summary. This paper demonstrates how UV light and an electric field can be used to create conductive channels (charged domain walls) in insulating barium titanate. The researchers show that the bulk photovoltaic effect is the key driver for this structural transformation, offering a way to create reconfigurable electronic and opto-electronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the mechanism behind the transformation of neutral domain walls (NDWs) into conductive charged domain walls (CDWs) in barium titanate under UV illumination and an electric field.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the bulk photovoltaic effect (BPVE) drives the conversion of NDWs to CDWs by providing necessary charge screening at the walls, a process supported by both experimental current measurements and phase-field simulations.

Method. The study combines experimental optical observation and current-voltage measurements on BTO crystals with phase-field simulations using the finite element method (COMSOL) and a Landau-Ginzburg-Devonshire framework.

Model / system. The physical system is barium titanate (BaTiO3) single crystals with an initial structure of neutral domain walls parallel to the (110) plane, subjected to UV illumination and a DC electric field.

Key observables. Domain wall configuration (NDW vs. CDW), photocurrent (J), photovoltaic coefficient, and current-voltage (I-V) characteristics.

Important parameters / regimes. UV wavelength (365 nm), electric field strength (~4 kV/cm), light intensity (~10 mW/cm2), and time scales for CDW formation (10 minutes to 2 hours).

Assumptions / limitations. The simulation assumes homogeneous, through-the-sample generation of photo-carriers and treats light polarization as random and uniform.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows experimental geometry and the resulting CDW stripes; Fig 2 shows the time-series evolution of domain structures; Fig 4 and 5 illustrate the schematic mechanism and stages of CDW growth; Fig 6 shows phase-field simulation results of domain evolution.

Paper structure. The paper begins with experimental setup and domain engineering, moves to the observation of current increases and domain transitions, proposes a mechanism based on the bulk photovoltaic effect, validates this via phase-field simulations, and concludes with a theoretical derivation of the photovoltaic coefficients.

Abstract

The optical control of domain structures in ferroelectrics is of great interest. In the present work, we demonstrate the reliable creation of charged domain walls - conductive channels in otherwise insulating barium titanate - by a combined effect of light and electric field. We propose a scenario for the documented process, in which the bulk photovoltaic effect plays the key role, providing charge screening at the walls. Our scenario is supported by the results of phase-field simulations. The results are of interest for future reconfigurable electronic and opto-electronic devices.

Physics-Constrained Learning of Dose-Dependent Spectral Degradation in Metal--Organic Frameworks from In Situ Low-Loss EELS

Authors: Gabriel T. dos Santos, Roberto dos Reis, Vinayak P. Dravid
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01697v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a machine learning framework using Physics-Informed Neural Networks to quantify how electron-beam irradiation degrades the chemical structure of MOFs. By encoding kinetic power-law equations into the neural network, the authors can extract meaningful degradation rates from sparse, noisy spectroscopic data. This approach allows for distinguishing between direct bond breaking and complex spectral redistribution during material damage.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Quantifying the dose-dependent degradation of beam-sensitive Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs) during in situ electron microscopy characterization.

Main result. A Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) successfully models the kinetic degradation of C-O and C-C bonds in MIL-101(Fe), identifying a half-integrity threshold near 1.0e3 e-/A2 and revealing oscillator-strength redistribution in the pi-pi* window.

Method. The researchers use a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) constrained by a power-law ordinary differential equation, monotonicity, and a hierarchy prior to model spectral evolution from EELS data.

Model / system. The physical system is the Metal-Organic Framework MIL-101(Fe) studied via in situ low-loss Electron Energy-Loss Spectroscopy (EELS) under 300 kV electron-beam irradiation.

Key observables. Window-integrated low-loss spectral areas for pi-pi* (1-3 eV), C-C (4-7 eV), C-O (10-15 eV), and M-O (20-25 eV) energy windows.

Important parameters / regimes. Degradation rate constants (ki), kinetic exponents (pi), and the half-integrity dose threshold (Phi_1/2).

Assumptions / limitations. The descriptors are relative window-integrated areas rather than absolute electron numbers; the model is applied to a very sparse dataset of only nine dose frames.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the PINN framework; Figure 2 displays in situ EELS spectra; Figure 3 presents inferred latent integrity trajectories and heatmaps; Figure 4 shows inferred degradation rates and exponents; Supplementary figures show ensemble predictions and ablation studies against unconstrained models.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of beam damage, describes the experimental EELS setup, details the PINN architecture and physics-based loss functions, presents the results of bond-specific degradation, and concludes with an ablation study comparing the PINN to standard neural networks.

Abstract

Electron-beam irradiation limits atomic-resolution characterization of beam-sensitive hybrid materials, yet quantitative models that connect \textit{in situ} spectroscopy to dose-dependent degradation remain scarce. Here we use a physics-informed neural network (PINN) to model beam-induced spectral evolution in MIL-101(Fe) from an in situ low-loss electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS) dose series. Each spectrum is reduced to fixed-window low-loss descriptors, $\tilde n_{\mathrm{eff},j}(Φ)=\int_{\mathcal{W}_j}S(E,Φ)\,dE$, evaluated over nominal $π$--$π^{*}$, C--C, C--O, and M--O windows. These descriptors are relative window-integrated low-loss spectral areas, not absolute f-sum-rule effective electron numbers. For each spectral channel, a latent integrity variable $C_i(Φ)$ obeys the same uncoupled power-law degradation equation in normalized dose space, $dC_i/dφ=-k_i C_i^{p_i}$, regularized by monotonicity, boundedness, and a single hierarchy prior $k_{\mathrm{C\text{-}O}}\geq k_{\mathrm{C\text{-}C}}$. Applied to nine dose frames spanning 152--1368~e$^-$/Å$^2$, the ensemble PINN identifies C--O and C--C as the most strongly dose-sensitive linker-associated channels, with half-integrity thresholds of approximately $1.0\times10^3$~e$^-$/Å$^2$. The 1--3~eV $π$--$π^{*}$-labelled window increases with dose and is therefore interpreted as a mixed low-energy response, likely involving oscillator-strength redistribution rather than direct monotonic loss of a single bond population. The framework provides a dose-dependent, spectroscopy constrained description of MOF degradation while also defining the limits of what fixed-window low-loss EELS can assign without independent chemical-state validation.

Polymorphic crystallites model for monolayer amorphous materials

Authors: Le-Ye Zhu, Xi Zhang, Yun-Peng Wang, Jieheng Shi, Junwei Zhang, Shixuan Du, Yu-Yang Zhang
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01881v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new model for 2D amorphous materials, suggesting they are composed of stable, distinct crystalline motifs embedded in a random network rather than being purely disordered. Using machine learning potentials and kinetic Monte Carlo, the authors demonstrate that this polymorphic crystallite model is a general and accurate way to describe various multi-element monolayers.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of accurately modeling the atomic-scale structure and stability of monolayer amorphous materials, which lack long-range order and involve complex, competing structural motifs.

Main result. The authors propose a 'polymorphic crystallite model' where amorphous 2D materials consist of stable, randomly distributed crystallites (e.g., o-B2N2 and o-B4N4 in maBN) within a continuous random network, a model validated across maBN, maLiCl, and maBCN systems.

Method. An energy-driven kinetic Monte Carlo (EDKMC) framework combined with an active-learning-based machine learning potential (MLP) trained from first-principles DFT energies.

Model / system. The study focuses on multi-element 2D amorphous monolayers, specifically amorphous boron nitride (maBN), lithium chloride (maLiCl), and boron carbon nitride (maBCN), modeled via a polymorphic crystallite paradigm.

Key observables. Degree of disorder (DOD), ring statistics (5-, 6-, and 7-membered rings), density of states (DOS), and atomic forces/energy differences.

Important parameters / regimes. Simulation temperature (reference parameter), kBT (0.5 eV to 3 eV), and the degree of disorder (DOD).

Assumptions / limitations. The EDKMC approach assumes the melting process must be avoided to prevent irreversible transitions to 3D structures.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the EDKMC workflow and MLP accuracy validation; Figure 2 compares atomic configurations of different models and identifies specific structural motifs; Supplementary figures show convergence, stability, and electronic properties.

Paper structure. Introduction of the modeling challenge; description of the EDKMC active-learning framework and MLP training; application to maBN to propose the polymorphic crystallite model; validation of the model in maLiCl and maBCN; discussion of stability and electronic properties.

Abstract

Modeling the atomic structure of amorphous materials has long been a critical challenge in materials science. Recent advances in monolayer amorphous materials enable direct observation of their atomic structures, paving the way for a better understanding of their atomic-scale models. Here, we investigate amorphous multielement monolayers using machine learning potential from first-principles total energies via energy-driven kinetic Monte Carlo based active-learning framework. A polymorphic crystallite model is proposed to describe the atomic configuration of monolayer amorphous boron nitride, as it consists of coexisting crystallite of $o-B_2N_2$ and $o-B_4N_4$ structural motifs. Generality of the polymorphic crystallite model is further validated in two other multielement monolayer amorphous systems. Monolayer amorphous LiCl shows coexisting hexagonal and tetragonal crystallites, while monolayer amorphous BCN contains a combination of graphene-like, h-BN-like, and borophene-like crystallites. These findings expand the classical picture of amorphous structure models and offer new insight into the microscopic structure of amorphous materials.

Summary. This study investigates Chromium-coated Zircaloy-4 as a potential material for accident-tolerant nuclear fuel cladding. The researchers found that the Cr coating effectively delays oxidation at high temperatures, though thermal excursions cause complex interfacial changes like Fe segregation and nano-bubble formation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature range from RT to 1100C, coating thickness of ~10 micrometers, and oxidation environments (air, steam, and vacuum).

Abstract

Following the Fukushima Daiichi disaster, an increasing number of studies concentrate on the development of Accident Tolerant Fuel (ATF) cladding materials for nuclear fuel, aiming to prevent the oxidation of zirconium during incidents such as Loss of Coolant Accident (LOCA) and to effectively lower the amount of heat and hydrogen released during emergency core cooling (ECC). Zirconium alloy cladding with a protective chromium (Cr) coating is considered one of the promising candidates, largely due to its relatively short timeline for deployment in nuclear power plants. In this study, Cr-coated and uncoated Zircaloy-4 claddings were evaluated using high temperature X-ray diffraction (HT-XRD) in vacuum over a temperature range from RT to 1100oC. The temperatures corresponding to the formation of oxide phases are >200oC and >600oC for the uncoated and Cr-coated samples, respectively. SEM and TEM characterisation of the sub-surface in Cr-coated specimen after HT-XRD revealed Fe segregation, formation of Zr(Fe,Cr)2 Laves phase and nano-bubbles at the former Cr / Z4 interface.

Quantum Limits of Electronic Transport in Nanostructured Macroscopic Conductors

Authors: Agnieszka E. Lekawa-Raus, John S. Bulmer, Teresa Kulka, Magdalena Marganska, Nick Papior, Dwight G. Rickel, Fedor F. Balakirev, Jacek A. Majewski, Krzysztof Koziol, Karolina Z. Milowska
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02295v1

Summary. This paper provides a new atomistic framework to explain how quantum interference at junctions governs the macroscopic electrical properties of carbon nanotube networks. By combining theory with high-field measurements, the authors show that transport is driven by junction-level physics rather than simple defects or doping. This offers a way to bridge the gap between nanoscale electronic properties and device-scale performance.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The microscopic principles governing charge transport in macroscopic assemblies of 1D and 2D materials remain unresolved, as current phenomenological models fail to connect electronic structure to macroscopic magnetotransport.

Main result. The study demonstrates that macroscopic transport in disordered low-dimensional networks is governed by junction-level quantum interference, where positive magnetoresistance is controlled by junction overlap length and negative magnetoresistance arises from lattice-mismatched heterojunctions.

Method. The authors develop a unified atomistic framework linking quantum-coherent transport, thermal disorder, and magnetic-field effects, which is then validated against ultrahigh-field magnetotransport measurements up to 60 T.

Model / system. The physical system consists of macroscopic assemblies of carbon nanotube fibres, treated as disordered networks of one- and two-dimensional materials.

Key observables. Magnetoresistance (positive and negative), conductivity, and junction transport properties.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic field strength (up to 60 T), temperature range, junction overlap length, and lattice mismatch.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of transport in macroscopic 1D/2D networks, presents a new atomistic theoretical framework, describes ultrahigh-field experimental measurements on carbon nanotube fibres, and concludes with a statistical analysis of the data to identify the origin of magnetoresistance.

Abstract

Macroscopic assemblies of one- and two-dimensional materials promise to translate nanoscale electronic properties into device-scale performance, yet the microscopic principles governing charge transport in such networks remain unresolved. In these systems, conductivity is often interpreted using phenomenological models that do not explicitly connect electronic structure to macroscopic magnetotransport. Here we develop a unified atomistic framework that links quantum-coherent transport, thermal disorder and magnetic-field effects, and combine it with ultrahigh-field magnetotransport measurements up to 60 T over a broad temperature range on carbon nanotube fibres. We show that positive magnetoresistance is controlled by junction overlap length, whereas negative magnetoresistance arises predominantly from lattice-mismatched heterojunctions rather than weak localisation alone. Statistical analysis of a large-scale numerical dataset reveals that the experimentally observed positive quadratic magnetoresistance originates from junction transport. These results show that macroscopic transport in disordered low-dimensional networks is governed primarily by junction-level quantum interference rather than solely by defects or doping.

Rational Mechanics of Material Strength in Brittle Solids

Authors: Arash Yavari, Aditya Kumar
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01156v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes a rigorous mathematical framework for defining material strength in brittle solids undergoing large deformations. It shows that for the theory to be physically consistent (covariant), strength must be a function of both stress and strain rather than stress alone. The work provides a unified description that encompasses classical linear elasticity, anisotropy, and the effects of residual stresses.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a mathematically consistent and representation-independent definition of material strength in the framework of finite elasticity, particularly regarding how strength depends on both stress and strain.

Main result. The authors prove that a covariant strength function must depend on both stress and strain measures to ensure spatial covariance, and they demonstrate that for isotropic solids, the safe domain is star-shaped under a proportional-reduction hypothesis.

Method. A geometric and symmetry-theoretic approach using rational mechanics, finite elasticity, and invariant theory under spatial diffeomorphisms.

Model / system. Brittle solids (ranging from glass/ceramics to elastomers) modeled within the framework of nonlinear finite elasticity, including isotropic, anisotropic, and anelastic regimes.

Key observables. Strength function, strength hypersurface, and the safe domain.

Important parameters / regimes. Stress measures (Cauchy, Piola-Kirchhoff), strain measures, material symmetry groups, and eigenstrains.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes a hyperelastic framework, focuses on strength-based criteria, and assumes that sufficiently large stresses are inadmissible.

Figures summary. A figure showing the eigenvalues of the Biot stress for isotropic solids.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of strength in finite elasticity, moves to a covariant formulation using stress and strain pairs, analyzes the geometric properties of the strength hypersurface and safe domain, extends the theory to anisotropic and anelastic solids, and finally shows the reduction to classical linear elastic criteria in the small-strain limit.

Abstract

Material strength is a classical concept with renewed importance in fracture mechanics, particularly in crack nucleation in brittle solids. We formulate material strength in finite elasticity and examine its geometric, constitutive, and symmetry-theoretic foundations. Spatial covariance requires a strength function to depend on both stress and the corresponding strain measure, so that strength is governed by the pair (stress,strain), not stress alone, and only then can representations based on different stress measures be consistently related, with classical stress-based criteria recovered as a special case. We analyze covariance under spatial diffeomorphisms and relate formulations based on the first Piola--Kirchhoff, second Piola--Kirchhoff, and Cauchy stresses. For stress-based criteria, we define the strength hypersurface as a subset of the constitutively admissible stress manifold and study the associated safe domain. Under standard regularity assumptions and the requirement that sufficiently large stresses are inadmissible, the strength surface is a smooth compact hypersurface of this manifold. For isotropic solids, we show that the safe domain is star-shaped under a proportional-reduction hypothesis. We extend the formulation to anelastic brittle solids, showing that residual stresses and eigenstrains modify the strength surface through the material metric, and discuss anisotropic strength via material symmetry. Finally, in the small-strain limit, the theory reduces to classical stress-based criteria.

Revealing the kinetics of interfacial surfactant phase transitions through multiscale simulations and in-situ plasmonic sensing

Summary. This paper develops a multiscale method to monitor surfactant phase transitions in real-time using plasmonic sensing. By linking molecular-scale hydration to macroscopic optical shifts, the researchers can identify specific interfacial morphologies and their formation kinetics. This provides a robust, label-free way to study complex soft-matter interfaces.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of resolving the structural morphologies and transition kinetics of surfactant phases at solid-liquid interfaces in situ.

Method. A multiscale approach combining atomistic Molecular Dynamics (MD) for morphology, Density Functional Theory (DFT) for dielectric properties, and Finite-Difference Time-Domain (FDTD) simulations for optical response, validated by in-situ LSPR sensing.

Paper structure. Abstract

Surfactant self-assembly at solid-liquid interfaces governs interfacial stability, transport, and reactivity across many technologies, yet resolving interfacial surfactant phases and their transition kinetics in situ remains challenging. Here, we establish an atomistically grounded plasmonic framework that quantitatively maps interfacial surfactant phases and phase transitions onto optical signatures. Distinct morphologies differ in packing and hydration, modifying the effective permittivity within the optical near field and producing surfactant phase-specific plasmonic extinction peak shifts. Using cetyltrimethylammonium bromide on silica as a prototypical surfactant-surface system, we combine atomistic simulations, electronic-structure calculations, and continuum electrodynamics to translate molecular morphologies into predicted spectral shifts for literature-reported surface phases. We experimentally confirm the predicted ordering and magnitude of steady-state peak shifts during stepwise concentration changes, and extract transition kinetics from exponential relaxations of the time-resolved peak shift. A key mechanistic signature is reversal of the spectral shift direction upon transition from an impermeable bilayer to a water-accessible, channel-containing phase, consistent with hydration-driven reduction of the local effective permittivity. Because the approach relies on dielectric contrast in the plasmonic near field and works through a dielectric overlayer, it provides a broadly applicable route for real-time identification of interfacial surfactant phases and their kinetics in aqueous conditions.

Reversible fully spin polarization in strain-engineered two-dimensional fully compensated magnets

Authors: Xiuli Zhang, Peng Jiang, Yurui Ma, Xiaodong Zhou, Linlin Liu, Hong-Mei Huang, San-Dong Guo, Tengfei Cao, Yan-Ling Li

Summary. This paper demonstrates that applying uniaxial strain to 2D altermagnets can break magnetic symmetries to induce a fully spin-polarized ferrimagnetic state. By controlling the direction of the strain, researchers can reversibly switch the spin polarization, providing a new framework for developing efficient, strain-controlled spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of achieving controllable and reversible full spin polarization in symmetry-compensated magnets, such as altermagnets, where crystalline symmetries enforce spin degeneracy.

Main result. Uniaxial strain can break the symmetry between magnetic sublattices, driving a transition from an altermagnetic state to a fully compensated ferrimagnetic state that enables 100% spin polarization and reversible spin switching.

Method. The study uses symmetry and spin-group analysis, a minimal tight-binding model, first-principles DFT calculations, Boltzmann transport theory, and ab initio molecular dynamics.

Model / system. The study focuses on 2D altermagnetic (AM) and ferroelastic fully compensated ferrimagnetic (fFIM) systems, specifically identifying monolayer Mn2SeO and V2SO as candidate materials.

Key observables. Spin polarization percentage, spin-polarized band structure, integrated density of states (IDOS), spin-dependent electronic occupation, and spin-resolved longitudinal conductivities.

Important parameters / regimes. Uniaxial strain (compressive/tensile), strain direction (orthogonal x and y), and the transition from AM to fFIM states.

Assumptions / limitations. The mechanism is assumed to be general for 2D systems where uniaxial strain can induce sublattice inequivalence.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the illustration of strain engineering and Brillouin zones; Fig 2 displays the atomic structure, spin density, and spin-polarized transport for Mn2SeO; Fig 3 demonstrates the reversal of spin polarization in Mn2SeO under different strain directions; Fig 4 compares the ferroelastic configurations of V2SO.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of spin degeneracy in compensated magnets, presents a symmetry-based mechanism using a tight-binding model, validates the mechanism with first-principles calculations on Mn2SeO and V2SO, and concludes with the implications for spintronic applications.

Abstract

Achieving controllable spin polarization and its reversal in symmetry-compensated magnets. Here we demonstrate, using symmetry analysis and a minimal tight-binding model, that uniaxial strain removes these constraints by inducing inequivalence between magnetic sublattices in two-dimensional (2D) system, driving an altermagnetic (AM) state into a fully compensated ferrimagnetic (fFIM) state and enabling fully spin polarization. Furthermore, strain along orthogonal directions gives rise to two energetically degenerate fFIM states with opposite spin polarization, enabling reversible spin switching. More importantly, the two symmetry-related fFIM states can be regarded as distinct ferroelastic variants, suggesting that this model or mechanism can be extended to ferroelastic fFIM systems. The generality of this mechanism is confirmed by combining spin-group analysis, first-principles calculations, and Boltzmann transport theory in representative candidates, including AM Mn$_2$SeO and ferroelastic fFIM V$_2$SO. Our results reveal a universal symmetry-driven framework for strain-controlled and -reversible fully spin-polarized transport and identify strain-engineered AM and ferroelastic fFIM systems as a promising platform for volatile and nonvolatile spintronic applications.

Revisiting the surface density of states of midgap Andreev edge states

Summary. This paper explains why the zero-energy peak in p-wave superconductors remains sharp despite surface roughness, unlike in d-wave superconductors where it broadens into a V-shape. The difference is attributed to the fact that d-wave states involve scattering between two different edge modes, whereas p-wave states involve only a single mode.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Abstract

Colloidal quantum dots (QDs) and their thin-films are increasingly used in electronic and photonic devices replacing traditional bulk semiconductors. However, thermal properties of the QDs are comparatively underexplored relative to device development efforts. This study shows the use of time-resolved X-ray diffraction as a contact-free method to probe the thermal response of QDs in device-like environments, providing in-situ insights for future thermal management strategies. Through the extraction of Debye-Waller Factors on a sub-nanosecond timescale, we use time-resolved X-ray diffraction to directly capture the heating and cooling of core/shell CdSe/CdS QDs following pulsed optical excitation. In a QD thin-film that actively provides optical gain, the thermal conductivity is found to be as low as 0.55 $\mathrm{W\,m^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$, because of the poor heat flow within close-packed QD solids. For QDs dispersed in liquids, interfacial thermal conductance is found to dominate the thermal relaxation with a conductance on the order of 15 $\mathrm{MW\,m^{-2}\,K^{-1}}$.

Abstract

We report on the pressure dependence of superconducting transition temperature Tc and upper critical field Bc2(0) through electrical transport of the Ti4Co2O superconductor (eg.,the superconducting transition temperature Tc = 2.5 K and the Bc2(0)=7.2T=2.9Tc). We find that the Tc exhibits non-monotonic pressure dependence:it rises monotonically at first with a pressure coefficient of dTc/dP=0.034 K/GPa, but rapidly decreases around 10-20 GPa, and then increases with the dTc/dP = 0.023 K/GPa, up to= 4.31 K at 69.7 GPa. Concurrently, the Bc2(0)exhibits a dome shaped pressure dependence, with its maximum at 5 GPa of almost twice the value at ambient pressure, exceeding the weak-coupling Pauli paramagnetic limit Bp throughout the whole pressure range. By comparing the normal-state and superconducting properties, we identify two distinct superconducting regimes, with a low-pressure superconducting phase characterized by an enhanced Bc2(0)values and Fermi-liquid normal-state electrical transport (the exponent n = 2), and a high-pressure superconducting phase with a monotonically increased Tc and an enhancement in phonon scatterings (the exponent n = 4). Room-temperature synchrotron X-ray diffraction indicates that there is no structural transition up to 55.8 GPa, which gives a relatively large bulk modulus of 192 GPa in comparison with other alloy superconductors. First-principles calculations suggest that the nonmonotonic Tc maybe closely related to the evolution of the density of states of Ti4Co2O upon compression, which is different from those of isostructural superconductors Ti4Ir2O and Nb4Rh2C. Our results show that even in the Ti4Co2O with weak spin-orbit coupling, superconductivity remains highly sensitive to the external stimuli such as pressure.
