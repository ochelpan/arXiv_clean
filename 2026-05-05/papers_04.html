Entanglement Generation During Distribution via Spatial Superposition Entanglement Generation

Authors: Claudio Pellitteri, Rajiuddin Sk, Marcello Caleffi, Angela Sara Cacciapuoti
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02564v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · interference shaping light 3/5

Summary. This paper shows that by superposing different communication paths, one can use quantum noise to generate entanglement between previously separable states. It proves that Bell and GHZ states can be created deterministically even in highly noisy or entanglement-breaking regimes. This provides a new method for distributed entanglement engineering in quantum networks.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems as it proposes a paradigm shift where environmental noise, typically seen as a source of decoherence, is actively utilized as a constructive resource for entanglement engineering.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of entanglement degradation due to noise during distribution in quantum networks and whether quantum noise can be harnessed as a resource for entanglement generation.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the coherent superposition of spatially distinct, noisy communication links can deterministically transform separable states into entangled Bell, GHZ, and W states, even in extreme noise regimes.

Method. The study uses a mathematical framework of spatial superposition of channels, employing Kraus operators, vacuum extensions, and optimization of vacuum amplitudes to engineer interference patterns.

Model / system. A quantum communication framework involving the coherent superposition of spatially distinct links (e.g., via interferometric setups like Sagnac or Mach-Zehnder) where a control system (qubit/qudit) governs the propagation of a target system through noisy channels.

Key observables. Fidelity (to Bell, GHZ, and W states) and Concurrence.

Important parameters / regimes. Noise probabilities (p, q) of depolarizing and bit/phase-flip channels, and vacuum amplitudes (alpha, beta) representing the physical configuration of the links.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that any relative phases introduced by different measurement outcomes or system sizes can be corrected via local unitary operations at the receiver.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a photonic setup for spatial superposition; Figure 2 and 3 illustrate the superposition of two and three links respectively; Figure 4 shows fidelity vs. noise probability; Figure 5 shows density plots of fidelity; Figure 6 shows concurrence vs. noise; Figure 7 and 8 show GHZ and W state generation via fidelity and concurrence.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the problem of noise-induced decoherence, proposes the spatial superposition framework, compares it to the Quantum Switch and Quantum Walks, details the deterministic generation of Bell, GHZ, and W states, and provides analytical proofs and numerical optimizations for various noise regimes.

Abstract

The exploitation of quantum coherence at the level of propagation represents a powerful paradigm for quantum communication networks. In this work, we show that the coherent superposition of spatially distinct communication links enables entanglement generation inherently during distribution. Specifically, separable quantum states can be deterministically transformed into entangled states, when the noisy communication links they traverse are coherently superposed. Contrary to the conventional view of noise as a detrimental effect, we demonstrate that quantum noise itself can be transformed into a constructive resource for entanglement generation for both bipartite and multipartite entanglement. Given the practical feasibility of implementing spatial superposition in interferometric setups, our approach provides a feasible method for distributed entanglement engineering, opening new directions for quantum communication and networked quantum technologies.

System driven out-of equilibrium by weak contacts with reservoirs

Authors: Thierry Bodineau, Bernard Derrida
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01900v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the dimensionality of a system affects the transport properties of particles driven by reservoirs. It identifies a breakdown of universality in 3D for microscopic contacts but proposes that mesoscopic-sized reservoirs restore predictable macroscopic behavior.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the dimensionality and geometry of reservoir contacts influence the current and non-equilibrium behavior of particle systems, specifically addressing why universality fails in 3D for point contacts.

Main result. The authors show that in 2D, three coupling regimes exist, whereas in 3D and higher, only a weak coupling regime exists that is sensitive to microscopic contact structure; however, universality is restored for mesoscopic reservoirs.

Method. The study utilizes the Green function approach, scaling analysis of reservoir rates, and the macroscopic fluctuation theory (additivity principle) to analyze steady-state density and current.

Model / system. The Symmetric Simple Exclusion Process (SSEP) on a d-dimensional lattice connected to reservoirs at specific sites or mesoscopic regions.

Key observables. Mean current (J), particle density profiles, and current fluctuations (large deviations/cumulants).

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension (d=1, 2, or >=3), coupling strength (A, B), reservoir densities (rho_a, rho_b), and contact size (point vs. mesoscopic).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the SSEP model in a steady state and assumes the validity of the additivity principle for mesoscopic reservoirs.

Figures summary. Figure 2 illustrates three mesoscopic reservoirs in a bulk, showing localized density variations near the reservoirs and a constant bulk density.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the SSEP model and reservoir dynamics, moves to a dimension-dependent analysis of coupling regimes (1D, 2D, 3D), explores current fluctuations and large deviations, and concludes with an extension of the additivity principle for multiple mesoscopic reservoirs.

Abstract

The non-equilibrium behavior of particle systems driven by reservoirs has been extensively studied in recent years. In one dimension, various regimes have been explored depending on the coupling strength to the reservoirs. In this paper, we investigate the role of the dimension and of the geometry of the contacts with the reservoirs. For the symmetric simple exclusion process with point contact reservoirs, we show that in dimension 2, as in one dimension, three different regimes occur depending on the coupling strength. On the other hand in dimensions 3 and higher, there exists only a weak coupling regime which is very sensitive to the microscopic structure of the contacts. We then argue that for reservoirs with mesoscopic size contacts the macroscopic fluctuation theory remains in force and we propose an extension of the additivity principle for multiple mesoscopic reservoirs.

A Critical Assessment of the Sample-Based Quantum Diagonalization for Heisenberg and Hubbard Models

Authors: Cedric Gaberle, Manpreet Singh Jattana
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02494v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that Sample-Based Quantum Diagonalization (SQD) faces an exponential scaling bottleneck for Heisenberg and Hubbard models. The authors prove that this difficulty arises from the intrinsic complexity and delocalization of the many-body wavefunctions rather than errors in quantum sampling. Consequently, successful scalable subspace methods will likely require more sophisticated basis constructions beyond simple configuration sampling.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for researchers developing quantum algorithms for many-body physics, as it identifies a fundamental scaling barrier for a specific class of hybrid quantum-classical subspace expansion methods.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates whether Sample-Based Quantum Diagonalization (SQD) is fundamentally scalable by determining if the required number of computational basis configurations grows polynomially or exponentially with system size.

Main result. The number of configurations required for accurate energy estimation grows exponentially with system size, a limitation driven by the intrinsic delocalization and configuration-space entropy of the many-body wavefunction rather than sampling inefficiencies.

Method. The authors use an idealized numerical approach, comparing deterministic inclusion of configurations (ordered by probability) against stochastic sampling using exact ground-state wavefunctions from classical diagonalization.

Model / system. The study focuses on 1D and 2D Heisenberg (antiferromagnetic spin-1/2) and Hubbard (fermionic, intermediate coupling U=2t) models on various lattice geometries.

Key observables. Ground-state energy fidelity (FE), configuration count (k), Shannon entropy (S), and cumulative probability mass.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (L), energy fidelity thresholds (90%, 95%, 99%), and interaction strength (U/t).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes access to the exact ground-state wavefunction to isolate intrinsic wavefunction properties from errors in state preparation and measurement.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows exponential growth of required samples vs. system size for Heisenberg models; Figure 4 plots energy fidelity against cumulative probability mass; Figure 5 demonstrates that high-probability configurations are efficiently sampled, ruling out sampling inefficiency.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SQD framework, defines the idealized simulation setup, presents scaling analysis for Heisenberg and Hubbard models, provides evidence that the bottleneck is intrinsic wavefunction delocalization, and concludes with implications for future subspace methods.

Abstract

Sample-based quantum diagonalization (SQD) constructs subspaces from computational-basis configurations obtained via measurements of a quantum state, with the goal of approximating low-energy eigenspaces of many-body Hamiltonians. The effectiveness of this approach relies on the assumption that physically relevant states admit a compact representation in the computational basis. We investigate this assumption by analyzing SQD subspaces constructed directly from configurations of exact ground states of Heisenberg and Hubbard model lattices. By eliminating state-preparation and measurement inefficiencies, we isolate the intrinsic configuration-space structure of the wavefunction. We determine the minimal number of configurations required to reproduce the ground-state energy within fixed accuracy thresholds and find that this number grows exponentially with the system size. Notably, this scaling persists even under optimal inclusion of configurations in order of decreasing probability, demonstrating that it originates from intrinsic delocalization of the wavefunction rather than sampling inefficiencies. Our results indicate that SQD effectively probes the configuration-space entropy but faces fundamental scalability limitations for these models.

Abstract

We demonstrate a delay-programmable two-color femtosecond source based on a chirped-seed noncollinear optical parametric amplifier. Introducing controlled dispersion into the seed enables spectral selection through pump-seed delay, allowing flexible generation of two independently tunable pulse components with adjustable relative timing at high repetition rate. The temporal and spectral properties are characterized using nonlinear optical cross-correlation and dispersion-scan measurements. As a benchmark application, the source is employed in a COLTRIMS-based multiphoton ionization experiment on trapped Li atoms, revealing delay-dependent ionization pathways and demonstrating its suitability for bichromatic ultrafast spectroscopy.

A measure for genuine tripartite entanglement

Authors: Shengjun Wu, Kaichen Zhong, Jeffery Wu
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02876v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a new mathematical functional that quantifies how much a three-qubit state exhibits the GHZ algebraic paradox. This allows for a clear, quantitative distinction between GHZ and W-type entanglement. The method is also shown to be extensible to higher-dimensional qudit systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new, quantitative, and device-independent tool for characterizing multipartite entanglement, which is crucial for verifying GHZ-type states in photonic or trapped-ion quantum computing platforms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a quantitative, device-independent measure to identify and distinguish genuine tripartite entanglement, specifically focusing on GHZ-type correlations.

Main result. The authors introduce a functional $I$ that acts as a witness for GHZ-type entanglement, reaching a maximum of 2 for GHZ states and strictly less for W states, with a derived measure $E_{GHZ}$ that ranges from 0 to 1.

Method. The study uses algebraic construction of a real-valued functional from Pauli correlation expectation values, analytical optimization over measurement directions, and parameterization via the Acin canonical family.

Model / system. Three-qubit quantum systems, with proposed generalizations to three-qudit systems using Heisenberg-Weyl operators and spin-S systems.

Key observables. Three-qubit correlation expectation values (Pauli spin observables) and the functional $I(\vec{n}_1, \vec{n}_2)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Measurement directions $\vec{n}_1, \vec{n}_2$; the Acin canonical family parameters; the dimension $d$ for qudit generalization.

Assumptions / limitations. The measure is analyzed primarily within the context of local unitary equivalence and the GHZ/W entanglement classes; monotonicity under the full SLOCC hierarchy is not yet established.

Figures summary. Fig 1(a) shows the monotonic increase of the functional relative to state parameters; Fig 1(b) compares the supremum of the functional across different entanglement classes (GHZ, W, biseparable, and product states).

Paper structure. Introduction of the functional, proof of the universal upper bound, analysis of the Acin canonical family, comparison with the W state, generalization to qudits, and discussion of experimental feasibility.

Abstract

We introduce a single real-valued functional $I(\vec{n}_1,\vec{n}_2)$, built from four three-qubit correlation expectation values, that turns the Greenberger--Horne--Zeilinger (GHZ) algebraic paradox into a \emph{quantitative} witness of genuine tripartite entanglement. We prove that for every three-qubit state $ρ$ and every pair of measurement directions $|I(\vec{n}_1,\vec{n}_2;ρ)|\le 2$, with the bound saturated if and only if the two measurement bases are mutually unbiased and $ρ$ is locally unitarily equivalent to the GHZ state. We obtain a closed-form expression for $I(\hat{x},\hat{y})$ on the five-parameter Acín canonical family of three-qubit pure states. For the W state we show that $I(\hat{x},\hat{y})=0$ and that $\max_{\vec{n}_1,\vec{n}_2}| I_{W}|=35/27\approx 1.296$, strictly below the GHZ value. The induced quantity ranges in $[0,1]$, equals one only on the GHZ class, and is therefore a device-independent indicator of GHZ-type genuine tripartite correlation. We also outline a generalisation of $I$ to three-qudit systems built from the Heisenberg--Weyl operators, recovering the standard qubit construction when $d=2$.

Exponential speedups in fault-tolerant processing of quantum experiments

Authors: Ishaan Kannan, Harald Putterman, Jordan Cotler
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.02057v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the exponential speedups of quantum learning can be preserved despite noisy experimental interfaces by using fault-tolerant quantum error correction to 'upload' states into encoded memory. The authors prove that this method outperforms adaptive classical strategies for tasks like shadow tomography and demonstrate its utility in a simulated astronomical imaging task.

Why it may be interesting. It addresses a critical bottleneck in quantum sensing and measurement—the interface between physical samples and quantum processors—and provides a theoretical pathway to maintain quantum advantages in noisy, real-world experimental settings.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The noise introduced when coupling a quantum processor to an experimental sample can exponentially degrade the efficiency of quantum learning, even if the processor itself is fault-tolerant.

Main result. By using a 'quantum uploading' procedure to embed unknown systems into high-distance quantum codes, exponential speedups in tasks like classical shadow tomography and cubic observable estimation can be recovered, even with noisy uploading stages.

Method. The authors introduce the 'Heisenberg learning tree method' to derive learning lower bounds under noise constraints and propose a quantum uploading procedure into encoded memory.

Model / system. The paper discusses a general framework for learning from quantum experiments, specifically illustrating the application to astronomical imaging of photons to locate an exoplanet.

Key observables. Classical shadow tomography and the estimation of cubic observables.

Important parameters / regimes. Quantum code distance, noise levels in the uploading stage, and the number of experimental shots.

Assumptions / limitations. The efficiency gains assume the ability to embed the system into an arbitrarily high-distance quantum code with constant error overhead.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of noise in quantum-experimental coupling, proposes the quantum uploading method, develops the Heisenberg learning tree method for lower bounds, and provides a numerical illustration using an astronomical imaging application.

Abstract

Quantum information processing has the potential to substantially enhance how we learn from physical experiments, but coupling a quantum processor to an experimental sample introduces noise that can exponentially degrade learning even when the processor itself is fault-tolerant. In this work, we show that fault tolerance can nevertheless be leveraged to recover exponential speedups by embedding the unknown system into an arbitrarily high-distance quantum code with only constant error overhead and running a fault-tolerant learning algorithm. Using this $\textit{quantum uploading}$ procedure, we prove that both classical shadow tomography and the estimation of cubic observables can be performed exponentially faster than by any adaptive strategy that does not immediately upload the state into encoded memory. These separations hold even when the uploading stage is substantially noisier than the bare experimental interface. To prove them, we introduce the Heisenberg learning tree method, a flexible tool for obtaining learning lower bounds when the limited resource is not quantum replicas but an experimentally motivated constraint such as noise. We numerically illustrate the speedups in an astronomical imaging application, where quantum processing of individual uploaded photons locates an exoplanet obscured by a bright star using orders of magnitude fewer shots than unencoded baselines. Our results establish fault-tolerant quantum computation as a valuable tool for learning from quantum experiments.

Fixed-detector tilt--defocus sensing by upstream source coding in a time-reversed Young interferometer

Authors: Jianming Wen

Summary. The paper proposes a method to monitor beam tilt and defocus using a fixed detector by applying optimized 'source codes' upstream in a Young interferometer. It proves that using physically accurate Fresnel-based codes enables near-perfect information recovery, whereas traditional Gaussian approximations fail significantly.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and precision measurement, as it demonstrates how architectural changes (moving analysis upstream) can enable high-precision tracking of wavefront aberrations without complex downstream hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient method for simultaneous monitoring of beam tilt and focus drift in compact optical systems using a fixed-detector architecture.

Main result. Optimized upstream source codes derived from a Fresnel model allow for near-lossless information compression (retaining >99.98% of Fisher information) into just two scalar channels, significantly outperforming simple Gaussian-based toy models.

Method. The study uses a Fisher Information Matrix formalism to quantify information retention and employs a noise-weighted Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization process to derive optimal, fringe-locked source codes.

Model / system. A Time-Reversed Young (TRY) interferometer based on a monochromatic scalar field and a finite-width double-slit Fresnel diffraction model.

Key observables. Tilt-like and defocus-like phase perturbations, local response functions (sensitivity maps), and coded scalar observables.

Important parameters / regimes. Wavelength (633 nm), slit width and separation, propagation distances, and the shot-noise-limited detection regime.

Assumptions / limitations. Small phase perturbations (linearized regime), monochromatic scalar field, and shot-noise-limited detection with a regularizing background floor.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the baseline TRY intensity response, the exact local score functions for tilt and defocus, and the numerically optimized, oscillatory, fringe-locked source codes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the sensing problem, defines the physical Fresnel model, derives the analytical response functions, implements the upstream source-coding optimization, and concludes with a comparative information-theoretic analysis against downstream sensing and Gaussian models.

Abstract

We propose a physically explicit sensing application of a time-reversed Young (TRY) interferometer: simultaneous monitoring of beam tilt and focus drift with a fixed detector. The task is relevant to compact optical relays, free-space links, fiber-coupling stages, and micro-optical alignment modules, where continuous tracking of pointing and focus is needed but downstream wavefront cameras or multiport analyzers are undesirable. Using a finite-width double-slit Fresnel model, we derive the exact local TRY response functions for tilt-like and defocus-like phase perturbations and compute the corresponding optimal upstream source codes numerically. The physical optimal codes are fringe-locked and differ qualitatively from the simple odd/even modes suggested by Gaussian toy models. Two source-coded scalar channels recover essentially all local Fisher information in the full source-resolved TRY record for the physical model considered here. Compared with downstream direct intensity sensing, TRY provides first-order access to the mixed tilt--defocus task with fixed detection; compared with ideal downstream matched-mode sorting, its advantage is architectural rather than fundamental.

Generation of heat pulses in mesoscopic conductors using light fields

Summary. This paper proposes a method to generate controllable, charge-neutral heat pulses in mesoscopic conductors using light fields. By modulating the electronic temperature via light-induced changes in tunnel coupling, the authors establish a framework for energy-based quantum information processing. The results show that these pulses can be precisely controlled and even described using the Schwarzian derivative.

Why it may be interesting. It explores the intersection of non-equilibrium thermodynamics and quantum transport, specifically how time-dependent driving can be used to manipulate energy flow (caloritronics) independently of charge flow.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the need for a way to generate and control time-dependent, charge-neutral heat pulses in mesoscopic conductors to enable 'on-demand caloritronics'.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that a high-frequency light field can modulate the electronic temperature in a reservoir, creating traveling heat pulses that carry energy without net electric current.

Method. The study uses a tight-binding model, full counting statistics (FCS), and Floquet scattering theory, supplemented by Conformal Field Theory to derive quantum corrections.

Model / system. A 1D mesoscopic conductor consisting of two electronic reservoirs connected by a quantum point contact (QPC), modeled as a tight-binding chain irradiated by a monochromatic light field.

Key observables. Time-dependent heat current (Ih), electric current (Iq), heat noise, and the second cumulant of transferred charge and energy.

Important parameters / regimes. High-frequency limit (light frequency Omega much larger than tunnel coupling gamma), driving amplitude alpha, Fermi velocity vF, and base temperature T0.

Assumptions / limitations. The light field is treated in the high-frequency limit (Omega >> gamma) with a spatially uniform field approximation (wavelength lambda >> lattice spacing a).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the schematic of heat pulse generation; Figure 2 shows the effect of light on band structure; Figure 3 displays time-dependent heat current under constant light; Figure 4 and 5 show the evolution of charge and heat noise/cumulants.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of heat pulse generation, describes the tight-binding model and light-matter interaction, presents the derivation of effective temperature modulation, evaluates observables using FCS, and concludes with the implications for caloritronics.

Abstract

We propose to generate heat pulses in mesoscopic conductors using light fields. In contrast to single-electron excitations such as levitons, which are created by accurate voltage drives, our approach relies on modulating the temperature of an electronic reservoir. To this end, we show that the interactions with a light field can induce a controllable time-dependent temperature in an electrode. The temperature modulations generate charge-neutral heat pulses that can be emitted into a mesoscopic conductor and detected in the outputs. We illustrate our approach by evaluating the time-dependent currents and their fluctuations using a tight-binding model of two electronic reservoirs connected by a quantum point contact. Our work establishes a route towards on-demand caloritronics, where energy rather than charge carries quantum information, and it paves the way for probing time-resolved heat transport and quantum coherence in thermally driven conductors.

Online Estimation of Partial Transpose Moments via Fast Classical Updates

Authors: Hyunho Cha, Jungwoo Lee
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.01433v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. This paper introduces a more efficient way to calculate entanglement-related quantities (PT moments) using classical shadows. By exploiting the local structure of quantum measurements, the authors reduce the computational complexity from cubic to subcubic scaling, enabling the analysis of larger quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The computational cost of updating partial-transpose (PT) moments in the classical shadows framework scales cubically with the Hilbert space dimension, making it inefficient for large systems.

Main result. The authors present a subcubic-time update algorithm for PT-moment estimators that maintains the same memory requirements as previous methods by exploiting the factorized structure of incoming snapshots.

Method. The method utilizes an online framework where accumulated matrices are updated via exact column-pair sweeps, leveraging the fact that fresh partially transposed snapshots remain factorized into local factors.

Model / system. The paper focuses on the framework of local Pauli classical shadows used for estimating nonlinear properties of quantum states.

Key observables. Partial-transpose (PT) moments, used for entanglement certification and phase diagnostics.

Assumptions / limitations. The algorithm assumes the use of local Pauli classical shadows and that the incoming snapshots can be treated as factorized local operators.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of cubic scaling in PT-moment updates, proposes a subcubic solution using column-pair sweeps on factorized snapshots, and provides an optimized Pauli basis update for the second PT moment.

Abstract

Partial-transpose (PT) moments are among the most practically relevant nonlinear quantities accessible from local Pauli classical shadows, because they directly underpin mixed-state entanglement certification and recent PT-moment-based phase diagnostics. The online framework of Marso \emph{et al.} rewrote the exact PT-moment statistic into a fixed-memory recurrence that updates a small collection of accumulated matrices after each new shadow snapshot. Its update cost is independent of the shot number, but each step treats the incoming partially transposed snapshot as a generic dense matrix. Therefore, the arithmetic cost scales cubically with the dimension of the Hilbert space. We show that the same estimator can be updated exactly in subcubic time per shot while retaining the same memory. The key point is that the accumulated matrices become dense, but the fresh partially transposed snapshot still factorizes into local factors. Right-multiplication by that factorized snapshot can therefore be executed by exact column-pair sweeps. For the second PT moment, we further optimize the process by utilizing a Pauli basis update.

Summary. The paper shows that the SUPER excitation scheme can reduce photon number coherence in quantum dot-cavity systems by using a laser-induced Stark shift to decouple the dot from the cavity during excitation. This reduction is beneficial for certain quantum cryptographic protocols like BB84. The study also quantifies how phonon-induced effects contribute to the loss of coherence.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems as it demonstrates how specific laser-driven protocols can be used to manipulate photonic coherence and decouple light-matter interaction via the Stark shift.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the SUPER (Swing-UP of quantum EmitteR population) excitation scheme affects the photon number coherence (PNC) of a quantum dot-cavity system, which is a critical metric for quantum cryptography security.

Main result. The SUPER scheme significantly decreases the PNC compared to resonant excitation because a laser-induced Stark shift effectively decouples the quantum dot from the cavity during the excitation pulse.

Method. Numerical solution of the Lindblad-form von Neumann equation using the process tensor matrix product operators implemented in the ACE code, accounting for phonon-induced dephasing.

Model / system. A two-level quantum dot embedded in a single-mode photonic microcavity, coupled to a longitudinal acoustic (LA) phonon environment and subject to radiative/cavity losses.

Key observables. Photon number coherence (PNC), QD electronic occupation, electronic coherence, and cavity Fock state occupation.

Abstract

To fulfill the security requirements of quantum cryptography, photon number coherence (PNC) of single photon sources has recently become an important figure of merit. Quantum dots (QDs) embedded in photonic microcavities offer a mature source of single photons, of which many properties can be tuned by the use of different excitation protocols or parameters. We show that the Swing-UP of quantum EmitteR population (SUPER) scheme can significantly decrease the PNC of the emitted photon, compared to resonant excitation. The reason for this is a laser-induced Stark shift, which effectively decouples the QD from the cavity during the SUPER excitation. Our calculations account for environmental effects such as phonons and radiative losses.

Numerical results show that this method becomes increasingly advantageous as the number of qubits increases.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for quantum information researchers working on quantum characterization, verification, and validation (QCVV), as it provides a more precise way to estimate state fidelity with minimal measurement resources.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Improving the efficiency and accuracy of Direct Fidelity Estimation (DFE) by replacing the existing OASIS method's coarse, outcome-wise surrogate objective with an exact spectral minimax approach.

Main result. The authors developed an exact spectral minimax optimization that minimizes the true worst-case variance over all physical states, outperforming the OASIS method in terms of Mean Squared Error (MSE).

Method. The problem is formulated as a spectral minimax problem and converted into a solvable Semidefinite Program (SDP) using a perspective lift and Sion's minimax theorem.

Model / system. The study considers n-qubit Hilbert spaces using a non-adaptive, single-copy, randomized measurement model based on the local Pauli family.

Key observables. Target fidelity F(rho) and one-shot worst-case variance.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (n), shot budget (N), and error/confidence parameters (epsilon and delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes non-adaptive, single-copy, linear estimators and is designed for generic target states rather than highly structured ones like GHZ states.

Figures summary. Table I compares the Mean Squared Error (MSE) for different qubit numbers (n=3 to 6), showing that the proposed algorithm's advantage increases with system size.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of DFE inefficiency, identifies the limitations of the OASIS surrogate, proposes a new spectral minimax framework, proves its exactness via SDP, and validates the method through numerical simulations under depolarizing noise.

Abstract

Direct fidelity estimation benefits from tailoring measurements to a fixed target, but the operator-aware shadow importance sampling (OASIS) method optimizes an outcome-wise linear-program surrogate rather than the exact worst-case variance over physical states. We propose an exact spectral replacement for arbitrary target states under the same non-adaptive single-copy measurement model. Specifically, we characterize unbiased linear estimators by a single operator identity, determine the state-wise optimal sampling law for fixed reconstruction coefficients, and convert the exact minimax problem into a semidefinite program. The resulting offline design and online estimator are presented as an algorithm and implemented with local Pauli measurements. Numerical simulations under depolarizing noise demonstrate that our exact spectral optimization outperforms the OASIS surrogate in terms of estimation variance.
