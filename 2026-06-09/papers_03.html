Summary. The paper establishes a robust quantum advantage of entangled measurements over classical/nonsignaling boxes in multi-party settings. By analyzing specific four-party and generalized $K+2$ party protocols, the authors prove that entanglement allows for correlations violating established inequalities, even when local wirings and global randomness are permitted.

Why it may be interesting. This work rigorously defines the boundary between quantum nonlocality witnessed by entanglement versus mere super-quantum correlations allowed by local no-signaling constraints, which is fundamental to understanding genuine multipartite quantum resources.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To determine if quantum entangled measurements provide an advantage over local wirings of nonsignaling nonlocal resources in multipartite networks when bipartite non-classical resources are shared.

Main result. The authors demonstrate a general separation, showing that for $K+2$ parties, quantum entangled measurements can witness behaviors impossible to achieve using only local wirings on nonsignaling boxes, even with global classical randomness.

Method. The analysis uses a resource-theoretic approach, comparing the expected values of Bell-type inequalities (like Mao-Bell) achievable via Quantum Entangled Measurements (QEM) versus those constrained by Nonsignaling Boxes (NSW).

Model / system. The model involves multipartite networks where parties share bipartite nonclassical resources. The comparison is between quantum entanglement/entangled measurements and correlations bounded only by the no-signaling principle.

Key observables. Violation of Bell inequalities, specifically the Mao-Bell inequality ($ ext{BM}$), which quantifies genuine multi-party nonlocality.

Important parameters / regimes. The fidelity ($F$) of the shared bipartite quantum states is a key parameter determining the threshold for quantum advantage (e.g., $F > 0.941$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that the NSW correlations are restricted to local wirings of at-most-$K$-partite nonsignaling resources, and it handles cases with or without globally shared classical randomness.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate network topologies (star vs. fully connected) for $K=2$ parties, and schemes involving GHZ state projections used to induce the testable multi-party behavior.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by establishing a general framework comparing QEM vs. NSW correlations in multipartite settings. It develops specific protocols (e.g., four-party star network) to witness the advantage, proves the separation for arbitrary $K$, and discusses generalizations regarding classical randomness.

Abstract

Quantum-entangled measurements are known to enable multi-party behaviors that are impossible with unentangled measurements on nonlocal resources, even those that are super-quantum and bound only by the no-signaling principle. This advantage can be witnessed by the entanglement swapping protocol, along with corresponding impossibility results for "nonlocality swapping". However, the advantage assumes the absence of pre-existing nonlocal resources shared by the swapped-to parties; it no longer holds if all pairs of parties are allowed to share bipartite nonlocal resources. Here, we consider a resource-theoretic perspective in which bipartite nonclassical resources are free resources that can be shared by any pair of parties in a multipartite network, and ask whether quantum entangled measurements can still provide an advantage over certain basic measurements, known as wirings, of nonsignaling nonlocal resources. We resolve this question in the affirmative by demonstrating an explicit four-party behavior that can be achieved with bipartite quantum resources subject to entangled measurements, and cannot be achieved if the bipartite resources are allowed to be more general nonsignaling nonlocal "boxes" so long as the measurements are restricted to local wirings, even also allowing for globally shared classical randomness. Furthermore, the argument generalizes: the same separation can be witnessed for K+2 parties with access to K-partite nonlocal resources for any K > 2. We also examine these results in different contexts, such as the star network configurations and scenarios not admitting globally shared classical randomness, further enhancing understanding of the capabilities of entangled measurements in multi-party configurations.

Summary. This theoretical paper analyzes how spin correlations develop during electron scattering processes by incorporating full relativistic effects, specifically Wigner rotations. Main problem. The study investigates the relativistic effects, particularly those arising from Wigner rotations, that generate spin correlations between electrons during QED scattering processes like Møller scattering.

Main result. Dipole-dipole and current-dipole interactions are responsible for generating these spin correlations; while total entropy is Lorentz invariant, local observables like spin expectation values are frame-dependent.

Method. The authors analyze scattering amplitudes using nonrelativistic approximations and calculate quantum information measures such as the von Neumann entropy of reduced density matrices in different reference frames.

Model / system. The system involves electron-electron ($e^-e^-$) or three-particle ($e^-e^-C$) interactions governed by Quantum Electrodynamics (QED). The analysis is performed using scattering theory formalism, including the use of density matrices and Lorentz transformations to account for relativistic kinematics.

Key observables. Spin correlations, von Neumann entropy ($S_{ ext{Neumann}}$), and spin expectation values ($\langle S_{x,y,z} angle$) of the outgoing particles.

Important parameters / regimes. Collision energy/rapidities ($\eta, \omega$), scattering angles ($ heta, \phi$), and the presence of a witness particle C.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to tree-level contributions in perturbation theory, omitting higher orders in the coupling constant. The initial states are analyzed as both separable and entangled (W-state).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate collision diagrams for $e^-e^-$ scattering, Feynman diagrams for Møller scattering, and plots showing the variation of von Neumann entropy and spin expectation values versus collision energy or rapidity for different interaction types.

Paper structure. Abstract

We study the relativistic nature of the interactions that, at tree level, generate spin correlations between two electrons in Møller scattering, as well as in an extended process involving a witness particle $C$. The corresponding processes, $e^{-}e^{-}\rightarrow e^{-}e^{-}$ and $e^{-}e^{-}C\rightarrow e^{-}e^{-}C$, are analyzed both in the center-of-mass frame and, for the former process, in a Lorentz-boosted frame where Wigner rotations arise. It is found that, through a nonrelativistic approximation of the scattering amplitudes, dipole-dipole and current-dipole interactions are responsible for the emergence of these correlations. This is evidenced by the variation of the von Neumann entropy of one electron for initially separable states, and of $C$ for an initially prepared three-particle entangled W-state. In Wigner rotations, the invariance of entropy under local unitary transformations is maintained at the expense of the emergence of quantum coherence in the density matrix at large rapidities. As a consequence, the final states of both particles are evaluated and shown to encode information about the scattering process through their spin expectation values. This framework is then used to comment on the correlations in the inelastic process $e^{-}e^{+}\rightarrowμ^{-}μ^{+}$, for which some research has reported differing results.

Summary. The paper theoretically explores how breaking generalized supersymmetry in Bose-Fermi mixtures leads to the appearance of fermionic Goldstino modes. It rigorously distinguishes between standard and inverse SUSY by analyzing their distinct signatures in measurable spectral functions. The findings propose specific radio-frequency spectroscopy experiments using alkali atom mixtures to detect these exotic quantum phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. This work connects abstract concepts from high-energy physics (supersymmetry) directly to observable phenomena in ultracold atomic systems, providing a concrete theoretical framework for novel spectroscopic measurements.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the emergence of fermionic Goldstino modes due to the breaking of generalized supersymmetry (gSUSY) in Bose-Fermi mixtures.

Main result. It distinguishes between standard SUSY and inverse SUSY (iSUSY), showing that iSUSY predicts unique, experimentally distinguishable features in the spectral functions of bosonic species.

Model / system. The system is a nearly degenerate Bose-Fermi mixture of ultracold quantum gases (specifically $^{39} ext{K}$ bosons and $^{40} ext{K}$ fermions) possessing internal spin states (pseudospin).

Key observables. Goldstino dispersion $\omega = D_{ij}(p)$, bosonic/fermionic spectral functions $A(p, \omega)$ measurable via RF or microwave spectroscopy.

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis depends on the relative strengths of interactions ($U_{bf}$) and gSUSY breaking parameters ($\Delta\mu_{ij}, \chi$).

Assumptions / limitations. Approximations include using RPA, expanding in small symmetry-breaking parameters ($\chi$), and assuming uniform particle density.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining gSUSY in the Hamiltonian to using RPA to find Goldstino poles. It then systematically compares the predictions of standard SUSY versus inverse SUSY by analyzing how these symmetries manifest in the momentum-dependent spectral functions.

Abstract

We investigate nearly degenerate Bose-Fermi mixtures and show that the breaking of generalized supersymmetry (gSUSY) between bosons and fermions, with up to two internal states, manifests itself through the emergence of fermionic Goldstino modes with up to four flavors. In particular, we draw a distinction between typical supersymmetry (SUSY), where bosons have pseudospin 0 and fermions have pseudospin 1/2, and inverse supersymmetry (iSUSY), where bosons have pseudospin 1/2 and fermions have pseudospin 0. In such systems, we highlight that the Goldstino pseudospin is carried by either its constituent fermion (SUSY) or boson (iSUSY). We then distinguish between these two cases by depicting their differing effects on the spectral functions of the bosonic and fermionic atomic species. Lastly, we propose radio-frequency- or microwave-spectroscopy experiments, analogous to momentum (angular) resolved photoemission in condensed matter physics, to measure the pseudospin-dependent spectral functions and detect the emergence of Goldstino modes in mixtures of $^{39}$K and $^{40}$K.

Summary. This work introduces affine filtering measurements as an advanced tool for decoding classical linear codes transmitted via pure-state quantum channels. By mathematically reducing the optimal measurement design from a difficult Semidefinite Program to a solvable Linear Program using character theory, the authors provide a powerful new quantum decoding framework. Simulations confirm that this method significantly outperforms existing symbol-wise decoding techniques on structured codes like LDPC.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical reduction of a complex quantum optimization problem (SDP) into a tractable linear program is a powerful technique applicable across quantum information theory, providing concrete tools for designing optimal measurements.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing efficient quantum decoding methods for classical linear codes transmitted over pure-state classical-quantum (CQ) channels.

Main result. The proposed affine filtering measurement combined with Gaussian elimination outperforms symbol-wise USD and PGM decoders in simulations on i.i.d. pure-state channels for LDPC codes.

Method. The optimal measurement design is formulated as a Semidefinite Program (SDP) which the authors successfully reduce to an equivalent Linear Program (LP) using character theory and symmetry arguments.

Model / system. The system models quantum information processing where classical linear codes are encoded into pure-state quantum channels. The core mathematical tools involve group characters, SDP/LP optimization, and measurement operators (POVMs).

Key observables. Success probability ($P_{ ext{success}}$) of decoding; the expected number of recovered linear equations.

Important parameters / regimes. Code parameters like $(k, D)$ for LDPC codes; channel noise parameter $\alpha$; field size $q$.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation relies heavily on exploiting symmetries (coset symmetry) to reduce the SDP to an LP, and in some cases, passing to a limit ($\epsilon o 0$) by perturbing the state family.

Figures summary. Figures compare the success probability thresholds of the affine-filtering+GE decoder against qubit-wise USD/PGM decoders for various regular LDPC codes. Table 2 compares the expected number of linear equations recovered by different measurement types.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the problem (USD measurements) to developing the mathematical machinery: first, establishing the SDP formulation; second, using symmetry arguments and character theory to reduce this SDP to an equivalent LP; finally, applying this LP framework to simulate and compare decoding performance on specific LDPC codes.

Abstract

Unambiguous state discrimination (USD) measurements are attractive because outcomes are either marked as conclusive (i.e., error free) or inconclusive (i.e., erased). We study affine filtering measurements, a structured variant of USD for decoding classical linear codes over pure-state classical-quantum channels, where a conclusive outcome identifies an affine subspace containing the transmitted codeword and an inconclusive outcome is treated as an erasure. For a group-covariant indexing of pure-state codewords, we show that the optimal design of affine filtering measurements is a semidefinite program that can be reduced to a linear program via character-based diagonalization. We use the resulting measurement to build a quantum decoding framework for local codes, and we demonstrate (via simulations on regular LDPC codes from Gallager ensembles using single parity check local constraints) that affine filtering based decoding can outperform symbol-wise USD and symbol-wise pretty good measurement based decoding methods on i.i.d. pure-state channels. In an independent and concurrent work, Buzet and Chailloux study similar fine-grained USD measurements for symmetric families of states. Their focus is on the code-agnostic setting whereas our focus is on code-aware constructions and decoding.

Summary. This paper rigorously studies $m$-resistant states, defining resistance based on how the state remains entangled after losing a few particles but becomes separable after losing slightly more. Using graph states and stabilizer formalism, the authors classify these robust states for small qubit numbers and prove general non-existence results for large cycle graphs.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides rigorous mathematical tools (stabilizer methods, NPT witnesses) to quantify entanglement robustness, which is crucial for designing fault-tolerant quantum communication protocols or understanding resource limitations in quantum computation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper characterizes the robustness of multipartite entanglement, defined by $m$-resistance, in quantum states under particle loss.

Main result. It establishes that the five-cycle graph state $|C_5\rangle$ is a 1-resistant state for five qubits, and provides detailed classification results showing specific existence patterns for small numbers of qubits ($N=5, 6, 7$).

Method. The authors develop a stabilizer-subgroup method using local stabilizers to certify separability and Negative Partial Transpose (NPT) witnesses to certify entanglement in reduced marginal states.

Model / system. The study focuses on graph states $|G\rangle$ defined on $N$ qubits, where the resistance property requires specific reduced density matrices (marginals) corresponding to losing $m$ or $m+1$ particles to exhibit distinct entanglement properties.

Key observables. Entanglement status of marginal states ($ ho_{ar{J}}$), measured via NPT witnesses; separability confirmed by local stabilizer conditions.

Important parameters / regimes. The resistance parameter $m$, the number of qubits $N$ (tested for 5, 6, and 7), and the structure of the underlying graph $G$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on sufficient conditions derived from local stabilizers for separability, and the classification is restricted to stabilizer/graph states.

Figures summary. Not specified in detail, but the notes reference tables summarizing existence results for $(N, m)$ pairs and figures showing representatives of found orbits (e.g., three orbits for 6-vertex 2-resistant states).

Paper structure. The paper first defines $m$-resistance using particle loss analysis. It then develops a formal stabilizer framework to test this property on graph state marginals, culminating in exhaustive classification results for small N and proving non-existence for large cycle graphs.

Abstract

We study particle-loss resistant entanglement within the framework of stabilizer and graph states. A pure state is \(m\)-resistant if it remains entangled after the loss of any \(m\) particles and becomes fully separable after the loss of any \(m+1\) particles. The smallest previously unresolved qubit case was the existence of a five-qubit \(1\)-resistant pure state, which is resolved here by the five-cycle graph state \(\ket{C_5}\). A stabilizer-subgroup method is also developed for verifying \(m\)-resistance in graph states, using local stabilizers to certify full separability and exact negative partial transpose~(NPT) witnesses to certify entanglement. Applying this to all graph states associated with non-isomorphic graphs on five, six, and seven vertices, we obtain a graph state classification up to local Clifford equivalence, which also classifies stabilizer states up to local Clifford equivalence. Thus, the five-qubit \(1\)-resistant stabilizer states are exactly the local Clifford class of \(C_5\). Six-qubit \(2\)-resistant stabilizer states exist in three distinct local Clifford classes, whereas no seven-qubit stabilizer state is \(m\)-resistant for any nonzero admissible \(m\). Finally, we prove that the cycle graph states \(\ket{C_N}\) with \(N\ge 7\) are not \(m\)-resistant for any \(0\le m\le N-2\).

Abstract

Denial-of-service (DoS) and distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) mitigation requires separating malicious traffic from benign traffic while minimizing disruption to legitimate users. Prior work proposed mapping honeypot traffic partitioning to a weighted MaxCut problem and solving the resulting graphs with variational quantum algorithms. We extend this proof of principle direction with a reproducible event-level honeypot-to-QUBO pipeline, labeled temporal bipartite benchmark graphs with 16, 32, 66, and 110 event nodes, QAOA executions on IBM quantum hardware, classical heuristic baselines, a noiseless matrix product state reference, and a routing overhead analysis across quantum processor architectures. The largest benchmark is a 110-node, 181-edge instance executed on three IBM backends. Our results show that a shallow QAOA can execute real traffic partitioning workloads at the utility scale, while backend architecture and routing overhead affect objective quality, security metrics, and observed runtime. Because simple classical heuristics can solve the current labeled benchmark graphs, these experiments are not a quantum advantage claim. Instead, we deliberately use a fixed, shallow QAOA implementation to enable controlled comparisons across problem sizes and hardware architectures. This work establishes a hardware feasibility and architecture benchmark framework, and demonstrates that MaxCut cost, security quality, routing overhead, and runtime must be reported as separate metrics for cybersecurity relevant quantum optimization.

Abstract

The echo squeezing protocols (ESPs) are techniques that amplify the phase shift in a quantum sensor with one-axis-twist squeezing (OATS). For atomic sensors, spontaneous emission (SE) in the OATS operations is an important imperfection, which, however, is prohibitively difficult to study. SE can transfer atoms to all the Zeeman substates, making the number of relevant collective states excessively large. In this paper, we focus on a relatively simple isotope, namely Sr-88, which only has one Zeeman substate in each of the two ground states. Nevertheless, studying the effect of SE is still challenging because SE will populate all collective states, either symmetric or asymmetric, thus putting the ensemble into a mixed state. Based on analytical derivation and numerical simulations, we conclude that the GESP is more resistant to SE than the conventional echo squeezing protocol (CESP). This is another advantage of the GESP that has not been realized formerly. We also find that for the GESP employing Sr-88, the SE-induced reduction in the signal contrast is the same as that for a Ramsey protocol without squeezing and the quantum noise is suppressed by SE. This is an unexpectedly favorable result. The SE-induced suppression of quantum noise constitutes a previously unrecognized effect that challenges both earlier conclusions and intuitive expectations.

Abstract

Responses to perturbations are key to understanding physical systems. The ability to contrast such responses by comparing how a system reacts under slightly different conditions provides a mechanism for learning. Here, we introduce Perturbative Contrastive Physical Learning (PCPL), a general framework in which learning emerges from measurable contrasts between physical states produced by controlled changes to inputs, boundary conditions, parameters, or interpreter functions. PCPL unifies and extends prior approaches: Equilibrium Propagation is rooted in contrasts between free and nudged equilibria in energy-based systems, while Frequency Propagation corresponds to contrasts extracted from sinusoidally driven, frequency-demodulated responses. We show that contrast-driven updates can reflect either local sensitivities or global inverse-problem structure, yet do not require centralized gradient computation. Instead, effective learning geometry emerges implicitly from the system's own physical response, allowing learning behavior to arise without an external processor or explicit backpropagation. We demonstrate PCPL in two platforms: (i) spring networks that update bond stiffness using measured displacements and forces, and (ii) continuous-variable photonic circuits trained via x quadrature measurements and finite-difference estimates of the Jacobian. Both platforms successfully learn classification tasks. We further show that a continuous-variable photonic circuit can be trained to implement analog multiplication, illustrating a step toward more autonomous physical learning systems.

Abstract

We study Family-Vicsek scaling of charge, spin, and energy fluctuations in the one-dimensional Hubbard model at infinite temperature. Using a quantum generating function approach, we compute time-dependent cumulants of transferred conserved quantities and analyze how the corresponding roughness depends on subsystem size and time. Abstract
