Quantum Kravchuk Transform using $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ fast-forwarding

Authors: Chaowen Guan, Akshit Katiyar
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.08443v1

Summary. The paper presents an efficient quantum algorithm for the Quantum Kravchuk Transform by exploiting its deep mathematical connection to the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ Lie algebra. By mapping the transform onto this algebraic structure and utilizing fast-forwarding simulation techniques, the authors achieve a polylogarithmic gate complexity. This demonstrates a powerful methodology for implementing transforms associated with discrete orthogonal polynomials on quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a concrete example of applying advanced algebraic structures ($\mathfrak{su}(2)$) and state-of-the-art quantum simulation techniques (fast-forwarding) to solve a problem in orthogonal polynomial theory, demonstrating potential quantum advantage over classical methods for specific transforms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is developing an efficient quantum algorithm for the Quantum Kravchuk Transform (QKT). This aims to achieve a computational complexity that scales logarithmically with system size, surpassing classical polynomial scaling.

Main result. An efficient quantum circuit implementing the QKT was developed by mapping the transform structure onto the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ Lie algebra. The resulting algorithm achieves a time complexity of $O((\log N + \log(1/\epsilon))^3 imes \log(1/\epsilon))$ gates.

Method. The method combines establishing a structural link between the Kravchuk transform and $\mathfrak{su}(2)$, followed by applying advanced fast-forwarding simulation techniques for $ ext{SU}(2)$ group elements in the oscillator representation.

Model / system. The system is based on the discrete harmonic oscillator, whose eigenfunctions are the Kravchuk polynomials/functions defined over a finite lattice $X_N = \{0, 1, \dots, N\}$. The dynamics and transform properties are modeled using operators satisfying $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ commutation relations.

Key observables. The QKT itself (mapping computational basis states to Kravchuk function states), the spectrum of the discrete harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian ($\hat{K}$), and the matrix elements of the transformation operators.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension $N$ (or lattice size), error bound $\epsilon$, and the logarithmic scaling factors derived from the fast-forwarding techniques.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach relies on successfully mapping the Kravchuk transform structure onto an irreducible representation of $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ to enable the use of established efficient simulation algorithms for Lie groups.

Paper structure. The paper builds by first defining the mathematical properties of the Kravchuk polynomials and their connection to the discrete harmonic oscillator. It then establishes this link to $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ via the Jordan-Schwinger map, allowing the application of fast-forwarding techniques developed for $ ext{SU}(2)$ evolution to construct the final quantum circuit.

Abstract

We present a quantum algorithm for the Kravchuk transform that scales logarithmically in both the dimension and the inverse of the error parameter. The quantum Kravchuk transform maps computational basis states to states with amplitudes proportional to Kravchuk functions. We achieve this by combining two key techniques: the structural relationship between the Kravchuk transform and the Lie algebras $\mathfrak{su}(2)$, and a recent fast-forwarding simulation method for $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ operators in the oscillator representation. More precisely, we first establish the map from Kravchuk transform in computational basis to $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ in Fock basis. Then built on this connection, we apply the fast-forwarding to achieve an efficient quantum Kravchuk transform.

REM universality and Poisson-Dirichlet Gibbs weights for linear random energy

Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.07757v1

Summary. This paper proves that the energy levels of a purely linear random Hamiltonian exhibit Random Energy Model (REM) universality after thinning. It rigorously determines that the resulting point process converges to a Poisson Point Process and identifies that the associated Gibbs weights converge to the Poisson-Dirichlet law, revealing a phase transition in the free energy at a critical temperature.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous proof of REM universality for this specific linear model provides deep insights into how complex statistical physics models simplify to universal limiting forms, which is relevant for understanding disordered systems in quantum mechanics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to establish Random Energy Model (REM) universality for energy levels derived from a purely linear random Hamiltonian and determine the limiting behavior of associated Gibbs weights.

Main result. The thinned point process of energy levels converges to a Poisson Point Process, and the joint distribution of ranked Gibbs weights converges to the Poisson-Dirichlet law in the low-temperature regime.

Method. The analysis uses techniques involving independent thinning processes, computing limit Laplace functionals for point processes, and applying large-deviation principles to analyze weight convergence.

Model / system. The system is defined by the Hamiltonian H_n(h, \sigma) = \sum_{i=1}^n h_i(\sigma_i - m), where h_i are i.i.d. random variables and \sigma_i are Ising spins. The analysis focuses on energy levels obtained after retaining an exponential number of configurations.

Key observables. Energy level point process, Gibbs weights, quenched free energy F(β).

Important parameters / regimes. β (inverse temperature), τλ (critical parameter related to the PPP intensity), and the thinning factor c_ό.

Assumptions / limitations. The proof relies on regularity hypotheses for h_1, including a linear small-ball condition, and assumes sharp large-deviation estimates from prior work.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the quenched large-deviation rate function G*(a) versus a, defining parameters τλ and α.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes REM universality for the point process convergence to PPP(D_{τλ}), then analyzes the Gibbs weights showing convergence to PD(τλ/β, 0), and finally computes the free energy exhibiting a phase transition at β = τλ.

Abstract

We study the Hamiltonian $H_n(h,σ)=\sum_{i=1}^n h_i(σ_i-m), $ where $(h_i)$ are i.i.d.\ real random variables and $(σ_i)$ are i.i.d.\ Ising spins. We consider the energy levels obtained after an independent thinning that retains an exponential number of configurations ($e^{O(n)}$). We prove that, after an $(h_i)$-dependent centering, the resulting point process converges in distribution to a Poisson point process with exponential intensity. Thus, the energy levels asymptotically has the one of the Random Energy Model (REM). Our results extend previous ones, where REM universality for this model was established only either for energy fluctuations of order $e^{-O(n)}$ or for $e^{o(\sqrt n)}$ randomly selected configurations. We also identify the limiting Gibbs weights, which converge to a Poisson--Dirichlet law, and the quenched free energy, which exhibits a freezing transition at $β=\tildeλ$. The proofs are presented here in compressed form; full details are given in the companion preprint.

Summary. This work reformulates Zermelo's river-crossing problem as an adiabatic quantum computation task using qutrits. By analyzing the system's evolution through avoided crossings via the Landau-Zener formula, the authors demonstrate that a seemingly complex optimization problem possesses an efficient classical solution scaling only quadratically with size. This highlights how quantum tools can reveal underlying classical structure.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a pedagogical example of mapping a real-world optimization problem onto a quantum annealer framework, allowing for analytical estimation of performance metrics like fidelity using established quantum dynamics tools.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses Zermelo's navigation problem, a classic optimization challenge, by formulating it as an adiabatic quantum computation task.

Main result. Despite the initial appearance of exponential complexity, the quantum formulation reveals an underlying classical structure that can be solved efficiently with only quadratic scaling in the problem size.

Method. The solution utilizes adiabatic quantum computing principles, estimating fidelity via the Landau-Zener formula to analyze the evolution through avoided crossings.

Model / system. The system models a discretized path across a river using qutrits (ternary variables) encoded into a Hamiltonian. The total Hamiltonian combines a cost term and a penalty term enforcing boundary conditions.

Key observables. Fidelity ($F$ or $F_{LZ}$), required annealing time ($T_{pen}$), and the optimal path $\vec{s}_{opt}$.

Important parameters / regimes. The problem size $n$, the annealing schedule parameters ($\alpha(t), eta(t)$), and the critical crossing values.

Assumptions / limitations. The validity of the Landau-Zener approximation, which assumes negligible interference effects in the large time limit ($T_{pen} o \infty$).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the optimal path (Fig. 1), discretized steps (Fig. 2), the three-phase annealing schedule (Fig. 3), and comparisons between actual vs. Landau-Zener fidelity versus $T_{pen}$ (Fig. 6).

Paper structure. The paper introduces Zermelo's problem, maps it to a qutrit Hamiltonian ($H = H_{ ext{cost}} + eta H_{ ext{pen}}$), details the adiabatic evolution using an annealing schedule, analyzes the fidelity via Landau-Zener theory, and concludes by deriving efficient classical algorithms that match the quantum prediction.

Abstract

The river-crossing problem, also known as Zermelo's navigation problem, is a classic example of an optimization problem with practical relevance and a scalable degree of complexity. It asks for the optimal trajectory of a vessel moving through a water flow field and provides a setting in which physics, variational methods, and optimization are naturally intertwined. We state a version of Zermelo's problem and then solve it as formulate it as an adiabatic quantum-computing problem using quantum trits, or qutrits for short. The construction includes a penalty term that enforces the prescribed boundary conditions and an exploration term that allows the system to move through intermediate configurations toward the optimal feasible path. In the adiabatic description, the evolution proceeds through a sequence of avoided crossings, so that the resulting fidelity can be estimated using the Landau-Zener formula. Remarkably, the regime in which this approximation is valid also provides a deterministic way to identify the correct solution with computational effort that scales only quadratically with the problem size. Thus, a quantum formulation initially motivated by the apparent exponential complexity of the problem reveals an underlying classical structure that can be exploited efficiently. Our approach also provides a pedagogical illustration of how a real-world optimization problem can be cast into a quantum-annealing framework and then analyzed using the Schrödinger equation, avoided crossings, and Landau-Zener theory.

Abstract

We study a Mach-Zehnder interferometer (MZI)-based optoelectronic magnonic parametric oscillator (OEMPO) incorporating a YIG-loaded magnonic branch and a tunable phase-shifter branch, enabling systematic investigation of adaptive interferometric oscillator dynamics under distributed phase perturbations. Through analysis of nondegenerate OEPO mode pairs and frequency-pulling behavior, the loop free spectral range (FSR) and effective delay time were quantitatively extracted. Despite the nominally frequency-pinned parametric operation, weak frequency pulling and OEPO mode softening were observed, revealing an additional adaptive interferometric degree of freedom introduced by the MZI architecture. Broadly, the present platform establishes a versatile framework for exploring adaptive nonlinear interferometric physics, coherent phase redistribution, and branch-dependent synchronization phenomena in hybrid magnonic-photonic oscillator systems.

Abstract

A supported nanoparticle straddling such a boundary experiences an asymmetric particle-substrate interfacial energy environment that constitutes a lateral thermodynamic driving force for migration. Here, using in situ transmission electron microscopy to track Pt nanoparticle motion statistically, paired with time-resolved diffraction and 4D-STEM analysis to characterize support crystallization, we demonstrate that propagating crystallization fronts in amorphous AlO$_x$ thin films actively drag supported Pt nanoparticles over long distances. Temporal correlation between the onsets of support crystallization and rapid particle migration, together with 4D-STEM virtual crystallinity maps, establishes that the front drives particle motion. Phase-field simulations confirm that particle-substrate interfacial energy contrast alone sustains particle drag, and identify curvature gradients along the particle surface as the mechanism by which the advancing front redistributes mass and displaces the particle. These results establish a general mechanism by which any propagating surface-energy boundary on a substrate can act as a deterministic driver of supported nanoparticle transport.
