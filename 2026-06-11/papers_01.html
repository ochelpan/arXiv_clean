Summary. The paper develops a theory to explain ferromagnetism in narrow bands of localized electrons found in moiré superlattices. It shows that the magnetic stability depends critically on the geometric overlap of single-particle wavefunctions rather than just band curvature. By analyzing spin excitations, the authors establish quantitative predictions for the critical interaction strength required to induce magnetism.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a concrete, geometrically driven mechanism for magnetism in correlated systems without translational symmetry, which is highly relevant for understanding emergent phenomena in moiré heterostructures and artificial quantum materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The research aims to determine the mechanism for ferromagnetism in narrow electronic bands formed by Anderson-localized states within moiré superlattices.

Main result. Ferromagnetism can be stabilized at critical interaction strengths ($U_c$) determined by the geometry of real-space overlaps between localized wavefunctions, providing a route distinct from traditional quantum geometry arguments.

Method. The analysis involves projecting the Hubbard model onto the narrow band subspace and studying the stability of the fully polarized state by examining spin-flip excitations (magnons).

Model / system. The system is modeled using an effective Hamiltonian for quasiperiodic moiré lattices, incorporating nearest-neighbor hopping and on-site Coulomb repulsion ($U$). The physics is analyzed in terms of localized single-particle eigenstates.

Key observables. Critical interaction strength ($U_c$), magnon excitation spectrum, Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR), and the energy difference between spin sectors ($\Delta E$).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength ($U$), hopping parameters ($t$, $V$), localization length ($\xi$), and wavefunction overlap geometry ($ ho_{\alphaeta}$).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory relies on projecting the full Hamiltonian onto a narrow-band subspace, assuming that low-energy physics is dominated by these localized states.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the evolution of the magnon spectrum (transition from continuum to bound modes) and show how $U_c$ depends on system parameters, confirming robustness checks across varying numbers of included high-energy states.

Paper structure. The paper systematically derives effective Hamiltonians for charge and spin excitations, calculates single-particle properties like IPR using locator expansions, and finally uses exact diagonalization to verify the global ground state transition point ($U_c$) by comparing low-spin vs. fully polarized energy sectors.

Abstract

We present a mechanism for ferromagnetism in narrow bands consisting of Anderson-localized states. We exploit single-particle localization to derive a controlled theory of exchange interactions within the narrow band. For quasiperiodic systems with a half-filled moiré band, we show that the critical interaction strength for ferromagnetism is highly sensitive to the geometry of real-space overlaps between localized orbitals: we find well-defined resonances at which ferromagnetism sets in for interaction energies that are far lower than the gap to other bands. Near these resonances, all the approximations in our theory are controlled, so our critical point predictions are quantitative. We show examples both in one and two dimensions. Our work identifies a route to ferromagnetism based on the geometry of real-space wavefunctions, distinct from previously found mechanisms based on the quantum geometry of Bloch bands.

Abstract

Fractional quantum Hall states are a cornerstone of topological physics, hosting fractionally charged quasiparticles with exotic statistics that promise to enable topologically protected quantum information processing. Among these, the Pfaffian state introduced by Moore and Read implements a p-wave pairing structure that supports excitations with non-Abelian exchange statistics. Despite extensive study in electronic systems, direct access to its pairing structure has remained limited. Here we realize a three-particle bosonic Pfaffian state of ultracold $^{87}\mathrm{Rb}$ atoms in an optical lattice subject to a Floquet-engineered synthetic magnetic field. Using a Bayesian-optimized adiabatic protocol, we prepare a state exhibiting Pfaffian pairing correlations. Site-resolved measurements of multi-point density correlations reveal a pronounced suppression of short-range three-body coincidences, reflecting the underlying pairing structure. We further probe the state's transport response through Hall drift measurements. Our results establish a bottom-up approach to engineering non-Abelian topological order and lay the groundwork for future explorations of anyonic braiding in synthetic matter.

Abstract

By forming hybrid light-matter states, optical cavities offer a route for engineering material properties, however, unambiguously probing the effects of light-matter coupling remains difficult. Here, we show that the polarization-resolved statistics of photons transmitted through a cavity, measurable via $g^{(2)}$, provide one such diagnostic. By relating $g^{(2)}$ to matter correlation functions such as the Raman structure factor, we link photon bunching and antibunching to material properties. By applying this method to the stripy-to-antiferromagnetic transition in the Kitaev-Heisenberg spin model, we find that polarization-dependent patterns of bunching and antibunching encode the magnetic point-group symmetries of each phase and characterize the behavior at the phase boundary. Finally, we predict measuring $g^{(2)}$ for output photon pairs polarized orthogonal to the input field will isolate higher-order light-matter scattering processes that probe higher-order material correlations.

Abstract

Abstract

Our results highlight the potential of waveguide-coupled emitter arrays as versatile quantum sensors, where collective radiative dynamics can be harnessed to achieve tunable, long-lived, and enhanced precision.

Abstract

Abstract

Participation entropy (PE) quantifies the spread of a many-body wavefunction across configuration space. While PE relaxes rapidly in generic chaotic systems, we show that $\mathrm{U}(1)$ conservation laws slow it down by imprinting with the slow hydrodynamic modes. Our results establish PE as a sensitive probe of hydrodynamic memory and suggest that slow relaxation is a generic consequence of conservation laws.
