Planar chiral nanoantenna for excitation-chirality-controlled hot spot modulation and emitter-coupled circularly polarized emission

Authors: Abhik Chakraborty, Xiaofei Wu, Ankit Kumar Singh, Fabian Scheidler, Min Jiang, Jürgen Popp, Bert Hecht, Jer-Shing Huang
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05065v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: interference shaping light 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper presents a design for a chiral plasmonic nanoantenna that allows for the active switching of electromagnetic hot spots using circularly polarized light. By controlling the light's handedness and ellipticity, researchers can modulate the near-field intensity by up to 100%. This architecture also enables the generation of nearly perfectly circularly polarized light from embedded quantum emitters.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics as it provides a method for engineering the spin-state of light-matter interaction at the nanoscale, specifically for creating scalable, nanoscale circularly polarized single-photon sources.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for active, deterministic control over light-matter interaction at the nanoscale, specifically moving beyond passive sensing to active near-field switching and modulation of plasmonic hot spots.

Main result. A planar chiral nanoantenna was designed to switch a plasmonic hot spot 'on' or 'off' by changing the handedness of circularly polarized light, and to generate highly circularly polarized emission from an embedded quantum emitter.

Method. 3D electromagnetic simulations using the Finite-Difference Time-Domain (FDTD) algorithm with a 1 nm spatial mesh resolution.

Model / system. A planar chiral plasmonic nanoantenna consisting of two asymmetric V-shaped gold elements with a 10 nm nanogap, placed on a glass substrate, potentially hosting a dipolar quantum emitter.

Key observables. Intensity Enhancement (IE), near-field dissymmetry factor (g), Degree of Circular Polarization (DoCP), and Amplitude Enhancement (AE).

Important parameters / regimes. Wavelength of 842 nm, nanogap width of 10 nm, and numerical aperture (NA) up to 0.9.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a monocrystalline gold structure and uses a simplified dipole model for the quantum emitter.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the antenna geometry, intensity enhancement spectra, and spatial distribution of the near-field; Figure 2 details the design criteria and modal interference; Figure 3 uses a Poincare sphere to show continuous modulation; Figure 4 shows the degree of circular polarization spectra.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of active near-field control, describes the chiral nanoantenna geometry, presents the numerical results for hot spot switching and modulation, analyzes the underlying modal interference mechanisms, and demonstrates the application to circularly polarized single-photon emission.

Abstract

A planar chiral plasmonic nanoantenna exhibiting an excitation-chirality-dependent hot spot in a nanogap is numerically investigated. Additionally, the underlying design principles are examined, providing a broadly applicable framework for engineering chiral nanoantennas through controlled geometrical or modal asymmetry. The hot spot can be turned on and off by changing the handedness of the exciting circularly polarized light (CPL). This effect stems from the rationally designed interference of plasmonic modes excited by the linearly polarized orthogonal components of CPL. The hot spot exhibits maximal near-field dissymmetry factor (about -2) at a wavelength of 842 nm. The intensity at the hot spot can also be continuously modulated by varying the excitation ellipticity and handedness, approaching a modulation depth of 100%. These attributes enable chirality- and ellipticity-dependent switching and dynamic modulation of the plasmonic near field. Moreover, placing a quantum emitter in the gap generates almost perfectly circularly polarized emission, offering a simple yet effective avenue to realize nanoscale circularly polarized single-photon sources.

Patterns in Time and Space from a Single Morphogen via Nonlinear Layering

Authors: N. Mahashri, Andrew L. Krause, M. Chandru, Thomas E. Woolley
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05063v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper shows that a single morphogen can create complex spatial and temporal patterns if it moves through a layered medium with nonlinear coupling. By using a thin-layer approximation, the authors prove that reactive interfacial coupling acts like additional chemical species, enabling Turing and Hopf instabilities.

Why it may be interesting. The mechanism of using spatial heterogeneity and nonlinear coupling to induce instabilities in a single-species system provides insights into how complex, emergent many-body dynamics can arise from simple, single-component constituents.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how a single morphogen in a multi-layered 2D medium can generate complex spatiotemporal patterns like Turing, Hopf, and Turing-wave instabilities, which are typically impossible in single-species scalar reaction-diffusion systems.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that nonlinear, reactive coupling between layers can drive pattern formation, effectively acting as additional dynamical degrees of freedom that broaden the class of systems exhibiting complex behavior.

Method. The study uses thin-domain asymptotic reduction to simplify the 2D model into a 1D N-component system, followed by linear stability analysis and numerical verification using the Finite Element Method.

Model / system. A multi-layered 2D reaction-diffusion system where a single species diffuses through N vertically arranged layers, coupled via nonlinear, flux-conserving boundary conditions at the interfaces.

Key observables. Spatial averages of concentration, oscillation amplitudes, waveform shapes, vertical gradients, and kymographs of concentration evolution.

Important parameters / regimes. Layer height (H), coupling strength (eta), diffusion coefficients (Di), and reaction kinetics (f_i, g_i).

Assumptions / limitations. The thin-layer reduction assumes H approaches zero and that solutions/nonlinearities are sufficiently smooth; the 1D model neglects vertical gradients.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the multi-layer model geometry; Figure 2 shows 2D simulations of pattern transitions; Figure 3 compares 1D and 2D temporal dynamics; Figure 4 shows 2D Hopf dynamics and vertical gradients; Figure 5 shows parameter sweeps of coupling strength; Figures 7-9 show wave propagation, kymographs, and dispersion relations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of single-species patterning, presents the multi-layer 2D model, derives a reduced 1D model via thin-layer asymptotics, performs linear stability analysis to identify instability drivers, validates the reduction with 2D numerical simulations, and explores various dynamical regimes (Turing, Hopf, Turing-wave).

Abstract

Spatial and temporal pattern formation in reaction-diffusion systems is typically studied with two or more equations, as scalar reaction-diffusion equations confined to convex domains do not admit stable inhomogeneous states in time or space on long timescales. Here, we show that a single morphogen diffusing across layered two-dimensional media, with nonlinear coupling between layers, is able to generate stable patterns in time and space. This $N$-layer model is analysed via a thin-domain limit, which reduces to an $N$-component reaction-diffusion system on a homogeneous one-dimensional domain. This reduced model can be analysed via linear stability techniques, showing that non-diffusive, or reactive, coupling between regions is necessary for pattern-forming instabilities, at least in the reduced model. This reduced system can exhibit Turing, Hopf, and Turing-wave instabilities, with emergent structures that are numerically shown to persist even away from the thin-domain regime of the full 2D single-morphogen system. These results suggest that heterogeneous stratification and nonlinear coupling can broaden the class of systems which exhibit complex spatiotemporal behaviours, which may be relevant in scenarios where only a single morphogen is known to act.

Mixed-Parity Altermagnetism in Collinear Spin-Orbital Magnets

Authors: Zheng-Yang Zhuang, Jin-Xin Hu, Song-Bo Zhang, Lun-Hui Hu, Zhongbo Yan
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05205v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies a new class of mixed-parity altermagnetism that bridges the gap between even- and odd-parity spin-splitting. It shows that this state can be actively manipulated using external tools like electric fields and circularly polarized light in spin-orbital magnets.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the existence and symmetry conditions of a 'mixed-parity' altermagnetism regime, which lies between the traditionally known even- and odd-parity spin-splitting forms.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that mixed-parity altermagnetism can emerge in collinear spin-orbital magnets, acting as an intermediate regime between even- and odd-parity states, and can be controlled via staggered potentials and circularly polarized light.

Method. The study employs spin-space group theory for symmetry analysis, Floquet theory for periodic driving, and the Random-Phase Approximation (RPA) to analyze magnetic instabilities.

Model / system. A two-sublattice, two-orbital, two-spin (eight-band) Hamiltonian on a 2D hexagonal lattice, specifically focusing on collinear spin-orbital magnets with zero net magnetization.

Key observables. Spin-resolved ARPES (spin-splitting symmetry) and spin-resolved orbital Edelstein effects (SOEE).

Important parameters / regimes. Staggered sublattice potential (delta), light helicity (eta), and spin-orbital order parameters (O1 through O6).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a high-frequency off-resonant regime for the Floquet-driven system and focuses on the collinear magnetic limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 classifies collinear antiferromagnets by symmetry; Figure 4 illustrates the symmetry-dependent Orbital Edelstein Effect (OEE) in different spin-orbital phases under varying staggered potentials.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the mixed-parity problem, establishes symmetry conditions using group theory, introduces a microscopic two-orbital model, analyzes the effects of driving with circularly polarized light, and concludes with experimental probes like the orbital Edelstein effect.

Abstract

Altermagnetism has so far mainly been understood in its even- and odd-parity forms. We show that collinear antiferromagnets with zero net magnetization can also host mixed-parity spin splitting, namely neither purely even nor purely odd in momentum. We identify the symmetry conditions for such mixed-parity altermagnetism and show that, in two dimensions, it can arise in spin-orbital magnets when the two antiparallel spin sectors are related by a single mirror symmetry. Using a two-sublattice two-orbital model, we demonstrate that circularly polarized light induces mixed-parity altermagnetism at finite staggered potential and odd-parity spin-orbital altermagnetism at zero staggered potential. Mixed-parity altermagnetism thereby emerges as the intermediate spin-split regime between even- and odd-parity altermagnetism when spin splitting and zero net magnetization are maintained. Spin-resolved orbital Edelstein effects provide a complementary electrical probe of the underlying spin-orbital order.

Nonlocal transport phenomena in coupled quasiperiodic Kitaev chains

Authors: Koki Mizuno

Summary. This paper explores how Fibonacci quasiperiodicity influences the topological properties of coupled Kitaev chains. It reveals that while Majorana signatures like zero-bias peaks are robust, quasiperiodicity can induce new phase transitions and cause asymmetric Majorana wave-function seepage between the chains.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates topological phase transitions and nonlocal transport phenomena in coupled quasiperiodic Kitaev chains, specifically focusing on how Fibonacci quasiperiodicity affects Majorana edge modes and wave-function seepage.

Main result. The authors identify new topological phase transitions induced by quasiperiodicity and discover asymmetric Majorana wave-function seepage between chains, while finding that zero-bias conductance peaks remain robust against quasiperiodic modulation.

Method. The study employs the Keldysh formalism and the recursive Green's function method to calculate differential conductance, alongside the pseudospectral method to determine topological indices.

Model / system. A coupled one-dimensional p-wave superconducting Fibonacci quasicrystal consisting of a superconducting (S) chain and a normal (N) chain, modeled via a Bogoliubov-de Gennes Hamiltonian with interchain hopping and pairing.

Key observables. Local and nonlocal differential conductance, zero-bias conductance peaks (ZBCP), and the topological index (nu).

Important parameters / regimes. Interchain hopping (tau_int), quasiperiodicity parameters (rho_S, rho_N), chemical potentials (mu_S, mu_N), and the ratio of hopping to pair potential (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. The energy gap is determined by the average value of the real-space pair potential, and the system is treated within a tight-binding framework with open boundary conditions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the schematic of the coupled system; Figure 2 compares differential conductance for various lead connection patterns in periodic and quasiperiodic cases; Figure 6 illustrates topological phase transitions; Figure 8 compares ZBCP in periodic and quasiperiodic chains.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the definition of the coupled Hamiltonian and quasiperiodic model, proceeds to derive the analytical framework for conductance using Keldysh Green's functions, analyzes topological invariants via the pseudospectral method, and concludes with a discussion on the equivalence to s-wave proximity-induced systems.

Abstract

We investigate the topological phases in a coupled one-dimensional p-wave superconducting Fibonacci quasicrystal modeled by the quasiperiodic Kitaev chain. Recent studies have shown that the coupled system can host topological edge modes with Majorana fermions and enhance their topological protection, depending on the pattern of quasiperiodicity. In this work, we elucidate the topological phases of the coupled system and demonstrate the dependence of differential conductance on the lead connecting pattern employing the Keldysh formalism and the recursive Green's function method. Our findings reveal the emergence of topological phases in the coupled system, which are characterized by the presence of Majorana edge modes and the seepage of the Majorana wave function. Furthermore, we identify a new topological phase transition induced by quasiperiodicity in the coupled system.

Summary. The researchers developed a method to create 2D magnonic crystals by coating 3D nanopyramids with a continuous magnetic film, avoiding the material loss of traditional etching. This approach allows for the creation of tunable band gaps and localized spin-wave modes through geometric curvature. This platform offers a versatile way to design components for future magnonic computing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Engineering the spin-wave dispersion relation and band structure in 2D magnonic crystals without removing material, which typically reduces spin-wave decay lengths.

Main result. Demonstrated that 3D curvature in a continuous Permalloy film can create a complete in-plane band gap and localized flat-band modes, with the ability to tune the gap via an external magnetic field.

Method. Experimental micro-focused Brillouin Light Scattering (BLS) and MOKE microscopy combined with 3D micromagnetic simulations using the finite-element method.

Model / system. A 50-nm-thick Permalloy thin film conformally grown on a 3D curvilinear template consisting of a square lattice of nonmagnetic aluminum nanopyramids (400 nm period).

Key observables. Spin-wave dispersion relations, frequency-dependent BLS signal intensity, magnetic domain patterns, and density of states.

Important parameters / regimes. 400 nm lattice period, 50 nm film thickness, external magnetic field (0 to 200 mT), and saturation magnetization of 740 kA/m.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a continuous film approximation and the reliance on reconstructed 3D SEM images for micromagnetic modeling accuracy.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows fabrication and domain imaging; Figure 2 compares FMR response of planar vs. curvilinear films; Figure 3 presents simulated dispersion and magnetization; Figure 4 shows field-dependent BLS spectra; Figure 5 illustrates real-space spin-wave localization.

Paper structure. The paper begins with fabrication and structural characterization, moves to magnetic property measurements (MOKE/VSM), presents spin-wave dispersion and band gap analysis via BLS and simulations, and concludes with evidence of spatial mode localization.

Abstract

Engineering the dispersion relation is one of the key ingredients enabling the application of spin waves in computational elements. One way to engineer the spin-wave band structure is to create an artificial magnonic crystal, which can be used to design specific band gaps or dispersion branches. However, creating a two-dimensional magnonic crystal usually requires removing material, which dramatically decreases the decay lengths of spin waves. Here, we present a method to manipulate the demagnetizing field landscape by utilizing large-area curvilinear nanotemplates consisting of three-dimensional nanopyramids arranged in a square lattice with a period of 400 nm. In a 50-nm-thick Permalloy film grown on these curvilinear templates, we experimentally observe a complete in-plane band gap together with flat-band modes that exhibit strong real-space localization of the spin waves in the pyramid valleys. Micro-focused Brillouin light scattering measurements corroborate the numerically predicted dispersion and reveal the possibility of opening and closing this gap by varying the external magnetic field. Our results establish three-dimensional-templated continuous films as a versatile platform for two-dimensional signal processing and magnonic computing elements.

Spin-wave bandgap engineering via mode hybridization in dipolar-coupled YIG film/CoFeB nanodisk magnonic crystals

Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04774v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers show that spin-wave bandgaps in a hybrid YIG/CoFeB magnonic crystal can be created through mode hybridization rather than traditional Bragg scattering. This mechanism allows for highly tunable and reconfigurable spin-wave transport by adjusting the geometry and magnetic state of the nanodisks. This provides a new pathway for designing advanced magnonic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Controlling spin-wave propagation in magnonic crystals by moving beyond conventional Bragg scattering-based bandgap formation.

Main result. Demonstrated that spin-wave bandgaps can be engineered via mode hybridization between fundamental magnonic-crystal modes and in-plane transverse standing modes induced by nanodisks.

Method. A combination of spin-wave spectroscopy, SNS-MOKE microscopy for 2D mapping, micromagnetic simulations (MuMax3), and Finite Element Method (FEM) simulations.

Model / system. A hybrid 2D magnonic crystal consisting of a low-damping YIG film coupled to a periodic array of CoFeB nanodisks, where the nanodisks can exist in various magnetic states, including a vortex configuration.

Key observables. Spin-wave transmission spectra (S21 parameters), 2D spin-wave maps, magnetization hysteresis loops, and spin-wave dispersion relations.

Important parameters / regimes. Disk diameter (d), lattice period (p), external magnetic field (Hext), and nanodisk magnetization state (vortex configuration).

Assumptions / limitations. The study operates in the linear spin-wave regime with excitation power set to -20 dBm.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Device geometry and SEM image of the array; Fig 2: MOKE hysteresis loops and magnetization evolution; Fig 6: Bare-film vs. frozen-spin dispersion; Fig 7: Spatial mode profiles and dispersion-related anticrossings.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the hybrid architecture, details the experimental and simulation methodologies, analyzes the magnetization dynamics of the nanodisks, demonstrates the formation of non-Bragg bandgaps through mode hybridization, and explores the effects of geometric tuning and magnetic state on the band structure.

Abstract

We investigate spin-wave transport in hybrid two-dimensional magnonic crystals comprising a low-damping yttrium iron garnet (YIG) film coupled to a periodic array of CoFeB nanodisks. Using propagating spin-wave spectroscopy, super-Nyquist magneto-optical Kerr effect microscopy, and micromagnetic simulations, we demonstrate the formation of pronounced and tunable bandgaps that do not originate from conventional Bragg scattering. Instead, these gaps arise from hybridization between the fundamental magnonic-crystal mode and in-plane transverse standing modes induced by the periodic nanodisk array. The spectral position and width of these gaps are controlled by geometric parameters and by the magnetic state of the nanodisks, including their vortex configuration, which governs both static and dynamic dipolar coupling. For larger lattice periods, additional gaps emerge through hybridization with modes quantized both transverse and parallel to the spin-wave propagation direction, reflecting dispersion folding in two dimensions. Our results establish mode hybridization as a versatile mechanism for engineering spin-wave band structures beyond the constraints of Bragg scattering and provide a pathway toward reconfigurable magnonic devices based on dipolar-coupled hybrid architectures.

Summary. The researchers developed a new way to grow vertical 2D heterostructures by using the way boron moves through iridium during cooling. This method allows them to place a layer of borophene underneath a layer of hBN, which also protects the sensitive borophene from oxidation. This 'growth from below' approach offers a scalable route for creating complex, multi-layered 2D materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Direct synthesis of vertical 2D material heterostructures via CVD is difficult because the first layer often suppresses the catalytic decomposition of precursors needed for subsequent layers.

Main result. The authors demonstrated a 'growth from below' method using intrinsic segregation to synthesize a vertical Ir(111)/borophene/hBN heterostructure, where hBN acts as a protective cap for the underlying borophene.

Method. A two-step ultrahigh vacuum CVD process using borazine as a single-source precursor, involving high-temperature boron dissolution followed by lower-temperature hBN growth and cooling-induced segregation.

Model / system. A vertical heterostructure consisting of a single-crystalline Ir(111) substrate, an intermediate borophene layer with (6x2) reconstruction, and an overlying hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) monolayer.

Key observables. SPA-LEED diffraction patterns (moiré spots and borophene periodicity), STM real-space morphology and height profiles, and LEEM real-time growth imaging.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature regimes of 1140 C (dissolution), 960 C (hBN growth), and 890 C (segregation); precursor pressures between 1e-7 and 1e-6 mbar.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows SPA-LEED patterns and theoretical convolution models for the heterostructure; Fig 2 displays STM images and height profiles of the layers; Fig 4 shows LEEM images of the Ir(111) surface during the growth process.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the synthesis challenge, describes the two-step CVD process and the intrinsic segregation mechanism, presents structural characterization via LEED and STM to confirm vertical stacking, demonstrates the protective role of hBN via stability tests, and concludes with the implications for scalable 2D heterostructure growth.

Abstract

We report on a two-step ultrahigh vacuum chemical vapor deposition synthesis of a vertical Ir(111)/borophene/hexagonal boron nitride heterostructure, using borazine as a single-source precursor. The process takes advantage of the finite solubility of boron in Ir: low precursor pressure at high temperature first establishes a boron reservoir in the near-surface region of the substrate, whereas subsequent growth at higher precursor pressure promotes the formation of a closed hexagonal boron nitride monolayer. During cooldown, the reduced boron solubility drives segregation to the surface, resulting in the formation of a borophene monolayer beneath the hexagonal boron nitride overlayer. The heterostructure, with micron sized grains, homogeneously covers the entire Ir substrate. The study is performed by complementary spot profile analysis low-energy electron diffraction, low-energy electron microscopy, and scanning tunneling microscopy measurements. This intrinsic segregation-assisted growth concept provides a promising route toward scalable synthesis of high-quality, vertical heterostructures of two-dimensional materials.

Building informative materials datasets beyond targeted objectives

Authors: Rafael Espinosa Castañeda, Ashley Dale, Hongchen Wang, Yonatan Kurniawan, Hao Wan, Runze Zhang, Adji Bousso Dieng, Kangming Li, Jason Hattrick-Simpers
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05104v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new method for building materials datasets that balances immediate research goals with long-term utility. By incorporating feature-space diversity into the selection process, the framework prevents the sampling bias that typically makes datasets useless for predicting properties not explicitly targeted during collection.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Materials science data collection often focuses on specific target properties, creating sampling bias that reduces the utility of datasets for future, unanticipated scientific tasks.

Main result. A diversity-aware framework improves predictive performance on untargeted properties by up to 10% and prevents the 40% performance degradation seen in purely targeted strategies, especially in noisy experimental datasets.

Method. The authors use a multi-objective optimization approach with the NSGA-II genetic algorithm to balance target property informativeness with feature-space diversity (using cosine dissimilarity).

Model / system. The study evaluates the framework using DFT-based repositories (Materials Project, JARVIS) and the experimental sysTEm thermoelectric dataset, utilizing structural and compositional descriptors via Voronoi tessellation.

Key observables. Electronic bandgap, elastic bulk modulus, formation energy, thermoelectric figure of merit (zT), thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, and Seebeck coefficient.

Important parameters / regimes. High-dimensional feature space (up to 273 dimensions), iterative selection of 1% of the pool per iteration, and weighting between informativeness and diversity.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that chosen properties are weakly linearly correlated to ensure true cross-objective generalization; assumes the utility of cosine similarity for diversity in high-dimensional spaces.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the data selection pipeline, RMSE curves for various properties, and comparisons of chemical manifold coverage (PCA space) between targeted and diversity-aware sampling.

Paper structure. Introduction of the sampling bias problem, description of the diversity-aware framework and NSGA-II implementation, evaluation on DFT and experimental datasets, and a detailed appendix of performance metrics across various property combinations.

Abstract

Materials science data collection can be expensive, making the reuse and long-term utility of datasets critical important for future discovery campaigns. In practice, researchers prioritize a subset of properties due to research interests. However, ignoring a subset of outcomes in data collection campaigns potentially generate datasets poorly suited for future learning tasks. Here, we present a framework for dataset construction that maximizes informativeness for target properties of interest while preserving performance on untargeted ones. Our approach uses diversity-aware selection to ensure broad coverage of the materials space. In noisy experimental dataset construction, we find that without our diversity-aware framework, prediction performance on untargeted properties can degrade by up to 40% relative to random sampling, whereas applying our framework yields improvements of up to 10% . For targeted properties, performance can degrade with respect to random sampling by up to 12.5% without diversity, while our framework achieves gains of up to 25%. Incorporating diversity into dataset construction not only preserves informativeness for the targeted properties, but also improves materials coverage for potential future objectives. As a result, the constructed datasets remain broadly informative across considered and unconsidered outcomes, ensuring unbiased quality entries and mitigating cold-start limitations in subsequent modeling and discovery campaigns.

Summary. This paper identifies 'kwinking' (coordinated dislocation slip and twinning) as the key mechanism behind the exceptional plastic deformability of Nitinol. By connecting microscopic lattice defects to macroscopic technological advantages, it provides a unified explanation for various unusual deformation phenomena in NiTi alloys.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the 'kwinking' mechanism—a combination of dislocation slip and twinning—that drives plastic deformation in the B19' martensite phase of NiTi alloys.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that 'kwinking' is the fundamental driver of NiTi's unique high ductility and explains long-standing phenomena like amorphization and high-degree cold working.

Method. A combination of 15 years of experimental research using TEM, in-situ synchrotron X-ray diffraction, and thermomechanical tensile testing, alongside theoretical analysis of deformation gradients.

Model / system. NiTi (Nitinol) shape memory alloys, specifically focusing on the B2 cubic austenite and B19' monoclinic martensite phases in nanocrystalline and cold-worked wires.

Key observables. Plastic strain, yield stress, (20-1) deformation bands, lattice rotations, martensite variant microstructures, and transformation temperatures (Af).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (ranging from cryogenic to >300 C), applied stress, dislocation slip systems (100M), and twinning-induced strain.

Assumptions / limitations. The direct TEM observation of specific dislocation defects is limited by their tendency to be bound to habit planes or grain boundaries.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the evolution of nanocrystalline NiTi during tensile testing; Figure 2 illustrates electro-pulse heat treatment effects; Figure 3 compares different grain elongation mechanisms; Figure 4 compares twinning vs. kwinking paths; Figure 5 shows B2-B19' crystallography.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the kwinking mechanism, traces its role in historical NiTi phenomena, details the microscopic mechanics of slip and twinning, discusses microstructural control via heat treatment, and concludes with implications for constitutive modeling and technology.

Abstract

Nitinol technology, besides utilizing the functional thermomechanical properties derived from the B2 cubic to B19' monoclinic martensitic transformation, also exploits the excellent plastic deformability of NiTi in the martensite state. It originates from the unique mechanism of plastic deformation of the B19' martensite by kwinking involving dislocation slip based kinking assisted by deformation twinning. Although the mechanism of plastic deformation of martensite by kwinking was revealed only very recently, various unusual phenomena that can only be rationalized by kwinking, have been reported in literature in the last 50 years. These phenomena include: 1) cold working with a high degree of reduction without introducing cracks, 2) excellent plastic deformability in the martensite state (plastic deformation up to~80% strain at stresses >1GPa), 3) refinement of austenitic microstructure to a quasi-amorphous state by tensile deformation, 4) observation of high density of {114} deformation bands in austenitic microstructures, 5) systematic ruptures of strengthened NiTi wires in tensile tests via necking at the onset of plastic yielding, 6) localized plastic deformation in tensile tests via propagation of Lüders band fronts with very large localized strain (~40%), 7) unusually long upper stress plateaus in superelastic tensile tests (>8% strain), 8) large plastic strains (> 20 %) generated in a single closed-loop cooling/heating cycle under constant stress, 9) shape setting of already annealed NiTi by heating under external constraint. Finally, we discuss how kwinking deformation was considered in constitutive modelling of thermomechanical behaviors of NiTi and, particularly, what is the role of the kwinking deformation in NiTi technology.

Predicting the Brittle-to-Ductile Transition in Amorphous Polymers

Authors: Valeriy V. Ginzburg, Oleg Gendelman, Alessio Zaccone
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04753v1

Summary. This paper proposes a simple scalar model to predict the brittle-to-ductile transition in amorphous polymers by identifying it as a stability limit of uniform flow. The model links the transition directly to the beta-relaxation timescale. It successfully reproduces experimental data for several common polymers.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a simple, unified model to predict the Brittle-to-Ductile Transition (BDT) in amorphous polymers as a function of chemistry, strain rate, and temperature.

Main result. The authors identify the BDT as the upper bound on the strain rate for uniform viscoplastic flow, which is inversely proportional to the Johari-Goldstein beta-relaxation time.

Method. A theoretical approach treating the BDT as a stability limit (similar to a spinodal line) using a scalar 1D visco-elasto-plastic model.

Model / system. The system consists of amorphous polymers (PS, PMMA, PVC) modeled via the Sanchez-Lacombe 'two-state, two-time-scale' (SL-TS2) framework, treating the material as a dynamic coexistence of solid and liquid domains.

Key observables. Stress-strain curves, yield stress, brittle fracture stress, and BDT temperature.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), strain rate (S), glass transition temperature (Tg), alpha and beta relaxation times, and the efficiency parameter (x).

Assumptions / limitations. The model is a 1D scalar approximation, assumes volume-preserving (incompressible) deformation, and neglects complex fracture mechanics like crack nucleation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows stress relaxation; Figure 2 shows the three mechanical response types; Figure 3 shows normalized elastic stress; Figure 4 shows the determinant function for phase boundaries; Figure 5 shows a phase diagram in the (B, Y) plane; Figures 6-8 compare model predictions to experimental BDT data for specific polymers.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the BDT problem, defines a 1D visco-elasto-plastic model based on SL-TS2 theory, derives a mathematical criterion for the loss of stability in flow, establishes a phase diagram for different mechanical regimes, and validates the model against experimental data for PS, PMMA, and PVC.

Abstract

Brittle-ductile transition (BDT) is an important characteristic of amorphous (and semicrystalline) polymers. For a given strain rate, at temperatures above BDT, the polymers exhibit strain softening followed by yield and strain hardening, while at temperatures below BDT, the same materials exhibit brittle failure at relatively low strains. Surprisingly, today there is no simple model describing BDT as a function of polymer chemistry, sample history, deformation type, and strain rate. Experimental data suggest that BDT is often, though not always, associated with the beta-transition. We formulate a simple scalar model to describe the visco-elasto-plastic shear stress-strain curves as functions of temperature and strain rate. We also show that within this model, there is always an upper bound on the strain rate where the material can have a uniform viscoplastic flow; this upper bound is taken to represent the BDT. We stipulate that this upper bound is inversely proportional to the Johari-Goldstein beta-relaxation time. Using our "general" Sanchez-Lacombe "two-state, two-(time)scale" (SL-TS2) model, we compute the BDT for three polymers (polystyrene, poly(methylmethacrylate), and poly(vinylchloride)) and found a good agreement with experimental data.
