Fundamental Limitations of Post-Quantum Cryptographic Architectures

Authors: Jiho Jung, Donghwa Ji, Mingyu Lee, Kabgyun Jeong
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04582v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper argues that the security of lattice-based post-quantum cryptography is not mathematically absolute but depends on the current difficulty of performing complex quantum state preparations. By framing LWE noise as a type of error that can be corrected using GKP-style protocols, the authors suggest that advancements in quantum error correction could eventually undermine these cryptographic foundations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep connection between cryptographic noise and physical noise in continuous-variable quantum systems, suggesting that quantum error correction protocols could be used to break lattice-based encryption.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper challenges the claim that lattice-based cryptography, specifically the Learning with Errors (LWE) paradigm, is unconditionally secure against quantum adversaries.

Main result. The authors argue that LWE security relies on transient physical and engineering bottlenecks, such as the difficulty of state preparation and QRAM access, rather than an impenetrable mathematical boundary, as the injected noise can be treated as a correctable displacement error.

Method. The study uses a combination of quantum complexity theory (T-depth analysis), information-theoretic thermodynamics (Landauer's principle), and quantum error correction analogies (GKP codes) to analyze the vulnerability of LWE.

Model / system. The model treats LWE samples as a coherent physical superposition state and maps the cryptographic Gaussian noise to continuous-variable displacement errors in a quantum Hilbert space, comparable to GKP error-correcting architectures.

Key observables. Probability amplitude of the target basis state, interference patterns of the error distribution, and T-depth complexity.

Important parameters / regimes. Integer modulus q, dimension n, error distribution chi, and the T-depth of quantum gate layers.

Assumptions / limitations. The argument assumes that a sufficiently coherent quantum computer can successfully map classical LWE instances into a continuous-variable Hilbert space.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the algebraic structure of the LWE problem with added error; Figure 2 illustrates complexity classes and the LWE/BQP relationship; Figure 3 compares Shannon's communication model to the LWE framework.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from the algebraic definition of LWE to a quantum superposition model, applies the QFT to demonstrate error amplification, identifies state preparation as a physical bottleneck, and concludes by mapping LWE to error-correctable continuous-variable systems.

Abstract

Modern lattice-based cryptography, particularly the learning with errors paradigm, relies on injecting artificial noise to secure data against quantum adversaries. This study systematically examines the theoretical and physical boundaries of this noise-reliant model across four interconnected domains: computational complexity, information-theoretic thermodynamics, quantum error correction, and quantum learning theory. Starting from the algorithmic foundation, our analysis notes that these frameworks rely on provisional complexity-theoretic assumptions that remain vulnerable to future quantum algorithmic advancements. Furthermore, by translating this cryptographic mechanism into physical thermodynamics, we illustrate that intentionally injected discrete Gaussian noise does not equate to the permanent erasure of information. Because the structural integrity of the cryptographic secret remains preserved within the ciphertext, advanced quantum error correction protocols and quantum learning models can efficiently extract the underlying mathematical kernel. Ultimately, we suggest that while lattice-based cryptography provides a robust transitional alternative, definitively classifying these frameworks as unconditionally post-quantum represents a premature classification relying on transient physical bottlenecks rather than impenetrable theoretical boundaries.

Scalar and Vector Airborne Platform Calibration Using Quantum and Classical Magnetometers and Inertial Sensors

Authors: Antonia Hager, Torleiv H. Bryne, Mia Jukić
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04951v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5 · QC/QI experiment 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper evaluates how different magnetometer calibration models perform under realistic airborne conditions. It demonstrates that while 3D vector models are highly sensitive to orientation errors, 1D scalar models are much more robust, and it highlights the potential for NV sensors to improve sensor integration in magnetic navigation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the fundamental error sources in airborne magnetic platform calibration, specifically comparing the robustness of 1D (scalar) versus 3D (vector) Tolles-Lawson calibration models against attitude and sensor errors.

Main result. The 1D scalar model is found to be highly robust to attitude errors, whereas the 3D vector model is intrinsically sensitive to first-order attitude inaccuracies. While NV sensors may not solve the 'attitude bottleneck,' they offer advantages in sensor integration and synchronization.

Method. The authors use a rigorous theoretical analysis of error dependencies combined with a simulation framework that employs realistic sensor noise models, frequency-domain noise synthesis, and simulated aircraft flight trajectories.

Model / system. The system is an airborne platform (aircraft/UAV) equipped with various magnetometers (OPM, Fluxgate, and NV diamond sensors) and an Inertial Navigation System (INS). The magnetic interference is modeled using the Tolles-Lawson model, accounting for permanent, induced, and eddy-current components.

Key observables. Calibration residuals (error in nT), total magnetic field, Earth's magnetic field, and attitude/angular errors.

Important parameters / regimes. Earth's field magnitude (50,000 nT), platform interference field, angular error (alpha), and sensor noise parameters (white noise floor, corner frequency, and spectral slope).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on static platform field components and ignores time-varying electronic/temperature-induced nonlinearities; it also assumes a constant, uniform geomagnetic background field.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the impact of field angle on projection errors; Figure 3/4 detail the sensor noise and error generation pipeline; Figure 6 shows sensor stability (ASD/ADEV); Figure 7/12 illustrate simulated flight trajectories and calibration/validation results.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a theoretical derivation of error dependencies in scalar and vector models, followed by the description of the sensor and noise models, the simulation framework for flight trajectories, a comparison of calibration results, and a discussion of the utility of NV sensors.

Abstract

Airborne magnetometry requires rigorous calibration to isolate geomagnetic signals from sensor errors and platform magnetic fields. This magnetic compensation is needed for applications like geophysical exploration and magnetic anomaly navigation. The standard approach utilizes a quantum scalar Optically Pumped Magnetometer (OPM) and a less sensitive fluxgate vector sensor for attitude information. This configuration typically results in a scalar approximation of the platform field. Advancements in high-sensitivity Diamond Nitrogen-Vacancy (NV) vector magnetometers now enable a re-evaluation of the standard hardware configuration and full vector calibration models. We show through rigorous theoretical analysis that scalar calibration models are robust to misalignment. Vector calibration models, however, are intrinsically first-order sensitive to attitude errors, irrespective of the accuracy of the magnetic field measurements. These errors arise from inaccurate representation of the background field direction in the body frame, and can amplify small orientation errors into noticeable calibration residuals. Using realistic sensor models and flight trajectories, we evaluate different sensor configurations for magnetic calibration and assess the use of onboard Inertial Navigation Systems (INS) as an independent attitude reference to enable stable compensation. Our results suggest that quantum vector magnetometers like NV sensors are not sufficient to solve the attitude bottleneck for airborne vector magnetic calibration. However, as a single sensor capable of providing both absolute field and directional measurements, they may offer benefits regarding sensor placement, synchronization, and scalar calibration accuracy.

Confidence uncertainty: position and momentum can be jointly determined with a guaranteed probability

Authors: Jia-Yi Lin, Xin-Yu Li, Wei Wang, Shengjun Wu
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04484v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a new way to measure uncertainty based on the probability of finding a particle within specific intervals rather than just the spread of the distribution. It reveals that while position and momentum can be jointly localized to arbitrary precision if the total confidence is low, a strict limit emerges when the required confidence levels are high. This provides a tighter alternative to the traditional Heisenberg uncertainty principle for specific probability-based applications.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a more operational way to understand joint localization in phase space, which is highly relevant for quantum optics and precision measurements where the probability of finding a particle in a specific window is more critical than the variance.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the limitations of standard-deviation and entropic uncertainty principles by quantifying the probability of finding a particle within specific small regions of position and momentum.

Main result. The authors introduce 'confidence uncertainty' and prove a two-regime phase diagram: for confidence levels where theta_x + theta_p <= 1, position and momentum can be jointly localized arbitrarily well, whereas for theta_x + theta_p > 1, a strict positive lower bound applies.

Method. The study utilizes Lenard's projection inequality, the Donoho-Stark operator-norm bound, and Slepian-Pollak-Landau theory involving prolate-spheroidal wave functions.

Model / system. A quantum particle in a pure state described by position (x) and momentum (p) representations.

Key observables. Confidence uncertainty (minimal Lebesgue measure of the support set), interval confidence uncertainty, and the probability mass (theta_x, theta_p) within specific regions.

Important parameters / regimes. Confidence levels (theta_x, theta_p), the time-bandwidth product (c), and the transition boundary defined by theta_x + theta_p = 1.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary results focus on pure quantum states, though the framework can be extended to mixed states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a phase diagram of the (theta_x, theta_p) plane; Figure 2 displays the largest Slepian eigenvalue on linear and log scales; Figure 3 compares the new Landau-Pollak bound against the elementary bound.

Paper structure. The paper introduces new uncertainty measures, establishes a phase diagram for two distinct regimes, provides analytical proofs for lower bounds using signal processing theory, performs numerical evaluations of Slepian eigenvalues, and compares the new bounds against established Heisenberg, entropic, and Donoho-Stark bounds.

Abstract

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle states that the position and momentum of a particle cannot be sharply determined simultaneously. Standard-deviation and entropic formulations capture the spread of the probability distribution but say little about the probability itself contained in a small region. We introduce the "confidence uncertainty" $Δ^{c}x(θ_x)$ as the minimal Lebesgue measure of the support set in which the particle is found with probability at least $θ_x$, and the companion "interval confidence uncertainty" $Δ^{I}x(θ_x)$ which restricts the support to a single interval. We prove two complementary uncertainty inequalities. (i) For $θ_x+θ_p\le 1$ both confidence uncertainties can be made arbitrarily small simultaneously, so that no nontrivial product bound holds; in particular, position and momentum can be jointly localised with probability at least~$50\%$. (ii) For $θ_x+θ_p>1$ a lower bound holds: combining Lenard's projection inequality with the Donoho--Stark operator-norm bound we obtain $Δ^{c}x\,Δ^{c}p\geq 2π\hbar\bigl(\sqrt{θ_xθ_p}-\sqrt{(1-θ_x)(1-θ_p)}\bigr)^{\!2}$, and for the interval version we obtain the sharp implicit Landau--Pollak bound $Δ^{I}x\,Δ^{I}p\geq 4\hbar\,λ_{0}^{-1}\!\bigl((\sqrt{θ_xθ_p}-\sqrt{(1-θ_x)(1-θ_p)})^{2}\bigr)$, where $λ_{0}(c)$ is the largest prolate-spheroidal eigenvalue. We support the analytical bounds with numerical evaluation of $λ_{0}(c)$, provide closed-form small-$c$ and large-$c$ asymptotics, compute the optimal Slepian-superposition states that saturate the interval bound, and compare the resulting product against the variance Heisenberg--Kennard, the Białynicki-Birula--Mycielski entropic, and the Donoho--Stark concentration bounds.

Dynamical pseudopotentials

Authors: Matteo Quinzi, Tommaso Chiarotti, Nicola Marzari
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04673v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new class of energy-dependent pseudopotentials that improve the transferability of first-principles calculations to high-energy and excited states. By using a sum-over-poles approach, it achieves high accuracy without the numerical instabilities typical of multi-projector methods. This provides a unified and consistent treatment of all-electron and pseudo-atom properties within many-body theory.

Why it may be interesting. The use of an embedding-problem formulation and the treatment of energy-dependent potentials are highly relevant to many-body dynamics and the development of frequency-dependent self-energies in correlated systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Conventional static pseudopotentials struggle with transferability in excited-state calculations and high-energy states, often suffering from numerical instabilities when increasing the number of projectors.

Main result. The authors develop a framework for dynamical (energy-dependent) pseudopotentials using a sum-over-poles representation that decouples the number of reference energies from the number of projectors, ensuring accurate all-electron scattering over wide energy ranges.

Method. The method uses an embedding-problem formulation, a sum-over-poles (SOP) representation, and an algorithmic-inversion method to solve the nonlinear eigenvalue problem, combined with a variational strategy to optimize the basis set.

Model / system. The framework treats the electronic structure of atoms and solids as an embedding problem where valence electrons are coupled to an auxiliary bath representing core degrees of freedom via an energy-dependent potential.

Key observables. Logarithmic derivative of the potential, dynamical augmentation charges, radial momentum distribution, and spectral density.

Important parameters / regimes. Core radius (Rc), reference energies, number of poles, and number of projectors.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes spherical symmetry for atomic calculations and assumes the availability of radial all-electron and pseudo-orbitals.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the logarithmic derivative vs. energy for Cu and Er, demonstrating the match between AE and dynPP; supplementary figures show radial momentum distributions and wavefunction profiles.

Paper structure. Introduction of the dynamical embedding perspective, derivation of generalized norm-conservation, introduction of the sum-over-poles representation and algorithmic inversion, demonstration of numerical stability and efficiency, and validation via momentum-space analysis.

Abstract

Pseudopotential theory has greatly driven first-principles calculations in materials, replacing the explicit treatment of the chemically inert core electrons with an effective potential acting only on the valence states. This is inherently an embedding problem, where tracing out the core electrons can be formulated in terms of a dynamical embedding potential. Motivated by this perspective, we first introduce a framework for dynamical (i.e., energy-dependent) pseudopotentials, showing how this leads to generalized norm-conservation conditions. Then, adopting a sum-over-poles representation, we disentangle the number of reference energies from the number of projectors; this allows to reproduce all-electron scattering at many reference energies with great accuracy and over very extended energy ranges. We further show that these pseudopotentials enter naturally into many-body total energy functionals, leading for the first time to a consistent and unified treatment of the all-electron atom, the pseudo-atom, and the solid within the same electronic-structure theory.

Estimating the expected output of wide random MLPs more efficiently than sampling

Authors: Wilson Wu, Victor Lecomte, Michael Winer, George Robinson, Jacob Hilton, Paul Christiano
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05179v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a way to calculate the expected behavior of large random neural networks without actually running samples through them. By propagating the statistical properties (cumulants) of the activations through the layers, the authors achieve much higher computational efficiency and better accuracy for predicting rare, high-risk events.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently estimating the expected output and loss of wide, random multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) without the high computational cost of Monte Carlo sampling.

Main result. The authors develop a sample-free cumulant propagation method that achieves a target mean squared error using significantly fewer FLOPs than Monte Carlo sampling, specifically outperforming it by a factor of n in the large-width limit.

Method. The method uses cumulant propagation via Hermite expansions and diagram summation to track the distribution of activations layer-by-layer, including factorized versions to reduce complexity.

Model / system. Wide, random, fully-connected feedforward MLPs with L hidden layers and width n, using He initialization and Gaussian inputs.

Key observables. Expected output (quenched average), expected loss, and higher-order cumulants (mean, variance, etc.).

Important parameters / regimes. Network width (n), network depth (L), maximum cumulant order (K), and error tolerance (epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes the large-width limit (n to infinity), fixed depth, and primarily focuses on polynomial or 'tame' activation functions like ReLU.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares MSE vs FLOPs for various algorithms against Monte Carlo; Figure 2 shows MSE vs FLOPs for different depths; Figure 3 shows MSE scaling with width n; Figure 4 compares relative RMSE for rare event probabilities; Figure 5 compares distillation learning curves.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of sampling inefficiency, presents the cumulant propagation algorithm and its factorized variants, provides theoretical complexity and error bounds using diagrammatic expansions, and demonstrates empirical superiority and applications in mechanistic distillation.

Abstract

By far the most common way to estimate an expected loss in machine learning is to draw samples, compute the loss on each one, and take the empirical average. However, sampling is not necessarily optimal. Given an MLP at initialization, we show how to estimate its expected output over Gaussian inputs without running samples through the network at all. Instead, we produce approximate representations of the distributions of activations at each layer, leveraging tools such as cumulants and Hermite expansions. We show both theoretically and empirically that for sufficiently wide networks, our estimator achieves a target mean squared error using substantially fewer FLOPs than Monte Carlo sampling. We find moreover that our methods perform particularly well at estimating the probabilities of rare events, and additionally demonstrate how they can be used for model training. Together, these findings suggest a path to producing models with a greatly reduced probability of catastrophic tail risks.

Finite-size scaling properties of classical random walk on various two-dimensional lattices

Authors: Nimish Sharma, Tanay Nag
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04492v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper examines how the geometry of 2D lattices influences the fractal properties of classical random walks. It demonstrates that while lattice connectivity causes measurable differences in fractal dimensions in finite systems, these differences vanish as the system approaches the thermodynamic limit of Brownian motion.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how different 2D lattice geometries and connectivity patterns affect the finite-size scaling properties, mass fractal dimension, and hull fractal dimension of classical random walks.

Main result. The study finds that while RMSD remains universally diffusive, the mass and hull fractal dimensions exhibit a weak ordering correlated with the average coordination number, though they eventually converge to Brownian motion values in the thermodynamic limit.

Method. Numerical simulations of ensembles of random walkers on various 2D lattices using box-counting methods, log-log scaling analysis, and data collapse techniques.

Model / system. Unbiased, non-self-avoiding classical random walks on various 2D lattices including Square, Kagome, Lieb, Honeycomb, and Dice lattices.

Key observables. Root Mean Square Displacement (RMSD), mass fractal dimension (df), and hull fractal dimension (dh).

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice connectivity (coordination number), number of steps (n), and the thermodynamic limit.

Assumptions / limitations. The random walks are assumed to be unbiased and non-self-avoiding.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the lattice structures; Figure 2 displays RMSD scaling; Figure 3 shows mass fractal dimension via box-counting; Figure 4 shows hull dimension scaling; Figure 6 illustrates the variation of hull dimension with step number and its data collapse.

Paper structure. The paper introduces various 2D lattice geometries, presents simulation results for RMSD and fractal dimensions, analyzes the influence of coordination number on scaling, and concludes with a data collapse analysis demonstrating convergence to the thermodynamic limit.

Abstract

We consider various two-dimensional lattices such as square, Kagome, Lieb, honeycomb, dice lattices of finite extent, to study the effect of lattice profile in terms of the number of nearest neighbour and connectivity patterns on the classical random walk in the unbiased scenario. We find that the standard deviation of distance travelled by the walker is insensitive to the non-uniformity of the lattice profile leading to diffusive transport even in the finite size lattices. Our study indicates that the mass fractal dimension varies within a window $1.50\pm 0.03$ for all finite-size lattices. A weak ordering within the above window, correlated with the average coordination number, is observed, while Lieb and square lattices yielding the minimum and maximum values, respectively. However, confidence intervals reveal substantial statistical overlap for several lattice pairs even though the lattice profiles vary as far as the average number of connecting bonds and directionality of bonds are concerned. We also study the scaling complexity of the circumference of the closed curve traced by the walker while investigating the hull dimension. We find similar trend for hull fractal dimension as well and that was found to within the window $1.37\pm 0.03$ for finite-size lattices. Within the above window, the ordering remains qualitatively unaltered as compared to mass dimension while the confidence interval rectifies the order quantitatively. The square lattice clearly exhibits the upper bound for hull fractal dimension and the remaining lattices show extensive statistical overlap within the above window. We exhibit a tendency of the mass and hull fractal dimension to reach their thermodynamic values given by Brownian motion when we allow more number of steps within the finite size of the lattice, as confirmed by a data collapse analysis.

Magnetic Brightening and Nanoscale Imaging of Spin-Polarized Helical Edge Modes

Authors: Samuel Haeuser, Richard H. J. Kim, Lin-Lin Wang, Thomas Koschny, Pedro M. Lozano, Genda Gu, Randall K. Chan, Joong-Mok Park, Martin Mootz, Liang Luo, Qiang Li, Jigang Wang

Summary. This paper demonstrates the ability to visually map spin-polarized edge states in a topological insulator using infrared near-field microscopy. By applying a magnetic field, the researchers induced a 'brightening' effect that reveals the underlying spin-splitting of the edge modes. This discovery highlights a regime where topological edge conduction remains robust at high frequencies, even when magnetic fields induce gaps that would otherwise suppress DC conduction.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of visualizing and characterizing the nanoscale spin-polarized helical edge modes and their magnetic-field-induced spin-splitting in the weak topological insulator ZrTe5.

Main result. The researchers observed 'magnetic brightening,' where an applied magnetic field enhances near-field conductivity at step edges by lifting spin degeneracy and creating imbalanced AC currents. They also demonstrated that these high-frequency edge states remain robust and scale linearly with the number of atomic layers, unlike DC transport which is suppressed by magnetic gaps.

Method. Cryogenic magneto-infrared scattering-type scanning near-field optical microscopy (cm-IR-sSNOM) was used at temperatures down to 1.8 K and magnetic fields up to 5 T.

Model / system. The physical system is ZrTe5, a weak topological insulator characterized by spin-polarized, counter-propagating helical edge modes at crystal step edges and domain walls.

Key observables. Near-field conductivity, infrared photocurrent oscillations, amplitude and phase contrast, and spatial width of edge modes.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperatures of 1.8-2 K, magnetic fields up to 5 T, photon energies around 100 meV, and atomic layer numbers ranging from 6 to 11.

Assumptions / limitations. The edge states are assumed to be effectively stacked and robust in multi-layer structures due to weak van der Waals coupling.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows a schematic of the AFM probe and the mechanism of magnetic brightening; Fig 2 displays AFM topography and sSNOM images showing enhanced signal at step edges under magnetic fields; Fig 4d visualizes the spatial extent of the magnetically separated edge modes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of nanoscale transport, describes the experimental setup and ZrTe5 system, presents the discovery of magnetic brightening and spin-splitting, validates the results with layer-scaling and control experiments, and concludes with implications for future microelectronics.

Abstract

Efficient sub-10 nm electric transport remains a major challenge for nanoelectronics due to high losses and impedance mismatches in conventional Drude metals. Despite their promise of dissipationless, reflection-free conduction, topologically protected chiral edge modes remain little explored in their nanoscale spin polarized transport-particularly regarding real-space visualization, magnetic field tunability, and high-frequency edge conductivity. Here, we report magnetic brightening and nanoscale visualization of highly spin-polarizable infrared helical edge states using cryogenic magneto-infrared scattering-type scanning near-field optical microscopy (cm-IR-sSNOM). Our measurements reveal magnetic field-induced near-field conductivity at step edges, uncovering quantum spin Hall spin-splitting modes with enhanced infrared polarizability and slightly narrowed near-field profiles. In addition, the infrared edge electrodynamic response scales nearly linearly with atomic layer number, providing compelling evidence that magnetic-field-induced gaps do not disrupt individual-layer edge states at energies of around 100 meV. These results sharply contrast with microwave and DC transport, where even small magnetically induced gaps decrease edge conduction. Magnetically tunable, topologically robust high-frequency edge modes open a pathway toward ultralow-loss nanoscale interconnects and quantum logic architectures for next-generation microelectronics, spintronics and quantum information science.

Quantum Magic in early FTQC: From Diagonal Clifford Hierarchy No-Go Theorems to Architecture Design Blueprints

Authors: Hsueh-Hao Lu, Yasunari Suzuki, Yasunobu Nakamura, En-Jui Kuo
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04758v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a fundamental framework for maximizing non-stabilizer resources in early fault-tolerant quantum computers. It proves that gate hierarchy and state-independent sequences are insufficient for magic generation, establishing that optimal architecture design must be state-aware and utilize multi-qubit interactions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing the generation of quantum magic (non-stabilizerness) in early fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC) architectures by selecting optimal gate sequences and input states.

Main result. The authors prove two no-go theorems: the Clifford hierarchy level alone cannot universally order magic, and no state-independent sequence of operations can guarantee monotonic magic improvement. They also identify a bottleneck in single-qubit rotation architectures and propose multi-qubit diagonal phases as a solution.

Method. The study uses the resource theory of magic, Pauli-spectrum analysis, and the algebraic properties of the Clifford hierarchy, employing symplectic formalism and phase-polynomial representations to derive analytical bounds.

Model / system. The paper analyzes early FTQC circuit models, specifically a shallow-layer model (one Clifford layer followed by a diagonal hierarchy gate) and an N-layer model (alternating Clifford and diagonal layers) inspired by the STAR architecture.

Key observables. Quantum magic (non-stabilizerness), Pauli spectrum (expectation values), stabilizer Renyi entropy, and Pauli rank (support size).

Important parameters / regimes. Clifford hierarchy level (k), number of pure-Z generators (r), number of layers (N), and phase-polynomial parameters.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on pure quantum states and assumes ideal, high-fidelity Clifford operations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the structural classification of the analyzed architectures; Figure 2 compares standard T-doped random circuits with the early FTQC framework; Figure 3 illustrates the organization of magic measures; Figure 4 depicts the shallow-layer and N-layer circuit models.

Paper structure. The paper begins by classifying magic measures and establishing a uniqueness theorem for Pauli-spectrum functionals. It then analyzes the shallow-layer model to derive zero-magic and maximal-magic conditions, followed by an investigation of the N-layer model to present no-go theorems regarding hierarchy and monotonic improvement. Finally, it identifies architectural bottlenecks and proposes multi-qubit rotation strategies.

Abstract

We address the circuit-design problem of maximizing quantum magic in early fault-tolerant quantum computing (early FTQC), where logical dynamics natively take the form of alternating Clifford layers and diagonal non-Clifford layers. To render this optimization analytically tractable, we first prove a uniqueness theorem: for operational magic functionals built from Pauli expectation values, the axioms of faithfulness and tensor-product additivity force a Rényi-type dependence on the Pauli-spectrum. Leveraging the closed phase-polynomial description of the diagonal Clifford hierarchy, we derive exact Pauli-spectrum expressions and tight bounds for a shallow-layer model. These bounds expose a zero-magic mechanism and prove that maximal magic strictly requires graph-state preconditioning. Consequently, we establish our first no-go theorem: hierarchy level alone cannot universally order operational magic. Extending our framework to the $N$-layer model motivated by the Space-Time Efficient Analog Rotation (STAR) architecture, we obtain an exact iterative update rule for the Pauli spectrum. This yields a second no-go theorem: no state-independent sequence of operations can guarantee monotonic magic improvement. Together, these theorems demonstrate that algebraic gate structures are fundamentally insufficient to dictate resource generation. To overcome this, we reframe early FTQC gate selection as a state-aware, differentiable optimization over continuous analog parameters. Finally, we identify a severe kinematic expressibility bottleneck in architectures restricted to single-qubit $Z$-rotations and show that introducing nonlinear diagonal phases, such as multi-qubit $Z$-rotation, shatters this bottleneck. This provides a fundamental principle for demonstrating early FTQC, establishing scalable magic generation as a foundational benchmark for evaluating early FTQC architectures.

Frustrated magnetic order in hybrid Kitaev spin-orbital models

Authors: Ivan Dutta, Aayush Vijayvargia, Anamitra Mukherjee, Onur Erten, Kush Saha
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05196v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5 · scars & prethermalization 1/5

Summary. This paper explores what happens when two different types of quantum spin liquids are merged into a single lattice. It reveals that the system can undergo 'magnetic fragmentation,' where magnetism and topological order coexist, and shows how tuning interactions can lead to complex phase transitions and restored exact solvability.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the emergent ground states and phase transitions that occur when two independent, exactly solvable spin-orbital Hamiltonians (Kitaev and Yao-Lee) are combined on a common lattice geometry.

Main result. The study discovers 'magnetic fragmentation,' where the spin sector develops magnetic order while the orbital sector retains topological order, and identifies a special point where the model restores exact solvability.

Method. Self-consistent Majorana mean-field analysis, perturbative calculations in the strong-Kitaev limit, and Luttinger-Tisza analysis for determining ordering wavevectors.

Model / system. A hybrid spin-orbital model on a modified honeycomb/square lattice featuring both Kitaev-type interactions and square-lattice-type interactions involving spin and orbital degrees of freedom.

Key observables. Magnetization (m_lambda), bond order parameter (chi_lambda), Majorana Dirac bands, Lifshitz transitions, and plaquette flux sectors.

Important parameters / regimes. Kitaev coupling strength (J_K), square-lattice coupling strength (J_S), and the ratio of Yao-Lee to square-lattice couplings (J_Y/J_S).

Assumptions / limitations. Use of mean-field decoupling for quartic terms and a unit cell approximation to handle incommensurate magnetic structures.

Figures summary. Schematic of the modified honeycomb lattice, color plots of Majorana conduction bands showing Lifshitz transitions, and energy per site plots for the mean-field solutions.

Paper structure. Introduction to spin-orbital models, description of the hybrid Kitaev and Yao-Lee Hamiltonians, mean-field methodology, analysis of the Kitaev-square regime (magnetic fragmentation), analysis of the Yao-Lee-square regime (band evolution), and discussion of the exact solvability point.

Abstract

Spin-orbital generalization of Kitaev model provides a robust extension to the original Kitaev model. However, real materials often exhibit competing interactions that break exact solvability which can give rise to new phases. Abstract

We propose a simple ansatz to construct exact wave solutions of the sourceless SU(2) Yang-Mills equations in (3+1) dimensions. The ansatz employs a $y$-dependent rotated Pauli basis and assumes a phase $θ=kz-ωt$ dependence for the gauge potentials. Owing to this ansatz, the nonlinear field equations reduce to nine algebraic constraints, whose complete solution yields three families of exact waves. Family I describes linear (Abelian) electromagnetic waves embedded in the non-Abelian theory; all commutator terms vanish and the dispersion relation is $ω=kc$. Family II represents genuinely nonlinear self-interacting waves that also propagate at the speed of light but exhibit a constant field offset, nonvanishing commutators, and do not obey superposition. The constant offset is gauge-invariant and gives rise to a non-zero time-averaged color-electric field. The energy density has nodes whose position ($θ=0$ or $θ=π$) is controlled by a discrete topological parameter $ξη=\pm1$, providing an observable signature. Family III is a pure gauge solution with vanishing field strengths, valid for arbitrary $k$ and $ω$ without any dispersion relation. All solutions are closed-form and provide new insights into how non-Abelian self-interactions fundamentally alter wave propagation.

Abstract

Rhombohedral graphene exhibits an exceptionally diverse array of correlated phases that depend sensitively on the displacement field. Compiling reported phases into a unified phase diagram reveals a pronounced field-dependent electron-hole asymmetry: correlated states on the hole-doped side emerge at small displacement fields, whereas the fractional quantum anomalous Hall effect (FQAHE) is observed exclusively on the electron-doped side under large displacement fields. This stark asymmetry highlights the need to understand how flat bands evolve with displacement fields. Here, we directly visualize the field-induced electron-hole asymmetric band flattening in rhombohedral pentalayer graphene (R5G) using nanospot angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy with electrostatic gating. Beyond gap opening and spectral weight redistribution indicative of layer polarization, the gating field drives a strongly asymmetric modification of the flat bands: the flat valence band (FVB) evolves into an M-shaped dispersion at high field, whereas the flat conduction band (FCB) progressively flattens with increasing field. Comparison with calculations identifies critical parameters governing the band curvature of R5G, from which the resulting finite Berry curvature and near-ideal quantum geometry support the emergence of topological phases under electron doping at large fields. These results establish a direct link between the asymmetric phase diagram, band structure evolution, and quantum geometry, providing a microscopic framework for understanding correlated and topological phases in rhombohedral graphene.
