Summary. This paper introduces a neural network-based approach to simulate the many-body dissipative dynamics of large atom arrays. By using ResNet and dCNN architectures, the authors successfully model super- and sub-radiant regimes in 1D and 2D systems beyond the limits of exact diagonalization. The work demonstrates that neural networks can efficiently capture the complex, long-range correlations characteristic of subradiant decay.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful new numerical tool (NQS) for studying the long-time subradiant regimes and many-body correlations in light-matter interfaces, which are otherwise inaccessible to standard methods.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of simulating many-body super- and sub-radiant dynamics in large-scale light-matter interfaces where traditional exact and tensor-network methods fail.

Main result. The first successful application of Neural Quantum States (NQS) to capture the dissipative dynamics of 1D and 2D atom arrays up to 40 atoms, demonstrating that dCNN architectures can efficiently capture long-range correlations with fewer parameters than ResNet.

Method. Neural Quantum States (NQS) using ResNet and dilated CNN (dCNN) architectures, implemented via a POVM-based representation and evolved using the Time-Dependent Variational Principle (TDVP) and Monte Carlo sampling.

Model / system. Ordered, densely packed 1D and 2D arrays of cold atoms in free space, modeled by an effective spin Hamiltonian with photon-mediated long-range dipolar interactions and a Lindblad master equation.

Key observables. Number of excited atoms (excitation density), connected spin-spin correlators, and cumulative TDVP error (KL divergence).

Important parameters / regimes. Inter-atom distance (d), decay rate (Gamma_0), network depth (d), number of features (f), and kernel size (k).

Assumptions / limitations. The light field is integrated out to provide an effective spin description; the use of a tetrahedral POVM for density matrix representation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 and 2 show benchmarking of NQS against exact quantum trajectories and convergence studies in 1D and 2D; Figure 3 compares excitation density across system sizes; Figure 4 provides a schematic comparison of ResNet and dCNN architectures.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of simulating large-scale dissipative dynamics, describes the NQS/POVM methodology and neural architectures (ResNet vs dCNN), presents benchmarking results against exact and semi-classical methods, and concludes with efficiency comparisons and future directions.

Abstract

There is significant interest in exploring novel phenomena in quantum light-matter interfaces, which are driven by the combination of structured dissipation and long-range interactions that are typical in such systems. To this end, it is important to develop new general numerical simulation techniques, which can access large system sizes and are not based on semi-classical approaches. Here, we report the first application of neural quantum states to obtain the dissipative dynamics of light-matter-coupled systems beyond what is accessible with exact and tensor-network calculations. We specifically apply this method to simulate the many-body emission dynamics of approximately 40 atoms, arranged in dense arrays in one and two dimensions. These systems have been chosen because they can support prominent subradiant dynamics at late times and could be realized with cold atomic quantum simulators.

Summary. This paper solves the problem of finding the fundamental quantum limit for estimating functions of multiple parameters in a general Hamiltonian. It proves that this complex task can be reduced to an optimized single-parameter problem and provides a practical, two-step adaptive protocol to reach this limit. This unifies previous results and extends them to the most general case of non-commuting generators.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a fundamental framework for quantum sensing, offering a way to achieve optimal precision when dealing with complex, non-commuting signal generators, which is highly relevant for many-body sensing and quantum metrology.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the ultimate quantum precision limit for estimating a scalar function of multiple unknown parameters encoded in a general, potentially non-commuting Hamiltonian.

Main result. The authors derive a tight, saturable lower bound on the variance of the estimator that reduces the complex multi-parameter function estimation problem to an optimized single-parameter quantum Cramér-Rao bound.

Method. The problem is mapped to an effective single-parameter estimation problem using a semidefinite programming (SDP) framework and a two-step adaptive protocol involving Hamiltonian reshaping and randomized Trotterization.

Model / system. A general time-independent signal Hamiltonian H0 = sum f_j(theta) g_j, where theta are unknown parameters, f_j are smooth functions, and g_j are linearly independent, traceless Hermitian generators, supplemented by a controllable, time-dependent control Hamiltonian.

Key observables. The variance of the estimator for the target function q(theta).

Important parameters / regimes. The precision parameter gamma (derived from an SDP), the total interrogation time t, the Jacobian matrix F of the couplings, and the conditioning constant kappa.

Assumptions / limitations. Parameters are time-independent, functions are twice continuously differentiable, and generators are linearly independent and traceless; the protocol assumes an asymptotic limit of large total time t.

Figures summary. Table I compares optimal precision bounds across various scenarios including single-parameter, commuting, and arbitrary generator sets.

Paper structure. The paper defines the multi-parameter function estimation problem, derives a tight lower bound via SDP duality, proposes a constructive protocol using Hamiltonian reshaping and adaptive estimation, and provides error analysis and stability theorems.

Abstract

Estimation of physical parameters encoded in a Hamiltonian is a central task in quantum sensing and learning. While the ultimate precision limit for estimating a single parameter coupled to a single generator is well established, the corresponding bound for estimating a function of multiple parameters-each coupled to distinct and possibly non-commuting generators-remains unknown in general. Here, we derive the ultimate quantum limit and present an estimation protocol for any function of parameters in a general Hamiltonian that attains this bound. We show that, although the task is fundamentally a multiparameter problem, our tight bound reduces to an optimized single-parameter quantum Cramér-Rao bound, even for arbitrary generator sets. Our result unifies and extends previous works, providing a general framework for optimal function estimation in quantum systems.

Summary. The paper discovers that local quantum quenches in chaotic systems create a hierarchical entanglement structure where different Renyi indices exhibit different scaling laws. It identifies a 'hidden' area-law sector that carries the linear response to the quench, suggesting that parts of the highly entangled state may be efficiently approximable by tensor networks.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to many-body dynamics as it identifies a specific, hidden structure in entanglement that could potentially allow for more efficient classical simulations of otherwise volume-law entangled states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the entanglement structure of quantum many-body systems following local quenches, specifically looking for hidden area-law sectors and hierarchical entanglement transitions.

Main result. The authors reveal a Renyi-index-tuned transition where S_alpha obeys an area law for alpha > 1 and a volume law for alpha <= 1, alongside a recursive, hierarchical structure of entanglement transitions in the Schmidt sectors.

Method. The study uses analytical derivations in a circuit model, exact diagonalization of random circuits and chaotic spin chains, and tensor network-based approximation analysis.

Model / system. The paper analyzes 1D random unitary circuits (Haar-random, Clifford+T, and U(1) symmetric) and local quantum quenches on the canonical purification of Gibbs states in the mixed-field Ising model.

Key observables. Renyi entropies (S_alpha), von Neumann entropy (S_1), Schmidt eigenvalues, and truncation error.

Important parameters / regimes. Renyi index (alpha), quench strength (theta or epsilon), inverse temperature (beta), and time (t).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes generic chaotic dynamics and uses a maximally scrambled limit for certain analytical bounds; assumes the existence of a large, time-independent Schmidt eigenvalue.

Figures summary. Figures compare expectation values and truncation errors against exact values, show the cumulative sum of Schmidt weights, illustrate the hierarchical scaling of Renyi entropies, and demonstrate the monotonic growth and saturation of entropy in both circuit and Ising models.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of entanglement structure, presents a circuit model to derive the Renyi-index-tuned transition, extends these findings to local quenches of Gibbs states, provides a recursive analysis of the hierarchical structure, and validates results via numerical simulations of random circuits and the Ising model.

Abstract

Chaotic many-body dynamics typically generates volume-law entanglement from initially low-entangled states. We reveal an intricate, hierarchical entanglement structure in local quantum quenches, both in the canonical purification of locally quenched Gibbs states and in a companion pure-state circuit model. In either setting, the full state exhibits a Renyi-index-tuned transition: at long times, $S_{α>1}$ obeys an area law, while $S_{α\le 1}$ is volume-law. More strikingly, the response linear in the quench strength is carried by only an O(1)-dimensional dominant Schmidt sector; the corresponding states exhibit their own area-to-volume-law transitions at critical indices $α_c<1$, implying polynomial-bond-dimension approximability in one dimension. We provide evidence that this hierarchy persists recursively: upon bipartitioning the dominant Schmidt states, their leading Schmidt sectors exhibit analogous structure. We derive the mechanism analytically in the circuit model, prove the $S_{α>1}$ area law for locally quenched Gibbs states, and support the hierarchy by exact diagonalization of random circuits and locally quenched Gibbs states of chaotic spin chains.

Summary. The researchers developed a method to control photon-magnon interactions by simply rotating a resonator in a planar architecture. By changing the orientation, they can switch between different photon modes and redistribute coupling strengths. This provides a scalable and robust way to engineer hybrid quantum-magnonic interfaces.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a versatile mechanism for 'polarization engineering' in hybrid systems, providing a way to control light-matter interaction channels and dissipation pathways via geometric orientation, which is highly relevant to circuit QED and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving precise, reconfigurable control over photon-magnon coupling (PMC) pathways in hybrid systems without relying on complex external tuning like magnetic fields or active components.

Main result. Demonstrated that rotating an electric-LC resonator (ELCR) enables polarization-selective switching between two orthogonal photon modes, allowing for the continuous redistribution of interaction strength between competing photon-magnon hybridization channels.

Method. The study combines full-wave electromagnetic simulations (CST), an equivalent circuit model (ABCD-matrix formalism), and an effective three-mode Hamiltonian using input-output theory to analyze experimental transmission spectra.

Model / system. A planar cavity-magnonic platform consisting of an electric-LC resonator (ELCR) side-coupled to a microstrip transmission line, integrated with a Yttrium Iron Garnet (YIG) thin film.

Key observables. Transmission coefficient (S21), photon-magnon coupling strengths (g31, g23), photon mode frequencies, and radiative (extrinsic) damping rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Rotation angle (theta), coupling strengths (g), intrinsic and extrinsic damping rates (beta, gamma), and the dimensionless damping order parameter (Phi_gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. Application of the Rotating-Wave Approximation (RWA) to the Hamiltonian and the Markov approximation for treating waveguide coupling strengths.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Device schematic, optical images, and transmission spectra at various angles. Fig 2: Simulated surface-current distributions showing mode-specific loop configurations. Fig 3: Equivalent circuit models. Fig 4: Extracted damping rates vs. mutual inductance and mode-splitting colormaps.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of coupling control, describes the planar ELCR-YIG platform, presents the electromagnetic and circuit models for polarization-dependent excitation, details the three-mode Hamiltonian for hybridization, and concludes with experimental validation of mode switching and coupling redistribution.

Abstract

This work presents polarization-selective photon-magnon coupling (PMC) in a planar cavity-magnonic platform consisting of an electric-LC resonator (ELCR) side-coupled to a microstrip transmission line and integrated with a yttrium iron garnet (YIG) thin film. The ELCR supports two orthogonal photon modes at $\sim 3.93$ GHz and $\sim 5.73$ GHz, whose excitation and radiative damping are governed by the resonator orientation relative to the microwave-field polarization. Rotating the resonator enables controlled switching between these modes and tunable photon-magnon hybridization. An equivalent circuit model including intrinsic and extrinsic damping successfully reproduces the polarization-driven mode switching, while an effective three-mode Hamiltonian accurately captures the coupled-mode evolution. The results reveal strong angular tunability of the PMC strength through redistribution between two competing interaction channels. At $θ= 0^\circ$, only the lower-frequency photon mode is excited, yielding $g_{31}=56.5$ MHz, while the higher-frequency mode remains inactive. As the angle increases, both channels become active: $g_{31}$ increases from $56.5$ to $98$ MHz over $0^\circ$-$60^\circ$ before vanishing at $90^\circ$, whereas $g_{23}$ decreases from $76$ to $30$ MHz over $30^\circ$-$90^\circ$. The observed evolution yields a measured transition near $25.7^\circ$ and a symmetry-related model-predicted transition near $154.3^\circ$. These findings establish resonator-orientation--driven polarization selectivity as a versatile mechanism for controllable photon--magnon interactions in planar architectures.

Summary. This paper develops a new framework to prove that different mixed-state quantum phases are equivalent by constructing local quantum circuits that transform one into the other. It successfully extends this proof beyond the usual renormalization fixed points to include intrinsically nontrivial phases. The method uses the parent Lindbladian construction to ensure that the circuits preserve the system's underlying symmetries.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a rigorous framework for characterizing the stability and equivalence of non-equilibrium mixed-state phases beyond standard fixed-point approaches.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Establishing phase equivalence for mixed-state quantum phases beyond the renormalization fixed-point paradigm, specifically when conditional mutual information remains finite.

Main result. The author analytically establishes phase equivalence within two distinct 1D mixed-state phases by constructing low-depth, quasi-local channel circuits using a parent Lindbladian construction.

Method. The work utilizes Matrix Product Operators (MPOs), the parent Lindbladian construction, and the Petz recovery map to generate channel circuits that preserve global symmetries.

Model / system. One-dimensional quantum systems described by Matrix Product Density Operators (MPDOs) and a path of mixed states $\rho(\lambda)$ connecting a trivial phase to an intrinsically nontrivial phase with anomalous $Z_2$ or $Z_N$ symmetry.

Key observables. Conditional Mutual Information (CMI), Markov length, entanglement entropy, and two-point correlation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. The control parameter $\lambda$ (defining the phase transition at $\lambda=0$), bond dimension, and the distance $d$ between regions.

Assumptions / limitations. The construction assumes a finite Markov length and relies on the existence of a pre-bialgebraic structure to ensure the locality of the channel circuits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a mixed-state phase diagram with trivial and nontrivial phases; Figure 2 illustrates the configuration of the constructed channel circuits; Figure 4 shows numerical decay of CMI and Renyi-2 CMI with distance.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining phase equivalence and the problem of finite CMI, introduces a model with a path of mixed states, develops a construction using the parent Lindbladian to create symmetry-preserving channel circuits, and concludes with generalizations to $Z_N$ symmetries and numerical verification of criticality.

Abstract

Understanding mixed-state quantum phases is a central challenge in the era of quantum simulation, where many existing studies focus on renormalization fixed points. In this work, we move beyond the renormalization fixed-point paradigm by constructing a quantum phase transition connecting two distinct one-dimensional fixed points, both exhibiting finite conditional mutual information and one of which is intrinsically nontrivial. We analytically establish phase equivalence within each of the two phases by explicitly constructing low-depth, quasi-local channel circuits that connect states within each phase. Crucially, our approach leverages the parent Lindbladian construction to generate the desired channel circuits. We further demonstrate that this framework generalizes naturally to a broad class of intrinsically nontrivial mixed-state quantum phases. Our method establishes a framework for rigorously analyzing phase equivalence of intrinsically non-trivial mixed states beyond the renormalization fixed points.

Summary. This paper investigates the efficiency of energy conversion into macroscopic order in nonequilibrium steady states. It demonstrates that while thermodynamic efficiency peaks at phase transitions, it diverges from inferential efficiency due to the violation of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem out of equilibrium.

Why it may be interesting. The study provides a fundamental link between thermodynamics and information theory in nonequilibrium systems, which is highly relevant to understanding dissipation and control in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining how efficiently nonequilibrium self-organizing systems convert consumed energy into macroscopic order and investigating the divergence between thermodynamic and inferential efficiency out of equilibrium.

Main result. Thermodynamic efficiency maximizes at phase transitions in both equilibrium and nonequilibrium steady states, while the gap between thermodynamic and inferential efficiency serves as a measure of the system's distance from equilibrium.

Method. The study uses Monte Carlo simulations and a refined mean-field approximation (treating density and magnetization as independent Gaussian variables) to analyze two models.

Model / system. The study focuses on two nonequilibrium models: the Persistent Ising Model, which uses a non-conservative drive to break detailed balance, and the Active Ising Model, which features self-propelled particles on a 2D lattice.

Key observables. Thermodynamic efficiency (eta), inferential efficiency (eta_triangular_triangle), Shannon entropy, infinitesimal work, and magnetization.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength (J), external drive (E0), self-propulsion (epsilon), external magnetic field (h), and lattice size.

Assumptions / limitations. The refined mean-field approach assumes density and magnetization are independent Gaussian random variables, which fails to capture the homogeneous bistable phase and becomes inaccurate in strong-coupling regimes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Persistent Ising model dynamics; Figure 7 shows phase plots and profiles for the Active Ising model; Figure 11 demonstrates efficiency peaks at phase transitions; Figure 12 shows finite-size effects on efficiencies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of energy-to-order conversion, defines the information-theoretic framework, applies it to the Persistent and Active Ising models, analyzes phase transitions and efficiency divergence, and concludes with a discussion on the Principle of Super-efficiency.

Abstract

Active matter generates order or patterns through nonequilibrium dynamics. An open research challenge is to determine how efficiently a nonequilibrium self-organising system can convert consumed energy into macroscopic order. We study an information-theoretic quantity that directly addresses this challenge by estimating the entropy reduction induced by a small control-parameter perturbation, relative to the generalised work required for the perturbation. This quantity has previously been considered mainly in an equilibrium or near-equilibrium context, and here we extend this framework and apply it to two nonequilibrium self-organising systems: persistent and active Ising models. We observe that the thermodynamic efficiency of nonequilibrium systems maximises at phase transitions, as in equilibrium systems. Furthermore, we compare thermodynamic efficiency and inferential efficiency across control parameters. While these two quantities are equal in equilibrium as a consequence of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, we report that they diverge out of equilibrium, and the gap reflects how far the system is from equilibrium.

Summary. This paper proposes a new platform for topological quantum computing using a 2D electron gas coupled to a superconducting LC resonator. Abstract

We introduce a tunable synthetic-dimension platform for realizing Kitaev-chain physics with high degree of control over Majorana zero modes. It is based on a generic Landau-quantized two dimensional electron system coupled to the magnetic flux of a superconducting LC circuit. These states serve as a synthetic dimension for the coveted fermionic Kitaev chain, with Majorana zero modes existing at the boundaries of the angular-momentum lattice. The crucial advantage of this proposal is the possibility of a robust, nonlocal readout and control of the Majorana states by a LC resonator. The platform relies on mature circuit QED and semiconductor technologies and provides a promising pathway to topological quantum computing.

Summary. This paper explores how dissipation modifies the spreading of correlations in an XXZ spin chain. It demonstrates that while dissipation eventually leads to exponential decay, the underlying transport universality classes and light-cone structures remain intact during the early-time dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. It provides critical insights into the robustness of many-body transport phenomena (like KPZ scaling) and the stability of causal light-cone structures in the presence of environmental decoherence, which is central to open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how dissipation (spin loss and pumping) affects the dynamical spin correlations and transport universality classes in a 1D XXZ spin chain.

Main result. The fundamental transport universality classes (ballistic, KPZ, and diffusive) and the light-cone structure are preserved at short times, while dissipation causes exponential damping of correlations at long times.

Method. The authors use vectorized Time-Evolving Block Decimation (TEBD) for interacting regimes and a third-quantization framework with a Pfaffian representation for the noninteracting limit.

Model / system. A 1D spin-1/2 XXZ spin chain subject to Lindblad dynamics with uniform local spin-loss and pumping, leading to a featureless product state steady state.

Key observables. Longitudinal and transverse two-point spin correlators, and longitudinal spin autocorrelation.

Important parameters / regimes. Anisotropy (Delta), spin fugacity (zeta), dissipation rate (Gamma), and the crossover time scale (t*).

Assumptions / limitations. The steady state is assumed to be a Gaussian product state; numerical results are subject to bond dimension and time-step constraints.

Figures summary. Figures show magnetization vs. fugacity, spatiotemporal structures of longitudinal correlations (identifying light cones and dynamical exponents), and the decay of transverse/longitudinal autocorrelations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the dissipative XXZ model, establishes the steady state, analyzes unitary evolution (quench) dynamics, extends the study to full Lindbladian dynamics, and provides exact analytical benchmarks for the noninteracting limit.

Abstract

We study dynamical spin correlations in a dissipative XXZ spin chain subject to uniform local spin-loss and pumping. Starting from a mixed steady state that is featureless albeit possessing finite magnetization, rich dynamics emerges in time-dependent two-point correlators evaluated on top of it. For unitary evolution in which the reservoir is absent, the longitudinal correlators reproduce the established hierarchy of spin-transport universality classes - ballistic, Kardar-Parisi-Zhang (KPZ) superdiffusive, and diffusive - across the phase diagram. However, for finite magnetization, additional ballistic light cone propagation gets superimposed on the previous universality classes, arising from magnon propagation.The transverse correlator displays very fast, exponential decay of correlations without wavefront propagation in the easy-plane case. At the isotropic point, it follows KPZ scaling due to $SU(2)$ symmetry, while in the easy-axis regime, it is characterized by ballistic spreading of correlations. Under full Lindbladian dynamics, the universality classes are preserved at early times, while the correlations acquire an overall exponential damping in the long-time limit. In terms of methods, we have used vectorized TEBD for numerical simulations and exact analytical results obtained via a Pfaffian representation and the third-quantization framework for the noninteracting XX case.

Quantum state texture of dynamical criticality

Authors: Lucas C. Céleri, Krissia Zawadzki, Ivan Medina, Diogo O. Soares-Pinto

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

Summary. This paper connects the concept of quantum state texture, called rugosity, to the identification of dynamical quantum phase transitions. It proves that rugosity can act as an order parameter for Type-I transitions and is directly related to the Loschmidt rate function in Type-II transitions. This establishes rugosity as a useful tool for diagnosing criticality in many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new information-theoretic perspective on nonequilibrium dynamics, linking the generation of quantum resources (texture) to the emergence of thermodynamic phase transitions and complexity.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates whether 'rugosity' (a measure of quantum state texture) can serve as a fundamental diagnostic tool for identifying and characterizing both Type-I and Type-II dynamical quantum phase transitions (DQPTs).

Main result. The authors establish that for Type-II transitions, the Loschmidt rate function is equivalent to the density of rugosity in a specific basis, and for Type-I transitions, the time-averaged rugosity in the pre-quench basis acts as an order parameter.

Method. The researchers use the resource theory of rugosity to analyze sudden-quench protocols, employing semiclassical analysis, finite-size scaling, and comparisons with other complexity measures like Krylov complexity and entropy production.

Model / system. The primary model used is the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) model, a collective spin system undergoing a sudden quench of the transverse field.

Key observables. Rugosity (R_epsilon), Loschmidt rate function (lambda_t), magnetization (order parameter), and rugosity density.

Important parameters / regimes. Quench protocol (h0 to hf), transverse field strength (h), and the thermodynamic limit (N -> infinity).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a sudden-quench protocol and relies on the existence of a well-defined semiclassical limit and separatrix structure.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows time-averaged magnetization and rugosity vs. quench intensity; Figure 2 displays the derivative of average rugosity vs. quench parameter; Figure 3 compares the rate function and rugosity density.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of rugosity, applies it to Type-I and Type-II DQPTs using the LMG model, provides a mechanistic explanation via semiclassical structures, and contextualizes the findings within broader complexity and entropy theories.

Abstract

We investigate the role of quantum state texture in dynamical quantum phase transitions by establishing a direct connection between critical nonequilibrium dynamics and the recently introduced notion of rugosity, a measure of the quantum state texture. Considering a generic quench protocol, we analyze both standard formulations of the dynamical quantum phase transition. For type-I transitions, defined through the long-time behavior of an order parameter, we show that the time averaged rugosity, evaluated in the eigenbasis of the pre-quench Hamiltonian, acts itself as an order parameter, sharply distinguishing the dynamical phases. In the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model, this behavior is traced back to the underlying semiclassical structure, where the crossing of the excited-state quantum phase transition separatrix controls the redistribution of the state over the pre-quench energy basis. Abstract

Spin squeezing can suppress quantum projection noise via interparticle entanglement, therefore enabling measurement sensitivities beyond the standard quantum limit. In practice, however, the Gaussian and finite intensity profiles of the optical probe beam induce spatially inhomogeneous atom-light interactions. As polarized atoms move within a vapor cell, they experience position-dependent optical intensities, generating transit noise that limits spin squeezing performance. Here, we investigate the transit noise in a coated rubidium vapor cell through combined theoretical analysis and experimental measurements. By varying the probe beam diameter, we quantify the dependence of transit noise on beam size and atomic Larmor frequency. Our results show that, for a vapor cell with fixed dimensions, the transit noise increases as the probe beam spot area decreases. Moreover, when the Larmor frequency is below the characteristic linewidth of the transit noise, the noise contribution becomes larger. We further calculated and measured spin squeezing for different beam sizes and found an experimental difference of $2.7 \pm 0.2$ dB between 2~mm and 0.6~mm, similar to the theoretical prediction of $3.0 \pm 0.3$ dB. Theoretical analysis under conditions of stronger squeezing shows that transit noise becomes an even more critical limiting factor. These results provide practical guidance for optimizing probe beam parameters and suppressing transit noise in spin squeezing experiments.

Abstract

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) enables Information-Theoretically Secure (ITS) key exchange, robust even against future quantum computing threats. However, a fundamental limitation of QKD is the requirement for an authenticated classical channel, which necessitates a pre-shared secret key.
