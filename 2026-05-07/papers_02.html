Dephasing Effects on the Dynamical Evolution of Quantum Correlations and Coherence in Neutrino Oscillations

Authors: Omar Bachain, Elhabib Jaloum, Mohamed Amazioug, Nazek Alessa, Wedad R. Alharbi, Rachid Ahl Laamara, Abdel-Haleem Abdel-Aty
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05015v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

Summary. This paper explores the degradation of quantum correlations in neutrino oscillations due to environmental decoherence. It identifies a clear hierarchy of robustness among quantum steering, entanglement, and coherence and demonstrates how non-Markovian memory effects can lead to the revival of certain quantum resources.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a detailed study of how different types of environmental noise (dissipative vs. dephasing) and memory effects (non-Markovianity) affect a hierarchy of quantum correlations, which is directly applicable to open quantum systems and quantum information processing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how environmental decoherence and non-Markovian memory effects impact the dynamical evolution and robustness of quantum resources (steering, entanglement, and coherence) in neutrino oscillation systems.

Main result. The study establishes a hierarchy of robustness where quantum steering is the most fragile, followed by entanglement, while quantum coherence is the most resilient. Non-Markovian effects induce partial revivals of entanglement and coherence through information backflow, though steering remains largely suppressed.

Method. The authors use an open quantum system approach with the Kraus operator formalism to model three noise channels (Amplitude Damping, Phase Flip, and Phase Damping) and compare Markovian and non-Markovian dynamics.

Model / system. A two-flavor neutrino oscillation framework is modeled as an effective bipartite two-qubit system using an occupation number representation and the PMNS mixing matrix.

Key observables. Quantum steering (S_A to B, S_B to A, and steering asymmetry), Logarithmic Negativity (entanglement), and l1-norm of quantum coherence.

Important parameters / regimes. Oscillation phase (phi), decoherence strength (tau), mass-squared difference (delta m^2), mixing angle (theta), and environmental correlation time (chi).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a two-flavor approximation, the ultra-relativistic limit, and that neutrinos are produced with equal momentum.

Figures summary. Figures show the oscillatory dependence of quantum resources on oscillation phase, the monotonic decay of resources under amplitude damping, and the symmetric decay-revival dynamics and linear decay under phase flip and phase damping channels across different experimental setups (KamLAND, Daya Bay, JUNO).

Paper structure. The paper begins by mapping neutrino oscillations to a two-qubit system, then derives analytical expressions for quantum resources under specific noise channels, followed by a comparative analysis of different noise models (AD, PF, PD) and dynamical regimes (Markovian vs. non-Markovian), and concludes with an experimental comparison using data from major neutrino collaborations.

Abstract

Neutrino oscillations confirm the presence of mode entanglement, as each flavor eigenstate is composed of a coherent superposition of distinct mass eigenstates. In this work, we investigate the dynamics of quantum resources in neutrino oscillation systems by analyzing quantum steering, logarithmic negativity, and quantum coherence within a two-flavor framework. Treating neutrino oscillations as an effective two-level quantum system, we study the influence of environmental decoherence on these nonclassical features by modeling the system as an open quantum system. Three representative noise channels are considered, namely amplitude damping (AD), phase flip (PF), and phase damping (PD), allowing us to capture both dissipative and dephasing mechanisms. We examine the evolution of quantum resources in both Markovian and non-Markovian regimes, highlighting the role of memory effects in the system-environment interaction. The results reveal a clear hierarchy in the robustness of quantum resources under decoherence. Steering is the most sensitive correlation in the hierarchy under decoherence effects. while logarithmic negativity exhibits intermediate robustness. Quantum coherence displays the highest resilience, persisting over a wider range of parameters. In the PF and PD channels, logarithmic negativity and coherence are shown to exhibit identical dynamical behavior, reflecting their common dependence on phase-related noise. In contrast, the non-Markovian regime leads to delayed decoherence and partial revivals of entanglement and coherence due to information backflow, whereas quantum steering remains strongly suppressed. These findings provide a comparison of different quantum resources in neutrino oscillation systems and offer new insights into the interplay between decoherence mechanisms and quantum correlations.

Summary. This paper establishes a mathematical framework to calculate the real-time evolution of bulk operators after an integrable boundary quench. By studying the Lee-Yang model, the authors demonstrate how particle interference leads to specific decay laws and provide a method to reconcile numerical TCSA results with continuum field theory.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insights into non-equilibrium dynamics and relaxation in integrable systems, specifically how boundary-induced excitations propagate and interfere, which is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and the study of quench-induced steady states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the real-time dynamics and expectation values of bulk fields following an integrable boundary quantum quench, where one boundary condition is suddenly switched to another.

Main result. The authors develop a general framework using form factor expansions to calculate post-quench vacuum-to-vacuum matrix elements and identify a modified $t^{-1}$ decay law due to destructive interference in the massive theory.

Method. The study employs a double form-factor expansion approach, conformal mapping to the Upper Half Plane, and the Truncated Conformal Space Approach (TCSA) for numerical validation and renormalization.

Model / system. The primary model used is the (1+1)-dimensional Lee-Yang model, covering both its Conformal Field Theory (CFT) point and its massive integrable perturbation on a strip or semi-infinite plane.

Key observables. Expectation values (VEVs) of local bulk fields, correlation functions, and the time evolution of the vacuum amplitude.

Important parameters / regimes. Boundary conditions (identity and primary field), strip width $L$, time $t$, spatial position $x$, and the mass scale $m$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to integrable boundary conditions and integrable bulk perturbations, and assumes the left boundary condition remains fixed or follows specific simplified scenarios.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the physical setup and geometry mapping, 3D plots of correlation functions, energy spectra from TCSA, and comparisons between analytical predictions and numerical data including light-cone effects.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the boundary quench protocol and conformal analysis to developing the massive theory framework via form factors, followed by numerical validation using TCSA and error analysis/renormalization.

Abstract

We investigate an integrable boundary quench, in which one integrable boundary condition is suddenly switched to another. We develop a general framework for analyzing the resulting real-time dynamics based on form factors of bulk and boundary-changing operators. We first study the problem at the conformal point of the Lee-Yang model and then extend the analysis to its massive perturbation, where we examine the time evolution of the pre-quench vacuum and compute the vacuum-to-vacuum matrix elements of local operators inserted after the quench. The analytical results are validated by numerical calculations using the truncated conformal space approach adapted to boundary-changing situations.

Summary. This paper demonstrates a successful implementation of a quantum machine learning algorithm for graph classification using a 256-qubit neutral atom simulator. By optimizing the quantum pulse sequence via Bayesian optimization, the researchers achieved performance that exceeds classical benchmarks on protein datasets. The work highlights the potential of using programmable Rydberg atom arrays for real-world quantum computing tasks despite current hardware noise.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a practical application of Rydberg-based quantum simulators for complex tasks like graph classification and shows how pulse optimization can mitigate hardware noise in many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Implementing Quantum Machine Learning (QML) for graph classification on real-world hardware, specifically overcoming the constraints of noise and limited connectivity in neutral atom simulators.

Main result. The optimized Quantum Evolution Kernel (QEK) on the 256-qubit Aquila platform achieved an F1 score of 46.3% on the PROTEINS12 dataset, outperforming both classical Shortest Path Kernels and unoptimized quantum protocols.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical pipeline using a Quantum Evolution Kernel (QEK) based on Jensen-Shannon divergence, optimized via Bayesian Optimization to find the best pulse sequence parameters.

Model / system. A 256-qubit neutral atom simulator (QuEra Aquila) using Rubidium atoms trapped in optical tweezers, where qubits are defined by Rydberg and ground states and interactions are governed by the Rydberg blockade and an Ising Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Energy distributions derived from atom measurements and the resulting probability distributions used for kernel computation.

Important parameters / regimes. Pulse parameters (Rabi frequency, detuning, and duration), kernel scaling factor (mu), and the number of measurement shots.

Assumptions / limitations. Graphs must be representable as Unit Disk Graphs to fit the physical connectivity of the atom array; the study assumes the effectiveness of the DEN model for graph embedding.

Figures summary. Paper structure. The paper introduces the QML problem for graph classification, details the physical platform and hardware constraints, describes the graph embedding and QEK algorithm, presents the Bayesian optimization of waveforms, and concludes with performance benchmarking against classical methods and noise analysis.

Abstract

Neutral atom platforms are analogue quantum simulators that offer the possibility to map graphs onto a 2D qubit register using programmable Rubidium atoms arrays, whose valence electrons' energy state is used as qubits, using optical tweezers. This makes it possible to implement algorithms for solving graph combinatorial optimization and Quantum Machine Learning (QML) tasks, such as graph classification. However, the restrictions of real hardware, as well as the very low number of publicly available machines, make such implementation non-trivial. In this work, we manage to compute the Quantum Evolution Kernel (QEK) to extract the features from graphs of the PROTEINS dataset using the 256-qubits Aquila platform (available through AWS) and then we apply classical Machine Learning (ML) techniques for the final classification. The method is benchmarked against classical kernels, resulting in slightly better performance, proving the effectiveness of the method, even in the case of a noisy quantum simulator.

Summary. The paper identifies a quantum Mpemba effect where the generation of nonstabilizerness (magic) follows counterintuitive trajectories, where less magical states can become more magical than their counterparts over time. This phenomenon is driven by the spatial arrangement of initial charges and is a general feature of symmetric and non-integrable quantum dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. It connects the resource theory of magic with non-equilibrium statistical mechanics, showing how symmetry and spatial configuration can lead to anomalous relaxation phenomena in many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the existence of a quantum Mpemba effect in the dynamical generation of nonstabilizerness (magic), specifically whether states with lower initial magic can overtake states with higher initial magic during evolution.

Main result. The authors discover an inverse Mpemba effect where magic growth trajectories cross, driven by the interplay between charge conservation and the spatial structure of initial states. This effect is shown to be robust across U(1) and SU(2) symmetric random circuits as well as non-integrable Hamiltonian dynamics.

Method. The study uses analytical derivations for late-time steady-state magic and numerical simulations of random circuits and the mixed-field Ising model to track early-time dynamics.

Model / system. The study examines U(1)-symmetric and SU(2)-symmetric brickwork random circuits, as well as the non-integrable mixed-field Ising model, using various tilted product state initial conditions.

Key observables. Second stabilizer Renyi entropy (M2), which quantifies nonstabilizerness or magic.

Important parameters / regimes. Tilt angle (theta), system size (N), time (t), and charge-sector distribution p(q).

Assumptions / limitations. The equivalence of certain state families is treated as an asymptotic property of the steady-state limit where intra-sector scrambling is complete.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the circuit structure and the concept of the Mpemba crossing; Figure 2 shows the evolution of magic for different initial states; Supplemental figures show dynamics in SU(2) circuits and the Ising model.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the quantum Mpemba effect in magic generation, provides analytical derivations for steady-state magic in U(1) circuits, explores the role of spatial structure via TNS and TDWS states, extends the findings to SU(2) and non-integrable Hamiltonian systems, and concludes with a detailed mathematical appendix.

Abstract

Quantum state preparation can be strikingly counterintuitive: the fastest route to a target state need not start from the apparently closest initial condition. We uncover such a quantum Mpemba effect in the dynamical generation of quantum magic (nonstabilizerness), quantified by the stabilizer Rényi entropy, in $\mathrm{U(1)}$-symmetric random circuits initialized from tilted product states. States with lower initial magic can generate magic faster than states with higher initial magic. The acceleration is not determined solely by the conserved-charge distribution. Two initial-state families with identical initial magic and identical charge distribution exhibit qualitatively different magic-growth dynamics, depending also on the spatial structure of the initial state within each charge sector. Analogous magic Mpemba effects in $\mathrm{SU(2)}$-symmetric circuits and under nonintegrable Hamiltonian dynamics further show that the phenomenon is tied neither to Abelian symmetry nor to random-circuit dynamics, establishing quantum magic as a distinct arena for Mpemba physics.

Summary. This paper presents a new strategy for simultaneous multi-parameter estimation in time-periodic quantum systems using Floquet theory. It demonstrates that operating near a topological phase transition significantly enhances sensing precision, even achieving super-Heisenberg scaling for the driving frequency.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel framework for quantum sensing in non-equilibrium driven systems, demonstrating how topological phase transitions can be leveraged to surpass standard precision limits in multi-parameter estimation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a quantum multiparameter estimation strategy for general time-periodically driven (Floquet) systems where a static effective Hamiltonian cannot be easily constructed.

Main result. The study reveals that near a periodic-driving-induced topological phase transition, estimation precision for magnetic fields reaches the Heisenberg limit, while the frequency parameter can achieve even higher super-Heisenberg scaling (t^4).

Method. The authors utilize Floquet theory within the Sambe space expansion and exact diagonalization of a truncated Floquet Hamiltonian to decompose the Quantum Fisher Information Matrix into contributions from eigenmodes, quasienergies, and multi-photon processes.

Model / system. A ring-shaped Rashba spin-orbit interferometer (modeled as a 2-level system) subject to a time-periodic Hamiltonian involving Rashba and Zeeman magnetic fields and a driving frequency.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM), Classical Fisher Information (CFI), winding number, measurement incompatibility, and transition probabilities.

Important parameters / regimes. Rashba field (B0), Zeeman field (B1), driving frequency (omega), Heisenberg limit (t^2), and super-Heisenberg scaling (t^4).

Assumptions / limitations. The approach assumes the system can be effectively modeled by a truncated Floquet matrix in Sambe space and focuses on the stroboscopic measurement regime.

Figures summary. Schematic of the interferometer, winding number plots, energy spectra near TPT, QFI scaling analysis over time, decomposition of QFI components, and measurement incompatibility plots.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Floquet multiparameter estimation, develops a mathematical framework using Floquet theory and Sambe space, applies this to a Rashba spin-orbit interferometer undergoing a topological phase transition, analyzes precision scaling and measurement incompatibility, and concludes with experimental feasibility mapping.

Abstract

Periodically driven systems provide a powerful platform for quantum multiparameter estimation. Constructing a static effective Hamiltonian in a proper rotating frame is commonly employed to assess the attainable precision. However, such an approach becomes nonfeasible for more general time-periodically driven systems. To tackle this dilemma, we develop a quantum multiparameter estimation strategy in the Floquet theory framework. The contributions of Floquet eigenmodes, quasienergies, and multi-photon processes to the quantum Fisher information matrix and measurement incompatibility are determined, respectively. Moreover, this approach is applied to a ring-shaped Rashba spin-orbit interferometer model exhibiting the topological phase transition (TPT). In the vicinity of the TPT boundary, we reveal a pronounced enhancement in the estimation precision of multiple parameters with the Heisenberg limit scaling and even higher. Meanwhile, the measurement incompatibility vanishes in an oscillatory manner, and the stroboscopic projective measurement enables the highest estimation precision achievable. This work provides a complete Floquet picture for time-dependent critical quantum multiparameter estimation.

Summary. This paper introduces an efficient online algorithm for reconstructing quantum states that have a Matrix Product Operator structure. By treating tomography as a tensor completion problem, the authors reduce the required measurement complexity from exponential to polynomial scaling. This allows for the scalable characterization of large-scale quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a scalable, efficient framework for reconstructing many-body density matrices, which is crucial for characterizing the states produced in experiments involving cold atoms or trapped ions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of performing efficient Quantum State Tomography (QST) for many-body systems without the exponential scaling of measurements and computation associated with traditional methods.

Main result. The authors develop an online Riemannian Gradient Descent (oRGD) algorithm that achieves linear convergence and requires a number of measurement settings that scales only quadratically with system size.

Method. The problem is reformulated as a low-rank tensor completion task on a real Tensor Train (TT) manifold, using an online gradient descent approach with a tailored spectral initialization.

Model / system. The target is an n-partite d-level quantum system (e.g., qubits) whose density matrix is represented as a Matrix Product Operator (MPO) with a bounded bond dimension.

Key observables. Pauli measurements for qubits and generalized Gell-Mann measurements for qudits.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (n), MPO bond dimension (r), spikiness (nu), incoherence (mu), and measurement noise level (sigma).

Assumptions / limitations. The target state must be representable by an MPO with bounded bond dimension, and the algorithm requires a 'warm start' initialization within a specific error bound.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a tensor network diagram of the MPO-Pauli connection; Figure 3 shows convergence of relative error for random and Ising states; Figure 4 shows the impact of step size and measurement shots on error; Figure 5 compares runtime and error between RGD and oRGD.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the MPO-based QST problem, establishes a mathematical equivalence between MPO reconstruction and low-rank tensor completion, proposes the oRGD algorithm and a spectral initialization method, provides theoretical convergence proofs, and validates the approach with numerical experiments on random and Ising model states.

Abstract

Matrix product operators (MPOs) provide a scalable approach for quantum state tomography (QST) by offering a compact representation of many-body mixed states with limited entanglement, using only a number of parameters that scales polynomially with the system size. In this paper, we study QST for quantum density matrices that can be represented by MPOs. We first derive an equivalent characterization of Hermiticity in terms of the MPO core tensors and show that the coefficient tensor of an MPO under the Pauli or generalized Gell-Mann basis admits a real-valued low tensor-train (TT) rank structure. This establishes an explicit connection between MPO-based QST and noisy low-rank tensor completion. Motivated by this formulation, we develop an online Riemannian gradient descent (oRGD) algorithm that sequentially incorporates measurement data during the reconstruction process. With a proper initialization, we prove that oRGD converges linearly to the target MPO and succeeds with a number of distinct measurement settings that scales quadratically with the system size. As a byproduct, our analysis also yields a significantly improved sample complexity bound for the low TT rank tensor completion task. Furthermore, we propose a tailored spectral initialization method and establish its theoretical guarantee. Numerical experiments on several classes of quantum states validate the effectiveness and scalability of the proposed method.

Summary. This paper explores how a molecule of multi-point coupled 'giant atoms' can be used to route single photons between parallel waveguides. It shows that non-Markovian delay effects and asymmetric coupling can be precisely tuned to switch between different quantum coupling regimes and achieve deterministic photon routing.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insights into how non-Markovianity and engineered dissipation can be used as tools for controlling quantum networks and light-matter interfaces in waveguide QED.

Main problem. Investigating single-photon scattering and routing in a waveguide-QED setup using a 'giant molecule' to understand how asymmetric coupling and non-Markovian retardation effects can control photon-path interference.

Main result. The study demonstrates that by tailoring decay-rate asymmetry, atomic detuning, and propagation delay, one can achieve deterministic photon routing and transition between weak- and strong-coupling regimes.

Abstract

We investigate single-photon scattering in a waveguide-QED setup, where a giant molecule composed of two frequency-detuned giant atoms is coupled to two parallel waveguides via multiple connection points. The competition between coherent atom--atom coupling and the effective decay rates dictates the splitting of a single resonance into a doublet in the transmission (reflection) spectra. By tailoring the asymmetry of the decay rates and the atomic detuning, one can engineer photon-path interference to optimize the transfer between waveguides; under chiral coupling conditions, this interference can be further harnessed to realize fully deterministic routing. In the non-Markovian regime, retardation effects can reshape the spectra and actively drive transitions between the weak- and strong-coupling regimes, converting an unsplit Markovian resonance into a clearly separated doublet, or conversely merging a split doublet back into a single resonance. For sufficiently long time delays, it further generates multiple resonances and avoided crossings, enriching the spectral response. Our results demonstrate how atomic detuning, decay-rate asymmetry, and non-Markovian retardation cooperate to provide versatile, interference-based control over single-photon routing in multi-port quantum networks.

Summary. The paper presents a new numerical method to calculate the self-energy of discrete many-body systems without the instability and causality issues found in standard Dyson equation subtraction. By using a symmetric estimator based on the Schur complement, the method ensures physical spectral weights and improves accuracy in DMFT calculations. It is compatible with various many-body solvers including NRG, DMRG, and Krylov-based methods.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and open quantum systems because it provides a numerically stable way to extract spectral properties and self-energies from discrete representations (like MPS or NRG) without introducing unphysical broadening or violating causality.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Calculating the single-particle self-energy in discrete many-body systems is numerically unstable due to the subtraction of nearly canceling quantities in the Dyson equation, often leading to causality violations and unphysical negative spectral weight.

Main result. The authors derive a symmetric estimator for the discrete self-energy that is guaranteed to remain causal at the numerical level and avoids the need for artificial broadening. This method achieves significantly improved accuracy for impurity properties and allows for a consistent discrete DMFT self-consistency loop.

Method. The method uses a discrete realization of Kugler's symmetric improved estimator, treating the self-energy as the Schur complement of an augmented Green's function. It involves a three-step process of inversion, projection, and re-inversion of a matrix-valued propagator.

Model / system. The method is applicable to any discrete many-body Hamiltonian, specifically focusing on quantum impurity models (like the Single-Impurity Anderson Model) and the Hubbard model on a Betra lattice.

Key observables. Single-particle self-energy, Green's functions, hybridization function, quasiparticle weight (Z), scattering-rate coefficient (C), and double occupancy.

Important parameters / regimes. Discretization parameter (Lambda), interaction strength (U/D), temperature (T), and Krylov space size (K).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the system is a discrete many-body system with a finite number of bath poles; the Schur complement formula requires the existence of the involved matrix inverses.

Figures summary. By using the Chinese Remainder Theorem, the authors reduce the complexity of verifying large-scale systems from exponential to polynomial time. This allows for the explicit construction of massive entangled states that were previously computationally inaccessible.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful algebraic framework for generating highly entangled states, which are essential resources for quantum error correction and quantum communication protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of finding a systematic approach for constructing Absolutely Maximally Entangled (AME) states and the exponential complexity involved in verifying their entanglement properties.

Main result. The authors establish a Rank-Purity Duality that links the Rényi-2 purity of subsystems to the rank of submatrices in a phase matrix, enabling the efficient construction and certification of large-scale AME states like AME(17, 10001).

Method. The study uses finite-field algebraic tools, including the Chinese Remainder Theorem for decomposing composite dimensions and a parallel tempering optimization algorithm to minimize rank deficits.

Model / system. A class of multipartite quadratic-phase quantum states (including stabilizer states) defined by symmetric matrices over finite fields or rings.

Key observables. Rényi-2 purity, Rényi-2 entropy, and submatrix rank.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of parties (N), local dimension (d), and the rank of the phase matrix submatrices.

Assumptions / limitations. The results primarily focus on the class of quadratic-phase quantum states and assume square-free dimensions for the CRT decomposition.

Abstract

Absolutely Maximally Entangled (AME) states are important resources in quantum information processing; however, a general systematic approach for constructing these states remains a formidable challenge. We identify a finite-field rank structure underlying multipartite entanglement in a class of quadratic-phase quantum states defined by symmetric matrices over $\mathbb{F}_p$. We prove an exact Rank-Purity Duality: the Rényi-2 purity of any subsystem is determined solely by the rank of the phase matrix. Within this ansatz, the existence of an AME state is equivalent to the existence of a generating phase matrix $P$ whose bipartition submatrices are of full rank, reducing the condition for maximal entanglement to a rank constraint on $P$. This establishes a direct correspondence between entanglement and cut-rank geometry in finite-field matrices. Furthermore, for square-free local dimensions, we show that the entanglement structure factorises via the Chinese Remainder Theorem into independent prime-field contributions, yielding an exact additive decomposition of Rényi-2 entropies. These results provide an algebraic characterisation of entanglement in the quadratic phase formalism and enable the systematic construction of highly entangled states.

Summary. This paper generalizes the concept of quantum criticality to systems that are dynamically stable but lack a traditional ground state. By introducing the Krein gap as the fundamental energy scale, the authors show that critical behavior, such as long-range correlations and entanglement scaling, can be rigorously defined for a much broader class of bosonic models.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified framework for studying phase transitions in non-equilibrium or thermodynamically unstable systems, which is highly relevant for researchers in quantum optics, photonics, and many-body dynamics where energy spectra are not bounded from below.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Extending the theory of quantum criticality to quadratic bosonic Hamiltonians (QBHs) that are dynamically stable but lack thermodynamic stability (i.e., no ground state).

Main result. The authors establish that criticality is well-defined for all dynamically stable QBHs via the closing of the 'Krein gap,' and that this gap serves as the relevant many-body gap for characterizing phase transitions, exceptional points, and long-range correlations.

Method. The study employs a generalized criticality framework using Bogoliubov transformations, pseudo-Hermitian matrix analysis, covariance-matrix formalism, and information geometry (quantum metric tensor and fidelity).

Model / system. Translationally invariant, finite-range quadratic bosonic Hamiltonians (QBHs) on a D-dimensional lattice, which include physically relevant platforms like photonics, cavity-QED, and magnonics.

Key observables. Krein gap, entanglement entropy, two-point correlation functions, Quantum Metric Tensor (QMT), and quantum fidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. Dynamical stability vs. thermodynamic stability, Exceptional Points (EP), Krein Collisions (KC), and the Krein gap.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on translationally invariant systems and the specific class of quadratic bosonic Hamiltonians; it does not fully account for finite-size effects or open boundary conditions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates a landscape of QBHs, distinguishing between thermodynamic and dynamical stability and showing how the quasiparticle vacuum remains relevant across different stability regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of stability, defines the quasiparticle vacuum (QPV) and the Krein gap, analyzes the nature of spectral singularities (EPs and KCs), proves the uniqueness of the QPV, and demonstrates the scaling of entanglement and correlations near critical points.

Abstract

For a many-body system in equilibrium, described by a thermodynamically stable Hamiltonian, quantum criticality is associated with structural changes of the many-body ground state. However, there exist physically relevant models, notably, certain quadratic bosonic Hamiltonians (QBHs), which fail to have a ground state. QBHs can be dynamically stable or unstable. We show the notion of criticality is meaningful for the entire class of QBHs that are dynamically stable or at the boundary of instability, regardless of thermodynamic stability, and that the key state for such QBHs is a naturally and unambiguously defined quasiparticle vacuum (QPV). This state is Gaussian, and coincides with the ground state if the QBH is thermodynamically stable. We identify a relevant spectral gap, the Krein gap, associated to the minimal spectral separation between creation and annihilation operators, and show that the QPV is unique when the Krein gap is positive. We prove that, for dynamically stable QBHs with finite-range couplings, correlations are exponentially bounded unless the Krein gap closes, which is associated with one of two spectral degeneracies: an exceptional point or a Krein collision. Consequently, long-range QPV correlations can ensue. Thus, the Krein gap takes the role of the spectral gap for dynamically stable QBHs, and the boundary of dynamical stability and criticality (associated to exceptional points) or multicriticality (associated to Krein collisions) are the same. We also find that bosonic critical behavior beyond thermodynamic stability is witnessed by the scaling of the entanglement entropy and other indicators of equilibrium criticality from information geometry. Our framework opens the door to investigating all dynamically stable QBHs through the lens of critical phenomena, including thermodynamically unstable ones from photonics, cavity-QED, and magnonics.
