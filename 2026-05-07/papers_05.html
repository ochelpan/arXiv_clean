Generative Quantum-inspired Kolmogorov-Arnold Eigensolver

Authors: Yu-Cheng Lin, Yu-Chao Hsu, I-Shan Tsai, Chun-Hua Lin, Kuo-Chung Peng, Jiun-Cheng Jiang, Yun-Yuan Wang, Tzung-Chi Huang, Tai-Yue Li, Kuan-Cheng Chen, Samuel Yen-Chi Chen, Nan-Yow Chen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04604v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a more efficient generative model for constructing quantum circuits used in electronic structure calculations. By replacing heavy transformer layers with quantum-inspired Kolmogorov-Arnold networks, the authors significantly reduce the classical computational cost while maintaining high accuracy for molecular energy estimations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The scalability and parameter overhead of GPT-style Generative Quantum Eigensolvers (GQE) in quantum chemistry workflows, particularly as molecular active spaces and circuit lengths increase.

Main result. The proposed GQKAE architecture achieves chemical accuracy comparable to GQE while reducing trainable parameters and memory by approximately 66% and improving wall-time performance.

Method. A generative framework that replaces standard transformer feed-forward networks with hybrid quantum-inspired Kolmogorov-Arnold network (HQKAN) modules using data re-uploading activations.

Model / system. The method targets molecular electronic structure problems, specifically estimating ground-state energies for systems including H4, N2, LiH, C2H6, H2O, and the H2O dimer using a UCCSD-derived operator pool.

Key observables. Ground-state energy, chemical accuracy (error < 1.6 mHa), and potential energy surfaces (PES).

Important parameters / regimes. Subspace truncation dimension (d_max), number of measurement shots (N_shots), and circuit sequence length (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The circuit sequence length L must be manually specified; the method assumes the availability of a classical simulator like CUDA-Q for evaluation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the HQKANsformer architecture; Figure 2 illustrates the GQKAE workflow; Figure 3 displays optimization history for various molecules; Figure 4 shows potential energy surfaces; Figures 6 and 7 show error convergence with respect to shots and subspace truncation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalability bottleneck of GQE, proposes the GQKAE architecture using HQKAN modules, details the training via GRPO and evaluation via QSCI, presents numerical benchmarks on various molecular systems, and discusses limitations and future directions.

Abstract

High-performance computing (HPC) is increasingly important for scalable quantum chemistry workflows that couple classical generative models, quantum circuit simulation, and selected configuration interaction postprocessing. We present the generative quantum-inspired Kolmogorov-Arnold eigensolver (GQKAE), a parameter-efficient extension of the generative quantum eigensolver (GQE) for quantum chemistry. GQKAE replaces the parameter-heavy feed-forward network components in GPT-style generative eigensolvers with hybrid quantum-inspired Kolmogorov-Arnold network modules, forming a compact HQKANsformer backbone. The method preserves autoregressive operator selection and the quantum-selected configuration interaction evaluation pipeline, while using single-qubit DatA Re-Uploading ActivatioN modules to provide expressive nonlinear mappings. Numerical benchmarks on H4, N2, LiH, C2H6, H2O, and the H2O dimer show that GQKAE achieves chemical accuracy comparable to the GPT-based GQE architecture, while reducing trainable parameters and memory by approximately 66% and improving wall-time performance. For strongly correlated systems such as N2 and LiH, GQKAE also improves convergence behavior and final energy errors. These results indicate that quantum-inspired Kolmogorov-Arnold networks can reduce classical-side overhead while preserving circuit-generation quality, offering a scalable route for HPC-quantum co-design on near-term quantum platforms.

Imaging GHz surface acoustic wave modes in electrostricted LaAlO$_3$/SrTiO$_3$ heterostructures

Authors: Ranjani Ramachandran, Sayanwita Biswas, Prithwijit Mandal, Kyoungjun Lee, Madeleine Msall, Chang-Beom Eom, Patrick Irvin, Jeremy Levy, Mingyun Yuan
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04402v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates how to generate high-frequency surface acoustic waves in LAO/STO heterostructures using electric-field-induced electrostriction. By identifying new acoustic modes and achieving extremely low signal loss, the work provides a new method for controlling electrons and other quantum states in oxide-based platforms.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of generating and controlling Surface Acoustic Waves (SAWs) in SrTiO3-based heterostructures due to the lack of intrinsic piezoelectricity at room temperature.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated GHz-frequency SAW generation via electrostriction in LAO/STO, identifying two distinct modes including a previously unobserved shear horizontal-type mode with ultra-low propagation loss.

Method. The study uses RF spectroscopy (VNA) to measure S-parameters and Atomic Acoustic Force Microscopy (AAFM) for sub-micron resolution imaging of SAW waveforms.

Model / system. The experimental platform consists of LaAlO3/SrTiO3 (LAO/STO) heterostructures where a DC bias induces electrostriction, creating a thin-film waveguide capable of supporting acoustic modes.

Key observables. S-parameters (transmission S21 and reflection S11), SAW propagation loss, SAW velocity, and spatial waveform imaging via AAFM.

Important parameters / regimes. GHz frequency range (up to 2.2 GHz), wavelengths of 2, 4, and 8 micrometers, and propagation loss on the order of 10^-3 dB per wavelength.

Assumptions / limitations. The identification of the second mode as SH-type is noted as a possibility rather than a definitive classification.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the electrostriction mechanism, experimental setup, and S-parameter transmission peaks; Figure 2 shows the enhancement of transmission with DC bias.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the motivation for SAW-based electron shuttling, describes the electrostriction-induced SAW generation mechanism, presents RF and AAFM experimental results, and discusses the implications for future quantum device integration.

Abstract

The LaAlO$_3$/SrTiO$_3$ (LAO/STO) interface hosts a gate-tunable superconducting two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) which can be programmed to create quantum devices such as ballistic electron waveguides and quantum dots. To fully exploit this platform for quantum transport, a key requirement is the ability to shuttle single electrons, electron pairs, and other exotic states between spatially separated devices with precision. Surface acoustic waves (SAWs), which travel along the surface of a solid, offer a powerful route to achieve this through their moving electrical potential that captures and transfers electrons. %acoustoelectric coupling. In particular, SAWs in the GHz regime enable fast, controlled transport of individual quantum particles. Although this approach is well-explored in GaAs-based 2DEG, SAW generation in STO remains largely unexplored due to the lack of intrinsic piezoelectricity at room temperature. Here, we investigate room-temperature SAWs in LAO/STO and observe SAW modes up to 2.2 GHz with very low propagation loss of the order $10^{-3}$ dB per wavelength. To directly visualize these modes, we employ Atomic Acoustic Force Microscopy (AAFM), achieving sub-micron resolution imaging of the SAW wave forms, providing insight into the electrostriction-induced SAW generation mechanism. Our measurements indicate a shear horizontal-type mode, which provides the ability to couple to in-plane degrees of freedom for future acoustoelectric and quantum device applications. This work studies the fundamentals of SAW excitation and propagation on STO, a widely used and commercially available substrate, enabling straightforward coupling of SAWs to a broad range of materials that can be grown or transferred onto STO.

Improving FMQA via Initial Training Data Design Considering Marginal Bit Coverage in One-Hot Encoding

Authors: Taiga Hayashi, Yuya Seki, Kotaro Terada, Yosuke Mukasa, Shuta Kikuchi, Shu Tanaka
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04825v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper improves the FMQA optimization framework by ensuring that all binary variables in a one-hot encoded system are represented in the initial training dataset. By using Latin Hypercube and Sobol' sampling, the authors prevent biased parameter updates, leading to more effective searches on Ising machines.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. In Factorization Machine with Quadratic-optimization Annealing (FMQA), one-hot encoding of variables can lead to 'uncovered' binary bits that never take the value one in the initial training data, causing vanishing gradients and biased QUBO coefficients.

Main result. The proposed LHS-FMQA and Sobol'-FMQA methods, which ensure complete marginal bit coverage, achieve higher mean final cruising speeds in wing-shape optimization benchmarks compared to the baseline FMQA.

Method. The authors use space-filling sampling techniques, specifically Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) and Sobol' sequences, to design initial training data that guarantees every binary variable in the one-hot encoding is activated at least once.

Model / system. The framework uses a Factorization Machine (FM) as a surrogate model for black-box optimization, which is then mapped to a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) form compatible with Ising machine hardware.

Key observables. Final mean cruising speed (m/s) and the optimization gain (improvement over initial best value).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of design variables (17 and 32), number of discrete levels (M=32), and initial training sample size (N0).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the use of one-hot encoding for discretized variables and focuses on marginal bit coverage rather than pairwise bit coverage.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the optimization progress for a 17-variable problem; Figures 3 and 4 show the distribution of bit occurrences, highlighting the reduction of 'never active' bits in the proposed methods.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the gradient vanishing problem in FMQA, proposes LHS and Sobol' sampling methods to ensure marginal bit coverage, validates these methods using HPA wing-shape optimization benchmarks, and discusses limitations regarding pairwise coverage and space-filling effects.

Abstract

Factorization machine with quadratic-optimization annealing (FMQA) is a black-box optimization method that combines a factorization machine (FM) surrogate with QUBO-based search by an Ising machine. When FMQA is applied to integer or discretized continuous variables via one-hot encoding, uniform random initial sampling can leave many binary variables never active in the initial training data, and the corresponding FM parameters receive no direct gradient updates from the observed responses. We address this by designing the initial training data to achieve complete marginal bit coverage, namely, ensuring that every binary variable obtained by one-hot encoding takes the value one at least once. We use two space-filling sampling methods, Latin hypercube sampling (LHS) and the Sobol' sequence, yielding LHS-FMQA and Sobol'-FMQA. On the human-powered aircraft wing-shape optimization benchmark with 17 and 32 design variables, both proposed methods achieved numerically higher mean final cruising speeds than the baseline FMQA, with the advantage more pronounced on the 32-variable problem.

Interaction-controlled localization in one-dimensional chain: From edges to domain walls

Authors: Rahul Samanta, Sudin Ganguly, Santanu K. Maiti
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05064v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that in an extended Hubbard SSH chain, the spatial character of localized states is determined by the competition between on-site and nearest-neighbor interactions rather than the band topology. It identifies a transition from edge-localized spin-density waves to mid-chain charge-density-wave domain walls based on the $2V/U$ ratio. This establishes that these localized excitations are intrinsic, correlation-driven phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates whether localized states in a 1D chain are driven by underlying band topology (SSH model) or by electron-electron interactions (Hubbard $U$ and $V$).

Main result. The localization of states is controlled by the ratio $2V/U$ rather than band topology, leading to edge-localized spin-density-wave (SDW) modes for $U > 2V$ and mid-chain charge-density-wave (CDW) domain walls for $U < 2V$.

Method. The authors use a self-consistent generalized Hartree-Fock mean-field approximation to solve the Hamiltonian.

Model / system. A half-filled, one-dimensional Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) chain described by an extended Hubbard model featuring alternating hopping amplitudes ($t_1, t_2$), on-site repulsion ($U$), and nearest-neighbor interaction ($V$).

Key observables. Energy spectrum (eigenvalues), local probability amplitudes, site-resolved charge density, and site-resolved spin density.

Important parameters / regimes. On-site interaction $U$, nearest-neighbor interaction $V$, the ratio $2V/U$, and hopping amplitudes $t_1$ and $t_2$ (covering topological, critical, and trivial regimes).

Assumptions / limitations. The study relies on the Hartree-Fock mean-field approximation, which neglects certain many-body correlation effects beyond the decoupling of interaction terms.

Figures summary. Figures show the energy spectrum, spatial localization of wavefunctions, and density profiles (charge and spin) across different interaction regimes and hopping phases, including the transition from edge modes to mid-chain domain walls.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SSH-Hubbard model, details the Hartree-Fock method, presents results for different interaction ratios ($U$ vs $V$) across topological, critical, and trivial hopping regimes, and concludes with a summary of interaction-driven localization.

Abstract

Using Hartree-Fock mean-field approach, we study the role of on-site ($U$) and extended ($V$) Hubbard interactions on the existence and evolution of edge modes in a half-filled Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) chain. We analyze the energy spectrum, local probability amplitudes, and site-resolved charge and spin density profiles across topological, critical, and trivial hopping regimes. We find that the localization of bound states is controlled by the ratio $2V/U$, with edge spin-density-wave modes for $U>2V$ and mid-chain charge-density-wave domain walls for $U<2V$, independent of band topology. These results establish the correlation-driven origin of localized states in finite one-dimensional chains.

Summary. This paper provides rigorous mathematical proofs regarding the growth rates of lattice-based tadpole structures. Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the asymptotic counting and growth rates of 'tadpoles' (graphs consisting of a cycle/head and a path/tail) embedded in d-dimensional hypercubic lattices, specifically looking at how topological constraints like knot type and surface piercing affect these rates.

Main result. The author proves that the exponential growth rate of tadpoles is generally the same as self-avoiding walks, but identifies specific scaling regimes where constraints like an unknotted head or a fixed head-to-tail ratio alter the growth rate.

Model / system. The model consists of self-avoiding walks (SAWs) and polygons on d-dimensional hypercubic lattices (Z^2 and Z^3), specifically focusing on the topology of tadpoles (lassos).

Key observables. The growth rate (connective constant) of the number of tadpoles, the knot type of the head, and the number of times the tail pierces the surface spanned by the head.

Important parameters / regimes. Total number of edges (n), number of edges in the head (h), the ratio of head to total edges (alpha), and the knot type (K).

Assumptions / limitations. The results assume the structures are embedded in hypercubic lattices and analyze specific scaling regimes where the head and tail grow linearly with n.

Figures summary. Figure 4 illustrates a tadpole with a trefoil knot head and a piercing tail; Figure 5 displays various graph topologies including twin-tailed and two-tailed tadpoles.

Paper structure. The paper presents rigorous proofs for the asymptotic behavior of tadpoles, moving from general growth rates to specific constraints on knot types and geometric configurations like surface piercing.

Abstract

We prove several rigorous results about the asymptotic behaviour of the numbers of tadpoles (or lassos) embedded in a lattice, including cases where the head is subject to a constraint like being unknotted, or where the tail pierces the surface spanned by the head.

Melting upon cooling in a quantum magnet

Authors: K. Jaksetič, T. Arh, M. Pregelj, M. Gomilšek, M. Dragomir, P. Prelovšek, M. Ulaga, L. Šibav, M. Malovrh, K. Železnikar, Z. Jagličić, P. Manuel, F. Orlandi, D. Khalyavin, M. D. Le, N. Bujault, E. Lhotel, J. van Tol, U. Jena, B. Sana, P. Khuntia, A. Zorko
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04611v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper reports the discovery of a 'spin Pomeranchuk effect' in the quantum magnet ErTa7O19, where cooling the system causes a long-range ordered magnetic phase to melt into a short-range correlated state. This counterintuitive phenomenon arises from the competition between exchange and dipolar interactions. The findings provide a new way to understand phase transformations in frustrated magnetic systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the 'spin Pomeranchuk effect' in the quantum magnet ErTa7O19, where the system undergoes an unconventional transition from a long-range ordered state to a short-range correlated state upon cooling.

Main result. The study demonstrates that the Ising-like triangular-lattice antiferromagnet ErTa7O19 exhibits 'melting upon cooling,' transitioning from a three-sublattice long-range ordered phase to a short-range correlated spin-stripe state due to the competition between exchange and dipolar interactions.

Method. The researchers used inelastic neutron scattering, electron spin resonance, and SQUID magnetometry for experimental characterization, combined with Crystal Electric Field (CEF) modeling, Finite-Temperature Lanczos Method (FTLM), and Monte Carlo simulations for theoretical analysis.

Model / system. The system is ErTa7O19, a rare-earth heptatantalate featuring Er3+ ions on a triangular lattice with strong Ising-like anisotropy. The model is an XXZ exchange Hamiltonian supplemented by long-range dipolar interactions.

Key observables. Magnetic propagation vectors (qK and qM), magnetic structure factor, magnetic entropy, specific heat, g-factors, and magnetization curves.

Important parameters / regimes. Néel temperature TN = 100 mK, transition temperatures Th = 65 mK and Tl = 25 mK, and high anisotropy ratio (gc/gab approx 10).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a 2D model to describe the physics, with 3D ordering treated via a mean-field approximation for interlayer interactions.

Figures summary. Figure 1A shows a schematic of Er3+ magnetic moments and interactions; Figure S1 shows XRD Rietveld refinement and Laue diffraction; Figure S2 shows low-temperature magnetization and specific heat; Figure S3 shows CEF-related data.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of inverse melting, describes the crystal structure and magnetic properties of ErTa7O19, presents experimental neutron and magnetic data, provides theoretical modeling via FTLM and CEF to explain the phase sequence, and concludes with the implications of the spin Pomeranchuk effect.

Abstract

Heating enhances thermal fluctuations and typically leads to melting of solids, but in exceptional cases, heating can also cause liquids to solidify. The paradigm of this counterintuitive phenomenon is solidification of liquid $^3$He upon increasing temperature, known as the Pomeranchuk effect. Here we show that such inverse melting also appears in quantum magnetism. We find that, on cooling, the Ising-like triangular-lattice antiferromagnet erbium heptatantalate first develops a three-sublattice long-range magnetic order -- analogous to a solid -- which then, unexpectedly, melts at even lower temperatures into a short-range correlated spin-stripe state -- analogous to a liquid. We propose that such an unprecedented ``spin Pomeranchuk effect" can generically arise from strong competition between spin-spin interactions in frustrated magnets, and provides a novel avenue to transformations between exotic magnetic phases.

Microscopic evidence for imaginary charge density wave in a kagome metal

Authors: S. Suetsugu, F. Hori, M. Shibata, S. Kitagawa, K. Ishida, T. Asaba, S. Nakazawa, Q. Li, H. -H. Wen, T. Shibauchi, H. Kontani, Y. Matsuda
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05101v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper reports the discovery of microscopic evidence for an imaginary charge density wave in the kagome metal CsV3Sb5. By using NMR/NQR spectroscopy, the authors identified spontaneous local magnetic fields arising from chiral loop currents. This establishes a new form of quantum order characterized by broken time-reversal symmetry.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether the anomalous electronic phenomena in the kagome metal CsV3Sb5 arise from real charge density waves or a controversial 'imaginary charge density wave' (iCDW) associated with spontaneous time-reversal symmetry breaking.

Main result. The study provides microscopic evidence for a pure iCDW state characterized by chiral loop currents, evidenced by spontaneous local magnetic fields of ~1 mT and a triple-q configuration.

Method. Nuclear Quadrupole Resonance (NQR) and Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy on the 121-Sb site, utilizing field-dependent and temperature-dependent measurements to disentangle magnetic and electric effects.

Model / system. The kagome nonmagnetic metal CsV3Sb5, specifically focusing on the Sb1 and Sb2 sites within the 2D V-Sb kagome layers, modeled via a triple-q loop current configuration.

Key observables. Spectral linewidth (FWHM), spectral centroid asymmetry, local hyperfine field strength (h_loc), and transition frequency shifts.

Important parameters / regimes. Transition temperatures T_CDW (~94 K), T* (~120 K), and local magnetic fields (~1 mT).

Assumptions / limitations. The assumption that the observed spectral asymmetry follows a triple-q distribution where sites experience hyperfine fields in a 1:3 ratio.

Figures summary. Figures show the temperature dependence of NQR linewidth broadening, the splitting of resonant frequencies under a c-axis magnetic field, and the successful fitting of experimental spectra using a triple-q model.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the controversy of iCDW, presents NQR/NMR evidence of anomalous broadening at T*, demonstrates the magnetic origin of this broadening through field-dependent NMR, validates the triple-q model, and concludes with the identification of spontaneous time-reversal symmetry breaking.

Abstract

Dissipationless charge transport without any energy loss is one of the most fascinating phenomena in condensed matter physics. This extraordinary state manifests in two well-established systems: superconductors and quantum Hall systems. A proposed third category is associated with chiral loop current order, characterized by the spontaneous formation of microscopic electric current loops. The microscopic origin of these currents stems from imaginary hopping terms, conceptualized as an imaginary charge density wave (iCDW). Despite extensive investigations, its existence remains highly controversial. Here we report site-selective spectroscopic evidence for a pure iCDW in the kagome nonmagnetic metal CsV$_3$Sb$_5$. Nuclear quadrupole resonance spectra at out-of-plane $^{121}$Sb site sensitive to in-plane currents reveal anomalous broadening below $T^*\approx$120 K, coinciding with the nematic transition well above the real charge density wave (CDW). Under magnetic fields, the spectra exhibit asymmetric lineshapes, demonstrating that this broadening purely originates from magnetic effects rather than from electric quadrupolar effects associated with CDW fluctuations. The observed lineshapes are quantitatively consistent with ~1 mT local fields induced by chiral loop currents, indicating spontaneous time-reversal symmetry breaking. This microscopic identification of the long-sought pure iCDW establishes a novel form of quantum order, potentially revolutionizing our understanding of exotic electronic states in quantum materials.

Quantum algorithm for solving differential equations using SLAC derivatives

Authors: Rakshit M. Gharat, Gopikrishnan Muraleedharan, Dominic W. Berry, Gavin K. Brennen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04861v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new quantum algorithm for solving partial differential equations using the SLAC derivative discretization. By combining LCU-based block-encoding with the Quantum Shannon Wavelet Transform, the authors achieve an efficient, multi-scale approach that avoids the unphysical artifacts of standard lattice methods.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and field theory simulations, as it provides a way to implement non-local operators (essential for avoiding fermion doubling) on quantum hardware with efficient scaling.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing efficient quantum algorithms to solve partial differential equations (PDEs) using SLAC derivatives, which are non-local and result in dense matrices that are difficult to simulate classically.

Main result. The authors construct efficient LCU-based block-encodings for first-order derivative and Laplacian operators in the SLAC representation, achieving polylogarithmic gate complexity and reduced condition numbers via wavelet preconditioning.

Method. The approach utilizes Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) for block-encoding, the Quantum Shannon Wavelet Transform (QSWT) for multi-scale representations, and a nested-box inequality-test framework for efficient state preparation.

Model / system. The framework applies to discretized continuum field theories and many-body quantum systems on a finite lattice, specifically focusing on translation-invariant pseudodifferential operators like the SLAC Laplacian and first-order derivative.

Important parameters / regimes. Complexity metrics including subnormalization factor (alpha), gate complexity (O(n^2)), and error scaling (epsilon); momentum scales (IR and UV sectors).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes coefficient functions are smoothly decaying, the lattice size is a power of two, and the right-hand-side state has support on the invertible subspace of the operator.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a quantum circuit implementation of the QSWT; Figure 2 illustrates a high-level circuit diagram for the state-preparation protocol; Figure 4 depicts the SELECT operation as a black box of adder circuits.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of SLAC discretization, proposes LCU-based block-encodings for derivatives, details a state-preparation protocol using nested-box inequality tests, implements multi-scale representations via QSWT, introduces a wavelet preconditioner, and concludes with a complexity analysis and comparison to general PDO frameworks.

Abstract

We present the construction of efficient linear-combination-of-unitaries (LCU)-based block-encodings for the first-order derivative and Laplacian operators in the SLAC representation. We use state-preparation techniques designed for smoothly decaying functions to efficiently prepare the dense LCU amplitudes with high success probability and low gate cost. Furthermore, we demonstrate how Shannon wavelet transforms can be applied to these block-encodings to efficiently obtain multi-scale representations of the SLAC derivative operators. We then show how to apply a diagonal preconditioner that reduces the condition number of these matrices in the multi-scale wavelet basis to a small constant. This approach enables the solution of partial differential equations with SLAC-discretised derivative operators on a finite lattice using the quantum linear solving algorithm (QLSA). Throughout this work, we analyse the computational complexity and error scaling of each implementation.

Summary. This paper uses advanced Monte Carlo techniques to study the topological and scaling properties of self-avoiding theta-graphs. It provides high-precision estimates for critical exponents and supports long-standing theoretical conjectures regarding polymer networks in two dimensions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to characterize the scaling behavior, critical exponents, and geometric properties of self-avoiding theta-graphs (spatial graphs with two vertices of degree 3) on 2D square and 3D cubic lattices.

Main result. The authors found evidence supporting Duplantier's conjecture for the entropic critical exponent in 2D monodisperse theta-graphs and provided precise estimates for exponents governing arm-length distributions and branch point separation.

Method. A combination of the Wang-Landau Monte Carlo algorithm for density of states estimation and a variant of the BFACF algorithm adapted for degree-3 vertices was used.

Model / system. The model consists of self-avoiding theta-graphs (polydisperse and monodisperse) embedded in 2D square and 3D simple cubic lattices, focusing on unknotted configurations in 3D.

Figures summary. Figures include lattice topology illustrations, examples of theta-graphs, schematics of BFACF moves, and various plots of scaling ratios and error convergence used to estimate critical exponents.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the theta-graph model, describes the modified BFACF and Wang-Landau algorithms, presents numerical results for 2D and 3D scaling exponents, and concludes with comparisons to theoretical conjectures.

Abstract

Theta-graphs are a type of spatial graph with two vertices connected by three edges. We investigate embeddings of theta-graphs in the square and simple cubic lattices, using a combination of the Wang-Landau Monte Carlo method with a variant of the BFACF algorithm which accommodates vertices of degree 3. This allows us to estimate the critical exponents governing the number of theta-graphs and the distributions of the different arm-lengths. For the cubic lattice these values can be compared to the corresponding exponents for prime knots. We also study the number of `monodisperse' theta-graphs where the three arms have the same lengths, and find evidence supporting a conjecture for the critical exponent in two dimensions.

Summary. This paper uses advanced ab initio methods to model the electronic and structural properties of the V_B- defect in hBN. It reveals how Jahn-Teller distortions and external pressure influence the spin-dependent optical transitions. These findings are essential for advancing the use of hBN defects in nanoscale quantum sensing.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental understanding of the spin-dependent decay pathways and the role of electron-phonon coupling in a 2D defect, which is critical for developing 2D quantum sensors and spin-photon interfaces.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty in providing a comprehensive description of the optical cycle and ODMR signal in the V_B- center of hBN due to the strongly correlated nature of its excited electronic states.

Main result. The study identifies the optical transitions, characterizes the Jahn-Teller distortions, and quantifies how external pressure and strain affect the zero-field splitting and intersystem crossing rates.

Method. High-level wave-function-based electron correlation method using CASSCF-NEVPT2 and quasi-degenerate perturbation theory for spin-orbit coupling.

Model / system. The negatively charged boron vacancy (V_B-) center in a hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) 2D sheet, modeled using a B60N60H27 cluster.

Key observables. Zero-phonon line (ZPL) energy, radiative transition rates, zero-field splitting (D and E tensors), and intersystem crossing (ISC) rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Jahn-Teller stabilization energy (355 meV), transition energy (~2.3 eV), and temperature regimes (static JT below 200 K, dynamic JT above 200 K).

Assumptions / limitations. The study uses a 1D effective phonon model to approximate transition rates and assumes a cluster model to represent the 2D defect environment.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the structural representation and orbital visualization of the defect; Figure 2 illustrates the temperature dependence of ZFS; Figure 3 provides a summary diagram of excitation and decay pathways.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the electronic structure and orbital basis, moves to the analysis of excited state fine structure and Jahn-Teller effects, then investigates the impact of temperature on ZFS, and concludes with the effects of external pressure and strain on the optical and spin properties.

Abstract

Ensembles of negatively charged boron vacancy (V$_{\text{B}}^-$) centers in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) have emerged as a two-dimensional spin qubit system interfaced with optics to advance nanoscale quantum sensing. However, a comprehensive description of its optically detected magnetic resonance (ODMR) signal remains challenging due to the strongly correlated nature of the excited electronic states involved in its optical cycle. In this work, we model the energetics, structural relaxation, and transition rates of the V$_{\text{B}}^-$ center using a high-level wave-function-based electron correlation method (CASSCF-NEVPT2). We provide a thorough analysis of the excited state fine structure and pseudo Jahn-Teller effects, singlet-triplet quasi-degeneracies, photoluminescence parameters, intersystem crossing pathways, and stress-dependence of the fine structure and decay parameters. Our findings not only clarify the fundamental behavior of the V$_{\text{B}}^-$ center in hBN but also establish the theoretical foundation for advancing the V$_{\text{B}}^-$ center's readout for integrated 2D quantum sensors.
