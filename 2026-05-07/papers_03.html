GHZ is All You Need: Quantum Sensing with VISTA

Authors: Oskar Novak, Christos N. Gagatsos, Narayanan Rengaswamy
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04203v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · quantum measurements 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

Summary. The paper presents VISTA, a new variational protocol for quantum sensing that uses a 'quantum twin' circuit to mimic noisy system dynamics. By using physics-informed optimization and a new quasi-normalization technique, it overcomes barren plateaus and achieves near-Heisenberg scaling even in the presence of environmental noise. This approach allows for the simultaneous and efficient extraction of both signal and noise parameters.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical, resource-efficient framework for near-term quantum sensors by combining passive evolution with variational optimization, specifically addressing the challenge of parameter estimation in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Improving the precision of quantum parameter estimation (metrology) in the presence of environmental noise, which typically degrades scaling from the Heisenberg Limit to the Standard Quantum Limit.

Main result. The introduction of the VISTA protocol, which achieves near-Heisenberg scaling for moderate noise and enables simultaneous estimation of signal and noise parameters using a physics-informed variational approach.

Method. A closed-loop variational protocol (VISTA) using a 'quantum twin' ansatz and a Swap Test to compare a passively evolving probe to a parameterized circuit, enhanced by a 'Quasi-Normalization' technique to sharpen gradients.

Model / system. An N-qubit GHZ state evolving under a Lindbladian master equation involving dephasing and amplitude damping channels, with a Hamiltonian proportional to the signal parameter theta.

Key observables. Hilbert-Schmidt inner product, Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), parameter error (delta theta), and purity.

Important parameters / regimes. Signal parameter (theta), noise rate (gamma), number of qubits (N), and the Heisenberg vs. Standard Quantum Limits.

Assumptions / limitations. The achievement of near-Heisenberg scaling may not persist in the asymptotic N to infinity limit; assumes availability of high shot counts for gradient stability.

Figures summary. Fig 1: VISTA protocol diagram; Fig 2: Error scaling vs N; Fig 5: FFT spectrum for traditional protocol; Fig 6-7: Dephasing and Amplitude Damping ansätze; Fig 9-10: Impact of Quasi-Normalization on loss gradients and convergence; Fig 14: Vector metrology results.

Paper structure. Introduction of the metrology problem and limitations of existing methods; description of the VISTA framework and quantum twin ansatz; introduction of the Quasi-Normalization technique; demonstration of performance in dephasing and amplitude damping models; extension to multi-parameter vector metrology; mathematical derivation of QFI lower bounds in the appendix.

Abstract

Quantum metrology holds the potential to enhance magnetic field sensing beyond current limits. However, in the presence of realistic noise, this advantage degrades to the Standard Quantum Limit. While recent algorithmic and variational techniques attempt to recover this scaling, they are hindered by stringent control requirements on the probe state that are infeasible in the near term, or by barren plateaus and interpretability issues inherent to black-box variational quantum circuits. Here, we introduce Variational Inference and Sensing with Twin Ansätze (VISTA), a closed-loop protocol that combines passive sensing, or where the probe state is left to evolve without any active control, with physics-informed variational optimization. In the VISTA framework, a probe state evolves under a Lindbladian master-equation, and is compared, via the Swap test, to a parameterized ``quantum twin", a shallow quantum circuit designed to mimic the underlying pure-state or Lindbladian master-equation dynamics. By restricting the optimization space to the physical parameters of interest, VISTA circumvents barren plateaus. We demonstrate that by coupling the protocol with a classical optimizer and high shot counts, VISTA can temporarily achieve near-Heisenberg scaling for moderately noisy qubits over a finite range of system sizes. Furthermore, we introduce a Quasi-Normalization technique that sharpens the loss gradients, enabling simultaneous extraction of both the coherent signal $θ$ and the environmental noise rate $γ$ with low absolute error. Finally, we extend VISTA to the multi-parameter vector metrology regime, enabling simultaneous parameter extraction from a transverse-magnetic-field Hamiltonian. By eliminating the need for complex, open-loop control and processing, VISTA offers a highly practical, resource-efficient framework for near- to intermediate-term quantum sensors.

Causal-Order Identification of Memoryless Sequential Quantum Processes from Restricted Projective Data

Summary. The paper provides a complete set of criteria to identify whether a quantum process is memoryless and sequential when only partial measurement data is available. It introduces a new algebraic consistency requirement that is essential to distinguish true sequential dynamics from processes with hidden memory. The findings are demonstrated through a tractable two-qubit Pauli model.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum networks, as it provides fundamental limits on how much causal structure can be inferred from incomplete experimental data.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the causal order and memory properties of quantum processes when available measurement statistics are not tomographically complete.

Main result. The author establishes that a distribution is compatible with a memoryless sequential process if and only if it satisfies directional Markovianity, positivity of the reconstructed Choi matrix, and a new algebraic consistency condition (C1).

Method. The paper uses the process-matrix framework, pseudo-density matrix (PDM) formalism, and distribution-level analysis to identify set-membership for different strategy classes.

Model / system. A sequential quantum process involving two players (Alice and Bob) interacting via an unobserved environment, specifically analyzed within a two-qubit Pauli setting.

Key observables. Local measurement statistics, joint input-output probability distributions, and temporal correlations.

Important parameters / regimes. Two-qubit Pauli regime, restricted projective measurements, and the dimension of the quotient space of the process matrix.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes restricted projective measurements and a non-tomographic regime where the underlying process cannot be reconstructed exactly.

Figures summary. Not specified.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of causal identification under restricted data, develops a mathematical framework using PDMs and Markovian chains, proposes a new algebraic consistency condition (C1), applies the theory to the two-qubit Pauli setting to prove the necessity of C1, and concludes with a discussion on the impossibility of full process tomography in this regime.

Abstract

Identifying causal order from restricted projective data is generally nontrivial. When two quantum players interact only through an unobserved environment, the available local measurement statistics are typically not tomographically complete, so the underlying process cannot in general be reconstructed exactly from the observed distribution. As a result, causal direction can be statistically identifiable in some cases but fundamentally indistinguishable in others. In this work, we determine necessary and sufficient conditions for deciding when an observed distribution is compatible with a memoryless sequential quantum process in a fixed direction. We show that directional conditional-independence structure and the positivity criterion based on the pseudo-density matrix, as developed in recent work by Liu, Qiu, Dahlsten, and Vedral, are not sufficient by themselves. The missing ingredient is an additional algebraic consistency requirement, and together these conditions yield a complete criterion for membership in the memoryless sequential class. We then specialize to the two-qubit Pauli setting, where the problem remains non-tomographic but becomes explicitly tractable. In this regime, we characterize when the two sequential directions are statistically indistinguishable, and we show by example that positivity alone does not exclude more general memoryful strategies, whereas the additional algebraic consistency requirement does.

Two-site Bose-Hubbard hopping and Schrödinger cat states

Summary. This paper provides an exact analytical solution for the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the hopping term in a two-site Bose-Hubbard model. It shows that the square of this hopping Hamiltonian can periodically transform coherent states into Schrödinger cat states. Additionally, it uncovers a new method for computing the properties of the spin x-projection operator.

Why it may be interesting. It provides an analytical framework for generating non-classical, macroscopically distinguishable superpositions (cat states) using a simple two-site model, which is highly relevant to quantum state engineering and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to characterize the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the hopping term in a two-site Bose-Hubbard model and study the generation of Schrödinger cat states.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the square of the dimer hopping Hamiltonian induces periodic evolution of coherent states into macroscopically distinguishable Schrödinger cat states and provide a new method for diagonalizing the spin x-projection operator.

Method. An inductive argument using bosonic canonical commutation relations is used to derive eigenvalues and eigenvectors, alongside an analysis of the dynamical evolution under the H^2 operator.

Model / system. A Bose-Hubbard dimer consisting of two lattice sites containing k spinless bosons, where the Hamiltonian focus is on the hopping term.

Key observables. Energy eigenvalues, probability distribution of energy measurements, and the formation of Schrödinger cat states.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of particles (k), hopping amplitude (t), on-site interaction strength (U), and chemical potential (mu).

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes the validity of Bose-Hubbard-type commutation relations for transformed operators and focuses specifically on the dynamics induced by the square of the Hamiltonian.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the relationship between the number of particles k and the resulting system properties.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the dimer model, presents an inductive proof for the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the hopping Hamiltonian, establishes the equivalence to the spin x-projection operator, and concludes with the dynamical analysis of coherent state evolution into cat states.

Abstract

The Bose-Hubbard Hamiltonian can be simplified to have only two lattice sites, in which case the system being described is referred to as a dimer. Due to its structure, the hopping term of the dimer Hamiltonian enjoys invariance in a family of subspaces indexed by a whole number $k$, each subspace corresponding to a system of only $k$ particles. We have invented an inductive argument using the bosonic canonical commutation relations to find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the dimer hopping Hamiltonian in its $k$-particle subspaces. In particular, this Hamiltonian, when restricted to one of the $k$-particle subspaces, is exactly the spin projection operator along the $x$-axis, where the number of particles $k$ in the dimer system yields the projection matrix for spin quantum number $s=k/2$. Thus, a new method for computing the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the $x$-axis spin projector has been unearthed. We use the explicit construction to study the dynamics of coherent states induced by the square of the dimer hopping hamiltonian. We find that it generates Schrödinger cat states in the two-site setting.

Finite steps optimise dissipation in stochastically controlled quantum systems

Authors: Theodore McKeever, Harry J. D. Miller, Ahsan Nazir
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04681v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

Summary. The paper demonstrates that classical noise in quantum control protocols prevents the use of infinitely slow processes by introducing new dissipative costs. It identifies a finite optimal number of control steps that balances deterministic and stochastic dissipation, providing a new guideline for energy-efficient quantum machine design.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a practical limit on how 'slow' a quantum control protocol can be before noise-induced heating outweighs the benefits of quasi-static driving.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the optimal number of steps in a quantum step-equilibration protocol to minimize thermodynamic dissipation when the control fields are subject to classical stochastic noise.

Main result. The authors show that while deterministic dissipation decreases with more steps, stochastic noise causes dissipation to increase linearly with the number of steps, leading to a finite optimal number of steps (N_opt) and overturning the intuition that slower protocols are always more efficient.

Method. The study utilizes stochastic linear response theory, quantum thermodynamic geometry, and the Cumulant Generating Function (CGF) to derive bounds on dissipated work and work fluctuations.

Model / system. The framework is applied to a driven qubit with strong system-bath coupling (Landau-Zener model) and a 1D transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) undergoing a Landauer erasure protocol.

Key observables. Dissipated work (W_diss), work variance (sigma^2_W), thermodynamic length (L), and magnetization.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of steps (N), noise variance (Phi), inverse temperature (beta), and noise processes (Gaussian White Noise, Wiener, and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck).

Assumptions / limitations. Weak Gaussian noise approximation, linear response regime, and a timescale separation between control and thermalization.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the quench process; Figure 5 compares dissipation scaling across different noise models (GWN, AR(1), Wiener); Figure 9 shows temperature dependence of optimal work and dissipation rates.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of stochastic control, develops a geometric framework for dissipation and fluctuations, applies the theory to specific noise models, and validates results using qubit and TFIM models.

Abstract

Motivated by the need for precise, energy-efficient, and experimentally realistic quantum control protocols, we investigate the thermodynamic cost of performing quantum step-equilibration processes under the influence of classical stochastic control fields. Whereas purely deterministic protocols exhibit dissipation that scales inversely with the number of steps, we show that weak Gaussian noise in the control variables induces dissipative contributions that grow linearly with the number of steps. Consequently, we derive the finite optimal number of steps and minimal achievable average dissipated work and its variance using the quantum thermodynamic length. These results are demonstrated using two paradigmatic examples: a Landau-Zener sweep of a qubit strongly coupled to a thermal bath and the erasure of a transverse-field Ising model.

Summary. This paper explores how the geometry of a 1D lattice, specifically the angle between sites, can be used to control topological phases in systems with orbital angular momentum. By mapping the system to a Creutz ladder, the authors show that tuning the angle induces transitions between different winding numbers. They propose and simulate a photonic waveguide implementation where these transitions are visible through changes in light localization.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a novel way to achieve geometric control over topological phases using OAM, which is highly relevant for designing robust topological photonic circuits and synthetic dimension engineering in quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the topological properties of a 1D staggered lattice can be geometrically controlled by tuning the relative angle between lattice sites using orbital angular momentum (OAM) modes.

Main result. The study demonstrates that tuning the interleg angle can induce topological phase transitions between different winding number regimes (W=0, -1, -2) and that these transitions are accompanied by observable band inversion and changes in light localization.

Method. The authors use analytical derivation of the Bloch Hamiltonian and winding number, numerical exact diagonalization for energy spectra, and finite-element simulations (COMSOL) for a photonic waveguide implementation.

Model / system. A 1D staggered lattice loaded with OAM states (l=1) that can be mapped to a Creutz ladder model in a synthetic dimension, with a proposed experimental implementation in a zig-zag array of coupled cylindrical optical waveguides.

Key observables. Winding number (W), number of topologically protected edge states, band inversion signature (sublattice-exchange operator expectation value), and light propagation/localization behavior.

Important parameters / regimes. Relative angle (phi), hopping strengths (J2, J2', J3, J3'), and the ratio of coupling strengths.

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on the regime phi in (0, pi/2) and assumes the winding number is well-defined (i.e., the bulk energy gap does not close).

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the lattice and Creutz ladder mapping; Fig 2 presents phase diagrams of the winding number; Fig 3 and 6 show energy spectra and edge state emergence; Fig 7 and 8 demonstrate band structures for different lattice geometries; Fig 9 simulates electric field propagation in a waveguide with a varying angle.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical model and its mapping to a Creutz ladder, provides analytical and numerical derivations of topological invariants and phase transitions, proposes a photonic waveguide implementation, and validates the theory with light propagation simulations.

Abstract

We study how the topological properties of a one-dimensional staggered lattice, loaded into states with orbital angular momentum $l=1$, can be controlled simply by tuning the relative angle between sites. The original system under consideration can be depicted as a Creutz ladder model when unwrapping the different state circulations in a synthetic dimension. Depending on the hopping strengths of the chain, different topological regimes may be accessed by changing the ladder angle, as determined by the value of the winding number of the chain. We analytically and numerically explore the different available regimes, and determine the number of topologically protected edge states that exist in each case. We also study the emergence of band inversion across topological transitions and show that it agrees with the winding number calculations, thus serving as an additional topological marker. Then, we propose a realistic experimental implementation in a photonic waveguide system, where the topological transition manifests as a sudden change of the behavior of the propagation of light in the system.

Role of mass fluctuations in the diffusion of clusters of Brownian particles with activity

Authors: Daniela Moretti, Pasquale Digregorio, Giuseppe Gonnella, Antonio Suma
Type: both · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.04812v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical explanation for the anomalous diffusion of active particle clusters by accounting for the stochastic fluctuations in cluster mass due to particle attachment and detachment. It demonstrates that these mass fluctuations create a new diffusion contribution that dominates at large cluster sizes, leading to the observed non-standard scaling.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Explaining the origin of anomalous diffusion in the center-of-mass of clusters of Active Brownian Particles (ABPs), specifically why the diffusion coefficient scales as D ~ N^(-1/2) instead of the conventional N^(-1).

Main result. The authors derive a theoretical framework showing that the diffusion coefficient is the sum of a conventional term and a fluctuation-driven term arising from mass exchange, predicting an anomalous scaling exponent alpha = 0.63 +/- 0.06 that matches simulations.

Method. The study combines an analytical derivation using coupled stochastic Langevin equations with large-scale numerical simulations of Active Brownian Particles.

Model / system. The system consists of clusters of Active Brownian Particles (ABPs) undergoing Motility-induced Phase Separation (MIPS), modeled using Active Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Particles (AOUPs) to account for mass exchange (attachment/detachment) with the surrounding phase.

Key observables. Center-of-mass diffusion coefficient (D), Mean-Square Displacement (MSD), cluster mass evolution N(t), and scaling exponents (alpha, beta, kappa, delta).

Important parameters / regimes. Mean cluster size (N0), mass fluctuation variance scaling (beta), mass fluctuation persistence time (kappa), and spatial dimension (d).

Assumptions / limitations. The mass evolution N(t) follows a Gaussian process; clusters are assumed to be roughly spherical; mass fluctuations are small relative to the total mass (|delta N| < N).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows cluster dynamics and mass exchange snapshots; Figure 2 illustrates the schematic of fragment attachment/detachment and fits for the diffusion exponents; Figure 3 shows the diffusion coefficient scaling with cluster size in 2D.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of anomalous diffusion, presents a theoretical framework based on coupled stochastic equations for COM and mass, validates the model against ABP simulations, and discusses the scaling regimes and dimensionality effects.

Abstract

Motivated by the anomalous diffusion observed in clusters of active Brownian particles (ABPs), where the center-of-mass diffusion coefficient scales as $D\sim N^{-1/2}$ with respect to the number $N$ of particles in the cluster, we derive a minimal theoretical framework starting from the single-particle Langevin equations. The model consists of two coupled stochastic equations: one for the cluster center-of-mass trajectory and one for the mass evolution $N(t)$, explicitly accounting for stochastic displacements induced by particle attachment and detachment. We specialize and validate the framework against ABP simulations of isolated clusters in stationary conditions, where $N(t)$ follows a Gaussian process with mean $N_0$, variance $\propto N_0^β$, and persistence time $\propto N_0^κ$. Analytical solution of the coupled equations yields the long-time diffusion coefficient as the sum of two contributions: a conventional term $\propto N_0^{-1}$) due to thermal noise plus summation of active forces, and a fluctuation-driven term $\propto N_0^{-δ}$ with $δ=2-2/d-β+κ$, where $d$ is the spatial dimension. We demonstrate that anomalous scaling emerges whenever the second term becomes dominant. The model predicts $D\sim N^{-α}$ with $α=0.63\pm0.06$, in good quantitative agreement with large-scale ABP simulations.

Summary. This paper presents a new second-quantized algebraic framework for 1D Abelian anyons that correctly accounts for both finite on-site occupancy and non-local exchange phases. It demonstrates an exact duality between pi/3 anyons and spin-1 operators, allowing anyonic models to be mapped to spin-1 XY-like models. This provides a new method for realizing anyonic statistics in physical systems like spin chains.

Why it may be interesting. The establishment of a duality between anyonic statistics and spin-1 operators offers a concrete theoretical pathway to engineer anyonic physics using well-understood spin-1 lattice models and synthetic gauge fields.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a consistent second-quantized formulation for Abelian anyons that simultaneously enforces local exclusion rules (finite on-site occupancy) and non-local exchange/braiding phases.

Main result. The authors establish a new algebraic framework for 1D anyons and an exact Jordan-Wigner duality between pi/3 anyons and spin-1 operators, providing a route to realize anyons via spin Hamiltonians.

Method. The study uses deformed commutation relations, a modified Jordan-Wigner transformation, and Exact Diagonalization (ED) via the QuSpin package to analyze the resulting spin-1 models.

Model / system. A 1D lattice of Abelian anyons with statistical phase theta = pi/N, featuring a tight-binding Hamiltonian with density-dependent phases and an on-site Hubbard-like interaction.

Key observables. Ground-state energy, total momentum, persistent current, energy gap, ground-state fidelity, and two-point correlation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Statistical angle theta, anyon density (filling fraction) nu, hopping amplitude t, Hubbard interaction U, and site occupancy limit N-1.

Assumptions / limitations. The algebraic construction and many-body states are restricted to 1D chains; the study focuses on Abelian anyons.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of anyon chains with periodic boundary conditions; Figure 6 and 7 illustrate anyon exchange processes and phase accumulation; Figure 8 plots the energy gap and persistent current as functions of anyon density for even and odd system sizes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a new algebraic framework for anyon second quantization, establishes a spin-anyon duality for the pi/3 case, performs numerical analysis of the resulting models using ED, and provides consistency checks via various exchange processes.

Abstract

Anyons exhibit a non-trivial interplay between local exclusion rules and non-local braiding and exchange phases, making a consistent commutation algebra and second-quantized formulation challenging. We develop an algebraic framework for Abelian anyons in one dimension with statistical phase $θ$ = $π$/N that enforces a finite on-site occupancy of N-1 anyons with the exchange phase $θ$ between different sites. Moreover, we introduce an exact Jordan-Wigner duality between $π$/3 anyons and spin-1 operators, allowing us to map a tight-binding anyon model to an XY-like spin-1 model. The model exhibits anyon-density-dependent flux, incompressible or gapless regions, and critical points with level crossings that appear as discontinuities in ground-state currents, momenta, fidelities, and correlation functions. Our second-quantization formalism establishes a novel spin anyon duality, offering a conceptually new route to realize anyons from spin Hamiltonians and to engineer corresponding device architectures.

Plasma effects on lifetimes and screening of Rydberg excitons

Summary. This paper explores how a background plasma influences the stability and screening of Rydberg excitons in Cu2O. It demonstrates that because excitons move faster than the plasma can respond, standard Debye screening models fail to accurately describe the system. The findings provide a potential explanation for observed deviations in exciton lifetime scaling at high quantum numbers.

Why it may be interesting. It addresses the breakdown of the adiabatic (Debye) approximation in systems where internal particle dynamics are faster than the environment's response, which is highly relevant to open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how a neutral electron-hole plasma affects the lifetimes and screening of Rydberg excitons in cuprous oxide (Cu2O), specifically testing the validity of the Debye screening model.

Main result. The study finds that plasma-induced scattering causes exciton lifetimes to scale with density, temperature, and principal quantum number, and that Debye screening overestimates the screening of the exciton's internal field due to high orbital frequencies.

Method. The authors use a combination of a classical orbit model and a semiclassical approach using the Truncated Wigner Approximation (TWA) to evolve the exciton state.

Model / system. A neutral electron-hole plasma interacting with Rydberg excitons in cuprous oxide (Cu2O), where excitons are modeled as classical Keplerian orbits or quantum harmonic oscillators.

Key observables. Exciton lifetime (tau), Debye screening length, electric field ratio (simulated vs. unscreened), and plasma-induced scattering rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Principal quantum number (n), plasma density (rho), temperature (T), and the adiabaticity ratio (omega_Ryd/omega_p).

Assumptions / limitations. The plasma is treated as a weakly correlated classical system (Gamma <= 0.2), and quantum correlations between electrons and holes are neglected.

Figures summary. Figure 1 validates the plasma model via Debye screening; Figure 2 shows exciton lifetime scaling with density; Figure 6 shows normalized lifetime vs. frequency ratio; Figure 8 presents a rho-T phase diagram of exciton observability and screening limits.

Paper structure. The paper begins by validating the plasma simulation against known Debye screening, moves to modeling exciton dynamics via classical and semiclassical methods, analyzes lifetime scaling and spectral loss mechanisms, and concludes with an analysis of screening efficiency and a phase diagram of exciton stability.

Abstract

We simulate the effects of a neutral electron--hole plasma on Rydberg excitons in cuprous oxide (Cu$_2$O), focusing on the validity of Debye screening and the role of plasma-induced thermalization. Unlike atomic Rydberg states, excitons in Cu$_2$O consist of quasiparticles with comparable effective masses whose orbital frequencies can exceed the plasma frequency, invalidating the assumption of a stationary screened charge. Using two complementary approaches, a classical orbit model and a harmonic-oscillator representation evolved via the truncated Wigner approximation, we study exciton lifetimes and interaction screening under realistic plasma conditions. We find numerically that plasma-induced scattering induces finite exciton lifetimes with specific scaling relations with plasma density, principal quantum number $n$ and temperature, possibly providing an explanation for experimentally observed deviations from the $n^3$ scaling at high principal quantum numbers. By explicitly computing time-averaged electric fields, we show that Debye screening overestimates the screening of the exciton's internal field, especially for high angular momentum states. Furthermore, we demonstrate that exciton-exciton interactions are not Debye screened at separations comparable to the Debye length for Rydberg excitons that are well resolvable in absorption measurements.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the mathematical structure of Shor's algorithm can be mapped onto the physical symmetries of molecules and periodic lattices. By showing that prime factors are encoded in the Fourier modes of these systems, it suggests that engineered matter could potentially be used to solve hard mathematical problems.

Why it may be interesting. It proposes a novel bridge between abstract quantum algorithms and tangible physical symmetries in matter, suggesting that synthetic molecular or lattice engineering could serve as a platform for physicalized quantum computation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the group-theoretic structure of Shor's algorithm and the Hidden Subgroup Problem (HSP) can be physically realized using the symmetries of molecular and condensed-phase systems.

Main result. The authors establish a formal mathematical mapping between the modular arithmetic used in Shor's algorithm and the periodic symmetries found in molecular orbitals and Bloch wavefunctions, suggesting that physical systems could potentially encode prime-factor information.

Method. The study uses group theory, specifically representation theory, the Chinese Remainder Theorem, and projection operators, to relate cyclic group rotations to symmetry-adapted linear combinations (SALCs) and Bloch's theorem.

Model / system. The models include molecular systems (e.g., hexahydroxybenzene, polyenes), condensed-phase assemblies (periodic lattices, quantum corrals), and optical beams with specific angular momenta, all modeled via rotation groups and periodic potentials.

Abstract

We describe a group theoretic analysis of Shor's algorithm and other related hidden subgroup problems in mathematics and relate these to symmetries of molecular and condensed phase assemblies. By recasting Shor's algorithm through the lens of group theory, we expose the possibility that physical systems such as molecular orbitals, condensed phase assemblies and optical beams may be designed such that these contain information pertaining to the solution to hard mathematical problems such as prime-factoring. We discuss real molecular systems, whose orbitals are constructed from symmetry-adapted linear combinations of atomic orbitals, and show that these contain information pertaining to the prime-factors of corresponding integers. Due to the broad significance of prime-factoring towards a variety of encryption problems in cyber-security, we believe this novel and fundamental approach may have broad impact.

This paper studies uniform mixing in continuous-time quantum walks. We show that for some unitary signing $σ$ the complete graph $K^σ_n$ has probabilistic uniform mixing. In contrast, it is known {\em no} complete graph has uniform mixing except for $K_2$, $K_3$, and $K_4$. Our technique is based on a stopping rule for quantum walks which reduces global to local uniform mixing. As a special case, we found an orientation of $H(n,4)$ that mixes to uniform faster than any other Hamming graphs. We also show that there are infinite families of oriented circulants with {\em average} uniform mixing. This is a chiral violation of Godsil's {\em No-Go} theorem which states that no graph has average uniform mixing except for $K_2$.

Abstract

We study the class of edge-coloured graphs arising from the graph-theoretic representation of quantum photonic experiments that generate multipartite W-states. Abstracting away physical amplitudes and phases, we introduce W-state graphs: matching-covered graphs equipped with a half-edge 2-colouring such that every perfect matching contains exactly one bichromatic edge and every vertex is incident with a red half-edge. Our main contribution is a complete structural characterization of W-state graphs. We show that a graph is a W-state graph if and only if each of its 3-connected components is a W-cone, a simple and rigid building block defined by a universal vertex and a factor-critical base. This characterization implies that no W-state graph is simple and yields a recognition algorithm running as fast as verifying whether a graph is matching-covered. We also show that the natural generalization to Dicke states encounters a complexity barrier: verifying one of the two Dicke state conditions is itself coNP-complete, resolving an open problem of Vardi and Zhang [IJCAI 2023]. Our results place W-state graphs firmly within classical matching theory and precisely delineate the combinatorial structures capable of realizing idealized W-states in the experiment-graph framework.
