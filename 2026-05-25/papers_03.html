Inviscid scaling in the Kuramoto-Sivashinsky equation from functional renormalization group and direct numerical simulations

Authors: Liubov Gosteva, Dipankar Roy, Nicolás Wschebor, Léonie Canet
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23364v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies a new intermediate scaling regime in the Kuramoto-Sivashinsky equation characterized by an inviscid Burgers exponent of z=1. This regime emerges because the effective viscosity vanishes as the system flows from its microscopic unstable state to its macroscopic KPZ state. The findings are robustly supported by both functional renormalization group theory and large-scale numerical simulations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The identification and characterization of a previously overlooked intermediate scaling regime in the one-dimensional Kuramoto-Sivashinsky (KS) equation.

Main result. The discovery of an 'Inviscid Burgers' scaling regime with a dynamical exponent z=1, which exists at intermediate scales between small-scale non-universal behavior and large-scale KPZ scaling (z=3/2).

Method. The study employs Functional Renormalization Group (FRG) using the Wetterich equation and Next-to-Leading-Order approximation, complemented by Direct Numerical Simulations (DNS) using a pseudo-spectral method.

Model / system. The one-dimensional Kuramoto-Sivashinsky (KS) equation, which describes systems with negative viscosity and positive fourth-order dissipation, relevant to chemical turbulence, flame fronts, and surface roughening.

Key observables. Dynamical exponent (z), effective viscosity (nu_kappa), two-point correlation function C(t, p), and time-reversal symmetry emergence.

Important parameters / regimes. Negative microscopic viscosity (nu < 0), positive fourth-order term (tau > 0), and the transition from the IB regime (z=1) to the KPZ regime (z=3/2).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a Next-to-Leading-Order (NLO) approximation within the FRG framework and the introduction of a noise term to facilitate the study of scaling regimes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the evolution of FRG functions and the convergence to a fixed point; Figure 2 shows DNS correlation function data collapse; Figure 3 compares the Fourier transform of the correlation function between FRG and DNS.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the KS equation and the problem of intermediate scaling, presents the FRG framework and results showing the vanishing effective viscosity, validates these findings with DNS, and concludes with the implications for universality classes.

Abstract

We show that the one-dimensional Kuramoto-Sivashinsky (KS) equation features a scaling regime characterized by the dynamical exponent $z=1$ at intermediate scales between the large-scale Kardar-Parisi-Zhang (KPZ) scaling with $z=3/2$ and the small-scale non-universal behavior. This scaling regime is intrinsic to the KS dynamics since it arises from the vanishing of the effective viscosity when evolving from its microscopic negative KS value, to its macroscopic effective positive KPZ value. This vanishing of the viscosity deeply imprints the behavior of correlations at intermediate scales, which exhibit a universal $z=1$ scaling. This behavior pertains to the inviscid-Burgers universality class, which corresponds to the zero-viscosity fixed point of the KPZ equation. We evidence and characterize this so-far-overlooked scaling regime using both functional renormalization group and direct numerical simulations.

Quantum fluctuations in quartet superfluid of two-dimensional Fermi mixture

Authors: Wei Wang, Yupeng Wang, Xiaoling Cui

Summary. This paper investigates quantum fluctuations in a 2D quartet superfluid made of one light and three heavy fermions. It demonstrates that including Gaussian fluctuations correctly recovers the expected bosonic equation of state in the deep binding limit. The work establishes a general framework for studying higher-order fermion correlations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical framework for extending fluctuation theories from standard pairing to higher-order correlations, which is highly relevant for understanding many-body dynamics and complex cluster formation in ultracold gases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to understand quantum fluctuations in a quartet superfluid (QSF) of 2D mass-imbalanced Fermi mixtures and to determine if these clusters behave as composite bosons in the deep binding limit.

Main result. The theory successfully reproduces the logarithmic dependence of the 2D equation of state in the deep binding regime, confirming that quartet clusters behave as composite bosons.

Method. The authors use a functional path integral approach with an extended Gaussian fluctuation theory that incorporates dominant four-body correlations.

Model / system. A two-dimensional (2D) Fermi mixture with mass imbalance (heavy and light fermions) in a (1+3) cluster configuration, specifically considering realistic examples like 6Li-40K and 6Li-53Cr.

Key observables. Thermodynamic potential (Omega), equation of state (EoS), gap equation, number equations, and the quartet chemical potential.

Important parameters / regimes. Mass imbalance (mh, ml), 2D scattering length (a), quartet binding energy (E4), and the deep binding limit (reduced chemical potential approaching zero).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on the zero-temperature (T=0) ground state and assumes a specific heavy-to-light number ratio of 3:1.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows diagrammatic representations of Green's functions and fluctuation matrix elements; Figure 2 plots the thermodynamic potential as a function of the logarithm of the reduced chemical potential for different mass-imbalanced systems.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of quartet superfluidity, presents a mean-field theory for (1+3) clusters, extends this to Gaussian fluctuation theory, provides analytical results for the deep binding limit, and validates the theory with numerical simulations.

Abstract

We study quantum fluctuations in quartet superfluid (QSF) of two-dimensional (2D) fermion mixtures with mass imbalance. Here QSF is a high-order superfluid that corresponds to the condensation of ($1+3$) clusters, each consisting of a light fermion and three heavy ones. By incorporating the Gaussian fluctuations respecting dominant four-body correlations in this system, our theory successfully produces the logarithmic dependence of the 2D equation of state in the deep binding regime, thereby offering a correct physical picture of quartet clusters behaving as composite bosons. By extending the Gaussian fluctuation theory from pairing to quartet superfluids, our results shed light on quantum fluctuations in general fermion superfluids with arbitrarily high-order correlations.

Two Operational Principles Single Out Quantum Theory

Authors: Kenji Nakahira
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23217v1

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the entire mathematical structure of finite-dimensional quantum theory can be derived from just two operational principles. By showing that local equivalence and a specific form of purification rule out classical and real-valued theories, it provides a powerful reconstruction of the complex quantum formalism.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum foundations and open quantum systems as it provides a fundamental justification for the use of density matrices, POVMs, and CPTP maps, showing they are necessary consequences of basic operational symmetries.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to determine if the standard complex quantum formalism can be uniquely derived from a minimal set of operational principles rather than being assumed as a single package.

Main result. The author proves that two operational principles—local input-output statistics identify channels and every state admits an equivalent-system purification—are sufficient to uniquely single out finite-dimensional complex quantum theory.

Method. The work uses a reconstruction approach within the framework of finite-dimensional Causal Operational Probabilistic Theories (OPTs), employing Euclidean Jordan Algebras and the classification of symmetric cones.

Model / system. The framework is an abstract, finite-dimensional Causal Operational Probable Theory (OPT) consisting of systems, states, measurements (POVMs), and channels (CPTP maps) defined within a strict monoidal category.

Key observables. Local input-output statistics, measurement outcomes, and probabilities.

Important parameters / regimes. Finite-dimensional systems, rank of the algebra, and informational dimension.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes a finite-dimensional causal framework, the existence of deterministic effects, and the validity of the two proposed operational principles.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the reconstruction process from a general OPT to quantum theory; Table I compares classical, real quantum, and complex quantum theories against the two postulates.

Paper structure. The paper follows a logical derivation: establishing the framework of OPTs, applying the two postulates to derive the geometry of state spaces (symmetric cones), proving the no-restriction hypothesis for measurements, and finally using the classification of Jordan algebras to identify the theory as complex quantum theory.

Abstract

Quantum theory combines density matrices, Born probabilities, tensor-product composites, positive-operator-valued measures (POVMs), and quantum channels. In a finite-dimensional causal operational theory, we prove that two postulates suffice: local input-output statistics identify channels, and every state admits an equivalent-system purification, unique up to reversible dynamics. The full complex quantum formalism follows; every consistent probability rule is realized as a POVM, so measurement no-restriction is derived rather than assumed.

Asymptotic Limits of Entanglement Distribution

Authors: Piotr Masajada, Aby Philip, Alexander Streltsov
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23443v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies a fundamental limit for quantum repeaters, proving that entanglement distribution over long distances is only possible if the channel allows for error correction. It quantifies the necessary resource overhead, showing that parallel channel uses must increase logarithmically with network length to prevent exponential entanglement loss.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the fundamental asymptotic limits of entanglement distribution in quantum networks, specifically determining the conditions under which entanglement can be preserved over arbitrarily long distances.

Main result. The authors establish a dichotomy: entanglement can be preserved asymptotically if and only if the channel admits a correctable subspace; otherwise, entanglement decays exponentially, requiring the number of parallel channels to scale at least logarithmically with the number of nodes.

Method. The study uses information-theoretic tools, including Kraus operator analysis, Stinespring dilation, and entanglement measures like concurrence and entanglement of formation, to derive scaling laws and trace distance bounds.

Model / system. A linear quantum network consisting of $n$ sequential noisy links (nodes) where each link is modeled as $m$ parallel uses of a qudit depolarizing channel.

Key observables. Entanglement of formation, concurrence, Schmidt rank, $G_k$-concurrence, and trace distance to the set of separable states.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of links $n$, the number of parallel channels $m$, the depolarizing parameter $p$, and the decay constant $\kappa$.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation for logarithmic scaling assumes qudit depolarizing channels; the analysis focuses on finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces and the asymptotic limit where $n o \infty$.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the entanglement distribution process through a sequence of $n$ noisy channels with intermediate stations applying LOCC filters.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the network model and the problem of entanglement decay, followed by a proof of the fundamental dichotomy based on correctable subspaces, an analysis of exponential degradation in the absence of such subspaces, a derivation of the logarithmic resource lower bound for parallel channels, and a discussion of implications for quantum error-correcting codes.

Abstract

Reliable distribution of quantum entanglement over long distances is a central challenge in quantum information science, fundamentally limited by decoherence in noisy communication channels. In this work, we investigate the asymptotic limits of entanglement distribution across quantum networks utilizing intermediate repeater stations and local operations and classical communication (LOCC). We establish a strict dichotomy: the asymptotic preservation of entanglement over arbitrarily long distances is possible if and only if the underlying quantum channel admits a correctable subspace. For channels lacking such a subspace, we prove that the transmitted state converges exponentially fast to the set of separable states, rendering standard LOCC filtering insufficient. To counteract this exponential degradation, we analyze networks employing parallel channel uses per link. We derive a fundamental lower bound on the physical resource requirements, proving that for channels without a correctable subspace, the number of parallel channels per link must scale at least logarithmically with the number of intermediate stations to sustain a non-zero amount of entanglement. This theoretical limit serves as a stringent benchmark for quantum repeater architectures and underscores the necessity of advanced quantum error-correcting codes, such as qLDPC codes, which show promise in saturating this optimal resource scaling.

Summary. This paper analyzes how underwater environmental factors like turbidity and background light affect the security of the BBM92 quantum key distribution protocol. It specifically quantifies the performance degradation when using non-maximally entangled states. The results highlight the severe limitations on secure communication distances in turbid waters.

Main problem. Investigating the performance and security of the BBM92 entanglement-based QKD protocol in realistic underwater optical channels, specifically addressing the impact of non-maximally entangled states and channel degradation.

Main result. The study finds that increasing water turbidity and reducing the degree of entanglement both significantly decrease the secure transmission distance and Secret Key Rate (SKR).

Model / system. An entanglement-based BBM92 QKD system where a source generates non-maximally entangled polarization states, propagating through an underwater channel modeled with amplitude-damping and depolarizing Kraus operators.

Important parameters / regimes. Entanglement parameter (beta), water turbidity (clear, coastal, turbid), atmospheric/background noise levels, and transmission distance (L).

Abstract

Underwater optical channels pose significant challenges to the security and reliability of quantum communication systems due to absorption and scattering. In this paper, we investigate the BBM92 entanglement-based quantum key distribution (QKD) protocol under realistic underwater channel conditions. Photon pairs are prepared in non-maximally entangled states, and the underwater propagation medium is modeled as a quantum channel incorporating both amplitude-damping and depolarizing effects, described within the Kraus operator formalism. The protocol performance is evaluated in terms of quantum bit error rate (QBER) and secret key rate (SKR), analyzed as functions of the entanglement degree and channel degradation parameters. Closed-form analytical expressions for the QBER and SKR are derived for the proposed channel model and validated through Monte Carlo simulations. The proposed framework is then applied to various realistic underwater scenarios, considering different water types, namely clear ocean, coastal, and turbid water, as well as varying atmospheric conditions.

A General Quantum Speed Limit for Non-Hermitian Systems

Authors: Zhanxi Wang, Xiaozhe Hao, X. X. Yi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23250v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper establishes new fundamental limits on how fast a non-Hermitian quantum system can evolve to an orthogonal state. By extending the well-known MT and ML bounds to non-Hermitian dynamics, the authors identify the specific initial states that allow for the fastest possible evolution.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics, as it provides fundamental limits on the speed of state evolution in dissipative or gain-driven environments, which are central to non-Hermitian physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to derive and establish tight, compact Quantum Speed Limit (QSL) bounds, specifically of the Mandelstam-Tamm (MT) and Margolus-Levitin (ML) types, for non-Hermitian quantum systems.

Main result. The authors derive two new, tighter bounds for non-Hermitian QSL that correspond to the MT and ML bounds in Hermitian systems and identify 'fastest initial states' (FIS) that achieve the minimum evolution time.

Method. The derivation utilizes biorthogonal basis theory and trigonometric inequalities applied to the real and imaginary parts of the state overlap within a non-degenerate non-Hermitian framework.

Model / system. A general non-degenerate non-Hermitian Hamiltonian with complex eigenvalues, including specific applications to a two-level system and a three-level PT-symmetric wireless power transfer (WPT) system.

Key observables. Quantum Speed Limit (tau), ML-type bound (F_ML), MT-type bound (F_MT), and Geometric-type bound (F_G).

Important parameters / regimes. Real and imaginary parts of eigenvalues (omega and gamma), the ratio of bounds (alpha), and the parameter delta for studying the approach to the shortest evolution time.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be non-degenerate and the Hamiltonian is time-independent; a shifted Hamiltonian is used to ensure non-negative eigenvalues.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares the difference between the combined QSL bound and the geometric bound against parameters theta and alpha, identifying regions of existence for solutions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QSL problem, develops the biorthogonal mathematical framework, derives the new MT and ML-type bounds, analyzes the tightness and saturation conditions of these bounds, and provides numerical illustrations using PT-symmetric systems.

Abstract

The quantum speed limit (QSL) refers to the maximum speed of a quantum system to evolve from an initial state to its orthogonal states. The bound on the QSL for Hermitian systems, for example the Mandelstam-Tamm (MT) and Margolus-Levitin (ML) as well as Sun-Zheng(SZ) bound, was studied respectively from the perspectives of average value and variance of the system Hamiltonian as well as the geometry of the system. While the compactness of the MT-type, ML-type and SZ-type bounds has been examined well for Hermitian systems, a compact QSL for non-Hermitian systems has not been well studied. In this work, based on the biorthogonal basis theory we derive two distinct and tighter bounds on the QSL for non-Hermitian systems, which correspond to the MT and ML bounds for Hermitian systems. We show that the shortest evolution time corresponding to the two bounds of the non-Hermitian system can be attained by certain initial states, showing the compactness and tightness of our bounds. These initial states dubbed fastest initial states(FIS) are different from that in Hermitian systems. A bound close to QSL for non-FIS is presented and comparison of our bound with others in literature is performed. To illustrate our results, we present a minimal non-Hermitian system to show QSL, and the condition for the shortest evolution time is derived analytically using the present theory.

Convexity and non-Markovianity of Weyl Maps

Authors: Wen Xu, Vinayak Jagadish
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23852v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper explores how the algebraic structure of discrete phase space influences quantum memory effects in higher-dimensional systems. It demonstrates that mixing non-Markovian maps can lead to Markovian dynamics and identifies new types of intrinsic, irreducible non-Markovianity in qudits.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems as it extends the theory of non-Markovianity beyond the qubit Pauli framework and reveals counter-intuitive phenomena like the suppression of memory effects through mixing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the emergence, generation, and suppression of non-Markovian dynamics in higher-dimensional (qudit) open quantum systems using Weyl dynamical maps.

Main result. The authors prove that convex combinations of eternally non-Markovian Weyl dephasing maps can generate Markovian semigroups, and they identify the existence of irreducible eternally non-Markovian maps in dimensions d >= 3.

Method. The study employs algebraic classification via the Hermite normal form, symplectic geometry, and number theory to analyze the subgroup structure of the discrete phase space Z_d x Z_d.

Model / system. The model consists of finite-dimensional qudit open quantum systems governed by Weyl dynamical maps acting on a d-dimensional Hilbert space, specifically focusing on Weyl dephasing maps.

Key observables. Decay rates (gamma_alpha(t)), which serve as the primary diagnostic for Markovianity and non-Markovianity.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension (d), subgroup order (K), parity of the ratio l = d/s, mixing coefficients (x_k), and the probability distribution parameter (r).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on cases where the effective Hamiltonian is zero and assumes specific functional forms for the probability distribution p(t).

Paper structure. The paper begins with an algebraic classification of Weyl maps using the Hermite normal form, proceeds to characterize Markovianity and semigroup properties of isotropic and anisotropic maps, analyzes the effects of mixing (convex combinations) on memory effects, and concludes with explicit qutrit examples.

Abstract

We investigate the emergence of non-Markovian dynamics in finite-dimensional open quantum systems governed by Weyl dynamical maps and their convex combinations. Using the Hermite normal form, we provide a complete classification of the subgroups of the discrete phase space $\mathbb{Z}_d \times \mathbb{Z}_d$, establishing the algebraic framework underlying the Weyl maps. We characterize isotropic Weyl dynamical maps that generate Markovian semigroups and show that anisotropic Weyl maps with nonuniform weight distributions cannot possess the semigroup property. Furthermore, we analyze the role of convexity in the generation and suppression of memory effects. Remarkably, we prove that convex combinations of eternally non-Markovian Weyl dephasing maps can generate Markovian semigroups, demonstrating that non-Markovianity is not additive under mixing. Conversely, we establish a general condition under which convex mixtures of $N$ distinct Weyl semigroups exhibit eternal non-Markovianity. In contrast to the qubit Pauli setting, we further identify the existence of irreducible eternally non-Markovian Weyl dephasing maps, namely, individual dynamical maps that display eternal memory effects without requiring any mixing mechanism. Finally, explicit qutrit examples illustrate the transition among Markovian, non-Markovian and eternally non-Markovian regimes. Our results uncover a fundamental connection among finite phase-space algebra, convex structures, and quantum memory effects, thereby extending the theory of non-Markovian dynamics beyond the Pauli framework.

Emergent heavy-tailed distributions from a Markovian random walk

Authors: Henrique S. Lima, Evaldo M. F. Curado
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22933v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that power-law statistics, often thought to require complex non-local or memory-dependent processes, can emerge from a simple, local Markovian random walk. By using a position-dependent step size, the authors show that a scale-free distribution with a -2 exponent is a natural consequence of a local feedback loop. This provides a new microscopic foundation for understanding heavy-tailed phenomena in various complex systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Explaining how heavy-tailed (power-law) probability distributions emerge in complex systems without relying on non-local jumps or non-Markovian memory.

Main result. A strictly local, Markovian random walk with a position-dependent step size generates a robust, non-Gaussian stationary state with a power-law decay of |x|^{-2}.

Method. The authors use a combination of continuum limit derivations, Fokker-Planck equations, Itô calculus, and the Onsager-Machlup path-integral formalism, validated by large-scale GPU-accelerated numerical simulations.

Model / system. A one-dimensional, discrete-time, Markovian random walk where the step size increases deterministically as the walker moves away from the origin, creating a positive feedback loop.

Key observables. Stationary probability distribution (rho_st), mean-squared displacement (MSD), effective velocity, and effective acceleration.

Important parameters / regimes. l (spatial threshold), r (rule parameter), delta_x (spatial increment), and tau (time step).

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes a specific Itô interpretation (step size determined by position before the increment) and a continuum limit approximation where k*s(x) is much less than 1.

Figures summary. Figure 1 displays the probability density versus position on log-linear and log-log scales for various parameters, visually demonstrating the power-law decay.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of heavy tails, defines the Markovian random walk model, provides analytical derivations via the continuum limit and SDEs, presents numerical validation, and discusses the physical implications of the acceleration mechanism.

Abstract

The emergence of heavy-tailed statistics in complex systems is conventionally attributed to non-local stochastic jumps or non-Markovian memory. Here, we present a one-dimensional random walk where power-law behaviors arise instead from a strictly local, discrete-time Markovian mechanism. The step length is governed by a deterministic function of the walker's position, establishing a positive feedback loop that induces strong effective correlations along the trajectories. Through analytical derivations in the continuum limit and extensive numerical simulations, we show that this rule yields a robust, non-Gaussian stationary state. The exact analytical solution is obtained in the closed form of a symmetric, Lorentz-like distribution, $ρ_{\text{st}}(x) \propto (|x|/l+rΔx)^{-2}$, confirming asymptotic power-law tails that decay as $|x|^{-2}$ over six decades. Furthermore, by employing the Onsager-Machlup path-integral formalism, we demonstrate that effective velocity and acceleration acquire physical meaning along the shortest fluctuation trajectories. Crucially, we find that a non-zero initial acceleration acts as the fundamental mechanism driving the walker away from the origin, ensuring both the emergence of scale-free statistics and the normalizability of the stationary distribution. This minimal pathway provides a new microscopic foundation for the widespread $-2$ power law observed across multidisciplinary complex systems.

Summary. This paper investigates how well the widely used tight-binding approximation describes light propagation in PT-symmetric waveguides with longitudinal modulation. While the approximation is reliable for tracking spatial trajectories, it fails to accurately predict phase-sensitive quantities like power oscillations and spectral fluctuations. This provides a critical warning for using discrete models to study complex non-Hermitian evolution.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics as it benchmarks a common approximation scheme used to study non-Hermitian dynamics and synthetic quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Evaluating the validity and accuracy of the tight-binding (TB) approximation compared to exact analytical solutions in PT-symmetric coupled waveguide arrays, particularly under longitudinal modulation.

Main result. The TB model accurately reproduces spatial dynamics like position and beam width but fails to capture complex phase dynamics, exhibiting significant phase shifts in total power and inaccuracies in spectral broadening.

Method. The authors use second-order supersymmetric (Darboux) transformations to derive exact analytical solutions and compare them against a discrete tight-binding framework calibrated via a spectral matching method.

Model / system. A system of two PT-symmetric coupled optical waveguides with longitudinal modulation, modeled by a non-Hermitian paraxial wave equation with a complex, z-dependent refractive index profile.

Important parameters / regimes. Longitudinal modulation strength, gain/loss parameter (alpha), coupling strength (kappa), and wave-number parameters (k1, k2, k3).

Figures summary. Figure 2 compares Hermitian evolution of spatial and momentum observables; Figure 3 compares PT-symmetric evolution, highlighting power phase shifts; Figure 4 and 6 analyze Hamiltonian expectation values and fluctuations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of TB validity, develops exact potentials using SUSY transformations, implements the TB approximation and calibration, and performs a comparative analysis of spatial and energetic dynamics.

Abstract

The tight-binding (TB) model is a widely adopted approximation scheme for describing light propagation in waveguide arrays. Despite its success, its validity in $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetric systems characterized by strong longitudinal modulation has not been rigorously benchmarked against exact analytical solutions. In this work, we address this gap by performing a comparative analysis between exact continuous solutions derived from $z$-dependent supersymmetric (SUSY) transformations and their corresponding discrete TB approximations. To achieve this, we develop a theoretical model for two PT-symmetric coupled waveguides subject to longitudinal modulation. We then evaluate the performance of the TB framework against the exact SUSY benchmark. Our results delineate the specific validity range of the TB approximation, demonstrating its proficiency in reproducing spatial intensity distributions. However, we also identify its limitations in accurately capturing the complex oscillatory phase dynamics inherent to this non-Hermitian evolution.

Abstract

We generalize the stabilizer formalism for entanglement-assisted quantum error-correcting codes with noisy ebits (EAQECCs-Ne) from the binary case to the general $q$-ary case, where $q$ is a prime power. By leveraging the structure of the generalized Pauli group over $\mathbb{F}_q$ and symplectic geometry over $\mathbb{F}_q^{2n}$, we establish a unified framework for constructing EAQECCs-Ne for qudit systems. Equivalent formulations in terms of symplectic geometry over $\mathbb{F}_q$ and additive codes over $\mathbb{F}_q^{2n}$ are derived. We further construct several families of $q$-ary EAQECCs with noise ebits and analyze their performance compared to optimal stabilizer codes. Our results demonstrate that under certain noise conditions, the proposed EAQECCs-Ne can outperform standard stabilizer codes with equivalent error-correcting capability, offering a promising approach for fault-tolerant quantum computation in high-dimensional quantum systems.
