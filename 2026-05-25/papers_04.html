Enhancing Blood Cells Classification using Hybrid Quantum Neural Networks

Authors: Guilherme Cruz, Nouhaila Innan, Alberto Marchisio, Gabriel Falcao, Muhammad Shafique
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23324v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that Hybrid Quantum Neural Networks can enhance the classification of complex medical images, such as blood cells, by providing superior feature representation. The researchers show that the quantum component improves the separability of visually similar cell types and remains robust even when executed on noisy IBM quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of accurately classifying microscopic blood cells due to subtle morphological variations and high intra-class similarity in medical imaging.

Main result. Hybrid Quantum Neural Networks (HQNNs) outperform both classical baseline and parameter-matched classical models, specifically improving macro F1-scores by up to 3.7% in challenging classes.

Method. A hybrid pipeline using a pre-trained ResNet-50 backbone for feature extraction, followed by a dimensionality reduction layer and a variational quantum circuit (VQC) for quantum-enhanced feature transformation.

Model / system. A 10-qubit variational quantum circuit using angle encoding (RY rotations) and a ring-structured CNOT entangling pattern, implemented via PennyLane and tested on both simulators and IBM quantum hardware.

Key observables. Accuracy, macro F1-score, precision, recall, and multiclass ROC-AUC.

Important parameters / regimes. 10 qubits, 4 variational layers, 2048-dimensional feature vector reduced to a 10-dimensional latent bottleneck.

Assumptions / limitations. The performance gains are attributed to quantum feature transformations rather than simple increases in parameter count; the study assumes the utility of the NISQ-era hardware for such tasks despite noise.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows blood cell classes; Figure 2 is a pipeline flowchart; Figure 3 is a VQC schematic; Figures 4-5 show training convergence; Figures 6-12 present performance comparisons, confusion matrices, and per-class F1-scores.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the medical problem, describes the HQNN architecture and comparison framework, details the experimental setup on datasets and hardware, presents performance results across different models and data scales, and concludes with limitations and future directions.

Abstract

Accurate classification of microscopic blood cells is still a critical task in medical image analysis, where subtle variations and limited data can challenge conventional deep learning models. As such, we investigate in this work the potential of Hybrid Quantum-Classical Neural Networks (HQNNs) to enhance feature representation and improve classification performance in this domain. We propose a modular architecture combining a pre-trained ResNet-50 backbone with a low-dimensional latent bottleneck and a variational quantum circuit, enabling a direct comparison between quantum-enhanced and purely classical transformation mechanisms. To isolate the contribution of the quantum component, we evaluate three architectures: a HQNN model, a Classical Matched Model with an additional nonlinear transformation layer of comparable capacity, and a baseline model without an intermediate transformation stage. Experiments conducted on two publicly available blood cell datasets, namely the Blood Cell Images dataset and the PBC dataset, demonstrate that HQNNs consistently achieve superior or more balanced performance across evaluation metrics. In the Blood Cell Images Dataset, the proposed approach improves macro F1-score by up to 3.7% compared to classical baselines, while improving the F1-score from 98.54% to 98.69% in the more challenging 8-class scenario with near-saturated performance. Additional evaluation on IBM quantum hardware shows that the model remains robust under noise, with only a modest performance degradation relative to simulated results. These results indicate that quantum feature transformations can enhance discriminative representations, particularly in challenging classification scenarios, and highlight the practical potential of HQNN models for medical imaging tasks.

Generalized Shift Vector as the Intrinsic Dipole of Many-Body Correlated Electronic States

Authors: Jiaming Hu, Sudipta Kundu, Zhichao Guo, Joshua J. P. Thompson, Wenbin Li, Hua Wang, Bartomeu Monserrat
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23431v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper reinterprets the shift vector as an intrinsic dipole moment of a single many-body correlated state rather than a property of transitions between states. This unified framework encompasses various phenomena including excitonic and electron-phonon-mediated processes. It demonstrates that the shift vector captures essential phase information within many-body correlations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified geometric and dipolar interpretation of many-body correlations, connecting internal phase coherence to observable transport and optical phenomena like the shift current.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Redefining the shift vector, a key quantity in nonlinear optics and transport, from a transition-based concept between two states to a fundamental intrinsic dipole property of a single many-body correlated state.

Main result. The authors establish that the shift vector is the intrinsic dipole moment of a correlated state, providing a unified framework that recovers optical, excitonic, and electron-phonon shift vectors as special cases.

Method. A theoretical derivation using many-body wavefunctions expanded in a Bloch-product basis, employing a branch-fixed approach to ensure gauge invariance and analyzing the expectation value of the electric dipole operator.

Model / system. Many-body correlated electronic states, specifically including N-particle states such as excitons (electron-hole pairs), Cooper pairs, trions, and biexcitons in periodic systems.

Key observables. Generalized shift vector (X_S), real-space joint probability density, linear Stark effect, and DC shift current.

Important parameters / regimes. Weak-correlation limit, two-band approximation, small-patch/short-range regime, and the phase-gradient of the many-body profile function.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes smooth U(1) gauge transformations, neglects edge effects in extended systems, and utilizes a weak-hybridization/small-relative-momentum expansion.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows schematics of real-space probability density displacement and the linear Stark effect; Figure 2 presents first-principles results for MoS2 illustrating the internal phase structure of different exciton states.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the shift vector problem, provides a many-body derivation of the generalized shift vector, proves gauge invariance, demonstrates the recovery of standard limits (optical/excitonic), and applies the framework to specific examples like MoS2 and Cooper pairs.

Abstract

Shift vectors play a central role in nonlinear optics and transport phenomena, where they are usually understood as charge-center shifts associated with transitions between quantum states. Here we show that the same geometric structure can be more fundamentally understood as the intrinsic dipole moment of a single correlated state. Our derivation clarifies the local and global aspects of gauge invariance, the origin of the phase-gradient term, and its connection to the internal coherence structure of many-body correlations. The single-state shift character appears both as a displacement of the real-space joint probability density and as a linear electric-field modification in energy space. Applying this framework to optically induced correlations, electron-phonon-mediated processes, and excitonic electron-hole states, we recover previously proposed shift vectors and the standard expression for the shift current as special cases. Our results establish a common physical foundation for shift vectors as intrinsic dipolar properties of correlated electronic states.

Indefinite probabilities in quantum spacetime: A deepening of unpredictability

Authors: Vittorio D'Esposito, Giuseppe Fabiano, Domenico Frattulillo
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23862v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores the concept of 'indefinite probabilities' arising from a non-commutative spacetime geometry. It shows that in a quantum-deformed universe, the probabilities of measurement outcomes do not commute, leading to a fundamental uncertainty in determining the relative orientation of different observers.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how non-commutative spacetime, modeled via the SUq(2) quantum group, leads to 'indefinite probabilities' where measurement outcome probabilities are non-commuting operators.

Main result. The authors derive an uncertainty principle for probability operators and rotation matrix elements, demonstrating that observers in a quantum-deformed spacetime cannot sharply measure their relative orientation.

Method. The study employs the algebraic framework of Doubly Quantum Mechanics (DQM) and braiding formalism using the R-matrix to compute commutators of probability operators in a leading-order expansion of the deformation parameter.

Model / system. A theoretical model of two observers (Alice and Bob) using spin-1/2 systems and Stern-Gerlach apparatuses within a non-commutative spacetime geometry described by the SUq(2) quantum group.

Key observables. Probability operators for spin outcomes (P(up) and P(down)) and rotation matrix elements (Rij) relating observer reference frames.

Important parameters / regimes. The deformation parameter q, which is related to the Planck length and cosmological constant, and the limit q -> 1 which recovers standard physics.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the validity of the SUq(2) quantum group as an effective description of spacetime and relies on a leading-order expansion in (1-q).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-commutative spacetime, presents the SUq(2) and DQM mathematical framework, derives the non-commutativity of probability operators via braiding, and concludes with the physical implications for observer orientation and uncertainty.

Abstract

Non-commutative spacetime and quantum groups have been argued to capture non-classical features of spacetime and its symmetries in the low-energy limit of quantum gravity. In this letter, we show that employing the $SU_q(2)$ quantum group to describe rotational symmetry for spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ systems and Stern-Gerlach apparatuses leads to the description of probabilities of outcomes of spin measurements in terms of non-commuting operators. As a result, we obtain an uncertainty principle between different probability operators, realizing a notion of indefinite probabilities. This is then reflected in the non-commutativity of the entries of the rotation matrix relating the reference frames of two observers, hence fundamentally preventing them from sharply measuring their relative orientation.

Multi-field Return Point Memory

Authors: Nathaniel Croce, Hossein Salahshoor, D. Zeb Rocklin
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23781v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper extends the concept of return-point memory from single-field to multi-field driving in disordered systems. It shows that while the order of applying different fields can lead to non-commutative behavior, the system can still be precisely controlled to return to exact previous microstates. This provides new insights into how complex, multistable physical systems can be trained or programmed.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether return-point memory (RPM)—the ability of a system to return to a previous exact microstate—persists when a system is driven by multiple independent control fields rather than a single scalar field.

Main result. The authors prove that multi-field driving leads to return-point memory where bounded field excursions restore the system to a previous microstate, while also demonstrating that non-monotonic field trajectories introduce non-commutativity.

Method. The study uses numerical simulations of a zero-temperature Ising model and mathematical frameworks involving the 'no-passing' property and partial ordering of microstates.

Model / system. A multi-field Ising model (MFIM) which generalizes the zero-temperature random-field Ising model (RFIM), featuring ferromagnetic coupling and multiple time-dependent control fields applied to different subsets of sites (e.g., checkerboard patterns).

Key observables. Magnetization (specifically even and odd site magnetizations), microscopic spin configurations, and the degree of partial ordering between states.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling constant J, disorder strength sigma, field trajectories (monotonic vs. non-monotonic), and the spatial pattern of field application.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is studied in the zero-temperature limit, meaning no thermal fluctuations are present and spins only flip to minimize energy.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows spin configurations and checkerboard field application; Figure 3 demonstrates the restoration of microstates in two-field and large-scale 10,000-field systems; Figure 4 illustrates spiral trajectories in field space and the resulting periodic returns to specific magnetization states.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of multi-field control, defines the multi-field Ising model Hamiltonian, analyzes monotonic vs. non-monotonic field paths, proves the existence of multi-field RPM, and explores complex spiral dynamics.

Abstract

Non-equilibrium systems display memory, a dependence not merely on their present environment but on previously applied fields. Multistable systems such as spin glasses, martensites and granular matter have exponentially many microstates consistent with an applied field, making their rich dynamics difficult to control. Control and order can be achieved through the concept of partial ordering, which we here generalize to systems subject to multiple control fields. We demonstrate, within the model system of the zero-temperature Ising model, that this leads to return-point memory, in which an applied sequence of fields restores the hysteretic system not only to a previous magnetization, but to a previous exact microstate. The multiplicity of fields grants more precise and complex control of the system, with different classes of operations displaying commutative and noncommutative behavior. This grants new insight into how physical systems can remember, learn, and be trained.

Orientable Surfactants on Thin Liquid Films: A Dynamic Density-Functional Theory Approach

Authors: Toby Kay, Serafim Kalliadasis
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23789v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a new theoretical framework for modeling thin liquid films containing polar surfactants by accounting for their molecular orientation. By using Dynamic Density-Functional Theory, the authors show that surfactant alignment leads to a new form of generalized surface tension. This provides a more physically accurate way to describe the complex hydrodynamic stability of surfactant-laden interfaces.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Traditional thin-film models treat surfactants as symmetric, point-like particles, neglecting their amphiphilic, polar head-tail structure and the resulting effects on interfacial dynamics.

Main result. The authors derive a set of coupled evolution equations for film height, surfactant concentration, and polarization, uncovering a novel generalized surface tension that depends on both concentration and surfactant orientation.

Method. The study uses Dynamic Density-Functional Theory (DDFT) combined with a long-wave (lubrication) approximation and a moment-based closure approach to derive macroscopic transport equations from microscopic statistical mechanics.

Model / system. A thin liquid film on a planar horizontal substrate with a free surface containing insoluble, orientable, polar uniaxial surfactant particles undergoing Brownian motion.

Key observables. Film height (h), surfactant concentration (c), polarization field (P), and the alignment tensor (Q).

Important parameters / regimes. Translational diffusion tensor (parallel and perpendicular components), rotational diffusion coefficient, surface tension, and anchoring strength.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a long-wave approximation, uses an adiabatic approximation for higher-order moments (slaving Q to P), and assumes surfactants are insoluble and confined to the interface.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a geometric sketch of the thin liquid film on a substrate with a defined coordinate system and the free surface height.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the physical system, moves to a microscopic DDFT derivation for uniaxial particles, applies a long-wave expansion and moment-based coarse-graining, derives the coupled evolution equations, and concludes by demonstrating thermodynamic consistency and reduction to known non-polar models.

Abstract

Thin liquid films are ubiquitous across many natural and engineering systems, including films which are laden with surface active molecules, i.e. surfactants. The presence of surfactants may have a destabilising effect on the film owing to their influence on surface tension, the so-called Marangoni effect, which in turn can induce flows in the film. Classical thin-film models for surfactant-laden films lead to paradigmatic gradient dynamics equations governing the film height and surfactant concentration and have been widely studied. However, in all these works, which are based on fluid dynamics or nonequilibrium thermodynamics, the shape of surfactants is neglected, and they have been treated as symmetric point-like particles. In general, this is a drastic oversimplification, as surfactants are amphiphilic with a polar head-tail structure. To account for this effect we use elements from the statistical mechanics of classical fluids, namely density-functional theory (DFT), and its dynamic extension (DDFT). Starting from DDFT and under the long-wave approximation, we derive the pertinent thin-film equations with the surfactants treated as polar uniaxial particles. These are equations which govern the film height, as well as the surfactant concentration and polarisation field. They preserve the gradient dynamics form by appropriately defining the free energy, which contains the usual interfacial contributions, as well as further contributions from the polarisation field. In doing so, we uncover a novel form of a generalised surface tension that is dependent on the surfactant polarisation as well as concentration, and show that it arises in a thermodynamically consistent way.

Sample-efficient benchmarking of shallow all-to-all random quantum circuits

Authors: Gregory Bentsen, Bill Fefferman, Soumik Ghosh, Michael J. Gullans, Yinchen Liu
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22909v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new nonlinear cross-entropy metric to verify quantum advantage in shallow-depth random circuits. It proves that this metric is resistant to classical spoofing and is highly sample-efficient even in the presence of depolarizing noise. The work provides exact analytical solutions for these benchmarks using many-body physics techniques.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing benchmarks like linear cross-entropy (LXEB) for random circuit sampling can be classically spoofed by noise, making it difficult to verify quantum advantage in shallow-depth circuits.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that nonlinear cross-entropy (log XEB) provides a sample-efficient benchmark that can distinguish noisy quantum computers from classical spoofers, and they develop a HOG-based classifier with logarithmic sample complexity.

Method. The study uses the replica trick and large-n mean-field techniques applied to Brownian circuit ensembles, supplemented by numerical simulations of discrete Haar-random unitary circuits.

Model / system. The model consists of n-qubit all-to-all Brownian quantum circuits with single-qubit depolarizing noise, which can be mapped to a quantum statistical mechanics problem with an effective many-body Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Nonlinear cross-entropy (log XEB), Heavy Output Generation (HOG) fingerprints, and the variance of the XEB score.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (parameterized by a), system size (n), and noise rate (gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes all-to-all connectivity and utilizes the large-n limit and the Brownian (continuous-time) approximation of discrete circuits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the required number of samples for XEB estimation vs system size; Figure 2 illustrates the mapping to a statistical mechanics problem; Figure 6 shows numerical estimation of expected XEB and variance for discrete circuits.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the benchmarking challenge, defines the Brownian circuit model and its mapping to an effective Hamiltonian, derives analytic expressions for the nonlinear XEB and its variance using the replica trick, validates these results with numerical simulations of discrete circuits, and proposes a new HOG-based classifier.

Abstract

Random circuit sampling (RCS) remains one of the most competitive frameworks for demonstrating quantum advantage in near-term noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) hardware. Unfortunately, absent error-correction, existing benchmarks to characterize these experiments, like linear cross-entropy, have been classically spoofed due to noise. Because of this, there are interesting regimes, like shallow-depth random quantum circuits, where sampling is plausibly classically intractable, but no existing benchmark can distinguish between a noisy quantum computer and an adversarial classical spoofer. In this paper, we demonstrate that the nonlinear cross-entropy provides a sample-efficient benchmark for shallow-depth all-to-all random quantum circuits whose score cleanly separates noisy quantum computers from state-of-the-art classical spoofers, even in the presence of depolarizing noise. Further, we develop a binary classifier based on the notion of heavy output generation that features logarithmic sample complexity at short depth. Our evidence comes from exact analytic expressions for all-to-all Brownian circuit ensembles derived using replica tricks, and numerical simulations that corroborate these results for discrete Haar-random unitary circuits.

Selective Fermi-Level Pinning: A Design Strategy for Giant Rectification in Molecular Junctions

Authors: Junnan Guo, Wenhui Fang, Jian Huang, Weikang Wu, Hui Li, Lishu Zhang
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23370v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. The paper introduces a design strategy called 'selective Fermi-level pinning' to create highly efficient molecular rectifiers. By using asymmetric anchoring and specific molecular structures, the researchers show how to force transport through unoccupied orbitals, leading to rectification ratios as high as 10^3. This provides a general principle for engineering functional single-molecule electronic components.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving high rectification ratios in single-molecule junctions by overcoming the intrinsic symmetry of resonant tunneling and complex interfacial energy-level alignment.

Main result. The proposal of a 'selective Fermi-level pinning' strategy that achieves giant rectification ratios exceeding 10^3 in cyclo[n]carbon-based junctions by breaking transport symmetry.

Method. Computational modeling using Non-Equilibrium Green's Function (NEGF) combined with Density Functional Theory (DFT) via the Quantum ATK framework.

Model / system. A donor-acceptor molecular junction featuring a non-coplanary long-chain donor (1,2-diphenylethane), a wide-bandgap acceptor (cyclo[n]carbon), and asymmetric anchoring groups (-SH and -CN) between gold electrodes.

Key observables. Rectification Ratio (RR), current-voltage (I-V) characteristics, transmission spectra, and energy-level alignment (HOMO/LUMO positions).

Important parameters / regimes. Bias window, HOMO-LUMO energy gap, and the electrostatic potential difference (EDP) across the junction.

Assumptions / limitations. The transport is primarily governed by the alignment of molecular frontier orbitals with the electrode Fermi levels under bias.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the schematic of the rectifier and the mechanism of asymmetric tunneling; Figure 2 presents the device configuration and I-V characteristics; Figure 3 details the electronic structure, including spatial orbital separation and transmission spectra; Figure 4 explores the effects of geometric changes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of symmetry in molecular junctions, proposes the selective Fermi-level pinning strategy, demonstrates the performance using cyclo[n]carbon systems, analyzes the impact of structural variations (donor length and ring size), and concludes with the robustness of the design.

Abstract

Molecular rectifiers are key functional components of molecular-scale integrated circuits, yet achieving high rectification ratios remains a longstanding challenge due to the intrinsic symmetry of resonant tunneling and the complexity of interfacial energy-level alignment. Here, we propose a rectifier design strategy based on selective Fermi-level pinning that breaks transport symmetry via pinning interactions between molecular frontier orbitals and electrodes. This framework enforces tunneling transport to be predominantly governed by unoccupied molecular orbitals, while substantially suppressing contributions from occupied states, thereby establishing a simplified and highly controllable rectification mechanism. The resulting cyclo[n]carbon-based molecular junctions exhibit giant rectification ratios exceeding 103, while retaining exceptional structural robustness against variations in both donor chain length and carbon ring size. This work reveals the critical role of selective Fermi-level pinning in molecular junctions and provides a general design principle for engineering functional single-molecule electronic devices.

Transition-state lattice modes and the breakdown of adiabatic tunneling for hydrogen and deuterium in bcc Nb

Authors: P. Graham Pritchard, James M. Rondinelli
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23212v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proves that the standard adiabatic approximation fails for hydrogen and deuterium tunneling in Niobium, showing instead that the process is a nonadiabatic, collective interaction with the lattice. This finding is crucial for understanding how interstitial defects contribute to decoherence in superconducting quantum circuits.

Why it may be interesting. The paper provides a rigorous microscopic foundation for understanding how defect-induced tunneling in solids contributes to decoherence in superconducting qubits, specifically through the modification of the quasiparticle density of states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the validity of the adiabatic approximation (separation of light-particle and lattice dynamics) for hydrogen and deuterium tunneling in bcc Niobium.

Main result. The study demonstrates that adiabatic separation is only valid for extremely light particles like muons, whereas H and D tunneling is a fundamentally nonadiabatic, collective process mediated by anharmonic lattice couplings.

Method. The authors use a 5D Lattice-Renostormalized Born-Oppenheimer (LRBO) framework, solving a 5D Hamiltonian numerically using inputs from Density Functional Theory (DFT) and cubic spline interpolation.

Model / system. The system consists of interstitial hydrogen (H) and deuterium (D) trapped by oxygen impurities in a body-centered-cubic (bcc) Niobium (Nb) lattice, modeled via a 5D subspace including three interstitial modes and two lattice modes (Q and T).

Key observables. Tunnel splitting (J), wavefunction separability metric (epsilon_psi), and lattice-mode probability densities.

Important parameters / regimes. Mass regimes (H, D, and mu+), energy asymmetry (delta), and elastic energy cost (Ec).

Assumptions / limitations. The accuracy of the tunnel splitting calculation is approximately 60-70% of experimental values; the derivation assumes the self-trapped H is stable at one site in a fixed lattice.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the tunneling subspace and lattice order parameters; Figure 2 presents potential energy surface and wavefunction contour plots for mu+ and H; Figure 5 displays lattice-mode probability density contours.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of adiabaticity in Nb, describes the 5D LRBO model and DFT methods, presents results comparing H/D to mu+ mass limits, discusses the breakdown of the adiabatic approximation, and concludes with implications for qubit decoherence.

Abstract

Interstitial hydrogen and deuterium in body-centered-cubic metals constitute archetypal quantum tunneling systems. Their relevance has been renewed by the connection between hydrogenic tunneling in Nb and defect-induced decoherence in superconducting qubits, motivating a predictive microscopic theory. Existing theoretical treatments invoke an adiabatic separation between the light interstitial and the host lattice, an assumption whose validity has not been rigorously established for hydrogenic species. Here, we show that the experimentally measured tunnel splittings of O-trapped H and D in bcc Nb are quantitatively reproduced only within a five-dimensional (5D) Lattice-Renormalized Born-Oppenheimer (LRBO) framework. This approach treats three interstitial modes and two judiciously selected lattice modes, which includes a transition-state mode, on equal quantum footing. By recasting nested Born-Oppenheimer hierarchies within this same formalism and benchmarking against modern \textit{ab initio} potential energy surfaces, we show that adiabatic separation of the light particle from lattice dynamics is satisfied only in the positive-muon ($μ^{+}$) mass limit. In contrast, tunneling for H and D is fundamentally a collective, nonadiabatic process mediated by anharmonic lattice couplings. Finally, we show that the breakdown of adiabaticity can be anticipated from simple energy estimates involving the ground-state light-particle energy evaluated at a small number of fixed lattice configurations, providing a practical criterion for assessing the validity of adiabatic tunneling theories in other systems.

Fractionalization, emergent SU($N$) symmetries, and fragmentation in layered quantum spin-orbital models

Authors: Pedro M. Cônsoli, Aayush Vijayvargia, Onur Erten
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22908v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5 · scars & prethermalization 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how stacking quantum spin-orbital layers can lead to emergent SU(N) symmetries and complex phase diagrams. It identifies a phenomenon called magnetic fragmentation, where different degrees of freedom (spin and orbital) exhibit fundamentally different types of order. This provides a theoretical framework for understanding intertwined orders and fractionalized quantum critical points.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates fractionalization, emergent SU(N) symmetries, and the coexistence of unconventional symmetry-breaking orders in layered quantum spin-orbital models.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that these models can realize a variety of competing phases, including flavor-selective localization and 'magnetic fragmentation,' where the spin sector exhibits local order while the orbital sector remains a quantum liquid with nontrivial string order.

Method. The study employs a Majorana fermion parton construction to map the Hamiltonian to an N-component Fermi Hubbard model, followed by mean-field theory and finite-size scaling analysis.

Model / system. A family of layered quantum spin-orbital models consisting of N stacked square-lattice layers with Kitaev-type intralayer interactions and Ising-type interlayer spin couplings.

Key observables. Intralayer fluxes, string order parameters, local magnetizations, charge density wave (CDW) order parameter, and flavor-selective density wave (FSDW) order.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of layers (N), intralayer interaction strength (K), interlayer coupling constants (J), and the anisotropy ratio (J'/J).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis uses a mean-field approximation for the interaction terms and assumes a fixed pi-flux gauge sector.

Figures summary. Figure 1(a) provides a schematic of a single square layer showing bond types and inequivalent plaquettes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the layered spin-orbital model, performs a parton mapping to an N-flavor Hubbard model, analyzes the emergent SU(N) symmetry, computes the phase diagram for N=3 using mean-field theory, and concludes by mapping mean-field orders back to physical spin-orbital observables like magnetic fragmentation.

Abstract

We propose a family of layered quantum spin-orbital models as a platform to study fractionalization, unconventional forms of symmetry-breaking order, and their possible coexistence. The models are built by stacking $N$ layers of a square-lattice system in which Kitaev-type interactions promote the formation of a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ quantum spin-orbital liquid and coupling the different layers via Ising spin interactions. Using a parton construction, we show how, at low energies, these Hamiltonians can be mapped to $N$-component Fermi Hubbard models on a $π$-flux square lattice at half filling. We also demonstrate that the models acquire an emergent SU($N$) symmetry in the limit of equal all-to-all interlayer couplings and argue that, for $N>2$, the proximity to this limit offers the potential to realize an array of competing phases. To illustrate this point, we compute the zero-temperature phase diagram of the effective $N=3$ Hubbard model within mean-field theory and uncover rich phenomena, including intertwined orders and flavor-selective localization. Mapping back to the original degrees of freedom reveals that the ground states realize distinct forms of magnetic fragmentation, wherein the orbitals remain in a quantum liquid state whereas the spins can present conventional long-range order or nonlocal order characterized by a nontrivial string order parameters. We highlight possible extensions of our construction as well as its potential to provide concrete microscopic models for different fractionalized quantum critical points.

Summary. This paper presents a systematic algebraic method to construct regular CSS quantum LDPC codes by using finite field cosets to manage parity-check matrix constraints. It demonstrates that this construction can produce large-scale, high-rate codes with excellent error-correction performance and verifiable distance properties.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of constructing regular, finite-length CSS quantum LDPC codes that simultaneously satisfy the CSS commutation requirement, maintain high girth (avoiding short cycles), and suppress low-weight logical operators.

Main result. The development of a two-branch multiplicative-coset construction over finite fields that systematically produces regular CSS bases, demonstrated by a high-performance [[10240, 4108, 10 <= d <= 32]] code with an extremely low frame error rate at p=0.058.

The approximate non-deterministic degree of a Boolean function $f$, denoted $\mathsf{ndeg}_ε(f)$ (written $\mathsf{N}_ε(f)$ for brevity), is the minimum degree of a real polynomial $p$ such that $0 \le |p(x)| \le ε$ whenever $f(x) = 0$, and $|p(x)| \ge 1$ whenever $f(x) = 1$. Unlike exact non-deterministic degree, which only requires the polynomial to be nonzero on $1$-inputs, this measure enforces a uniform gap: the polynomial must stay close to zero on all $0$-inputs and bounded away from zero on all $1$-inputs. The rational degree conjecture, open for over three decades, was recently resolved by Kothari, Kovacs-Deak, Wang, and Yang, who showed that for every total Boolean function $f$, \[ deg(f) \le \widetilde O\!\left(\operatorname{rdeg}(f)^3\right). \] In their paper, they explicitly propose a stronger conjecture: that approximate degree is polynomially bounded by $\mathsf{N}_ε(f)$ and $\mathsf{N}_ε(\overline{f})$ jointly, i.e., for every total Boolean function $f$ and every constant $0<ε<1$, \[ \widetilde{deg}(f) \le \operatorname{poly}(\mathsf N_ε(f), \mathsf N_ε(\overline f)). \] This conjecture, if true, would imply a polynomial version of the rational degree result and bring us closer to resolving de Wolf's longstanding non-deterministic degree conjecture. In this work, we make the first systematic progress on this problem, establishing the conjecture for several broad and natural function classes: monotone and unate functions, functions of bounded alternation number, symmetric functions, $k$-uniform hypergraph properties, and read-$k$ Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF) formulas.

Abstract

Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computation (FTQC) permits parallel execution of mutually commuting Pauli Product Rotations (PPRs), but per-qubit access point/port limits (e.g. two X and two Z edges on the surface code) force commuting groups that exceed the budget to be split, inflating circuit depth. We propose two heuristics for reducing this hardware-limited depth: 1. clique reshuffling, which permutes commuting products and re-forms port-constrained groups, and 2. generator restructuring, which rewrites each group as an equivalent generating set with reduced per-qubit port pressure. On QASMBench circuits compiled to PPRs, we combine the two heuristics and observe an average hardware-limited depth reduction of $10-20\%$ over a non-reordering baseline, with up to $50\%$ reduction. These observed gains scale with the per-qubit port budget and saturate near $20$ ports, suggesting these heuristics remain relevant as hardware exposes more access points.
