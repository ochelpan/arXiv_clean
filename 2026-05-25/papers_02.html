Weak wave turbulence as a precursor to universal coarsening in a homogeneous Bose gas

Authors: Simon M. Fischer, Martin Gazo, Sebastian J. Morris, Nikolai Maslov, Haoyu Zhang, Jiří Etrych, Gevorg Martirosyan, Christoph Eigen, Zoran Hadzibabic
Type: experiment · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22906v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 4/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium universality 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This study experimentally demonstrates that the initial relaxation of a far-from-equilibrium Bose gas is governed by Weak Wave Turbulence. It shows that particle transport follows a universal power-law distribution and scaling laws that act as a precursor to the later stage of universal coarsening.

Why it may be interesting. This paper provides a direct experimental link between the kinetic regime of weak wave turbulence and the late-time universal coarsening dynamics, offering a complete picture of non-equilibrium many-body relaxation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the relaxation and condensation dynamics of a far-from-equilibrium, homogeneous Bose gas, specifically identifying the universal description of the initial stage of particle transport that precedes universal coarsening.

Main result. The researchers demonstrate that the initial stage of particle transport follows a universal Weak Wave Turbulence (WWT) description, characterized by a power-law momentum distribution with exponent gamma = 2.4(1) and transport times scaling as (na)^-2.

Method. The study uses an experimental platform of 39K atoms in an optical box trap, evolving the gas by tuning the s-wave scattering length via Feshbach resonance and monitoring the time-dependent momentum distribution.

Model / system. A homogeneous, low-energy, far-from-equilibrium Bose gas of 39K atoms held in a cylindrical optical box trap with tunable density and interaction strength.

Key observables. Spectral population density N_k(k), momentum distribution n_k, peak momentum k_p, and the exponent gamma.

Important parameters / regimes. Density n, s-wave scattering length a, healing length xi, and the WWT vs. coarsening regimes.

Figures summary. Fig 3(a) shows identical N_k/N curves for different n and a; Fig 3(b) plots transport time delta t versus na; Fig 4(a) shows the evolution of the gas momentum distribution.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Bose gas relaxation, describes the experimental setup with 39K atoms, presents the observation of WWT scaling in momentum distributions and transport times, and concludes by showing the transition from WWT to the universal coarsening regime.

Abstract

Relaxation and condensation of an isolated low-energy Bose gas provide an ideal setting for the study of the universal features of far-from-equilibrium many-body dynamics and the emergence of long-range order. Conceptually, the emergence of such order involves two steps: the formation of local coherence, on a system-specific microscopic lengthscale, and the spreading of coherence, over lengthscales much larger than any microscopic scale. The latter is understood in terms of universal phase-ordering kinetics, or coarsening, characterized by an algebraic growth of the coherence length. Here, for a homogeneous Bose gas with tunable interactions, we show that the former also has a universal description, within the framework of weak wave turbulence (WWT). Specifically, the initial transport of particles to low momenta corresponds to an inverse turbulent cascade that is, in agreement with the WWT theory, characterized by a power-law momentum distribution, with exponent $γ= 2.4(1)$, and transport times ${\propto} (na)^{-2}$, where $n$ is the gas density and $a$ the $s$-wave scattering length.

Quantum resource redistribution drives spectral splits in dense neutrino gases

Authors: Michael Hite, Pooja Siwach

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the emergence of spectral splits in dense neutrino gases is driven by a specific redistribution of entanglement and non-local magic. By mapping these dynamics in a quantum resource phase space, the authors show that splits occur at the intersection of maximal entanglement and minimal non-local complexity. These findings provide a roadmap for optimizing both classical tensor network and future quantum circuit simulations of neutrino environments.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a quantitative link between abstract quantum information measures (like magic and entanglement) and observable astrophysical phenomena, offering insights into how complexity landscapes dictate the efficiency of many-body simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how the redistribution of quantum resources, specifically entanglement and non-local magic, drives the emergence of spectral splits in dense neutrino gases.

Main result. Spectral splits arise from a structured redistribution of quantum resources where bipartite entanglement is maximized and non-local magic is locally minimized, rather than from generic complexity growth.

Method. The authors use tensor network simulations, specifically Matrix Product States (MPS) with the Time-Dependent Variational Principle (TDVP) and Global Subspace Expansion (GSE).

Model / system. A dense, two-flavor N-neutrino system modeled by an all-to-all spin Hamiltonian in the mass basis, representing neutrino-neutrino forward scattering in core-collapse supernovae.

Key observables. Entanglement entropy, non-local magic (non-stabilizerness), MPS bond dimension, spectral splits (flavor swaps), and antiflatness.

Important parameters / regimes. Neutrino-neutrino interaction strength (mu), vacuum oscillation frequency (omega), system size (N=8, 12, 14), and initial flavor configurations.

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on the two-flavor sector and utilizes bond dimension truncation as a proxy for computational complexity.

Figures summary. Figures show the dynamics of entanglement and magic, trajectories in the entanglement-magic phase space, the relationship between resource metrics and bond dimension, and asymptotic values of flavor survival probability across different system sizes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of resource organization, describes the neutrino Hamiltonian and tensor network methods, presents results on resource redistribution and phase space dynamics, and concludes with implications for classical and quantum simulations.

Abstract

Whether a quantum many-body system can be efficiently simulated hinges not only on its size but also on how quantum resources are organized within it. We characterize the quantum resource landscape of collective neutrino oscillations using entanglement entropy, non-local magic, and matrix product state bond dimension. Using tensor network simulations of neutrinos in the two-flavor sector, we demonstrate that spectral splits--sharp energy-dependent flavor swaps--emerge precisely where entanglement entropy is maximized and non-local magic is minimized locally. This anticorrelation reveals that spectral splits arise not from generic resource growth but from a structured redistribution among flavor modes. The resource dynamics trace constrained arcs in the entanglement-magic phase space, bounded by entanglement spectrum normalization. These findings establish a direct, quantitative link between quantum resources governing computational complexity and astrophysical observables, informing the design of tensor network and quantum circuit simulations of dense neutrino environments.

A Compilation Framework for Quantum Simulation of Non-unitary Dynamics

Authors: Qifan Huang, Minbo Gao, Li Zhou, Mingsheng Ying
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23358v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper introduces a new compilation framework that treats quantum channels as primary objects rather than decomposing them into unitaries prematurely. By using a specialized intermediate representation, it enables algebraic optimizations that drastically reduce the gate and ancilla overhead for simulating open quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a specialized compilation infrastructure for simulating dissipative and decohering processes, which is essential for modeling realistic many-body dynamics and open quantum systems on near-term and fault-tolerant hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing quantum compilers are optimized for unitary (closed-system) circuits, making them inefficient for simulating open-system dynamics where non-unitary quantum channels are the natural objects.

Main result. The proposed ChannelIR framework achieves up to a 99% reduction in gate count and significantly improves compilation scalability compared to traditional circuit-first Stinespring dilation methods.

Method. A channel-first compilation pipeline using a hierarchical intermediate representation (ChannelIR) that supports Kraus form and Pauli-sum structures, combined with algebraic rewriting and structure-aware circuit synthesis (LCU and channel-LCU).

Model / system. The framework models open quantum systems using Lindbladian master equations, representing continuous-time dynamics through Kraus operator-sum representations and short-time channel approximations.

Key observables. Gate count (CX, T, etc.), circuit depth, qubit count (width), and trace distance error.

Important parameters / regimes. Markovian dynamics, Lindbladian generators, and simulation error tolerances.

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics are assumed to be Markovian, and the framework focuses on the high cost of non-Clifford (T) gates in fault-tolerant computing.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the ChannelIR Kraus form, circuit optimization for block-encoding, the complete compilation workflow, the syntax of the IR, and performance comparisons of gate counts and simulation errors against theoretical bounds.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of abstraction mismatch, presents the ChannelIR framework and its algebraic rewrite rules, details the LindFront frontend and circuit synthesis backend, provides formal proofs for rule correctness and optimization complexity, and concludes with benchmarks on Lindbladian simulation and scalability.

Abstract

Most quantum compilers assume programs are reversible unitary circuits. This fits closed-system algorithms, but not open-system simulation, where the natural program objects are quantum channels describing non-unitary dynamics. We present a channel-first compilation framework that treats channels as first-class compilation objects. Our core IR, ChannelIR, represents channels explicitly in Kraus form, a standard channel representation, with Pauli-sum structure, enabling algebraic rewrites before circuit synthesis. We instantiate the framework with LindFront, a frontend that lowers continuous-time Lindbladian generators to short-time channels, and a backend that compiles these channels to executable circuits with structure-aware optimizations. On Lindbladian and channel-simulation benchmarks, the optimized pipeline reduces gate count by up to 99% over an unoptimized channel-first baseline and scales better than circuit-first Stinespring compilation.

Summary. This paper establishes a new analytical bound that quantifies the precision loss in multiparameter quantum estimation caused by measurement incompatibility. It also introduces a systematic way to design optimal measurements that reach these limits, demonstrated through a refined uncertainty relation for quantum radar.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental tool for quantum metrology and sensing, specifically addressing how to optimize simultaneous measurements in the presence of non-commuting observables, which is critical for quantum-enhanced sensing technologies.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The fundamental difficulty in simultaneous multiparameter quantum estimation due to the incompatibility of optimal measurements, which creates a precision gap between achievable and ultimate quantum limits.

Main result. The derivation of a new tight analytical bound for the precision trade-off in pure state estimation and a systematic methodology for designing optimal measurements that saturate this bound.

Method. A geometric approach using the complex geometric tensor (combining QFI and Berry curvature) and an optimization strategy that models POVMs as projective measurements on an augmented system-ancilla state.

Model / system. The framework is applied to pure quantum states and specifically demonstrated using a quantum radar model involving entangled biphotons used for simultaneous range and velocity estimation.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information matrix (F_Q), imaginary part of the geometric tensor (F_Im), and the precision metric Gamma.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of parameters (n), entanglement parameter (kappa), and the eigenvalues (lambda_q) of the normalized incompatibility matrix.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary focus is on pure quantum states and the assumption that parameters are locally identifiable (invertible QFI).

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a geometric visualization of measurement incompatibility; Figure 2 shows a schematic of a quantum radar setup; Figure 3 plots uncertainty relations for different entanglement levels.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of measurement incompatibility, presents a new analytical bound for precision trade-offs, provides a constructive method for optimal measurement design, and validates the theory through a quantum radar application.

Abstract

A fundamental challenge in multiparameter quantum estimation arises from the incompatibility of optimal measurements for different parameters, leading to intricate precision trade-offs that obscure the understanding of ultimate quantum limits. Here, we present an approach that precisely quantifies these trade-offs for an arbitrary number of parameters encoded in pure quantum states. Our approach not only derives tight analytical bounds for the trade-offs induced by measurement incompatibility but also provides a systematic methodology to design optimal measurement strategies that saturate these limits. To demonstrate the practical significance of our findings, we apply our framework to quantum radar and obtain a refined Arthurs-Kelly relation that characterizes the ultimate performance for the simultaneous estimation of range and velocity with any given amount of entanglement. This showcases the transformative potential of our findings for a wide range of applications in quantum metrology, sensing, and beyond.

Twirled Perfect Tensor Networks: Computationally covariant holographic tensor networks

Authors: Gurbir Arora, Matthew Headrick, Albion Lawrence, Martin Sasieta, Brian Swingle, Connor Wolfe
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23670v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper identifies that standard random tensor networks fail to accurately model the complexity predicted by the Python's Lunch Conjecture due to a lack of computational covariance. To fix this, the authors introduce 'twirled perfect tensor networks' which successfully recover the predicted complexity bounds and maintain holographic properties. This provides a more robust framework for studying the computational complexity of black hole interiors.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the discrepancy between the Python's Lunch Complexity (PLC) prediction and the complexity of generic tensor network models, specifically addressing the lack of computational covariance in standard random tensor networks.

Main result. The authors propose a new class of 'twirled perfect tensor networks' that satisfy computational covariance, thereby aligning the complexity of the network with the PLC prediction and obeying a lattice Ryu-Takayanagi formula.

Method. The study uses geometric analysis (min-max theory, Mean Curvature Flow), random matrix theory (Wishart matrix analysis, Marchenko-Pastur law), and complexity quantification via circuit complexity and isometry decomposition.

Model / system. The paper focuses on holographic tensor network models (including Random Tensor Networks, MERA, and MPS) representing the 'Python's Lunch' geometry in AdS/CFT, characterized by a bulge and a constriction in the entanglement wedge.

Key observables. Python's Lunch Complexity (PLC), entanglement entropy (von Neumann and generalized), circuit complexity, and the Morse index of extremal surfaces.

Important parameters / regimes. Large N limit, large bond dimension limit, Markov length, and the ratio of dimension between input and output ancilla qubits.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis primarily focuses on time-symmetric settings and the regime where the area term dominates the generalized entropy; it also assumes a compact and connected manifold for geometric proofs.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Python's Lunch geometry with minimal, bulge, and outermost surfaces; Figure 2 illustrates the tensor network model and the map as a postselected unitary; Figure 3 shows the foliation and cuts; Figure 18 compares different length scales.

Paper structure. The paper begins by identifying the failure of random tensor networks to match PLC predictions, introduces the concept of computational covariance, proposes the twirled perfect tensor network model, proves its properties using geometric and statistical tools, and concludes with discussions on limitations and implications for holography.

Abstract

We define a novel class of tensor networks motivated by the Python's Lunch Conjecture (PLC) in local tensor network models of the black hole interior. We start from the observation that, for extended black brane states with short-range correlations, the PLC predicts a complexity that is smaller than the upper bound for generic short-range correlated states. We argue that the PLC makes implicit assumptions about the fine structure of the relevant tensor networks modeling gravity that render them non-generic. We demonstrate this explicitly in random tensor network models of the python's lunch, where the exponential complexity is not generally controlled by the PLC exponent. We trace the difference with the PLC to a lack of "computational covariance" in random tensor networks: while the PLC is motivated by an ability to arbitrarily decompose space into low-complexity units provided certain basic rules are followed, we show that random tensor networks do not generically have this property. We propose another class of tensor networks built from what we call "twirled perfect tensors" that do satisfy the computational covariance property and have a complexity bounded by the PLC value. We still find a discrete limitation from local postselection that appears to be absent in gravity. Moreover, we show that this class of tensor networks combines desirable holographic features of perfect tensor networks and random tensor networks, for example, it obeys a lattice Ryu-Takayanagi formula for arbitrary boundary subregions. Though motivated by holography, these tensor networks provide a flexible framework with potential applications beyond quantum gravity.

Kondo singlet from ferromagnetic coupling: an analog of Anderson-Morel superconductivity in the magnetic channel

Summary. This paper shows that purely ferromagnetic exchange can lead to a Kondo singlet ground state if the coupling is energy-dependent. This mechanism is an analog of Anderson-Morel superconductivity and can lead to a heavy Fermi liquid state that is preferred over magnetic ordering.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether a Kondo spin-singlet ground state can emerge from purely ferromagnetic (FM) spin-exchange coupling between a magnetic impurity and a conduction electron sea.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that an energy-dependent FM coupling can renormalize to an antiferromagnetic (AFM) coupling at low temperatures, leading to a Kondo singlet and a heavy Fermi liquid state in a lattice, effectively creating an 'inverted' Doniach phase diagram.

Method. The study employs perturbative Renormalization Group (RG) to analyze coupling flow and a specialized two-mode Numerical Renormalization Group (NRG) to study the strong-coupling regime.

Model / system. A Kondo model featuring a magnetic impurity coupled to a conduction sea via a non-separable, energy-dependent, and fully SU(2)-symmetric ferromagnetic exchange interaction $J_{kq}$. The model also considers a Kondo lattice of such impurities.

Key observables. Impurity entropy contribution, fixed-point spectrum, hybridization order parameter, and Kondo temperature ($T_K$).

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange couplings $J_1$ and $J_2$, energy window width $W$, bandwidth $D$, and Fermi energy $\epsilon_F$.

Assumptions / limitations. The interaction strength is reduced within a specific energy window $W$ around the Fermi level; the conduction band is treated as two distinct channels (one gapless, one gapped).

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows electron scattering and the structure of $J_{kq}$ and impurity entropy; Fig 2 shows RG trajectories; Fig 3 illustrates the hybridization fields and real-space hybridization dip; Fig 4 shows the modified Wilson chain; Fig 5 plots the Kondo temperature scaling.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the FM coupling problem, presents the energy-dependent Hamiltonian, details the RG and 2-mode NRG methodology, analyzes the single-impurity singlet formation, extends the results to the Kondo lattice (heavy Fermi liquid), and concludes with experimental realization routes.

Abstract

We consider magnetic impurities coupled to a conduction sea via a fully isotropic ferromagnetic spin-exchange term, the strength of which depends on the conduction-electron modes involved in the scattering. In the single-impurity case we show both analytically and numerically that there exists a parameter regime in which the conventional Kondo effect develops at low temperatures, leading to a singlet ground state. In the case of a lattice of impurities, we show that this leads to a heavy Fermi liquid state that is energetically favored over magnetic ordering in a broad parameter range. We argue that these effects are analogs of Anderson-Morel superconductivity, and discuss routes to their experimental realization.

Summary. This paper provides an analytical solution for the entanglement between two disjoint intervals in a 1+1D massless scalar field. It identifies 'negativity cores,' which are the specific localized modes that constitute the entanglement. The results bridge the gap between local field theory and the geometric structure of quantum correlations.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides fundamental insights into the spatial structure of vacuum entanglement and the identification of specific field modes that carry correlations, which is highly relevant for understanding entanglement in continuous variable systems and quantum field theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to provide a complete characterization of the entanglement structure between two bounded, spacelike-separated regions in a (1+1)-dimensional free massless real scalar field, specifically identifying the localized modes that carry the entanglement.

Main result. The authors derive closed-form analytical solutions for the logarithmic negativity and identify the 'negativity cores,' which are the specific localized field modes responsible for the entanglement.

Method. The study employs Gaussian state methods and a framework based on the Kähler structure of Gaussian states, reducing the problem to a boundary value problem in the complex plane involving the diagonalization of an integral operator.

Model / system. A (1+1)-dimensional free massless real scalar quantum field in its vacuum state, where the system consists of two disjoint, bounded intervals on the real axis.

Key observables. Logarithmic negativity (En) and negativity cores (localized smearing functions/modes).

Important parameters / regimes. The cross ratio (eta) characterizing the geometry, the ratio of separation to interval length (d/l), and the large separation (eta -> 0) and small separation (eta -> 1) regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. Observers are assumed to couple only to the derivatives of the field rather than the field amplitude, and the field is assumed to be in its vacuum state.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the spatial localization of the negativity cores by displaying the real part of the smearing functions for the first three modes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of vacuum entanglement, defines the (1+1)D scalar field model, presents the analytical derivation of the negativity and its mode decomposition using Gaussian state methods, validates the results with a lattice-based numerical model, and discusses asymptotic scaling regimes.

Abstract

Vacuum entanglement is a fundamental feature of quantum field theory exhibiting rich structure that is not completely understood. Here, we provide a complete characterization of the entanglement between two bounded spacelike-separated regions in a (1+1)-dimensional free massless real scalar field. Employing Gaussian state methods, we analytically compute the logarithmic negativity and construct closed-form solutions for the localized modes carrying it, called negativity cores. These results deepen our understanding of quantum fields and suggest extensions to higher dimensions and fermionic fields.

Abstract

Aharonov-Bohm (AB) caging is the complete wavefunction localization effect in translational-invariant lattices induced by destructive phase interference. Summary. This paper introduces a quantum ghost spectroscopy technique using entangled photons to overcome the Fourier uncertainty limit in ultrafast spectroscopy. Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a quantum-enhanced spectroscopic technique that bypasses fundamental classical limits (Fourier uncertainty) to resolve ultrafast nonadiabatic dynamics, which is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems.

Main problem. Conventional ultrafast spectroscopy is limited by the Fourier uncertainty principle, creating a trade-off between temporal and spectral resolution that obscures hidden electronic and vibrational coherence dynamics.

Main result. Time-resolved quantum ghost spectroscopy (tr-QGS) using entangled photons enables independent control of time and energy scales, successfully revealing previously hidden 0.7 eV electronic coherence and the subsequent transfer to vibrational coherence.

Abstract

Ultrafast spectroscopy of molecular systems is fundamentally constrained by the Fourier uncertainty principle: high temporal resolution smears out electronic state signatures, while high spectral resolution obscures dynamic information. Here we overcome this limitation using time-resolved quantum ghost spectroscopy (tr-QGS) with entangled photon pairs, which enables independent control of temporal and spectral scales. We apply this approach to perylene bismide (PBI-1) trimers for energy transfer,by combining a quantum description of light-molecule interaction with time-dependent density matrix renormalization group (TD-DMRG) simulations. This explicitly includes five vibrational modes and nonadiabatic coupling between electronic states. Our simulations reveal that tr-QGS uniquely captures electronic coherence oscillating at 0.7 eV for >50 fs, a signature of nonadiabatic coupling that was obscured in conventional time-resolved fluorescence due to Fourier-limited broadening. Moreover, we observe a direct transfer from electronic to vibrational coherence at 200 fs, providing real-time visualization of vibronic relaxation pathways. The entangled photon correlation enables a sensitivity below the shot-noise limit and suppresses photobleaching artifacts that plague classical measurements. These results establish tr-QGS as a transformative tool for interrogating nonadiabatic dynamics in molecular aggregates, light-harvesting complexes, and photocatalysts, offering a route to reveal quantum coherence in chemistry with unprecedented time-energy precision.

Using this equivalent model, we derive the post maximal-ratio combining (MRC) SINR and obtain tractable expressions for the conditional and spatially averaged coverage probabilities. The analytical results show that RAQRs outperform conventional receivers in sparse deployments. However, when the base station (BS) density becomes large, nonlinear distortion reduces this advantage and may make RAQRs perform worse. Simulation results validate the analytical expressions and confirm that the central design tradeoff is between linear gain and cubic nonlinearity.

Main problem. The lack of a quantitative theoretical framework to connect realistic waveguide parameters to measurable non-classical photon statistics in hybrid light-matter integrated circuits.

Main result. The development of a comprehensive framework (qPIC) that predicts quantum signatures like photon antibunching and demonstrates that slow-light engineering can amplify nonlinearities to achieve non-Gaussian statistics.

Abstract

Strong light-matter coupling between a guided electromagnetic mode and an excitonic semiconductor transition gives rise to exciton-polaritons with optical nonlinearities far exceeding those of conventional photonic platforms. Utilizing these nonlinearities in the few-particle regime, where quantum signatures such as photon antibunching, sub-Poissonian statistics and non-trivial inter-mode correlations become accessible, is a central goal of integrated quantum photonics. Yet, a quantitative theoretical framework connecting realistic waveguide parameters to measurable non-classical photonic output is absent. Here, we present a comprehensive framework for predicting and benchmarking quantum photon statistics in polaritonic integrated circuits, using state-of-the-art experimentally achieved device parameters for (Al)GaAs waveguide platforms. By mapping the pulsed nonlinear waveguide dynamics onto a bosonic quantum circuit representation that explicitly incorporates dissipation, we identify experimentally accessible quantum signatures across two circuit configurations: a single waveguide in a free-space interferometric configuration and a fully integrated multimode coupled-waveguide circuit. We further show that slow-light engineering of the polariton dispersion offers a practical route to amplifying the effective nonlinearity, pushing quantum signatures beyond Gaussian statistics.
