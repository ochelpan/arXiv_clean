Order-Disorder Tricriticality in $\mathrm{A}_n \mathrm{B}_n$ Star Polymer Melts

Authors: Minhoon Kim, Wonjun Kang, Daeseong Yong, Junhan Cho, Jaeup U. Kim
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23758v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the architecture of a star polymer, specifically the number of arms, can act as a thermodynamic tuning parameter to induce tricriticality. By finding a specific arm number where the order-disorder transition changes from continuous to first-order, the study provides a rare analytically tractable example of architecture-induced phase transitions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the nature of the order-disorder transition (ODT) in symmetric star polymer melts and identifies the mechanism that drives the transition from second-order to first-order.

Main result. The authors analytically identify a tricritical arm number, n_tc approximately 5.4475, where the transition character changes, and show that the transition is driven by inter-arm correlations at the common junction.

Method. The researchers use a sixth-order free-energy expansion within the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) and validate the results using Self-Consistent Field Theory (SCFT) and Langevin Field Theory simulations.

Model / system. The system consists of incompressible melts of symmetric A_nB_n star polymers, where each star has n arms of type A and n arms of type B connected to a common junction, modeled using a Gaussian chain model.

Key observables. Order parameter (density modulation amplitude), phase boundary shift, and the quartic and sextic Landau coefficients.

Important parameters / regimes. Arm number (n), Flory-Huggins interaction parameter (chi*N), and composition fraction (f).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes incompressible melts and uses a mean-field framework (RPA and SCFT) within a Gaussian chain approximation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows free-energy curves illustrating continuous vs. discontinuous transitions; Figure 2 shows the vanishing and sign change of the quartic coefficient at the tricritical point.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of transition character, develops a sixth-order RPA expansion, provides analytical predictions for the tricritical point and phase boundaries, validates these with SCFT, and discusses the physical origin via inter-arm correlations.

Abstract

Tricriticality usually requires tuning an additional thermodynamic parameter. Here we show that, in symmetric $\mathrm{A}_n\mathrm{B}_n$ star-polymer melts, the arm number $n$ itself plays this role and drives the order--disorder transition (ODT) from second order to first order. By developing a sixth-order free-energy expansion within the random phase approximation and comparing it with self-consistent field theory (SCFT) calculations, we analytically identify a tricritical arm number, $n_{\mathrm{tc}}\approx 5.4475$. For $nn_{\mathrm{tc}}$, the transition becomes first order, and $(χN)_{\mathrm{ODT}}$ shifts below $(χN)_{\mathrm{s}}$ with a quadratic dependence near the tricritical point. SCFT calculations confirm the predicted transition character and phase-boundary shift. The origin of this behavior is traced to inter-arm correlations generated by the common junction. We further show that the noninteger tricritical arm number can be effectively realized in binary mixtures of star polymers. This provides a rare analytically tractable example of architecture-induced tricriticality in a microphase-separating polymer system.

Summary. This paper maps the vortex phase diagram of amorphous Re6Zr thin films, revealing that the 'inverse melting' transition is highly sensitive to film thickness. It shows that intermediate thicknesses allow for a re-entrant phase where increasing temperature induces order, while extreme thicknesses favor either a permanent liquid or a stable solid state.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the vortex state phase diagram in amorphous Re6Zr thin films, specifically focusing on the phenomenon of 'inverse melting' where a vortex liquid transitions into a crystalline solid as temperature increases.

Main result. The researchers demonstrate that the inverse melting transition is thickness-dependent: 5 nm films remain in a disordered liquid state, 20 nm films exhibit re-entrant liquid-solid-liquid behavior, and 50 nm films maintain a stable crystalline solid.

Method. The study employs Scanning Tunneling Spectroscopy (STS) for real-space imaging, magnetotransport measurements (R-T and I-V characteristics), and two-coil mutual inductance for AC magnetic screening.

Model / system. The physical system consists of amorphous Re6Zr (a-ReZr) thin films with varying thicknesses (5 nm, 20 nm, and 50 nm) in the Type II superconducting regime, where vortex dynamics are influenced by the competition between pinning, thermal fluctuations, and vortex-vortex interactions.

Key observables. Differential conductance maps (dI/dV), orientational order parameter (psi_6), 2D Fourier Transforms (2DFT), critical current density (Jc), resistance (R), and complex magnetic susceptibility (chi' and chi'').

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic field (H), temperature (T), film thickness (5, 20, 50 nm), upper critical field (Hc2), and Maki parameter (alpha).

Assumptions / limitations. The transition is assumed to be broadened by quenched random disorder rather than being a sharp first-order transition; the Gittleman-Rosenbaum model is used to treat vortex motion as an overdamped harmonic oscillator.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows STS conductance maps and 2D-FFTs for different thicknesses; Figure 2 shows temperature evolution of vortex configurations; Figure 5/6 shows I-V characteristics, Jc dependence, and resistance peaks; Figure 7 shows AC magnetic screening response (chi' and chi'').

Paper structure. The paper introduces the inverse melting phenomenon, describes the experimental setup and material preparation, presents thickness-dependent imaging and transport results, analyzes the phase boundaries and magnetic response, and concludes with a discussion on the interplay of dimensionality and disorder.

Abstract

In Type II superconductors, the vortex lattice can exhibit "inverse melting," transitioning from a liquid to a crystalline solid as temperature increases. While recently observed via scanning tunneling microscopy in a 20 nm thick amorphous Re6Zr thin film, this work investigates the corresponding d.c. transport and low-frequency magnetic screening responses. By identifying distinct signatures of these transitions and integrating scanning tunneling spectroscopy imaging, we construct a comprehensive vortex-state phase diagram in the magnetic field-temperature parameter space. Furthermore, we demonstrate that inverse melting is thickness-dependent: a 5 nm film retains an inhomogeneous liquid state, while a 50 nm film maintains a crystalline solid structure except near the upper critical field.

Pulsed thermal annealing enables switching of chiral antiferromagnetic order with a sub-millitesla field in Mn$_3$Sn

Authors: Xiaokang Li, Jing Zhang, Xiaodong Guo, Zengwei Zhu
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23205v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that heating Mn3Sn above its Neel temperature allows for the switching of its chiral antiferromagnetic order using extremely weak magnetic fields. This 'thermal softening' approach significantly reduces the energy barrier for magnetic manipulation. The findings suggest that thermal effects should be integrated with spin-torque mechanisms for efficient future spintronic device design.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of reliably switching the chiral antiferromagnetic order in the Weyl antiferromagnet Mn3Sn using minimal external magnetic fields.

Main result. By using pulsed thermal annealing to heat the material above its Neel temperature, the magnetic anisotropy barrier is removed (thermal softening), allowing switching with sub-millitesla fields.

Method. The researchers used pulsed thermal annealing on Mn3Sn single crystals on a resistive heater chip, monitoring the Anomalous Hall Effect (AHE) during heating and cooling in small magnetic fields.

Model / system. The physical system is the Weyl antiferromagnet Mn3Sn, which possesses a non-collinear 120-degree spin structure on a kagome lattice and a cluster magnetic octupole order.

Key observables. Anomalous Hall Effect (AHE) signal, Hall resistivity, and the threshold magnetic field (B0) required for switching.

Important parameters / regimes. Neel temperature (TN approx 425 K), magnetic field scales as low as 0.1 mT, and pulse duration/temperature rise in nanoscale devices.

Assumptions / limitations. The switching mechanism assumes that crossing the Neel temperature is necessary to erase the magnetic anisotropy barrier.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup, AHE hysteresis, and the decrease of the threshold field near TN; Figure 2 shows the time evolution of the AHE signal during annealing and the switching ratio vs. cooling field; Figure 3 compares annealing results above and below TN.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of AFM switching, describes the experimental setup for thermal annealing, presents the temperature dependence of the switching field, demonstrates the necessity of crossing TN, and provides a thermal model for device-scale applications.

Abstract

The manipulation of antiferromagnetic (AFM) order is a central theme in modern spintronics. In this work, we achieve reliable switching of the chiral AFM state in the Weyl antiferromagnet Mn$_3$Sn using a heat pulse combined with a very small magnetic field as small as 0.1 mT. By systematically measuring the anomalous Hall effect (AHE) in high-quality single crystals, we show that the field needed for switching decreases as the temperature approaches the Néel temperature $T_N$, and vanishes at $T_N$. Pulsed thermal annealing above $T_N$ followed by cooling in a tiny external field enables full and reproducible switching of the magnetic octupole order. Our results show that thermal softening (heating above $T_N$ to temporarily remove the magnetic anisotropy) is a key step that lowers the energy barrier to nearly zero. This allows an extremely weak directional field (like the effective field from spin-orbit torque in thin-film devices) to set the final magnetic state during cooling. We also provide a simple model to estimate the temperature rise in nanoscale devices under current pulses, giving practical guidance for device design. This work highlights that thermal effects are not a side issue but an important partner to spin torques, and suggests that future work should take both into account.

Reinforcement Learning for Microcanonical Graph Ensemble with Assortativity Constraints

Authors: Hoyun Choi, Junghyo Jo, Deok-Sun Lee
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23285v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a deep reinforcement learning approach to generate random graphs with exact structural constraints. By treating graph rewiring as a policy-guided search, the method significantly outperforms traditional statistical mechanics-based sampling in both speed and precision. This allows for the creation of precise null models to study how specific network properties drive complex system functions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of efficiently generating microcanonical graph ensembles that satisfy hard structural constraints, such as a specific target assortativity, without the computational burden of parameter tuning or structural fluctuations.

Main result. The introduction of the Deep Microcanonical Graph Generator (DMGG), an RL framework that achieves target assortativity with much higher precision and an order of magnitude faster than traditional ERGM methods while preserving configurational diversity.

Method. A Reinforcement Learning framework using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and a Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) to perform directed, degree-preserving edge rewirings.

Model / system. Random graphs and complex networks with varying topologies (e.g., ER, BA, WS, SBM) characterized by a fixed degree sequence and a target assortativity coefficient.

Key observables. Assortativity (rho), clustering coefficient (C), dyad-independent entropy (SDI), and the joint-degree matrix (J).

Important parameters / regimes. Target assortativity (rho*), precision tolerance (epsilon), and the number of rewiring steps (T).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes the ability to perform degree-preserving rewirings and focuses on the constraint of assortativity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares ERGM and DMGG dynamics; Figure 2 shows convergence trajectories and scaling of precision; Figure 3 evaluates ensemble diversity and rewiring cost; Figure 4 shows rewiring steps as a function of target rho and structural evolution; Figure 5 visualizes the joint-degree matrix and flux changes.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the limitations of canonical ensembles, introduces the DMGG RL framework, details the neural architecture and training process, benchmarks the model against ERGM in terms of speed and precision, and analyzes the scaling and structural implications of the generated ensembles.

Abstract

How network structure determines function is a fundamental question, and it can be investigated by graph ensembles with precisely controlled structural properties. Canonical approaches, formulated as exponential random graph models (ERGMs), enforce constraints only in expectation, allowing individual realizations to fluctuate around the target. Conversely, microcanonical ensembles impose hard constraints exactly, but practical sampling methods beyond fixing the degree sequence have remained out of reach. Here we introduce the Deep Microcanonical Graph Generator (DMGG), a reinforcement learning (RL) framework that transforms any given graph through degree-preserving rewirings to exactly reach a prescribed assortativity, which characterizes the degree--degree correlation of adjacent nodes. Instead of relying on the entropically dominated Metropolis--Hastings dynamics of the ERGM, DMGG employs a policy-guided search that maximally alters the joint-degree matrix. This eliminates exhaustive parameter tuning and accelerates generation by at least an order of magnitude while preserving configurational diversity. As DMGG generalizes across various graph sizes, sparsities, and topologies, it provides exact null models that allow for the quantitative isolation of secondary observables, such as the clustering coefficient. These results establish RL as a practical and powerful paradigm for generating hard-constrained graphs, opening avenues to investigate structure-function relationships free from ensemble artifacts.

SAFE ma-QAOA: Surrogate-Assisted and Fine-Tuning Enhanced Multi-Angle QAOA with Parameter Distillation

Authors: Hyunwoo Kim, Youngseok Lee
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23377v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces the SAFE framework to optimize Multi-Angle QAOA by using a classical surrogate to pre-train parameters and a distillation step to prune unnecessary angles. This approach significantly reduces the quantum computational resources required for fine-tuning without sacrificing the quality of the final solution.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The high-dimensional parameter space of Multi-Angle QAOA (ma-QAOA) makes training computationally expensive and difficult due to the large number of required exact energy and gradient evaluations on quantum hardware.

Main result. The SAFE framework achieves a 64.3% reduction in active parameters and a 94.5% reduction in estimated QPU workload while maintaining high approximation ratios and accelerating convergence.

Method. A three-stage pipeline: (1) LWPP pre-training using a truncated classical surrogate, (2) parameter distillation to remove near-zero angles, and (3) exact fine-tuning using the full energy objective.

Model / system. The study evaluates ma-QAOA on Ising-type Hamiltonians, specifically the Sherrington-Kirkpatrick model, 2D square-lattice spin glasses, and Max-Cut problems on random graphs.

Key observables. Approximation ratio, first-hit step (convergence speed), and estimated QPU workload (ballpark cost).

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (p), number of qubits (n), LWPP truncation weight (w_max), and parameter distillation threshold.

Assumptions / limitations. The effectiveness relies on the assumption that many rotation angles remain near zero (identity) after the surrogate pre-training stage.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the LWPP truncation process; Figure 2 visualizes the problem instances; Figure 3/4 shows approximation ratios across different settings; Figure 5 compares convergence speed; Figure 6 shows exact fine-tuning trajectories; Figure 7 compares energy gaps and cosine similarity.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the ma-QAOA bottleneck, presents the three-stage SAFE framework (pre-training, distillation, fine-tuning), evaluates the method on various spin glass and combinatorial models, and analyzes the efficiency gains in terms of parameter count and hardware workload.

Abstract

The multi-angle Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (ma-QAOA) extends the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) by assigning a larger number of independent variational parameters, thereby increasing expressivity and improving performance at low circuit depths. However, this larger parameterization makes training more difficult and requires repeated circuit evaluations for gradient-based optimization. In this work, we propose the Surrogate-Assisted and Fine-tuning Enhanced (SAFE) framework. SAFE first uses Low-Weight Pauli Propagation (LWPP) as a classical surrogate for pre-training ma-QAOA parameters before exact optimization. SAFE then applies parameter distillation, which removes angles that remain near zero after surrogate pre-training. Finally, SAFE performs exact fine-tuning by optimizing the remaining active parameters using the exact energy objective. We evaluate SAFE on instances of the Sherrington-Kirkpatrick model, two-dimensional square-lattice spin glass, and Max-Cut. SAFE with distillation provides the strongest overall results relative to exact-only: (i) a 64.3 percent reduction in active parameter count and (ii) a 94.5 percent reduction in estimated QPU workload. Within the SAFE workflow, adding distillation further reduces the optimizer steps to the near-optimal regime by 44.4 percent relative to without distillation. These results provide evidence that SAFE ma-QAOA can accelerate convergence to high-quality solutions while reducing the required quantum resources for exact fine-tuning, offering a resource-efficient route toward expressive ma-QAOA on NISQ hardware.

Tuning J$_1$-J$_2$ in Quasi-2D Triangular Lattice Antiferromagnet $α$-SrCr$_2$O$_4$ via Uniaxial Pressure

Authors: Jazzmin Victorin, Shreenanda Ghosh, Seyed Koohpayeh, Chris Lygouras, Natalia Drichko
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.23133v1

Summary. This paper explores how applying uniaxial pressure can tune the magnetic frustration and anisotropy in the triangular lattice antiferromagnet alpha-SrCr2O4. By using Raman spectroscopy, the authors show that mechanical strain can shift magnetic excitation energies, providing a method to control the magnetic ground state. This work is significant for understanding how lattice distortions influence quantum magnetic phases.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the ability to tune the magnetic anisotropy and exchange interaction ratio (J2/J1) in a frustrated triangular lattice antiferromagnet using in-situ uniaxial pressure.

Main result. The study demonstrates that compressive pressure reduces magnetic anisotropy, while tensile pressure increases it, effectively tuning the frustration in the system. The researchers also confirmed efficient pressure transmission through shifts in Raman-active phonon frequencies.

Method. In-situ Raman spectroscopy was used under applied uniaxial strain via a piezoelectric cell, complemented by Linear Spin Wave Theory (LSWT) and two-magnon density of states calculations.

Model / system. The system is alpha-SrCr2O4, a quasi-2D S=3/2 triangular lattice Heisenberg antiferromagnet with an orthorhombic structure and incommensurate helical magnetic order below 43 K.

Key observables. Two-magnon excitations (at 15.5 meV and 40 meV), Raman-active oxygen phonon frequencies, phonon line shapes (Fano asymmetry), and Gruneisen parameters.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange anisotropy ratio (J2/J1), Neel temperature (TN = 43 K), strain range (-0.41% to +0.16%), and Gruneisen parameter (gamma = 0.45).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumed a Poisson ratio of 0.2 and that phonon width temperature dependence follows the Klemens model; used the approximation of Linear Spin Wave Theory for magnon dispersion.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Crystal structure and exchange paths; Fig 2: Schematic of strain application; Fig 3: Temperature dependence of phonons in the strain cell; Fig 4: Raman spectra under different pressures; Fig 5: Temperature-dependent Raman spectra; Fig 7: LSWT calculations for spin wave dispersion and two-magnon DOS.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the frustrated triangular lattice problem, describes the experimental setup for uniaxial pressure Raman spectroscopy, presents the results of phonon frequency shifts to verify strain, details the observation of two-magnon excitation tuning, and concludes with theoretical spin-wave modeling to interpret the magnetic anisotropy changes.

Abstract

Triangular lattice antiferromagnets first attracted attention as a frustrated magnetic lattice which can serve as a platform to realize the resonating valence bond state. While the triangular lattice itself was shown to support classical 120 degree order, many theoretical phase diagrams suggest a quantum spin liquid state within a small range of parameters. One possible avenue to achieve such a state is to tune the anisotropy of the triangular lattice antiferromagnet by applying uniaxial pressure. This motivated our Raman scattering study of quasi-two-dimensional antiferromagnet $α$-SrCr$_2$O$_4$ under applied uniaxial pressure. Under ambient conditions, $α$-SrCr$_2$O$_4$ develops long-range helical magnetic order below T$_N$ = 43 K. We identify two-magnon excitations associated with this long-range antiferromagnetic order below T$_N$ at 15.5 meV and 40 meV by comparison with spin wave calculations. We observe the two features from the two-magnon excitation shift away from (towards) each other under applied tensile (compressive) pressure, indicating a decrease (increase) in anisotropy. Raman active phonons show a shift to higher (lower) frequencies under applied compressive (tensile) pressure, indicating efficient transmission of pressure and tuning of the lattice. We show spin wave and two-magnon density of states calculations under uniaxial pressure are consistent with our experimental results.

Abstract

Abstract

Abstract

Magnetic topological semimetals provide a promising platform for emergent quantum phenomena driven by the interplay between magnetism and relativistic fermions, including anomalous transport effects and tunable topological phases. Here, we investigate the electronic structure and orbital character of EuAgAs, a magnetic topological Dirac semimetal candidate, using density functional theory (DFT) and polarization dependent angle resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES). Fermi surface mapping and constant energy contours measured at 9 eV reveal ring like features that systematically expand with increasing binding energy, consistent with nearly linear low energy Dirac like dispersion. ARPES measurements at different photon energies hint at the presence of a van Hove singularity predicted by DFT calculations. Furthermore, this indicates that the photoemission matrix elements are highly sensitive to the excitation energy, allowing different photon energies to selectively probe distinct orbital characters. Polarization dependent ARPES measurements performed in s- and p-polarized geometries exhibit pronounced variations in spectral intensity, indicating symmetry selective orbital contributions to electronic states. These matrix element driven intensity modulations are well reproduced by DFT calculations. Furthermore, the observed Dirac like states remain nearly unchanged over the temperature range from 9 K to 30 K, suggesting that the magnetic ordering has minimal influence on the electronic structure. Our combined experimental and theoretical results provide detailed insight into the orbital selective electronic structure of EuAgAs and its implications for magnetic topological quantum states.

Abstract

The interplay between disorder and vortex--vortex interactions in strongly disordered superconductors in a magnetic field can stabilize a vortex-glass state, characterized by strong pinning and the absence of positional order. Yet its role in the destruction of superconductivity at the field-driven superconductor--insulator transition has remained unresolved. Here we use plasmonic microwave spectroscopy of superconducting resonators patterned from amorphous indium oxide thin films to directly track the superfluid density up to the critical field $B_c$. We find an unexpected resilience of the superfluid density, which decreases only logarithmically over nearly three orders of magnitude in field, in stark contrast to the rapid power-law suppression expected for vortex lattices. We attribute this anomalously slow decay to a collective vortex-pinning mechanism counterintuitively enhanced by vortex--vortex interactions. The superfluid density then vanishes linearly at $B_c$, where independent magnetoresistance measurements identify a continuous quantum critical point, unlike the abrupt transition observed at zero field. We further uncover an exceptionally large nonlinear electromagnetic response of the vortex glass, manifested as a pronounced positive-Kerr effect with potential for quantum sensing. These results show how disorder controls the critical magnetic field and identify the vortex glass as the key intermediate state governing the magnetic-field-induced superconductor--insulator transition.
