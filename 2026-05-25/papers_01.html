Summary. The paper shows that interference between different sequences of local measurements can create entanglement between two qubits. While the entanglement is limited when measurement outcomes are averaged, the protocol can still produce Bell nonlocality, providing a new way to generate quantum correlations.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for open quantum systems and quantum optics as it identifies a new mechanism for generating entanglement through non-unitary, measurement-driven dynamics, extending the concept of quantum interference beyond unitary evolution.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether two different histories of quantum evolution, composed of local measurements, can interfere with each other to generate quantum correlations.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that interference between measurement-induced histories can generate entanglement and Bell nonlocality, even when the individual measurement branches are non-entangling.

Method. The study uses Kraus operators to describe measurement-induced evolution and employs a protocol involving an auxiliary control qubit to place measurement branches in a coherent superposition.

Model / system. A bipartite system of two qubits (A and B) initially in a separable state, subject to local generalized measurements controlled by an auxiliary qubit (C).

Key observables. Concurrence (to measure entanglement) and the CHSH parameter/Horodecki criterion (to measure Bell nonlocality).

Important parameters / regimes. Measurement strength (g), measurement-axis rotation angle (theta), and the postselection of the control qubit state.

Assumptions / limitations. The proof of the impossibility of maximal entanglement after averaging assumes that the resulting averaged state must be pure to reach maximal entanglement.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the protocol, illustrating how a control qubit selects between two measurement branches on qubits A and B.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of measurement-history interference, presents a protocol using a control qubit, analyzes entanglement generation in postselected and averaged regimes, provides a microscopic Hamiltonian derivation, and concludes with a mathematical proof regarding the limits of entanglement.

Abstract

The evolution of a quantum system comprises two fundamental processes--continuous unitary dynamics and stochastic measurement-induced jumps. The latter are often viewed as a source of decoherence. Can two histories of such an evolution, made up of local measurements, interfere with each other? Here, we answer this question in the affirmative. A manifestation of this interference is the generation of entanglement between two parts of the system that are individually coupled to distinct detectors. Specifically, we develop a protocol in which two alternative local measurement processes act on a pair of qubits, and show how interference of histories is generated under coherent control, leading to entanglement. Furthermore, we find that averaging over the detectors' readouts still results in an entangled (albeit not maximally entangled) state. Our results extend the notion of quantum interference beyond unitary evolution to genuinely measurement-driven dynamics, and identify limits on the generation of quantum correlations using interference of measurement histories.

Summary. This review paper explores the emergence of different types of time crystals in a Bose-Einstein Condensate coupled to an optical cavity. It demonstrates how varying the driving protocol (intensity, phase, or constant driving) can induce commensurate, incommensurate, or continuous time crystalline phases. The work highlights the utility of atom-cavity systems as powerful platforms for simulating complex dynamical phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a blueprint for using cavity-BEC systems as versatile quantum simulators for complex non-equilibrium phenomena and explores the emergence of exotic time-translation symmetry breaking in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to identify, characterize, and classify three distinct types of time crystalline states (commensurate, incommensurate, and continuous) emerging in driven-dissipative cavity-BEC systems.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that cavity-BEC systems can host various time crystals, ranging from period-doubling states induced by intensity modulation to continuous time crystals occurring under constant driving, and show that long-range order is achieved in the thermodynamic limit.

Method. The study uses semiclassical dynamics via Heisenberg-Langevin equations, numerical integration with the Heun algorithm, and quantum-classical rescaling to investigate the thermodynamic limit.

Model / system. The system consists of a Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC) inside a high-finesse optical cavity under transverse pumping, which can be mapped onto the open Dicke model using two-mode or three-mode expansions.

Key observables. Cavity mode photon correlation functions (c_ph), checkerboard density wave correlation functions (c_dw), and bond density wave correlation functions (c_bdw).

Important parameters / regimes. Pump intensity, cavity detuning, photon decay rate (kappa), and the atom number (N_a) used for scaling to the thermodynamic limit.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated in the dispersive regime where atoms occupy a single internal state, and the dynamics are approximated using a few-mode expansion (two-mode or three-mode).

Paper structure. Abstract

The understanding of light-induced dynamical states continues to be a challenging and fruitful pursuit of science. This pursuit is supported by quantum simulation of dynamical phenomena, e.g., in ultracold atom systems. Typically, ultracold atom dynamics are read out destructively, via time-of-flight imaging, limiting a detailed analysis. However, atom-cavity systems provide a real-time readout of the photonic state via photon emission from the cavity, making the system ideally suited for the simulation of dynamical phenomena. Here, we review three distinct time crystalline states, predicted and realized in a cavity-BEC system. We give an example for each of them, based on minimal few-mode models. We characterize the time crystalline states via correlation functions of the cavity mode, and characteristic momentum modes of the condensate. This supports a clear distinction between these time crystals. More generally, the sequence of studies reviewed here, serves as a blueprint for setting up minimal models and their characterization, for dynamical phenomena.

Abstract

Microscopic two-level systems (TLS) -- ubiquitous atomic-scale defects in solid-state quantum devices -- are a dominant source of qubit decoherence, yet their role is often considered local and short-memoried. Here, we report the observation of a coherent TLS that couples simultaneously to two spatially distant superconducting qubits. The TLS is identified to reside within the tunable coupler linking the qubits, enabling controllability of the TLS-qubit coupling strength via coupler frequency -- a capability absent in earlier studies. This tunability allows us to systematically probe how TLS distorts qubit dynamics, revisiting the decoherence model in the presence of non-Markovian TLS dephasing noise. This is corroborated by the reconstructed $1/f$ noise spectrum of TLS frequency fluctuation spanning more than ten orders of magnitude (0.1\,mHz -- 1\,MHz) that reveals discrete fluctuator signatures. Quantum process tomography further unveils TLS-induced correlated qubit dynamics, highlighting the long-lived TLS as an effective source of non-Markovianity. Our findings expose a previously overlooked interaction mechanism in scalable quantum architectures: defects embedded in coupling elements can simultaneously affect multiple qubits with variable impact. Beyond immediate implications for system characterization and calibration, this situation provides a powerful testbed for studying defect-driven quantum dynamics, refining error suppression strategies, and advancing architecture design for scalable quantum technologies.

Summary. This paper introduces a way to find and stabilize non-thermal phenomena like quantum scars and chiral quasiparticles within chaotic spin chains using interference-protected subspaces. By adding a specific perturbation, the authors show that leakage into the chaotic bulk can be suppressed, allowing for persistent coherent dynamics. This work connects several disparate concepts like quantum cages and many-body scars into a single unified theory.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new theoretical framework for engineering and stabilizing non-thermal, coherent dynamics (like quantum scars and topological modes) within otherwise chaotic many-body environments, which is highly relevant for controlling many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the mechanism behind weak ergodicity breaking and how certain non-thermal states, such as quantum scars and chiral quasiparticles, can maintain coherence and evade thermalization in chaotic many-body systems.

Main result. The authors identify an interference-protected subspace in a family of chaotic spin chains where non-thermal phenomena are stabilized by destructive interference and rapid oscillations, providing a unified framework for scars, quantum cages, and topological edge modes.

Method. The study employs a quantitative leakage theory based on a Dyson expansion in the interaction picture, a tight-binding mapping of the protected subspace, and numerical simulations of real-space dynamics and fidelity.

Model / system. A family of local, chaotic spin-1/2 chains based on an inversion-symmetric version of the kinetically constrained East model, specifically a perturbed Hamiltonian H_PX with a stabilization term g*H_PXP.

Key observables. Real-space dynamics, fidelity revivals, leakage rate, entanglement entropy, local expectation values, and level-spacing statistics.

Important parameters / regimes. The stabilization strength g, momentum k, modulation frequency q, and blockade range alpha.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the existence of a structured tight-binding subspace and uses a first-order Dyson expansion for the leakage theory.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows wavepacket and scarred state constructions and chiral dynamics; Figure 2 illustrates leakage suppression mechanisms; Figure 4 compares quasiparticle decay at g=0 vs. stability at g=10; Figure 5 shows fidelity revivals; Figure 6 relates to the parent Hamiltonian construction.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model and the concept of protected subspaces, develops a quantitative leakage theory, demonstrates various non-thermal phenomena (scars, chiral particles, edge modes), provides a stabilization mechanism via a perturbation term, and concludes by connecting these findings to broader frameworks like parent Hamiltonians.

Abstract

Generic quantum many-body systems are expected to thermalize, scrambling initial coherence while local observables relax to equilibrium values. Weak ergodicity breaking, often associated with quantum many-body scarring of homogeneous states, provides rare exceptions with long-lived coherence. We introduce a family of local spin-1/2 chains with a structured subspace that hosts a much broader range of nonthermal phenomena, such as scars, chirally propagating quasiparticles or approximate topological edge modes. These nonthermal phenomena happening at high energy densities can be understood via structured subspaces that are protected by destructive interference. We develop a quantitative leakage theory predicting which states retain coherence and suggest ways to improve the stability by inducing fast oscillations in the complement subspace. Our framework connects asymptotic scars, quantum cages, and parent-Hamiltonian constructions, and shows that weak ergodicity breaking in chaotic systems extends well beyond revivals of homogeneous initial states.

Abstract

Summary. The paper introduces 'Observational Entropy' as a scalable tool to detect quantum phase transitions and quantify chaos. Abstract

Coarse-grained measurements offer a scalable alternative to full state tomography for characterizing complex quantum dynamics. We show that observational entropy (OE), an information-theoretic entropy defined directly from finite-resolution measurement outcomes, provides a unified and experimentally accessible framework for quantifying chaos and probing criticality. From probing the insulator-metal crossover in the Aubry-Andre model to tracking the gradual destruction of Kolmogorov-Arnold-Moser tori in the Kicked Rotor, derivatives of OE provide an accurate and unified diagnostic of probing these transitions. In both cases, the critical points extracted from dynamical evolution and eigenstate analyses converge to the exact theoretical values once the observational resolution exceeds a finite threshold. In the chaotic limit, OE exhibits a linear behavior within the Ehrenfest time regime, and its slope defines an observable Lyapunov exponent. Using a Pretty Good Measurement correction to the Husimi phase-space distribution, this entropy-production rate quantitatively reproduces the classical Lyapunov exponent in both the standard and singular kicked rotors. Our results establish OE as a compact information-theoretic bridge between classical instability, quantum criticality, and realistic finite-resolution measurements.

Abstract

In classical thermodynamics, the Mpemba effect refers to the counterintuitive observation that hot water can freeze faster than cold water, manifesting as an anomalous crossing of dynamical trajectories. While analogues of this phenomenon have been explored in quantum radiative systems and spin-chain entanglement asymmetry, its connection to the finite-time decoupling of quantum correlations remains elusive. In this Letter, we uncover a distinct quantum Mpemba effect associated with entanglement sudden death (ESD). By analyzing two qubits interacting with local amplitude damping reservoirs, we demonstrate that a more strongly entangled initial state can experience a faster collapse into a separable state than a more weakly entangled one. We provide an exact analytical derivation of the trajectory crossover dynamics and the ESD time. Finally, we map the phase diagram of initial state parameters to delineate the regime where this anomalous entanglement Mpemba effect occurs, offering insights into the control of quantum resource lifetimes in dissipative environments.

Abstract

Many applications of Green's functions (GFs) require their evaluation over intervals or at multiple points, motivating quantum algorithms that return an efficiently computable functional representation rather than mere point estimates. We introduce a robust quantum Arnoldi method (ROQAM) that achieves this goal. Its robustness is derived from formulation in terms of orthogonal polynomials, which preserves the upper-Hessenberg structure of the projected matrices despite finite-precision estimation. We also show that as the iteration depth increases, the precision required for matrix-element estimation can be reduced. Resource estimates for the spectral function of a quantum impurity model indicate that ROQAM outperforms pointwise estimation via quantum singular value transformation by multiple orders of magnitude. Finally, we show that the ROQAM can be used to estimate GFs at nonzero temperatures using only a single Krylov subspace.
