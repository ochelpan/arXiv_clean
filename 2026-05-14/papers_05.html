Neural QAOA$^{2}$: Differentiable Joint Graph Partitioning and Parameter Initialization for Quantum Combinatorial Optimization

Authors: Zubin Zheng, Jiahao Wu, Shengcai Liu
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13072v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a machine learning-driven approach to improve the performance of QAOA for large-scale optimization. By using a differentiable neural network to jointly optimize graph partitioning and quantum circuit parameters, it overcomes the limitations of traditional heuristic methods and enables better scalability on near-term quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The scalability of the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) is limited by hardware qubit constraints and the inefficiency of heuristic graph partitioning and random parameter initialization.

Main result. The Neural QAOA2 framework outperforms heuristic baselines, ranking first on 101 out of 183 tested instances, and demonstrates zero-shot generalization across unseen graph topologies and scales.

Method. An end-to-end differentiable framework using a Generative Evaluative Network (GEN) that jointly generates graph partitions and initial circuit parameters via a differentiable quantum evaluator surrogate.

Model / system. The study focuses on combinatorial optimization problems including MaxCut, QUBO, and Ising spin glass models, represented by cost Hamiltonians constructed from Pauli-Z operators.

Key observables. Performance ratio (rho), solution quality relative to baseline, and Modularity Score.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (p), number of variables (N ranging from 21 to 1000), and qubit capacity constraints (max nodes per subgraph).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that parameters trained at depth p=1 can be effectively expanded to deeper circuits (p > 1) and relies on the accuracy of a surrogate neural network evaluator.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the misalignment between heuristic metrics and quantum performance; Figure 2 details the GEN architecture; Figure 5 compares the correlation of the evaluator vs. modularity; Figure 6 shows performance across varying qubit counts.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalability problem, proposes the Neural QAOA2 architecture (GEN, Joint Generator, and Quantum Evaluator), details the training strategy (distribution-level and instance-level adaptation), presents extensive benchmarking across various datasets and scales, and concludes with robustness and scalability analyses.

Abstract

The quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA) holds promise for combinatorial optimization but is constrained by limited qubits. While divide-and-conquer frameworks like QAOA$^{2}$ address scalability by partitioning graphs into subgraphs, existing methods suffer from two fundamental limitations: i) misalignment between heuristic partitioning metrics and quantum optimization goals, and ii) topology-blind parameter initialization that leads to optimization cold starts. To bridge these gaps, we propose Neural QAOA$^{2}$, an end-to-end differentiable framework that jointly generates graph partitions and initial parameters. By integrating a generative evaluative network (GEN), our method utilizes a differentiable quantum evaluator as a high-fidelity performance surrogate to provide direct gradient guidance, enabling the joint generator to learn the intrinsic mapping from graph topology to high-quality partition and parameter configurations. Extensive experiments on 183 QUBO, Ising, and MaxCut instances (21 to 1000 variables) demonstrate that our gradient-driven approach broadly outperforms heuristic baselines, ranking first on 101 instances. It exhibits zero-shot generalization across out-of-distribution graph topologies and scales.

PACSim: A Flexible Simulation Framework for Polymer-Attenuated Coulombic Self-Assembly

Authors: Philipp Höllmer, Nicole Smina, John P. Marquardt, Michael S. Chen, Steven van Kesteren, Stefano Sacanna, Glen M. Hocky
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12870v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The authors present PACSim, an open-source molecular dynamics framework designed to simulate the self-assembly of polymer-coated charged colloids. It allows researchers to easily model complex experimental protocols, such as dynamic changes in salt concentration, to study phase transitions and nucleation. The framework is highly extensible, supporting machine learning integration and enhanced sampling techniques.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for a flexible, open-source simulation framework to study the self-assembly, nucleation pathways, and crystal stability of Polymer-Attenuated Coulombic (PACS) colloidal systems.

Main result. The development of PACSim, a framework that successfully demonstrates how tuning parameters like Debye length can shift nucleation from classical to non-classical pathways and how crystal structures compete via Ostwald ripening.

Method. A molecular dynamics (MD) framework built on OpenMM that utilizes custom GPU-accelerated forces, integration with PLUMED for enhanced sampling, and Python-based utilities for complex configuration generation.

Model / system. The system consists of charged spherical colloidal particles coated with a polymer brush in an aqueous electrolyte solution, featuring a potential composed of polymer-brush repulsion (Alexander-de Gennes) and screened Coulombic interactions.

Key observables. Crystal structure formation, lattice spacing, radial distribution functions g(r), cluster size evolution, and potential energy trajectories.

Important parameters / regimes. Debye length (lambda_D), particle radius, surface potential, salt concentration, temperature, and polymer brush length/density.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the Derjaguin approximation for pair potentials, the low-potential approximation (psi < 60 mV), and the assumption that the polymer brush effectively suppresses strong electrostatic overlap.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the effect of tuning lambda_D on the interaction potential; Figure 2 illustrates the configuration generation workflow; Figures 9-10 demonstrate nucleation trajectories and the melting of crystal seeds.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the PACS assembly mechanism, describes the PACSim software architecture and features, details the configuration generation utilities, presents simulation results regarding nucleation and stability, and discusses performance and future directions.

Abstract

Polymer-Attenuated Coulombic Self-Assembly (PACS) is a flexible experimental approach for generating crystals from simple colloidal building blocks. The central components are charged spherical particles coated with a polymer brush that prevents irreversible aggregation. Whether oppositely charged colloids crystallize, and which structures they form, depends on several factors, including colloid concentration, charge, and size, as well as the salt concentration of the solution. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations are a powerful tool for predicting the outcomes of PACS assembly experiments and also provide particle-level insight into the assembly processes. Here, we present an open-source simulation framework, PACSim, that enables MD simulation studies of assembly by PACS across a range of experimentally relevant scenarios. PACSim is built on top of OpenMM, a flexible MD simulation framework that readily supports the implementation of different interaction potentials, as well as integration with other tools such as enhanced-sampling and machine-learning frameworks. We describe the motivation for PACSim, outline its features, report methodological advancements inspired by this framework, and provide examples of its use.

Parallel Scan Recurrent Neural Quantum States for Scalable Variational Monte Carlo

Authors: Ejaaz Merali, Mohamed Hibat-Allah, Mohammad Kohandel, Richard T. Scalettar, Ehsan Khatami
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13807v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces Parallel Scan Recurrent Neural Quantum States (PSR-NQS), a new method to make recurrent neural networks scalable for quantum simulations. By using parallel scan algorithms, the authors overcome the sequential bottleneck of traditional RNNs, enabling high-precision simulations of large 2D spin lattices.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new, scalable numerical tool for simulating large-scale many-body dynamics and ground states using efficient, parallelizable machine learning architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the scalability limitations of Recurrent Neural Network Quantum States (RNN-NQS), which are traditionally viewed as computationally slow due to their sequential nature.

Main result. The development of Parallel Scan Recurrent Neural Quantum States (PSR-NQS) allows for efficient, large-scale 2D simulations (up to 52x52 lattices) with accuracy comparable to Quantum Monte Carlo.

Method. Utilizing parallelizable recurrence via parallel scan algorithms and State Space Models (SSMs) like the Linear Recurrent Unit (LRU) and minGRU within a Variational Monte Carlo framework.

Model / system. Quantum many-body systems in 1D and 2D, specifically the 1D Transverse-Field Ising Model (TFIM) and the 2D square-lattice Heisenberg antiferromagnet.

Key observables. Ground-state energy per site (E/N) and relative error compared to exact solutions or QMC data.

Important parameters / regimes. System sizes up to 52x52 sites, 1D critical point at h=1, and asymptotic scaling of O(L log L) in 1D and O(L^3 log L) in 2D.

Assumptions / limitations. The autoregressive sampling process remains O(N) time, and the SSM matrix is assumed to be diagonal in practice to ensure efficient computation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares runtime per training step between sequential and parallel scan versions; Figure 2 shows finite-size scaling of ground-state energy with low relative error.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalability problem, presents the PSR-NQS architecture (LRU and minGRU), demonstrates performance via 1D and 2D benchmarks, and provides scaling and error analysis.

Abstract

Neural-network quantum states have emerged as a powerful variational framework for quantum many-body systems, with recent progress often driven by massively parallel architectures such as transformers. Recurrent neural network quantum states, however, are frequently regarded as intrinsically sequential and therefore less scalable. Here we revisit this view by showing that modern recurrent architectures can support fast, accurate, and computationally accessible neural quantum state simulations. Using autoregressive recurrent wave functions together with recent advances in parallelizable recurrence, we develop variational ansätze, called parallel scan recurrent neural quantum states (PSR-NQS), which can be trained efficiently within variational Monte Carlo in one and two spatial dimensions. We demonstrate accurate benchmark results and show that, with iterative retraining, our approach reaches two-dimensional spin lattices as large as $52\times52$ while remaining in agreement with available quantum Monte Carlo data. Our results establish recurrent architectures as a practical and promising route toward scalable neural quantum state simulations with modest computational resources.

Phase Matching for a Generalized Grover's Algorithm

Authors: Chris Cardullo, Min Kang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13758v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores optimizations for Grover's algorithm by allowing the phase rotation angles to vary. It reveals that the traditional 'phase matching' rule breaks down as the search nears completion, providing a more efficient way to reach a target probability of 1.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how to optimize the phase changes in a generalized Grover's algorithm to maximize the probability of finding a target state, specifically looking for deviations from the standard pi-phase and phase-matching conditions.

Main result. The authors find that while classical Grover's algorithm and phase matching are optimal when the target probability is low, they deviate from these conditions as the probability approaches 1. They also demonstrate that the optimized algorithm can achieve a target probability of 1.0 in certain small-scale systems where the classical version falls short.

Method. The study uses amplitude condensation to reduce the problem to a 2D linear map, followed by critical point analysis (gradient and Hessian) and numerical optimization using MATLAB's global optimization toolbox.

Model / system. An unstructured quantum search model in a finite space of size N = 2^n, utilizing a generalized Grover iterate involving phase rotations and a Fourier transform.

Key observables. The probability of observing the target state P(|tau>).

Important parameters / regimes. Phase changes (phi and psi), search space size (N), target probability, and the complexity (phase difference theta) of the amplitude vector.

Assumptions / limitations. The analytical proofs often assume a sufficiently large N to focus on leading-order terms and focus specifically on the case of a single target element.

Figures summary. Graphs showing the relationship between the optimal psi phase change and the phi phase change for different values of N.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the generalized Grover problem, defines the mathematical model and amplitude condensation, presents theorems for optimal phase changes and the large N limit, provides numerical validation and a 5-qubit case study, and concludes with future research directions.

Abstract

We study the fully generalized Grover's algorithm to find the optimal phase changes for each step of the iteration to maximize gain in probability of observation of the target, and when phase matching is required. We find that classical Grover's algorithm and phase matching remains to be optimal till the target probability gets close 1. However, as the probability of observation approaches 1, the optimal phase changes differ from $π$ and no longer observe phase matching. We provide the optimization statement to find the optimal phase changes given the current amplitude vector and the size of the set. To analyze this formula, we approach it from a numerical and analytical perspective, with the analytical perspective focusing on special cases that simplify the optimization and allow for general statements about its behavior. Finally, we provide an example of a 5 qubit system and show that for the final iteration the optimal phase changes differ from traditional Grover's algorithm and do not observe phase matching, but lead to an increase in the probability of the target.

Physics Guided Generative Optimization for Trotter Suzuki Decomposition

Authors: WenBin Yan
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13268v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a machine learning framework to automate the optimization of quantum circuit compilation for Trotter-Suzuki simulations. By combining generative diffusion models with physics-informed neural networks, it achieves massive reductions in circuit depth and gate counts compared to standard heuristic-based toolchains.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing Trotter-Suzuki decomposition for Hamiltonian evolution on NISQ hardware by simultaneously minimizing circuit depth/CNOT count and maximizing simulation fidelity.

Main result. The method achieves significant circuit compression, reducing depth to ~21.8% and CNOT count to ~19.2% of a 4th-order Qiskit baseline while maintaining high fidelity, and shows strong generalization to molecular Hamiltonians.

Method. A closed-loop generative-and-evaluate pipeline using a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to encode Hamiltonian structure, a hybrid Diffusion model to propose decomposition strategies, and a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) to provide differentiable fidelity feedback via REINFORCE optimization.

Model / system. The study focuses on Hamiltonian time evolution using the Transverse Field Ising Model (TFIM) and Heisenberg chains for training/testing, as well as molecular Hamiltonians (H2 and LiH) obtained via Jordan-Wigner transformation.

Key observables. Fidelity, circuit depth, CNOT count, and PDE residual of the PINN.

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit counts (n=4, 6, 8), coupling strength J, transverse field h, and the trade-off parameter lambda for multi-objective optimization.

Assumptions / limitations. The PINN assumes the ability to fit the Schrödinger equation accurately; the effectiveness of the method is dependent on the specific training regime and hyperparameter tuning (e.g., CFG).

Figures summary. Figures include distributions of the TFIM training dataset, scaling of depth/fidelity in Heisenberg chains, and molecular scans showing fidelity and depth reduction for H2 and LiH across varying bond lengths.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the optimization problem, describes the GNN-Diffusion-PINN architecture, details the reinforcement learning training loop, presents performance comparisons against standard SDKs (Qiskit, etc.), and evaluates generalization to out-of-distribution Heisenberg and molecular systems.

Abstract

Product formulas for Trotter Suzuki simulation remain a practical route to Hamiltonian evolution on noisy intermediate scale quantum (NISQ) hardware, yet their accuracy hinges on three coupled choices: term grouping, product formula order, and timestep allocation. Toolchains such as Qiskit and Paulihedral lean on hand tuned heuristics, while the discrete nature of grouping and order makes naive gradient based optimization awkward. We describe a generate and evaluate loop: a conditional diffusion model proposes strategies, a physics informed neural network (PINN) supplies differentiable fidelity feedback, and a graph neural network (GNN) encodes commutator structure. Training spans a hybrid space (discrete grouping and order, continuous time steps); the closed loop uses REINFORCE and a Pareto tracker. On the transverse field Ising model (TFIM), under our primary comparison setup, the method reaches 85.6% of the fidelity of a fourth order Qiskit baseline (0.856) at roughly 21.8% of the circuit depth and 19.2% of the baseline CNOT count. Under an equal depth budget, fine tuning in the loop reached a best observed fidelity of 0.9994. Updated ablations show that, for a fixed training budget and default guidance knobs, module contributions depend on the training recipe and guidance hyperparameters CFG in particular needs to be tuned jointly with compute budget. Overall, the results suggest that "generative model and physics supervision" is a viable angle for NISQ oriented compilation, though where it wins still depends on the operating point.

Paper structure. Abstract

We introduce a new family of CSS codes obtained from rate-1 precoded polar codes, which harnesses the precoding benefits obtained for classical short blocklength polar codes. We optimize the rate profile and precoder of these codes with a genetic algorithm, and present codes of dimension $ [![256, 2 ]!] $ and $ [![512, 2]!] $ that have logical error rates similar to the $ [![1201, 1, 25 ]!] $ surface code over the depolarizing channel.

Reentrant behavior and possible $2/3$ magnetization plateau on the double-trillium langbeinite K$_2$Ni$_2$(SO$_4$)$_3$

Authors: Matías G. Gonzalez, Yurii Skourski, Johannes Reuther, Ivica Živković
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13263v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores the magnetic properties of a complex 3D frustrated lattice, identifying a potential 2/3 magnetization plateau and reentrant phase behavior. By combining high-field experiments with classical simulations, the authors show that the observed phase is a characteristic feature of the trillium lattice geometry. This provides a roadmap for searching for quantum plateau states in related langbeinite compounds.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the complex magnetic phase transitions, magnetization process, and the potential existence of a 2/3 magnetization plateau in the double-trillium langbeinite K2Ni2(SO4)3.

Main result. The study identifies a series of phase transitions and a 'dome' structure in the phase diagram that suggests a 2/3 magnetization plateau, characterized by an up-up-down configuration on the strong-TL sublattice and a fully polarized weak-TL sublattice.

Method. High-field pulsed magnetic field measurements (up to 40 T) are combined with classical Monte Carlo simulations using 3-component classical vectors.

Model / system. The system is K2Ni2(SO4)3, a member of the langbeinite family consisting of two intertwined S=1 trillium lattices (strong-TL and weak-TL) described by a highly frustrated 3D Heisenberg Hamiltonian with five exchange interactions.

Key observables. Magnetization (M), magnetization derivative (dM/dH), specific heat (cv), energy per site, and spin structure factors (Szz and Sxy).

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange parameters J1 through J5, magnetic field (up to 40 T), and temperature (down to 1.1 K).

Assumptions / limitations. The classical Monte Carlo approach assumes that quantum effects are suppressed by the 3D dimensionality and the spin magnitude (S=1).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the evolution of longitudinal and transverse spin structure factors, showing the appearance of new Bragg peaks in the up-up-down phase and the reduction of background fluctuations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the langbeinite system, describes the experimental setup and Hamiltonian, presents magnetization and derivative data, details the Monte Carlo simulation results (phase diagrams and spin configurations), and concludes with the universality of the observed plateau-like behavior.

Abstract

K$_2$Ni$_2$(SO$_4$)$_3$ is a member of the langbeinite family, consisting of two intertwined $S=1$ trillium lattices, out of which one is strongly coupled (strong-TL) and the other is weakly coupled (weak-TL). Further inter-trillium interactions give rise to a highly-frustrated Heisenberg Hamiltonian. Despite ordering at low temperatures, K$_2$Ni$_2$(SO$_4$)$_3$ lies close in parameter space to a spin-liquid region that surrounds the tetratrillium limit, where each triangle belonging to strong-TL turns into a tetrahedron by connecting to a single spin from weak-TL. Here, we compare the experimentally determined magnetization process using pulsed magnetic fields up to $40$ T with classical Monte Carlo calculations, uncovering a series of phase transitions at both low and intermediate fields. Furthermore, we reveal a signature of a $2/3$ magnetization plateau consisting of a $1/3$ phase on strong-TL and a fully polarized phase on weak-TL. Although in the classical limit no plateau is expected, we find a very prominent dome structure reflecting the tendency of the system to stabilize this particular spin configuration. The presence of a dome leads to a reentrant phenomenon in which the system recovers the Hamiltonian symmetries when increasing the magnetic field. Finally, we show that this plateau-like phase is also present in the classical Heisenberg model on the single trillium and tetratrillium lattices, indicating its possible presence in the large family of double-trillium langbeinite compounds. Our findings motivate future studies on the presence of the plateau phase in the quantum limit of both trillium and double-trillium materials within the langbeinite family.

Simulation of vibrational dynamics using qubits and qudits

Authors: Erik Lötstedt, Kaoru Yamanouchi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12866v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates using qubits and qudits to simulate the vibrational dynamics of CO2 and H2O molecules. It finds that qudit encoding is superior for noisy quantum hardware because it requires fewer gates and is more resilient to decoherence. This makes qudits a promising approach for high-fidelity molecular spectroscopy on near-term quantum devices.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a practical comparison of qudit vs. qubit architectures for molecular spectroscopy, demonstrating how higher-dimensional encodings can mitigate noise in NISQ-era simulations of many-body molecular dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Comparing the efficiency and accuracy of different quantum encoding schemes (binary qubit, direct qubit, and qudit) for simulating the vibrational dynamics of molecules under the influence of noise.

Main result. Qudit encoding provides the most accurate and efficient simulation because its Hamiltonian contains fewer terms, leading to reduced cumulative error from depolarizing noise and fewer required gates.

Method. The study uses first-order Suzuki-Trotter decomposition for time evolution and a completely depolarizing noise model to evaluate the impact of gate errors on vibrational population transfer.

Model / system. The physical models consist of the vibrational dynamics of CO2 (two-mode) and H2O (three-mode) molecules, using an anharmonic Hamiltonian that includes harmonic oscillator terms and third-order anharmonic coupling.

Key observables. Time-dependent vibrational populations and their Fourier transforms (vibrational spectra).

Important parameters / regimes. Maximum vibrational quantum number (v_max), gate error rates (10^-3 and 10^-5), and the number of two-qubit/qudit gates.

Assumptions / limitations. The anharmonic potential is limited to third-order terms, and the noise is modeled strictly as a completely depolarizing channel.

Figures summary. Figures show the scaling of Hamiltonian terms and operator orders, time-dependent populations and Fourier spectra for H2O under different noise levels, and the decay time comparison between encodings.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the encoding schemes, defines the molecular Hamiltonians for CO2 and H2O, details the simulation and noise models, presents comparative results for gate counts and accuracy, and discusses error thresholds and experimental relevance.

Abstract

We investigate the quantum computing of the vibrational dynamics of CO$_2$ and H$_2$O by constructing the vibrational Hamiltonian in qubit and qudit form by two types of qubit encodings (binary and direct) and a qudit encoding. We simulate the time-dependent vibrational population transfer using the three different encodings, including the effect of noise and find that the qudit encoding leads to the most accurate results both for CO$_2$ and H$_2$O because of the small number of terms in the qudit Hamiltonian as long as the same values of the entangling gate error rates are adopted.

Singular spin fluctuations in the strange-metal phase of La2-xSrxCuO4

Authors: B. Costarella, L. Soriano, I. Vinograd, H. Mayaffre, S. Li, J. Yang, J. Luo, R. Zhou, J. Yao, G. Gu, Q. Li, J. M. Tranquada, M. -H. Julien
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13573v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers used high-field NMR to show that singular spin fluctuations persist deep into the overdoped regime of LSCO, potentially explaining the origin of strange-metal transport. They also demonstrate that these fluctuations are spatially inhomogeneous, which may reconcile their presence far from the known stripe-order boundary. This provides a new perspective on the electronic state from which strange-metal behavior emerges.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining whether singular spin fluctuations are the microscopic origin of strange-metal behavior in the overdoped regime of cuprates and resolving the paradox of their persistence beyond the spin-stripe critical doping.

Main result. The study finds that low-energy dynamical spin susceptibility increases continuously down to the lowest temperatures at x=0.25, suggesting quantum-critical-like fluctuations, while also revealing that spatial electronic inhomogeneity may drive this behavior.

Method. High-field (up to 26 T) 139La NMR spectroscopy was used to suppress superconductivity and probe the intrinsic normal state, providing a more reliable measurement than 63Cu NMR which suffers from signal wipeout.

Model / system. Overdoped La2-xSrxCuO4 (LSCO) single crystals at x=0.25, specifically investigating the strange-metal phase characterized by linear-in-temperature resistivity.

Key observables. Spin-lattice relaxation rate (1/T1T), dynamical spin susceptibility (chi''(q, omega)), stretching exponent (beta), and NMR lineshape broadening.

Important parameters / regimes. Hole-doping level p=0.25, magnetic field up to 26 T, temperature down to 1.34 K, and the spin-stripe critical doping x=0.19.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes that 139La NMR provides a representative probe of the bulk spin dynamics and that the observed 1/T1T increase is not an artifact of the density of states (Van Hove singularity).

Figures summary. Fig 1a shows 1/T1T fits to Curie-Weiss and power-law forms; Fig 1b shows the stretching exponent beta; Fig 4 shows the dependence of 63Cu 1/T1T on delay time tau; Fig 5 shows various fits for 139La 1/T1T; Fig 6 shows static inhomogeneity via NMR lineshape.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of strange-metal transport, details the experimental NMR methodology and the advantages of 139La over 63Cu, presents the discovery of singular spin fluctuations, discusses the role of electronic inhomogeneity, and concludes with implications for the quantum critical origin of the phase.

Abstract

Although spin fluctuations are central to the physics of high-Tc cuprates, their relevance to strange-metal behavior in the overdoped regime remains unclear. Here, we use high magnetic fields to suppress superconductivity and an NMR protocol tailored to electronic inhomogeneity to show that the low-energy limit of the dynamical spin susceptibility χ''(q,omega) at x=0.25 in La2-xSrxCuO4 increases continuously down to our lowest temperatures. This behavior is suggestive of quantum-critical fluctuations, a leading candidate mechanism for strange-metal transport, yet is observed well beyond the spin-stripe critical doping x=0.19. Our data further reveal that the spin dynamics are spatially inhomogeneous, suggesting that nanoscale electronic inhomogeneity may underlie this apparent paradox. These observations provide new insight into the electronic state from which strange-metal behavior emerges.

The critical slowing down in diffusion models

Authors: Luca Maria Del Bono, Giulio Biroli, Patrick Charbonneau, Marylou Gabrié
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12597v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies that diffusion models inherit the critical slowing down characteristic of traditional sampling methods when approaching phase transitions. It demonstrates that architectural improvements, such as increasing network depth or implementing local score approximations, can significantly accelerate training and generation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the phenomenon of critical slowing down in the training and generation processes of diffusion models near phase transitions.

Main result. The authors show that while one-layer architectures suffer from critical slowing down (scaling as L^2), using overparameterized two-layer architectures or local score approximations can reduce training time scaling to logarithmic (log L).

Method. The study uses an analytical framework based on the n -> infinity limit of the O(n) model, analyzing gradient descent dynamics and error propagation in Fourier space.

Model / system. The Gaussian limit (n -> infinity) of the O(n) model of statistical field theory, characterized by a Gaussian action with a kinetic term and a mass parameter.

Key observables. Training timescales, correlation time, Wasserstein distance, KL divergence, Frobenius norm of the covariance error, and effective mass.

Important parameters / regimes. Effective mass (m_eff), system size (L), diffusion time (t), coupling constant (g), and learning rate (eta).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is performed in the large-n limit (n -> infinity) to ensure analytical tractability and assumes a Gaussian action.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the scaling of training time with system size, the impact of architecture depth on convergence, and the error scaling of the trained score kernel.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of critical slowing down, derives the analytical score function for the O(n) model, analyzes the training dynamics of one-layer vs. two-layer networks, and evaluates the impact of locality and discretization errors.

Abstract

Computational sampling has been central to the sciences since the mid-20th century. While machine-learning-based approaches have recently enabled major advances, their behavior remains poorly understood, with limited theoretical control over when and why they succeed. Here we provide such insight for diffusion models-a class of generative schemes highly effective in practice-by analyzing their application to the $O(n)$ model of statistical field theory in the Gaussian limit $n \to \infty$. In this analytically tractable setting, we show that training a score model with a one-layer network architecture matching the exact solution exhibits a form of critical slowing down in parameter learning. This slowing down also impacts the generation process, indicating that the well-known difficulties of sampling near criticality persist even for learned generative models. To overcome this bottleneck, we demonstrate the power of combining architectural depth with physical locality. We find that using a two-layer architecture drastically reduces the critical slowing down, with the training time scaling logarithmically rather than quadratically with system size. By introducing a local score approximation we show that this acceleration in training time can be achieved without increasing the number of neural network parameters. Abstract

Control of light polarization and propagation in sub-wavelength architectures is foundational to nanophotonic technologies. A frontier direction is to leverage strong optical spin-orbit interactions to realize polarization-selective light steering, known as the photonic spin Hall effect. In this context, hyperbolic plasmon polaritons (HPPs) are of particular interest as they offer large optical spin-orbit coupling from strong confinement and dielectric anisotropy, as well as ray-like propagation. Despite theoretical predictions, however, the hyperbolic spin Hall effect in natural materials has remained elusive. Here, we demonstrate the hyperbolic spin Hall effect in the visible and near-infrared range in the natural hyperbolic van der Waals metal MoOCl2. Enabling this discovery is a novel far-field pump-probe microscope that facilitates the launching and imaging of HPPs with exceptional sensitivity through interference with a high-momentum reference field. This approach preserves excellent control over light polarization, overcoming a key barrier to polarization-selective interrogation of hyperbolic materials. We show that both hyperbolic and surface plasmons in MoOCl2 display chiral fields, and that their propagation direction can be completely switched upon light helicity reversal. Our results demonstrate on-demand steering of chiral plasmons, firmly establishing natural hyperbolic materials as ideal components for reconfigurable nanophotonics and chiral light-matter coupling.
