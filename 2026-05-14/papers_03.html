Random Access Code protocols: Quantum advantage related to intraparticle entanglement-based contextuality

Authors: Nilaj Saha, Sumit Mukherjee, Dipankar Home
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13350v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the increase in success probability in quantum Random Access Code protocols is quantitatively tied to the violation of single-particle noncontextuality inequalities. By using intraparticle entanglement between path and spin, the authors show that the quantum advantage scales directly with the strength of the underlying quantum resource. This establishes a measurable, fundamental connection between communication complexity and quantum contextuality.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental link between information-theoretic tasks (RAC) and quantum foundations (contextuality), showing how single-particle resources can be used to quantify quantum advantage, which is highly relevant to quantum optics and quantum information processing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to establish a quantitative correspondence between the quantum advantage in Random Access Code (RAC) protocols and the magnitude of intraparticle entanglement-based contextuality violations.

Main result. The authors prove that the enhancement in success probability for n-to-1 RAC protocols is directly commensurate with the violation of single-particle noncontextuality Bell-type inequalities, providing a universal relation between success probability and the Bell-type parameter.

Method. The study uses an algebraic isomorphism between path and spin Hilbert spaces to map RAC protocols onto Bell-CHSH-type inequalities, employing a concatenation scheme to generalize results from 2-to-1 and 3-to-1 subunits to n-to-1 protocols.

Model / system. The physical model involves single-particle systems utilizing intraparticle entanglement between co-measurable degrees of freedom, such as path and spin/polarization, implementable via Mach-Zehnder interferometry or photonic setups.

Key observables. Success probability (P), Bell-type correlation variables (C_n), and detector counts in interferometric setups.

Important parameters / regimes. n-bit encoding (n-to-1), quantum advantage (delta P), and the magnitude of noncontextuality violation (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes the validity of noncontextuality for single-particle path-spin measurements and the mathematical equivalence between intraparticle and interparticle entanglement descriptions.

Figures summary. Figure 2 shows schematic diagrams of 2-to-1 and 3-to-1 RAC subunits and examples of 4-to-1 and 5-to-1 protocol concatenations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of RAC enhancement, establishes the classical bounds using noncontextuality, develops the quantum framework using intraparticle entanglement, provides a mathematical mapping via Hilbert space isomorphism, generalizes the result to n-bit protocols via concatenation, and discusses experimental feasibility.

Abstract

The quantum enhancement of success probability in the Random Access Code (RAC) protocols remains unexplored from two important perspectives. First, the use of entanglement between two co-measurable degrees of freedom of a single particle (intraparticle entanglement) in achieving such quantum enhancement has not been investigated. Second, no explicit quantitative correspondence has been established between the predicted/observed quantum advantage and the underlying quantum resource responsible for it. In this work, we address both these aspects simultaneously by harnessing a single-particle resource. For this purpose, the RAC protocol is formulated in terms of intraparticle entanglement between, for instance, spin/polarization and path degrees of freedom of a single particle. Within this framework, a relevant Bell-type inequality, derived from the assumption of noncontextuality for single particle path-spin measurements, is used. Based on these ingredients, the formulated analysis reveals that the magnitude of quantum-mechanical violation of such Bell-type inequality, signifying a form of quantum contextuality, is quantitatively commensurate with the quantum enhancement of success probability in any intraparticle entanglement-assisted $n$-bit RAC protocol. In particular, the maximal success probability of a quantum $n \mapsto 1$ RAC protocol corresponds to the maximal quantum violation of the relevant Bell-type inequality. This correspondence is empirically testable using a readily implementable single-particle interferometric setup requiring coherence preservation only for a single particle.

Summary. This paper solves a long-standing open problem by proving that the 14-qubit state Phi_E8 is entangled. By combining symmetric extensions with moment matrices and graph theory, the authors provide an explicit, mathematically rigorous entanglement witness. This method offers a scalable framework for detecting entanglement in complex multipartite systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to solve an open problem by providing an explicit entanglement witness to prove that the 14-qubit state Phi_E8 is entangled.

Main result. The authors successfully prove the entanglement of the Phi_E8 state by providing a rational infeasibility certificate for a semidefinite program, effectively constructing an operational entanglement witness.

Method. The approach combines the symmetric extension hierarchy with the non-commutative optimization hierarchy (moment matrices) and utilizes symmetry reduction via the Terwilliger algebra to make the semidefinite program (SDP) computationally tractable.

Model / system. The system is a 14-qubit multipartite state, Phi_E8, which is constructed from projectors onto symmetric and antisymmetric subspaces of qubit pairs and is related to the existence of AME(7,2) states.

Key observables. The entanglement witness W_Phi_E8, derived from the Lovasz theta number of the Pauli anti-commutativity graph.

Important parameters / regimes. The weight of Pauli strings, the anti-commutativity graph G_4+, and the weight threshold delta = 4.

Assumptions / limitations. The proof relies on a relaxation of the SDP via group averaging, though the authors note that infeasibility of the relaxed version implies infeasibility of the original.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a visual summary of the proof sketch, showing the transition from a putative separable state to a constrained three-copy extension.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Phi_E8 entanglement, describes the construction of the symmetric extension and moment matrix, details the symmetry reduction and SDP formulation, presents the numerical infeasibility certificate, and concludes with the construction of the explicit entanglement witness.

Abstract

We solve an open problem in entanglement theory posed by Yu et al., {\it Nature Communications 12, 1012 (2021)}. The problem is to show, via an entanglement witness, that the $14$-qubit state $Φ_{\text{E8}}$ is entangled. Inspired by a method from quantum codes, we combine symmetric extension with moment matrices to prove that $Φ_{\text{E8}}$ is entangled. The proof has the form of a rational infeasibility certificate for a semidefinite program, yielding an explicit entanglement witness. Our approach unifies and extends several earlier methods that involve the Lovász theta number of the Pauli anti-commutativity graph, promising scalability and flexibility in further applications.

Nagaoka supermetal in the particle-doped triangular Hubbard model

Summary. The paper identifies a new quantum state called the Nagaoka supermetal in the particle-doped triangular Hubbard model. This state arises from an interaction-induced higher-order Van Hove singularity that causes anomalous transport properties like sublinear resistivity. The findings provide a new perspective on non-Fermi liquid behavior in geometrically frustrated systems.

Why it may be interesting. The discovery of an intrinsic, interaction-driven non-Fermi liquid state with power-law divergences is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and is directly accessible in ultracold atom experiments.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the nature of the metallic state emerging upon particle doping in a frustrated Mott insulator and identifying the origin of its non-Fermi liquid behavior.

Main result. Discovery of the 'Nagaoka supermetal' state, characterized by sublinear temperature-dependent resistivity and power-law divergences in charge compressibility, driven by an interaction-induced higher-order Van Hove singularity.

Method. Dynamical Cluster Approximation (DCA) using a continuous-time hybridization expansion solver, supplemented by second-order perturbation theory and effective low-energy Hamiltonian derivation.

Model / system. The two-dimensional triangular-lattice Hubbard model in the strongly correlated regime (U/t = 12), specifically focusing on the particle-doped regime.

Key observables. DC resistivity, charge compressibility, zero-frequency spectral weight, density of states, and nearest-neighbor spin correlation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (U/t = 12), doping level (delta), temperature (T/t), and kinetic renormalization factor (Z).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglect of vertex corrections in resistivity calculations and the use of the bubble approximation for transport properties; cluster size is limited to Nc <= 6 due to the fermion sign problem.

Figures summary. A phase map showing the transition from Mott insulator to various regimes (FL, SM, PG, NS) and a finite-temperature phase diagram demonstrating the robustness of the NS state.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of doped Mott insulators, describes the Hubbard model and DCA methodology, presents the discovery of the Nagaoka supermetal and its transport signatures, derives the effective Hamiltonian and the higher-order Van Hove singularity mechanism, and concludes with implications for experimental observations.

Abstract

While the interplay of correlations and geometric frustration in doped Mott insulators provides a fertile ground for exotic quantum phases, the nature of the metallic state emerging upon particle doping remains poorly understood. In this work, we investigate the triangular-lattice Hubbard model with particle doping and provide compelling evidence for an intrinsic, interaction-driven quantum state, which we term the Nagaoka supermetal. This state is characterized by a sublinear temperature dependence in the DC resistivity, along with singular behaviors in the charge compressibility and zero-frequency spectral weight. To understand the origin of these singular properties, we derive an effective low-energy model and demonstrate that a higher-order Van Hove singularity emerges from the reconstructed dispersion. This singularity gives rise to a power-law divergence in the density of states, capturing the anomalous properties observed in the supermetallic regime. Our findings offer a new perspective on non-Fermi liquid states in geometrically frustrated systems and are directly accessible in current ultracold atom experiments.

Summary. The researchers developed an all-optical method to precisely select single or double layers of Dysprosium atoms within a superlattice. By using parametric heating to remove unwanted layers, they achieved a stable platform for high-resolution microscopy of 2D dipolar gases. This technique is particularly useful for studying systems with strong long-range interactions.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a robust, all-optical tool for preparing 2D layers in highly magnetic systems, which is essential for studying long-range dipolar interactions and Casimir-Polder forces in a controlled, single-atom-resolved manner.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of performing high-resolution microscopy on 2D dipolar quantum gases due to the challenge of selecting individual atomic layers, especially for lanthanide atoms where standard magnetic-gradient techniques are ineffective.

Main result. The demonstration of an all-optical method for the deterministic spatial selection of single- and bilayer samples of cold Dysprosium atoms using site-selective parametric heating in a beat-note superlattice.

Method. The researchers use a beat-note superlattice (BNSL) created by retroreflecting two collinear laser beams and apply site-selective parametric heating via amplitude modulation to remove atoms from unwanted planes.

Model / system. A 2D system of bosonic 164-Dy (Dysprosium) atoms in an optical beat-note superlattice (BNSL) with a tight-binding regime and site-dependent local band gaps.

Key observables. Atom number after heating, local excitation frequencies (local band gap), site-resolved contrast, and in-situ fluorescence imaging of the atomic cloud.

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice spacing of ~536.8 nm, axial confinement frequency of 25 kHz, initial temperature of 123 nK, and a wavelength ratio n approx 25 for the BNSL.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes potential depth equality (V1 approx V2) and utilizes the harmonic oscillator approximation for the tight-binding regime.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the BNSL setup and atom number loading; Fig 2 shows absorption images and loss spectra after heating; Fig 3 shows the spatial variation of the local band gap; Fig 5 shows fluorescence imaging and lattice contrast measurements.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of layer selection, describes the BNSL experimental setup, details the mechanism of site-selective parametric heating, demonstrates the preparation of single and bilayer planes, and concludes with characterization of the resulting 2D gases.

Abstract

High-resolution microscopy of two-dimensional dipolar quantum gases requires selecting individual atomic layers, a task complicated for strongly magnetic lanthanide atoms by the limited applicability of standard magnetic-gradient techniques. We present an all-optical method for the deterministic spatial selection of single- and bilayer samples of cold dipolar atoms using spatially selective parametric heating within a beat-note superlattice. By utilizing a high-resolution microscope objective as a common retroreflector for both optical frequency components, the lattice planes are passively stabilized. This renders their positions exceptionally robust against experimental drifts and structure-borne vibrations, even eliminating the need for active laser stabilization over millimeter-scale separations from the reflecting surface. We validate this approach by demonstrating the robust isolation of one or two atomic layers in precise coincidence with the focal plane of our objective. This enables future single-atom-resolved studies of long-range interacting systems.

OAM-Induced Lattice Rotation Reveals a Fractional Optimum in Fault-Tolerant GKP Quantum Sensing

Authors: Simanshu Kumar, Nandan S Bisht
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13271v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · interference shaping light 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. This paper shows that using fractional orbital angular momentum to rotate GKP lattices can significantly suppress logical errors in quantum sensing. By optimizing the lattice geometry to match the noise channel, the researchers achieved a 23.9-fold reduction in error rates without sacrificing sensing precision.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a geometric design principle for noise-adaptive quantum sensors, demonstrating how structural coupling between encoding (OAM) and error correction (GKP) can be exploited to enhance fault-tolerant metrology.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing the geometry of GKP (Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill) error-correcting codes to simultaneously maximize sensing sensitivity and maintain fault tolerance in the presence of asymmetric noise (photon loss and dephasing).

Main result. The discovery of a fractional optimal OAM charge of l=1.5 (theta=67.5 degrees) that reduces the logical error rate by 23.9x compared to a square lattice while maintaining nearly identical Quantum Fisher Information.

Method. An end-to-end differentiable programming framework using Strawberry Fields and TensorFlow to jointly optimize lattice rotation, aspect ratio, and energy envelope via gradient descent.

Model / system. Continuous-variable bosonic modes using GKP states in a phase-space lattice, subject to a photon-loss channel and an anisotropic dephasing channel.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), Logical Error Rate (P_err), and Metrological Capacity (C).

Important parameters / regimes. OAM topological charge (l), lattice rotation angle (theta), lattice aspect ratio (r), photon loss transmissivity (eta), and dephasing rate (gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes the fault-tolerant regime where error probabilities are small; the model uses a Fock-space truncation (D=30).

Figures summary. Training convergence for QFI and error rates, visualizations of Wigner functions showing interference fringes and noise impact, and performance comparisons of error reduction across different OAM charges.

Paper structure. Introduction to GKP sensing optimization; description of the OAM-induced lattice rotation mechanism; presentation of the differentiable optimization framework; results showing the fractional optimum and error reduction; analytical derivation of the optimal angle and its monotonicity; discussion of experimental implementation routes and robustness.

Abstract

Photon loss and dephasing rapidly degrade the sensitivity of quantum sensors, yet systematic methods for designing error-correcting codes whose geometry is simultaneously adapted to the sensing task and the noise channel do not exist. Here we establish that orbital-angular-momentum (OAM) encoding and Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) lattice geometry are structurally coupled: an OAM mode of topological charge $\ell$ induces a phase-space rotation $θ_\ell=\ellπ/\ell_{\max}$, corresponding to a family of twisted GKP stabilizer lattices. Using an end-to-end differentiable Strawberry Fields--TensorFlow circuit, we jointly optimise $\ell$, the lattice aspect ratio $r$, and the finite-energy envelope $ε$ to maximise quantum Fisher information subject to $P_{\rm err}\leq10^{-3}$. The optimum occurs at the fractional charge $\ell=1.5$ ($θ=67.5^\circ$), implementable with a half-integer spiral phase plate, which reduces $P_{\rm err}$ by $23.9\times$ relative to the square-lattice baseline while leaving $\mathcal{F}_Q$ unchanged to within $0.2\%$. This surpasses the best integer value ($\ell=2$, $15.7\times$) and arises from an exact $180^\circ$ periodicity of the $P_{\rm err}(θ)$ landscape, confirmed analytically and numerically. We derive a transcendental balance equation for the optimal angle $θ^*(η,γ,r)$ and prove that it decreases with both $γ$ and $η$. A Shannon-inspired metrological capacity $\mathcal{C}=\mathcal{F}_Q\cdot(-\ln P_{\rm err})$, maximised at $\ell=1.5$ with a $41\%$ gain over the square lattice, quantifies the joint sensitivity--fault-tolerance resource. These results establish a geometric design principle for noise-adaptive quantum sensors and a fully open-source differentiable template extensible to other bosonic code families.

Decoherence of spatial superpositions along stationary worldlines

Summary. This paper analyzes how a particle's spatial superposition decays due to relativistic effects while moving along non-inertial paths. It distinguishes between decoherence caused by the thermal-like Unruh effect and that caused by gravitational time dilation. The findings show that both effects contribute to the loss of quantum coherence and the emergence of new dispersive forces.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental link between relativistic effects (Unruh effect and time dilation) and open quantum system dynamics, specifically how spacetime geometry induces decoherence and forces in a quantum particle.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the mechanisms of decoherence and the emergence of dispersive potentials for the center-of-mass motion of a particle moving along stationary, non-inertial worldlines in the Minkowski vacuum.

Main result. The authors identify two distinct decoherence components: one arising from the Davies-Unruh effect (field spectrum modification) and another from differential time dilation across the particle's spatial wavefunction.

Method. The study employs a quantum Brownian motion master equation derived under the Born-Markov approximation, utilizing spectral decomposition of field operators and a separation of time scales between internal and external dynamics.

Model / system. A polarizable particle with an internal oscillator (internal degree of freedom) and a quantized center-of-mass motion (external degree of freedom) interacting with a 3+1 dimensional scalar field.

Key observables. Decoherence rates (Davies-Unruh and time-dilation components), momentum diffusion, dissipation, and dispersion potentials.

Important parameters / regimes. Local acceleration, redshift factor, Davies-Unruh temperature, and the ultra-relativistic limit for circular motion.

Assumptions / limitations. Stationary worldlines, separation of time scales between internal and external dynamics, Born-Markov approximation, and the particle's center-of-mass wavefunction is sufficiently localized.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares decoherence mechanisms in different spacetimes; Figure 2 provides a schematic of the particle model; Figure 3 illustrates the two physical perspectives of decoherence (inertial vs. co-moving).

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the particle model and interaction, proceeds to derive an effective red-shifted polarizability, constructs a quantum Brownian motion master equation, and concludes by evaluating specific cases of hyperbolic and circular motion.

Abstract

We analyze the decoherence of a particle's spatial superposition moving along a stationary worldline through the Minkowski vacuum. The particle is modeled via an internal degree of freedom that couples to a scalar field, and an external degree of freedom, i.e., its quantized center-of-mass motion around the stationary worldline. Assuming a separation of time scales between the particle's internal and external dynamics, we first obtain an effective red-shifted polarizability of the particle, characterizing the trajectory-dependent linear response of the internal oscillator to the field. We then derive a quantum Brownian motion master equation for the particle's center of mass, under the Born-Markov approximation, which describes its decoherence in the position basis, as well as, Hamiltonian modifications corresponding to a dispersive potential. The resulting decoherence has two components: (1) arising from a modified field spectrum observed by the particle; and (2) due to a differential time-dilation over the particle's extended spatial wavefunction. For stationary trajectories, both contributions take an effectively thermal form. We evaluate the decoherence rates for two specific cases of hyperbolic and uniform circular motion.

Summary. This paper proposes a new theoretical framework called Neural LoFi to explain how deep neural networks learn hierarchical features. It treats deep learning as a sequence of spectral filtering steps, providing a mathematically tractable way to predict when specific features will emerge from data based on sample complexity.

Why it may be interesting. The use of spectral theory, phase transition analogies (BBP/EA transitions), and renormalization-like coarse-graining to describe feature evolution may resonate with researchers studying the dynamics of many-body systems and renormalization group flows.

Main result. The authors introduce Neural LoFi, a spectral surrogate that predicts feature emergence as a phase transition and demonstrates that depth allows networks to learn complex functions by decomposing them into low-degree transitions.

Abstract

Understanding how deep neural networks learn useful internal representations from data remains a central open problem in the theory of deep learning. We introduce Neural Low-Degree Filtering (Neural LoFi), a stylized limit of gradient-based training in which hierarchical feature learning becomes an explicit iterative spectral procedure. In this limit, the dynamics at each layer decouple: given the current representation, the next layer selects directions with maximal accessible low-degree correlation to the label. This yields a tractable surrogate mechanism for deep learning, together with a natural kernel-space interpretation. Neural LoFi provides a mathematically explicit framework for studying multi-layer feature learning beyond the lazy regime. It predicts how representations are selected layer by layer, explains how emergence of concepts arises with given sample complexity,and gives a concrete mechanism by which depth progressively constructs new features from old ones through low-degree compositionality. We complement the theory with mechanistic experiments on fully connected and convolutional architectures, showing that Neural LoFi improves over lazy random-feature baselines, recovers meaningful structured filters, and predicts representations aligned with early gradient-descent feature discovery with real datasets.

Matrix-noise Jacobians in stochastic-calculus inference and optimal paths

Authors: Surachate Limkumnerd

Summary. The paper reveals that in multidimensional systems with matrix-valued noise, a unique Jacobian term arises that is ignored in simpler scalar models. This term can significantly bias the estimation of stochastic calculus conventions and physically alter the predicted most-likely paths in a system.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for open quantum systems and many-body dynamics, as it shows that neglecting off-diagonal noise correlations can lead to incorrect physical conclusions about the underlying stochastic dynamics and transition paths.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how matrix-valued multiplicative noise affects stochastic-calculus inference and the calculation of optimal paths in multidimensional systems.

Main result. The paper identifies a specific matrix-noise Jacobian term, J_sigma, that is absent in scalar-noise models and demonstrates that it causes measurable shifts in inferred stochastic prescriptions and deforms optimized transition paths.

Method. The author uses a finite-step path-likelihood framework, short-time expansions, and a variational Hamiltonian approach to derive and test the impact of the matrix-Jacobian term.

Model / system. The study uses multidimensional stochastic differential equations with multiplicative noise, specifically testing two models: Model A (a 2D system for inference validation) and Model B (a variational setting for path deformation).

Key observables. The matrix-noise Jacobian (J_sigma), the paired estimator shift (theta_diag - theta_full), and the deformation of the Onsager-Machlup optimal path.

Important parameters / regimes. The stochastic discretization parameter (theta), the noise-amplitude coefficients (b and c), and the time-step (delta t).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes smooth coefficients, non-singular noise amplitude matrices, and the small time-step limit (delta t -> 0).

Figures summary. Figure 1 (not fully described) and Figure 3 show the most likely transition paths for Model B, visually demonstrating a 'bowing' effect caused by the matrix-Jacobian term.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of matrix-noise ambiguity, formulates a path-likelihood framework, identifies the J_sigma term via short-time expansion, and then validates the findings through numerical inference tests (Model A) and variational path optimization (Model B).

Abstract

Multiplicative noise makes stochastic dynamics depend on how the white-noise limit is interpreted. In multidimensional systems with matrix-valued noise amplitudes $σ(x)$, this dependence includes a local Jacobian contribution that is absent from the scalar examples most often used to build intuition. We formulate a finite-step path-likelihood framework for $θ$-discretized diffusions and show that its short-time expansion isolates the scalar $J_σ=\partial_jσ_{ik}\partial_iσ_{jk}-(\partial_iσ_{ik})(\partial_lσ_{lk})$. For a specified noise-amplitude representation $σ$, this quantity vanishes in one-dimensional, scalar-isotropic, and strictly diagonal cases, but can survive when state-dependent noise directions mix different components. We then test its consequences using paired comparisons that hold the drift, diffusion matrix, interpolation point, and Gaussian increment term fixed. In Model A, removing only the off-diagonal determinant contribution produces a shift in the fitted stochastic prescription that vanishes when $J_σ=0$. In Model B, removing the corresponding state-dependent action term changes a stable optimized transition path. These results show that a genuinely matrix-noise part of the short-time path measure can survive the scalar cancellations familiar from simpler settings and produce measurable changes in fitted stochastic prescriptions and Onsager--Machlup paths.

The Physical and Contextual Limits of Quantum Speedup

Authors: Karl Svozil
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12675v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper refutes the idea that quantum computers work by performing many classical computations in parallel. Instead, it argues that speedup is a result of using interference to detect global algebraic structures, a process that is highly sensitive to any information leakage into the environment.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insights into how decoherence and environmental coupling (leakage) specifically quench the interference mechanisms required for quantum advantage, which is central to open quantum systems and quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to clarify the fundamental physical and contextual limits of quantum speedup, specifically debunking the misconception that quantum computing relies on 'branchwise parallelism' of classical computations.

Main result. The author demonstrates that quantum speedup arises from engineered interference patterns and algebraic embeddings rather than parallel classical branches, and that this speedup is destroyed if information leaks into an inaccessible environment (witness register).

Method. The paper uses a theoretical framework involving unitary dynamics, information theory (Holevo bound, no-cloning/no-deletion theorems), and a modified Bernstein-Vazirani algorithm to model interference loss.

Model / system. The study focuses on n-qubit systems governed by closed unitary dynamics, specifically analyzing a modified Bernstein-Vazirani algorithm involving a query register, an answer register, and a witness register.

Key observables. Measurement probability distribution Pr(z) in the computational basis and fringe visibility/interference patterns.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of qubits (n), the size of the information leakage to the witness register (k), and the hidden string (a).

Assumptions / limitations. The witness register is assumed to be inaccessible (modeled by tracing it out), and the dynamics are assumed to be closed and unitary.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a conceptual clarification of quantum speedup, moves to a review of physical constraints (no-cloning, contextuality, unitarity), introduces a mathematical model of information leakage via a modified Bernstein-Vazirani algorithm, and concludes with a discussion on complexity and universality.

Abstract

Quantum computation is frequently mischaracterized as the simultaneous execution of exponentially many classical computations. This article offers a conceptual clarification of why this "branchwise parallelism" picture is misleading, demonstrating that the components of a quantum superposition cannot be treated as independently readable classical branches. Quantum speedups arise instead from reversible embeddings of algebraic structure made accessible through engineered interference patterns. We review this mechanism through several constraints: unitary garbage erasure is impossible, copying and deletion are context-dependent, and contextuality obstructs a single global classical history. We also distinguish circuit or unitary universality from Turing universality: dense generation of unitaries is not the same as symbolic computation over unbounded inputs with recursion, uniformity, and self-reference. In closed unitary dynamics there is no nontrivial absorbing halting state of the classical many-to-one kind; operational termination requires clocks, flags, measurements, open-system records, or external control. Exponential Hilbert-space dimension supplies a geometry for interference and high-dimensional embedding, not unlimited classical readout.

Abstract

Local interactions among electrons underlie many complex properties of correlated materials. While the Jordan-Wigner transformation can preserve this locality along one spatial dimension, interactions along the remaining dimensions typically incur substantial overhead. We show how to simulate all geometrically local interactions on an $N$-site two-dimensional fermion lattice with no asymptotic overhead in the number of interactions and no space overhead. The primary overhead of our method is circuit depth, which on a qubit lattice matches that of fermionic swap networks, scaling as $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{N})$, but reduces to $\mathcal{O}(\log N)$ on reconfigurable qubit arrays and to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ in lattice-surgery-based surface-code architectures. This is enabled by dynamically reorienting the Jordan-Wigner transformation to switch the lattice dimension along which locality is preserved. Furthermore, we study fermion routing, as required for the simulation of non-local interactions. When using qubit lattices, we reach resource scaling that asymptotically matches that of qubit routing, whilst on fully connected qubit devices, a depth scaling arbitrarily close to $\mathcal{O}(\log N)$ is reached. This allows the fermionic fast Fourier transform to be implemented on qubit lattices with asymptotically optimal resource scaling under these locality constraints. Notably, all of our constructions naturally extend to $d$-dimensional lattices. Beyond scaling improvements, we show explicit examples of our method, including Fermi-Hubbard-model simulations of the square-, Lieb- and kagome lattice and the fermionic fast Fourier transform.

Abstract

The self-duality of the transverse-field Ising model is an archetype for dualities that, alongside symmetry and topology, are used as an organizing principle throughout modern physics. This duality, however, is not exact. The original and dual models have different symmetries and numbers of ground states, and the duality is implemented by a non-invertible operator giving rise to a non-invertible symmetry at the quantum critical point. Here, we show that by adjusting the model to accommodate open rather than periodic boundary conditions, it allows for an exact duality implemented by a unique invertible operator. In the model with exact duality, the symmetry at the quantum critical point is also exact, and hence invertible. Moreover, we find that the exact duality necessitates the presence of an anomalous edge degree of freedom, thus realizing a duality rather than topology based bulk-boundary correspondence. Finally, the exactness of the duality implies that the spontaneous breakdown of a global symmetry in terms of the original model can equivalently be described as spontaneously breaking a local symmetry in the dual system. We show that this seeming contradiction of Elitzur's theorem can be explained by the original and dual models obtaining different sensitivities to spatially local perturbations in any physical implementation of the Hamiltonian. Although the dual partners are mathematically equivalent, their physical implementations therefore are not. In analogy to the spontaneous breakdown of symmetries, we term this emergent distinction due to arbitrarily small environmental influences spontaneous duality breaking.
