Highly Efficient Exciton Modulation in MoSe$_2$/PdSe$_2$ Heterostructures

Summary. The researchers demonstrate that placing MoSe2 in contact with PdSe2 significantly boosts its light emission efficiency at room temperature. This is achieved through interlayer electronic coupling that redirects excitons into radiative pathways, offering a non-invasive way to engineer 2D optoelectronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to efficiently control exciton recombination and enhance photoluminescence quantum yield in 2D semiconductors without using invasive methods like chemical doping or strain.

Main result. A 6-fold enhancement of room-temperature A-exciton emission in MoSe2/PdSe2 heterostructures is achieved via interlayer electronic coupling that redistributes exciton populations toward radiative channels.

Method. The study combines experimental optical spectroscopy (PL, PLE, power/temperature-dependent PL) and structural characterization (AFM, Raman) with ab initio DFT simulations.

Model / system. A van der Waals heterostructure consisting of a monolayer of MoSe2 and a multilayer (approx. 6L) of PdSe2 on an hBN substrate.

Key observables. Photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY), A-exciton and B-exciton emission intensities, B/A intensity ratio, and power-law exponent for exciton-exciton annihilation.

Important parameters / regimes. Room temperature, 6-fold enhancement of PLQY, broadband enhancement range of 450-725 nm, and ~200 meV splitting between A and B excitons.

Assumptions / limitations. The 4L PdSe2 model used in DFT is considered an efficient approximation of the 6L experimental system due to similar electronic structures.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the spatial arrangement of the heterostructure flakes and the electronic band structures/projected density of states. Supporting information includes Raman spectra for thickness verification.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of exciton control, describes the MoSe2/PdSe2 heterostructure fabrication and characterization, presents the observed PL enhancement and B-exciton quenching, provides mechanistic evidence via power/temperature dependence and PLE, and supports the findings with DFT simulations and control experiments.

Abstract

Controlling exciton recombination in atomically thin semiconductors is central to their optoelectronic functionality, as the competition between radiative and non-radiative decay channels governs emission efficiency. Existing approaches, such as defect passivation, chemical doping, dielectric engineering, and strain tuning, primarily aim to suppress non-radiative losses. Here, we report a pronounced $\sim$6-fold enhancement of room-temperature A-exciton emission in a type-I MoSe$_2$/PdSe$_2$ van der Waals heterostructure, yielding a photoluminescence quantum yield of 6 %, compared to $\sim$1 % for as-exfoliated monolayer MoSe$_2$. This enhancement is accompanied by strong quenching of the B-exciton, consistent with interlayer electronic coupling that redistributes exciton populations toward the radiative A-exciton channel. Power- and temperature-dependent measurements reveal a suppression of exciton-exciton annihilation and a crossover to quenched emission at low temperature, indicating a redistribution of exciton relaxation pathways. Photoluminescence excitation spectroscopy further reveals a broadband enhancement spanning 450-725 nm, ruling out a resonance-specific mechanism. These results demonstrate that interlayer electronic coupling can be used as an efficient means to redirect exciton populations toward radiative channels, enhancing emission efficiency in two-dimensional semiconductors without chemical modification or strain.

Interface controlled spin filtering and nonreciprocal transport in Altermagnet/Ising superconductor junctions

Authors: Arindam Boruah, Saumen Acharjee, Prasanta Kumar Saikia
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13317v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the unique spin textures of altermagnets and Ising superconductors can be harnessed at a spin-active interface to create highly efficient spin filters. It shows that by tuning the interface magnetization and the altermagnet orientation, one can achieve significant spin polarization and direction-dependent (nonreciprocal) transport, providing a platform for superconducting spintronics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the interplay between momentum-dependent spin splitting in altermagnets and intrinsic spin-orbit coupling in Ising superconductors can be used to control spin-polarized transport and nonreciprocal effects via a spin-active interface.

Main result. The study demonstrates that a spin-active interface enables tunable spin filtering (up to 86% efficiency) and nonreciprocal transport, with transport properties highly dependent on the relative orientation of the altermagnet spin texture and interface magnetization.

Method. A modified Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) framework within the Blonder-Tinkham-Klapwijk (BTK) scattering formalism is used to calculate spin-resolved conductance and symmetry analysis.

Model / system. A heterostructure junction consisting of an Altermagnet (AM) with d-wave symmetry exchange field and an Ising Superconductor (ISC) with strong spin-orbit coupling, separated by a spin-active interface with spin-dependent potential.

Key observables. Spin-resolved charge and spin conductance, spin polarization (P), spin-filter efficiency (eta), and nonreciprocal transport signatures.

Important parameters / regimes. AM orientation angle (delta), interface magnetization angle (chi_m), spin-mixing strength (rho), and Ising spin-orbit coupling strength (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The altermagnet exchange field is assumed to lie in the xy-plane; the interface is modeled as a delta-function potential.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the heterostructure schematic; Fig 2 shows conductance spectra vs AM orientation; Fig 5 shows conductance density plots across different ISOC regimes; Fig 6 shows angular modulation of spin polarization and efficiency; Fig 7 shows variation of parameters.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the AM/ISC junction, defines the Hamiltonian and interface model, derives the transport formalism, analyzes transport in different ISOC regimes, and discusses spin-filtering efficiency and nonreciprocity.

Abstract

We investigate theoretically spin-resolved transport, spin filtering, and nonreciprocal effects in an Altermagnet/Ising superconductor (AM/ISC) junction with a spin-active interface. Using a modified Bogoliubov-de Gennes framework within the scattering formalism, we demonstrate that the interplay among intrinsic spin-orbit coupling (ISOC), anisotropic AM spin texture and spin-dependent interfacial scattering gives rise to strongly anisotropic charge and spin conductance. In the weak spin-mixing regime, transport remains predominantly helicity conserving and exhibits pronounced angular dependence governed by the relative orientation between the AM spin texture and interface magnetization. Increasing ISOC enhances spin conductance and leads to spin-selective Andreev reflection resulting in finite spin filtering. In contrast, the strong spin-mixing regime exhibits enhanced angular anisotropy and robust spin-polarized transport over a broad energy range. Conventional Andreev reflection becomes strongly suppressed, accompanied by substantial spectral redistribution. We further show that nonreciprocal transport persists throughout the single-band, intermediate and double-band ISC regime. The spin polarization and spin-filter efficiency exhibit nonmonotonic dependence on system parameters, reaching values up to $\sim 86\%$, with characteristic angular modulation determined by the AM spin texture. Finite-energy analysis reveals enhanced spin selectivity at low energies and suppression near the superconducting gap. Furthermore, strong spin mixing at the AM/ISC junction produces asymmetric conductance patterns, indicating nonreciprocal transport. Our results establish AM/ISC junctions as a versatile platform for tunable superconducting spintronics and directional spin transport.

Summary. The study tracks how the electronic band gap of MoS2 changes as it grows from a single layer to five layers. It reveals that the band gap drops below the known bulk value due to extra electronic screening caused by defects like grain boundaries and stacking faults inherent in the growth process.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the layer-thickness dependence of the electronic band gap in MoS2 from monolayer to 5 layers and determining why the 5-layer band gap is lower than the bulk value.

Main result. A drastic decrease in the band gap is observed as layer number increases, reaching values below the bulk limit, which is attributed to additional screening from growth-induced defects.

Method. Scanning Tunneling Spectroscopy (STS) was used for experimental measurement, while Density Functional Theory (DFT) and GW calculations were used for theoretical comparison.

Model / system. MoS2 layers (1 to 5 atomic layers) grown via Molecular Beam Epitally on a graphene/Ir(111) substrate using a Stranski-Krastanov growth mode.

Key observables. Band gap (Eg), conduction band and valence band edge positions relative to the Fermi level, and dI/dU differential conductance.

Important parameters / regimes. Layer thickness (1-5L), temperature (5.5 K), and screening/doping weights.

Assumptions / limitations. The theoretical model assumes a defect-free experimental structure and uses empirically estimated doping weights to match experimental data.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows STM topographies of different coverage regimes (1-2L, 2-4L, and 3-5L); Figure 2 shows normalized dI/dU curves and extracted band gap data.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the importance of band engineering in TMDs, describes the MBE growth and STM/STS experimental setup, presents the thickness-dependent band gap measurements, compares results with DFT/GW calculations, and concludes with a discussion on the role of growth-induced screening.

Abstract

In light of the rise of transition metal dichalcogenides as 2D semiconductors for device applications, band engineering becomes very important from an application point of view. In many of these materials, such as the canonical example of MoS$_{2}$, the semiconductor band gap depends on the layer number. It changes from indirect to direct as it evolves from a bulk semiconductor to a monolayer. Interestingly, it was predicted and experimentally confirmed that, by thinning the material from bulk to a bilayer, the indirect transition shows a strong blue-shift. Here, we present the results of scanning tunnelling spectroscopy measurements on MoS$_{2}$ that has been grown \textit{in situ} via molecular beam epitaxy on graphene on Ir(111) at thicknesses ranging from 1 to 5 layers. We find a drastic decrease of the band gap with increasing layer number, to values even below the band gap in bulk. We also observe that the pinning of the conduction band vanishes above 4 layers. Comparing our experimental data with density functional theory and \textit{GW} calculations indicates that an additional screening is introduced by the sample growth conditions.

Summary. This paper demonstrates a method to speed up hydrogen storage in magnesium by using high-pressure torsion to create magnesium-graphene interfaces. These interfaces act as high-speed pathways for hydrogen diffusion, significantly increasing the reaction frequency without changing the energy barrier.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The slow hydrogen absorption and desorption kinetics of magnesium, which is a promising but kinetically limited hydrogen storage material.

Main result. Creating magnesium-graphene interphase boundaries via high-pressure torsion enhances hydrogen storage kinetics by significantly increasing the frequency factor, without altering the activation energy.

Method. High-pressure torsion (HPT) is used for severe plastic deformation and grain refinement, followed by Sieverts-type measurements for kinetics and Kissinger analysis for thermal parameters.

Model / system. Magnesium (Mg) and magnesium-graphene composites (0, 2, 5, and 10 wt% graphene) processed via high-pressure torsion to create nanostructured bimodal morphologies.

Key observables. Hydrogen absorption/desorption rates, hydrogen storage capacity (wt%), activation energy (Ea), frequency factor (A), and grain size.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (623 K), hydrogen pressure (4.03 MPa absorption, 0.01 MPa desorption), and graphene weight percentage.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the kinetic improvement is driven by the density of active sites rather than changes in the fundamental energy barrier.

Figures summary. XRD profiles for phase identification; SEM/EBSD/TEM images showing grain refinement and graphene distribution; DSC/TGA data for thermal analysis; and Kissinger plots showing changes in the frequency factor.

Paper structure. Introduction of the kinetic bottleneck; experimental synthesis via HPT; microstructural characterization (XRD, SEM, TEM); hydrogen storage kinetic measurements; kinetic modeling and Kissinger analysis; and discussion of the mechanism shift from 3D reaction to 2D diffusion.

Abstract

A strategy to overcome sluggish hydrogenation/dehydrogenation of magnesium is demonstrated by creating magnesium-graphene interphase boundaries via high-pressure torsion (HPT). HPT reduces the grain size of pure magnesium from 1 mm to 850 nm, with 70% of grain boundaries having high misorientation angles. Graphene addition leads to even finer grain sizes of 10-500 nm with a bimodal morphology. The magnesium-graphene composites exhibit superior kinetics at 623 K while maintaining high air resistance. Kinetic modeling reveals that the rate-controlling mechanism transits from interfacial reaction in coarse-grained magnesium to atomic diffusion in magnesium-graphene nanocomposites. Kissinger analysis shows that the activation energy for hydrogen desorption remains unchanged at 145 +/- 2 kJ/mol, regardless of the presence of grain or interphase boundaries. However, the frequency factor (number of successful attempts to overcome the activation energy) increases with the generation of interfaces, which serve as sites for hydrogen diffusion and heterogeneous metal/hydride nucleation. These findings highlight the impact of interphase boundary engineering via severe plastic deformation for enhancing the kinetics and air resistance of hydrogen storage materials.

Magnetization-dependent and stacking-tunable Edelstein effect in two-dimensional magnet 2H-VTe2

Authors: Weiyi Pan, Jaroslav Fabian
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13763v1

Summary. This paper explores how the Edelstein effect in the 2D magnet 2H-VTe2 can be tuned by rotating magnetization or changing the stacking configuration of bilayers. It identifies a mechanism for nonvolatile control of spin-orbit torque through interlayer sliding. These findings suggest 2H-MX2 materials are promising for high-efficiency, multifunctional spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the Edelstein effect (current-induced spin accumulation) in 2D magnetic systems can be controlled via magnetization orientation and crystal symmetry (stacking configuration).

Main result. The study demonstrates that in 2H-VTe2, the Edelstein effect is magnetization-dependent and that additional spin components can be reversibly switched by changing the stacking from AB to BA via interlayer sliding.

Method. First-principles calculations using Quantum Espresso, Wannier90, and WannierTools, with the Edelstein response tensor evaluated using the Kubo formula via the Linres code.

Model / system. The system is the 2D in-plane ferromagnetic semiconductor 2H-VTe2, analyzed in both monolayer (D3h symmetry) and bilayer (C3v symmetry) configurations with AB and BA stacking.

Key observables. Edelstein response tensor (chi), spin accumulation components (delta S_x, delta S_y, delta S_z), and time-reversal-even/odd contributions.

Important parameters / regimes. Fermi energy (Ef), magnetization angle (phi), electric field (E), and Hubbard U = 1 eV.

Assumptions / limitations. The reference electric field of 1 V/A used in calculations is noted as difficult to realize experimentally; calculations assume a specific thickness for monolayer and bilayer.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows atomic structure and band structure; Figures 2-3 show symmetry-allowed spin components for monolayers; Figures 4-6 show bilayer structures and stacking-dependent spin accumulation; Figures 7-11 show k-resolved spin accumulation and angular dependence.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of spintronic efficiency, presents the structural and electronic properties of 2H-VTe2, analyzes the monolayer symmetry and spin response, extends the analysis to bilayer stacking and its tunability, and concludes with the generalizability of the findings to other 2H-MX2 materials.

Abstract

The Edelstein effect in magnetic systems enables magnetization switching via the coupling between current-induced spin accumulation and intrinsic magnetic order, and is therefore highly promising for next-generation spintronic devices. Realizing and manipulating the Edelstein effect in two-dimensional (2D) magnetic systems is particularly desirable for achieving high-efficiency and multifunctional spintronic applications. In this work, based on first-principles calculations and symmetry analysis, we demonstrate that the Edelstein effect can intrinsically arise in the 2D in-plane ferromagnetic semiconductor 2H-VTe2, with its behavior strongly dependent on the magnetization orientation. For monolayer 2H-VTe2 with D3h crystal symmetry, under an applied current along the +x direction, only the time-reversal-even z component and the time-reversal-odd y(x) component of the spin accumulation are allowed when the magnetization is aligned along +x (+y). For ferromagnetic bilayer 2H-VTe2 in AB or BA stacking, where the crystal symmetry is reduced to C3v, additional spin components emerge with the presence of in-plane magnetization. Specifically, for magnetization along +x (+y), besides dSz_even and dSy_odd (dSz_even and dSx_odd), extra components such as dSy_even and dSz_odd (dSy_even) become allowed. Notably, these additional components can be reversibly switched by changing the stacking configuration from AB to BA via interlayer sliding. Our results not only deepen the understanding of current-induced spin accumulation in 2D magnetic systems from both symmetry and first-principles perspectives, but also identify 2H-MX2 materials as a promising platform for realizing intrinsic and tunable Edelstein effects in high-efficiency spin-orbit torque devices.

Abstract

This work presents a systematic study of the magnetocaloric effect in the nanostructured perovskite series $La_{0.6}Sr_{0.4}Fe_{1-x}Co_{x}O_3$ (x = 0, 0.2, 0.5, 0.8, and 1.0), synthesized by a pore-wetting method using polymeric membranes with pore diameters of 200 nm and 800 nm. All samples were calcined at 1000°C. Structural characterization was made by X-ray diffraction and confirmed the formation of a single-phase perovskite with distorted rhombohedral symmetry, without detectable secondary phases. We observed significant influence of substitution of Fe by Co on the morphology, as the analysis by scanning electron microscopy revealed a clear evolution from smaller to larger particles and from thin to thicker nanotubes, as the Co content increased. Magnetic measurements showed that the cationic substitution enhances ferromagnetic coupling, increasing both the saturation magnetization (MS) and the Curie temperature (TC). The magnetocaloric properties, determined through the Maxwell relations, exhibit a maximum entropy change of 1.13 J/(kg K) under an applied field of 3 T for the sample with x = 1. These results demonstrate that the combination of Co doping and controlled nanostructuring effectively optimizes the magnetocaloric response.

Summary. This paper reveals that the complex variety of charge density wave phases in monolayer VSe2 is not a collection of isolated states, but a continuous cascade of structural transitions driven by phonon instabilities. By tracking how different unstable modes lead to the same stable ground state, the authors provide a systematic framework for understanding hierarchical phase formation in 2D materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the complex, hierarchical structural evolution and the causal relationships between competing Charge Density Wave (CDW) phases in monolayer 1T-VSe2.

Main result. The study identifies a phonon-driven cascade mechanism where multiple unstable wave vectors drive sequential symmetry-lowering distortions that eventually converge toward a common, low-energy stable configuration.

Method. First-principles Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations using the OpenMX package combined with an iterative phonon-driven relaxation algorithm to identify subsequent instabilities.

Model / system. Monolayer 1T-VSe2, a two-dimensional transition-metal dichalcogenide (TMD) undergoing structural phase transitions.

Key observables. Phonon dispersion/spectra, imaginary-frequency phonon modes, CDW ordering vectors (Q-vectors), and total energy changes.

Important parameters / regimes. Wave vectors QA, QB, and QC; supercell sizes; energy reduction per atom (meV/atom).

Assumptions / limitations. The study relies on the accuracy of the GGA-PBE exchange-correlation functional and the finite-displacement method for phonon calculations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the phonon-driven cascade including phonon dispersions and lattice distortions; Figure 2 displays the phonon dispersion of the pristine phase along high-symmetry paths.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of CDW hierarchy, describes the DFT and iterative phonon analysis methodology, presents the identification of first-generation and second-generation CDW phases, demonstrates the convergence of different pathways to a common ground state, and concludes with a unified framework for CDW formation.

Abstract

Charge density wave (CDW) formation in two-dimensional materials is governed by complex competing lattice instabilities that remain incompletely understood. Here, we investigate the structural evolution of monolayer $\mathrm{1T-VSe}_{2}$ using first-principles electronic and phonon calculations. The pristine phase exhibits several imaginary-frequency phonon modes associated with dominant instability wave vectors $\mathrm{Q}_{CDW}$, which generate the first-generation CDW phases. Subsequent phonon analyses reveal that several of these intermediate structures remain dynamically unstable and undergo further symmetry-lowering distortions into larger superstructures. Through iterative phonon-driven relaxations, we identify multiple transformation pathways that converge toward the same low-energy $2\sqrt{3}\times4$ CDW configuration. Although these pathways originate from distinct intermediate CDW states, they ultimately reach nearly degenerate energetically stable phases, demonstrating that different phonon-driven routes can lead to the same ground-state configuration. The results establish a unified phonon-driven cascade mechanism for hierarchical CDW formation in monolayer $\mathrm{1T-VSe}_{2}$ and provide a systematic framework for understanding competing ordered phases in low-dimensional quantum materials.

Nodal Topological Superconductivity Driven by Crystalline Antiunitary Symmetry in Altermagnets

Summary. This paper demonstrates how the unique symmetry of altermagnets can naturally drive nodal topological superconductivity. It identifies specific phases hosting Majorana flat bands and edge states that are robust even when certain symmetries are spontaneously broken. The study provides specific tunneling signatures to help experimentalists identify these phases in candidate materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of realizing intrinsic topological superconductivity without relying on engineered platforms, strong spin-orbit coupling, or fine-tuned symmetry breaking.

Main result. The discovery of a symmetry-driven mechanism in altermagnets where crystalline antiunitary symmetry forces spin-singlet and spin-triplet mixing, leading to robust nodal-point and nodal-loop topological superconducting phases.

Method. Group theory (projection operators and Wigner test) for pairing classification, solving the linearized gap equation using Matsubara Green's functions, and topological analysis via winding numbers and Z2 invariants.

Model / system. A two-sublattice square lattice model of fourfold rotational collinear altermagnets featuring momentum-dependent spin splitting and crystalline antiunitary symmetry (T C4z).

Key observables. Superconducting transition temperature (Tc), Majorana flat bands (MFBs), chiral Majorana edge states, and tunneling conductance signatures in altermagnet-half-metal junctions.

Important parameters / regimes. Hopping parameters (t1-t5), exchange coupling (J), chemical potential (mu), interaction strengths (V0, V1), and the gap scale (D0 = 0.1t1).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the regime of fourfold rotational collinear altermagnets and assumes the validity of the linearized gap equation approach.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the lattice and spin-split band structure; Fig 2 presents the phase diagram of superconducting instabilities; Fig 3 illustrates edge states and phase winding; Fig 4 shows gap distributions and nodal loop spectra.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the altermagnetic system, applies group theory to classify pairing channels, solves the gap equation to find instabilities, analyzes the resulting topological phases (nodal points and loops), and concludes with proposed experimental tunneling signatures.

Abstract

Topological superconductivity hosts protected quasiparticles and is central to topological quantum computation, yet its realization in intrinsic materials remains challenging and often relies on engineered platforms. Here we uncover a symmetry-constrained mechanism for nodal topological superconductivity in altermagnets. Focusing on fourfold rotational collinear altermagnets, we show that the native crystalline antiunitary symmetry $\mathcal{T}C_{4z}$ generically forbids pure spin-singlet pairing and selects pairing structures that admit Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) Hamiltonians with emergent chiral symmetries. These symmetries further give rise to robust nodal topological phases over broad parameter regimes, including a nodal-point phase hosting Majorana flat bands (MFBs) and two distinct nodal-loop phases with chiral Majorana edge states. Notably, the nodal structure persists even after spontaneous breaking of the antiunitary symmetry, indicating that the topology originates from symmetry-constrained pairing rather than direct symmetry protection. Finally, we propose tunneling signatures that can distinguish these nodal phases and probe symmetry breaking experimentally.

OpenAaaS: An Open Agent-as-a-Service Framework for Distributed Materials-Informatics Research

Authors: Peng Kang, Bixuan Li, Xiaoya Huang, Shuo Shi, Weiqiao Zhou, Zhen Li, Yu Liu, Lei Zheng

Summary. The paper introduces OpenAaaS, a framework that allows researchers to use AI agents to query and analyze materials data located at different institutions without moving the raw data. This approach preserves data privacy and solves the bandwidth bottleneck of transferring massive datasets, enabling a secure, interconnected network for automated materials discovery.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The 'last mile' problem in materials informatics: the lack of organizational infrastructure to securely integrate specialized, proprietary, or large-scale materials data across institutional boundaries without compromising data sovereignty.

Main result. Model / system. An open-source distributed framework for materials informatics, featuring a Master Agent layer for task orchestration, a Rust-based Network Hub for routing, and localized Agent Cores for executing tasks near proprietary datasets.

Key observables. AlphaAgent performance scores (4.66/5.0 on analytical questions), data transfer reduction (7 orders of magnitude smaller than raw database size), and alloy ductility improvements.

Important parameters / regimes. 17.4 TB database scale, 194-dimensional descriptor vectors, 5.4 x 10^10 candidate compositions, and 50 MB file relay limit.

Abstract

The Materials Genome Initiative catalyzed the proliferation of centralized platforms--SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS--that aggregate computational and experimental resources for accelerated materials discovery. In parallel, breakthroughs in large language models (LLMs) and autonomous agents have created powerful new reasoning capabilities for scientific research. Yet a critical "last mile" problem remains: while we possess world-class models and vast repositories of materials data, we lack the organizational infrastructure to compose these capabilities securely across institutional boundaries. The development of structural and functional materials for harsh service environments--high-temperature alloys, radiation resistant steels, corrosion-resistant coatings--remains characterized by long-term iteration, mechanistic complexity, and high domain expertise--demands that exceed both monolithic agent systems and traditional centralized platforms. To address this gap we propose OpenAaaS, an open-source hierarchical and distributed Agent-as-a-Service framework that enables organized multi-agent collaboration for intelligent materials design. OpenAaaS is built on a single foundational principle: code flows, data stays still. A Master Agent plans and decomposes complex research tasks without requiring direct access to subordinate agents' managed data and computational resources. Sub-agents, deployed as near-data execution nodes, retain full sovereignty over local datasets, proprietary algorithms, and specialized hardware. Overall, these results demonstrate the effectiveness of Naph as a UV emission enhancer and highlight Naph-doped PVT as a promising candidate for compact and radiation-resistant scintillation detectors.

Abstract

Spatial variations of the valley splitting in a quantum well present a key challenge for conveyor-mode shuttling of electron spins in Si/SiGe, giving rise to Landau-Zener-like excitations that cause leakage outside the qubit subspace. Here, we propose an unconventional two-electron qubit encoding, based on valley-singlet states, that is largely immune to Landau-Zener leakage processes. In contrast to single-electron spins, the shuttling fidelity actually improves for small valley splittings, in this case. We show that high fidelities can be achieved without applying any special procedures, such as fine-tuning of the shuttling path.

Abstract

Natural hyperbolic materials offer a powerful platform for light-matter interactions by supporting highly anisotropic electromagnetic modes without the need for artificial patterning. In this work, we experimentally demonstrate that the nodal-line semimetal PbTaSe$_2$ exhibits robust hyperbolic optical behavior in the visible to near-infrared spectral range, which arises intrinsically from its anisotropic electronic structure and layered crystal symmetry. By combining first-principles calculations, ellipsometry, Drude-Lorentz modeling, and reflectance measurements, we establish a consistent experimental and theoretical picture of bulk hyperbolicity in this material. This hyperbolicity is of plasmonic origin and is characterized by a competitive quality factor ($Q_\mathrm{max} \approx 2.8$) and a very large anisotropy parameter ($|R| \approx 231$) at 0.78 eV.
