Quasilinear evolution versus von Neumann selective measurement

Authors: Jakub Rembieliński, Karol Ławniczak
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13756v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 quantum measurements 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper replaces the instantaneous von Neumann collapse with a continuous quasilinear evolution that describes state reduction as a smooth dynamical process. The proposed model preserves fundamental quantum principles like the no-signaling principle and the Born rule while identifying specific parameter regimes where the model might deviate from standard theory, offering potential experimental tests.

Why it may be interesting. It offers a novel way to unify unitary evolution with measurement-induced reduction through a continuous nonlinear dynamical process, which is highly relevant to the foundations of open quantum systems and measurement theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Addressing the conceptual issues of the von Neumann projection postulate, specifically the instantaneous and discontinuous nature of state collapse.

Main result. The authors propose a quasilinear evolution that provides a continuous, deterministic description of state reduction that is consistent with the no-signaling principle and the Born rule.

Method. The study uses a nonlinear generalization of the von Neumann equation, employing Bloch-vector representation, SL(2,C) group invariants, and numerical simulations of Riccati-type equations.

Model / system. A two-level quantum system (qubit) undergoing evolution driven by a time-dependent potential, with specific applications to the Stern-Gerlach experiment.

Key observables. Spin projection (observable Omega), Bloch vector trajectories, and the rate of change of the state vector.

Important parameters / regimes. The driving potential magnitude (g0), the orientation angle (Theta), and the Casimir invariants (C1, C2) of the evolution.

Assumptions / limitations. The validity of the model relies on the preservation of ensemble equivalence to ensure consistency with Einstein causality.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the parameter space as a tetrahedron, the evolution of Bloch vectors under different potentials (Morse and Stern-Gerlach), and the rate of state change in various regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the quasilinear framework, proves its consistency with no-signaling and the Born rule, analyzes the dynamics of two-level systems using geometric invariants, explores structural instability and experimental testability, and applies the model to the Stern-Gerlach experiment.

Abstract

In this article, we introduce a new form of quantum selective measurement in which the von Neumann projection postulate is replaced by quasilinear evolution, governed by a nonlinear generalization of the von Neumann equation. We demonstrate that this equation preserves the equivalence of quantum ensembles and, consequently, satisfies the no-signalling principle, ensuring consistency with both quantum mechanics and Einstein causality. Our approach eliminates the need for instantaneous, discontinuous state collapse and provides a unified description of the postmeasurement quantum state reduction as a form of quantum state evolution. Notably, it does not require invoking concepts such as the quantum state assigned to a classical apparatus. At the same time, the stochastic character of selective measurement and the Born rule remain unchanged. We present several numerical solutions of the evolution equation for quasilinear selective measurement in two-level quantum systems and compare them with the standard von Neumann projection. The results demonstrate agreement between the two measurement schemes in their fundamental properties. Furthermore, we investigate phenomena associated with the structural instability of the evolution equation and identify very narrow parameter regions in which the outcomes deviate from those predicted by the von Neumann projection. These regions may offer opportunities to test the proposed approach experimentally. Finally, using specific analytical solutions, we discuss the Stern-Gerlach experiment within the framework of quasilinear measurement.

Summary. The paper introduces QCIVET, a framework designed to verify that the quantum stages of a hybrid computing pipeline behave according to their intended specifications. It bridges the gap between classical supply-chain security and quantum hardware noise by using observable-based contracts to detect malicious or erroneous circuit substitutions. The framework was successfully validated on real IBM quantum hardware, demonstrating its ability to catch 'sneaky' attacks that pass simple verification tests.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Ensuring the integrity and auditability of hybrid quantum-classical pipelines against adversarial threats like specification tampering, hardware-level poisoning, and 'sneaky' circuit substitutions that bypass classical verification.

Main result. The QCIVET framework successfully detects 'sneaky-subtype' attacks and hardware drift on real IBM Heron processors, providing a low-latency, hardware-validated runtime audit mechanism.

Method. A dual-layer auditing approach using hash-chained audit trails for structural integrity and calibrated observable-deviation tests based on Liskov-Wing behavioral subtyping for semantic integrity.

Model / system. Hybrid quantum-classical pipelines involving quantum subroutines (e.g., VQE, quantum kernels) executed on IBM Quantum processors, specifically the ibm_fez (Heron r2) and Eagle r3 architectures.

Key observables. Self-adjoint operators (Pauli expectation values) used for observable-deviation contracts and informationally complete observable families.

Important parameters / regimes. Error tolerance threshold (epsilon), diamond-norm distance between quantum channels, and classical commit latency (sub-millisecond).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the adversary cannot forge external anchors or SHA-256 hashes, and that the observable family used for verification is sufficiently informationally complete.

Figures summary. Figures include a landscape map of quantum software contracts, a calibration window showing noise floor vs. logical deviation, and visualizations of various hash-chain tampering scenarios.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the integrity gap in hybrid pipelines, proposes the QCIVET framework, provides formal proofs for soundness and completeness, demonstrates experimental validation on real IBM hardware across three application domains (VQE, fraud detection, cloud auditing), and compares the framework against existing classical and quantum security literature.

Abstract

Hybrid quantum--classical pipelines increasingly support applications such as drug discovery, fraud detection, and cloud quantum processing unit (QPU) auditing, yet existing integrity-verification methods remain largely classical and fail to capture quantum-stage behaviour. We propose QCIVET, a contract-based integrity-verification framework that models a hybrid pipeline as a sequence of stages with explicit specifications and audits it at both syntactic and semantic levels. Syntactic integrity is enforced through a hash-chained audit trail with optional external anchoring, while semantic integrity at quantum stages is verified using a calibrated observable-deviation test grounded in the behavioural-subtyping discipline of Liskov and Wing. We prove soundness under the diamond-norm distance between quantum channels, conditional completeness for informationally complete observable families, and compositionality under inheritance chains. We further identify a class of Z-only-sneaky overrides that evade weak single-Pauli contracts but are exposed by multi-Pauli contracts. The framework is evaluated under calibration-derived noise models from IBM Quantum Eagle r3 and Heron r2 processors, and the subtype-separation protocol is validated end-to-end on a real ibm_fez (Heron r2) processor. QCIVET is instantiated on three representative applications: variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) for drug discovery, quantum-assisted fraud detection, and customer-side auditing of cloud QPU services. The reference implementation, including a real-time verification engine with sub-millisecond per-stage commit latency, is released as open source.

A Quantum Multi-Programming Framework to Maximize Quantum Resources for the LUCJ Ansatz

Summary. The paper introduces a quantum multi-programming framework designed to run multiple quantum chemistry circuits simultaneously on a single QPU to increase throughput. By using a robust post-processing protocol (SQD/ext-SQD), the authors demonstrate that the accuracy of molecular energy estimations can be maintained despite the increased hardware cross-talk associated with parallel execution.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. How to maximize quantum hardware throughput on cloud platforms via multi-programming without allowing hardware cross-talk to degrade the accuracy of quantum chemistry simulations.

Main result. The proposed multi-programming framework effectively mitigates cross-talk-induced noise, achieving energy deviations from the classical reference as low as 0.001 kcal/mol at the final SQD iteration.

Method. Development of a novel parallel experiment class for the LUCJ ansatz using Qiskit, combined with Sample-based Quantum Diagonalization (SQD) and its extended version (ext-SQD) for error mitigation.

Model / system. Simulated ethanol molecules in two configurations: equilibrium (EtOHEq) and a stretched O-H bond (EtOH1.2), executed on the IBM Quantum ibm_boston QPU.

Key observables. Ground-state energy deviation relative to the Heat-bath Configuration Interaction (HCI) reference.

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit layouts (serial vs. parallel), number of ancilla qubits (2 to 5), and SQD sample sizes (1K, 3K, and 5K).

Assumptions / limitations. Manual qubit partitioning was used; circuits are kept at a single repetition; the HCI method is assumed to be a reliable ground-truth reference.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the multi-programming workflow; Figure 2 illustrates different qubit layouts with buffers; Figure 3 displays the distribution of energy deviations across layouts and modes; Table 1 and 2 summarize energy errors at different SQD stages and sample sizes.

Paper structure. Introduction to resource management challenges, description of the multi-programming framework and LUCJ ansatz, experimental setup on IBM hardware, results comparing serial vs. parallel execution, and an analysis of sampling convergence and ancilla scaling.

Abstract

In the context of quantum computing, efficient resource management is crucial for optimizing throughput on cloud-based platforms and maximizing hardware utilization. In the present work, we propose an approach to tackle quantum chemistry problems via quantum multi-programming of the Local Unitary Cluster Jastrow (LUCJ) ansätze. The ground-state energy of the molecular system is obtained via Sample-based quantum diagonalization (SQD), further refined by its extended version (ext-SQD). Building upon the Qiskit Experiments package, which already supports parallel execution functionality for general tasks, we developed a novel parallel experiment class tailored for quantum chemistry problems. Cross-talk is a known issue in the multi-programming frameworks and can corrupt the ground-energy estimation of the simulated systems. To assess its impact within our approach, we simulated two conformations of the ethanol molecule: one at the equilibrium state (EtOH$_{Eq}$), and one with the O-H bond stretched to 1.2 ${Å}$ (EtOH$_{1.2}$). We defined three different layouts that we executed in a randomized fashion, alternating serial and parallel execution within 10 independent replicates. The single modality of each circuit was kept as a baseline to evaluate the effect of cross-talk induced by quantum multi-programming. The energies obtained at the first-, last- and ext-SQD iteration were compared to the classical Heat-bath Configuration Interaction (HCI) reference. Our findings highlight the viability of a quantum multi-programming workflow for quantum chemistry as the robust post-processing protocol effectively mitigates possible cross-talk induced noise. At the final step of the configuration recovery process, the energy difference relative to the HCI reference is negligible, within 0.001 kcal/mol.

Summary. This paper reports the first experimental realization of an aperiodic polariton lattice using the recently discovered 'einstein' monotile. By sculpting these tiles in a GaAs microcavity, the authors demonstrate that the unique geometry leads to distinct spectral features and a complex phase-locking pattern unlike any periodic or conventional quasicrystalline system.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it demonstrates how a programmable, driven-dissipative artificial material can use aperiodicity to engineer complex, non-trivial synchronization and spectral responses.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to realize and investigate the physical properties of a newly discovered class of aperiodic quasicrystals composed of a single 'einstein' (hat) monotile, specifically focusing on how this unique geometry affects the propagation of coherent excitations and phase synchronization.

Main result. The researchers successfully imprinted 'einstein' monotile structures in an inorganic microcavity, observing six-fold symmetric Bragg peaks, Dirac-like spectral features, and a complex phase-locking pattern that is distinct from both periodic and Penrose quasicrystalline tilings.

Method. The study uses optical sculpting via a Spatial Light Modulator (SLM) to create the tiling, combined with energy-resolved momentum-space tomography and homodyne interferometry for phase reconstruction.

Model / system. The system is an inorganic GaAs-based microcavity hosting nonresonantly excited exciton-polariton condensates, modeled by the 2D Gross-Pitaevskii equation coupled to a reservoir rate equation.

Key observables. Bragg peaks, Dirac-like spectral fingerprints, real-space polariton density, and relative phases of the condensates.

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice constant (D) ranging from 22.6 to 27.2 micrometers, number of tiles (M = 1, 4, 13), and pump power relative to threshold (P approx 1.15 P_thr).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the validity of the driven-dissipative Gross-Pitaevskii framework for describing the polariton dynamics in the microcavity.

Figures summary. Fig 1 compares different tiling geometries and shows real/reciprocal space PL for various tile numbers; Fig 2 presents 3D energy tomography and energy-resolved slices; Fig 3 compares phase locking patterns across periodic, Penrose, and 'einstein' lattices.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of the 'einstein' monotile, describes the experimental setup and optical sculpting technique, presents the spectral and spatial results of the aperiodic tiling, compares the phase synchronization of the monotile to other lattices, and concludes with numerical validation.

Abstract

A plethora of unconventional localization phenomena and fractal features of linear spectrum observed in quasiperiodic structures have been accompanied by a long-standing quest for the geometrical elements and structures that permit tilings of the plane, but only in a non-periodic manner. Until 2024, it was believed that such quasiperiodic structures, or quasicrystals, could only be composed of at least two different tiles. Surprisingly, a newly discovered class of quasicrystals requires only one elementary monotile. However, its physical realization and study of propagating coherent excitations in this novel setting remained elusive. Here we optically sculpt aperiodic quasicrystals composed of "einstein" monotiles in an inorganic microcavity and observe nontrivial relative phases of the exciton-polariton condensates nonresonantly excited at the vertices of each monotile. Utilizing energy-resolved tomography in momentum-space, we reveal the formation of distinct Bragg peaks with six-fold symmetry and Dirac-like spectral fingerprints, intrinsic to the underlying graphene-like structure, while interferometric phase reconstruction shows a nontrivial synchronization pattern distinct from both periodic triangular lattices and Penrose quasicrystals. Our work demonstrates that monotiles can be converted into a programmable driven-dissipative artificial material, where long-range coherence coexists with enforced geometric aperiodicity, producing synchronization and spectral responses distinct from both periodic and conventional quasicrystalline tilings.

Summary. This paper introduces a scalable deep-learning method called NESIF to quantify directed information flow along individual stochastic trajectories. It overcomes the computational bottlenecks of traditional measures, allowing for the detection of hidden cooperative structures in complex systems like oscillators and biological cells. Abstract

Stochastic information flow (SIF) quantifies information flow at the trajectory level, overcoming the limitations of conventional symmetric, ensemble-averaged measures. Its applications to an exactly solvable two-particle model, Kuramoto oscillators, and empirical trajectories of interacting motile cells demonstrate the utility of SIF as a data-driven indicator of cooperative structures.

Summary. This paper demonstrates how a nonlinear system with memory can be used to trap light in a 'ghost state' near a bifurcation point. By using the thermal relaxation of olive oil, the authors show that trajectories can be steered into phase-space bottlenecks, creating extremely long-lived intensity plateaus.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a way to experimentally access and control long-lived transient states in non-equilibrium systems using memory-induced steering, which is highly relevant to open quantum systems and dissipative phase transitions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of experimentally observing and accessing 'ghost states'—long-lived, non-stationary bottlenecks in phase space that occur near a saddle-node bifurcation.

Main result. The researchers identified that a nonlinear response with memory can steer relaxation pathways into the bottleneck, enabling the observation of ghost states with lifetimes exceeding the cavity photon lifetime by over ten orders of magnitude.

Method. The study combines experimental measurements in a microcavity with numerical simulations using the xSPDE toolbox and an overdamped Langevin equation approach.

Model / system. A plano-concave Fabry-Perot microcavity filled with olive oil, which provides a thermo-optical nonlinearity with a time-delayed (memory) response, driven by a continuous-wave laser.

Key observables. Cavity transmission/intensity, plateau duration (ghost lifetime), and phase-space velocity.

Important parameters / regimes. Memory time (tau), laser-cavity detuning (Delta), dissipation rate (Gamma), and nonlinearity strength (U).

Assumptions / limitations. The simulations use a simplified memory time shorter than the experimental value to manage computational costs, and the 1D effective potential approximation assumes dynamics are primarily along the nullcline.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows experimental and simulated transmission plateaus; Fig 2 displays scaling laws for plateau duration; Fig 3 illustrates relaxation trajectories in the complex plane; Fig 6 provides a parameter space map of the bifurcation.

Paper structure. Introduction to ghost states and the bottleneck problem, description of the experimental microcavity setup, presentation of the stochastic model and simulations, analysis of scaling laws and memory effects, and discussion of the implications for non-stationary states.

Abstract

We report the observation of a long-lived non-stationary state of light in a single-mode optical cavity. The observed state is a ghost of a saddle-node bifurcation which creates a bottleneck in phase space. While such ghosts are known to exist, accessing them is challenging because it requires a mechanism that steers the relaxation pathway away from the true attractor and into the bottleneck where the ghost emerges. Here we identify such a mechanism, namely a nonlinear response with memory. Our experimental system leverages this mechanism, enabling us to observe ghost states with lifetimes exceeding the cavity photon lifetime by more than ten orders of magnitude, even in the presence of strong fluctuations. The ghost manifests as a plateau in the relaxation dynamics of the cavity transmission, reminiscent of prethermalization. We show how the ghost lifetime depends on the memory time and the distance to the bifurcation, and we observe signatures of scaling in the distribution of ghost lifetimes at fixed driving conditions. Our work establishes minimal conditions for realizing parametrically long-lived non-stationary states.

Summary. The paper introduces a universal algorithm for non-uniform cellular automata that works on both Euclidean and hyperbolic lattices. By using a lattice-deforming procedure, the authors successfully model complex physical phenomena such as subsystem symmetries and directed percolation in hyperbolic spaces.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and quantum information as it provides a new mathematical framework to study topological order and phase transitions in non-Euclidean geometries, which could impact the development of hyperbolic quantum error-correcting codes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Standard uniform cellular automata (CA) are incompatible with hyperbolic lattices due to their non-Abelian translation symmetry and exponential growth, making it difficult to model physical phenomena like subsystem symmetries and directed percolation on these geometries.

Main result. The authors develop a universal higher-order non-uniform cellular automata (NUCA) algorithm that applies to both Euclidean and hyperbolic lattices, enabling the generation of subsystem symmetry-protected topological (SSPT) states and the simulation of directed percolation.

Model / system. The study utilizes various models including linear NUCA, non-uniform Clifford quantum cellular automata (CQCA), and probabilistic NUCA applied to regular Euclidean and hyperbolic lattices (specifically {5,4} and {6,6} lattices).

Key observables. Subsystem symmetries (regular and irregular), subsystem symmetry-protected topological (SSPT) states, multi-point strange correlators, and directed percolation thresholds.

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice geometry (Schläfli symbols {p,q}), curvature, update rule order, and the probability parameter in probabilistic NUCA.

Assumptions / limitations. The algorithm assumes the ability to embed hyperbolic lattices into a Euclidean square lattice via a splitting method, which introduces geometric distortion that must be compensated for by the non-uniform rules.

Abstract

Lattice geometry profoundly shapes physical phenomena such as subsystem symmetry and directed percolation (DP). Among various lattice geometries, hyperbolic lattices are characterized by constant negative curvature and non-Abelian translation symmetry, offering a rich platform for investigating this geometry-physics interplay. However, the exponentially growing lattice size and nontrivial translation symmetry make approaches developed for Euclidean lattices incompatible, a limitation particularly evident in uniform cellular automata (CA). To resolve this, we develop a higher-order non-uniform cellular automata (NUCA) algorithm applicable to both translationally invariant regular Euclidean and hyperbolic lattices. In the algorithm, the non-uniform update rules incorporate nontrivial geometric data through a lattice-deforming procedure. We demonstrate the broad applicability of our algorithm to hyperbolic lattices through several applications on the hyperbolic $\{5,4\}$ lattice. By applying a linear NUCA, we generate subsystem symmetry-protected topological (SSPT) states and spontaneous subsystem symmetry-breaking states associated with regular or irregular subsystem symmetries unattainable on Euclidean lattices. We design the multi-point strange correlators to detect nontrivial SSPT states and derive a sufficient condition for non-Abelian translationally invariant NUCA-generated models. Furthermore, by generalizing the NUCA to non-uniform Clifford quantum cellular automata (CQCA), we generate subsystem symmetries of the hyperbolic cluster state, extending the established correspondence between translationally invariant CQCA and subsystem symmetries. Moreover, we simulate the DP process via a probabilistic NUCA that inherits the treelike structure of the lattice, and numerically estimate percolation thresholds and the phase diagram.

Emergence of information interference in stochastic systems with non-diagonal noise and switching environments

Authors: Andrea Marchetti, Daniel Maria Busiello, Giorgio Nicoletti
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13556v1

Summary. This paper explores how anisotropic noise and changing environments create 'information interference,' preventing the simple summation of individual physical contributions to a system's mutual information. By studying hydrodynamic and chemical systems, the authors demonstrate that these interplays can either enhance or mask statistical dependencies. This establishes a new way to quantify how complex environments shape information in stochastic processes.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a general information-theoretic perspective on how correlated noise and environmental fluctuations fundamentally alter the statistical dependencies and information flow in non-equilibrium systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how non-diagonal noise (anisotropy) and stochastically switching environments lead to 'information interference,' where total mutual information cannot be decomposed into simple additive contributions from deterministic interactions, noise, and environmental changes.

Main result. The authors identify two sources of interference: a static term from the interplay between deterministic coupling and noise anisotropy, and a dynamic term from the interplay between internal processes and environmental switches. They also show that hydrodynamic interactions can either mask or enhance information depending on the degree of non-reciprocity.

Method. The study uses an information-theoretic framework based on Mutual Information (MI) and Kullback-Leibler divergence applied to multivariate Ornstein-Uhlenbeck processes, utilizing matrix decomposition and timescale separation (fast/slow-jump limits).

Model / system. The framework is applied to linearized stochastic systems, specifically including hydrodynamically coupled Brownian particles (using the Rotne-Prager-Yamakawa tensor) and fuel-driven chemical reaction networks modeled via the Linear Noise Approximation.

Key observables. Mutual Information (MI), Information Interference (static and dynamic terms), and entropy of the jump process.

Important parameters / regimes. Deterministic coupling strength (g), noise anisotropy (a), non-reciprocity (beta), environmental switching rates (tau_jumps), and chemical potential (delta_mu).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to linearized stochastic systems with Gaussian steady-state solutions and assumes the noise covariance matrix is positive definite.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows MI estimates vs. the ratio of internal to environmental timescales; Figure 2 illustrates the setup of particles with hydrodynamic coupling and non-reciprocal forces; Figure 3 depicts a chemical reaction cycle with driving forces.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of information interference, develops a mathematical framework for decomposing MI in the presence of non-diagonal noise and switching environments, and then applies this framework to three distinct physical cases: temperature switching, hydrodynamic interactions, and chemical reaction networks.

Abstract

Stochastic forces in natural systems are rarely isotropic. From hydrodynamically coupled colloids to chemical reaction networks, noise contributions are inherently correlated. Together with internal interactions and changing environments, they shape the dependencies between the degrees of freedom of real-world systems, as quantified by their mutual information. In this work, we focus on linearized stochastic systems with both non-diagonal noise matrices and stochastically switching environments. We study how their presence leads to the emergence of information interference, so that the total mutual information cannot be decomposed as the sum of the contributions from deterministic interactions, noise anisotropy, and environmental switching alone. We identify two distinct sources of information interference: a static term, arising from the simultaneous presence of deterministic coupling and noise anisotropy; and a dynamic term, emerging from the interplay between internal processes and environmental switches. We then apply this framework to different physical systems. In the presence of switching temperatures, the mutual information disentangles exactly into internal and environmental contributions. When the noise anisotropy arises instead from hydrodynamic interactions, we find that the presence of a shared fluid can either mask or enhance the information stemming from a non-conservative force depending on its degree of non-reciprocity. Finally, in a fuel-driven chemical reaction network, we show that information interference is controlled by the non-equilibrium driving. These results establish a general information-theoretic perspective on how anisotropic noise and environmental variability shape statistical dependencies in stochastic systems.

Summary. This paper proposes that the emission of gravitons (Bremsstrahlung) during particle motion provides a microscopic origin for wave function collapse. Main problem. Investigating whether the wave function collapse described by phenomenological models (like CSL or Diósi-Penrose) can be derived from a microscopic quantum field-theoretic framework, specifically through graviton-induced decoherence.

Abstract

The protection of superconducting qubits from certain noise sources often comes at the cost of increased sensitivity to other decoherence channels. Here, we explore a route to avoid this tradeoff by encoding quantum information in quantum states of a transmon entangled with the spin of a trapped Andreev quasiparticle. We term such devices spinmons. We lift the spinmon Kramers degeneracy by introducing a Zeeman field and develop two routes for full qubit control via electrostatic gates and an AC flux drive, providing multiple directions for experimental implementations. Finally, we compute coherence times and verify the qubit robustness against flux and charge noise sources.

Abstract

Multivariate quantum signal processing (M-QSP) has recently been shown to be applicable for non-Hermitian Hamiltonian simulation, opening several problems regarding the optimization landscape, angle-finding, and constant-factor analysis. We resolve several of these problems here. We find the anti-Hermitian query complexity $d_I = Θ(\betaI T + \log(1/\varepsilon)/\log\log(1/\varepsilon))$ to be tight, established via Chebyshev coefficient bounds, modified Bessel function asymptotics, and Lambert~$W$ inversion. Fast-forwarding to $d_I = \mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\betaI T})$ is impossible in the bivariate polynomial model, though a linear state-dependent improvement to $d_I = \mathcal{O} β_{\mathrm{eff}} T + \log(1/\varepsilon)/\log\log(1/\varepsilon))$ is achievable. The optimization landscape of M-QSP admits spurious local minima, but a warm-start basin guarantee ensures the two-stage algorithm converges. CRC-exploiting block peeling reduces angle-finding from $\mathcal{O}(d^3)$ to $\mathcal{O}(d^2)$ classical operations, and optimized error allocation yields a leading constant of approximately~$2$ relative to the information-theoretic lower bound. A constant-ratio condition extends to non-identical signal operators, enabling time-dependent non-Hermitian simulation with query complexity $\mathcal{O}(\int_0^T(\alphaR(s) + \betaI(s))\,ds + \log(1/\varepsilon)/\log\log(1/\varepsilon))$. Block-encoding overhead $e^{-2\betaI T}$ holds across all function classes within the walk-operator oracle model, and dilational methods (Schrödingerization) achieve the walk-operator barrier. A precisely characterized direct-access construction achieves the intrinsic barrier $e^{-2ωT}$ (with $ω< \betaI$ for non-commuting Hamiltonians) on a restricted domain, though extension to the full bitorus remains open.
