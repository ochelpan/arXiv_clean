Terahertz detection within charge density wave state

Authors: Zhi Li
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12949v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Frenkel-Kontorova 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes using the collective electronic response of charge density wave materials to overcome the frequency limits of traditional THz detectors. It shows that applying a static electric field allows for continuous tuning of the resonance frequency and significantly boosts the nonlinear signal. This provides a theoretical foundation for developing high-performance, room-temperature THz optoelectronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Conventional semiconductor-based THz detectors suffer from performance degradation above 1 THz due to the Drude limit of free electrons. The paper seeks to determine if the collective electronic response of charge density wave (CDW) materials can provide a tunable and enhanced alternative.

Main result. The study demonstrates that a static bias electric field can continuously tune the THz resonant absorption frequency of CDW states from 0 to a high cutoff frequency and enhance the nonlinear rectification current by over an order of magnitude.

Method. A combined classical real-space and quantum momentum-space approach is used, involving the diagonalization of a Hamiltonian with a periodic modulation potential and solving the equation of motion for an electron under static and AC fields.

Model / system. The system consists of Charge Density Wave (CDW) materials, specifically Van der Waals layered transition metal dichalcogenides like 1T-TaSe2 or 1T-TaS2, modeled as an electron in a periodic modulation potential subject to static and THz-frequency electric fields.

Key observables. Resonant absorption frequency (omega_0), nonlinear conductivity, and nonlinear rectification current (shift current).

Important parameters / regimes. THz frequency range (0.1–15 THz), modulation potential (Vm approx -0.1 eV), critical depinning field (Eb,max approx pi x 10^8 V/m), and energy gap (1–100 meV).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a weak THz signal (small oscillating amplitude), uses an infinitesimal damping parameter for causality, and adopts the static electron mass.

Figures summary. Figure 2a shows the shift of the electron center position as a function of bias field; Figure 2b illustrates the tuning range of the resonance frequency; Figure 3a shows the normalized static displacement.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the limitations of the Drude model in semiconductors, presents a theoretical model for CDW response, derives the frequency tunability and current enhancement under a bias field, and concludes with the implications for next-generation THZ detectors.

Abstract

Terahertz (THz) technology enables multi-Tbps satellite communications, but conventional semiconductor detectors suffer from fundamental performance degradation above 1 THz due to the Drude limit of free electrons. Here, we theoretically demonstrate that charge density wave (CDW) materials offer a paradigm-shifting solution via their collective electronic response. We show that a static bias electric field can continuously tune the THz resonant absorption frequency of CDW states from 0 to cutoff frequency, and enhance the nonlinear rectification current by more than one order of magnitude. This unprecedented electric-field tunability makes CDW materials ideal candidates for next-generation ultrafast THz detectors working at room-temperature.

Whitham modulation equations for the regularized Boussinesq equation with cubic nonlinearity

Authors: Mark A. Hoefer, Anna Vainchtein
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12900v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper analyzes the stability of nonlinear waves in a Boussinesq model representing FPU-type lattices. It establishes a direct link between the mathematical loss of hyperbolicity in the Whitham modulation equations and the physical emergence of modulational instability in periodic waves.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the Whitham modulation equations for a regularized Boussinesq equation with cubic nonlinearity, specifically focusing on the relationship between the loss of hyperbolicity in the modulation equations and the onset of modulational instability in periodic traveling waves.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the loss of hyperbolicity (complex characteristic speeds) in the Whitham system directly corresponds to the onset of modulational instability in the underlying wave system, and they identify both long-wavelength and short-wavelength (superharmonic) instabilities.

Method. The study employs Whitham's averaged variational principle to derive modulation equations, the Floquet-Fourier-Hill method for spectral analysis of the linearized operator, and numerical simulations of initial value problems.

Model / system. A regularized Boussinesq equation acting as a dispersive, long-wave (quasicontinuum) approximation of the Fermi-Pasta-Ulam (FPU) lattice with a general cubic interaction force.

Key observables. Hyperbolicity (real vs. complex characteristic speeds), genuine nonlinearity, and modulational/superharmonic instability spectra.

Important parameters / regimes. Nonlinearity coefficients (alpha and beta), wave amplitude, mean strain, wavenumber, and frequency; regimes include solitary-wave, kink, and harmonic limits.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a slowly varying periodic traveling wave (Whitham ansatz) and a long-wave approximation of the discrete lattice.

Figures summary. Phase plane trajectories of periodic and solitary waves; parameter space plots showing regions of lost hyperbolicity and nonlinearity; and spectral plots showing the development of instabilities.

Paper structure. The paper first introduces the Boussinesq model and its relation to FPU lattices, then classifies periodic traveling wave solutions using Jacobi elliptic functions, derives the Whitham modulation equations via an averaged Lagrangian, analyzes the convexity and hyperbolicity of these equations, and finally verifies the link between loss of hyperbolicity and wave instability through spectral and dynamical simulations.

Abstract

A regularized Boussinesq equation is studied as a dispersive, long-wave (quasicontinuum) approximation of the Fermi-Pasta-Ulam lattice with a general cubic interaction force. Explicit periodic traveling wave solutions in terms of Jacobi elliptic functions are classified, and their solitary-wave, kink, and trigonometric limits are obtained. The Whitham modulation equations describing slow modulations of periodic traveling wave solutions are derived using an averaged variational principle. The convexity (strict hyperbolicity, genuine nonlinearity) of the resulting hydrodynamic-type equations is examined numerically in general and analytically in the solitary-wave and harmonic limits. In particular, the loss of hyperbolicity and the formation of complex conjugate characteristic speeds is shown to lead to modulational instability of periodic traveling waves. The onset of modulational instability is verified by numerical computations of linearized spectra for periodic traveling waves and initial value problems that also reveal additional short-wave instabilities.

Collision Dynamics of False-Vacuum Oscillons

Authors: J. G. F. Campos, N. S. Manton, Azadeh Mohammadi
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12633v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the collision of stable, oscillating field configurations called oscillons can trigger a phase transition in a metastable vacuum. It demonstrates that sufficiently energetic collisions can overcome a potential barrier to create new field structures, effectively initiating vacuum decay.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the collision dynamics of localized oscillons in (1+1)-dimensional scalar field theories and determines if these collisions can trigger a phase transition from a false vacuum to a true vacuum.

Main result. Oscillon collisions exhibit complex outcomes including reflection, crossing, and merging, with high-energy collisions capable of crossing the sphaleron barrier to create kink-antikink pairs and initiate vacuum decay. The inter-oscillon force decays exponentially with separation and is modulated by their relative phase.

Method. The authors use the Fodor et al. expansion for small-amplitude analytical solutions and perform numerical simulations using a Fourier spectral method for spatial derivatives and a fourth-order Runge-Kutta integrator.

Model / system. The system consists of (1+1)-dimensional scalar field theories featuring two classes of potentials: a 'normal' class with a positive quartic self-interaction and an 'inverted' class with a negative quartic term obtained via analytic continuation.

Key observables. Oscillon frequency, inter-oscillon force, collision outcomes (reflection, crossing, merging, vacuum decay), power spectrum, and sphaleron mass.

Important parameters / regimes. Collision velocity (v), relative phase between oscillons (delta), scaling/size parameter (s), and amplitude/width parameter (epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. The study relies on the assumption that the long-lived nature of oscillons can be captured via small-amplitude expansions and numerical integration, though the exact mathematical nature of oscillons remains partially understood.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the potential landscapes (normal and inverted), sphaleron profiles and stability potentials, the small oscillation spectrum, and spacetime field evolution showing various collision outcomes and quasi-fractal patterns.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalar field models, develops analytical solutions for small-amplitude oscillons, presents numerical results for single-oscillon and sphaleron stability, details the dynamics of two-oscillon collisions (including phase and velocity dependence), and concludes with implications for vacuum decay.

Abstract

We study the collision dynamics of localized oscillons in two classes of $(1+1)$-dimensional scalar field theories with metastable false vacua, a normal class with a positive quartic self-interaction term and an inverted class with a negative quartic term. We construct small-amplitude oscillon solutions around the false vacuum using the Fodor {\emph{et al.}} expansion, and show that the force between oscillons decays exponentially at large separation, with a strength modulated by their relative phase. Numerical simulations of two-oscillon collisions exhibit reflection, crossing, and formation of excited oscillons. Resonance windows occur, similar to those found in kink-antikink collisions. In the normal theory, if the oscillons have sufficient energy, the field can pass over a sphaleron barrier and evolve into a kink-antikink pair, initiating a phase transition to the true vacuum. We also simulate the collision of oscillons evolved from a slightly perturbed sphaleron.

From Film to Flakes: Electronic Properties and Magnetization Variations in Yttrium Iron Garnet

Authors: Pia M. Düring, Seema, Roman Hartmann, Sebastian Sailler, Michaela Lammel, Andrei Gloskovskii, Christoph Schlueter, Angelo Di Bernardo, Sebastian T. B. Goennenwein, Martina Müller
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13441v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper compares the electronic and magnetic properties of YIG thin films and sub-micron flakes. It reveals that the exfoliation process creates metallic iron components that reduce the overall magnetization of the flakes. These findings are crucial for designing reliable YIG-based spintronic and magnonic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the morphology of Yttrium Iron Garnet (YIG) changes from bulk-like thin films to sub-micron flakes, specifically regarding the impact of exfoliation on electronic structure and magnetic properties.

Main result. The study finds that mechanically exfoliated YIG flakes exhibit a significant reduction in magnetization and Kerr contrast compared to thin films, driven by a surface reconstruction that introduces metallic Fe0 components.

Method. The researchers used Hard X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (HAXPES) at different photon energies to probe oxidation states and Longitudinal Kerr Microscopy to measure magnetic hysteresis and domain patterns.

Model / system. The physical system consists of a 50 nm sputtered YIG thin film and sub-micron-sized YIG flakes exfoliated from a single crystal. A substitution model was used where a fraction of Fe3+ ions are replaced by Fe0 to simulate the observed magnetic reduction.

Key observables. Fe 2p, Y 3p, and O 1s core level spectra, Kerr rotation intensity, magnetic hysteresis loops, and saturation field.

Important parameters / regimes. Photon energies of 2.8 keV and 6 keV for depth-sensitive HAXPES; flake lateral dimensions of 5-20 micrometers squared; YIG thickness of 50 nm.

Assumptions / limitations. The YIG lattice does not undergo significant distortion during Fe0 substitution, and the Fe0 is assumed to be distributed uniformly across octahedral and tetrahedral sites.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Fe 2p, Y 3p, and O 1s core level spectra for both the film and the flake at different photon energies to highlight differences in oxidation states.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the importance of YIG in spintronics, describes the experimental setup for films and flakes, presents HAXPES results showing Fe0 presence in flakes, uses Kerr microscopy to quantify magnetization reduction, and concludes with a substitution model to explain the findings.

Abstract

Yttrium iron garnet (YIG) is a ferrimagnetic insulator valued for its high Curie temperature, very low magnetic damping, and ability to support long-range spin-wave transport. These qualities have established it as a cornerstone material in the field of spintronics and magnonics. Most studies on YIG so far have been focused on bulk crystals, thin films, and nanoparticles, including variants with substitutions at the yttrium or iron site. New morphologies such as sub-micron flakes have drawn interest recently as their geometry and mechanical flexibility might enable different device architectures. However, detailed investigations combining their electronic structure and magnetic behavior remain scarce. In this work, we present a comparative study of the electronic and magnetic properties of a bulk-like YIG film and a sub-micron-sized YIG flake. Our results highlight the distinct behavior that emerges in sub-micron dimensions and point toward future uses for flake-based YIG in compact spintronics devices.

Negative Differential Resistance and Ultra-High TMR in Altermagnetic Tunnel Junctions

Authors: Sajjan Sheoran, Luke Keenan, Declan Nell, Stefano Sanvito
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12711v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper predicts that altermagnetic tunnel junctions can exhibit significant non-linear transport effects, such as negative differential resistance and TMR sign inversion, at low bias voltages. These effects arise from the unique momentum-space electronic structure of altermagnets. This discovery suggests potential applications for altermagnetic components in high-performance, low-power spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the non-linear transport properties of altermagnetic tunnel junctions (AMTJs) at finite bias, specifically looking for negative differential resistance (NDR) and ultra-high tunneling magnetoresistance (TMR).

Main result. The study predicts a Lambda-type negative differential resistance and a TMR sign inversion at approximately 0.13 V, driven by the momentum-space mismatch of spin-polarized Fermi surfaces in the parallel configuration.

Method. Non-equilibrium Green's functions (NEGF) implemented with Density Functional Theory (DFT) using the Smeagol package and Siesta engine.

Model / system. An altermagnetic tunnel junction consisting of KV2Se2O electrodes separated by a 5-monolayer MgO barrier. The system utilizes the orbital-ordered, d-wave altermagnet KV2Se2O with a quasi-2D Fermi surface.

Key observables. Current-voltage (I-Vb) curves, Tunneling Magnetoresistance (TMR), and k-parallel-resolved transmission T(k_parallel).

Important parameters / regimes. Bias voltage (Vb), parallel (P) and antiparallel (AP) magnetic configurations, and the spin-momentum anisotropy parameter (tJ).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on the maximally spin-anisotropic regime and assumes a vertical junction setup with a specific orbital-ordered structure.

Figures summary. The notes do not provide specific figure descriptions, though they mention the presence of k-parallel-resolved transmission and I-V curves.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of altermagnetic tunnel junctions, describes the electronic structure and transport modeling of the KV2Se2O/MgO/KV2Se2O system, presents the results for current-voltage characteristics and TMR, and discusses the physical mechanism of NDR and TMR sign inversion.

Abstract

Altermagnets can replace ferromagnets in tunnel junctions, yielding large tunneling magnetoresistance, ultrafast switching, and low-power functionality. While most studies explore the linear-response regime, interesting features emerge at finite bias, where the peculiar electronic structure of altermagnets gives rise to complex non-linear behaviour. Using non-equilibrium Green's functions implemented with density functional theory, we predict that a large low-bias negative differential resistance can be observed in an altermagnetic tunnel junction. Our proposed junction incorporates the orbital-ordered altermagnet KV2Se2O, whose quasi-2D Fermi surface plays a crucial role in realizing the negative differential resistance. Upon the application of a finite bias voltage, the current in the parallel configuration first increases sharply and then decreases, to be almost completely suppressed at around 0.14 V. At the same time, the antiparallel configuration displays a monotonic current-voltage curve. This behaviour, in addition to the negative differential resistance, supports a large tunneling magnetoresistance with sign inversion at 0.13 V. Our results suggest that altermagnetic tunnel junctions can be used as components in applications requiring strong non-linear response at low bias.

Thermoelectric enhancement from an asymmetric spectral-conductivity cusp in spin-1 chiral fermions

Authors: Risako Kikuchi, Junya Endo, Ai Yamakage
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13323v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper shows that the asymmetric features in conductivity caused by impurities can actually be leveraged to boost thermoelectric efficiency in spin-1 chiral fermion systems. By analyzing the interplay between band curvature and scattering, the authors identify specific regimes where the Seebeck coefficient and figure of merit are significantly enhanced.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how impurity scattering, which creates an asymmetric cusp in spectral conductivity, enhances the electronic thermoelectric response in spin-1 chiral fermion systems.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the asymmetric cusp markedly enhances the Seebeck coefficient, power factor, and electronic figure of merit, with the effect being strongest when the cusp is sharp, asymmetric, and has low background conductivity.

Method. The researchers use linear-response theory within the self-consistent Born approximation (SCBA) and employ a minimal cusp model to isolate the underlying transport mechanism.

Model / system. The system consists of spin-1 chiral fermions in topological semimetals, modeled by a Hamiltonian with two linearly dispersing bands and one nearly flat trivial band, subject to Gaussian finite-range impurity scattering.

Key observables. Density of states (DOS), spectral conductivity, Seebeck coefficient, power factor, electronic figure of merit (ZT_el), and Lorenz number.

Important parameters / regimes. Scattering strength (W), dimensionless band curvature (c_tilde), reduced temperature (T_tilde), and cusp asymmetry/sharpness parameters (gamma, p, q).

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses only on electronic contributions to thermal conductivity and assumes a low-temperature regime where impurity scattering dominates over phonon contributions.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the band structure, conductivity cusp, and ZT enhancement; Fig 2 displays temperature dependence of transport coefficients; Figs 8-10 present color maps of the Seebeck coefficient and Lorenz ratio in the (p, q) parameter plane.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the spin-1 chiral fermion model and the impurity-induced cusp, presents numerical results using SCBA, transitions to a generalized minimal cusp model to clarify the mechanism, and concludes with an analytical discussion of the parameter space for thermoelectric optimization.

Abstract

A recent study showed that, in spin-1 chiral fermion systems composed of two linearly dispersing bands and one trivial band, impurity scattering produces an asymmetric cusp in the spectral conductivity. We demonstrate that this asymmetric cusp markedly enhances the electronic thermoelectric response. Using linear-response theory within the self-consistent Born approximation, we find low-temperature enhancements in both the Seebeck coefficient and the electronic figure of merit. Increasing the curvature of the trivial band further strengthens this cusp-induced enhancement, even though the corresponding density of states becomes smoother. To clarify this mechanism, we introduce a minimal cusp model for the spectral conductivity and show that the enhancement is most pronounced when the cusp is sharp and strongly asymmetric, and when the spectral conductivity at the cusp energy is small.

Defect annihilation mechanism in the formation of dodecagonal quasicrystals

Authors: Rong Liu, Gang Cui, Tiejun Zhou, Kai Jiang
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13253v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies the specific structural pathway for defect repair in dodecagonal quasicrystals. It demonstrates that annihilation occurs through a sequential process of phason flips, defect aggregation, and decomposition. This mechanism is driven by the reduction of potential energy and the restoration of twelvefold rotational symmetry.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic mechanisms and kinetics of defect annihilation during the formation of dodecagonal quasicrystals (DDQCs).

Main result. The defect annihilation process follows a three-stage sequence: phason flip, aggregation of shield-like defects, and decomposition of defects, driven by potential energy gradients and resulting in increased structural symmetry.

Method. A combination of the string method and the spring pair method (SPM) is used to determine the minimum energy path (MEP) and locate index-1 saddle points.

Model / system. A two-dimensional system of 1024 identical particles interacting via a Lennard-Jones-Gauss (LJG) potential, which features two characteristic length scales to stabilize quasicrystalline order.

Key observables. Potential energy, twelvefold rotational symmetry order parameter (Phi_12), structure factor S(q), and particle-specific potential energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Lennard-Jones-Gauss potential parameters: epsilon_0 = 0.5, sigma_0 = 0.88, h = -0.874, r_G = 1.879, and sigma_G^2 = 0.02.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes a zero-temperature regime and uses a particle-based model with free boundary conditions.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the LJG potential; Fig 2 compares defective and defect-free structures; Fig 3 displays the transition pathway and energy diagram; Fig 4 shows diffraction patterns; Fig 10-12 illustrate phason flips, energy distributions, and defect evolution (aggregation/decomposition).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of defect kinetics, describes the LJG potential model and the string/spring pair methods, presents the identified three-stage annihilation pathway, and concludes with the energetic and symmetric drivers of the process.

Abstract

Understanding defect evolution is essential to the structural stability of quasicrystals, yet the kinetics of defect repair remain poorly understood. Here, by combining the string method and the spring pair method, we determine the minimum energy path from defective to defect-free dodecagonal quasicrystals using a particle model with the Lennard-Jones-Gauss potential. We find that defect annihilation proceeds via three stages: phason flip, aggregation and decomposition of shield-like defects. These sequential transformations are driven by potential energy gradients and accompanied by an increase in structural symmetry. The three stages act synergistically in promoting defect annihilation, offering new insights into the microscopic repair mechanisms of quasicrystals.

Ultrafast electron dynamics of electron-irradiated graphene

Authors: Yifan Yao, Andre Schleife
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.12854v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This study uses first-principles simulations to show that classical models are insufficient for describing electron irradiation of graphene at energies around 400 eV. It identifies a specific energy regime where quantum wave-packet effects significantly alter backscattering yields. These findings provide guidance for using electron beams in high-resolution microscopy and nanofabrication.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the fundamental interactions between incident electrons and graphene during irradiation, specifically determining when classical point-charge descriptions fail and quantum mechanical wave-packet effects become necessary.

Main result. Identified a specific energy window around 400 eV where quantum effects like diffraction and reflection lead to a finite backscattered electron yield, whereas classical descriptions predict zero backscattering.

Method. Real-time time-dependent density functional theory (rt-TDDFT) using Ehrenfest dynamics and the INQ code.

Model / system. A single-layer graphene sheet (112 carbon atoms) subjected to incident electrons modeled either as a Gaussian quantum wave packet or a classical ensemble of point-charge trajectories.

Key observables. Kinetic energy loss (electronic stopping), secondary electron emission (SEE) yield, and backscattered electron yield/momentum distribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Incident kinetic energy (100 eV to 800 eV), de Broglie wavelength, and wave packet width.

Assumptions / limitations. Use of the adiabatic local density approximation (ALDA) for exchange-correlation and Gamma-point Brillouin zone sampling.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the simulation cell and electron density change; Figure 2 compares momentum distributions for different electron models; Figure 3 decomposes kinetic energy loss; Figure 4 shows secondary electron yields; Figure 5 and 6 show backscattering trends and kinetic energy spectra.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of electron-material interaction, describes the rt-TDDFT methodology and validation, presents results comparing classical vs. quantum dynamics across energy regimes, and concludes with implications for materials design.

Abstract

Electron irradiation is essential for materials characterization and modification, though the fundamental interactions between incident electrons and host materials remain under investigation. Here, we employ first-principles simulations to study electron dynamics under external electron irradiation. We quantify differences in key observables, including kinetic energy loss, secondary electron emission, and backscattered electrons, between classical and quantum mechanical descriptions of the incident electron. Around 400 eV incident energy, we identify significant differences in backscattered electron yields between classical point-charge and quantum wave-packet descriptions, whereas the quantum-mechanical effects diminish at incident energies above 600 eV. These differences highlight the critical importance of quantum effects in electron irradiation phenomena that occur in a specific energy range of the incident electron. Our results provide clear guidance for selecting appropriate incident, electron descriptions based on kinetic-energy regimes, identify a targeted experimental window for isolating quantum-only backscattering, and enable the rational design of 2D materials for nanofabrication and high-resolution electron-beam technologies.

Acoustic Chirality

Authors: Alex J. Vernon, Konstantin Y. Bliokh
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13249v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper identifies a new continuous symmetry and conservation law in the equations of linear isotropic elasticity. It provides a rigorous theoretical framework for defining acoustic chirality and helicity, distinguishing them from spin angular momentum. The work establishes that integral chirality is fundamentally linked to the population imbalance of right- and left-handed phonons.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a fundamental quantitative characterization of chirality and helicity in generic inhomogeneous acoustic wave fields, and the ambiguity in distinguishing between true chirality and spin angular momentum in chiral phonons.

Main result. The discovery of a previously unknown continuous dual symmetry and a corresponding conservation law in linear isotropic elasticity, which establishes acoustic chirality as a fundamental property of elastic waves.

Method. The authors use Helmholtz decomposition and Noether's theorem applied to the linear elasticity equations, recasting second-order wave equations into a first-order system of fields.

Model / system. The system consists of linear isotropic homogeneous elastic waves in solids, described by displacement, velocity, and an auxiliary vector field representing the curl of displacement.

Key observables. Acoustic chirality density, acoustic chirality flow density, acoustic helicity, spin angular momentum, and 'false chirality'.

Important parameters / regimes. Lamé coefficients, mass density, transverse and longitudinal wave velocities, and the population imbalance between right- and left-handed transverse phonons.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is primarily focused on the regime of linear isotropic elasticity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates two cases of standing transverse waves: one with same-helicity components producing non-zero chirality but zero spin, and another with opposite-helicity components producing non-zero spin but zero chirality.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of acoustic chirality, establishes a mathematical framework using first-order equations, identifies a new continuous dual symmetry, derives the resulting conservation laws, introduces new concepts like acoustic helicity and false chirality, and validates the theory with interference field examples.

Abstract

We reveal a previously unknown continuous symmetry and conservation law in the equations of linear isotropic elasticity, which describe the chirality of elastic waves. We show that the integral chirality is determined by the population imbalance between right- and left-handed transverse phonons, whereas the local chirality density generally involves both transverse and longitudinal wave components. We also introduce the related concepts of acoustic helicity and ``false chirality''. The theory is illustrated with simple interference fields exhibiting distinct distributions of chirality, spin angular momentum, and false chirality. Our results establish chirality as a fundamental property of elastic waves and provide a general theoretical framework for chiral acoustic phenomena.

Anisotropic Dopant and Strain Architectures in WS$_2$ Nanocrystals Driven by Growth Kinetics

Authors: Frederico B. Sousa, Raphaela de Oliveira, Matheus J. S. Matos, Elizabeth Grace Houser, Igor Ferreira Curvelo, Zhuohang Yu, Mingzu Liu, Felipe Menescal, Gilmar Eugenio Marques, Leandro M. Malard, Mauricio Terrones, Bruno R. Carvalho, Helio Chacham, Marcio D. Teodoro
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.13577v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper shows that dopant distribution in WS2 can be controlled by tuning growth kinetics rather than relying on random processes. By harnessing non-equilibrium growth, the authors created predictable patterns of chemical and mechanical strain. This provides a new route for the deterministic engineering of 2D semiconductor architectures.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The spatial distribution of dopants in 2D semiconductors is typically stochastic, preventing deterministic defect engineering and leaving the origin of optical anomalies in WS2 domains unresolved.

Main result. The study demonstrates that non-equilibrium growth kinetics drive the anisotropic segregation of vanadium along crystallographic bisectors, creating predictable strain architectures and unique vibrational modes.

Method. The researchers combined synchrotron X-ray fluorescence (XRF) microscopy, hyperspectral Raman imaging, and AFM with an Adsorption-Growth-Diffusion (AGD) model and DFT calculations.

Model / system. The physical system consists of vanadium-doped WS2 monolayers grown via chemical vapor deposition (CVD), modeled using an AGD continuum framework that captures the competition between corner adsorption and thermal diffusion.

Key observables. Vanadium and tungsten concentrations, Raman modes (E', A'1, J2, J3), local tensile strain (epsilon), and topographic uplift/wrinkling.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimensionless kinetic parameter beta (approx. 10-100), vanadium concentration (up to 33%), and tensile strain (approx. 0.70%).

Assumptions / limitations. Vanadium substitutes tungsten at lattice sites; growth occurs at a constant velocity; the 2H phase remains the thermodynamic ground state; and the growth process is terminated by rapid quenching.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows XRF intensity maps and concentration profiles of W, S, and V; Fig 2 presents the AGD model schematic and experimental fits; Fig 3 displays Raman spectra and spatial maps of vibrational modes and strain.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of stochastic doping, presents XRF evidence of anisotropic V-segregation, applies the AGD model to explain the kinetic origin of this segregation, uses Raman and AFM to link dopant channels to mechanical strain, and concludes with the potential for programmable defect landscapes.

Abstract

Dopant distribution in two-dimensional semiconductors is typically assumed to be stochastic, limiting deterministic defect engineering. Here, we show that non-equilibrium growth kinetics can be harnessed to define dopant-driven strain architectures in vanadium-doped WS$_2$ monolayers. Using synchrotron X-ray fluorescence, we identify preferential vanadium incorporation, anti-correlated with tungsten content, along crystallographic bisectors. An adsorption-growth-diffusion model with a single kinetic parameter quantitatively captures the dopant segregation arising from preferential corner adsorption and limited diffusion during chemical vapor deposition growth. Hyperspectral Raman imaging demonstrates mechanically induced vibrational responses, revealing localized tensile strain ($\varepsilon \approx0.70\%$) channels associated with the anisotropic dopant distribution. This regime is marked by the depletion of W-site-sensitive in-plane modes and the emergence of a localized $J2$ mode (210~cm$^{-1}$), which our ab-initio calculations attribute to antiphase V$-$V oscillations. These findings establish kinetic segregation as a route to deterministic chemical and strain architectures in 2D semiconductors, enabling programmable defect landscapes and strain engineering during synthesis.

Apparent Ferroelectric Polarization Hysteresis and a Simple Method to Observe Piezoelectric Strain Loops with a Microphone

Observed phenomena confirm the increasing plasticity of the material and it is postulated to be related to the bond-switching mechanism that accelerates at high temperatures. The post-mortem transmission electron microscopy characterization confirmed that the loaded material was non-crystalline over the entire range of the indentation temperatures. The thermal stability of the structure was further studied in-situ up to 1050C by X-ray diffraction. The implemented methodology allowed us to follow the dynamic process of phase transitions occurring in the material above 650C. First, thermally activated crystallization was observed at 700C. Intermediate alumina phases were present up to 950C, while above this temperature, exclusively the thermodynamically stable alpha-Al2O3 was observed. The in-situ high-temperature characterization of the evolution of thin films boosts the understanding of the application limits of the coating systems at elevated temperatures. The added value is that the paper demonstrates the potential usefulness of combining high-temperature techniques to characterize the complete behavior of thin films at elevated temperatures.
