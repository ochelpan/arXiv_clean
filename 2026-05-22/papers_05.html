dSABRE: A SABRE-Style Router for Multi-Core Distributed Quantum Computers

Authors: Sanjiang Li
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21960v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper presents dSABRE, a new routing algorithm designed to optimize circuit execution on distributed quantum computers. By specifically addressing core congestion and optimizing the cost of inter-core entanglement, it significantly reduces the number of required EPR pairs compared to existing methods. This is crucial for scaling quantum computing architectures where remote entanglement is a bottleneck.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Minimizing the consumption of EPR pairs and intra-core SWAP operations when routing quantum circuits on a multi-core distributed quantum computer (DQC).

Main result. The dSABRE router reduces geometric-mean EPR consumption by 41-44% over TELESABRE and 16-68% over pytket-dqc, demonstrating high scalability up to 360 qubits.

Method. A SABRE-style, lookahead-driven routing algorithm using a five-term gate-centric teleportation score, a proactive congestion-relief mechanism, and a BFS-layer construction for the inter-core extended set.

Model / system. A multi-core distributed quantum architecture consisting of multiple small quantum processing units (cores) interconnected via EPR links, modeled as a weighted graph with high-cost inter-core edges.

Key observables. Geometric-mean EPR consumption, intra-core SWAP costs, and inter-core teleportation costs.

Important parameters / regimes. SWAP cost (c_swap), teleportation cost (c_tele), capacity penalty (c_cap), lookahead decay parameter (gamma), and core capacity/demand thresholds.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the initial layout and communication-primitive selection stages are handled by other parts of the compilation pipeline; assumes a fixed grid-of-grids topology.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a circuit representation; Figure 3 illustrates the dSABRE routing workflow; Figure 4 provides a running example of inter-core teleportation scoring on an H-grid; Table I details scoring for specific candidates.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the routing problem, describes the dSABRE algorithm and its scoring mechanisms, presents ablation studies to validate individual components, compares performance against state-of-the-art baselines, and demonstrates scalability via large-scale QFT circuits.

Abstract

Minimising EPR consumption is the dominant objective when routing a quantum circuit on a distributed quantum computer (DQC). We present dSABRE, a SABRE-style router for multi-core processors that, on each iteration of a lookahead-driven loop, first resolves any intra-core front-layer gates by SWAP scoring and only falls back to scoring inter-core teleportation candidates when the intra-core front is empty. Three mechanisms drive the improvement over the state of the art: a five-term gate-centric teleportation score that generalises the local SWAP heuristic to the inter-core setting, whose explicit capacity-penalty term keeps the scorer from teleporting into saturated cores; a proactive congestion-relief pass that redistributes idle qubits out of high-demand cores before deadlock; and a BFS-layer construction of the inter-core extended set that respects DAG dependencies layer by layer rather than mixing wires in topological order. Across 18 MQT-Bench circuits at 25, 36, and 64 logical qubits, dSABRE reduces geometric-mean EPR consumption by 41-44% over TeleSABRE and by 16-68% over the gate-teleportation-based pytket-dqc, using standard Qiskit SabreLayout for the initial layout. A large-circuit QFT sweep at 100-360 qubits confirms scalability. Code and online appendices are available at https://github.com/ebony72/dsabre.

Effect of Pb Substitution at the Mo site on the Magnetic Properties of the Polar Magnet Fe$_2$Mo$_3$O$_8$

Authors: Takumi Shirasaki, Taichi Ishikawa, Shungo Nakayama, Hideki Kuwahara
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21841v1

Summary. This study demonstrates that substituting non-magnetic ions into the Mo sites of the polar magnet Fe2Mo3O8 breaks up spin-singlet clusters, creating active spins that induce room-temperature ferromagnetism. This approach provides a strategy for designing new magnetoelectric materials by controlling non-magnetic cluster states.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how substituting non-magnetic Pb4+ and Zr4+ ions into the Mo sites of the polar magnet Fe2Mo3O8 disrupts Mo spin-singlet trimers to induce new magnetic functionalities.

Main result. The disruption of Mo spin-singlet trimers induces active spins in the Mo layer, leading to the emergence of ferromagnetic-like behavior that persists up to room temperature.

Method. Solid-state reaction synthesis followed by powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) with Rietveld refinement and magnetic measurements using a vibrating sample magnetometer (VSM) up to 8 T.

Model / system. The system is the ternary transition-metal oxide Fe2Mo3O8, a polar magnet with a layered structure of magnetic Fe honeycomb lattices and non-magnetic Mo kagome lattices containing Mo4+ spin-singlet trimers.

Key observables. Magnetization (M) vs. temperature (T) and magnetic field (H), Neel temperature (TN), and magnetization increment (delta M).

Important parameters / regimes. Substitution level (x) from 0 to 0.20, Neel temperature TN approx 60 K, and effective spin S = 1/2 per active Mo ion.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a single dopant ion severs two of the three spin-singlet bonds in a trimer, leaving one intact dimer with localized spins.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the material and the problem of Mo-site substitution, describes the experimental synthesis and structural characterization, presents magnetic measurement results showing emergent ferromagnetism, and provides a quantitative statistical model to explain the observed magnetization increase.

Abstract

The ternary transition-metal oxide Fe$_2$Mo$_3$O$_8$ is a polar magnet characterized by a layered structure of magnetic Fe honeycomb lattices and non-magnetic Mo kagome lattices. Whereas previous studies have primarily focused on the chemical substitution at the Fe sites to modulate the magnetic properties, the Mo sites have remained largely unexplored due to the strong spin-singlet trimerization of Mo$^{4+}$ ions. In this study, we investigated the effect of substituting non-magnetic Pb$^{4+}$ and Zr$^{4+}$ ions into the Mo sites to intentionally disrupt the Mo trimers. Our results reveal that the disruption of the Mo spin-singlet state induces active spins within the Mo layer, resulting in the emergence of a ferromagnetic-like behavior that persists even at room temperature. Quantitative analysis that takes into account the weight fraction of the main phase suggests an effective spin $S = 1/2$ state per active Mo ion upon trimer disruption. These findings demonstrate that controlling non-magnetic cluster states within a polar host via chemical substitution is a promising approach for designing room-temperature magnetoelectric materials.

Emergent magnetic and charge ordered phases in freestanding ultrathin \ce{LaVO3}

Summary. This paper uses DFT and Monte Carlo simulations to show how the magnetic and electronic properties of LaVO3 change when it is thinned down to a monolayer. It discovers that thickness and charge transfer from polar catastrophes can induce new ferromagnetic and charge-ordered phases.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the intrinsic electronic and magnetic properties of freestanding ultrathin LaVO3 films, specifically how thickness and charge transfer drive emergent magnetic and charge-ordered phases.

Main result. The study reveals a thickness-dependent magnetic transition from bulk-like C-type AFM to stripe-AFM in monolayers, and identifies that polar catastrophe-driven charge transfer in thicker films creates FM and stripe-AFM surface states.

Method. Density functional theory (DFT) using VASP with a Hubbard U correction, supplemented by classical Monte Carlo simulations using the Vampire code.

Model / system. Freestanding ultrathin LaVO3 films ranging from monolayer to 8 layers, modeled with an orthorhombic/monoclinic perovskite structure and a Heisenberg model for magnetic interactions.

Key observables. Magnetic phases (C-AFM, stripe-AFM, FM, Neel-AFM), charge ordering patterns, magnetic moments, and exchange interaction parameters (J1, J2, J_perp).

Important parameters / regimes. Film thickness (1L to 8L), doping concentrations (0.5h/fu, 0.25h/fu, etc.), and Hubbard U = 3 eV.

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a classical Monte Carlo approach for magnetic transitions and the approximation of electron correlations via the DFT+U method.

Figures summary. Figure 1(a) shows the supercell structure; Figure 5 illustrates stripe and checkerboard charge ordering patterns and their relative energies.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the motivation of studying freestanding membranes, moves to the computational methodology, details the thickness-dependent magnetic transitions, explores polar catastrophe-driven charge transfer in thicker films, and concludes with an analysis of doping-induced charge ordering.

Abstract

Transition metal oxide perovskites are an ideal platform for exploring the interplay between spin, orbital, charge and lattice degrees of freedom. Among them, \ce{LaVO3} has been extensively studied in heterostructures and superlattices, where exotic phases have been reported. Motivated by the advances in freestanding oxide membranes, we investigate the intrinsic properties of freestanding ultrathin \ce{LaVO3} films using density functional theory. Our calculations reveal a sequence of magnetic phase transitions with thickness, starting from stripe-AFM in monolayer until the bulk like C-AFM is recovered. Beyond four layers, polar catastrophe driven charge transfer dopes the surface layers giving rise to stripe-AFM and ferromagnetic surface states while the central layers remain bulk like. We further explore this fact by studying charge doped monolayer, discovering that hole doping drives the system into ferromagnetic state. Doping also induced charge ordering in the system. A striped charge ordering pattern is observed at 0.5 h/fu, while a 3:1 stripe pattern emerges at 0.25 h/fu, indicating that the periodicity of the superstructure changes with doping concentration.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the transient 'hidden' metallic phase of 1T-TaS2 can be stabilized as a stable equilibrium state by controlling the thickness of exfoliated flakes. The findings have significant implications for developing new phase-change technologies and quantum electronic devices.

Important parameters / regimes. Material thickness (ranging from 2.5 nm to 55 nm), temperature (50 K to 380 K), and out-of-plane momentum (kz).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows dimensional control and thickness-dependent spectra; Figure 2 illustrates kz periodicity and Fermi surface maps in 25 nm flakes; Figure 3 details temperature-dependent EDCs and MDCs; Figure 4 presents a schematic phase diagram as a function of thickness and temperature.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of metastable phases, describes the experimental setup and sample preparation, presents thickness-dependent spectroscopic results, analyzes temperature-driven phase evolution, and concludes with a proposed phase diagram.

Abstract

Electronic phases that lie outside the equilibrium ground state offer a route to explore competing configurations in correlated materials. In 1T-TaS2, ultrafast excitation accesses a metallic hidden phase that is distinct from the commensurate insulating ground state. Here we use angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy to show that an equivalent electronic configuration is stabilized in exfoliated intermediate-thickness 1T-TaS2 flakes, where it persists up to room temperature before evolving through a different sequence of electronic transitions. This equilibrium hidden-phase-like state hosts a metallic band with finite Fermi-level spectral weight while retaining the characteristic hybridization gaps associated with the star-of-David band folding. These results establish a platform for controlling competing electronic states in layered materials, with implications for both quantum science and phase change technologies.

GeneCS: Synthesizing Resource-Efficient Code Surgery for Arbitrary Quantum Stabilizer Codes

Authors: Junyu Zhou, Ali Javadi-Abhari, Gushu Li
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21746v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces GeneCS, a compiler designed to automate the creation of efficient 'code surgery' protocols for quantum error correction. By treating the construction of ancilla systems as a graph optimization problem, it significantly reduces the physical qubit overhead required for logical operations without sacrificing error protection.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The high resource overhead (ancillary qubits and checks) required for performing fault-tolerant code surgery and cross-code communication between arbitrary stabilizer codes.

Main result. The GeneCS compiler achieves an average reduction of over 85% in ancillary qubits and checks for both single-code and cross-code operations while preserving logical error rates and scalability.

Method. A graph-based synthesis framework using three optimization strategies: structure-aware graph expansion, dynamic expansion-congestion optimization, and degree-constrained optimization via graph thickening and cellulation.

Model / system. The framework operates on quantum stabilizer codes, including topological codes (surface, color) and QLDPC codes (Bivariate Bicycle, Hypergraph Product, Quantum Tanner), modeled as low-dimensional chain complexes.

Key observables. Ancillary qubit/check overhead, code distance, logical error rate, and maximum stabilizer weight (degree).

Important parameters / regimes. Cheeger constant (expansion), congestion (edge overlap), and maximum qubit/check degrees.

Assumptions / limitations. The scalability results observed in surface codes are assumed to extend to other CSS stabilizer codes due to their locally repetitive structure.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the GeneCS workflow; Figure 2 maps surface codes to graphs; Figure 3 details graph modification; Figure 5-7 illustrate expansion, congestion, and graph thickening; Figure 9 shows logical error rate stability.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the code surgery problem, defines a graph-theoretic model for ancilla systems, presents the GeneCS compiler algorithms (expansion, congestion, and degree optimization), provides experimental benchmarks on various code families, and discusses scalability and error rate performance.

Abstract

Efficiently realizing logical operations on general stabilizer codes remains a long-standing challenge in fault tolerant quantum computing. While code surgery provides a general framework with provable guarantees by joint logical measurements, existing constructions are largely theoretical and incur substantial ancilla overhead in practice. In this work, we propose GeneCS, a resource-efficient compiler for synthesizing code surgery protocols for arbitrary stabilizer codes. Our approach leverages structure-aware optimizations to eliminate redundancy in graph construction, dynamically balance expansion and congestion, and incorporate code degree constraints. Experimental results show that GeneCS achieves an average reduction of over $85\%$ in ancillary qubits and checks for both single-code and cross-code logical operations, while preserving logical error rates. Moreover, our compiler scales to codes with more than $10^4$ qubits with an amortized compilation time of about one second per instance. These results enable practical logical operations and efficient cross-code communication, thereby supporting the deployment of modern QLDPC codes and heterogeneous quantum architectures.

Large-flavor route to a stable U(1) Dirac spin liquid on the maple-leaf lattice

Authors: Yunchao Zhang, Andreas Feuerpfeil, Subir Sachdev, Ronny Thomale, Yasir Iqbal
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21587v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates whether a large number of fermion flavors can stabilize a U(1) Dirac spin liquid on the maple-leaf lattice. It identifies that the stability is not symmetry-protected but depends on the dynamical irrelevance of specific allowed monopoles. The work provides specific spectral predictions to guide numerical studies using exact diagonalization.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the stability of a U(1) Dirac spin liquid (DSL) on the maple-leaf lattice against the proliferation of monopoles that could cause confinement.

Main result. The authors show that the maple-leaf lattice realizes a large-flavor QED3 theory with Nf=12, and while five spin-singlet monopoles are symmetry-allowed, their stability depends on whether they are dynamically irrelevant.

Method. Theoretical framework using fermionic parton construction, projective symmetry group (PSG) analysis, 't Hooft anomaly matching, and Wannier-center decomposition.

Model / system. A U(1) Dirac spin liquid on a frustrated maple-leaf lattice, described by an effective (2+1)D QED3 theory with Nf=12 Dirac fermions.

Key observables. Monopole quantum numbers (spin, lattice, and time-reversal), scaling dimensions of monopole operators, and symmetry-resolved spectral content.

Important parameters / regimes. Flavor number Nf=12, scaling dimension of the charge-one monopole (Delta_1), and hopping parameters (th, td, tt).

Assumptions / limitations. The stability of the phase is assumed to depend on the dynamical irrelevance of symmetry-allowed monopoles rather than symmetry protection.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the maple-leaf lattice structure and mean-field bond amplitudes; Figure 2 illustrates spinon dispersions and the Nf=4 regime.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the maple-leaf lattice as a large-flavor platform, derives the effective QED3 theory, performs a symmetry classification of monopoles, verifies anomaly matching, and provides numerical predictions for testing the phase.

Abstract

The $\mathrm{U}(1)$ Dirac spin liquid provides a useful organizing framework for frustrated magnets: it offers an algebraic parent state from which competing orders, confinement patterns, and low-energy spectral features can be understood. Whether such a state can occur as a stable ground state of a two-dimensional spin Hamiltonian remains an open question, because monopole events of the compact gauge field can proliferate and confine the spinons. Here, we show that the maple-leaf lattice provides a distinct route to this problem. Its Dirac spin liquid realizes QED$_3$ with $N_f=12$ Dirac fermions, substantially more than the $N_f=4$ theories of the triangular and kagome lattices. We classify the fundamental monopoles under the full microscopic symmetry group and find five charge-one spin-singlet monopoles that are trivial under lattice symmetries, time reversal, and spin rotation. The phase is therefore not protected by symmetry in the usual sense: its stability depends on whether these allowed monopoles are dynamically irrelevant. Available large-$N_f$ and Monte Carlo estimates place the charge-one monopole dimension close to the relevance threshold in $(2+1)$ dimensions, making the maple-leaf lattice a concrete large-flavor platform for testing the stability of compact QED$_3$ in a quantum magnet. The same monopole classification gives direct numerical predictions, identifying the symmetry sectors in which singlet, triplet, and quintet monopole excitations should appear. This provides a route to testing the $N_f=12$ Dirac spin liquid through symmetry-resolved exact diagonalization and variational studies of maple-leaf spin Hamiltonians.

Observation of Altermagnetic Order Switching in Bulk MnTe by Polarized Neutron Diffraction

Authors: Zheyuan Liu, Shinichiro Asai, Shingo Takahashi, Hiraku Saito, Taro Nakajima, Takatsugu Masuda

Summary. This paper provides direct experimental evidence for the existence and controllability of altermagnetic order in bulk MnTe. By using polarized neutron diffraction, the authors show that the Néel vector can be manipulated with very small magnetic fields, which is a crucial step toward using altermagnets in spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining if altermagnetic order in bulk MnTe can be directly observed and controlled by external magnetic fields in the absence of net magnetization.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated that the altermagnetic Néel vector and the associated weak ferromagnetic moment (WFM) can be switched using milli-Tesla-scale magnetic field cooling, providing direct evidence of a net Néel vector in the bulk.

Method. Polarized Neutron Diffraction (PoND) in half-polarized mode was used to probe nuclear-magnetic interference terms, complemented by magnetic susceptibility and spontaneous magnetization measurements.

Model / system. The study focuses on bulk MnTe, a prototype altermagnet with a centrosymmetric hexagonal lattice (space group P63/mmc) and an A-type antiferromagnetic ground state.

Key observables. Nuclear-magnetic interference (NMI) vector R, weak ferromagnetic moment (WFM), Néel vector (n), and magnetic susceptibility.

Important parameters / regimes. Néel temperature TN = 307 K, magnetic field scales in the milli-Tesla range, and the presence of spin-orbit coupling (SOC).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the WFM arises from an imbalance in antiphase domains rather than from domain walls.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the MnTe domain states and the NMI vector mechanism; Fig 2 illustrates Néel vector configurations and susceptibility/WFM measurements; Fig 3 presents the NMI vector components under different cooling conditions and their temperature dependence.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of altermagnetism, describes the experimental setup for polarized neutron diffraction, presents the observation of NMI as evidence for the Néel vector, demonstrates domain switching via field cooling, and discusses the origin of the weak ferromagnetic moment.

Abstract

Altermagnetic order, characterized by the Néel vector, breaks time-reversal symmetry (TRS) even in the nonrelativistic limit. Although spin-polarized and anomalous transport phenomena emerge with this order, they are mutually compensated by TRS-connected antiphase domains with opposite Néel vectors. Here we employ polarized neutron diffraction to directly probe the altermagnetic order in MnTe. Pronounced nuclear-magnetic interference terms were observed, providing direct evidence of a net Néel vector in the bulk crystal. Moreover, a weak ferromagnetic moment (WFM), originating from relativistic spin-orbit coupling, was found to be coupled with the altermagnetic order. Both the altermagnetic order and the WFM can be switched by milli-Tesla-scale magnetic field cooling.

Persistence of asymptotic variance under transport: from hyperfluctuation to stealthy hyperuniformity

Authors: Luca Lotz, Michael A. Klatt
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22803v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a mathematical framework to understand how density fluctuations, ranging from hyperfluctuation to stealthy hyperuniformity, behave under random mass transport. It solves an open problem regarding the persistence of these fluctuations in dimensions d >= 3 and introduces new, efficiently simulatable point processes.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the conditions under which the scaling of density fluctuations (p-uniformity) is preserved when a random measure undergoes a random transport process.

Main result. The authors establish a persistence theorem stating that p-uniformity is preserved under transport if the transport distance has a finite (d+p)-th moment and certain expansion terms are controlled.

Method. The study uses Fourier analysis of the Bartlett spectral measure, Taylor expansions of the transport kernel, and moment conditions on transport distance.

Model / system. The model involves stationary random complex measures on R^d, including point processes and mass-redistribution (transport) kernels that move mass from a source to a destination.

Key observables. p-uniformity exponent, asymptotic variance, Bartlett spectral measure, and structure factor.

Important parameters / regimes. Spatial dimension (d), hyperuniformity exponent (p), and transport distance moments.

Assumptions / limitations. The measures are assumed to be stationary (translation-invariant) and locally square-integrable; the expansion requires smoothness of test functions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the construction of a random fair tiling and a resulting point process, demonstrating an isotropic process with a very high scaling exponent.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining p-uniformity and transport kernels, develops a Taylor expansion for the destination measure, proves the main persistence theorem, and concludes with new point process constructions and an outlook on the converse statement.

Abstract

We introduce $p$-uniformity to characterize the scaling of density fluctuations in spatial random systems in $\mathbb{R}^d$, ranging from hyperfluctuation to stealthy hyperuniformity. Our central theorem establishes sufficient conditions to preserve $p$-uniformity under transport. The first condition, a finite $(d+p)$-th moment of the transport distance, allows for a Taylor expansion of the transport. The second condition controls the corresponding terms. We thus solve a previously stated open problem; indeed we extend it, since our result applies to a general $p$-uniform source in any dimension, and the source and transport may be dependent. As an application, we construct new classes of point processes that are isotropic and $p$-uniform with arbitrarily high $p$, and that can be simulated in linear time. We conclude with an outlook on a converse statement.

Reinforcement learning for ion shuttling on trapped-ion quantum computers

Authors: Maximilian Schier, Lea Richtmann, Christian Staufenbiel, Tobias Schmale, Daniel Borcherding, Michèle Heurs, Bodo Rosenhahn
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22463v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces RLIonS, a reinforcement learning-based compiler designed to optimize the movement of ions in QCCD-based quantum computers. By treating shuttling as a sequential decision problem, the method significantly reduces the number of required transport operations compared to existing heuristics. This reduction is crucial for executing larger quantum circuits within the limited coherence times of trapped-ion qubits.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for the development of scalable quantum hardware, as it addresses the critical classical control bottleneck of managing qubit movement to maintain coherence in trapped-ion systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing ion shuttling sequences in QCCD-based trapped-ion quantum computers to minimize latency and overhead caused by qubit transport.

Main result. The proposed RLIonS reinforcement learning compiler achieves up to a 36.3% reduction in shuttling operations compared to state-of-the-art heuristics and remains near-optimal relative to exact SAT solvers.

Method. A reinforcement learning approach using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and a ResNet architecture, formulated as a Semi-Markov Decision Process (SMDP) with a novel sinusoidal encoding for chip states.

Model / system. The study focuses on the Quantum Charge-Coupled Device (QCCD) architecture for trapped-ion quantum computing, specifically evaluating X-chip and Q-chip (storage ring) layouts.

Key observables. Number of shuttling operations (steps), shuttling duration, and compilation/inference time.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (k_lookahead), number of ions (up to 50), and the discount rate (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes deterministic transitions between states and that gates beyond a specific lookahead depth do not influence immediate decisions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Q-chip layout; Figure 2 illustrates the RLIonS encoding pipeline from chip state to matrix; Figure 3 compares compile time and shuttling duration across methods; Figure 4 shows performance scaling with Quantum Volume; Figure 6 presents an ablation study on sinusoidal vs. linear encoding.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the shuttling bottleneck, describes the QCCD architectures, details the RLIonS RL framework (SMDP formulation and encoding), presents benchmarking against SAT and heuristic methods, provides ablation studies on encoding and rewards, and concludes with future directions.

Abstract

Scalable trapped-ion quantum computing is commonly realized with modular chips that feature distinct zones with specific functionalities, such as storage, state preparation, and gate execution. To execute a quantum circuit, the ions must be transported between these zones. This process is called ion shuttling. To achieve reliable computation results, the shuttling process must be optimized. However, as the number of ions increases, this becomes a high-dimensional optimization problem where optimal solutions cannot be computed efficiently. We demonstrate, to the best of our knowledge, the first use of reinforcement learning (RL) for the optimization of ion shuttling. RL is well-suited for such scenarios, as it enables learning a strategy through direct interaction with the problem. We show that our RL approach outperforms current state-of-the-art heuristic techniques, yielding a reduction in shuttling operations of up to 36.3 %. Furthermore, we show that our method is easily applicable to various chip architectures. Our approach offers a versatile method to study shuttling efficiency during chip design and, therefore, a highly relevant tool for future, more complex architectures.

These random-wave features coexist with non-chaotic one-dimensional boundary modes arising from nontrivial polariton reflection at the billiard edge. As the billiard boundary becomes increasingly complex, the Fourier transforms of the measured signals evolve toward ring-like structures consistent with Berry's random-wave conjecture. We develop a numerical framework based on the Helmholtz equation with generalized boundary conditions that encode angle-dependent reflection phase shifts. The calculated level statistics exhibit a crossover from Poisson-like behavior in integrable billiards to Wigner-Dyson-like behavior in chaotic geometries, with small deviations from the canonical form arising from nonlinear boundary conditions that require self-consistent bulk-boundary analysis. Theoretical analysis based on dissipative Green's functions qualitatively reproduces the near-field data. These results establish mesoscopic van der Waals billiards as a rich platform for studying generalized chaotic dynamics of hybrid light-matter polaritons.

Klein tunneling of the laser coherence

Authors: Konstantin Manannikov, Sagie Gadasi, Ekaterina Mironova, Eran Bernstein, Andrei Poliakov, Nir Davidson, Alexander N. Poddubny

Summary. The study shows that laser arrays with linear (Dirac) dispersion can maintain frequency synchronization across much larger frequency barriers than those with standard parabolic dispersion. This enhanced robustness is attributed to the 'Klein tunneling' of laser coherence, where modes delocalize through the barrier.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a direct analogy between a fundamental relativistic particle phenomenon (Klein tunneling) and the macroscopic synchronization of non-equilibrium, non-Hermitian classical oscillators, which is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the robustness of frequency synchronization between two laser arrays separated by a spectrally detuned barrier, specifically exploring how different dispersion laws (linear/Dirac vs. parabolic) affect the synchronization threshold.

Main result. For linear (Dirac) dispersion, synchronization persists for barrier heights approximately an order of magnitude higher than for parabolic or flat dispersion, an effect interpreted as the Klein tunneling of laser coherence.

Method. Numerical solution of nonlinear laser rate equations (Lugiato-Lefever-type) combined with linear analysis via diagonalization of a non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian.

Model / system. 1D and 2D (hexagonal/honeycomb) arrays of coupled lasers described by a non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian including dispersive and dissipative coupling components.

Key observables. Probability of desynchronization (P_desync), nonlinear transmission of coherence (T), mode localization/delocalization, and complex eigenfrequencies (dispersion and loss).

Important parameters / regimes. Barrier height (delta_B), barrier width (N_B), pump rate (p/gamma), and dispersion type (linear/Dirac, parabolic, or flat).

Assumptions / limitations. The nonlinear lasing solutions can be approximated by linear combinations of the lowest-loss eigenmodes of the effective Hamiltonian.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the 1D setup, coupling scheme, and band structure/transmission; Fig 2 compares desynchronization probability across different dispersion regimes; Supplemental figures show mode loss, localization, and the effect of pump strength.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the synchronization problem in laser arrays, presents the 1D model and the discovery of enhanced synchronization via Klein tunneling, extends the findings to 2D honeycomb lattices, and provides a detailed analysis of mode localization and symmetry effects in the supplement.

Abstract

We study theoretically the lasing synchronization of the two arrays of lasers with the complex mode dispersion, separated by a spectrally detuned barrier. We demonstrate that for lasing at the Dirac point, the synchronization persists for an order of magnitude higher barriers than in the arrays with a usual parabolic dispersion or a purely dissipative coupling. We interpret this effect as the Klein tunneling of the laser coherence through the barrier. Our numerical findings are supported by an analysis of the delocalization of the linearized eigenmodes of the arrays, which enhances the synchronization.
