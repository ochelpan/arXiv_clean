Sudden death of entanglement, rebirth of magic

Authors: Chenfeng Cao
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22603v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that local amplitude damping can cause the 'sudden death' and subsequent 'rebirth' of magic in quantum states, even after entanglement has been lost. It reveals a fundamental symmetry between entanglement death and magic rebirth and provides a classification of stabilizer states based on their ability to generate magic under noise.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insights into the resource theory of magic and the non-intuitive behavior of quantum resources in open quantum systems, specifically how dissipation can act as a resource for generating non-stabilizer states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the dynamics of entanglement and magic (non-stabilizerness) under local Markovian amplitude damping, specifically focusing on the phenomenon of magic rebirth after entanglement death.

Main result. The paper establishes that while entanglement is irreversibly lost under amplitude damping, magic can be 'reborn' after an initial period of disappearance, following a complementarity relation gamma_e + gamma_+ = 1. It also classifies stabilizer states into magic-generators and magic-insulators based on their Hamming-weight support.

Method. Analytical approach using Kraus operators, Stinespring dilation, and the stabilizer polytope framework, combined with mathematical tools like partial transposition and combinatorial enumeration.

Model / system. n-qubit systems, specifically the GHZ-type family and Bell states, subject to local Markovian amplitude damping channels.

Key observables. Magic robustness (R), concurrence (C), entanglement negativity, and stabilizer polytope membership.

Important parameters / regimes. Damping strength (gamma), number of qubits (n), initial state amplitude ratio (alpha/beta), and phase-twist angle (phi).

Assumptions / limitations. The noise model is restricted to local Markovian amplitude damping (non-unital) and certain phase-covariant channels.

Figures summary. Schematic of trajectories in the stabilizer polytope; plots of magic-entanglement complementarity; visualization of the super-exponential decay of the stabilizer window width; and threshold deformation as a function of phase.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of magic vs. entanglement dynamics, analyzes the GHZ-X manifold under amplitude damping, establishes the complementarity relation, classifies stabilizer states into generators and insulators, and explores the asymptotic behavior and phase-twist effects.

Abstract

Local Markovian noise cannot bring entanglement back, but it can bring magic back. Unlike separability, stabilizer membership is not preserved by local channels, allowing dissipation to push states out of the stabilizer polytope as well as in. Under local amplitude damping, the $n$-qubit GHZ family $α|0^n\rangle+β|1^n\rangle$ ($0<α<β$) loses its magic at a lower damping strength $γ_-$ and regains it at a higher one $γ_+$, while entanglement is irreversibly lost at $γ_e$. This magic-entanglement complementarity, $γ_e+γ_+=1$ for every $n$, reflects a system-environment duality of amplitude damping and persists for a broader class of dissipative channels. For small $α$, the reborn magic resides in a fully separable state with all proper marginals stabilizer, yet parity-syndrome extraction concentrates it onto a single qubit for magic-state distillation. Local dissipation further divides pure stabilizer states into magic-generators and magic-insulators: at two qubits, the Bell state $|Φ^+\rangle$ generates magic immediately, while its Bell-state partner $|Ψ^+\rangle$ remains stabilizer. Together, magic and entanglement reveal a symmetry invisible to either alone.

Q-PhotoNAS: Hybrid Quantum Neural Architecture Search Framework on Photonic Devices

Authors: Farah Elnakhal, Alberto Marchisio, Nouhaila Innan, Gabriel Falcao, Muhammad Shafique

Summary. The paper introduces Q-PhotoNAS, an automated framework for searching the optimal architecture of hybrid photonic quantum-classical neural networks. By using a genetic algorithm to jointly optimize classical and quantum components, the authors achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on image classification tasks. The study also quantifies the hardware-specific latency and the unique feature-extraction capabilities of the photonic layer.

Why it may be interesting. It presents an automated approach to optimizing variational quantum circuits on specific photonic hardware, specifically addressing the synergy between classical preprocessing and quantum phase encoding.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Designing effective hybrid photonic quantum-classical architectures is challenging due to a massive, interdependent design space involving classical preprocessing, quantum phase encoding, and photonic circuit structure.

Main result. The Q-PhotoNAS framework achieves high validation accuracies (99.44% on Digits, 99.78% on MNIST) and demonstrates that the photonic layer extracts non-redundant, complementary features to the classical pathway.

Method. A Neural Architecture Search (NAS) framework using a Genetic Algorithm (GA) to evolve 19 hyperparameters across six gene groups, combined with a learnable quantum phase encoding mechanism.

Model / system. A hybrid quantum-classical neural network targeting the Quandela Ascella photonic QPU, utilizing a 12-mode silicon nitride chip with thermo-optic phase shifters, directional couplers, and superconducting nanowire single-photon detectors.

Key observables. Photon-number probability vectors in the Fock basis, validation accuracy, inter-class cosine similarity, and hardware-estimated inference latency.

Important parameters / regimes. 19 hyperparameters including network depth/width, phase encoding scale and bias, learning rate, and 12-mode photonic circuit configuration.

Assumptions / limitations. The evaluation is primarily simulation-based using the Merlin backend; hardware latency estimates assume specific thermal-optic reconfiguration costs and photon loss rates.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the motivation for NAS via architecture complexity; Figure 2/3/5 illustrate the hybrid model pipeline and flow; Figure 4/7/8/9 detail the Q-PhotoNAS GA framework, including crossover and mutation; Figure 10 shows the hardware latency model.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the design challenge, describes the Q-PhotoNAS GA-based framework and learnable encoding, details the experimental setup/simulation, presents performance results on MNIST/Digits, performs quantum contribution analysis, and concludes with hardware latency estimations.

Abstract

Photonic quantum computing is a promising platform for scalable quantum machine learning, but designing effective hybrid architectures remains challenging under hardware and optimization constraints. Existing approaches rely on manually tuned architectures that fail to account for the collaboration between classical preprocessing, phase encoding, and photonic circuit structure, limiting both accuracy and hardware compatibility. In this paper, we propose a neural architecture search framework for hybrid photonic quantum-classical models that combines genetic algorithm-based search with learnable quantum phase encoding to systematically explore the joint design space of classical and quantum components. Our framework encodes 19 hyperparameters across six gene groups and evolves a population of hybrid architectures using group-based crossover, per-gene mutation, and elitism, evaluating each candidate on a short training budget before full retraining of the best found design. We evaluate our framework on two image classification benchmarks, Digits and MNIST, achieving final validation accuracies of 99.44% and 98.78%, respectively, with first-principles execution time estimates on the Quandela Ascella photonic QPU projecting single-image inference at 67 ms (Digits) and 149 ms (MNIST). Our quantum contribution analysis further shows that the photonic layer extracts non-redundant features orthogonal to the classical pathway, providing a measurable accuracy advantage over classical-only baselines. Our results demonstrate that automated architecture search is both practical and impactful for hybrid photonic systems, opening the way for systematic design space exploration of quantum AI on photonic devices.

Summary. The paper presents QuCtrl-BELL, a new compiler-driven software stack designed for scalable trapped-ion quantum control. By separating control logic from hardware parameters, it enables sub-700 ns feedback loops and efficient parameter sweeps. This architecture bridges the gap between user-friendly Python programming and the strict timing requirements of large-scale quantum experiments.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for AMO physicists as it provides the necessary classical control infrastructure for implementing complex, real-time feedback protocols essential for scalable trapped-ion gates and quantum error correction.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of scaling trapped-ion quantum computing requires a control system that provides both high-level software programmability and sub-microsecond, deterministic hardware feedback latency.

Main result. The QuCtrl-BELL stack achieves sub-700 ns end-to-end feedback latency by decoupling control flow from hardware state data, significantly reducing data transfer and memory pressure.

Method. A compiler-driven approach using a Python-embedded DSL that employs a six-stage transpilation pipeline (including CFG, SSA, and register allocation) to separate instruction streams from hardware step-tables.

Model / system. A distributed control platform for trapped-ion quantum computing based on a 9-slot PXIe chassis, featuring RISC-V processors, DDS/AWG boards, and a Trigger & Clock Manager (TCM) for star-topology synchronization.

Key observables. Feedback latency (measured at 690 ns), execution queue timing resolution (4 ns), and step-table compactness.

Important parameters / regimes. Sub-microsecond feedback threshold (<700 ns), 4 ns clock resolution, and 100 ns hardware broadcast delay.

Assumptions / limitations. The 700 ns synchronization overhead per readout event may limit throughput for high-frequency multi-round error correction; camera-based detection is not yet integrated into the compiler.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a standard trapped-ion experiment sequence; Figure 2 depicts the PXIe hardware architecture; Figure 3 and 4 detail the software architecture and compilation pipeline; Figure 5 illustrates the synchronization protocol; Figure 6 provides an oscilloscope trace confirming the 690 ns feedback latency.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the control tradeoff problem, describes the QuCtrl-BELL hardware and software architecture, details the six-stage compilation pipeline, presents performance evaluations regarding latency and memory efficiency, and concludes with future work.

Abstract

As trapped-ion quantum computing scales to larger qubit registers and more complex control protocols, classical control systems face a fundamental tradeoff: sub-microsecond board-level feedback requires tight hardware coupling, whereas maintainability and extensibility require clean, modular software abstractions. This paper presents QuCtrl-BELL (Bell), a compiler-driven software stack for trapped-ion quantum control. The design resolves this tradeoff by decoupling control flow -- including loops, branches, and synchronization -- from hardware state data. A Python-embedded domain-specific language (DSL) is lowered through a six-stage transpilation pipeline covering control flow graph (CFG) construction, static single-assignment (SSA) conversion, liveness analysis, and graph-coloring register allocation. The compiler generates deterministic distributed board-level programs and compact step-table data. A cross-board synchronization protocol supports feedback loops with latency below 700~ns without host intervention. Bell is deployed and evaluated on the QuCtrl-BELL platform (RISC-V + PXIe), demonstrating that a compiler-based infrastructure can provide programmability, deterministic timing, and modularity for scalable trapped-ion quantum control.

A journey through Flatland: What does the antiflatness of a spectrum teach us?

Authors: Barbara Jasser, Daniele Iannotti, Alioscia Hamma
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21664v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. The paper introduces a new framework to study the 'antiflatness' of quantum entanglement spectra, focusing on the fluctuations of eigenvalues rather than just their average. It establishes a new partial ordering for quantum states and proves that maximal spectral deviation is achieved by a specific class of 'jump' spectra. This provides a deeper tool for characterizing quantum correlations and resource theories.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and open quantum systems as it provides a new way to quantify the 'richness' of entanglement beyond simple entropy, specifically looking at how the internal structure of the entanglement spectrum evolves.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to characterize the structural fluctuations within the entanglement spectrum of a quantum state by moving beyond standard average measures like purity or von Neumann entropy.

Main result. The authors introduce 'antiflatness' as a measure of spectral inhomogeneity and develop a new partial ordering called 'antiflat majorization' based on the Rényi entropy spread. They also identify a continuous Pareto frontier of extremal states that achieve maximal antiflatness.

Method. The study employs information geometry (escort distributions and Bregman divergences), mathematical proofs involving Lagrange multipliers and Zorn's Lemma, and statistical analysis of Haar, Bures-Hall, and t-doped Clifford random state ensembles.

Model / system. The framework focuses on bipartite quantum systems with reduced density operators, considering various random state ensembles and contexts such as topological order and many-body localization.

Key observables. Entanglement spectrum, Rényi entropy spread, Capacity of Entanglement, Linear Rényi spread, and Logarithmic antiflatness.

Important parameters / regimes. Rényi parameter (alpha), purity (Tr[rho^2]), dimension of the Hilbert space (dA), and the weight of the largest eigenvalue (r_max).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes bipartite structures and focuses on the properties of the reduced density matrix; it notes that standard majorization is structurally blind to the spectral fluctuations being studied.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates different eigenvalue profiles (flat, decaying, and maximal antiflat) and summarizes information extractable from the second-order moment of the spectrum; Figure 2 shows Rényi entropy curves for different states.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining antiflatness and the new partial ordering, moves to establishing the mathematical properties of the ordering and flatness-preserving operations, explores the geometric structure of the accessible antiflatness volume, and concludes with the identification of maximal antiflatness states and their statistical properties.

Abstract

We explore the concept of antiflatness to characterize the structural fluctuations within the entanglement spectrum of a quantum state (i.e., the spectrum of its reduced density operator). As a measure of the interplay between entanglement and magic, two fundamental quantum resources, antiflatness provides second-order information about quantum correlations that standard average measures fail to capture. Recognizing that standard majorization theory fundamentally orders states by purity and is structurally blind to spectral fluctuations, we introduce a novel partial ordering known as antiflat majorization, based on the Rényi entropy spread. We define Flatness-Preserving Operations (FPOs), establishing new necessary conditions for state convertibility. Furthermore, we unify different measures of antiflatness-such as Capacity of Entanglement, Linear Rényi spread, and Logarithmic antiflatness-using the frameworks of escort distributions and Bregman divergences. We prove that the Capacity of Entanglement can be expressed as a second derivative of the Kullback-Leibler divergence along the escort trajectory, connecting it with the Quantum Fisher Information. Finally, we demonstrate that absolute maximal antiflatness is not achieved by a single universal state, but rather by a continuous Pareto frontier of extremal states with jump spectra, and we analyze the typicality of these spectral fluctuations using Haar, Bures-Hall and t-doped Clifford random state ensembles.

Factorization rule for multitime correlations in non-Markovian open quantum systems

Authors: Thomas K. Bracht, Moritz Cygorek
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22386v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 methods for driven-dissipative 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper presents a new exact factorization rule for calculating multitime correlation functions in non-Markovian systems. By breaking down complex n-time correlations into simpler lower-order parts, the method achieves massive computational speed-ups without sacrificing accuracy. This is particularly useful for studying the optical properties of quantum dots coupled to phonon environments.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful computational shortcut for simulating complex non-Markovian dynamics, allowing for the efficient calculation of high-order correlations and spectra that were previously too expensive to compute.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Calculating multitime correlation functions in non-Markovian open quantum systems is computationally expensive because the breakdown of the Quantum Regression Theorem (QRT) requires long-time, brute-force simulations.

Main result. The authors derive an exact factorization rule that decomposes n-th order correlations into products of lower-order correlations, providing a speed-up of two to three orders of magnitude while maintaining exact accuracy.

Method. The method uses an eigenvector decomposition of the stationary dynamical map within a Markovian embedding framework to decouple time steps.

Model / system. The study focuses on non-Markovian open quantum systems with time-independent Hamiltonians and finite memory times, specifically using semiconductor quantum dots coupled to a super-Ohmic phonon bath as a primary example.

Key observables. Multitime correlation functions, emission spectra S(omega), second-order coherence G(2)(t, tau), and integrated second-order coherence g(2)[0].

Important parameters / regimes. Memory time (tau_c), phonon dynamics (picosecond scale), radiative decay (nanosecond scale), and temperature (4 K).

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonian is time-independent, the initial system-environment state is an uncorrelated product, and the environment has a finite memory time.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the stationary dynamical map and the visual concept of factorization; Figure 2 compares the accuracy of the factorization method against brute-force and QRT for emission spectra; Figure 3 shows time-integrated second-order coherence.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-Markovian correlations, derives the mathematical factorization rule using Liouvillian eigenbasis decomposition, applies the method to quantum dots coupled to phonons, and validates the efficiency and accuracy against brute-force simulations.

Abstract

Experiments performed on quantum systems often measure multitime correlation functions. When quantum systems are weakly coupled to their environment, the time evolution of such correlation functions can be reduced to that of the reduced density matrix by the quantum regression theorem (QRT). While no QRT is available for general non-Markovian open quantum systems, we show that for time-independent Hamiltonians and finite memory times $τ_c$, an exact factorization rule exists that relates higher-order multitime correlations to products of lower-order correlations. Consequently, all information needed to reconstruct $n$-time correlations is contained in a temporal volume of $\mathcal{O}(τ_c^n)$. On the example of quantum dots coupled to phonons, we demonstrate that this factorization makes numerical calculations of multitime correlations extremely efficient and even enables semianalytical solutions in systems where the standard QRT breaks down.

Summary. This paper investigates whether a single quantum measurement run with a definite outcome can be described by a joint unitary evolution. It establishes that if such a unitary process exists, the final state of the environment must retain a trace of the initial system state. This provides a way to experimentally test the compatibility of unitary dynamics with the emergence of definite outcomes.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a concrete, experimentally testable criterion to distinguish between the standard von Neumann/conditioning interpretation and models where individual measurement runs are fundamentally unitary.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Resolving the discrepancy between the von Neumann measurement model, which predicts entangled states, and the empirical reality of single, definite measurement outcomes.

Main result. The author derives a lower bound on the dependence of the post-measurement environment state on the initial system state, providing an experimentally testable signature of unitary dynamics in individual measurement runs.

Method. The study uses mathematical tools such as the trace norm, diamond norm, and the gentle measurement lemma to analyze information transfer and derive bounds on state distinguishability.

Model / system. A tripartite system consisting of a measured system (S), a measurement apparatus (A), and an environment (E), where different measurement outcomes are driven by different underlying unitary maps.

Key observables. The trace distance between the reduced final states of the environment conditioned on the same measurement outcome.

Important parameters / regimes. delta (state deviation), eta (environment fluctuation), and gamma (unitary mechanism fluctuation).

Assumptions / limitations. The multiplicity assumption (different outcomes correspond to different unitaries) and the universality requirement (the mechanism for an outcome is independent of the input state).

Paper structure. The paper identifies the problem of outcomes, proposes an alternative unitary model with outcome-dependent maps, derives a mathematical lower bound for environment state dependence, and discusses experimental observability and noise limitations.

Abstract

We investigated the possibility that a single measurement run with a definite outcome is a joint unitary evolution of all the participating systems, and measurement runs with different definite outcomes correspond to different unitary maps. With reasonable assumptions, we derived a lower bound of the dependence of the environment after measurement on the state of the system before measurement, conditioned on the same measurement outcome. An experimental test of this dependence relation can either serve as evidence that the unitary dynamics and the definite outcome in the orthodox sense cannot be true together or suggest a deviation from the von Neumann measurement model plus a "conditioning" interpretational step.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the variation in Rényi mutual information and stabilizer Rényi entropy in electroweak scattering follows the same dependence on the weak mixing angle. This shared behavior is explained by treating the Yukawa mass insertion as a quantum gate in chirality space. The results suggest that entropic measures can serve as a diagnostic tool for the chiral structure of particle interactions.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel bridge between high-energy particle physics and quantum information theory by interpreting fundamental symmetry breaking as a specific quantum gate operation acting on chirality space.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the relationship between electroweak symmetry breaking (EWSB) and quantum information theoretic measures, specifically looking for a correspondence between Rényi mutual information and stabilizer Rényi entropy.

Main result. The authors establish that RMI and SRE exhibit an identical dependence on the weak mixing angle after angular averaging, a correspondence traced to the Yukawa mass insertion acting as a -iY quantum gate in chirality space.

Method. The study uses helicity amplitude formalism, quantum state tomography to reconstruct density matrices, and angular averaging over the celestial sphere to evaluate entropic probes.

Model / system. The system consists of 2 to 2 elastic scattering processes (e.g., e- mu- to e- mu-) within the Standard Model framework, specifically focusing on neutral-current channels involving Z boson exchange across the EWSB transition.

Key observables. Rényi mutual information (RMI) in the helicity basis and stabilizer Rényi entropy (SRE) in the fixed beam basis.

Important parameters / regimes. Weak mixing angle (sin^2 theta_W), scattering angle (theta), and fermion coupling strengths (kappa_L, kappa_R); high-energy limit (sqrt(s) = 10 TeV).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis uses the high-energy limit to isolate energy-independent contributions and assumes tree-level 2 to 2 elastic scattering.

Figures summary. Paper structure. The paper introduces a unified framework for QFT and QI, describes the scattering model and the quantum gate interpretation of mass insertion, presents the calculation of entropic probes, analyzes the correspondence between RMI and SRE, and discusses the diagnostic role of these measures.

Abstract

We establish a correspondence between two independent entropic probes -- the variation of Rényi mutual information (RMI) across the electroweak symmetry breaking (EWSB) transition and the stabilizer Rényi entropy (SRE) -- in tree-level $2\to 2$ elastic scatterings. After angular averaging, the RMI (helicity basis) and the SRE (fixed beam basis) exhibit identical dependence on $\sin^2θ_W$ within each neutral-current channel. We trace this correspondence to a common physical origin that it's the Yukawa mass insertion acts as a $-\mathrm{i}Y$ quantum gate in chirality space. Minimizing entropies across all processes yields $\sin^2θ_W$ values matching purely axial vector-like couplings in $Z$ boson exchanged channel.

Diagrammatic Monte Carlo for Fermionic Rényi Entanglement Entropy

Authors: Boyuan Shi
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21632v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new Diagrammatic Monte Carlo method to directly compute Renyi entanglement entropy in interacting fermionic systems. By using a graded-swap representation and connected-determinant sampling, the method avoids the traditional sign problem and scales to much larger lattice sizes than exact diagonalization. This provides a powerful tool for studying entanglement in complex, strongly correlated regimes.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a scalable numerical framework to calculate the Renyi entanglement entropy for interacting lattice fermions, specifically overcoming the sign problem and computational costs in 2D and finite-temperature regimes.

Main result. The authors successfully implemented a Diagrammatic Monte Carlo (DiagMC) framework that provides accurate order-by-order interaction corrections for large systems, validated against exact diagonalization for small clusters and applied to a 40x40 lattice.

Method. A direct diagrammatic Monte Carlo framework using a fermionic graded-swap representation, connected-determinant sampling (CDet), and a many-configuration Markov-chain Monte Carlo sampler.

Model / system. The repulsive Hubbard model on a square lattice with periodic boundary conditions, focusing on the entanglement of a square subregion A.

Key observables. Second Renyi entanglement entropy (S2) and its interaction-dependent coefficients.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (U), chemical potential (mu), inverse temperature (beta), and subregion size/geometry.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary computational bottleneck is memory usage rather than the conventional auxiliary-field sign problem; the method is oriented toward finite-size/finite-region calculations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares order-by-order CDet Monte Carlo coefficients against exact diagonalization for a 3x3 cluster and presents production results for a 40x40 lattice.

Paper structure. The paper follows a logical progression from the theoretical formulation of the graded-swap path integral to the derivation of the determinant expansion, followed by the description of the MCMC sampler, validation via benchmark, and finally large-scale production results.

Abstract

We develop a direct diagrammatic Monte Carlo framework for the Renyi entanglement entropy of interacting lattice fermions. The method starts from the fermionic graded-swap representation of Z_n[A]=Tr_Aρ_A^n, which converts the entropy problem into a replicated path integral with mixed temporal boundary conditions on the entangling region. In this representation the replica momenta are half-shifted, q_m=(2m+1)π/n, and the interaction expansion has a determinant form suitable for connected-determinant summation. We combine this expansion with a many-configuration Markov-chain Monte Carlo sampler to obtain order-by-order corrections for very large systems to very high orders. As a benchmark, we compare the order-by-order coefficients of a 3*3 Hubbard cluster with exact diagonalization. We then report a production calculation for a large periodic lattice with a square subregions. The dominant system-size limitation is therefore memory rather than a conventional auxiliary-field sign problem. The results provide a step toward diagrammatic calculations of fermionic entanglement observables in regimes where direct quantum Monte Carlo sampling is costly or sign-problem limited.

Long-range nonstabilizerness of topologically encoded states from mutual information

Authors: David Aram Korbany, Tyler D. Ellison, David T. Stephen, Lorenzo Piroli
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22424v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5

Summary. The paper investigates how mutual information between disjoint regions on a torus can detect long-range nonstabilizerness in topological phases. It provides a complete classification for the Toric Code and shows that while the method is powerful, it has limitations in non-abelian systems. The results also impose fundamental constraints on which logical gates can be implemented fault-tolerantly.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and quantum information as it provides a new information-theoretic tool to detect non-stabilizer resources in topological phases, which is crucial for understanding the limits of fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to characterize and detect long-range nonstabilizerness (LRN) in topologically encoded states in 2D, specifically determining if mutual information can serve as a reliable diagnostic tool.

Main result. The authors prove that for the Z2 toric code, non-integer mutual information between non-overlapping regions containing non-contractible loops provides a full classification of LRN, whereas for non-abelian Fibonacci models, the method detects LRN for all but a finite subset of states.

Method. The study uses information-theoretic analysis of mutual information, modular transformations (S and T matrices) of the torus, and entropic uncertainty relations (Maassen-Uffink bound) to relate anyon properties to LRN.

Model / system. The research focuses on 2D topologically ordered systems defined on a torus, specifically the Z2 Toric Code and non-abelian string-net models like the doubled Fibonacci topological order.

Key observables. Mutual information (I), von Neumann entropy, and anyon labels/flux.

Important parameters / regimes. Shallow-depth local quantum circuits (depth O(polylog(L))), thermodynamic limit (N -> infinity), and modular group transformations.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is a regular 2D lattice; regions are sufficiently distant and large enough to encompass Wilson loop operators; the study focuses on minimal-entropy states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Toric Code operators and non-contractible cycles; Figure 2 illustrates spatial regions A and B; Figure 3 shows Wilson loop operator mappings; Figure 4 depicts the string-net lattice; Figure 5 shows a Dehn twist via lattice shear; Figure 6 shows a pictorial derivation of trace equations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces LRN and the problem of 2D detection, establishes a theorem for LRN detection using mutual information, applies this to the Z2 Toric Code for full classification, extends the analysis to non-abelian string-net models, and concludes with implications for fault-tolerant logical gates.

Abstract

We study long-range nonstabilizerness (LRN), namely the obstruction to remove nonstabilizerness with shallow-depth local quantum circuits. In one-dimensional settings, the mutual information between disconnected spatial regions has proven to be a powerful tool to diagnose LRN. In this work, we focus on encoded states of two-dimensional topologically-ordered systems, and explore the ability of the mutual information to serve as a diagnostic of LRN. Focusing on the concrete setting of lattice models defined on a torus, we show that information about LRN can be gained from the analysis of the mutual information between non-overlapping regions containing non-contractible loops, and of the change of such mutual information under modular real-space transformations. We exemplify this idea in the toric code and the non-abelian string-net model with doubled Fibonacci topological order. In the former case, we show that the mutual information provides a full classification, certifying LRN for all encoded non-stabilizer states. In the latter case, instead, our approach does not lead to a full classification, as it detects LRN for all states except from a finite subset with special transformation properties under the modular group. Finally, we discuss how our results on LRN constrain the logical gates that can be implemented fault-tolerantly on the torus.

Summary. This paper introduces a generalized framework for entanglement measures based on 'unified entropy,' which encompasses Tsallis and Renyi entropies. We investigate the solid-state implementation of a Dicke-like quantum battery consisting of a two-dimensional material-based Josephson junction inductively coupled to a resonator, using graphene as a representative example. In this configuration, Andreev bound states naturally act as non-interacting, energetically non-degenerate two-level systems, and the setup allows for both single-photon and two-photon resonant processes. The coupling between the LC-circuit flux and the supercurrent through the junction gives rise to peculiar longitudinal interaction terms that have no counterpart in the conventional Dicke model. These additional couplings can enhance energy storage for a proper range of parameters. The proposed architecture also enables an alternative, but equivalent, charging protocol that relies on tuning the superconducting phase difference across the junction.

Summary. This paper establishes a unified theoretical framework to predict the phase sensitivity of SU(1,1) interferometers for any arbitrary input state. Abstract

We provide a general theoretical derivation of the phase sensitivity achieved by SU(1,1) interferometers under homodyne detection. The general expressions obtained accommodate arbitrary input states and include internal and external losses. In this systematic review, both full SU(1,1) interferometers with two parametric amplifiers and the truncated interferometers with only one parametric amplifier are examined. We investigate scenarios involving both single-output ports and joint homodyne detection, and consider parametric amplifiers with equal gains or with a boosted gain second amplifier. Our analytical formulation provides physical insight and understanding of the improvements in the sensitivity, which are shown to originate from noise reduction and/or signal amplification, depending on the configurations and practical implementations. Surprisingly, the configuration with single-output mode detection and parametric amplifiers with equal gains exhibits the highest robustness to very high internal losses. We finally apply this framework to a ubiquitous $|α,0\rangle$ input two-mode coherent probe state. This approach permits the comparison of different strategies and the optimization of the interferometer performance in the presence of losses. In particular, we determine which amplification and detection configurations provide the best performance, depending on the level of losses. This exemplifies how this general analytical approach provides a powerful tool to design quantum-enhanced interferometers and achieve optimal sensitivity with selected probe states and homodyne detection.
