⭐ Interaction-enabled metal-insulator phase transition in a driven quantum gas

Highlighted author(s): Manuele Landini
Authors: Camilo Cantillano, Karthick Ramanathan, Zekai Chen, Ang Yang, Emilio Aguilera-Valdes, Lei Ying, Manuele Landini, Hanns-Christoph Nägerl, Yanliang Guo

Summary. The researchers experimentally observed a metal-insulator phase transition in a 3D driven Bose-Einstein condensate. By tuning interactions and driving strength, they identified a sharp boundary between a localized phase (MBDL) and a diffusive phase. This work clarifies how interactions and quantum coherence jointly govern transport in isolated many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a direct experimental realization of a dynamical phase transition in a many-body system, demonstrating how interactions can actively reorganize Hilbert space to enable transport, which is fundamental to understanding the emergence of classicality and ergodicity.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the competition between quantum interference, which promotes localization, and interactions, which promote ergodicity and diffusion, in a driven many-body system.

Main result. Discovery of an interaction-enabled metal-insulator phase transition in a 3D quantum gas, characterized by a sharp dynamical boundary between many-body dynamical localization and diffusive energy absorption.

Method. Experimental implementation of a 3D Quantum Kicked Rotor using a Cesium BEC, combined with finite-time scaling analysis and interaction-quench tests to verify the transition's nature.

Model / system. A 3D quantum gas consisting of approximately 10,000 133-Cs atoms in a Bose-Einstein Condensate, subject to a periodic optical lattice pulse protocol (Quantum Kicked Rotor) with tunable s-wave scattering via Feshbach resonance.

Key observables. Momentum distribution n(k), energy proxy E(Np) (second moment of momentum distribution), and spatial density distribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Kick strength (kappa), interaction strength (as), number of kicks (Np), and pulse period (T).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated as a closed Floquet many-body system; the scaling analysis assumes an effective dimensionality of d=3.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the experimental setup and momentum distributions; Fig 2 presents the localization-delocalization phase diagram; Fig 3 demonstrates the reversibility of the transition via interaction ramps; Fig 5 shows interaction quench effects on peak width; Fig 6 shows energy proxy evolution at the critical point.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of interaction vs. coherence, describes the 3D BEC experimental platform and QKR protocol, presents the mapping of the phase diagram and scaling analysis, demonstrates the reversibility of the transition, and concludes with the implications for the quantum-to-classical transition.

Abstract

Particle transport and energy flow are central for our understanding of a wealth of phenomena in physics and the natural sciences. Interactions are generically expected to promote ergodicity and diffusive behavior, yet quantum interference can arrest transport and prevent energy absorption, defying classical expectations. How interactions and quantum coherence compete remains a fundamental open question. Here, we experimentally investigate their interplay in a periodically driven, three-dimensional (3D) quantum gas with tunable interactions. Strikingly, we find that interactions give rise to a sharp dynamical boundary that separates localization from diffusive energy absorption. By tuning the driving amplitude and interaction strength, we map the localization-delocalization phase diagram and characterize the boundary via finite-time scaling. On the insulating side, we observe many-body dynamical localization (MBDL) for a wide range of parameters, finding arrested transport in momentum space. Near the boundary, transport becomes subdiffusive, whereas in the delocalized regime we observe classical diffusion, yielding a metal-insulator transition that we interpret in terms of localization in many-body Hilbert space. Our results exemplify an interaction-enabled dynamical phase transition in a closed Floquet many-body system and clarify how coherence and interactions jointly govern the quantum-to-classical transition.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that local monitoring of particle loss at a quantum point contact fundamentally changes entanglement growth from logarithmic to linear, followed by a decay. The study links these entanglement dynamics to charge depletion and provides an analytical quasiparticle framework to explain the transition.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep connection between measurement-induced dynamics, non-equilibrium transport, and the Page curve, offering a bridge between mesoscopic physics and the study of open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how continuous local monitoring of particle losses at a quantum point contact (QPC) reshapes the entanglement dynamics and charge transport compared to the unitary case.

Main result. Local monitoring transforms entanglement growth from logarithmic to a three-regime process: linear growth (volume-law), power-law decay, and eventual exponential decay to zero. This dynamics is driven by an emergent bias voltage created by the losses.

Method. The authors use a quantum jump protocol described by a stochastic Schrodinger equation and a Lindblad master equation, supplemented by a quasiparticle picture and full counting statistics (FCS) analysis.

Model / system. A quantum point contact connecting two metallic leads modeled as fermionic tight-binding chains with open boundary conditions, subject to a monitoring protocol that detects particle losses at a specific site.

Key observables. Entanglement entropy (S_L,R), particle imbalance, total charge depletion, and full counting statistics (FCS) of charge transfer.

Important parameters / regimes. Monitoring/dissipation rate (gamma/J), system size (L), and the hopping parameter (J).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is modeled as free fermions; the quasiparticle picture assumes entanglement production is a binomial process of fermion detection/non-detection.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the system schematic; Figure 2 displays entanglement entropy dynamics and its volume-law scaling; Figure 3 illustrates particle imbalance and the relationship between entanglement and total particle number.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the monitored QPC model, presents the numerical results for entanglement and charge dynamics, develops a quasiparticle-based analytical framework to explain the observed regimes, and connects the findings to experimental probes like FCS.

Abstract

We compute the entanglement dynamics across a monitored quantum point contact, where particle losses are recorded on a given site, and demonstrate how this single-site local monitoring substantially reshapes the entanglement production. Contrary to the unitary case, where entanglement entropy grows logarithmically in time, here we find first a linear growth, up to a maximum value displaying volume-law scaling, and then a slow decay to zero, as the system empties out. We capture this crossover using a quasiparticle picture, where the first linear growth arises due to an emergent bias voltage established by the losses, which eventually decays away as the system depletes. We connect our results to studies of the Page curve and to experimentally relevant probes, via full counting statistics of charge transfer across a subregion, with only a single channel to unravel leading to a favorable scaling of the postselection overhead. Natural platforms for this setting include mesoscopic systems and ultracold atoms.

Summary. This paper shows that spatially structured dissipation can be used to select and control nonlinear resonant fronts in a driven-dissipative Bose-Hubbard lattice. By tuning the dissipation gradient and detuning ramp, one can navigate between pinned, chaotic, and localized phases. This provides a mechanism for creating reconfigurable transport barriers in programmable quantum simulators.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates how engineered dissipation can act as a control knob for creating and stabilizing complex nonequilibrium structures like interfaces and transport barriers in bosonic lattices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how spatially structured dissipation and a Stark-induced detuning ramp can be used to organize driven quantum matter and create reconfigurable transport barriers or nonequilibrium interfaces.

Main result. The study identifies a mechanism where a nonlinear resonance condition fixes a pinned density front, which can transition into pattern-locked states, spatiotemporal chaos, or localized states depending on the dissipation gradient and detuning slope.

Method. The authors use the generalized Gross-Pitaevskii equation (gGPE) for large-scale dynamics and the Truncated Wigner Approximation (TWA) to incorporate quantum fluctuations, supplemented by Bogoliubov-de Gennes stability analysis.

Model / system. A two-dimensional driven-dissipative Bose-Hubbard lattice subject to a single-photon drive, a linear Stark potential (detuning ramp), and a spatially graded loss rate.

Key observables. Local density, total particle number, center-of-mass, generalized imbalance, transverse spectra, and front width.

Important parameters / regimes. Stark tilt (F), dissipation gradient (gamma), hopping strength (J), interaction strength (U), and drive strength (Omega).

Assumptions / limitations. The TWA is assumed to be valid in the large-occupation regime, and the 2D dynamics are analyzed using a 1D reduction for the pinned-front regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the model; Figure 3 presents global and high-resolution phase diagrams in the (F, gamma) plane, illustrating the staircase structure and particle number transitions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model and the concept of dissipation-selected fronts, develops the theoretical framework for front scaling and stability, presents a phase diagram of dynamical regimes, and concludes with the implications for interface engineering.

Abstract

Spatially structured dissipation organizes driven quantum matter beyond Hamiltonian control. We show that a dissipation gradient combined with a Stark-induced detuning ramp selects a nonlinear resonance slice in a two-dimensional driven-dissipative Bose-Hubbard lattice, producing a pinned density front in generalized Gross-Pitaevskii simulations. The underlying resonance condition fixes the front position, while its Airy-like profile obeys a width scaling set by tunneling stiffness and the effective detuning slope. Treating the front as an emergent interface explains how tuning the selected resonance toward the minimum-loss side yields Peierls-Nabarro depinning steps, discrete transverse pattern locking, spatiotemporal chaos, and minimum-loss localization. Center-of-mass and generalized-imbalance diagnostics map these outcomes into a dynamical phase diagram as detuning-ramp slope and dissipation-gradient strength vary. The results suggest structured dissipation as a mechanism for reconfigurable transport barriers and nonequilibrium interfaces in programmable bosonic lattices.

Summary. This paper reviews the emerging field of dissipative quantum chaos, focusing on how the spectral properties of open quantum systems can identify chaotic versus integrable dynamics. It connects non-Hermitian random matrix theory with physical models like the Lindblad equation to provide tools for characterizing non-unitary evolution. The work is significant for its ability to bridge the gap between quantum-to-classical transitions and the study of many-body open systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a comprehensive synthesis of how dissipation modifies the signatures of chaos, which is crucial for understanding decoherence and many-body dynamics in NISQ devices, ultracold atoms, and superconducting qubits.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to establish a theoretical framework for dissipative quantum chaos (DQC) by extending the concepts of Hamiltonian quantum chaos to open quantum systems, specifically focusing on distinguishing between integrable and chaotic regimes in non-unitary dynamics.

Main result. The authors review the recent revival of DQC, demonstrating that the spectral properties of Lindbladians and CPTP maps (such as level spacing statistics and complex spacing ratios) serve as robust signatures of chaos, while also addressing the nuances of the quantum-to-classical correspondence.

Method. The study utilizes Random Matrix Theory (non-Hermitian ensembles like Ginibre), spectral analysis of Lindblad operators, semiclassical approximations (such as the Truncated Wigner Approximation), and free probability theory.

Model / system. The review covers various models including the Lindblad master equation (GKLS form), CPTP maps, the dissipative quantum kicked rotor/top, and many-body systems like the Dicke model and Bose-Hubbard dimers.

Key observables. Spectral statistics (level spacing distributions, complex spacing ratios, cubic-level repulsion), steady-state density operators, Wigner/Husimi functions, and photon-emission statistics.

Important parameters / regimes. Dissipation strength (Gamma), Hamiltonian vs. dissipative weight (alpha), number of decay channels (rank R), and the semiclassical limit (large spin S or particle number N).

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes Markovian dynamics (Lindblad form) and relies on the validity of non-Hermitian Random Matrix Theory as a proxy for universal chaotic behavior.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the relationship between Lindblad operators, CPTP maps, and quantum states, highlighting how spectral properties are used to construct measures of chaos. Figure 4 shows the spectral density of random Lindbladians under varying Hamiltonian weights.

Paper structure. The paper follows a logical progression from the fundamental definitions of DQC and its historical development to the mathematical frameworks of Lindbladians and CPTP maps, followed by symmetry classifications, spectral signatures of chaos, semiclassical limits, and finally addressing challenges and discrepancies in the theory.

Abstract

Dissipative quantum chaos is an emerging theory that is expected to extend the ideas, concepts, and methodology of conventional Hamiltonian quantum chaos from coherent evolution to open quantum dynamics. The new theory should provide a set of tools to distinguish chaotic open quantum systems from integrable ones, as well as quantitative measures of their chaoticity (or, conversely, integrability). The foundations of this theory were laid in the late 1980s, and from the very start it was clear that, like its Hamiltonian predecessor, it had to be based on the spectral properties of the operators governing open quantum evolution. After these first steps, the field remained relatively quiet for many years and it is only over the last decade that the development of dissipative quantum chaos has received a strong boost, as confirmed by a large number of publications on this topic and, very recently, the first experiments performed to test its theoretical predictions. In this chapter, we review these recent developments and outline the basic foundations of dissipative quantum chaos.

Summary. This paper proposes a method to charge remote quantum batteries using waveguide-mediated interference instead of direct coupling. By tuning propagation and coupling phases, the researchers show how to achieve unidirectional, nonreciprocal energy flow. The study also reveals that quadratic driving can create non-passive battery states where work extractability (ergotropy) becomes a distinct metric from stored energy.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a versatile design principle for directional energy transfer in quantum networks using interference and reservoir engineering, which is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing efficient protocols for remote quantum battery charging where the charger and battery are spatially separated, specifically overcoming the need for direct local Hamiltonian couplings and achieving unidirectional energy transfer.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that phase-tunable waveguide-QED architectures can realize nonreciprocal charging, with the 3P+M configuration achieving both perfect nonreciprocity and battery-dominated storage, while 3P setups offer distance-insensitive directionality.

Method. The study uses the SLH formalism to derive a quantum master equation and analyzes the time evolution of first and second moments under linear and quadratic driving regimes.

Model / system. A waveguide QED architecture consisting of two single-mode cavities (a driven charger and a remote battery) coupled to a 1D waveguide, exploring four configurations: 4P, 4P+M, 3P, and 3P+M (open and mirror-terminated).

Key observables. Nonreciprocal ratio (R), relative storage ratio (eta), stored energy (Eb), ergotropy (W), and anomalous second moments.

Important parameters / regimes. Propagation phases (phi_w), coupling phases (theta), waveguide coupling rate (gamma), driving amplitude (Omega), and cavity decay rates (kappa).

Assumptions / limitations. The system assumes a waveguide-mediated regime with no direct local interaction, negligible retardation effects, and small frequency mismatch between modes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows schematic illustrations of the four waveguide-QED configurations; Figure 2 shows time evolution of scaled energies; Figure 4 shows steady-state relative storage ratios.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of remote charging, describes the waveguide-QED models and configurations, provides analytical derivations for the master equation coefficients, compares the performance of the four setups, and extends the analysis to quadratic driving and ergotropy.

Abstract

Remote quantum batteries require directional and controllable energy transfer between spatially separated quantum nodes, yet most existing protocols rely on direct charger-battery Hamiltonian couplings. Here we propose a phase-tunable waveguide-QED architecture for remote quantum-battery charging, in which a driven charger and a remote battery are coupled solely via engineered waveguide-mediated interference, without any direct local interaction. We systematically compare four configurations: two-giant-emitter and giant-small-emitter hybrids, each with open or mirror-terminated waveguides. By engineering the propagation and coupling phases, the waveguide-mediated coherent exchange interaction and collective dissipation can be balanced to suppress the backward channel while retaining a finite forward channel, thereby realizing cascaded-like unidirectional charging. Our analysis shows that nonreciprocity and storage efficiency can be independently engineered, offering design flexibility for different quantum network scenarios. The giant-small-emitter mirror-terminated configuration simultaneously achieves perfect nonreciprocity and battery-dominated storage, while both giant-small-emitter configurations exhibit distance-insensitive directionality. Extending the scheme to quadratic driving, we show that anomalous second moments render the battery state non-passive, making ergotropy a performance metric distinct from stored energy. These results establish phase-tunable waveguide networks as a versatile platform for remote quantum-energy transfer and provide design principles for directional and work-extractable energy storage in quantum networks.

Summary. The paper proposes an experimental setup using a Bose-Einstein Condensate to test the response of a particle detector in a superposition of locations. Abstract

We propose a realizable experiment to test the response of a particle detector prepared in a superposition of locations interacting with a relativistic quantum field. Using a beamsplitter to prepare two superposed branches of a modulated laser probe, these branches are directed to intersect a pancake-shaped Bose-Einstein condensate at two separate locations. The branches are then recombined with another beamsplitter. Heterodyning one of the outputs, the response function corresponding to an Unruh-deWitt detector in a superposition of locations interacting with a (2+1)-dimensional massless scalar field is shown to appear in the difference photocurrent power spectrum. Summary. The paper proposes a new way to describe dissipative non-Abelian plasmas by treating the environment as a dynamical color-charged sector. By keeping environmental variables explicit, the authors transform difficult non-local memory effects into a local, gauge-covariant theory. This framework successfully recovers standard results like Hard Thermal Loops while ensuring thermodynamic consistency.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems as it provides a rigorous framework for 'Markovian embedding'—representing non-Markovian, non-local dynamics through an enlarged local system-environment model while preserving fundamental gauge symmetries.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a local, gauge-covariant effective field theory (EFT) for non-Abelian gauge theories that accounts for non-local memory effects, dissipation, and fluctuations without violating fundamental symmetries like the color Ward identity.

Main result. The authors construct a 'bottom-up' open EFT where non-local and non-Markovian effects (such as Hard Thermal Loops) emerge naturally from a local, Markovian system-environment theory by explicitly retaining slow environmental response variables.

Method. The study uses the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism and a 'Markov embedding' approach, where environmental variables are integrated out using retarded boundary conditions to recover non-local kernels.

Model / system. A non-Abelian (Yang-Mills) gauge theory interacting with a dynamical color environment, modeled via a system-environment EFT featuring a color-frame variable, a Stueckelberg-like field, and a color-current sector.

Key observables. Color transport, color-charge storage, susceptibility, conductivity, relaxation, noise kernels, and the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) induced current.

Important parameters / regimes. High-temperature non-Abelian plasmas, the collisionless limit, the relaxation rate (Gamma), and the Debye mass scale.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a separation of scales between soft modes and microscopic degrees of freedom, and a factorized initial density operator.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic comparison between the proposed non-Abelian open EFT and a dissipative gravitational EFT, illustrating how energy-momentum or color charge is exchanged with the environment.

Paper structure. The paper reviews the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism and gravitational analogies, constructs the environmental color frame and sources, introduces the velocity-resolved hard response, and uses an appendix to demonstrate the dynamical KMS completion of the dissipative color response.

Abstract

We develop a bottom-up open effective field theory (EFT) for non-Abelian gauge theories within the Schwinger--Keldysh formalism. Instead of integrating out the environment completely and starting from a nonlocal influence functional, we retain the slow environmental response variables explicitly and construct a local system-environment EFT. The environmental sector is described by a dynamical color-frame variable, Stückelberg-like field, and an associated color-current sector, which gives the nontrivial interactions and dissipation between the system and the environment. The resulting construction provides a gauge-covariant Markov embedding of nonlocal and non-Markovian color response. After integrating out the retained environmental variables with retarded boundary conditions, the reduced system theory acquires nonlocal dissipative kernels and stochastic sources. We show that the hard thermal loop response arises naturally as a particular realization of the retained environmental response. Our framework provides a local open-EFT description of color transport, memory effects, and fluctuation-dissipation structure in non-Abelian plasmas, and offers a systematic starting point for dissipative Yang--Mills EFTs with dynamical environments.

Authors: Arunabha Das, Sayan Mondal, Debarupa Saha, Ujjwal Sen
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22583v1

Summary. This paper explores how replacing a thermal reservoir with quantum measurements can optimize a quantum Otto engine. It finds that generalized measurements (POVMs) and non-adiabatic processes can increase work extraction and efficiency, even when the energy cost of resetting the measurement apparatus is considered.

Why it may be interesting. It provides fundamental insights into the thermodynamics of measurement-driven processes and demonstrates how controlling non-adiabaticity and measurement type can be used to optimize quantum heat engines.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the optimal work extraction and efficiency of measurement-based quantum Otto engines, specifically comparing PVM and POVM implementations under adiabatic and non-adiabatic regimes.

Main result. POVM-based engines can outperform PVM-based engines even after accounting for the thermodynamic cost of resetting the auxiliary system, and non-adiabaticity can enhance both work output and efficiency in certain compression regimes.

Method. Analytical optimization of measurement parameters and unitary operators, combined with numerical optimization using the dual annealing algorithm and Naimark's dilation theorem.

Model / system. A single-qubit working substance undergoing a four-stroke Otto cycle, where the hot-bath thermalization is replaced by quantum measurements (PVM or POVM) using an auxiliary system.

Key observables. Extractable work, efficiency, heat exchange, and net work output after accounting for auxiliary reset costs.

Important parameters / regimes. Compression ratio (frequency ratio), non-adiabaticity parameter (transition probability), cold bath temperature, and measurement basis angles.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to the non-inverted population regime and focuses on two-outcome POVMs implemented via a single auxiliary qubit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the schematic of the measurement-based Otto cycle; Figure 2 plots extractable work versus non-adiabaticity; Figure 4 illustrates the trade-off between POVM work gain and auxiliary reset cost as a function of temperature.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the measurement-based engine model, analyzes the adiabatic limit for PVM and POVM, extends the analysis to the non-adiabatic regime, evaluates the thermodynamic cost of POVM implementation, and concludes with operational guidelines.

Abstract

Measurement-based quantum heat engines have attracted significant interest as alternatives to conventional thermal engines, as they replace the hot thermal reservoir with quantum measurements, thereby offering greater controllability and simpler implementation. Motivated by these advantages, we investigate a measurement-driven quantum Otto engine with a qubit working substance and study the optimal work extractable from such engines, including whether their performance can surpass that of conventional quantum Otto cycles. We analyze the engine in both the infinite-time (adiabatic) and finite-time (non-adiabatic) regimes, considering two distinct implementations obtained through optimization over all projection-valued measurements (PVMs) and over all two-outcome positive operator-valued measurements (POVMs). We show that measurement-based engines can outperform conventional quantum Otto engines within specific parameter regimes and that POVM-based engines can yield higher optimal work extraction than PVM-based ones. Furthermore, by incorporating the thermodynamic cost associated with resetting the auxiliary system required for POVM implementation, we demonstrate that the resulting net work output can still exceed that of PVM-based engines under suitable conditions on the spectral gaps and cold bath temperature. We also identify regimes in which non-adiabatic implementations can yield higher work output and efficiency than their adiabatic counterparts. Our study provides operational guidelines for designing improved measurement-driven quantum Otto engines.

Realizing tunable non-Hermitian skin effects in dynamical quantum systems via the relative phase between multiple time-periodic driving

Authors: Huan-Yu Wang
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21959v1

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper shows that the relative phase between different periodic drives can be used to turn the non-Hermitian skin effect on or off. It also demonstrates that this phase can control the direction in which particles accumulate at the boundaries in 2D systems. This discovery offers a new way to engineer skin density profiles in platforms like optical lattices or circuit lattices.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel mechanism for Floquet engineering of non-Hermitian topology, offering a way to dynamically tune the spatial distribution of modes in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Controlling the emergence and the localization direction of the non-Hermitian skin effect (NHSE) in dynamical quantum systems.

Main result. The relative phase between multiple time-periodic driving components can act as a switch to activate or deactivate NHSE and can alter the spatial direction of skin mode localization in higher dimensions.

Method. Floquet theory and Magnus expansion are used to analyze the long-time averaged effective Hamiltonian and the winding number topological invariant.

Model / system. Non-Hermitian quantum chains and 2D lattices (such as the NH-QWZ model) subject to multiple time-periodic driving (Floquet systems) with non-reciprocal tunneling.

Key observables. Winding number (C), Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR), and skin density profile.

Important parameters / regimes. Relative phase (phi) between driving components, driving frequency (omega), and tunneling amplitudes.

Assumptions / limitations. High-frequency limit (omega >> Hamiltonian scales) for the Magnus expansion; focus on PT-symmetric systems.

Figures summary. Figure 1 demonstrates the switching of NHSE on and off by tuning the relative phase and shows the corresponding change in the winding number.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of NHSE control, presents a 1D model demonstrating phase-induced switching, extends the analysis to 2D systems for directional control, and discusses potential experimental realizations.

Abstract

We demonstrate how the relative phase between the multiple time periodic driving can decide the emergence and the favorable localization direction of non-Hermitian skin modes. For the static non-Hermitian quantum chain with parity time symmetry, non-Hermitian skin effects (NHSEs) can be prohibited. As the dynamical driving is turned on, NHSEs get artificially reactivated, where the relative phase can serve as the controlling switch by breaking the temporal symmetry constraints. Meanwhile, a change of relative phase can also alter the spatial structures of the long-time averaged effective Hamiltonian, which will consequently lead to the variation of skin localization direction for systems of higher dimensions. Our formalisms can be generally realized in diverse optical and mechanical platforms, and will pave the way for realizing tunable skin density profiles.
