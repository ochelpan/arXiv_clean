Adiabatic Quantum Phase Estimation

Authors: Alexander Schmidhuber, Seth Lloyd
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22770v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces an adiabatic protocol for Quantum Phase Estimation that achieves optimal Heisenberg-limited scaling. By using a specialized 'superadiabatic' scheduling technique, the authors suppress errors exponentially, making adiabatic hardware a viable platform for high-precision phase estimation.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum computing and open quantum systems as it provides a way to implement fundamental algorithms on analog/adiabatic hardware with optimal scaling and robustness to dephasing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an adiabatic version of Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) that achieves optimal Heisenberg-limited scaling in both precision and failure probability while avoiding the deep circuits required by gate-based methods.

Main result. The authors present a superadiabatic adiabatic protocol using a Denjoy-Carleman clock that achieves near-exponential suppression of diabatic leakage, resulting in a total runtime of O(1/epsilon * log(1/delta)).

Method. The protocol uses a multi-stage adiabatic evolution with a reparameterized Roland-Cerf schedule and a non-quasianalytic Denjoy-Carleman clock to ensure vanishing derivatives at the boundaries of each stage.

Model / system. An n-qubit system with Hamiltonian Hs coupled to an m-qubit ancilla register, utilizing transverse fields, pairwise ZiZj interactions, and a coupling between the system energy and a single ancilla qubit.

Key observables. Diabatic leakage (transition probability), precision (epsilon), and failure probability (delta).

Important parameters / regimes. Precision epsilon, failure probability delta, adiabatic parameter A, and the number of stages m.

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonian must be smooth (C-infinity) with vanishing derivatives at the endpoints, and the eigenvalues must remain separated by a minimum energy gap.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares linear, Roland-Cerf, and superadiabatic schedules; Figure 2 shows numerical evidence that superadiabatic schedules suppress transitions exponentially compared to power-law decay in standard schedules.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of adiabatic QPE, defines the multi-stage Hamiltonian model, develops the mathematical framework for superadiabatic estimates using Denjoy-Carleman clocks, proves leakage bounds and runtime complexity, and demonstrates applications to adiabatic Grover search.

Abstract

Quantum phase estimation (QPE) is a central algorithmic primitive that estimates eigenvalues of a Hamiltonian up to precision $ε$ in Heisenberg-limited time $T=Θ(1/ε)$. Standard gate-based implementations of QPE require deep controlled time-evolution circuits and are not native to analog hardware. Here, we present a simple adiabatic protocol for QPE that achieves (up to logarithmic factors) the optimal Heisenberg-limited scaling $T = O\left( \frac{1}ε \log\left(δ^{-1}\right)\right)$ in both the precision $ε$ and failure probability $δ$. By encoding eigenvalues in populations of computational basis states rather than complex phases, our approach is naturally robust against certain dephasing errors. The adiabatic protocol only requires the ability to couple a single ancilla qubit to the system Hamiltonian as well as pairwise couplings within the ancilla register.

Summary. This Comment argues that a recently reported quantum clock based on a double quantum dot is actually a classical system. The author shows that the reported entropic costs are merely consequences of experimental engineering, such as measurement amplification, rather than fundamental limits of quantum timekeeping.

Why it may be interesting. This is relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and non-equilibrium thermodynamics as it addresses the fundamental distinction between engineering dissipation and the intrinsic thermodynamic costs of quantum-to-classical information extraction.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper challenges the claim that a double quantum dot system functions as a quantum clock and disputes the reported fundamental thermodynamic costs of timekeeping.

Main result. The author demonstrates that the system exhibits classical behavior lacking intrinsic temporal correlations and that the reported entropy production is due to engineering dissipation rather than fundamental quantum timekeeping costs.

Method. The author performs a formal critique of the original work's thermodynamic analysis and statistical estimators, specifically analyzing the nature of entropy production and the role of measurement amplification.

Model / system. A double quantum dot (DQD) system acting as an open system exchanging charge and energy with a thermal environment, including charge sensors (dc and rf reflectometry).

Key observables. Entropy production per tick (Sigma_tick), combined entropy from amplification and measurement (P/T), and time estimators (Theta_net and Theta_opt).

Important parameters / regimes. Bias voltage (V_DQD) and the reported entropic cost of approximately 60 kB per tick.

Assumptions / limitations. The original work assumes DQD dynamics can be extended to coherent dynamics and relies on a Markov process assumption for the Theta_opt estimator.

Figures summary. Table I decomposes the DQD system into its constituent parts (DQD, sensors, and environment) to classify their quantum or classical nature.

Paper structure. The paper begins by identifying two primary flaws in the original Letter, then proceeds to critique the quantum nature of the device, followed by a critique of the statistical time estimation methods, and concludes with a rebuttal of the thermodynamic conclusions.

Abstract

A recent Letter by Wadhia et al. reports a realization of a quantum clock using a double quantum dot (DQD) [Phys. Rev. Lett. 135, 200407 (2005)]. This Comment identifies two fundamental issues: (I) the claimed ``quantum clock" exhibits only classical behavior and lacks intrinsic temporal correlations between ticks; it is not sufficient for accurate time as a good clock. (II) the thermodynamic analysis misassigns entropy production and conflates amplification with measurement; the reported combined entropy is an engineering dissipation, not a fundamental cost of quantum timekeeping.

Competing incommensurability, electronic correlations, and superconductivity in a hybrid transition metal dichalcogenide

Authors: Jean C. Souza, Moshe Haim, Lorenzo Crippa, Hyeonhu Bae, Edanel Fishbein, Jonathan Ruhman, Binghai Yan, Amit Kanigel, Roser Valentí, Nurit Avraham, Haim Beidenkopf

Summary. This paper identifies that an intrinsic incommensurate potential in 4Hb-TaS2 regulates electronic correlations and charge transfer between its 1T and 1H layers. This modulation creates a doped Mott regime and establishes a complex competition between the incommensurate landscape, charge-density waves, and superconductivity. The findings highlight the importance of bulk incommensurability in tuning many-body physics in hybrid materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to understand the origin of the electronic structure and the zero-bias conductance peak in the hybrid transition metal dichalcogenide 4Hb-TaS2, specifically investigating how incommensurate potentials and charge transfer influence electronic correlations and superconductivity.

Main result. The researchers discovered that an intrinsic incommensurate potential, caused by a 1.1% lattice mismatch between 1T and 1H layers, modulates interlayer hybridization and charge transfer, driving the system into a doped Mott regime where superconductivity competes with the incommensurate landscape.

Method. The study combines experimental Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM) and Spectroscopy (STS) with computational Density Functional Theory (DFT) and Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (DMFT) using a periodic Anderson model.

Model / system. The physical system is the bulk transition metal dichalcogenide polytype 4Hb-TaS2, consisting of alternating correlated 1T and metallic 1H layers. The theoretical model is a periodic Anderson model accounting for correlated d-orbitals and uncorrelated c-electrons.

Key observables. Zero-bias conductance peak (ZBCP), Upper and Lower Hubbard Bands (UHB/LHB), charge transfer (CT) magnitude, and spectral weight at the Fermi energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice mismatch (~1.1%), Hubbard interaction (U = 100 meV), superconducting transition temperature (Tc approx 2.6 K), and Selenium doping concentration (x = 0% to 5%).

Assumptions / limitations. The computational approach uses a local-registry approximation for the incommensurate potential rather than a full moire supercell, and DMFT is applied to a simplified periodic Anderson model.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the crystal structure and STM topography; Fig 2 illustrates the modulation of charge transfer and hybridization by lattice mismatch; Fig 3 shows DMFT calculations of ZBCP and Hubbard band evolution; Fig 4 demonstrates the effect of superconductivity on CDW configuration transitions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the hybrid TMD system, presents STM/STS evidence for an intrinsic incommensurate potential, uses DFT/DMFT to model the charge transfer and many-body effects, and concludes by discussing the competition between this potential and superconductivity.

Abstract

The engineering of superlattices in two-dimensional van der Waals materials has enabled the realization of rich phase diagrams hosting topological and strongly correlated phases. While incommensurability is widespread in three-dimensional systems, the role of moiré potentials in bulk materials remains largely unexplored. Here, using scanning tunneling microscopy, we demonstrate that a bulk transition-metal dichalcogenide polytype, 4Hb-TaS$_2$, hosts an emergent incommensurate potential between its alternating 1T and 1H layers. Interplay with a concomitant incommensurate charge-density wave suppresses the long-range order of this potential, leading to intricate coupling with electronic correlations in the doped 1T surface layer. Combining density functional theory with dynamical mean-field theory, we show that the lattice mismatch locally modulates the interlayer distance, thereby tuning both hybridization and charge transfer between the correlated 1T and metallic 1H layers. This redistribution of charge drives the system towards a doped Mott regime, in which the remaining local moments become self-screened, giving rise to a zero-bias resonance. We further find that bulk superconductivity competes with both the underlying landscape and the associated charge transfer. Our results establish incommensurate potentials as a previously overlooked ingredient in hybrid transition-metal dichalcogenides, highlighting their central role in the interplay between electronic correlations, charge-density-wave order, and unconventional superconductivity.

Summary. This paper introduces a hybrid quantum-classical framework for predicting future interactions in evolving networks. By using an adaptive mechanism to only update quantum embeddings when significant changes occur, it reduces computational overhead and improves prediction accuracy. The model is shown to be effective even under the noisy conditions of near-term quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of performing efficient dynamic link prediction in evolving networks by overcoming the limitations of classical temporal graph models and the high computational cost of re-encoding quantum states at every timestep.

Main result. The A2QTGN framework achieves superior predictive performance across multiple temporal graph benchmarks and demonstrates robustness to noise and limited shot counts on real IBM quantum hardware.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical approach using Adaptive Amplitude Encoding (AAE) to selectively update quantum embeddings only when temporal feature variations exceed a threshold, integrated with a Temporal Graph Network (TGN) backbone.

Model / system. A hybrid quantum-classical neural network architecture utilizing PennyLane for quantum circuit execution, specifically employing amplitude encoding on 8 qubits and evaluating performance on IBM quantum devices (e.g., ibm_torino).

Key observables. Expectation values of Pauli-Z operators, prediction accuracy, precision, AUC, and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR).

Important parameters / regimes. Activity factor (alpha), update threshold (tau), number of qubits (8), and measurement shot counts (100-shot vs 2048-shot).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes that temporal feature variations can be effectively captured by a threshold-based re-encoding strategy and that the quantum-classical interface can be optimized via gradient-based learning.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the concept of dynamic graphs; Figure 2 details the A2MTGN pipeline including the adaptive update loop; Figure 3 displays performance convergence curves; and various tables present ablation studies and hardware-aware performance metrics.

Paper structure. The paper identifies limitations in classical and static quantum graph models, proposes the A2QTGN architecture and its adaptive mechanism, details the experimental setup on various benchmarks, presents ablation studies on encoding and update strategies, and concludes with hardware-aware performance evaluations.

Abstract

Dynamic link prediction is important for modeling evolving interactions in complex systems, including social, communication, financial, and transportation networks. Classical temporal graph models capture sequential dependencies, but they may struggle to represent concurrent and rapidly changing node-edge interactions in large dynamic graphs. We propose A2QTGN (Adaptive Amplitude Quantum-Integrated Temporal Graph Network), a hybrid quantum-classical framework that combines adaptive amplitude encoding with a Temporal Graph Network backbone. The proposed mechanism represents node interaction features as quantum states and selectively refreshes amplitude embeddings based on temporal activity, preserving stable node states while emphasizing meaningful structural changes. This design reduces unnecessary quantum re-encoding and improves temporal representation for link prediction. Experiments on five Temporal Graph Benchmark datasets show that A2QTGN achieves strong predictive and ranking performance across diverse dynamic graphs. Ablation studies confirm the importance of both the quantum embedding module and the adaptive update strategy, while hardware-aware inference using a noisy backend and limited real-device execution supports the feasibility of near-term quantum-assisted temporal graph learning.

Geometric Construction of Optimal Teleportation Witnesses

Authors: Yanning Jia, Fenzhuo Guo, Mengxuan Bai, Mengyan Li, Haifeng Dong, Fei Gao
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22226v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5

Summary. The paper introduces a geometric method to determine if a quantum state can provide a quantum advantage for teleportation. By calculating the shortest distance to a set of 'useless' states, the authors provide a necessary and sufficient criterion and a way to construct optimal witnesses for arbitrary two-qudit systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful tool for characterizing the operational utility of entanglement in higher-dimensional systems, which is essential for designing robust quantum networks and protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a systematic, constructive framework to identify whether an arbitrary two-qudit entangled state is useful for quantum teleportation, overcoming the limitations of existing criteria that are either restricted to qubits or only provide sufficient conditions.

Main result. The authors present a geometric method to construct optimal teleportation witnesses and establish that the shortest distance between a state and the set of useless states serves as a necessary and sufficient criterion for teleportation usefulness.

Method. A two-layer iterative cutting-plane algorithm is used to solve a convex semi-infinite programming problem, finding the projection of a target state onto the convex set of useless states via an outer-layer SDP and an inner-layer randomized fixed-point iteration.

Model / system. The study considers arbitrary bipartite two-qudit entangled states, specifically testing the method on two-qubit Werner states, two-qubit Maximally Entangled Mixed States (MEMS), and asymmetric two-qutrit non-maximally entangled states.

Key observables. Average teleportation fidelity, Maximal Entangled Fraction (MEF), and the geometric Frobenius distance D(rho) from the set of useless states.

Important parameters / regimes. The visibility parameter p, the dimension of the qudit system d, and the critical threshold pc where teleportation advantage begins.

Assumptions / limitations. The inner optimization layer uses a randomized fixed-point iteration which may converge to a local optimum, potentially providing a lower bound on the MEF.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the geometric construction of the witness via a separating hyperplane; Figures 2, 3, and 4 show the geometric distance D(rho) as a function of visibility p for Werner, MEMS, and qutrit states, respectively, marking the onset of usefulness.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of teleportation usefulness, defines the geometric framework and the cutting-plane algorithm, presents the construction of the optimal witness, and validates the method through numerical applications to various two-qubit and two-qutrit state classes.

Abstract

Not all entangled states are useful for quantum teleportation. We present a geometric method to construct optimal teleportation witnesses, which provide operational necessary and sufficient criteria for identifying the teleportation usefulness of arbitrary two-qudit entangled states. Specifically, by developing a two-layer iterative cutting-plane algorithm to solve the shortest distance problem from the target state $ρ$ to the convex set $S$ of useless states, we obtain the projection point $σ^* \in S$ and then construct the optimal teleportation witness from the projection geometry. Moreover, the shortest distance $D(ρ)$ obtained during this construction also serves as a necessary and sufficient criterion for usefulness. We apply our method to identify the teleportation usefulness of three classes of entangled states.

Summary. This paper proves that exact entanglement embezzlement is a self-testing protocol for Cuntz isometries and a specific quasi-free state. It further characterizes the underlying von Neumann algebras as Type III factors, where the specific subtype is determined by the Schmidt coefficients of the embezzled state.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides deep mathematical insights into the resource theory of entanglement and the fundamental algebraic constraints required for exact entanglement manipulation, which is relevant to the study of infinite-dimensional quantum systems and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the self-testing properties and algebraic structure of exact bipartite entanglement embezzlement protocols.

Main result. It proves that exact entanglement embezzlement acts as a self-test for Cuntz isometries and a unique quasi-free state, and classifies the resulting von Neumann algebras as AFD Type III_lambda or Type III_1 factors.

Method. The author employs operator algebra techniques, including von Neumann algebra theory, Cuntz algebra properties, modular theory (Tomita-Takesaki), and GNS representations.

Model / system. A bipartite entanglement embezzlement protocol where two parties use a shared catalyst state psi in a von Neumann algebra M and its commutant M' to obtain a target entangled state phi via local unitaries or contractions.

Key observables. Schmidt coefficients of the target state, modular operator eigenvalues, and the Connes spectrum.

Important parameters / regimes. Schmidt coefficients (alpha_i), dimension (d), and the parameter lambda for Type III_lambda factors.

Assumptions / limitations. The target state phi has full Schmidt rank; the von Neumann algebra M is non-degenerate; the marginal states are faithful.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the bipartite embezzlement protocol and reducing it to a monopartite problem, to establishing the self-testing of Cuntz isometries via GNS representation, and finally using modular theory to classify the resulting von Neumann algebra types.

Abstract

We consider bipartite exact entanglement embezzlement with a catalyst state vector $ψ$ in a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ using unitaries (or more generally, contractions). If $\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ is a von Neumann algebra and $U \in M_d \otimes \mathcal{M}$ and $V \in \mathcal{M}' \otimes M_d$ are unitaries (or more generally contractions), then such a protocol is of the form $(U \otimes I_d)(I_d \otimes V)(e_0 \otimes ψ\otimes e_0)=\sum_{i=0}^{d-1} α_i e_i \otimes ψ\otimes e_i$, where each $α_i>0$ and $\sum_{i=0}^{d-1} α_i^2=1$. We show that any such protocol must arise from a unique state on the tensor product $\mathcal{O}_d \otimes \mathcal{O}_d$ of the Cuntz algebra with itself. As a result, we prove that exact entanglement embezzlement is a self-test for a collection of $d$ Cuntz isometries for each party and a unique quasi-free state on the Cuntz algebra $\mathcal{O}_d$ in the sense of \cite{Iz93}. Moreover, we use modular theory to show that the von Neumann algebra generated by the copy of $\mathcal{O}_d$ is the unique separable approximately finite-dimensional Type $\text{III}_λ$ factor for some $0<λ\leq 1$, where $λ$ can be determined by an algebraic condition on the Schmidt coefficients of the state $\varphi=\sum_{i=0}^{d-1} α_i e_i \otimes e_i$.

The relative entropy of magic and its nonadditivity

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous geometric characterization of magic states in the resource theory of magic and proves that the relative entropy of magic is nonadditive for multi-qubit systems. This finding is significant for understanding how quantum resources scale in stabilizer-based quantum computing architectures.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the properties of the relative entropy of magic, specifically focusing on its geometric distribution in single-qubit systems and its nonadditivity in multi-qubit tensor product states.

Main result. The authors prove that the relative entropy of magic is nonadditive for an infinite class of n-qubit states where the magic states and their closest stabilizer states do not commute. Method. The study uses the resource theory of magic, employing geometric approaches in the Bloch ball, analytical derivations using the Hadamard product, and a proof by contradiction to establish nonadditivity.

Model / system. The framework is the resource theory of magic applied to single-qubit and multi-qubit systems, where the set of free states is defined by the stabilizer polytope (the convex hull of stabilizer states).

Key observables. Relative entropy of magic (R_rel)

Important parameters / regimes. The parameter t representing the distance from a stabilizer state, the Bloch vector length, and the commutation relation between magic and stabilizer states.

Paper structure. Abstract

In most stabilizer-based quantum computing schemes, so-called magic states are a necessary resource for implementing non-transversal quantum gates. With the resource theory of magic, it is possible to analyze and quantify the generation of the non-stabilizer states. The relative entropy is a measure used in various resource theories. For single qubits, we characterize magic states and their closest stabilizer states by applying analytical results known from the relative entropy of entanglement and show that the magic states and their closest stabilizer states are arranged symmetrically around the states at the centers of the faces of the stabilizer octahedron. For tensor products of single-qubit states, we prove analytically that the relative entropy of magic is nonadditive in almost all cases.

We introduce a novel framework for implementing quantum decision trees using a four-level laser-driven atomic system. Method. ATHENA uses Utility-driven Lookahead with Multi-Candidate Block Scheduling (UMS) to avoid early commitment to sub-optimal schedules and EPR-Capacity-Aware Early Scheduling (EES) to increase concurrency by scheduling future operations early.

Model / system. A multi-chip Distributed Quantum Computer (DQC) architecture, specifically modeled on neutral-atom platforms using dual-species ($^{8TL}$Rb and $^{171}$Yb) for compute and communication qubits, featuring nearest-neighbor chip connectivity and finite EPR capacity.

Key observables. Teleportation count, program latency, program fidelity, and effective teleportation count ($T_{eff}$).

Important parameters / regimes. EPR capacity, latency-weighting parameter ($\alpha$), decay factor for lookahead ($eta$), and lookahead window size.

Assumptions / limitations. The lookahead window is limited to a manageable size (e.g., 4 blocks) to maintain computational tractability, and the solution tree is pruned to the top-$w$ candidates.

Abstract

Distributed Quantum Computers (DQCs) enable large system sizes by connecting smaller chips via photonic interconnects. DQCs use teleportation to relocate qubits and execute CNOTs between qubits on different chips. However, non-local CNOTs are 4.3-7.7$\times$ slower and 4$\times$ more error-prone than local CNOTs within a chip, which degrades program fidelities. Existing compilers group CNOTs with overlapping qubits into blocks and collectively optimize teleportations for each block. However, block-level scheduling has two key drawbacks. First, it lacks lookahead ability across blocks because it selects the optimal schedule for one block before proceeding to the next. As a result, it cannot assess the impact of a teleportation on future blocks. Our studies show that naively expanding the lookahead window to include subsequent blocks does not address this issue. Second, existing approaches do not schedule future block operations or the teleportations they require until preceding blocks are fully scheduled, introducing delay and latency overheads. We propose ATHENA, a DQC compiler that addresses these limitations using two key insights: Utility-driven Lookahead with Multi-Candidate Block Scheduling (UMS) and EPR-Capacity-Aware Early Scheduling (EES). UMS schedules a block by considering only useful future blocks in its lookahead window. A future block has utility if it shares overlapping qubits with the current block being scheduled. UMS also maintains multiple schedules during compilation, allowing it to defer commitment to globally sub-optimal schedules early in the compilation process. EES enables ATHENA to schedule future operations and their relocations early when EPR resources are available. Our evaluations show that ATHENA reduces teleportations by 34% on average and up to 65%, and reduces latency by 2$\times$ on average and up to 2.9$\times$ compared to the state-of-the-art.
