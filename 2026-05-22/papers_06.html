Nonlinear Photonic Tripartite Phase

Authors: Xiangrui Hou, Fangyu Wang, Zhaoxin Wu, Shuming Zhang, Shan-Zhong Li, Lei Ying, Haiqing Lin, Baile Zhang, Zhi Li, Shi-Liang Zhu, Zhaoju Yang
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21983v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5 · quantum optics experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper experimentally demonstrates a tripartite phase in a nonlinear quasiperiodic photonic lattice where localized, critical, and extended states coexist. It reveals that nonlinearity acts selectively, capable of driving low-energy localized states into a critical window before eventually inducing self-trapping. This discovery offers a mechanism for using interactions to control state access in quasiperiodic landscapes.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rare experimental demonstration of a tripartite phase and shows how nonlinear interactions can be used as a dynamical tool to navigate between different localization regimes, which is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and controlled transport in disordered systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study addresses the lack of experimental realization of a tripartite phase (coexisting localized, critical, and extended states) in quasiperiodic systems and investigates whether nonlinearity can provide controlled access to the critical window.

Main result. The researchers experimentally realize a tripartite phase in a nonlinear photonic lattice and discover a state-selective mechanism where weak nonlinearity 'thaws' low-energy localized states into the critical window, while stronger nonlinearity leads to self-trapping.

Method. The study uses femtosecond-laser-written waveguide arrays to implement a quasiperiodic diamond lattice, employing site-selective excitation and tracking wavepacket dynamics and fractal dimension evolution.

Model / system. A nonlinear quasiperiodic diamond photonic lattice implemented via waveguide arrays, governed by the discrete nonlinear Schrodinger equation with a quasiperiodic potential applied to one sublattice.

Key observables. Fractal Dimension (FD), Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR), wavepacket spatial intensity evolution, and energy-dependent transport regimes.

Important parameters / regimes. Disorder strength (lambda), nonlinearity strength (gP/J), and energy levels (low-energy vs. high-energy localized states).

Assumptions / limitations. The paraxial approximation is used to map spatial propagation in waveguides to temporal evolution in quantum systems.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the waveguide array schematic and excitation sites; Fig 2 visualizes spatial intensity evolution; Fig 3 displays the fractal dimension as a function of nonlinearity and its dynamical evolution; Fig 4 shows the monotonic drive of certain states into the soliton regime.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the experimental gap, describes the photonic lattice platform and Hamiltonian, presents the realization of the tripartite phase in the linear regime, explores the state-selective response to Kerr nonlinearity, and concludes with the underlying mechanism of spectral flow.

Abstract

Anderson localization is usually understood as a transition between extended and localized phases, with criticality confined to a single mobility edge. Recent advances predict that quasiperiodic systems can instead host a finite critical window bounded by mobility edges, in which localized, critical and extended states coexist. Yet both the experimental realization of this regime and whether interactions can provide controlled access to it remain unknown. Here, we realize such a tripartite phase in a nonlinear quasiperiodic photonic lattice and show that Kerr nonlinearity, acting as an effective interaction, enables state-selective access to the critical window. By tracking wavepacket dynamics, we distinguish localized, critical and extended transport regimes and uncover a state-selective response: rather than simply reinforcing localization through self-trapping, weak nonlinearity drives low-energy localized states into the critical window, whereas stronger nonlinearity restores localization. By contrast, critical, extended and high-energy localized states evolve monotonically towards self-trapped behaviour. Our results reveal a state-selective mechanism by which interactions provide controlled access to a pre-existing critical window in quasiperiodic systems.

Generation of Quantum Turbulence by Neutrino Cooling in Neutron Stars

Authors: J. A. Sauls
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22768v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the rapid neutrino cooling in young neutron stars acts as a thermal quench that triggers the Kibble-Zurek mechanism. This process generates a high density of quantized vortices, resulting in a state of quantum turbulence within the star's superfluid core. These findings have significant implications for understanding pulsar rotation and thermal evolution.

Why it may be interesting. The paper explores non-equilibrium phase transitions and the generation of topological defects via rapid quenches, which is highly relevant to the study of dynamics in many-body systems and the formation of defects in condensed matter.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the rapid, non-equilibrium neutrino cooling in young neutron stars triggers the generation of quantum turbulence through the formation of topological defects.

Main result. The application of the Kibble-Zurek mechanism (KZM) predicts that rapid cooling leads to a very high density of quantized vortices and loops, creating a random network of quantum turbulence in the neutron star interior.

Method. The author uses the Kibble-Zurek mechanism (KZM) and scaling relations derived from the Cooper pair fluctuation propagator to calculate vortex densities based on Urca and modified Urca cooling rates.

Model / system. The system is the interior crust and liquid core of a young neutron star, modeled as a quantum liquid mixture of neutron and proton superfluids and a relativistic electron liquid undergoing a second-order phase transition.

Key observables. Areal density of phase vortices (nv), inter-vortex spacing (dv), and the quench rate (1/tau_Q).

Important parameters / regimes. Neutrino cooling timescales (tau_Q), transition temperatures (Tc), pairing gaps (Delta), and neutron density (kfn).

Assumptions / limitations. The cooling is modeled as a temperature quench; the fraction of phase windings (f) is non-universal; the system is treated within the weak-coupling limit for pairing.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows neutron superfluid pairing calculations; Figure 2 shows a diagrammatic representation of the Cooper pair propagator; Figure 3 and 4 show the areal density of vortices as a function of neutron density for different cooling processes and nuclear potentials.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the neutron star interior system, applies the Kibble-Zurek mechanism to the non-equilibrium phase transition, derives scaling relations from the Cooper pair propagator, calculates vortex densities for specific cooling mechanisms (Urca/modified Urca), and compares results to standard pulsar rotation models.

Abstract

The interior crust and much of the liquid core of neutron stars is believed to be a quantum liquid mixture of neutron and proton superfluids and a relativistic electron liquid. Quantized vortices in the neutron superfluid and quantized flux lines in the proton superconductor are topological defects of these hadronic condensates. I consider the formation of the superfluid state in young neutron stars under non-equilibrium conditions imposed by the neutrino cooling rate. The nonequilibrium phase transition implies that the onset of superfluidity is accompanied by the generation of quantized vortices based on the mechanism envisioned by Kibble in the context cosmic string formation in an evolutionary models of an expanding universe, and further developed by Zurek for nonequilibrium phase transitions in quantum liquids such as \Hefour. I discuss the Kibble-Zurek mechanism (KZM) and scaling relations for topological defect formation starting from the Cooper pair fluctuation propagator for temperatures approaching $T_c$. I then calculate the predicted vortex densities based on Urca and modified Urca cooling mechanisms in the cores of neutron stars for several models of the superfluid gap and transition temperature of the interior neutron superfluid. In all cases studied the KZM leads to a large density of topological defects in the condensate phase, which in 3D form a random network of vortex lines and loops, i.e. the generation of quantum turbulence.

Nonlinear frequency shift and bistability of magnon-polarons

Authors: Kevin Künstle, Matthias Wagner, Philipp Knaus, Yannik Kunz, Ephraim Spindler, Katharina Lasinger, Matthias R. Schweizer, Philipp Pirro, John F. Gregg, Mathias Weiler
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22157v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that in a SAW-driven resonator, the excitation of counterpropagating spin waves leads to a unique positive nonlinear frequency shift and bistable 'foldover' behavior. These findings provide a new mechanism for controlling magnon-phonon interactions and suggest potential for wave-based information processing and power-limiting devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the origin of nonlinear frequency shifts and the emergence of bistability in strongly coupled magnon-phonon hybrids (magnon-polarons).

Main result. The researchers discovered that the nonlinear frequency shift is positive and dominated by a cross-shift term arising from the simultaneous excitation of counterpropagating spin waves (+k and -k) by the SAW resonator.

Method. The study combines microwave reflection measurements (VNA) with polarization-resolved micro-focused Brillouin light scattering (uBLS) and utilizes a vector Hamiltonian formalism for theoretical modeling.

Model / system. A magnetoacoustic resonator consisting of a YIG/ZnO heterostructure in a backward-volume magnetic configuration, where surface acoustic waves (SAWs) are strongly coupled to finite-wave-vector spin waves (SWs).

Key observables. Electrical reflection (S11), frequency-resolved magnon and phonon spectra, and power-dependent redistribution of magnon populations.

Important parameters / regimes. Strong-coupling regime (g/2pi = 5.05 MHz), acoustic wavelength (2 um), and magnetic field detuning relative to the anticrossing point.

Assumptions / limitations. The nonlinear dynamics are modeled using the vector Hamiltonian formalism for nonlinear spin-wave dynamics.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the device geometry and measurement setup; Fig 2 details the linear coupling and experimental parameters; Fig 3 presents nonlinear coefficients and calculated dispersion relations; Fig 4 and 5 illustrate the power-dependent nonlinear shifts, hysteretic power sweeps, and the resulting bistability/foldover behavior.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the experimental setup and linear regime characterization, moves to the observation of power-dependent frequency shifts and nonlinear scattering, provides a theoretical explanation via the vector Hamiltonian formalism, and concludes with the demonstration of bistability and potential applications.

Abstract

We investigate the nonlinear dynamics of strongly coupled surface acoustic waves (SAWs) and spin waves (SWs) in a magnetoacoustic resonator based on a YIG/ZnO heterostructure by combining microwave reflection measurements with microfocused Brillouin light scattering spectroscopy. In the linear regime, the electrical response reveals clear hybridization between standing SAW cavity modes and finite-wave-vector SWs, resulting in pronounced avoided crossings. At elevated drive powers, the hybrid system exhibits a strongly field-dependent nonlinear response characterized by a positive frequency shift of the driven SW mode. Using the vector Hamiltonian formalism for nonlinear spin-wave dynamics, we show that this shift is dominated by a cross-shift term. In our resonator geometry, this contribution becomes significant because the standing SAW cavity mode simultaneously excites counterpropagating SWs with wave vectors $+k$ and $-k$. For suitable field detuning, the nonlinear shift drives the SW mode into resonance with the SAW excitation, leading to a strong enhancement of the magnon population, broadband nonlinear scattering, and bistable foldover behavior. Beyond the foldover threshold, both the magnon and phonon responses stabilize. These results establish SAW-driven $k \neq 0$ magnon-phonon hybrids as a promising platform for nonlinear magnetoacoustics and wave-based information processing.

Anomalous acoustoelectric signatures of chiral superconductivity

Authors: A. N. Osipov, V. N. Ivanova, V. M. Kovalev, I. G. Savenko
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21936v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes using Surface Acoustic Waves to detect chiral p-wave superconductivity in 2D materials. It demonstrates that the asymmetric scattering of quasiparticles generates a measurable transverse DC current. This provides a high-sensitivity electrical alternative to traditional optical methods for identifying unconventional pairing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of identifying chiral p-wave pairing symmetry in 2D superconductors using traditional optical probes due to low signal-to-noise and substrate interference.

Main result. The authors propose that the anomalous acoustoelectric effect, specifically a transverse DC current caused by skew scattering, serves as a robust electrical signature of chiral order parameters.

Method. A kinetic equation approach using the Boltzmann equation and T-matrix approach to analyze subgap dynamics and asymmetric scattering probabilities.

Model / system. A 2D chiral p-wave superconductor (such as MoS2 or twisted bilayer graphene) placed on a piezoelectric substrate where Surface Acoustic Waves (SAWs) induce a dragging potential.

Key observables. Transverse (Hall) current density, longitudinal current density, and the induced superconducting condensate phase difference across sample boundaries.

Important parameters / regimes. Superconducting gap (Delta), transition temperature (Tc), SAW frequency (omega), momentum relaxation time (tau_p), and the diffusive limit (kl << 1).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is in the diffusive limit, the SAW frequency is much smaller than the superconducting gap, and the acoustic wave velocity is much less than the Fermi velocity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows an experimental setup of a 2D superconductor on a piezoelectric substrate with IDTs; Figure 2 shows the longitudinal and Hall current densities as functions of temperature and gap magnitude.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of detecting chiral pairing, proposes the acoustoelectric effect, develops the kinetic theory framework including skew scattering, derives the current density components, and discusses the experimental observability of the results.

Abstract

The identification of unconventional pairing in two-dimensional materials is a central challenge in modern condensed matter physics. While chiral p-wave superconductivity offers a promising platform for topological quantum computing, its detection remains elusive due to the inherent limitations of optical probes in the two-dimensional limit. We propose the anomalous acoustoelectric effect as a robust, alternative to optical signature of p-wave paring symmetry. We demonstrate that an acoustic wave induces a transverse dc current resulting in a measurable condensate phase difference on sample boundaries originating from the anisotropic scattering of quasiparticles in the absence of an external magnetic field. Crucially, the quasiparticle-mediated acoustoelectric response dominates near the critical temperature and, unlike the superconducting condensate, is not suppressed by electron-hole asymmetry factor. These results establish the anomalous acoustoelectric effect as a high-sensitivity electrical probe of the chiral order parameter, providing a tool for experimental detecting of unconventional pairing in superconductors.

Current-driven reduction of topological protection in multichannel superconductors

Authors: Alfonso Maiellaro
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22460v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that injecting a charge current into a multichannel superconductor breaks time-reversal and chiral symmetries, which suppresses higher-order topological phases. It identifies that while the two-mode phase is fragile under current, it can be stabilized if the superconducting channels remain decoupled.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how a finite charge current induces symmetry breaking that reduces the topological protection and suppresses higher-multiplicity Majorana modes in multichannel superconductors.

Main result. The study shows that a finite current drives the system from the BDI class to the D class, making the two-mode topological phase fragile, unless the channels are decoupled (interchain pairing is zero).

Method. The authors use a real-space Hamiltonian approach, combining bulk topological invariants (Pfaffian) with real-space entanglement diagnostics like edge-edge quantum conditional mutual information (Iee).

Model / system. A Kitaev ladder model consisting of two coupled spinless p-wave superconducting chains with longitudinal and interchain hopping and pairing.

Key observables. Topological invariants (Z and Z2), Bogoli-de Gennes (BdG) energy spectra, Majorana Bound State (MBS) probability densities, and edge-edge quantum conditional mutual information (Iee).

Important parameters / regimes. Chemical potential (mu), longitudinal hopping (t), interchain hopping (t1), longitudinal pairing (Delta), interchain pairing (Delta1), and current-induced momentum (q).

Assumptions / limitations. The current is modeled via an effective Hamiltonian using a Peierls-like substitution (q-dependent phase factor).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows phase diagrams comparing equilibrium and current-carrying regimes; Figure 2 shows energy spectra and mode densities illustrating the lifting of the two-mode configuration; Figure 3 shows the persistence of the two-mode phase when interchain pairing is zero; Figure 4 shows energy spectra as a function of chemical potential and interchain hopping.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of current-induced symmetry breaking, defines the Kitaev ladder model and current-carrying Hamiltonian, presents bulk and real-space topological analysis, discusses the transition from BDI to D class, and concludes with the implications for superconducting nanostructures.

Abstract

We investigate the robustness of topological phases in a Kitaev ladder composed of two coupled superconducting chains under the perturbing influence of a finite charge current. By introducing an effective Hamiltonian depending on the quasiparticle momentum induced by the current, we show that the two-mode topological phase, present in the isolated ladder, is fragile against a finite current flux. To characterize this behavior, we combine bulk topological invariants with real-space diagnostics, including the edge-edge quantum conditional mutual information Iee, which provides an entanglement-based signature of topological order. Our results provide an effective framework to describe how current injection and measurement processes can affect topological protection in superconducting nanostructures.

Quantum-metric Bloch oscillations in weakly inhomogeneous electric fields

Authors: M. Maneesh Kumar, Md Kaif Faiyaz, Sayan Sarkar, Amit Agarwal
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22227v1

Summary. The paper demonstrates that an inhomogeneous electric field can drive Bloch-like oscillations through the quantum metric, even in the absence of Berry curvature. It shows that this quantum-metric-driven current behaves differently from conventional Bloch oscillations, specifically approaching a finite saturation in high fields. This provides a new mechanism for observing band-geometric effects in synthetic lattices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether a band-geometric analog of Bloch oscillations can exist in systems where the Berry curvature vanishes by utilizing the quantum metric.

Main result. A weakly inhomogeneous electric field introduces a distinct quantum-metric term to semiclassical dynamics, generating oscillatory real-space motion even when Berry curvature is zero.

Method. Extended semiclassical equations of motion for wavepacket dynamics and the semiclassical Boltzmann equation using a relaxation-time approximation.

Model / system. A tilted Dirac model used as an illustrative system, considering electrons in a periodic potential subject to a weakly inhomogeneous electric field.

Key observables. Real-space position (drift and oscillatory components), quantum-metric-induced current (longitudinal and transverse), and differential conductance.

Important parameters / regimes. Berry curvature, quantum metric, electric field gradient, static electric field, and chemical potential.

Assumptions / limitations. The electric field varies slowly over the wavepacket size (weak-gradient approximation) and the semiclassical wavepacket approximation is valid.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows schematics of Berry-curvature-driven vs. quantum-metric-induced Bloch oscillations; Figure 2 shows quantum-metric components in a tilted Dirac model; Figure 3 compares the scaling of quantum-metric current (saturation) vs. conventional Bloch current (negative differential conductance).

Paper structure. The paper identifies the problem of Berry-curvature-independent oscillations, derives modified semiclassical equations of motion including field gradients, applies these to a tilted Dirac model, calculates transport signatures (current and conductance), and discusses experimental platforms like moire superlattices.

Abstract

Geometric analogs of Bloch oscillations studied so far have relied on Berry curvature. We show that a weakly inhomogeneous electric field adds a distinct quantum-metric term to semiclassical wavepacket dynamics, generating an oscillatory real-space contribution even when the Berry curvature vanishes. The associated transport response comprises an intrinsic and a scattering-time-dependent part. In the regime studied, the latter can dominate and approach finite saturation at high field when the relative field inhomogeneity is held fixed. A tilted Dirac model illustrates the mechanism. Realistic platforms will likely require synthetically engineered superlattices, with a finite quantum metric and an adequate band gap.

Signatures of the Quantum Geometric Dipole of Interlayer Excitons in Counterflow Conductivity

Authors: Fanuel I. Mendez, Luis Brey, H. A. Fertig
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22810v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the counterflow conductivity of interlayer excitons can serve as a tunable probe for their internal quantum geometric dipole. By manipulating periodic potentials and electric fields, one can observe how the quantum geometry of many-body excitations influences macroscopic transport.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical bridge between the abstract quantum geometry of many-body wavefunctions and measurable transport coefficients, which is highly relevant for understanding topological transport in correlated bilayer systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how the internal quantum geometric dipole (QGD) of interlayer excitons manifests in transport properties, specifically looking for experimental signatures in counterflow conductivity.

Main result. The authors show that the off-diagonal counterflow conductivity $\sigma_{xy}^{(CF)}$ is proportional to the gradient of the QGD with respect to momentum, and that this conductivity can be tuned by varying the periodic potential strength and the layer-antisymmetric electric field.

Method. The study employs a semiclassical Boltzmann transport approach including inter-band Landau-Zener tunneling and numerical diagonalization of a tridiagonal Hamiltonian in an extended zone scheme.

Model / system. A 2D bilayer system (such as TMDs or graphene heterostructures) in a strong perpendicular magnetic field, subject to a unidirectional 1D periodic potential (superlattice).

Key observables. Counterflow conductivity tensor components ($\sigma_{xy}^{(CF)}$, $\sigma_{yx}^{(CF)}$, $\sigma_{xx}^{(CF)}$, $\sigma_{yy}^{(CF)}$) and the Quantum Geometric Dipole (QGD).

Important parameters / regimes. Periodic potential strength ($W$), layer-antisymmetric electric field ($E_-$), dielectric constant ($\kappa$), magnetic length ($\ell$), and layer separation ($d$).

Assumptions / limitations. Strong magnetic field limit (lowest Landau level), high-temperature/dilute limit (ignoring BEC and exciton-exciton interactions), and neglect of electron/hole recombination.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows schematics of exciton counterflow and the QGD definition; Figure 2 illustrates the QGD vector structure and its dependence on potential strength; Figure 3 shows interband tunneling at avoided crossings; Figure 4 displays exciton band structures; Figure 6 shows conductivity dependence on $\kappa$ and $E_-$.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the QGD and its relation to exciton polarization, moves to the semiclassical equations of motion and the Boltzmann transport framework, analyzes the effects of periodic potentials and electric fields on conductivity, and concludes with an appendix deriving the explicit QGD-conductivity link.

Abstract

Collective excitations of many-body electron systems can carry internal structure, supporting novel quantum geometric and topological properties. Among these are a quantum geometric dipole (QGD), which for excitons have direct significance as an internal polarization. For interlayer excitons of a bilayer system, this represents an in-plane dipole moment, which can be used to drive them with in-plane electric fields. In this work, we consider counterflow electric currents associated with driven excitons in such a bilayer system as a probe of their QGD structure. As a simple but non-trivial example, we analyze a structure with a one-dimensional periodic potential in a strong perpendicular magnetic field. The resulting magnetoexciton bands host QGD structure that distinguishes it from the exciton QGD of a uniform system. To model exciton transport we adopt a Boltzmann approach that includes inter-band tunneling, allowing us to consider non-equilibrium momentum distributions that result from strong layer-antisymmetric driving fields. We show how linear response to a layer-symmetric component of the driving fields provide information about the QGD, and that the broad QGD structure of the exciton bands can be probed by the varying the layer-antisymmetric field. Our results demonstrate that counterflow conductivity serves as a tunable probe of the internal quantum geometric structure carried by the interlayer excitons, connecting transport to the quantum geometry of many-body excitations.

Summary. This paper demonstrates how electrical current can be used to actively engineer the band structure of a magnonic crystal. By applying inhomogeneous spin-orbit torque, the researchers show they can induce mode hybridization and create tunable band gaps. This provides a pathway for developing programmable, reconfigurable magnonic and spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study addresses the need for active, electrical control over spin-wave propagation and band structures in magnonic crystals, moving beyond passive systems.

Abstract

Spin wave propagation and dynamic control are essential for reconfigurable magnonic and spintronic devices. Here, tunable mode coupling and band hybridisation are demonstrated in a nanopatterned bicomponent magnonic crystal consisting of a Permalloy/heavy metal bilayer patterned with a two dimensional array of Co nanodots. Using the plane wave method and the linearised Landau Lifshitz equation with a field like torque term, we show that inhomogeneous current induced spin torque produces periodic modulation of the magnonic frequency, enabling dynamic control of spin-wave dispersion. A pronounced avoided crossing between localised and propagating Damon Eshbach modes is observed, leading to tunable hybridisation gaps, band deformation, and enhanced mode mixing. The spin torque induced modulation enables controlled mode conversion and reconfigurable hybrid magnonic states, demonstrating efficient electrical tuning of nanoscale spin-wave dynamics.

Summary. The researchers developed a way to use light to precisely control the number of electrons and the amount of disorder in graphene devices. This technique allows them to switch between different transport regimes and reveal complex quantum Hall states that were previously hidden by disorder. This provides a new, accessible tool for engineering high-quality 2D materials for studying correlated physics.

Why it may be interesting. The paper demonstrates a method to deterministically tune the strength of Coulomb interactions and disorder in a 2D system, which is highly relevant for studying many-body dynamics and the stability of correlated phases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for precise, non-destructive, and in situ control over carrier concentration and electronic disorder in 2D van der Waals heterostructures to study fragile correlated states.

Main result. Demonstrated that photoinduced modulation doping can reversibly tune carrier density and mobility, enabling the transition from diffusive to quasi-ballistic transport and the observation of quantum Hall isospin ferromagnetic states.

Method. Using standard white light sources to induce charge trapping at the hBN-SiO2 interface (photodoping) and prolonged illumination to reduce disorder (photoannealing).

Model / system. hBN-encapsulated graphene Hall bar heterostructures on a SiO2 backgate, analyzed using Drude and Landauer transport models.

Key observables. Carrier mobility, carrier density, residual carrier density, scattering length, and Landau level degeneracy lifting.

Important parameters / regimes. Transition between diffusive and quasi-ballistic transport regimes; tuning of mobility between 200,000 and 300,000 cm2/V-s; magnetic fields up to 9 T.

Assumptions / limitations. The scattering model assumes 2D Coulomb disorder located at a distance of more than a few nm from the graphene channel.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Schematic of the device and photodoping mechanism. Fig 2: Transfer curves showing CNP shifts, mobility extraction, and reversible tuning of transport regimes. Fig 3: Quantum Hall measurements showing the emergence of split plateaus (degeneracy lifting) after photoannealing.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the photodoping technique, demonstrates its ability to control carrier density and reduce disorder via photoannealing, quantifies the transport improvements using Drude and Landauer models, and concludes by showing the emergence of many-body correlated states in the high-quality regime.

Abstract

Modulation doping, a well-established technique for traditional semiconductor heterostructures, is a promising approach for tailoring carrier concentration in 2D materials devices. In this letter we report on photoinduced modulation doping in hBN-graphene-hBN-SiO2 heterostructures utilizing standard white light sources and no additional fabrication complexity. We establish the use of this technique to both dope the channel material and to photoanneal devices, providing control over electronic doping and disorder in the graphene channel. We analyze the transport properties by employing Drude and Landauer transport models, highlighting the ability to reversibly tune the mobility and mean scattering length of the graphene with a high degree of accuracy. This tunability allows us to switch our device between the diffusive and quasi-ballistic transport regimes in situ. We utilize the exceptional control our technique provides over local disorder to realize quantum Hall isospin ferromagnetic states in a device whose initial quality would otherwise leave such states unobservable. These results demonstrate precise manipulation of carrier density and charge disorder in van der Waals heterostructures, providing a highly accessible approach to creating high-quality devices capable of realizing correlated electronic states.

(abridged) We aim to predict the most important parameters for grain-grain collision outcomes for models of interstellar grain population evolution on astrophysical scales: the threshold velocity above which colliding grains shatter, the threshold for vaporization, and resulting distributions of grain sizes. We use molecular dynamics simulations which evolve the dynamics of each atom in a dust grain to explore the outcomes of collisions between silicate grains of radii $a \in [5,50]~Å$ at velocities $0.1-20$ km/s. We run simulations of grains with two materials: amorphous SiO$_2$ and an amorphous silicate of composition suggested by Draine \& Hensley (2021). With these simulations, we quantify the collision velocity dependence of shattered and vaporized mass fractions, and the resulting size distributions of shattering products. We find grain shattering thresholds are $\sim$6 km/s for both amorphous SiO$_2$ and astrodust material, which is a factor of $\sim$2 higher than the canonical value for silicates of 2.7 km/s from Jones et al. (1996). This discrepancy is mostly alleviated by correcting an error in the expression for these velocity thresholds derived in Tielens et al. (1994). We find that the size distributions of shattered products are generally not consistent with the power law distributions predicted by this previous model. We also find that their expression fails to predict the fraction of shattered or vaporized material observed in our numerical simulations. The model of Hirashita \& Kobayashi (2013) for the same quantities similarly fails to match the simulations. We provide updated shattering velocity thresholds for standard candidate grain materials to the astrophysics community. Broadly, our updated threshold velocities that astrophysical dust grains may be more robust to shattering in the interstellar medium than previously assumed.

Abstract

In this work, we simulate the functionality of artificial neuron and synapse using spin-orbit torque-based spintronic devices and implemented a fully connected artificial neural netwrok (ANN). These neuro-synaptic devices are emulated using transverse domain wall dynamics in a rectangular magnetic nanotrack comprised of heavy metal/ferromagnet (HM/FM) heterostructures. The ReLU activation function of the neuron has been mimicked using the domain wall motion induced by a 3 ns current pulse. The synapse has been modelled using current-induced domain wall (DW) dynamics through a corrugated HM/FM nanotrack under the influence of a 10 ns current pulse with varying current density. The semicircular corrugations are in the form of notches, which are symmetrically located on both sides of the nanotrack. By applying 10 ns current pulses of varying densities, we achieve controlled DW pinning, revealing a step-like motion caused by temporary pauses at each pinning center. The electrical conductance of the pinned DW across various pinning points, act as stable synaptic weights for our ANN. Furthermore, we observe a threshold-dependent delay effect where each depinning event is influenced by previous ones, successfully mimicking synaptic memory and adaptability in neuromorphic systems. The fully connected ANN has been modeled using the conventional float32 synaptic weights for the MNIST and Fashion MNIST datasets with an accuracy of ~97 % and ~86 %, respectively, which serves as a test bed of our neuromorphic simulations. With the aim of implementing a sparse and low memory footprint ANN, we quantize the trained synaptic weights into discrete quantized level and tested the network, which demonstrate an accuracy of ~95% and ~62%, for the MNIST and Fashion-MNIST dataset, respectively.
