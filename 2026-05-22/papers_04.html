A sharp interaction-degree threshold for simulating QAOA

Authors: Ralfs Āboliņš, Andris Ambainis
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22758v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes a sharp complexity threshold for simulating QAOA based on the interaction degree of the cost function. It proves that while degree-2 QAOA is efficiently simulatable, degree-3 QAOA is classically hard to sample from, providing a fundamental boundary for quantum advantage in optimization algorithms.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether there is a sharp threshold for the interaction degree of cost functions that determines the classical simulability of the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA).

Main result. The authors prove a sharp transition: at interaction degree 2, depth-p QAOA is classically simulatable in polynomial time for p = O(log n), whereas at degree 3, sampling from depth-1 QAOA is hard enough to collapse the polynomial hierarchy.

Method. The study uses complexity reductions from PostBQP to PostQAOA, circuit transformation techniques to bound interaction degrees, and the Markov-Shi algorithm for tensor network contraction.

Model / system. The model consists of depth-p QAOA circuits with 2-local cost functions defined on an interaction graph, utilizing a mixing operator B = sum of X_j.

Key observables. Marginal output probabilities and sampling distributions from the QAOA circuit.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction degree (specifically the transition between degree 2 and 3), circuit depth p, and multiplicative error c.

Assumptions / limitations. The hardness result for degree 3 assumes a multiplicative error model; the simulation efficiency for degree 2 assumes p = O(log n).

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the circuit preprocessing technique, showing how intermediate Hadamard gates are replaced by diagonal couplings to auxiliary qubits to bound the interaction degree.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of interaction-degree thresholds, presents a preprocessing method for circuit transformation, proves the hardness of degree-3 sampling via complexity reduction, proves the tractability of degree-2 simulation using tensor network contraction, and discusses the distinction between sampling hardness and optimization advantage.

Abstract

We identify a sharp interaction-degree threshold for the classical simulation of QAOA with $2$-local cost functions. At degree $3$, classical sampling from depth-$1$ QAOA with small multiplicative error would collapse the polynomial hierarchy to its third level. At degree $2$, exact classical sampling from depth-$p$ QAOA on $n$ qubits runs in time $n^{O(1)}$ whenever $p = O(\log n)$. The hard degree-$3$ instances have trivially optimizable cost functions, so sampling hardness does not by itself imply a quantum optimization advantage.

A Utility-Driven Bounded-Confidence Model for Opinion Dynamics

Authors: Alex Siebenmorgen, Juan G. Restrepo
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21844v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops an analytical framework for opinion dynamics where the desirability of certain states drives social influence. It shows that the system behaves like a thermodynamic system with an effective temperature, allowing for the prediction of opinion polarization and cluster merging using tools from statistical mechanics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how a utility function (representing the desirability of certain states) influences the evolution, polarization, and clustering of opinions in a bounded-confidence social dynamics model.

Main result. The authors derive that the mean opinion follows a Gibbs-like stationary distribution proportional to U(x)^a, where the effective temperature is controlled by the system size and learning rate, and they provide analytical estimates for opinion switching and cluster merging times.

Method. The study uses a small-spread expansion to derive a low-dimensional stochastic differential equation (SDE) and employs the Fokker-Planck equation, Kramers escape theory, and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck processes for analytical descriptions.

Model / system. An agent-based model of N agents with continuous opinions in [0, 1], interacting via a bounded-confidence mechanism where updates are driven by an asymmetric rule based on a utility function U(x) and subject to additive noise.

Key observables. Mean opinion (Xt), cluster variance (sigma^2), stationary distribution (p*(x)), Mean First-Passage Time (MFPT) for switching, and cluster merging time (Tm).

Important parameters / regimes. Learning rate (mu), confidence bound (epsilon), number of agents (N), noise intensity (Delta), and the utility function landscape U(x).

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes a single-cluster regime, a first-order expansion of the utility function around the mean, and that fluctuations are weakly anti-correlated.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows time series of mean opinion fluctuations and comparisons between utility and empirical histograms; Figure 2 illustrates utility functions; Figure 5 validates the theoretical scaling of escape times against simulation data.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the utility-driven model, derives the SDE and stationary distribution for the single-cluster regime, analyzes metastability and switching in bimodal landscapes, calculates cluster merging dynamics, and validates results via numerical simulations.

Abstract

We introduce a utility-driven bounded-confidence model of opinion dynamics in which opinions associated with higher utility exert stronger social influence. In the regime where all agents belong to a single opinion cluster, we derive a stochastic differential equation for the mean opinion and show that its stationary distribution is Gibbs-like, with an effective potential determined by the utility landscape and an inverse temperature controlled by the learning rate and the number of agents. For multimodal utility functions, the dynamics exhibit metastability and spontaneous switching between competing opinion states. The reduced stochastic description also captures the evolution and merging of multiple opinion clusters, in agreement with agent-based simulations.

Algebraic locality and non-invertible Gauss laws

Authors: Nicholas Holfester, Jonathan Sorce
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21591v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how non-invertible symmetries, such as those found in string-net models, affect the locality of physical operators on a lattice. It demonstrates that while standard locality principles like Haag duality can fail due to geometric 'cusps,' they can be recovered using a modified definition of local regions. This provides a rigorous foundation for understanding the algebraic structure of topological order with non-invertible symmetries.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the validity of algebraic locality principles, specifically Haag duality and disjoint additivity, in lattice models subject to non-invertible Gauss laws.

Main result. The authors show that non-invertible magnetic constraints violate exact Haag duality for 'cusped' regions, but a weak form of duality is restored by adding a 'collar' to the region. Exact locality is recovered for 'cuspless' regions, such as those on trivalent lattices.

Method. The study employs the framework of von Neumann algebras and the representation theory of finite-dimensional C*-Hopf algebras to analyze operator algebras within constrained subspaces.

Model / system. The models studied are 2+1D lattice gauge theories, specifically quantum double models and extended string-net models, where a magnetic constraint is imposed exactly via a non-invertible symmetry.

Key observables. Haag duality, disjoint additivity, Wilson line operators, and character operators.

Important parameters / regimes. The presence of non-invertible (non-group-like) symmetries and the geometric properties of the lattice (cusped vs. cuspless regions).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to bosonic algebras, closed lattices, and finite-dimensional C*-Hopf algebras with an involutive antipode.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the geometric action of Wilson lines, the difference between cusped and cuspless vertices, and the construction of 'collared' regions to restore duality.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-invertible Gauss laws, develops the Hopf-algebraic framework for magnetic constraints, provides counterexamples for Haag duality violation in cusped regions, proves weak duality/additivity for general regions, and demonstrates exact locality for trivalent lattices.

Abstract

We study algebraic locality principles on a 2+1D closed lattice in the presence of a Gauss law for a non-invertible symmetry. Prior work in arXiv:2509.03589 showed that when enforcing the Gauss law of an invertible symmetry, the principle of "Haag duality" is preserved exactly, and "disjoint additivity" is preserved after appropriate treatment of discreteness artifacts. Here we show that for a large class of non-invertible on-site symmetries, Haag duality is preserved exactly only for sufficiently nice, "cuspless" regions. For cusped regions, we instead have a weak form of Haag duality that requires adding a collar. Our results apply to double models with a purely magnetic constraint, and to the more general framework of constraints induced by the on-site action of a Hopf algebra. In particular, we treat a class of extended string-net models explicitly. We also demonstrate disjoint additivity for double models based on a group, and a weakened form of disjoint additivity in the setting of a general Hopf algebra.

Conductivity of a Non-Galilean--Invariant Fermi Liquid: Exact Solution of the Kinetic Equation

Authors: Tatia Kiliptari, Vladimir I. Yudson, Dmitrii L. Maslov
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21774v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides an exact solution for the conductivity of a non-Galilean-invariant Fermi liquid under various interaction models. It corrects previous misconceptions by demonstrating that electron-electron interactions influence transport only through the quasiparticle lifetime. The work also provides a way to access the optical response in the hydrodynamic regime, which is otherwise inaccessible via perturbation theory.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to find an exact expression for the conductivity of a non-Galilean-invariant Fermi liquid across various temperature and frequency regimes, specifically clarifying the role of electron-electron interactions in current relaxation.

Main result. The authors derive an exact solution showing that electron-electron interactions affect conductivity solely through the quasiparticle scattering time, rather than a separate current relaxation rate, and provide the optical response in the hydrodynamic regime.

Method. The study solves the linearized semi-classical kinetic equation by mapping it onto an inhomogeneous Legendre-type differential equation and using the Eliashberg approximation for the Hertz-Millis model.

Model / system. A 2D non-Galilean-invariant Dirac system subject to both screened Coulomb interactions (treated via RPA) and $z=3$ Pomeranchuk critical interactions (Hertz-Millis model) in the presence of impurity scattering.

Key observables. DC resistivity, optical conductivity (real part), and quasiparticle scattering rate.

Important parameters / regimes. Frequency ($\Omega$), temperature ($T$), impurity scattering rate ($\gamma_i$), quasiparticle scattering time ($ au_{ee}$), and the Planckian scale ($\Omega \sim T$).

Assumptions / limitations. Neglect of exchange processes in the Coulomb model, neglect of mass renormalization in the Coulomb model, and the use of the Eliashberg approximation (local self-energy and small vertex corrections).

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the kinetic equation and the physical models (Coulomb and Hertz-Millis), proceeds to the mathematical mapping onto a Legendre-type equation, derives the exact scaling form for conductivity, and concludes with an analysis of specific regimes (collisionless vs. hydrodynamic) and the role of mass renormalization.

Abstract

We obtain an exact expression for the conductivity of a disordered, non-Galilean-invariant Fermi liquid by solving the kinetic equation with both screened Coulomb and $z=3$ Pomeranchuk critical interactions. While consistent with previous asymptotic results, our solution shows that electron-electron interactions enter the conductivity solely via the quasiparticle scattering time, $τ_\mathrm{ee}$. Accordingly, the crossovers between the collisionless and hydrodynamic regimes occur when $1/τ_\mathrm{ee}$ becomes comparable to the larger of the impurity scattering rate and the probe frequency, $Ω$. In addition, the exact solution yields the optical response in the hydrodynamic regime, $Ω\ll 1/τ_\mathrm{ee}$, which is inaccessible within perturbation theory. Near a $z=3$ Pomeranchuk quantum critical point, consistency between the kinetic-equation and Kubo approaches requires proper inclusion of mass renormalization within the Eliashberg approximation, which also ensures that the crossover between the collisionless and hydrodynamic regimes in the optical conductivity occurs at the Planckian scale $Ω\sim T$.

Directed extended-range percolation

Authors: Wenbo Liu, Yiwen Zeng, Xueming Liu, Ginestra Bianconi
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22646v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores a new model of directed extended-range percolation inspired by quantum communication. It demonstrates that network directionality can simplify the percolation problem and that degree correlations can lead to a vanishing percolation threshold in scale-free networks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how network directionality and degree correlations affect the percolation threshold and critical behavior in extended-range percolation models.

Main result. The authors show that directionality reduces combinatorial complexity and that in maximally correlated scale-free networks, the percolation threshold can vanish for certain power-law exponents.

Method. The study uses a message-passing approach and linearization of recursive equations to derive analytical solutions, which are then validated using Monte Carlo simulations.

Model / system. The model is Directed Extended-Range Percolation (DERP), where connectivity between trusted nodes is enabled by directed paths of maximum length R, potentially using untrusted nodes as relays.

Key observables. Percolation threshold (pc), order parameters (S, U, S(+), U(+)), and critical exponents (beta, beta_S, beta_U).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction range (R), power-law exponent (gamma), and degree correlations (Uncorrelated vs. Maximally Correlated).

Assumptions / limitations. The analytical derivation assumes locally tree-like structures and omits specific discussion of logarithmic corrections at the critical point gamma = 3.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the DERP mechanism; Figure 3 compares simulation results with theoretical predictions for order parameters; Figure 4 shows the dependence of the threshold on parameters; and supplementary figures show effective exponent plateaus and scaling plots.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the DERP model and its motivation, develops a message-passing framework for analytical solutions, analyzes critical behavior across different network topologies (UC and MC), and validates results via Monte Carlo simulations.

Abstract

While for standard percolation directionality is known to increase the combinatorial complexity of percolation, here we show that when connectivity is ensured by paths of length $R\geq 2$, network directionality, impeding backtracking, can significantly reduce the complexity of percolation. To illustrate this finding, we introduce Directed Extended-Range Percolation (DERP), defined directed networks with non-reciprocal edges, motivated by applications in quantum communication. In this framework, message transmission is enabled between trusted nodes separated by a directed path of length at most $R$. Using a message-passing approach, we show that directionality enables an exact determination of the percolation threshold and the anomalous critical indices on locally tree-like structures. On random directed networks we find that the critical behavior of DERP depends sensitively on degree correlations. These analytical predictions are corroborated by extensive Monte Carlo simulations, highlighting the profound impact of directionality and correlations on long-range connectivity in complex networks.

DMFT analysis of Hopfield network with plasticity

Authors: Yoshinori Hara, Yoshiyuki Kabashima
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22254v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper studies how synapses that change alongside neurons affect memory in Hopfield networks. It finds that a moderate amount of plasticity helps the network remember patterns more robustly, but too much plasticity causes the network to mistakenly memorize the starting noise instead of the intended memory.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how online, activity-dependent synaptic plasticity affects the memory retrieval dynamics, capacity, and stability of Hopfield-type associative memory networks.

Main result. Moderate plasticity enhances retrieval by creating a positive delayed feedback that stabilizes the target memory, but excessive plasticity leads to 'cue-imprinting failure' where the network stabilizes spurious attractors based on the initial cue.

Method. The authors use the generating-functional formalism to derive a Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (DMFT) that reduces the many-body dynamics to an effective single-site stochastic process, which is then validated via large-scale numerical simulations.

Model / system. A fully connected Hopfield-type associative memory network where neural states and synaptic couplings coevolve. The model includes fixed Hebbian couplings and time-dependent plastic couplings that evolve via an online Hebbian update rule.

Key observables. Memory retrieval overlap (M), memory load (alpha), basin of attraction size, and integrated feedback kernels (Onsager and plastic components).

Important parameters / regimes. Plasticity strength (beta or kA), memory load (alpha), learning rate (gamma), and initial overlap (M0).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the thermodynamic limit (N to infinity), a fully connected network architecture, and an odd activation function (tanh).

Figures summary. Figures show the time evolution of overlap, heat maps of the basin of attraction, comparison of DMFT with direct simulations, and the decomposition of feedback kernels into Onsager and plastic components.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of coevolving dynamics, derives the DMFT using generating functionals, presents numerical validation, analyzes the mechanism of feedback-driven stabilization, and discusses the optimization of plasticity strength.

Abstract

We study a fully connected Hopfield-type associative memory network with online activity-dependent synaptic plasticity, where neural states and synaptic couplings coevolve during retrieval. Using the generating-functional formalism, we derive a dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT) in the large-system limit with extensively many stored random patterns, and show that the many-body dynamics reduces to an effective single-site stochastic process with colored Gaussian crosstalk noise and delayed feedback terms. Numerical solutions of the DMFT equations agree well with direct simulations. We find that moderate plasticity enlarges the basin of attraction and increases the maximum retrievable memory load by generating a positive delayed feedback that stabilizes retrieval against crosstalk noise. However, excessively strong plasticity causes the network to imprint the imperfect initial cue itself, leading to spurious attractors and degraded retrieval performance. Consequently, an optimal plasticity strength emerges from the trade-off between memory stabilization and premature cue imprinting. These results extend the DMFT description of associative memory to networks with coevolving neural and synaptic dynamics.

Ising surface defects can get dirty

Authors: António Antunes, Apratim Kaviraj, Baishali Roy
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22628v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how random magnetic fields on a surface can create new types of boundary conditions in critical systems. It specifically shows that these 'dirty' boundaries lead to the emergence of complex logarithmic structures in the theory's spectrum. This provides deeper insight into the interplay between disorder and criticality in the 3D Ising model.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how localized random magnetic fields on a 2D surface defect affect the boundary conditions and stability of the 3D Ising universality class.

Main result. The authors identify a new 'dirty' boundary condition fixed point and demonstrate the emergence of logarithmic conformal field theory structures in the replica limit.

Method. The researchers use the replica method for disorder averaging combined with Renormalization Group (RG) analysis and one-loop perturbative expansion in 4-epsilon dimensions.

Model / system. A massless interacting scalar field theory (Wilson-Fisher fixed point) in 4-epsilon dimensions featuring a 2D surface defect subject to a Gaussian random magnetic field and a local temperature term.

Key observables. Anomalous dimensions of bilinear defect operators, scaling dimensions of local operators, and one-point correlation functions of bulk composite operators.

Important parameters / regimes. The expansion parameter epsilon, the number of replicas N (taking the N to 0 limit), and the defect couplings h1 and h2.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the validity of the epsilon expansion and focuses on the co-dimension 1 limit for the physical 3D case.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Feynman diagrams for same-replica contributions; Figure 2 shows diagrams for different-replica contributions; Figure 3 displays an RG flow plot with fixed points; Figure 4 illustrates diagrams for defect correlation functions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model of surface defects, applies the replica method to handle disorder, performs RG calculations to find fixed points and stability, analyzes the resulting operator spectrum, and discusses the emergence of logarithmic structures.

Abstract

Real critical systems, such as uniaxial ferromagnets in the 3d Ising universality class, are constrained by boundaries and subject to random couplings. We consider the Wilson-Fisher fixed point in $4-ε$ dimensions subject to a random magnetic field localized on a two-dimensional surface, which becomes co-dimension 1 in the physical $ε\to1$ limit. Using the replica method for the disordered field, we find that the ordinary boundary condition is stable under disorder but also discover a non-trivial ``dirty" boundary condition which can be reached by tuning the disorder strength or the local temperature. We also investigate the logarithmic structure of the defect spectrum and how it emerges via the replica formalism.

MetaDNS: Enhancing Exploration in Discrete Neural Samplers via Well-Tempered Metadynamics

Authors: Xiaochen Du, Juno Nam, Jaemoo Choi, Wei Guo, Sathya Edamadaka, Junyi Sha, Elton Pan, Yongxin Chen, Molei Tao, Rafael Gómez-Bombarelli
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21722v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces MetaDNS, a method that adds a history-dependent bias to neural samplers to prevent them from getting stuck in single energy minima. This allows the sampler to explore complex landscapes and accurately calculate free energies for systems like alloys and spin models. It provides a significant speedup in both exploration and inference compared to classical Monte Carlo methods.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Discrete neural samplers like MDNS suffer from mode collapse and fail to explore high-energy barrier regions, preventing accurate free energy estimation and phase transition analysis.

Main result. The MetaDNS framework successfully overcomes mode collapse, enabling the reconstruction of free energy landscapes and achieving faster exploration and inference compared to traditional MCMC-based metadynamics.

Method. Integration of well-tempered metadynamics into discrete diffusion or autoregressive samplers using an adaptive, history-dependent bias potential along selected collective variables.

Model / system. The method is tested on the Ising model, the Potts model (q=3), and a Cu-Au binary alloy system using cluster expansion Hamiltonians.

Key observables. Magnetization, two-point correlation functions, energy distributions, gold concentration, free energy landscapes (PMF), and Jensen-Shannon divergence.

Important parameters / regimes. Inverse temperature (beta), bias factor (gamma), hill width (sigma), and initial hill height (h).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the neural sampler stays close to the biased target distribution and that the learning timescale is much faster than the bias update timescale.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the MetaDNS scheme; Figure 2 compares MDNS mode collapse vs MetaDNS in the Ising model; Figure 3 shows Potts model CV distributions; Figure 4 shows free energy convergence; Figure 5 illustrates Cu-Au alloy structures and concentration distributions; Figure 16 compares learning dynamics between MetaDNS and WT-MetaD.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the mode collapse problem in neural samplers, proposes the MetaDNS framework, details the mathematical integration of metadynamics with diffusion/autoregressive models, validates the method using Ising and Potts models, demonstrates application to the Cu-Au alloy, and performs sensitivity analyses on hyperparameters.

Abstract

Sampling from discrete distributions with multiple modes and energy barriers is fundamental to machine learning and computational physics. Recent discrete neural samplers like MDNS suffer from mode collapse and fail to sample high-energy barrier regions between modes, which is critical for free energy estimation and understanding phase transitions. We propose Metadynamics Discrete Neural Sampler (MetaDNS), a general framework integrating well-tempered metadynamics into discrete diffusion or autoregressive samplers. By maintaining an adaptive, history-dependent bias potential along selected low-dimensional coordinates, MetaDNS forces exploration of previously inaccessible regions, enabling free energy reconstruction infeasible with standard neural samplers due to a lack of high-energy samples. On challenging low-temperature benchmarks including Ising, Potts, and the copper-gold binary alloy, MetaDNS reproduces the thermodynamic distribution. Compared to MCMC-based metadynamics, MetaDNS also achieves comparable exploration requiring fewer bias deposition steps.

Posterior Collapse as Automatic Spectral Pruning

Authors: Johannes Hirn
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22691v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper reframes the 'failure' of posterior collapse in VAEs as a useful feature that automatically prunes unimportant latent dimensions. By treating the regularization parameter as an effective temperature, the author shows that latent modes disappear in a predictable sequence based on their contribution to data reconstruction.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Reinterpreting posterior collapse in beta-VAEs from a training failure to a functional mechanism for automatic spectral pruning and identifying the relationship between latent mode collapse and reconstruction utility.

Main result. In linear Gaussian VAEs, the collapse threshold, reconstruction utility, and PCA explained variance coincide, demonstrating that increasing regularization triggers a predictable cascade of mode collapses.

Method. The author employs a Landau-style stability analysis to treat latent mode collapse as a phase transition and uses a beta-scan protocol to rank latent modes by their utility.

Model / system. The study focuses on beta-Variational Autoencoders (beta-VAEs), specifically analyzing the linear Gaussian case and testing the framework on the WorldClim global climatology dataset.

Key observables. Posterior signal fraction (M^2), collapse threshold (T_k), reconstruction utility (delta D_k), signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and the Jensen gap.

Important parameters / regimes. Regularization strength (beta), effective temperature (T), and decoder variance (sigma^2_dec).

Assumptions / limitations. The primary theoretical derivation assumes a linear Gaussian VAE with a fixed isotropic decoder variance; the thermodynamic analogy is structural rather than a literal marginalization over microstates.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the collapse scan via posterior mean-square and signal fraction; Figure 2 compares truncated distortion to PCA; Figure 3 demonstrates the utility-threshold duality; Figure 4 shows posterior-scale and Jensen-gap diagnostics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of posterior collapse, develops a Landau-style stability theory for mode inactivation, establishes the duality between collapse and utility in Gaussian models, and validates these findings via experiments on the WorldClim dataset.

Abstract

We show that posterior collapse in $β$-VAEs implements automatic spectral pruning. A latent mode collapses if its contribution to reconstruction is below the cutoff set by $β$. Equilibrium solutions with different $β$ thus reveal a cascade of collapses as latent modes decouple from least to most useful. We derive this as a consequence of the loss via a Landau stability analysis. We define a latent-rescaling-invariant order parameter that ranks active latent modes and whose collapse thresholds identify which effective variables to inspect first. In the linear Gaussian case, the collapse spectrum, utility spectrum, and normalized PCA spectrum coincide, and each collapse follows a mean-field law. We test these predictions on the WorldClim dataset.

Quantum circuit design via dynamic Pauli constraints

Authors: James R. Wootton, Merlin Incerti-Medici, Daniel Bultrini, Pierre Fromholz
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.22744v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new way to program quantum computers by specifying desired physical observables instead of complex gate sequences. It proves that this 'Motte model' is as powerful as the standard circuit model and provides a natural interface for NISQ-era algorithms like quantum imaginary time evolution.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for a software-oriented quantum computing interface that uses physically observable quantities (Pauli expectation values) rather than complex unitary matrices, specifically addressing the constraints of the NISQ era.

Main result. The authors introduce and prove the universality of the 'Motte model,' a framework where gates are specified by Pauli constraints, showing it is equivalent to the standard circuit model with polynomial overhead.

Method. The model uses k-body quantum state tomography to reconstruct reduced density matrices, which are then used to analytically deduce unitaries via eigenstate or density matrix transformations.

Model / system. A software-oriented model of quantum computation (the Motte model) acting as an interface for a standard gate-model QPU with N qubits and a connected coupling graph G.

Key observables. Pauli expectation values (X, Y, Z) and k-local Pauli correlators.

Important parameters / regimes. N (number of qubits), D (circuit depth), k (tomographic locality), and delta (statistical sampling error).

Assumptions / limitations. The underlying QPU is assumed to be noiseless for the theoretical definition, and statistical noise is assumed to be sufficient to lift eigenvalue degeneracies.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the Motte model workflow: user-provided Pauli targets lead to classical gate deduction, execution on a QPU, and iterative feedback via shot statistics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Motte model, establishes its computational universality (BQP equivalence), addresses the technical challenge of phase ambiguity in gate reconstruction, demonstrates practical applications like QITE and GHZ state preparation, and provides a complexity analysis.

Abstract

We introduce a novel software-oriented model of quantum computation motivated by the practical constraints of near-term quantum hardware. In this model, gates are specified by constraints expressed in terms of Pauli observables, with each disjoint layer of gates accompanied by a pairwise or $k$-local quantum state tomography of the device. We prove that the model is equivalent to the coupling-graph-restricted circuit model and hence universal for BQP, with a polynomial overhead: simulating a depth-$D$ circuit on $N$ qubits requires at most $O(D^2 N \log N)$ complexity. The model formalizes an idiom shared by existing work that ranges from quantum imaginary time evolution for the study of quantum systems to the use of quantum computers for procedural generation in games. It therefore provides a natural interface for designing quantum software entirely in terms of physically observable quantities, relevant for the NISQ era and into fault-tolerance.

Statistical Interpretation of the Procedures Measurement of Physical Quantities

Summary. This paper provides a mathematical and conceptual synthesis of algebraic quantum theory and quantum probability. It argues for an operationalist approach where physical quantities are defined by their measurement procedures rather than purely abstract mathematical axioms. The result is a framework that maintains a connection between formal algebraic structures and empirical laboratory practice.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to bridge the gap between abstract algebraic quantum theory (such as C*-algebras) and the practical, operational reality of laboratory measurement procedures.

Main result. The author develops a conceptual framework that links algebraic formulations with quantum probability, demonstrating how a statistical model of measurement can lead to the reconstruction of algebraic structures like von Neumann algebras.

Method. The work uses a synthesis of existing models (von Neumann, Mackey, Segal) employing mathematical tools from measure theory, functional analysis, and operator algebra theory.

Model / system. The framework is based on a hypothetical 'Laboratory System' involving experimental procedures, measurement instruments, and a localized space-time region equipped with a clock.

Key observables. Physical quantities defined through measurement, including compatible/incompatible observables, joint spectra, and temporal evolution of observables.

Important parameters / regimes. L-uniform continuity of state transformations, spectral properties, and the distinction between axioms, postulates, and technical assumptions.

Assumptions / limitations. The laboratory is spatially limited; time is measured by an internal clock; and physical quantities are only considered to exist if an effective experimental measurement method is available.

Paper structure. The paper is structured into two main parts: Part I focuses on laboratories and experimental procedures (statistics, reproducibility, and entropy), while Part II establishes the mathematical framework for these procedures (Mackey's model, spectral properties, and algebraic interpretation).

Abstract

This work develops a conceptual framework for the foundations of quantum physics, linking two main approaches: the algebraic formulation and quantum probability.
