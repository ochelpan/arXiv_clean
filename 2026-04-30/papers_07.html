Accelerated Prediction of Surface Stability and Particle Morphology in Ionic Crystals via Electrostatic Screening

Authors: Sourav Baiju, Payam Kaghazchi
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26264v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable

Main problem. Predicting surface stability and equilibrium crystal morphology in ionic materials is computationally expensive when using conventional Density Functional Theory (DFT).

Main result. An electrostatic-based screening approach provides a fast, scalable way to predict surface stability and morphology, capturing dominant trends across 3D and 2D materials without the cost of DFT.

Method. The method uses electrostatic energy evaluation of stoichiometric slab terminations, surface dipole moment calculations, and replica-exchange Monte Carlo simulations for surface reconstruction.

Summary. This paper introduces a computationally efficient method for predicting the surface stability and particle shape of ionic crystals. By using electrostatic analysis instead of expensive DFT calculations, the researchers can rapidly screen large numbers of surface configurations. The method successfully identifies stable facets and handles polar surfaces through Monte Carlo simulations. It is particularly useful for studying large-scale systems and high-index surfaces that are otherwise too complex for standard quantum mechanical simulations.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on ionic crystals, including simple and complex 3D materials as well as 2D layered oxides.

Key observables. Surface stability, equilibrium crystal morphology, surface dipole moment, and electrostatic energies.

Important parameters / regimes. Surface dipole moment (used as a descriptor), electrostatic screening, and high-index surface configurations.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach assumes that electrostatic interactions capture the dominant trends in relative surface stability, bypassing the need for full electronic structure calculations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a new electrostatic-based screening method, describes the implementation for identifying polar surfaces and reconstruction, validates the method against DFT and experimental data for 3D and 2D materials, and discusses the implications for high-throughput materials discovery.

Abstract

This work presents a fast and scalable approach for predicting surface stability and equilibrium crystal morphology in ionic materials using electrostatic analysis. The method constructs stoichiometric slab terminations and evaluates their electrostatic energies, enabling high-throughput screening of surface configurations at a fraction of the cost of conventional approaches. Polar surfaces are identified through surface dipole moment calculations and stabilized via electrostatics-based reconstruction using replica-exchange Monte Carlo simulations. The surface dipole moment further emerges as an effective descriptor to distinguish the behavior of different classes of materials. By bypassing expensive Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations, the approach extends naturally to large systems and high-index surfaces that are typically inaccessible to DFT. Electrostatic interactions are shown to capture the dominant trends in relative surface stability across diverse material systems. The method is validated on simple and complex 3D materials as well as 2D layered oxides, where the predicted dominant facets are consistent with reported density functional theory and experimental observations. Importantly, the framework also reveals cases where high-index surfaces play a non-negligible role in the equilibrium morphology. These results establish electrostatics as a fast and reliable route for high-throughput prediction of surface stability and particle morphology, opening a pathway for accelerated materials discovery and providing a robust starting point for more detailed calculations in complex energy materials.

Accelerating finite-element-based projector augmented-wave density functional theory calculations with scalable GPU-centric computational methods

Authors: Kartick Ramakrishnan, Phani Motamarri
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26037v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of performing accurate, large-scale Kohn-Sham density functional theory (DFT) calculations for complex systems (up to 10^5 electrons) on modern exascale GPU architectures without the communication bottlenecks of plane-wave methods.

Main result. The development of a GPU-centric, finite-element-based PAW (PAW-FE) method that achieves up to 20x speedup on AMD GPUs and significantly reduces time-to-solution compared to plane-wave and norm-conserving FE methods, demonstrating scalability to 130,000 electrons.

Method. A finite-element discretization of the Projector Augmented-Wave (PAW) formalism using a residual-based Chebyshev Filtered Subspace Iteration (R-ChFSI) eigensolver, optimized with mixed-precision arithmetic (FP32/TF32/BF16) and computation-communication overlap.

Summary. This paper presents a new computational method (PAW-FE) for large-scale electronic structure calculations. By combining finite-element discretization with the Projector Augmented-Wave method, the authors avoid the global communication bottlenecks of traditional plane-wave codes. The implementation is specifically designed for modern GPUs, using mixed-precision arithmetic and optimized kernels to achieve massive speedups. This allows for the simulation of much larger and more complex material systems, such as moiré superlattices, on exascale supercomputers.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The framework uses Kohn-Sham DFT within the PAW formalism and the frozen-core approximation, applied to large-scale systems such as Pt nanoclusters, WS2 slabs, and twisted bilayer WTe2.

Key observables. Ground-state electronic structure, pseudo electron density, magnetization density, total energy, and ionic forces.

Important parameters / regimes. System sizes up to 130,000 electrons; use of GGA exchange-correlation; multi-resolution quadrature; and various GPU precision modes (FP32, TF32, BF16).

Assumptions / limitations. Uses the frozen-core approximation and assumes a collinear spin formalism; relies on the efficiency of the R-ChFSI algorithm's tolerance to inexact matrix-multivector products.

Figures summary. The paper includes diagrams of the operator action for the PAW Hamiltonian, algorithmic descriptions of the SCF cycle and subspace construction, and performance comparison plots (speedup and scaling) against Quantum ESPRESSO and previous FE implementations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational bottleneck of large-scale DFT, defines the PAW-FE mathematical framework and discretization, details the GPU-centric algorithmic innovations (R-ChFSI and precision strategies), presents performance benchmarks on various GPU architectures, and demonstrates scalability on massive systems.

Abstract

Accurate large-scale Kohn-Sham density functional theory (DFT) calculations are essential for modeling complex material systems, including interfaces, defects, nanoclusters, and twisted two-dimensional heterostructures. Achieving chemical accuracy at scales of $10^4$-$10^5$ electrons with practical time-to-solution, however, remains challenging for existing DFT implementations. We present GPU-centric computational methods and algorithmic innovations within a finite-element (FE) discretized projector augmented-wave (PAW) formulation (PAW-FE) for accurate, efficient, and scalable electronic-structure calculations on modern exascale systems. The FE discretization, developed within a collinear spin formalism, accommodates generic boundary conditions and employs multi-resolution quadrature for accurate evaluation of atom-centered PAW integrals on coarse grids. The resulting generalized Hermitian eigenproblem is solved using residual-based Chebyshev filtered subspace iteration (R-ChFSI). Exploiting R-ChFSI's tolerance to inexact matrix-multivector products, we employ an approximate inverse PAW overlap matrix, mixed-precision arithmetic (FP32/TF32), and low-precision nearest-neighbor communication (BF16) during filtered subspace construction, along with block-wise computation-communication overlap to reduce cost while preserving robustness. These strategies yield up to $8\times$ and $20\times$ CPU-GPU speedups on Intel and AMD GPU architectures, respectively. Compared to plane-wave PAW methods, PAW-FE achieves close to 8$\times$ reduction in time-to-solution for 10,000-electron systems on NVIDIA GPUs, with larger gains at scale, and around 6$\times$ over norm-conserving FE approaches. We demonstrate scalability to 130,000-electron systems, establishing PAW-FE as an exascale-ready method for chemically accurate first-principles simulations.

Achieving Large Uniaxial and Homogeneous Strain in Two-Dimensional Materials

Authors: Yangchen He, Jessica Kienbaum, Wuzhang Fang, Hongrui Ma, Ying Wang, Ping Yuan, Daniel A. Rhodes
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26164v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Existing methods for strain engineering in 2D materials are limited to low strain values (<1.5%), suffer from poor reproducibility, and exhibit low strain transfer at cryogenic temperatures.

Main result. The authors developed a high-yield, reliable platform using PCL-functionalized Si substrates and a piezo-stack to achieve large, reversible, and homogeneous uniaxial strain up to the intrinsic failure limits of materials, including a record 5.5% in Td-WTe2.

Method. A piezo-stack-based strain cell is used with a custom Si substrate featuring an etched trench, functionalized with a polycaprolactone (PCL) film to enhance adhesion and strain transfer via a reversed dry transfer method.

Summary. This paper presents a new experimental platform for applying large and uniform mechanical strain to 2D materials. By using a specialized silicon substrate functionalized with a polymer, the researchers achieved strain levels far exceeding previous limits, reaching up to 5.5% in WTe2. The method is highly reproducible, works at cryogenic temperatures, and even allows for the creation of controlled strain gradients. This enables the systematic study of strain-induced phase transitions and new physical phenomena like flexoelectricity in 2D systems.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The platform is demonstrated on various 2D materials, specifically CrSBr as a primary model for strain calibration, and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) including 2H-MoTe2, 1T'-MoTe2, and Td-WTe2.

Key observables. Raman spectroscopy (peak shifts, FWHM, and mode splitting), Raman mapping for strain distribution, and optical imaging of cracks/failure.

Important parameters / regimes. Strain magnitudes up to 5.5%, temperature range down to 2 K, strain gradients up to 0.06%/um, and piezoelectric voltage (Vp) as a control parameter.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that the PCL layer provides sufficient adhesion and that the piezoelectric voltage serves as a linear proxy for applied strain.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the substrate setup and transfer process; Figure 5 illustrates the evolution of Raman spectra and peak positions for various MoTe2 phases under strain; supplementary figures show strain distribution maps and DFT validation.

Paper structure. Introduction to strain engineering limitations, description of the experimental strain platform and fabrication, validation using CrSBr, demonstration of broad applicability across TMD phases, and theoretical validation via DFT.

Abstract

Strain engineering is a powerful tool for tuning the electronic, magnetic, and topological properties of two-dimensional (2D) materials and thin films - particularly at high values of strain (>3%) where many electronic, magnetic, and structural transitions have been predicted. However, most approaches to tuning strain in 2D materials are limited below 1.5%, with poor repeatability when cycling strain and low strain transfer when cooling to cryogenic temperatures. Here, we report a high-yield sample preparation and device strain platform that overcomes these limitations, enabling precise, reversible strain tuning up to the intrinsic strain-to-failure of the materials tested herein. In addition, we show that this platform can be used to controllably design uniform linear strain gradients across of 10's of $μ$m, providing a novel route to systematically investigate flexoelectric and flexomagnetic phenomena. Using CrSBr as a model system, we demonstrate uniform uniaxial strain, up to ~4%, with negligible slippage and linear strain gradients of up to 0.06%/$μ$m. We further show that our strain approach is applicable to a broad class of 2D materials, validating its performance for three different phases of transition metal dichalcogenides: 2H-MoTe$_2$, 1T$^\prime$-MoTe$_2$ and T$_\mathrm{d}$-WTe$_2$. In T$_\mathrm{d}$-WTe$_2$, verified by theoretical calculations, we show a continuous redshift of the A$_1^3$ mode, up to a record-breaking ~5.5% strain, with a clear separation of the A$_1^3$ and A$_1^2$ modes starting at 2% strain.

Abstract

Authors: William R. T. Tait, Sriram Murali, Chao-Hua Hsu, Jantakan Nedsaengtip, Christina Lee, R. Paxton Thedford, Vibha Kalra, Joerg G. Werner, Ulrich B. Wiesner

Main problem. The challenge of creating a 3D nanoarchitected, all-organic electrochemical energy storage device that achieves efficient electrode separation and high capacity without traditional 2D-layered components.

Main result. The realization of the first all-organic, 'self-separating' 3D nanoarchitected lithium-ion device, where an in-situ generated SEI acts as the separator, achieving a discharge capacity of 267 mAh/g.

Method. The researchers used ultra-large molar mass block copolymer-directed self-assembly to create a porous carbon anode, electropolymerized a redox-active polymer (PAQEDOT) into the pores, and used electrochemical cycling to grow a solid electrolyte interphase (SEIT) as a separator.

Summary. This paper reports the development of a novel 3D-structured, all-organic battery architecture. By using block copolymer self-assembly, the authors created a porous carbon framework that is infiltrated with a redox-active polymer. Instead of a physical separator, they used electrochemical cycling to grow a solid electrolyte interphase that naturally separates the anode and cathode. While the device currently faces challenges with rapid capacity fade, it demonstrates a new 'self-separating' manufacturing paradigm for energy storage.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 3D nanoarchitected, all-organic, solid-state full cell consisting of a monolithic carbon anode with a co-continuous pore network and an interpenetrating cathode of the conductive polymer PAQEDOT.

Key observables. Discharge capacity (mAh/g), open circuit potential (OCP), electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) signatures, and redox potentials via cyclic voltammetry.

Important parameters / regimes. Pore size (~90-100 nm), voltage window (-0.6 V to 4.2 V vs. Li/Li+), and mass ratios of carbon to polymer (e.g., 3:1 or 2:1).

Assumptions / limitations. The design assumes that the in-situ generated SEI will be sufficiently insulating to prevent short circuits while remaining ionically conductive.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares 2D vs 3D architectures; Figure 3 shows carbonization and SEM morphology; Figure 5 illustrates the electrochemical processing steps for SEI formation; Figure 7 shows SEM of membrane-templated carbons and cycling stability.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the 3D architecture problem, details the synthesis of the BCP and monomers, describes the fabrication of the carbon anode and polymer cathode, explains the electrochemical 'activation' process for SEI formation, and concludes with performance analysis and degradation mechanisms.

Abstract

This work realizes a three-dimensionally (3D) nanoarchitected, all organic, "self-separating" lithium-ion electrochemical energy storage (EES) device that is cycled as a solid-state full cell. The device is enabled by a monolithic carbon anode with a co-continuous pore network, derived from the structure direction of resols by an ultra-large molar mass block copolymer (BCP), poly(styrene-block-2-dimethylaminoethyl methacrylate) (SA). Electropolymerization of a single-phase conductive and redox-active material, poly((2,3-dihydrothieno[3,4-b][1,4]dioxin-2-yl)methyl 9,10-dioxo-9,10-dihydroanthracene-2-carboxylate) (PAQEDOT), into the pore space provides the cathode of the cell. The device is electronically contacted to the relevant electrode network enabled by the co-continuous nature of each electrode. Electrochemical processing via cycling against external lithium in an electrolyte generates a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) as a separator and lithiates the cell electrodes, after which the EES device is cycled in the solid state. While the full cell does not demonstrate high cyclability, the best full cell demonstrates a discharge capacity of 267 milliamp hours per gram (mAh/g). This work marks, to the best of knowledge of the authors, the first example of an all-organic materials derived 3D nanoarchitected EES device, as well as the first design of "self-separating" cell fabrication. Furthermore, generalization of the design to another co-continuous carbon form factor is demonstrated.

Anomalous, pre-yield grain-boundary sliding in copper revealed with in-situ high-resolution strain mapping

Authors: Benjamin Poole, David Lunt, Luke Hewitt, Chris Hardie
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26515v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating the mechanism and timing of grain boundary sliding (GBS) in copper at room temperature, specifically challenging the view that GBS is primarily a high-temperature phenomenon.

Main result. The study reveals that grain boundary sliding occurs extensively prior to macroscopic yield and before the onset of significant crystallographic slip, exhibiting pure in-plane, pure out-of-plane, and mixed-mode sliding.

Method. In-situ mechanical loading within a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) using high-resolution digital image correlation (HRDIC), electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD), and laser scanning confocal microscopy (LSCM) for 3D height mapping.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that grain boundary sliding in copper occurs at room temperature during the elastic regime, before the material reaches its macroscopic yield point. By using advanced in-situ microscopy techniques, the authors show that sliding can be in-plane, out-of-plane, or mixed-mode. This discovery challenges the conventional understanding that grain boundary sliding is a phenomenon reserved for high-temperature deformation. The findings provide new insights into the early stages of plastic deformation in polycrystalline metals.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Oxygen-free high-conductivity (OFHC) copper subjected to quasi-static tensile testing at room temperature.

Key observables. Effective strain, in-plane rotation, crystallographic slip (trans-granular bands), and height gradient magnitude (as a proxy for out-of-plane deformation).

Important parameters / regimes. Nanoscale resolution (98 nm strain field resolution), 20 um x 20 um field of view, and the distinction between pre-yield and post-yield regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. A 1.5 um dilation of grain boundaries was required to account for spatial smearing and potential misalignments between EBSD and HRDIC maps.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Experimental workflow; Fig 2: In-situ stress-strain curve and strain/rotation maps; Fig 3: Time-series snapshots of strain evolution; Fig 4: Quantitative comparison of strain/rotation in grain cores vs. boundaries; Fig 5: Classification of sliding modes (in-plane, out-of-plane, and mixed).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of GBS timing, describes the experimental setup and multi-modal imaging workflow, presents time-series strain maps and quantitative comparisons of grain cores vs. boundaries, characterizes the 3D nature of sliding, and discusses the implications for bulk vs. surface deformation.

Abstract

Grain boundary sliding is typically associated with high temperature deformation in engineering alloys. Here, we examine grain boundary sliding at room temperature in oxygen-free high-conductivity copper under quasi-static tensile testing. By using high-resolution digital image correlation (HRDIC) conducted in-situ within a scanning electron microscope to produce time-series strain maps, we unexpectedly observe that grain boundary sliding occurs extensively prior to macroscopic yield, and before the onset of significant crystallographic slip. Extreme values in strain and in-plane rotation are found to be associated with grain boundaries immediately prior to yield and during the initial stages of plastic deformation, which are higher than those associated with crystallographic slip. By combining laser scanning confocal microscopy height mapping with the strain maps and orientation maps from electron backscatter diffraction, grain boundary sliding character is determined, finding evidence of pure in-plane, pure out-of-plane and mixed-mode sliding.

Main problem. The study investigates how to engineer programmable 3D spin textures in synthetic antiferromagnets (SAFs) by using chemical asymmetry and competing magnetic interactions to move beyond conventional skyrmion types.

Main result. The researchers demonstrated the emergence of two distinct skyrmion families (conventional and inverse polarity) localized exclusively in the CoFeB layer, driven by an effective field resulting from the competition between external fields and RKKY exchange.
