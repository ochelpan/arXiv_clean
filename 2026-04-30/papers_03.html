Rethinking Nonlocality: Locality, Counterfactuals, and the EPR-Bell Argument

Authors: Partha Ghose
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26916v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · quantum measurements 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper re-examines whether violations of Bell inequalities necessarily imply nonlocal causation or if they can be interpreted as evidence of contextuality.

Main result. The author demonstrates that Bell violations do not logically mandate nonlocality, but rather signal the failure of global value assignment (contextuality) across incompatible measurement contexts.

Method. The study employs a logical deconstruction of the EPR-Bell argument, a historical re-analysis of Einstein's formulations, and modern sheaf-theoretic approaches to model measurement contexts.

Summary. This paper challenges the standard conclusion that Bell inequality violations prove the nonlocality of nature. The author argues that these violations actually demonstrate contextuality—the impossibility of assigning definite values to all possible measurements simultaneously. By focusing on the role of counterfactual reasoning, the work suggests that locality can be preserved if one accepts that physical quantities are context-dependent. This interpretation aligns with modern mathematical approaches to quantum contextuality.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The analysis focuses on entangled quantum systems and the framework of the EPR setup and CHSH-type experiments involving spatially separated, non-interacting systems.

Key observables. Non-commuting observables (A, A', B, B') and the existence of a joint probability distribution for all observables.

Assumptions / limitations. The paper challenges the assumption of global value assignment (counterfactual definiteness) and evaluates the tension between locality, measurement independence, and contextuality.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a re-evaluation of the EPR argument, moves into a logical deconstruction of the assumptions in Bell's theorem, applies sheaf-theoretic frameworks to define contextuality, and concludes by contrasting Einstein's and Bohr's interpretations.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum information and foundations, as it provides a structural, sheaf-theoretic alternative to the standard interpretation of nonlocality, potentially reframing how we understand entanglement and contextuality.

Abstract

The widespread claim that violations of Bell inequalities establish the nonlocality of nature is critically reexamined. It is argued that this conclusion is not logically compelled by either the Einstein--Podolsky--Rosen (EPR) argument or Bell's theorem. The analysis highlights the central role of counterfactual reasoning -- the assumption that outcomes of unperformed measurements possess definite values -- in deriving Bell inequalities. It is shown that these inequalities follow not from locality alone, but from the conjunction of locality with a global assignment of values across incompatible measurement contexts. Their experimental violation therefore signals the impossibility of such a global assignment, i.e.\ contextuality, rather than necessarily implying nonlocal causation. This interpretation aligns with Bohr's emphasis on the contextual character of physical quantities and is naturally formulated within modern sheaf-theoretic approaches to contextuality.

Amplitude Encoding of Slater-Type Orbitals via Matrix Product States: Efficient State Preparation and Integral Evaluation on Quantum Hardware

Authors: Sorin Bolos
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26314v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of efficiently encoding and preparing physically accurate Slater-Type Orbitals (STOs) on quantum hardware to enable exact evaluation of quantum chemistry integrals without the exponential cost of generic state preparation.

Main result. The study establishes that 3D Cartesian amplitude encoding of STOs has bounded complexity rather than exponential scaling, with the MPS bond dimension saturating at approximately 138. It also demonstrates successful 1D integral pipeline validation on IBM Heron processors.

Method. The authors use Matrix Product States (MPS) for amplitude encoding and derive analytical constructions for 1D orbitals. They employ SVD truncation to manage bond dimension and use Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) for hardware error mitigation.

Summary. This paper presents a method for encoding Slater-Type Orbitals (STOs) on quantum computers using Matrix Product States (MPS). By using amplitude encoding, the authors show that the complexity of representing 3D orbitals does not grow exponentially but instead reaches a bounded bond dimension. They successfully demonstrate a 1D integral evaluation pipeline on IBM Heron hardware and prove that SVD truncation can significantly reduce circuit complexity without losing accuracy. This establishes a viable path for performing exact quantum chemistry calculations on near-term and fault-tolerant quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on hydrogen-like 1s and 2s orbitals in 1D, 3D spherical, and 3D Cartesian coordinate systems, validated on IBM Heron-generation quantum processors.

Key observables. Overlap integrals, kinetic energy integrals, and nuclear attraction integrals.

Important parameters / regimes. MPS bond dimension (chi), SVD truncation threshold, qubit count (n), and decay constant (zeta).

Assumptions / limitations. The efficiency of Cartesian encoding may depend on orbital localization; current hardware noise levels currently limit the feasibility of large-scale 3D Cartesian and nuclear attraction circuits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows discretization convergence for 1D integrals; Figure 2 shows 3D overlap convergence; Figure 3 plots bond dimension saturation and sensitivity to decay constants; Figure 4 illustrates the bond dimension profile across the MPS chain; Figure 5 and 6 show hardware validation and error scaling on IBM processors.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Gaussian vs. Slater trade-off, presents analytical 1D MPS constructions, extends the framework to 3D Cartesian and spherical coordinates, analyzes entanglement scaling and bond dimension saturation, validates results on IBM hardware, and discusses limitations regarding two-electron integrals and hardware noise.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant as it provides a scalable, tensor-network-based framework for mapping continuous molecular wavefunctions onto qubits, potentially enabling high-precision quantum chemistry simulations that bypass the systematic errors of Gaussian approximations.

Abstract

Slater-type orbitals (STOs) provide the physically correct description of atomic wavefunctions but have been largely replaced by Gaussian-type orbitals in computational chemistry due to the lack of closed-form multi-center integrals. We present a systematic study of amplitude encoding of STOs on quantum computers using matrix product states (MPS). For one-dimensional orbital functions of the form $p_d(x) e^{-ζx}$, we derive analytical MPS constructions with constant bond dimension $χ= d + 1$, requiring $O(n)$ classical and quantum resources for $n$-qubit registers with no grid sampling. We demonstrate a complete one-electron integral pipeline -- overlap, kinetic energy, and nuclear attraction -- in one dimension, validating the overlap and kinetic energy on IBM Heron processors at 5~qubits with 0.67\% hardware-induced error using Zero-Noise Extrapolation. In three dimensions, we compute multi-center overlap integrals between 1s and 2s orbitals in Cartesian coordinates with 0.02\% discretization error at 18~qubits. A systematic entanglement analysis reveals that the MPS bond dimension of three-dimensional STOs in Cartesian coordinates saturates with increasing grid resolution -- reaching $\sim$138 for the hydrogen 1s orbital at 12~qubits per coordinate -- establishing bounded encoding complexity rather than the exponential scaling initially expected. The SVD truncation threshold provides a practical resource parameter, reducing the bond dimension to 39 at threshold $10^{-6}$ with negligible accuracy loss. These results map the entanglement landscape for amplitude encoding of atomic orbitals and establish MPS-based state preparation as a viable path toward exact STO basis sets on quantum computers.

An Exponential Advantage for Adaptive Tomography of Structured States under Pauli Basis Measurements

Authors: Alireza Goldar, Zhen Qin, Zhihui Zhu, Zhe-Xuan Gong, Michael B. Wakin
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26043v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Determining whether adaptive measurement selection provides a fundamental advantage in sample complexity for quantum state tomography of structured states.

Main result. The paper proves an exponential separation in sample complexity, showing that an adaptive protocol achieves polynomial copy complexity while any non-adaptive design requires exponentially many copies for a specific family of structured states.

Method. The authors use a constructive stagewise prefix recovery algorithm for the adaptive upper bound and a rare-prefix mechanism based on KL divergence analysis for the non-adaptive lower bound.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that for certain structured quantum states, choosing measurements adaptively can reduce the number of required quantum copies from exponential to polynomial. By focusing on a hierarchical 'prefix/tree' family of states under local Pauli measurements, the authors prove a rigorous exponential advantage in sample complexity. This result highlights how sequential measurement strategies can significantly optimize state reconstruction on near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A multi-qubit system of n qubits using a structured 'prefix/tree' family of density matrices, where information is distributed hierarchically across Pauli basis strings, measured via single-copy local Pauli basis measurements.

Key observables. Trace distance between the estimated and true state, and the expectation values of prefix Pauli operators.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (n), error tolerance (epsilon), success probability (eta), and signal coefficients (alpha and beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The results are specific to the defined local Pauli measurement architecture and the constructed prefix/tree family of states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the adaptive budget growing with a cubic fit (n^3); Figure 2 shows the non-adaptive budget growing exponentially; Figure 3 provides a log-scale comparison illustrating the scaling gap.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of adaptive vs non-adaptive tomography, defines the structured prefix/tree state family and measurement model, provides a constructive adaptive upper bound, establishes a non-adaptive lower bound using KL divergence, and concludes with numerical verification of the scaling gap.

Abstract

Broad claims about whether adaptivity helps in quantum state tomography can be misleading unless the state family, measurement architecture, and error metric are specified carefully. We study a restricted but physically important regime: single-copy quantum state tomography under local Pauli basis measurements, where the allowed measurement settings are tensor-product measurement operators built from local single-qubit Pauli operators, and performance is measured in trace distance with high probability in a minimax sense over a known structured family. We construct an explicit discrete prefix/tree family of states for which adaptive measurement selection achieves polynomial copy complexity, while every non-adaptive design requires exponentially many copies in the worst case. The adaptive upper bound comes from stagewise prefix recovery using hierarchical breadcrumb information revealed by partial prefix matches. The non-adaptive lower bound is based on a rare-prefix mechanism: every fixed design under-samples some deep prefix subset, and outside that subset the competing hypotheses induce identical one-shot laws, so only an exponentially small fraction of the measurement budget contributes to the KL divergence between the full data distributions. The result isolates a concrete regime in which adaptivity provably changes the sample-complexity scaling under the experimentally common local Pauli measurement architecture.

Fast, powerful, low-noise optical pumping of an atomic vapor with semiconductor optical amplifiers

Authors: Diana Méndez-Avalos, Théo Louzada Meireles, Morgan W. Mitchell, Aleksandra Sierant
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26721v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum optics experiment 3/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Evaluating the noise properties and performance of different pulsed optical pumping strategies, specifically comparing semiconductor optical amplifier (SOA) amplitude modulation against traditional FM and AOM-AM methods for atomic magnetometry.

Main result. The SOA-AM method introduces negligible additional noise and achieves significantly higher magnetic sensitivity (up to 80 fT/sqrt(Hz)) and longer unpumped coherence times compared to FM and AOM-AM methods.

Method. The researchers compared three modulation techniques (FM, AOM-AM, and SOA-AM) in a Bell-Bloom and FID-mode $^{87}$Rb magnetometer, measuring Faraday rotation, spin polarization, and magnetic sensitivity.

Summary. This paper investigates using a semiconductor optical amplifier (SOA) to perform high-power, low-noise pulsed optical pumping in an $^{87}$Rb atomic vapor magnetometer. By comparing SOA-based amplitude modulation to traditional frequency and amplitude modulation, the authors show that the SOA introduces negligible extra noise while providing much higher power. This approach enables magnetic sensitivities up to two orders of magnitude better than previous methods and preserves spin coherence more effectively. The results suggest SOA-AM is a powerful tool for next-generation high-sensitivity atomic sensors.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An experimental platform consisting of an $^{87}$Rb atomic vapor in a 3 cm glass cell with 100 torr N2 buffer gas, operated in Earth-field conditions using a Bell-Bloom and Free-Induction-Decay (FID) magnetometer setup.

Key observables. Faraday rotation angle, spin polarization (Pz), coherence time (T2), and magnetic-field sensitivity (fT/sqrt(Hz)).

Important parameters / regimes. Larmor frequencies of ~500 kHz, duty cycles of 10%, and sensitivities reaching 80 fT/sqrt(Hz) at 600 Hz.

Assumptions / limitations. The sensitivity at higher pump powers is limited by environmental noise rather than the SOA itself.

Figures summary. Fig 1a shows the Bell-Bloom OPM setup; Fig 3a shows the PSD of polarization noise under SOA-AM; Fig 3b compares total spin noise between FM and SOA-AM.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of pulsed optical pumping, describes the experimental setup and three modulation methods, presents a comparative noise and coherence analysis, and concludes with the demonstrated sensitivity improvements.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for quantum optics and AMO physicists as it demonstrates a way to achieve high-power, low-noise control of atomic spin states, which is critical for developing high-bandwidth quantum sensors and interfaces.

Abstract

We use a $^{87}\text{Rb}$ atomic vapor, suitable for an optically-pumped magnetometer (OPM) in Earth-field conditions, to study the noise properties of three strategies for generating pulsed optical pumping. We compare a frequency-modulated (FM) laser, amplitude modulation (AM) via an acousto-optic modulator (AOM), and amplitude modulation via a semiconductor optical amplifier (SOA). Pumping the ensemble to operate as a Bell-Bloom OPM, and with an equal degree of spin polarization, the three methods give nearly identical sensitivity, showing that the SOA, despite being an active device, can introduce negligible additional noise. Pumping the ensemble to operate as a free-induction-decay OPM, we observe longer unpumped coherence times with the SOA-AM method than with the FM method. Finally, using the higher power available from the SOA, we demonstrate an environment-limited sensitivity of $80\text{fT}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ at $600\text{Hz}$ and 200fT$200\text{fT}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ at $4\text{kHz}$, one to two orders of magnitude beyond what was achievable with the other pumping methods.

Main problem. Developing a mathematical framework to recast classical Maxwell equations into a unitary, Schrödinger-like form suitable for implementation via Quantum Lattice Algorithms (QLA).

Main result. The authors demonstrate that by using Riemann-Silberstein-Weber (RSW) vectors, Maxwell's equations can be transformed into a linear, unitary evolution equation that approaches the continuous classical limit with second-order accuracy.

Method. The paper utilizes tensor calculus, linear algebra, and the construction of a Quantum Lattice Algorithm (QLA) involving streaming and entanglement operators on a discrete lattice.

Summary. This paper establishes the mathematical foundation for using quantum computers to simulate classical electromagnetic wave propagation. By reformulating Maxwell's equations into a unitary, Schrödinger-like structure using Riemann-Silberstein-Weber vectors, the authors propose a Quantum Lattice Algorithm (QLA). This approach allows for the simulation of waves in dielectric media and plasmas, potentially overcoming the computational bottlenecks of classical numerical methods. The work highlights how the linear nature of classical electromagnetism can be mapped onto the logic of qubits and quantum gates.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The system involves electromagnetic wave propagation in vacuum, dielectric media, and magnetized plasmas, modeled using the covariant form of Maxwell's equations and the Minkowski metric.

Key observables. Electric and magnetic field vectors (E, B), electric displacement (D), magnetic intensity (H), and the electromagnetic field tensor (F_mu_nu).

Important parameters / regimes. Plasma frequencies (electron/ion), cyclotron frequencies, Debye length, and the continuum limit where lattice spacing approaches zero.

Assumptions / limitations. The plasma is treated as a linear dielectric medium (linear response theory), and the current formulation assumes no external charge or current sources.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from fundamental linear algebra and tensor calculus to the application of Dirac notation and quantum postulates, eventually deriving the unitary representation of Maxwell's equations and the specific steps of the Quantum Lattice Algorithm.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in quantum optics and open quantum systems as it provides a rigorous mathematical bridge for using quantum hardware to simulate classical wave phenomena and complex plasma-wave interactions.

Abstract

Can quantum computers effectively simulate the propagation and scattering of electromagnetic waves in a classical plasma? This chapter introduces some of the basic concepts in mathematics and physics essential to answering that question. The numerical simulations of Maxwell equations for wave propagation in dielectrics are constrained by technological limitations of the present-day computers. In contrast, there has been ample fanfare around quantum computers and their potential to far exceed the performance of traditional computers. Whether the enhanced capabilities of a quantum computer can be put to use for simulating topics in classical physics is a source of intrigue and curiosity.

Phases and dynamics of an impurity immersed in one-dimensional quantum droplets

Authors: Dimitrios Diplaris, Ilias A. Englezos, Friethjof Theel, Peter Schmelcher, Simeon I. Mistakidis
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26528v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study investigates how a single impurity affects the ground-state properties, phase separation, and expansion dynamics of a one-dimensional two-component quantum droplet.

Main result. The impurity can selectively deform the droplet density profiles, causing density humps for attractive interactions and phase separation for repulsive interactions; furthermore, strong attractive interactions can stabilize the system against expansion following trap release.

Method. The authors use the ab-initio Multi-layer Multi-Configuration Time-Dependent Hartree method for Bosons (ML-MCTDHB) and compare it with the extended Gross-Pitaevskii equation (eGPE) and mean-field approximations.

Summary. This paper explores the physics of a single bosonic impurity embedded in a 1D quantum droplet. By tuning the interaction between the impurity and the droplet components, the researchers show that one can control density deformations, induce phase separation, or even stabilize the droplet against expansion. The study highlights the importance of many-body correlations beyond the standard Gross-Pitaevskii description. These findings suggest that impurities could serve as powerful tools for probing the internal structure of quantum droplets.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 1D three-component bosonic mixture consisting of a two-component quantum droplet (species A and B) and a single impurity (species C) in a harmonic trap.

Key observables. One-body density profiles, two-body coherence functions (G2), impurity localization, density overlap parameter, and fidelity/impurity dressing.

Important parameters / regimes. Impurity-droplet coupling strengths (gAC, gBC), interaction signs (attractive vs. repulsive), and the LHY fluid regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated in the 1D regime via strong transverse confinement; the eGPE approach assumes the validity of the Lee-Huang-Yang correction within the local density approximation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows ground-state densities for non-interacting cases; Figure 2 demonstrates density humps and dips across different coupling strengths; Figure 3 compares density overlap using different models; Figure 4 shows G2 correlation patterns; Figure 7 illustrates expansion dynamics following trap release; Figure 8 shows density profiles in a box potential.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical system, details the many-body and effective field methods, analyzes ground-state density deformations and correlation patterns, explores phase separation regimes, investigates expansion dynamics after trap release, and concludes with implications for impurity-based probing of droplets.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insights into how impurities can be used as local probes to manipulate and sense the structure of self-bound quantum droplets, bridging impurity physics with the study of quantum fluids.

Abstract

We explore the ground-state properties of a single impurity immersed in a one-dimensional quantum droplet medium formed by a two-component Bose mixture. Relying on ab-initio simulations, we demonstrate that tuning the impurity-droplet interactions allows to controllably reshape the droplets density profiles and associated correlation patterns. For attractive impurity-medium couplings, the impurity becomes localized within the droplet which exhibits a density hump at the vicinity of the impurity, while repulsive interactions facilitate their phase-separation. Comparing our many-body results to the appropriate extended Gross-Pitaevskii description, we find adequate agreement for the droplet density profiles, with the effective field approach systematically overestimating impurity localization. Following a release of the external trap, we unveil that the sign and magnitude of the interactions between the impurity and the droplet hosts dictate the response of the three-component setting which experiences expansion unless strongly attractive intercomponent couplings are present. These results corroborate the role and presence of correlations in impurity-droplet mixtures and inspire future investigations on impurity physics for probing droplet configurations.

The most discriminable quantum states in the multicopy regime

Authors: Maria Kvashchuk, Polina Chernyshova, Lucas E. A. Porto, Ties-A. Ohst, Lucas B. Vieira, Marco Túlio Quintino
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26927v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Identifying the sets of quantum states that achieve the highest success probability in minimum-error state discrimination when multiple identical copies of an unknown state are provided.

Main result. The authors prove that for large enough N, quantum k-designs are the most discriminable pure state sets, and that mixed states can outperform pure states when N exceeds the size of a k-design. They also demonstrate a quadratic advantage in discriminability for quantum systems over classical ones.

Method. The work utilizes semidefinite programming (SDP), the Holevo-Helstrom theorem, and the theory of quantum k-designs, alongside analytical tools like the symmetric subspace and multiplicative Bayes capacity.

Summary. This paper investigates the fundamental limits of distinguishing quantum states when multiple copies are available. It establishes that quantum k-designs are optimal for pure states and reveals that mixed states can actually be more discriminable than pure states in certain regimes. Furthermore, it proves that quantum systems possess a quadratic advantage over classical probability distributions in terms of discriminability. The study also explores how restricting states to real-valued (rebit) systems reduces this advantage.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An ensemble of N d-dimensional quantum states (qudits) provided in k identical and independent copies, with a uniform prior probability for each state.

Key observables. k-copy discriminability (maximal average probability of correctly guessing the state).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of states (N), dimension of the Hilbert space (d), number of copies (k), and the distinction between pure, mixed, classical, and real quantum states.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a uniform prior distribution for the states in the ensemble.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the k-copy discrimination scenario with Alice and Bob; subsequent figures show Bloch sphere representations of optimal state configurations for various parameters.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the k-copy discrimination problem, reviews single-copy results, introduces mathematical tools like k-designs and symmetric subspaces, analyzes pure and mixed state regimes, compares quantum to classical/real systems, and concludes with computational techniques and asymptotic scaling.
