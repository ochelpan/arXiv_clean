Influence of strain on the anomalous Hall and Nernst effects in Fe thin films

Authors: Ao Nakagawa, Ryo Toyama, Keisuke Masuda, Weinan Zhou, Hirofumi Suto, Kodchakorn Simalaotao, Yoshio Miura, Yuya Sakuraba, Tetsunori Koda
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26257v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to disentangle whether the strain-induced changes in the anomalous Hall effect (AHE) and anomalous Nernst effect (ANE) in Fe thin films are driven by intrinsic Berry curvature modulation or extrinsic scattering mechanisms.

Main result. The AHE is found to be predominantly governed by intrinsic mechanisms that follow theoretical predictions of Berry curvature modulation, whereas the ANE is strongly influenced by extrinsic contributions, leading to a significant deviation from theoretical predictions.

Method. The researchers used epitaxial Fe thin films on MgO and MAO substrates to vary lattice strain, performed scaling law analysis on magnetotransport data, and compared experimental results with first-principles DFT calculations.

Summary. This paper investigates how epitaxial strain affects the anomalous Hall and Nernst effects in iron thin films. By comparing films with different c/a ratios to theoretical Berry curvature calculations, the authors show that the Hall effect is primarily driven by intrinsic electronic structure changes. However, they find that the Nernst effect deviates significantly from theory due to strong extrinsic contributions. This distinction highlights the complexity of using strain to tune transverse transport in magnetic materials.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system consists of 20 nm thick (001)-oriented epitaxial Fe thin films grown on MgO (tensile strain) and MgAl2O4 (compressive strain) substrates, covering a range of c/a ratios from 0.9825 to 1.0108.

Key observables. Anomalous Hall conductivity (AHC), anomalous Nernst conductivity (ANC), longitudinal resistivity, Hall resistivity, Seebeck coefficient, and c/a ratio.

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice strain (tensile vs. compressive), tetragonal distortion (c/a ratio), temperature (15 K to 300 K), and magnetic field.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that using a single-element ferromagnet like Fe minimizes complexities from multicomponent stoichiometry, though it notes that impurities from different sputtering systems can affect the skew scattering sign.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows XRD profiles demonstrating epitaxial growth and strain-induced lattice constant shifts; Figure 2 presents scaling analysis for separating AHE components; Figure 3 shows Nernst electric field and coefficients as a function of c/a ratio; Figure 4 compares experimental c/a dependence with theoretical energy dependence.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of strain-induced effects, describes the experimental preparation of Fe films, presents structural characterization via XRD, details the magnetotransport and scaling analysis, compares results to DFT calculations, and concludes with a discussion on the differing physical origins of AHE and ANE.

Abstract

The anomalous Hall effect (AHE) and anomalous Nernst effect (ANE) are the transverse transport phenomena in magnetic materials, which reflect the Berry curvature arising from the electronic structure near the Fermi level. Lattice strain provides a direct means to tune these effects by modifying the electronic structure; however, disentangling the strain-induced effect through the Berry curvature modulations in multicomponent materials is challenging due to complexities arising from extrinsic contributions by impurities and disorder, as well as difficulties in simple direct comparison with first-principles calculations. In this study, we focus on Fe, a prototypical single element ferromagnet with a well-established electronic structure, and tune the sign and magnitude of the strain in epitaxial thin films of by varying the substrates and deposition conditions to investigate the strain effect on the AHE and ANE. Scaling law analysis revealed that the intrinsic anomalous Hall conductivity (AHC) exhibits a clear tetragonal distortion (c/a) dependence, in good agreement with theoretical calculations based on Berry curvature modification. In contrast, the anomalous Nernst conductivity (ANC) shows a pronounced deviation from the theoretical values and markedly different c/a dependence. These results demonstrate a crucial difference in the physical origin between the AHC and the ANC in the Fe films; the AHC is predominantly governed by intrinsic mechanisms, whereas the ANC is strongly influenced by the extrinsic contribution.

Kinetics of segregation of topologically-modified ring polymers in cylindrical confinement

Authors: Harsh Doshi, Shreerang Pande, Sathish K. Sukumaran, Apratim Chatterji
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26087v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating how topological modifications, specifically the introduction of internal loops, affect the kinetics and rate of segregation of ring polymers under cylindrical confinement.

Main result. Increasing the number of small loops decreases the segregation time due to enhanced entropic repulsion, although antiparallel initial configurations and certain loop sizes can significantly delay segregation.

Method. Langevin dynamics molecular dynamics simulations using the LAMMPS software package with a Velocity Verlet integration scheme.

Summary. This study explores how the presence of loops in ring polymers affects how quickly they separate when confined in a cylinder. By simulating modified polymers, the authors show that adding more loops increases entropic repulsion, which actually speeds up the segregation process. The research also highlights how the initial orientation of the polymers can create kinetic barriers that slow down separation. These findings provide insight into how bacteria might use loop-extruding proteins to control the timing of chromosome segregation during cell division.

Detailed structure

Model / system. Two unconcatenated, topologically modified (ToMo) ring polymers (Kremer-Grest bead-spring chains) confined within a cylindrical geometry, mimicking E. coli chromosome segregation.

Key observables. Time of segregation (tau_seg), normalized distance between centers of mass (delta z_COM/L), and monomer density distributions.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of loops, contour length of loops, degree of confinement (2Rg/D), and initial relative orientation (parallel vs. antiparallel).

Assumptions / limitations. The polymers are topologically constrained (no chain crossing), which does not account for the presence of topoisomerases in vivo.

Figures summary. Schematics of various topologies (Arc-0 to Arc-1-10), visualization of initialization protocols, time evolution of center of mass separation, and monomer density distributions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the biological motivation, defines the polymer models and topological modifications, details the simulation methods and initialization protocols, presents results on the effect of loop number and size, analyzes the impact of initial orientation, and discusses biological implications and limitations.

Abstract

In Escherichia coli (E. coli), entropic repulsion between the two daughter DNA ring polymers under cylindrical confinement is believed to be an important factor governing chromosomal segregation. The repulsion can be enhanced by topological modifications, i.e., by the introduction of internal loops at certain locations along the contour of the circular DNA. However, the effect of topological modifications on the rate of segregation of ring polymers remains unclear. Therefore, we systematically varied the number and the contour length of loops introduced at selected locations by crosslinking monomers. The appropriate crosslinking was motivated by observations that extruded loops are located mainly near the origin of replication (ori-proximal) region of the E. coli chromosome. This resulted in the chains becoming intrinsically anisotropic. Using Langevin dynamics simulations of these topologically modified bead-spring polymers, we calculated the time required for segregation under cylinder confinement. With certain caveats, we found that increasing the number of loops resulted in a decrease in the time of segregation. In line with past work, we propose that this is due to the increase in the entropic repulsion between the polymers upon increasing the number of loops. In addition to the number of loops, the contour length of the loops and the mutual orientation of the (anisotropic) chains in the initial configurations played a role in determining the time of segregation.

Linear poroelastic response of thin permeable gel films

Authors: Caroline Kopecz-Muller, Joshua D Mcgraw, Thomas Salez
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26464v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Deriving the linear poroelastic Green's function to predict the space- and time-dependent mechanical response of a thin, permeable gel layer bounded to a rigid substrate.

Main result. The study provides an analytical expression for the poroelastic response, showing that deformation is localized to a radius comparable to the layer thickness and transitions from an incompressible to a compressible elastic state over time.

Method. The authors use an analytical approach involving Hankel and Laplace transforms in reciprocal space to solve the coupled poroelasticity equations and numerical inversion (Talbot algorithm) for real-space profiles.

Summary. This paper develops a mathematical framework to model how thin, porous gel coatings deform when a force is applied. It specifically addresses the challenge of finite thickness, showing that the mechanical response is confined to a region near the contact point. The researchers derived a Green's function that allows for the prediction of how these coatings relax from an incompressible to a compressible state. These findings are highly applicable to microfluidics, soft robotics, and biological membrane studies.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A thin, hydrophilic, and permeable poroelastic gel layer of thickness tau, bounded at the bottom by a rigid, impermeable substrate and exposed to a solvent bath at the top.

Key observables. Surface normal deformation profile w(r, t) and the poroelastic Green's function G_tau(r, t).

Important parameters / regimes. Layer thickness (tau), shear modulus (G), permeability (k), Poisson ratio (nu), and solvent viscosity (eta).

Assumptions / limitations. Linear poroelasticity (small deformations), isotropic material, incompressible solvent, and a rigid/impermeable substrate.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the system and applied force; Figure 2 displays deformation in reciprocal space across different temporal frequencies; Figure 3 shows scaled deformation vs. spatial frequency; Figure 4 shows real-space deformation profiles at different times/thicknesses; Figure 5 illustrates the relaxation of a gate-shaped pressure field.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a system definition and governing poroelastic equations, moves to a mathematical derivation using potential functions and transforms in reciprocal space, presents asymptotic analyses of various physical limits, and concludes with numerical results and applications to axisymmetric pressure fields.

Abstract

When a hydrophilic and deformable porous material is immersed in a bath, it may absorb the solvent and expand by several times its volume, thus forming a highly soft and porous hydrogel. A stress applied on the soft hydrogel surface deforms it and forces the absorbed solvent to move by flowing through the network of pores. This coupled phenomenon sets the framework of poroelasticity. Moreover, polymeric gels are often used in ultra-thin coatings to tune surface properties. Together with the characteristic poroelastic coupling, this thinness challenges the modelling of their response. In this article, we derive the point-force mechanical response of a thin, permeable and poroelastic layer bounded to a rigid substrate. We show that the gel surface is only deformed around the indentation point, within a radius on the order of the layer thickness. The obtained Green's function can be directly used to predict the space- and time-dependent surface deformation of the gel. Our findings are relevant for a broad range of applications, such as indentation experiments on swollen gels, thin membranes or soft and living systems, as well as lubrication problems involving a soft and porous wall, for instance in microfluidics.

Mixture of Experts Framework in Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials for Atomistic Simulations

Authors: Gabriel de Miranda Nascimento, Marc L. Descoteaux, Laura Zichi, Chuin Wei Tan, William C. Witt, Nicola Molinari, Sriteja Mantha, Daniil Kitchaev, Mordechai Kornbluth, Karim Gadelrab, Charles Tuffile, Boris Kozinsky
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26143v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The high computational cost of deep equivariant machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) limits large-scale atomistic simulations, while multi-fidelity approaches often suffer from mechanical mismatch and artificial stresses at the interface of different models.

Main result. The authors developed a Mixture-of-Experts framework that achieves over 2x computational speedup compared to high-fidelity models while maintaining predictive accuracy and physical consistency (energy conservation and mechanical alignment) in a Pt+CO catalytic system.

Method. A multi-fidelity 'Mixture-of-Experts' framework using a co-training strategy with agreement constraints to penalize energy and force discrepancies between high-fidelity and low-fidelity models on shared bulk environments.

Summary. This paper introduces a way to speed up large-scale atomistic simulations by using different levels of machine learning model complexity for different parts of a system. By applying expensive, high-accuracy models only to chemically active regions (like surfaces) and cheaper, low-accuracy models to the bulk, the researchers achieved a 2x speedup. Crucially, they used a special training technique to ensure that the boundary between these two models does not create unphysical stresses or energy errors. This allows for much larger and longer simulations of complex materials like catalysts without losing the accuracy of high-end methods.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An E(3)-equivariant Allegro architecture applied to a 1,040-atom Pt+CO catalytic system, where the domain is spatially partitioned into a high-fidelity region (reactive interface) and a low-fidelity region (bulk lattice).

Key observables. Per-atom energy, atomic forces, equation of state, bulk modulus, and energy conservation.

Important parameters / regimes. Alpha (weight of the agreement loss), number of layers and tensor features in the Allegro models, and the energy shift correction (E_ghost).

Assumptions / limitations. The domain decomposition is static (atom assignments are fixed at initialization) and the system can be partitioned into disjoint subsets.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows training dataset diversity and the atom partitioning strategy; Figure 2 displays validation errors and model disagreement during training; Figure 3 shows performance on held-out test sets; Figure 4 illustrates the Pt+CO slab structure and compares wall-clock time efficiency.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational bottleneck of MLIPs, presents the Mixture-of-Experts framework and the co-training strategy, details the implementation on a Pt+CO system, validates accuracy and mechanical consistency, and concludes with efficiency benchmarks and future directions.

Abstract

First-principles atomistic simulations are essential for understanding complex material phenomena but are fundamentally limited by their computational cost. While Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials (MLIPs) have drastically improved cost for a given accuracy, their inference cost remains a bottleneck for massive systems or long timescales. To address this, we introduce a multifidelity "Mixture-of-Experts" framework based on the E(3)-equivariant Allegro architecture. Our method spatially partitions the simulation domain into a chemically complex region (e.g., reactive interfaces) and a simple region (e.g., bulk lattice), assigning models of varying capacity to each. Among the challenges in such static domain decomposition, the mechanical mismatch between models at the interface is particularly critical, as it can generate artificial stress fields and instability. We address this challenge with a co-training strategy in which the loss function includes agreement constraints -- penalties on per-atom energy and force discrepancies between models evaluated on shared bulk environments -- forcing the independent models to learn a consistent physical description of the bulk material. We validate this approach on a realistic Pt+CO catalytic system, demonstrating that the co-trained models maintain exact energy conservation, align their bulk mechanical response (e.g., equation of state and bulk modulus), and achieve predictive accuracy comparable to a full high-fidelity simulation at more than twice the computational speed.

This work highlights both the power of transferable ML potentials and the complexity of transport dynamics in multicomponent metallic liquids.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the ML potential and system, presents structural and thermodynamic validation against experiment, analyzes composition-dependent structural changes, investigates transport properties (viscosity and diffusion), and discusses the breakdown of standard transport models in specific concentration regimes.

Abstract

We investigate the structural and dynamical properties of binary aluminum-titanium liquid metallic alloys, as a function of temperature and composition. We make use of MD-simulations, using a transferable machine-learning potential developed by Song et al. [Nature Communications 15, 10208 (2024)], and compare our results to experimental data. Although this potential was initially trained on solid properties, we find good agreement between the experimental data and the simulation results for the liquid state. The excess volume and compositional changes of the structure are captured well by the machine-learned potential. The simulation allows to disentangle local packing from chemical-ordering effects; the latter are found to be weak in Al-Ti. Dynamical quantities like the viscosity and the diffusion coefficients are also discussed.

Negative magnetoresistance in strained $α$-Sn and $α$-SnGe films in an in-plane magnetic field

Authors: Sunny Phan, Andrei Kogan, Jesse Thompson, Trent Johnson, Alexander Khaetskii, Arnold Kiefer
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26168v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to determine if the chiral anomaly is the physical origin of negative magnetoresistance (MR) in strained alpha-Sn and alpha-SnGe films.

Main result. The authors find that negative MR persists in both the Dirac semimetal and the 3D topological insulator phase, leading them to reject the chiral anomaly as the primary mechanism and suggest a mechanism related to strong spin-orbit interaction.

Method. Magnetotransport measurements were performed on MBE-grown epitaxial films at 5 K using a 9-Tesla solenoid magnet, with structural characterization via X-ray diffraction and RHEED.

Summary. This paper investigates the origin of negative magnetoresistance in strained alpha-Sn and alpha-SnGe films. By comparing the transport properties of the Dirac semimetal phase and the 3D topological insulator phase, the researchers demonstrate that the negative MR occurs even when the chiral anomaly is absent. This suggests that the effect is likely driven by strong spin-orbit coupling rather than the chiral anomaly. The study also highlights how material quality and phase impurities like beta-Sn can complicate the interpretation of topological transport phenomena.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system consists of strained, epitaxial thin films of pure alpha-Sn (a Dirac semimetal) and alpha-SnGe alloy (a 3D topological insulator) grown on CdTe substrates.

Key observables. Magnetoresistance (MR), Hall resistivity, carrier density, and transport mobility.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature of 5 K, magnetic field strength up to 8 T, and in-plane vs. out-of-plane field orientations.

Assumptions / limitations. The films are assumed to be elastically strained with negligible relaxation, and the analysis assumes the absence of significant current shunting from the substrate.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Dirac point schematic and Hall bar geometry; Figure 2 displays MR for out-of-plane and parallel magnetic fields; Figure 3 shows in-plane MR for different orientations; Figure 4 and 5 present XRD scans and reciprocal space maps for strain analysis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of negative MR, describes the material growth and device fabrication, presents magnetotransport results for different field orientations, discusses the rejection of the chiral anomaly hypothesis, and concludes with an analysis of material quality and potential confounding factors like beta-Sn formation.

Abstract

To test the hypothesis that the chiral anomaly is responsible for negative magnetoresitance (MR) in \atn{}, we have studied magnetotransport in strained, epitaxial films of pure \aSn{} and the alloy \aSnGe{} that are in the Dirac semimetal and 3D topological insulator state, respectively. We have observed for both states a negative MR with current either parallel or transverse to the in-plane magnetic field, but with a different dependence of MR on $\vec{B}$ strength. Our results are inconsistent with the chiral anomaly and suggest that other mechanisms may be responsible for negative MR in the Dirac/Weyl semimetal phase of \aSn{}. We also discuss several factors in sample design and material quality that may be contributing to the incongruous observations of MR reported in studies of strained \atn{} films.

Negative nonlocal and local voltages (resistances) in a quasi-one-dimensional superconducting aluminum structure

Authors: V. I. Kuznetsov, O. V. Trofimov
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26814v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study investigates the origin of anomalous negative nonlocal and local voltages (and resistances) in a quasi-one-dimensional superconducting aluminum structure near the critical temperature.

Main result. The negative voltages are attributed to quasiparticle charge imbalance at the N-S interface, and the experimental temperature dependence is best described by incorporating equilibrium superconducting fluctuations (AL and MT corrections).

Method. Low-frequency DC measurements were performed on aluminum wire networks in zero and varying magnetic fields, with experimental data compared against the nonequilibrium SBT model and equilibrium fluctuation theories.

Summary. This paper explores the phenomenon of negative local and nonlocal resistances in aluminum superconducting wires. The researchers found that when a narrow wire is in a normal state while a wider wire is superconducting, injecting current creates a quasiparticle charge imbalance. This imbalance leads to voltages with a sign opposite to the applied current. The study successfully reconciles experimental observations with theories involving both nonequilibrium charge imbalance and equilibrium superconducting fluctuations.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A quasi-one-dimensional aluminum structure consisting of a wide superconducting wire and adjacent narrow wires, operating in a heterogeneous normal-superconducting (N-S) regime where $T_{cn} < T < T_{cw}$.

Key observables. Nonlocal voltage ($V_{NL}$), local voltage ($V_L$), nonlocal resistance ($R_{NL}$), local resistance ($R_L$), and critical currents ($I_c$).

Important parameters / regimes. Critical temperatures ($T_c \approx 1.486$ K), coherence length ($\xi$), quasiparticle diffusion length ($\lambda_Q$), and magnetic field ($B$).

We find that the conductance increases linearly with magnetic field at low fields and saturates beyond a critical field to a constant value that is independent of microscopic details such as interface coupling, arc geometry, and lattice-scale parameters. This universal saturation reflects a transport mechanism governed by the topological charge pumping associated with the Chern layers, rather than magnetic breakdown between Fermi arcs. We further show that, under specific conditions, a junction between two distinct Weyl semimetals can exhibit a similar saturation behavior, thereby clarifying the topological origin of the observed universality.

Abstract

The high-temperature ceramic compound Al5C3N with promising application usage belongs to the scarcely studied Al-C-N system. It was originally reported as an ordered compound in the non-centrosymmetric space group P63mc and described as a nanolaminate with an -Al2C-AlN-Al2C2- stacking sequence. The recently reported structural disorder in the related compound Al4SiC4 led us to question this proposed structure for Al5C3N and investigate the possibility of a disordered structure in the centrosymmetric space group P63/mmc. In the present work, we employed different synthesis routes to maximize the yield and quality of the desired phase, and applied a variety of techniques to probe the Al5C3N crystal structure. Our single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis clearly indicates that the non-centrosymmetric space group P63mc must be rejected. From a joint refinement of single-crystal X-ray and powder neutron diffraction data, the occupancies of C and N were refined at two sites in P63/mmc resulting in the stacking sequence -Al2C-Al(C/N)-Al2(C/N)2-. Furthermore, DFT calculations show that a centrosymmetric disordered structure described in a supercell has the lowest energy, 0.2 eV per formula unit, relative to the previously reported P63mc structure. The calculated band structure shows both direct and indirect band gaps which lead to implications for the physical properties. Finally, STEM analysis provides additional evidence that the crystal structure of Al5C3N is better described in the centrosymmetric space group P63/mmc.

Abstract

We study experimentally and theoretically the Stokesian settling of a well-known class of porous shapes: Bravais lattice unit-cells, whose porosity we vary controllably by changing their lattice spacing. In our experiments, conducted in a square cuboidal container with its long-axis aligned along gravity, we find that the settling speed U and the solid fraction φ of these lattice units obey a power-law relationship U $\propto$ φ^γ , with an exponent γ = 0.43 independent of their shape. To understand the observed scaling exponent, we analytically and numerically investigate the settling of the simple cubic structure under different approximations. We find that the walls of the container, though far from the sinking object, have a defining effect. Our Stokesian boundary integral simulations show that the Faxen's boundary correction captures the wall-effects accurately and enables us to discount the wall-effect from the experimental data, yielding a power-law exponent γ = 0.30 for settling in an unbounded domain. The power-law relating sinking speed and porosity is a step towards predictively understanding the sedimentation fluxes of complex objects in the clouds and the oceans. However, the applicability of this universal scaling to irregular and biologically richer aggregates found in nature remains an open direction.
