**Main problem.** Determining the asymptotic behavior and typicality of entanglement entropy in many-body systems subject to fixed global abelian (U(1)) and non-abelian (SU(2)) charge conservation.

**Main result.** The authors derive a general formula for the average entanglement entropy that can be expressed via a single quantity representing local thermal entropy, and show that the variance is exponentially suppressed, making the entropy 'typical'.

**Method.** The study employs asymptotic analysis using Laplace's method and the saddle-point approximation, alongside group theory (character theory and Haar measures) and information theory (Kullback-Leibler divergence).

**Summary.** This paper provides a general framework for calculating the typical entanglement entropy in many-body systems with fixed global charges. By mapping the problem to a thermodynamic context, the authors show that the entanglement entropy scales according to a local thermal entropy function. The results cover both abelian U(1) and non-abelian SU(2) symmetries, demonstrating that the entropy is highly concentrated around its average. This work offers a powerful tool for studying the entanglement properties of quantum chaotic systems and the effects of symmetry on information scrambling.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.** The framework applies to any many-body system with a local single-charge symmetry group in the thermodynamic limit, including specific models like qubits, qutrits, and spin-trimers under U(1) and SU(2) symmetries.

**Key observables.** Average entanglement entropy, variance of entanglement entropy, and local entropy/heat capacity.

**Important parameters / regimes.** Thermodynamic limit (N approaching infinity), subsystem fraction (f), global charge density (s), and local Hilbert space dimension (k).

**Assumptions / limitations.** The analysis assumes a finite-dimensional local Hilbert space and focuses on the thermodynamic limit; it also assumes the smoothness of the integrand except in specific critical regimes.

**Figures summary.** Figure 1 plots local entropy versus charge density for various U(1) models; Figure 2 is mentioned but its specific content is not detailed in the notes.

**Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of charge-conserved entanglement, develops a thermodynamic mapping using relative entropy, provides asymptotic expansions for U(1) and SU(2) symmetries, analyzes specific model examples, and discusses the implications for quantum chaos and typicality.

**Why it may be interesting.** This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a universal thermodynamic interpretation of entanglement scaling and establishes how global symmetries influence the entanglement properties of random states, which is crucial for understanding thermalization and quantum chaos.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We consider a many-body Hilbert space with a fixed global charge and show that the typical entanglement entropy of a subsystem, at the leading and subleading order in the thermodynamic limit, can be expressed in terms of a single quantity which represents the local thermal entropy at fixed charge density. We find a general formula which applies both to abelian U(1) symmetry and non-abelian SU(2) symmetry, including the case of a local Hilbert space which transforms under a general reducible representation of the symmetry group. We illustrate the general formula with model systems and discuss the relevance of the results as a probe of quantum chaos for physical Hamiltonians.

**Main problem.** The paper investigates whether maximal entanglement is a necessary condition for 'full nonlocality' (correlations with zero local content) and seeks to identify the conditions under which pure entangled states exhibit such nonlocality.

**Main result.** The authors prove that in all bipartite dimensions d >= 3, non-maximally entangled states can be fully nonlocal, and that all pure entangled states can be 'activated' to show full nonlocality in the many-copy scenario.

**Method.** The study establishes a link between full nonlocality and the 'antidistinguishability' of quantum states, using geometric interpretations of Bell sets, Semidefinite Programming (SDP), and analysis of Schmidt coefficients.

**Summary.** This paper addresses the long-standing question of whether maximal entanglement is required for full nonlocality. The authors demonstrate that for dimensions 3 and higher, non-maximally entangled states can indeed exhibit full nonlocality. Furthermore, they show that any pure entangled state, no matter how weakly entangled, can be activated to produce fully nonlocal correlations if enough copies of the state are provided. This work provides new sufficient conditions for nonlocality based on Schmidt coefficients.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.** The study focuses on bipartite quantum systems in d x d Hilbert spaces, specifically considering pure entangled states in Schmidt form and their tensor products (many-copy scenario).

**Key observables.** Local content (LC), Bell functionals, and the overlap of post-measurement states.

**Important parameters / regimes.** Schmidt coefficients (lambda_max, lambda_min), dimension (d), and the number of copies (k).

**Assumptions / limitations.** The results primarily focus on pure states and assume the availability of certain measurement sets like Mutually Unbiased Bases (MUBs).

**Figures summary.** Figure 1 shows a standard Bell scenario; Figure 2 illustrates the geometry of Local, Quantum, and Non-signaling sets and the PR box; Table I compares local deterministic strategies with the PR box.

**Paper structure.** The paper introduces the concept of full nonlocality, establishes the link to antidistinguishability, proves the existence of non-maximally entangled fully nonlocal states for d >= 3, demonstrates the activation of nonlocality via multiple copies, and provides bounds on local content.

**Why it may be interesting.** This is highly relevant for quantum information theorists as it provides a fundamental limit on the utility of entanglement for tasks like quantum pseudo-telepathy and clarifies the relationship between entanglement and non-classical correlations.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

It is a well-established fact that some quantum correlations can be nonlocal, meaning that they cannot be described by a local hidden variable model. Certain quantum correlations have a form of nonlocality so strong that they cannot be reproduced even by models having an arbitrarily small local hidden variable component. These correlations are called fully nonlocal and lead to Bell inequalities in which the maximum quantum value saturates the non-signaling bound. A well-known example of this effect, which is also referred to as quantum pseudo-telepathy or all-versus-nothing proofs of nonlocality, is the quantum distribution fulfilling the Peres-Mermin square, in which the underlying state is a $4\times4$ dimensional maximally entangled state. Other examples of full nonlocality are known but, so far, all of them are for maximally entangled states and it is an open question whether maximal entanglement is necessary for full nonlocality. In this work, we first establish a link between full nonlocality and the concept of antidistinguishability of quantum states. We use this connection to show that in every bipartite $d\times d$ Hilbert space, with $d\geq3$, there are non-maximally entangled states that are fully nonlocal. In fact, we derive simple sufficient conditions for full nonlocality that are only based on the smallest and largest Schmidt coefficients. We also show that in every dimension there exist pure entangled states that do not exhibit full nonlocality. Finally, we show that all pure entangled states can be activated to show full nonlocality in the many-copy scenario.

</details>

**Main problem.** The high circuit depth and gate count required by generic quantum compilers for Hamiltonian simulation lead to excessive noise and decoherence on NISQ devices.

**Main result.** A structure-aware compilation framework achieves significantly higher hardware fidelity and lower CX gate counts (up to 136x reduction) compared to generic blackbox transpilation, even outperforming exact synthesis in noisy regimes.

**Method.** A three-stage pipeline combining native hardware placement, a greedy oracle for adaptive Trotter block selection, and variational refinement using a Trotter-initialized ansatz.

**Summary.** This paper presents a new way to compile quantum circuits for simulating time-evolving Hamiltonians. Instead of treating the evolution as a generic operation, the method uses the known structure of the Hamiltonian to create much shorter circuits. By combining adaptive Trotter steps with a variational refinement step, the authors show that these shorter, approximate circuits actually perform better on real noisy hardware than deeper, exact circuits. This approach significantly reduces the number of error-prone two-qubit gates and helps avoid the vanishing gradient problem in quantum optimization.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.** 1D chains of 3-8 qubits using Heisenberg, Ising, and XY models, validated on IBM Torino and IBM Heron-based hardware architectures.

**Key observables.** Process fidelity (F), hardware fidelity (Fhw), Hellinger fidelity, CX gate count, and gradient variance.

**Important parameters / regimes.** Number of qubits (n=3-8), coupling strength (J=0.5, 1.0), evolution time (t), and error regimes (optimistic, typical, pessimistic).

**Assumptions / limitations.** The Hamiltonian has a known local structure (nearest-neighbor) and the framework is limited by classical computational costs for larger qubit counts.

**Figures summary.** Figures show hardware fidelity comparisons between the pipeline and blackbox methods, process fidelity under various depolarizing noise levels, and gradient landscape analysis demonstrating the avoidance of barren plateals.

**Paper structure.** The paper introduces the problem of compilation bottlenecks, details the three-stage adaptive native pipeline, compares it against standard Trotter and blackbox baselines, provides hardware validation on IBM Torino, and analyzes scaling and noise resilience.

**Why it may be interesting.** It provides a practical strategy for executing many-body dynamics on near-term hardware by prioritizing circuit shallowness and structural preservation over exactness, which is crucial for simulating many-body physics.

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Compiling time-evolution operators of the form $U(t)=e^{-iHt}$ into hardware-native gate sequences is a central bottleneck for digital quantum simulation on noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. Generic transpilation treats $U(t)$ as an arbitrary unitary, discarding the structure of Hamiltonian dynamics and producing circuits whose depth exceeds hardware coherence limits. We introduce a structure-aware compilation framework that treats product-formula decompositions as synthesis primitives rather than simulation approximations. The method combines (i) native placement of Hamiltonian terms onto the hardware coupling map, (ii) adaptive selection of Trotter blocks via a greedy discretization procedure, and (iii) variational refinement using a Trotter-initialized ansatz. Across Heisenberg, Ising, and XY models with $n=3$--$8$ qubits, the compiled circuits achieve fidelities $F>0.996$ with approximately linear scaling in the number of entangling gates, while generic synthesis produces circuits that are orders of magnitude deeper. On IBM Torino hardware, we observe a regime in which shorter approximate circuits outperform deeper exact decompositions: a 27-CX circuit achieves higher hardware fidelity ($F_{\mathrm{hw}}=0.987$) than a 187-CX exact circuit. These results demonstrate that, in the NISQ regime, structure-aware approximate compilation can outperform exact structure-agnostic synthesis, providing a practical pathway for executing Hamiltonian dynamics without requiring pulse-level control.

</details>

**Main problem.** The study investigates how a quadratic, momentum-dependent spin texture in a two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) modifies Kondo transport properties and the Kondo temperature (TK).

**Main result.** The Kondo temperature is strongly suppressed by the quadratic spin-splitting coupling, leading to a 'Kondo collapse' at a critical coupling value where TK drops by approximately 37%.

**Method.** A Green's function formalism and many-body approach are used to calculate electronic self-energy and resistivity corrections to the third order in the s-d exchange coupling.

**Summary.** This paper explores how momentum-dependent spin-orbit coupling affects the Kondo effect in a 2D electron gas. The research also details how mass anisotropy influences longitudinal resistivity. These findings are important for understanding transport in materials with complex spin textures like BaSbPt.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

**Model / system.** An anisotropic two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) with S=1/2 Kondo impurities, described by a low-energy effective k.p model featuring quadratic spin-splitting and anisotropic effective masses.

**Key observables.** Longitudinal resistivity corrections (delta rho_xx/rho_xx_0 and delta rho_yy/rho_yy_0) and the renormalized Kondo temperature (TK).

</details>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

We investigate the transport properties of an anisotropic two-dimensional electron gas with a quadratic spin texture, described by a low-energy effective $ k\cdot p$ model, in the presence of $S=1/2$ Kondo impurities. We develop a Green's function framework that captures the interplay between the spin texture and the Kondo scattering for calculating transport properties in such systems. Using this framework, we evaluate the longitudinal resistivity corrections $Δρ^{xx}/ρ^{xx}_0$ and $Δρ^{yy}/ρ^{yy}_0$ to third order in the $s$-$d$ exchange coupling and analyze their temperature dependence. We further determine the Kondo temperature $T_K$ and show that it is strongly suppressed with increasing quadratic spin-splitting coupling $α$. In particular, we identify a critical coupling $α_{cr}$ marking the onset of a Kondo collapse, at which $T_K$ is reduced by approximately 37\% relative to that of the conventional two-dimensional electron gas.

**Main problem.** The paper investigates the minimum-error discrimination of multi-copy states, specifically comparing the success probabilities of quantum (qubit) strategies versus classical (bit-like) strategies under different measurement hierarchies.

**Main result.** The authors prove that for all n > k, a qubit strategy exists that outperforms all bit strategies, and they identify instances of nonlocality without entanglement (NLWE) where certain bit-like theories outperform the best qubit strategies using classical measurements.

**Method.** The study employs mathematical frameworks from Generalized Probabilistic Theories (GPTs), using Positive Operator-Valued Measures (POVMs), the Pretty Good Measurement (PGM), and Semidefinite Programming (SDP) to calculate success probabilities and bounds.

**Summary.** This paper explores how having multiple copies of a quantum state changes the advantage of quantum theory over classical theory during state discrimination. The results provide general bounds for operational theories and show how the success probability scales with the number of copies.

<details><summary>Detailed structure</summary>

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Quantum state discrimination is a fundamental information processing task that serves as a building block for numerous applications and provides implications at the foundational level. In this work, we consider minimum error discrimination of multi-copy states, where instead of preparing a single system we assume that multiple instances of the same state are prepared. Now the discrimination allows for measurements from multiple parties with different measurement strategies varying from global measurement strategy to ones restricted to different forms of local operations and classical communication strategies. By comparing the average success probabilities in quantum and classical cases, we find a qubit strategy that outperforms all the bit strategies. However, we find that there are other bit-like operational theories which can outperform the best qubit strategies even with a classical measurement strategy and we are able to identify instances of different theories where different measurement strategies are optimal. In this way, we are able to find instances of nonlocality without entanglement as well as provide general bounds for bit-like operational theories.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

The pure-loss channel is a fundamental model for describing noise in bosonic quantum platforms. It is characterised by a single parameter, the transmissivity, which quantifies the fraction of the input energy that reaches the output of the channel. In realistic scenarios, however, such as free-space quantum communication, the transmissivity is not fixed but fluctuates from one channel use to another. In this setting, the overall channel is effectively described as a convex combination of pure-loss channels, known as a fading channel. Despite its practical relevance, the quantum Shannon theory of the fading channel has remained largely unexplored. Here, we address this gap, specifically investigating degradability, anti-degradability, entanglement breakingness, and capacities of the fading channel. Of particular relevance to practical quantum-internet applications, we prove that entanglement distribution and quantum key distribution can always be achieved at a strictly positive rate over any fading channel, no matter how noisy it is or how strongly the transmissivity fluctuates, provided the channel is not completely noisy. Moreover, we prove that thermal states, which are optimal for a broad class of static bosonic Gaussian channels, fail to achieve the entanglement-assisted classical capacity of fading channels: non-Gaussian Fock-diagonal states strictly outperform all Gaussian encodings. Most strikingly, we identify regimes where the coherent information of thermal inputs vanishes, while optimized non-Gaussian states achieve strictly positive values, thereby activating the channel for quantum communication. For a paradigmatic binary fading model we establish this result analytically, deriving the exact capacity-achieving state in closed form. For general fading distributions, we design an iterative variational algorithm to optimize the coherent and mutual information.

<details><summary>Abstract</summary>

Quantum annealing targets low-energy solutions of Ising/QUBO problems, but reliable assessment requires more than best-energy comparisons. This dissertation develops a benchmarking framework for D-Wave quantum annealers that combines strong classical baselines, sampling and diversity metrics, and thermodynamic cost.   Its first contribution, SpinGlassPEPS.jl, is a topology-aware tensor-network heuristic for optimization and sampling on Pegasus/Zephyr-like graphs. It maps Ising instances to local Potts clusters, represents the partition function with PEPS, and performs branch-and-bound search in probability space. Benchmarks show that it is a physically interpretable reference solver, though approximate contractions limit its competitiveness on the largest instances.   The second contribution treats quantum annealers as effective thermal machines, relating success probability and solution quality to dissipation, entropy production, and effective temperature. Carefully placed pauses can improve performance while reducing thermodynamic cost, although longitudinal fields may become harmful in paused schedules.   The thesis also introduces reinforcement-learning post-processing to improve returned samples and exact small-system simulations to probe annealing dynamics. Overall, it argues for quantum-annealing benchmarks that jointly measure algorithmic performance and physical expenditure.
