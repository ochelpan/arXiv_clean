Electronic structure, quasiparticle renormalizations, and magnetic correlations in the alternating single-layer bilayer nickelate La$_5$Ni$_3$O$_{11}$

Authors: I. V. Leonov
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26627v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Understanding the microscopic origins of the anomalous normal-state electronic structure, magnetism, and orbital-dependent correlations in the hybrid nickelate La5Ni3O11 (1212-LNO).

Main result. The study reveals an orbital-selective Mott insulating state in the single-layer Ni ions and strongly renormalized quasiparticle bands in the bilayer Ni ions, with magnetic correlations shifting from single-layer to bilayer-dominated due to correlation effects.

Method. Fully charge self-consistent DFT+DMFT using plane-wave pseudopotentials and the continuous-time hybridization expansion quantum Monte Carlo (CTQMC) method.

Summary. This paper uses advanced DFT+DMFT calculations to study the hybrid nickelate La5Ni3O11. It identifies a unique orbital-selective Mott insulating state in the single-layer Ni ions while the bilayer ions remain metallic but heavily renormalized. The research also shows how electronic correlations reconstruct the magnetic spectrum and how high pressure can drive a transition from an insulator to a metal. These findings are crucial for understanding the interplay between structural complexity and strong electron correlations in new superconducting materials.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system is the alternating single-layer/bilayer Ruddlesden-Popper nickelate La5Ni3O11 (1212-LNO), consisting of structurally distinct NiO6 slabs.

Key observables. Quasiparticle renormalization (m*/m), spectral functions, magnetic susceptibility (spin/charge density waves), and local magnetic moments.

Important parameters / regimes. Hubbard interaction U = 6 eV, Hund's exchange J = 0.95 eV, and high-pressure regimes (>20 GPa).

Assumptions / limitations. Use of the particle-hole bubble approximation for momentum-resolved static magnetic susceptibility, neglecting higher-order vertex corrections.

Paper structure. The paper investigates the electronic and magnetic properties of 1212-LNO, detailing the orbital-dependent correlations in single-layer vs. bilayer Ni ions, the effects of pressure on the Mott transition, and the reconstruction of magnetic instabilities.

Abstract

Using DFT+DMFT we study the normal-state electronic structure and magnetic correlations of the recently discovered alternating single-layer bilayer Raddlesden-Popper nickelate La$_5$Ni$_3$O$_{11}$ (1212-LNO). Our results exhibit qualitative differences for the structurally distinct single-layer and bilayer Ni ions, implying the importance of confinement and orbital-dependent correlations. The Ni $e_g$ electronic states originating from the bilayer Ni ions form strongly renormalized quasiparticle bands with a large enhancement factor $m^*/m \sim 3.5$ and 4.2 for the Ni $x^2-y^2$ and $3z^2-r^2$ orbitals, respectively. Moreover, the $e_g$ states of the single-layer Ni ions exhibit an orbital-selective Mott insulating state, with a narrow energy gap for the Ni $3z^2-r^2$ states and metallic, strongly incoherent (non-Fermi-liquid) Ni $x^2-y^2$ ones. Our analysis of magnetic correlations suggests the formation of intertwined spin and charge density wave stripes in the bilayer NiO$_6$ slab, in close similarity to the double-layer material. We refine two major instabilities, a leading one is associated with a wave vector ${\bf Q}=(\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{3})$ (up-down-0" spin pattern), competing with the bicollinear $(\frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{4})$ (up-up-down-down") stripe state. The single-layer Ni $3d$ electrons exhibit instability towards the Néel-type magnetic state. Under pressure, 1212-LNO undergoes an orbital-selective Mott insulator-to-metal phase transition, associated with metallization of the single-layer Ni $e_g$ states. As a result, the single-layer Ni $e_g$ bands exhibit non-Fermi-liquid (bad metal) behavior with strongly incoherent spectral weights near $E_F$. We note that correlation effects result in a reconstruction of magnetic correlations as compared to that obtained within DFT. In fact, we observe a crossover from single-layer to double-layer dominated magnetic correlations.

Emergence of $π$ from Equatorial Quantum Localization

Authors: Bin Ye, Ruitao Chen, Lei Yin
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26638v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating whether the mathematical constant pi emerges from non-radial, angular geometric mechanisms during the classicalization of quantum states.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that pi emerges from equatorial localization on a sphere, where the quantum rigidity index is exactly related to the Wallis partial product.

Method. The study uses analytical derivations of expectation values for spherical harmonics, asymptotic analysis of Gamma functions, and the application of the correspondence principle.

Summary. This paper shows that the mathematical constant pi can be derived from the way quantum probability clouds localize around the equator of a sphere. By studying the highest-weight spherical harmonics, the authors find that a specific geometric measure of localization, called the rigidity index, is exactly linked to the Wallis product. As the quantum number increases, the system approaches classical behavior, and the Wallis formula for pi is recovered. This reveals that certain mathematical structures in quantum mechanics are signatures of geometric localization rather than specific radial dynamics.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The paper analyzes the highest-weight branch of spherical harmonics within the frameworks of a rigid rotor and a thin spherical shell.

Key observables. Equatorial Rigidity Index (Rm), Wallis partial product (Wm), and angular width (delta theta).

Important parameters / regimes. Large-quantum-number limit (m to infinity), highest-weight branch (m = l), and the regime of radial freezing.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation is specific to the highest-weight branch of spherical harmonics and assumes a fixed spherical radius (radial freezing).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of pi emergence, defines the geometric rigidity index, applies the derivation to the rigid rotor and thin shell models, and concludes with the asymptotic recovery of the Wallis formula in the semiclassical limit.

Abstract

We present a genuinely non-radial quantum-mechanical route by which $π$ emerges from equatorial localization on the sphere. For the highest-weight branch of spherical harmonics, this localization is captured by a natural geometric rigidity index, whose exact finite-quantum-number value is a Wallis partial product. The mechanism is realized in two settings: the standard rigid rotor and the surface sector of a thin spherical shell, where radial freezing reduces the dynamics to the same angular problem. In the large-quantum-number limit, the probability cloud collapses toward the equator, the rigidity index approaches its classical value, and the Wallis formula is recovered through the correspondence principle. The result shows that Wallis-type structures in quantum mechanics can arise as exact signatures of semiclassical localization encoded by a simple geometric observable.

En Route to a Standard QMA1 vs. QCMA Oracle Separation

Authors: David Miloschewsky, Supartha Podder, Dorian Rudolph
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26921v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper investigates the computational power of quantum witnesses under perfect completeness, specifically seeking oracle separations between the complexity classes QMA1 and QCMA.

Main result. The authors construct classical oracles that separate QMA1 from QCMA under bounded adaptivity and derive an in-place permutation oracle separation. They also establish lower bounds on the circuit complexity required to prepare approximate ground states of certain sparse Hamiltonians.

Method. The study employs oracle constructions, derandomization of permutation-based models, and the Feynman-Kitaev history state construction. They use techniques like the parallel-query hybrid method, quantum phase estimation, and transcript analysis to prove lower bounds.

Summary. This paper addresses fundamental questions in quantum complexity theory regarding whether quantum witnesses provide more power than classical ones when perfect completeness is required. By constructing specific oracle models, the authors demonstrate a separation between QMA1 and QCMA. Furthermore, they bridge complexity theory and many-body physics by showing that these separations imply high circuit complexity for preparing the ground states of certain sparse, frustration-free Hamiltonians.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The work focuses on complexity-theoretic models including sparse Hamiltonians (O(1)-sparse), permutation oracles (in-place and forward), and the Hamiltonian query model (Orc_H).

Key observables. Completeness and soundness gaps, ground-state energy, and circuit gate complexity.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of adaptive rounds (R), number of parallel queries (q), Hamiltonian sparsity (d), and the precision of the energy gap (exponentially small vs. fixed).

Assumptions / limitations. The separation results are sensitive to the number of adaptive rounds and the specific oracle model used; some results assume a specific universal gate set.

Paper structure. The paper begins by introducing the QMA1 vs. QCMA problem, followed by the construction of classical oracles for bounded adaptivity. It then moves to the derandomization of permutation oracles to achieve in-place separation, analyzes the impact of small energy gaps, and concludes by applying these complexity results to derive lower bounds on Hamiltonian ground-state preparation.

Abstract

We study the power of quantum witnesses under perfect completeness. We construct a classical oracle relative to which a language lies in $\mathsf{QMA}_1$ but not in $\mathsf{QCMA}$ when the $\mathsf{QCMA}$ verifier is only allowed polynomially many adaptive rounds and exponentially many parallel queries per round. Additionally, we derandomize the permutation-oracle separation of Fefferman and Kimmel, obtaining an in-place oracle separation between $\mathsf{QMA}_1$ and $\mathsf{QCMA}$. Furthermore, we focus on $\mathsf{QCMA}$ and $\mathsf{QMA}$ with an exponentially small gap, where we show a separation assuming the gap is fixed, but not when it may be arbitrarily small. Finally, we derive consequences for approximate ground-state preparation from sparse Hamiltonian oracle access, including a bounded-adaptivity frustration-free variant.

Fault-Tolerant Resource Comparison of Qudit and Qubit Encodings for Diagonal Quadratic Operators

Authors: Samuel Godwood, Doğa Murat Kürkçüoğlu, Gabriel N. Perdue, Marina Maneyro, Alessandro Roggero
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26792v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Comparing the fault-tolerant resource costs, specifically non-Clifford gate counts, between qudit and qubit encodings for implementing diagonal quadratic operators.

Main result. While qubit encodings are asymptotically superior, qudits can provide meaningful constant-factor savings in specific finite-dimensional windows, particularly in the LCU framework and time-dominated regimes.

Method. The authors analyze two simulation paradigms (product-formula and LCU/block-encoding) using a resource metric of non-Clifford gate counts after synthesis into a discrete gate set.

Summary. This paper investigates whether using higher-dimensional qudits can reduce the computational overhead of simulating scalar field theories compared to standard qubit encodings. By analyzing non-Clifford gate costs in both Trotter-based and LCU-based simulation regimes, the authors find that while qubits win asymptotically, qudits offer significant resource savings for small, finite dimensions. The work provides explicit thresholds for quantum compilers to determine when qudit encodings are advantageous.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on the implementation of diagonal quadratic operators, such as the mass term $e^{-it\phi_x^2}$ in a discretized real scalar field theory using a uniform grid.

Key observables. Non-Clifford gate counts (T-count), rotation counts, and the ratio of resource costs between qudit and qubit encodings.

Important parameters / regimes. Local dimension $d$, number of qubits $n_b$, synthesis prefactor $a$, target precision $\epsilon$, and simulation time $t$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes an idealized, negligible-overhead code-switching model between qubits and qudits and focuses on the synthesis cost of embedded $SU(2)$ rotations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the break-even synthesis prefactor versus dimension; Figure 2 compares non-Clifford resources in precision-dominated and time-dominated regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of qudit vs. qubit encoding, analyzes the product-formula regime, evaluates the LCU framework, performs a secondary analysis using a code-switching model, and concludes with synthesis targets for compilers.

Why it may be interesting. It provides critical guidance for designing hardware-efficient quantum algorithms for lattice field theories by identifying specific dimensions where qudit-based architectures outperform standard qubits.

Abstract

Finite local Hilbert-space truncations arise naturally in quantum simulations of lattice field theories and motivate qudit encodings, but their fault-tolerant advantage over qubit encodings remains unclear. We compare the non-Clifford cost of implementing quadratic diagonal evolutions, exemplified by $U=e^{-itφ_x^2}$ in a uniform field-amplitude discretization of a real scalar field, using either one logical $d$-level qudit or $n_b=\lceil \log_2 d\rceil$ logical qubits. We analyze two standard settings: product-formula simulation and LCU/block encoding, taking the resource metric to be the number of non-Clifford gates after synthesis into a discrete logical gate set. Because tight synthesis bounds for general single-qudit rotations are not known, we express the qudit constructions in terms of embedded two-level $SU(2)$ rotations and derive explicit finite-$d$ break-even conditions for their synthesis cost; these serve as compiler targets for when qudit encodings can outperform the qubit baseline. Within the constructive models studied here, product-formula implementations would require an exponentially stronger per-primitive synthesis advantage for qudits to win asymptotically, while in the LCU setting the qubit encoding is asymptotically cheaper in $d$. Nevertheless, the finite-$d$ threshold analysis identifies low dimensional regions in which qudits can yield meaningful constant-factor savings, particularly for LCU-based implementations. As a secondary analysis of the LCU construction, we use an idealized negligible-overhead qubit-qudit code-switching model to give an absolute $T$-count comparison, and reinterpret the savings as an allowable per-switch overhead budget.

Finite-Temperature Ferromagnetic Correlations of the Kagome Lattice Hubbard Model

Authors: Francisco Correia, Kyle Corbett, Ehsan Khatami
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26827v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study investigates the development of ferromagnetic correlations and the metal-insulator transition in the Kagome lattice Hubbard model across varying densities and interaction strengths.

Main result. The authors find that repulsive interactions enhance ferromagnetic correlations at high densities and that increasing interaction strength extends these correlations toward half-filling, connecting flat-band ferromagnetism to Nagaoka ferromagnetism. They also provide an accurate estimate for the critical interaction strength of the Mott transition at $U_c/t = 6.41(5)$.

Method. The study utilizes two exact finite-temperature numerical methods: Numerical Linked-Cluster Expansion (NLCE) and Determinant Quantum Monte Carlo (DQMC).

Summary. This paper explores how magnetism evolves in the Kagome lattice Hubbard model. By using exact numerical methods, the authors show how ferromagnetic correlations transition from being driven by flat-band physics to being driven by Mott physics (Nagaoka ferromagnetism) as interaction strength increases. They also precisely locate the metal-insulator transition at half-filling. These findings are significant for understanding strongly correlated electrons in frustrated geometries and provide a roadmap for experiments using ultracold atoms in optical lattices.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The Kagome lattice Fermi-Hubbard model, characterized by geometric frustration, a non-interacting flat energy band, and van Hove singularities.

Key observables. Spin correlations, magnetic susceptibility, charge compressibility, density of states, charge gap, and Curie-Weiss temperature.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength ($U/t$), temperature ($T/t$), and electron density ($ ho$).

Assumptions / limitations. The NLCE convergence is limited by the size of the largest clusters at low temperatures, and DQMC is subject to the fermion sign problem.

Figures summary. Figures show the equation of state, magnetic susceptibility across different densities and temperatures, Curie-Weiss temperature dependence, and compressibility/double occupancy as functions of interaction strength and temperature.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Kagome Hubbard model, describes the NLCE and DQMC methodologies, presents results on magnetic correlations and susceptibility, analyzes the Mott metal-insulator transition via compressibility, and concludes with implications for future studies.

Abstract

The Kagome lattice Fermi-Hubbard model is one of the most physically rich, and at the same time most challenging, models to study in strongly-correlated physics. Among its special features are geometric frustration and a flat energy band that create conditions favorable to ferromagnetism near the band insulating limit. Here, we utilize two exact finite-temperature methods, the numerical linked-cluster expansion and the determinant quantum Monte Carlo, to study the extent as well as doping and interaction dependence of ferromagnetic correlations and other thermodynamic properties of the model. We find that repulsive interactions enhance ferromagnetic correlations at high electron densities and that increasing the interaction strength, extends the region with strong ferromagnetic correlations towards half filling, smoothly connecting it to Nagaoka ferromagnetism near the Mott insulating region. We also use the charge compressibility to obtain an accurate estimate for the critical interaction strength for the metal-insulator transition at half filling. These results improve our understanding of the magnetic tendencies of the model away from half filling and pave the way for further studies, including with ultracold atoms in optical lattices.

Abstract

Tricationic protactinium monofluoride ($^{229}$PaF$^{3+}$) has been proposed as a candidate for probing physics beyond the Standard Model of particle physics. Since studies with $^{229}$PaF$^{3+}$ require significant experimental advances, we exploit the stable, valence-isoelectronic dicationic cerium monofluoride (CeF$^{2+}$) as a surrogate. Gas-phase fluorinated-cerium molecular ions are formed and identified using the Off-Line Ion Source and TITAN mass measurement facilities at TRIUMF. Quantum chemical calculations are performed on the electronic structure of CeF$^{2+}$, revealing a parallel to that of $^{229}$PaF$^{3+}$. Moreover, these calculations provide estimates on the sensitivity of CeF$^{2+}$ itself to various $\mathcal{P,T}$-odd properties. A brief discourse on the specifics of the quantum control of CeF$^{2+}$ is presented which anticipates future searches for symmetry violations.

Functional Dimensional Regularization for O(N) Models

Authors: P. Beretta, A. Codello
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.26207v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The paper aims to extend the Functional Dimensional Regularization (FDR) scheme to the O(N) universality class and evaluate its efficiency and accuracy in calculating critical exponents.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that FDR provides highly efficient and accurate results for critical exponents in the O(N) model, with precision competitive with state-of-the-art methods like the Conformal Bootstrap and higher-order Non-Perturbative Renormalization Group.

Method. The study utilizes Functional Dimensional Regularization (FDR) at the second-order derivative expansion (DE2) level, involving a polynomial expansion of the potential and wave-function renormalizations.

Summary. This paper investigates the Functional Dimensional Regularization (FDR) scheme applied to the O(N) universality class. The authors show that FDR is a lightweight and efficient method that can produce critical exponents comparable to much more computationally intensive non-perturbative renormalization group and conformal bootstrap methods. By explicitly deriving the flow equations, they prove that the scheme's success is not coincidental. The results demonstrate rapid convergence and high accuracy across a wide range of N values.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on O(N) symmetric models/universality classes in three dimensions, including specific cases such as the Ising model (N=1), XY model (N=2), Heisenberg model (N=3), and self-avoiding random walks (N=0).

Abstract

Abstract

Abstract

Predicting species persistence within ecological communities is a fundamental challenge for both empirical and theoretical ecology. Existing methods span from mechanistic models, whose parameters are difficult to estimate from data, to statistical tools whose context-specific parameters are less interpretable. Here, we present a general framework, grounded in the statistical physics of complex systems, that integrates the key processes governing species survival into a single measurable quantity: the competitive balance.
