Summary. This paper introduces the Generalized Multilevel Amplitude Damping (GMAD) channel to model qudit dynamics in thermal environments. It reveals that these channels can exhibit counterintuitive thermodynamic behaviors, such as non-monotonic work extraction and a quantum-driven Mpemba effect.

Why it may be interesting. It provides new insights into how dissipative dynamics and thermal environments can be exploited for work extraction and reveals purely quantum thermodynamic effects like the coherent Mpemba effect.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Characterizing the thermodynamic performance and energetic capacities of a new class of quantum channels (GMAD) for work extraction.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that GMAD channels exhibit non-monotonic ergotropic capacitance with respect to temperature and can drive a coherent ergotropic Mpemba effect.

Method. The study uses Kraus representation derivation, ergotropy analysis (total, coherent, and incoherent), and asymptotic analysis of work functionals (shell-maximal and ball-maximal).

Model / system. A d-level quantum system (qudit) coupled to a thermal environment at inverse temperature beta, modeled via the Generalized Multilevel Amplitude Damping (GMAD) channel.

Key observables. Ergotropy (total, coherent, and incoherent), work capacitance, and Maximal Asymptotic Work-Energy Ratio (MAWER).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (beta), input energy (E), coupling constants (g_ij), and the number of channel iterations.

Assumptions / limitations. The system Hamiltonian is assumed to be non-degenerate, and the interaction is energy-preserving.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a qualitative sketch of the GMAD channel on a 4-level system; Figure 2 displays ergotropy as a function of energy for various temperatures; Figure 3 shows local ergotropic capacitance; Figure 6 illustrates the decomposition of unitary evolution.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the GMAD channel, derives its microscopic Kraus representation, analyzes its thermodynamic properties (ergotropy and capacitance), and demonstrates specific phenomena like the Mpemba effect.

Abstract

We introduce a new class of quantum channels, the Generalized Multilevel Amplitude Damping (GMAD) channels, to model noise and decoherence effects in a qudit coupled to a thermal environment. The degradation of energetic resources under GMADs is investigated by evaluating work functionals and ergotropic capacitances, with particular attention to the coherent and incoherent contributions to ergotropy, for which we introduce new quantifiers. Our analysis sheds light on how to optimally prepare a qudit in a thermal environment in order to preserve its value from the perspective of work extraction, and reveals several counterintuitive phenomena: the ergotropic capacitance of a GMAD channel is not monotonic in the temperature of the environment; moreover, iterating the map can lead to crossings between ergotropic functionals at different temperatures, indicating the presence of a Markovian Mpemba effect.

Quantum statistical mechanics: Gauge invariance, operator shifting, hyperdensity functionals, and nonequilibrium sum rules

Authors: Johanna Müller, Matthias Schmidt
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26650v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper establishes a formal gauge theory for quantum statistical mechanics based on the invariance of observables under operator shifting. It introduces a 'hyperdensity functional theory' and derives exact sum rules that connect local correlations to global observables. The framework is applicable to both equilibrium and time-dependent quantum many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for deriving exact sum rules and density functionals, which are essential for understanding many-body dynamics and developing new consistency checks for numerical many-body simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a generalized statistical mechanical theory of gauge invariance against operator shifting in quantum many-body systems and establishing its connection to hyperdensity functional theory.

Main result. The authors prove that a shifting superoperator induces exact sum rules (force, hyperforce, and two-body) and demonstrate that this gauge invariance is compatible with both particle exchange symmetry and canonical quantization.

Method. The framework utilizes superoperator algebra, the Mori product (inner product in Liouville space), and Lie theory to implement gauge transformations via a shifting field.

Model / system. Quantum many-body systems of N fermions or bosons in thermal equilibrium, non-equilibrium, or ground states, described by a Hamiltonian with kinetic energy, interparticle interaction, and external potentials.

Key observables. Density, current density, force density, hyperforce density, hyperfluctuation profile, and hyperobservables.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), chemical potential (mu), particle mass (m), and the shifting field (epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. The Hamiltonian is assumed to be additive for the reduction of many-body averages; the shifting field is assumed to be smooth; the system is initially thermalized for the nonequilibrium extension.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the microscopic model and equilibrium gauge invariance to describing operator shifting properties, developing hyperdensity functional theory, extending to nonequilibrium dynamics, and finally relating the quantum theory to its classical counterpart via Dirac's correspondence principle.

Abstract

We provide an extended acount of the recent statistical mechanical theory of gauge invariance against operator shifting in quantum many-body systems (arXiv:2509.20494). The gauge transformation is enacted by a shifting superoperator that displaces the fundamental position and momentum degrees of freedom. The shifting superoperator constitutes a map between Hilbert space operators and it features Lie algebra commutator structure. Averages of general observables remain invariant under the shifting both in and out of thermal equilibrium, as well as in groundstates. The gauge invariance induces exact sum rules that interconnect global observables and associated locally resolved correlation functions. In particular we describe the resulting one-body force, hyperforce, product, and two-body sum rules. We relate the shifting superoperator to a previously formulated quantum canonical transformation and present the generalization of quantum shifting to multi-component systems. The gauge theory respects fundamental fermionic and bosonic particle properties, as we demonstrate by proving the compatibility of operator shifting and exchange symmetry. We formulate the quantum version of hyperdensity functional theory to provide formal access to hyperforces as well as to general averaged quantum observables via universal density functionals. For time-dependent situations, we describe quantum dynamical gauge invariance and prove exact dynamical sum rules for nonequilibrium situations, as generated by Hamiltonian time dependence. We argue for the fundamental status of statistical mechanical gauge invariance based on the compliance of the underlying geometry with canonical quantization according to Dirac's correspondence principle. Analogies and differences of the quantum mechanical sum rules with their classical counterparts remain indicative of the respective levels of description.

Summary. The paper presents an analytical framework for the analytic continuation of Green's functions by treating the thermal kernel through the lens of Slepian information theory. It identifies the Shannon number as the fundamental limit for information capacity and introduces a deterministic method for optimal data sampling. This allows for high-fidelity spectral reconstruction using significantly fewer data points than traditional dense grids.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a rigorous, information-theoretic foundation for compressed sensing in many-body dynamics, offering a way to reduce the computational cost of processing large Quantum Monte Carlo datasets without losing essential spectral information.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The ill-posed inverse problem of analytic continuation, specifically reconstructing real-frequency spectra from noisy imaginary-time Green's functions in quantum many-body physics.

Main result. The paper derives an analytical singular-value decomposition for the thermal kernel and provides a deterministic, information-theoretically optimal sampling grid using a colleague-matrix construction.

Method. The author applies Slepian information theory to map the thermal kernel to a finite Fourier transform problem, utilizing Sturm-Liouville algebra and Legendre colleague matrices for optimal sampling.

Model / system. The study focuses on the Fredholm integral equation of the first kind representing the relationship between imaginary-time Green's functions and real-frequency spectra for fermionic and bosonic many-body systems.

Key observables. Real-frequency spectra A(omega), imaginary-time Green's functions G(tau), and the Shannon number Nc.

Important parameters / regimes. The Shannon number Nc = betaomega_max/pi, the bandwidth/temperature scale c = betaomega_max/2, and the compression factor.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the ability to separate the universal dynamical Laplace transmission factor from the statistics-dependent thermal weight.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the exponential decay of singular values (the eigenvalue cliff) around the Shannon number. Figure 2 demonstrates that reconstruction error for a four-peak Gaussian spectrum drops significantly when the number of sampling nodes reaches the Shannon scale.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the analytic continuation problem, introduces the Slepian mapping to decompose the kernel, derives the analytical SVD via Sturm-Liouville theory, proposes a deterministic sampling grid via colleague matrices, and validates the method with a numerical compression test.

Abstract

Analytic continuation from imaginary-time Green's functions to real-frequency spectra is a central ill-posed inverse problem in quantum many-body physics. We show that the thermal kernel admits an analytical generalized singular-value structure once its purely dynamical part is separated from the statistical weight imposed by the heat bath. The dynamical kernel is the imaginary-bandwidth continuation of Slepian's finite Fourier transform and is governed by the same Sturm-Liouville algebra that yields prolate spheroidal wave functions. Fermionic and bosonic statistics then enter as gauge transformations of the frequency-space inner product, producing self-adjoint effective potentials but no numerical kernel diagonalization. The Shannon number, $N_c=β\wmax/π$, fixes the upper information capacity of this pure Laplace channel. Finally, the optimal sampling points are obtained as eigenvalues of a Legendre colleague matrix, giving a deterministic compressed-sensing grid without iterative root searches.

Bose-Einstein thermal operators for semidefinite optimization

Authors: Michele Minervini, Nana Liu, Mark M. Wilde
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27228v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a framework for solving semidefinite programs by recasting them as Bose-Einstein free-energy minimization problems. By leveraging the spectral properties of the dual operator, it provides quantum algorithms that scale much more efficiently than classical methods in the presence of a spectral gap. This approach also introduces a new quantum relative entropy specifically designed for unbounded positive operators.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel way to map optimization problems to many-body physics, specifically using bosonic thermal states to bypass the dimension-dependent bottlenecks of classical SDP solvers.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Efficiently solving semidefinite programs (SDPs) over the unbounded positive semidefinite cone, which is challenging for classical and existing quantum solvers due to exponential scaling and the lack of an upper bound on the primal trace.

Main result. Established a mathematical equivalence between SDPs and bosonic thermodynamic systems, providing a regularized framework that achieves an exponential advantage in error bounds when the dual slack operator has a spectral gap.

Method. A quantum-classical hybrid algorithm using Hamiltonian simulation, Hadamard tests, and Cauchy sampling to perform stochastic gradient and Newton methods on a regularized Bose-Einstein free-energy minimization problem.

Model / system. A thermodynamic system of independent, non-interacting bosonic modes where the optimization variable represents expected occupation numbers and the dual slack operator acts as a grand canonical Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Expected occupation numbers, total expected energy, thermal expectation values, and the Bose-Einstein quantum relative entropy.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), chemical potential (mu), spectral gap (Delta), ground-space degeneracy (d0), and the minimum eigenvalue of the dual slack operator (lambda_min).

Assumptions / limitations. The dual slack operator is strictly positive definite (lambda_min > 0) and the problem satisfies Slater's condition (strict feasibility).

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the equivalence between SDPs and bosonic thermodynamics, then introduces a regularized free-energy minimization approach. It develops the information geometry of a new Bose-Einstein relative entropy, derives the complexity of hybrid quantum algorithms for gradient and Hessian estimation, and concludes with error analysis and contraction properties under affine maps.

Abstract

We establish that semidefinite programs (SDPs) over the unbounded positive semidefinite cone are mathematically equivalent to thermodynamic systems of independent bosonic modes: the eigenvalues of the optimization variable play the role of expected occupation numbers, the linear objective plays the role of total expected energy, and the linear equality constraints play the role of conserved non-commuting charges. Building on this perspective, we recast general SDPs as bosonic free-energy minimization problems at strictly positive temperature, regularized by the Bose-Einstein entropy; the original SDP is recovered in the zero-temperature limit. The optimal primal variable takes the form of a Bose-Einstein thermal operator parametrized by the dual variables. We prove an approximation-error bound that depends on the ground-space degeneracy and the spectral gap of the dual slack operator, improving on the linear-in-dimension worst-case duality gap of interior-point methods. We also introduce the Bose-Einstein quantum relative entropy as a Bregman divergence on the unbounded positive semidefinite cone, generated by the negative Bose-Einstein entropy. We propose it as a natural divergence for unnormalized positive operators, for which the standard Umegaki relative entropy can become negative, and we show that it satisfies a restricted monotonicity property under affine maps modeling bosonic Gaussian channels. Finally, we develop hybrid quantum-classical algorithms for the regularized SDP using only Hamiltonian simulation, Hadamard tests, and classical sampling, and bound their runtime in closed form. Unlike existing quantum SDP solvers, whose runtimes scale polynomially with an a priori upper bound on the primal trace, our framework operates directly on the unbounded cone, replacing this bound with a dependence on the spectral structure of the dual slack operator.

Conserving relaxation-time approximation for electron-electron collisions

Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26207v1

Summary. This paper introduces a new 'conserving' relaxation-time approximation that preserves particle number, energy, and momentum in kinetic equations. It provides a highly accurate and simple framework for calculating transport properties in Fermi liquids beyond the standard semiclassical limits.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The standard relaxation-time approximation (RTA) fails to enforce fundamental conservation laws (particle number, energy, and momentum) and is often limited to the Fermi surface projection approximation, making it inaccurate for transport quantities sensitive to energy-dependent quasiparticle distributions.

Main result. The authors developed a conserving relaxation-time approximation (cRTA) that explicitly enforces conservation laws via an energy-resolved projection, achieving quantitative agreement (within 1-6%) with exact solutions for DC, longitudinal, and optical conductivity across various regimes.

Method. The cRTA uses an algebraic construction of the collision integral and an energy-resolved projection onto a hydrodynamic space of collision invariants, utilizing Wigner transforms and Moyal products to handle frequency-dependent driving terms.

Model / system. The framework is applied to single-band electron systems with arbitrary dispersion, including Galilean-invariant and non-Galilean-invariant Fermi liquids, such as 2D Dirac and 3D Weyl fermions, with both clean and disordered limits.

Key observables. DC conductivity, longitudinal conductivity, thermal conductivity, and optical conductivity.

Important parameters / regimes. Scattering times (ee and impurity), frequency (Omega), wavevector (q), temperature (T), and frequency-to-temperature ratio (Omega/T).

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a quasiparticle approximation, linear response to electric fields, and an energy-independent electron-electron scattering rate (neglecting logarithmic corrections in 2D).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the relative deviation between cRTA and exact results for different parameters and an inset displaying the temperature-dependent part of the exact solution.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the cRTA framework, details its mathematical construction and enforcement of conservation laws, benchmarks the method against exact results in various transport regimes (hydrodynamic, collisionless, viscoelastic), and discusses its application to specific systems like Weyl fermions.

Abstract

We develop a conserving relaxation-time approximation (cRTA), which explicitly enforces conservation of particle number, energy, and momentum and employs an energy-resolved projection onto the full space of collision invariants. This makes the cRTA applicable to transport quantities sensitive to the energy dependence of the nonequilibrium quasiparticle distribution near the Fermi surface. We benchmark the cRTA against several exact and asymptotically exact results. In particular, it reproduces the low- and high-temperature limits of the dc conductivity of a non-Galilean-invariant Fermi liquid (FL) with disorder, the hydrodynamic and collisionless limits of the finite wavevector longitudinal conductivity of a clean Galilean-invariant FL, and the asymptotic scaling forms of the optical conductivity of a clean non-Galilean-invariant FL beyond the semiclassical limit. For several observables, the agreement with exact solutions is quantitative at the percent level. These results demonstrate that the cRTA provides a simple and accurate framework for transport beyond the Fermi surface projection approximation.

Summary. This paper extends the theoretical description of a unitary Fermi gas from spherical to cylindrical trap geometries. It demonstrates how the interplay between longitudinal and transverse confinement modifies the dynamic structure factor. These results are crucial for accurately interpreting experimental measurements of density fluctuations in trapped quantum gases.

Why it may be interesting. It provides essential corrections for interpreting Bragg spectroscopy data in ultracold atom experiments, specifically regarding how trap anisotropy breaks isotropy and modifies the phonon dispersion relation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of interpreting experimental spectra in trapped unitary Fermi gases, specifically how cylindrical trap geometries modify the dynamic structure factor and phonon dispersion relation.

Main result. The study derives how cylindrical confinement modifies the dynamic structure factor, finding that longitudinal confinement contributes a convex correction while transverse confinement contributes a concave correction, with the net effect being convex for typical experimental parameters.

Method. The authors use an Effective Field Theory (EFT) framework based on superfluid hydrodynamics, employing a Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) expansion and a hierarchy of scales approach to analyze different energy regimes.

Model / system. A unitary Fermi gas (a strongly coupled, nonrelativistic Conformal Field Theory) realized using ultracold 6Li atoms, confined in a cylindrical geometry with power-law radial potentials and axial light sheets.

Key observables. Dynamic structure factor S(q, omega), phonon dispersion relation, and the energy spectrum.

Important parameters / regimes. Bertsch parameter (xi), speed of sound (cs), dispersion parameter (gamma), aspect ratio (epsilon), and low-energy constants (c0, c1, c2).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated in the zero-temperature limit, the radial potential is assumed to approximate a hard-wall box, and the Bragg pulse is assumed to be in the linear response regime.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the condensate in cylindrical geometry; Figure 2 shows the energy spectrum and the accuracy of the WKB fit using numerical diagonalization.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of trap-induced distortions, extends a spherical EFT framework to cylindrical geometry, identifies three physical energy regimes, derives the modified dynamic structure factor, and compares analytical WKB results with numerical diagonalization.

Abstract

The unitary Fermi gas serves as a tunable realization of a strongly coupled CFT, making it a powerful system for probing universal quantum many-body phenomena. Precise measurement of its properties remains experimentally challenging: finite-temperature effects and spatial inhomogeneity introduced by external trapping potentials can significantly distort observables. Cylindrical trap geometries are commonly used in experiments. This note extends an existing theoretical framework to this geometry, deriving how the cylindrical confinement modifies the dynamic structure factor at zero temperature. These results provide a necessary correction for the interpretation of experimental spectra in trapped unitary gases.

Summary. This paper presents a GPU-accelerated version of the Finite-Temperature Lanczos Method designed for workstation hardware. By using a matrix-free approach and optimized indexing, it allows for much larger spin cluster simulations than traditional methods. The study demonstrates that single-precision GPU arithmetic is sufficient for calculating thermodynamic observables accurately.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a highly efficient, accessible tool for simulating the thermodynamic properties of large-scale quantum many-body systems using standard workstation hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The exponential growth of memory requirements for storing the Hamiltonian in sparse format prevents large-scale finite-temperature calculations on workstation-grade GPUs.

Main result. The authors developed a matrix-free GPU implementation of the Finite-Temperature Lanczos Method (FTLM) that achieves 10x speedups over CPUs and enables Hilbert space dimensions of ~10^8 using single-precision arithmetic without sacrificing physical accuracy.

Method. A matrix-free, row-wise gather formulation using two state-to-index strategies: a Compressed Lookup Table (CLT) and a Combinatorial-Ranking (CR) scheme, implemented in MATLAB/CUDA.

Model / system. Heisenberg spin Hamiltonians for exchange-coupled spin clusters and magnetic solids, specifically applied to icosahedron and icosidodecahedron geometries with varying spin magnitudes (s = 1/2 to 2).

Key observables. Partition function (Z), magnetic susceptibility (chi), heat capacity (C), and magnetization.

Important parameters / regimes. Hilbert space dimension ~10^8, spin magnitude (s), total magnetization sector (M), and single-precision (FP32) vs. double-precision (FP64) arithmetic.

Assumptions / limitations. The implementation is optimized for workstation GPUs rather than distributed clusters and currently relies on S_z symmetry.

Figures summary. Figure 4 and 5 show the distribution of Ritz eigenvalues and the impact of ghost eigenvalues in FP32 vs FP64; Table 3 compares wall-clock time scaling between CPU and GPU kernels.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational bottleneck of Hamiltonian storage, presents the matrix-free GPU implementation and its indexing strategies (CLT and CR), evaluates numerical precision and error scales, and concludes with performance benchmarks and scaling studies.

Abstract

We present a GPU implementation of the finite-temperature Lanczos method (FTLM) for Heisenberg spin Hamiltonians that targets workstation hardware rather than distributed-memory clusters. The Hamiltonian action is evaluated matrix-free in a row-wise gather formulation. We introduce and compare two state-to-index strategies: a compressed lookup table (CLT), which reduces lookup memory by a factor of 16 relative to a full table while retaining a fixed, branch-light access pattern, and a GPU-adapted combinatorial-ranking scheme that removes the lookup table altogether. Numerical tests against FP64 CPU references show that FP32 GPU arithmetic changes heat capacities and magnetic susceptibilities by amounts several orders of magnitude below the stochastic uncertainty of the FTLM trace estimator at typical sample sizes. Benchmarks show speedups of up to about one order of magnitude over optimized multicore CPU calculations and enable Hilbert-space sectors of dimension ~10^8 on a single workstation GPU. The MATLAB/CUDA implementation, including example input files and benchmark scripts, is openly available at https://github.com/ghasdeke/ftlm-gpu (archived at DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.20378647) under the Apache-2.0 license.

Nonasymptotic bounds for quantum purity amplification

Authors: Thilo Scharnhorst, Jack Spilecki, John Wright
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27262v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides new, nonasymptotic bounds for quantum purity amplification that hold for finite numbers of noisy quantum state copies. Unlike previous works that only describe the infinite-sample limit, this result shows how the required number of samples scales with the desired fidelity and the spectral gap of the input state. The derivation uses advanced combinatorial tools like random Young diagrams and the RSK correspondence.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for quantum information processing and open quantum systems, as it provides rigorous, finite-sample guarantees for state purification protocols which are essential for practical quantum error mitigation and state preparation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the problem of quantum purity amplification (QPA), specifically seeking to establish nonasymptotic bounds on the number of noisy state copies required to prepare a high-fidelity target pure state.

Main result. The authors establish a nonasymptotic guarantee that n copies suffice to achieve fidelity 1-delta if n scales with k, delta, and the spectral gap between the two largest eigenvalues, independent of the dimension d.

Method. The proof utilizes the combinatorics of random Young diagrams, the Robinson-Schensted-Knuth (RSK) correspondence, and the representation theory of the symmetric and unitary groups (Schur-Weyl duality).

Model / system. The model involves n copies of a d-dimensional noisy density matrix rho, where the task is to transform these copies into k copies of the principal eigenstate.

Key observables. Output fidelity relative to the target state, sample complexity (n), and the spectral gap (p_d - p_{d-1}).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of input copies (n), number of output copies (k), error parameter (delta), and the eigenvalues of the input state (p_i).

Assumptions / limitations. The principal eigenstate is assumed to be unique (p_{d-1} < p_d), and the bounds are derived for arbitrary spectra.

Figures summary. Figure 2 illustrates the concept of 'overhang' in a Young diagram by showing how removing boxes labeled d affects the diagram structure.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QPA problem and the gap in existing asymptotic literature, defines the QPA channel using representation theory, develops a combinatorial fidelity formula using RSK correspondence, and concludes with the derivation of the nonasymptotic complexity bound.

Abstract

In quantum purity amplification, one is given $n$ copies of a noisy quantum state $ρ\in \mathbb{C}^{d \times d}$ and asked to prepare $k$ copies of its principal eigenstate $|v_d\rangle$. Several prior works have derived information-theoretically optimal algorithms for this problem, but the bounds they prove are only shown in the asymptotic regime as the number of samples $n$ tends to infinity. In this paper, we establish the following nonasymptotic guarantee: if $ρ$'s eigenvalues are sorted $p_1 \leq \cdots \leq p_d$ and $p_{d-1} < p_d$, then \begin{equation} n = O\Big(k + \frac{k}δ \cdot \frac{1-p_d}{(p_d-p_{d-1})^2}\Big) \end{equation} copies suffice to output a state with fidelity at least $1-δ$ with $|v_d^{\otimes k}\rangle$. Our bound holds for arbitrary spectra, and is independent of the dimension $d$. In the case of depolarizing noise, our finite-sample guarantee matches the optimal asymptotic scaling. Our proof is based on the combinatorics of random Young diagrams.

Our results reveal quantum advantages (more precisely, $Q/C\ge3/2$) for the above utility functions when the protected private data are $n$-ary with $n\ge3$.

Trade-off Relation for Black Hole Entropy Fluctuations

Summary. This paper establishes a fundamental limit on the precision with which a black hole can record quantum information, showing that entropy fluctuations are unavoidable. It proves that the relative variance of the entropy change is inversely proportional to the number of infalling photons. This result links the stochastic nature of semiclassical gravity to the information-carrying capacity of quantum fields.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether black hole horizons can record quantum information with arbitrarily small entropy fluctuations and seeks to establish a fundamental trade-off relation between entropy variance and the amount of infalling quantum information.

Method. The study utilizes stochastic semiclassical gravity and the Einstein-Langevin equations, employing the Magnus expansion, the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, and the gravitational phase space method to analyze fluctuations in the energy-momentum tensor.

Model / system. The system consists of an asymptotically flat, stationary Schwarzschild black hole interacting with infalling quantum matter fields (photons) within the framework of the Danielson-Satishchandran-Wald decoherence mechanism.

Important parameters / regimes. Planck length (l_P), Schwarzschild radius (r_h), effective dipole moment (ql), and the number of entangling photons.

Assumptions / limitations. The field state is assumed to be a quasifree Hadamard state, and the spacetime is assumed to be stationary and asymptotically flat.

Main result. A reinforcement learning-based residual policy achieves a 36% reduction in total shots compared to uniform allocation while maintaining high solution reliability and generalizing to unseen problem sizes.

Method. A two-pronged approach using a hand-crafted heuristic baseline and a tabular Double Q-learning agent that learns a residual correction to the baseline under a Lagrangian-constrained objective.

Model / system. The study focuses on the weighted Max-Cut problem on d-regular graphs, modeled via an Ising Hamiltonian, using a depth-1 Recursive QAOA (RQAOA) framework.

Key observables. Pairwise correlations (expectation values of Pauli-Z operators), success rate (approximation ratio >= 0.99), and effective shots per success (ESP).

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth p=1, target success rate p*=0.95, and the 4D state descriptor including variable count, z-gap, conflict ratio, and graph distance.

Assumptions / limitations. The study is restricted to the noiseless regime and a fixed circuit depth (p=1) to isolate recursion-level effects from hardware noise or deeper circuit complexities.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the RQAOA pipeline and the RL loop; Figure 3 shows normalized shot usage vs. success rate; Figure 4 shows mean ESP ratio by problem size; Figure 5 shows training diagnostics for the Lagrangian multiplier; Figure 6 compares allocation patterns between different controllers.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the RQAOA bottleneck, proposes a reinforcement learning framework for adaptive shot allocation, details the MDP formulation and Double Q-learning implementation, presents performance evaluations across various graph sizes and complexity levels, and discusses the robustness and limitations of the learned policy.

Abstract

Recursive QAOA (RQAOA) solves combinatorial optimization problems by using shallow quantum circuits to estimate pairwise correlations and recursively eliminate variables until a classical solver can handle the residual instance. Each elimination step requires measurement shots, and the total shot cost grows with the number of recursive stages. On near-term quantum devices, increasing shot counts can translate directly into greater exposure to hardware-level noise sources such as readout errors and decoherence, making shot-efficient execution not merely a cost-reduction measure but a factor with direct implications for solution reliability. While shot reduction has been studied broadly across NISQ algorithms, step-wise measurement control inside the recursive loop of RQAOA has received little attention. We formulate this step-wise allocation as a sequential decision problem and propose two strategies for depth-1 RQAOA on weighted Max-Cut instances. A hand-crafted heuristic assigns shots based on local indicators of step difficulty, and a tabular Double Q-learning agent learns a residual policy that adjusts this baseline under a Lagrangian-constrained objective. Both methods are evaluated under a fixed-cap fairness protocol that equalizes the per-step budget across all strategies, and the elimination rule itself is kept unchanged so that the contribution of adaptive measurement control can be isolated. On a diverse set of weighted graph instances spanning a range of sizes and structures, the heuristic reduces total shots by approximately 23% relative to uniform allocation, and the RL policy achieves a 36% reduction with a lower effective shots per success ratio than both baselines. The improvement persists on problem sizes not seen during training, suggesting that reinforcement learning can discover efficient, instance-adaptive measurement strategies in recursive quantum optimization.
