Statistical and Algorithmic Foundations of Probing Quantum Systems with Compressive Measurements: A Review

Authors: Zhen Qin, Michael B. Wakin, Zhihui Zhu
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27191v1

Summary. This review provides a unified perspective on performing quantum state tomography on large-scale systems by exploiting structural priors. It connects the theory of measurement design and optimization algorithms to the field of compressed sensing, showing how to reduce the complexity of state reconstruction from exponential to polynomial scaling.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a comprehensive theoretical bridge between signal processing (compressive sensing) and quantum information, offering scalable strategies for characterizing many-body dynamics and large-scale quantum devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The exponential scaling of Quantum State Tomography (QST) makes reconstructing general quantum states computationally and statistically impossible for large systems, necessitating methods that exploit inherent state structures.

Main result. The review establishes a unified framework for QST by connecting compact state representations, measurement design, and reconstruction algorithms, demonstrating that QST can be treated as a structured inverse problem.

Method. The paper surveys various approaches including compressive sensing, randomized measurements (Haar-random, t-designs, and classical shadows), and optimization-based reconstruction (convex relaxations and non-convex methods).

Model / system. The review focuses on large-scale quantum processors, programmable simulators, and error-corrected computers, specifically targeting structured states such as low-rank, tensor-network (MPS, MPO, PEPO), and neural quantum states.

Key observables. Fidelities, expectation values of Pauli observables, trace distance, and entanglement measures.

Important parameters / regimes. Hilbert-space dimension (d), number of measurement shots (M), number of POVMs (Q), and intrinsic dimension/complexity of the state.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the existence of underlying structure (e.g., low-rankness or tensor-network compressibility) and focuses on the statistical error from finite-shot measurements rather than hardware-specific noise.

Paper structure. The paper is organized around three interconnected themes: compact state representations, measurement design, and computational algorithms, framing QST as a structured inverse problem.

Abstract

Quantum state tomography (QST) is a fundamental task in quantum information science that aims to reconstruct unknown quantum states from measurement data. However, the exponential growth of Hilbert-space dimension with system size makes full tomography of general quantum states statistically and computationally prohibitive. This challenge has motivated extensive research on structured quantum state tomography, where prior structure, such as low-rankness, tensor-network representations, shallow quantum circuits, and neural quantum states, can substantially reduce the effective degrees of freedom and enable scalable recovery. In this review, we provide a unified perspective on QST for structured quantum states through three closely related themes: compact state representations, measurement design, and computational algorithms. After reviewing common models for structured quantum states, we survey existing work on geometric preservation properties of measurement frameworks, ranging from informationally complete POVMs to randomized measurements, and their implications for sample complexity. On the algorithmic side, we review optimization methods for reconstructing structured quantum states from empirical measurements. By connecting QST with broader principles from compressive sensing, matrix sensing, and structured inverse problems, this survey highlights common theoretical foundations underlying sample complexity, measurement efficiency, and scalable recovery.

Summary. Researchers have successfully integrated two independently tunable squeezed-light sources on a single thin-film lithium niobate chip. Abstract

Scalable generation of nonclassical light sources on an integrated platform is a key requirement for photonic quantum information processing. In particular, realizing multiple indistinguishable squeezed light sources on a single chip is an essential step toward continuous-variable quantum computing. Here, we demonstrate the fabrication of two indistinguishable and independently controllable optical parametric oscillators on a thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN) platform. The device design focuses on reproducibility, independent tunability, and compatibility with larger telecom-wavelength continuous-variable photonic circuits. We observe up to 0.5 dB of directly measured squeezing below the shot-noise level from each source. By interfering the two modes on a beam splitter, we generate an EPR-type two-mode squeezed state and verify continuous-variable entanglement through violation of the Duan-Simon inseparability criterion. This is the first demonstration of two independently tunable squeezed-light sources on a single TFLN chip and their use for generating continuous-variable entanglement.

Practical Tests and Witnesses of Fermionic non-Gaussianity

Summary. This paper presents new, efficient protocols for detecting and quantifying non-Gaussianity in fermionic systems using both single-copy and two-copy measurement schemes. It introduces a noise-robust witness for mixed states and demonstrates the practical utility of these tools on a real quantum processor. The work establishes a fundamental link between non-Gaussianity and the complexity of generating pseudorandom fermionic states.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and quantum computing as it provides tools to certify non-trivial correlations and 'magic' resources in fermionic systems, which are harder to detect than standard entanglement or coherence.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing scalable and practical methods to detect, witness, and quantify fermionic non-Gaussianity, which is essential for benchmarking quantum devices and certifying fermionic magic resources.

Main result. The authors introduce efficient two-copy Bell measurement and single-copy protocols for estimating fermionic antiflatness (FAF), demonstrating improved scaling in qubit number and error tolerance, and showing that noise can both reduce and enhance non-Gaussianity.

Method. The study utilizes two-copy Bell measurements on pairs of states and a single-copy scheme based on commuting Majorana bilinear matchings, alongside a new purity-corrected FAF witness for mixed states.

Model / system. The system consists of n fermionic modes described by 2n Majorana operators, mapped to n qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation, and tested on the IQM Garnet quantum computer.

Key observables. Fermionic Antiflatness (FAF1), Gaussian fidelity (F_AF), purity-corrected FAF witness (W_FAF), and quadratic Fock-basis coherence (C2).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (n), distance to the Gaussian set (epsilon_G), noise parameter (p), and non-Gaussian gate angle (theta).

Assumptions / limitations. The single-copy protocol assumes the ability to implement Clifford circuits and Pauli measurements; the two-copy protocol assumes access to two copies of the quantum state.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the two estimation protocols; Figure 2 details the Bell-measurement readout; Figure 3 shows experimental results on IQM Garnet; Figure 4 illustrates the evolution of the witness under various noise models; Figure 5 depicts the three-copy convolution test.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-Gaussianity detection, develops new two-copy and single-copy estimation protocols, provides complexity analysis, introduces a robust witness for mixed states, validates results on a quantum computer, and explores the link to pseudorandomness and gate complexity.

Abstract

Detecting when a quantum state leaves the efficiently simulable fermionic Gaussian regime is a central task for benchmarking quantum devices and certifying fermionic magic resources. We develop practical tests and witnesses based on fermionic antiflatness (FAF), a covariance-matrix-based measure of non-Gaussianity. For $n$-qubit states, we estimate FAF using two complementary protocols: two-copy Bell measurements and a single-copy scheme based on commuting matchings of Majorana bilinears. These yield testers that distinguish pure Gaussian states from states $ε$-far from the Gaussian set, using $O(n^2/ε^2)$ two-copy Bell measurements or $O(n^3/ε^4)$ single-copy measurements, improving the state of the art in the dependence on both $n$ and $ε$. For mixed states, we introduce a purity-corrected FAF witness that certifies non-Gaussianity and is highly robust to noise. With our witness, we demonstrate on the IQM quantum computer that noise can both reduce and enhance non-Gaussianity. Finally, by examining pseudo non-Gaussianity, we show that the cryptographic task of pseudorandom-state generation requires extensive fermionic non-Gaussianity. Together, these results provide experimentally accessible tools for detecting, witnessing, and quantifying non-Gaussian fermionic resources.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of dipolar decoherence, defines the Hamiltonian and control framework, presents the ZENITH sequence construction using group theory, validates the protocol via numerical simulations, and demonstrates its utility for DC magnetometry.

Abstract

Dipolar interactions are a major source of decoherence in dense ensembles of color centers in diamond. Current protocols demand using bias magnetic fields detrimental in many scenarios. We present ZENITH (Zero-field Ensemble Neutralization via Interleaved Trilevel Handling), a pulsed sequence to cancel dipolar interactions among V-degenerate spin-1 systems. We reveal excellent coherence survival, compatibility with existing sensing sequences, and improved DC detection, advancing a general framework to control interacting degenerate multilevel systems, of broad interest in quantum technologies.

Single-Ensemble Multiparameter Squeezing with Qudits

Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26377v1

Summary. The paper shows that using qudits instead of qubits allows a single ensemble to perform simultaneous multi-parameter sensing. It establishes a mathematical framework for this effect and demonstrates significant metrological gain in a simulated trapped-ion system.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new route to multiparameter metrology by expanding the local Hilbert space, offering a distinct advantage over distributed architectures in the fixed-sensor-budget regime, which is highly relevant for quantum sensing and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving simultaneous multiparameter sensing (e.g., vector magnetic field detection) within a single collective ensemble, which is typically limited to single-parameter metrology in qubit-based systems.

Main result. By promoting local sensors from qubits to qudits, the authors demonstrate that a single ensemble can support multiparameter squeezing, achieving up to 12 dB enhancement in a trapped-ion qutrit chain.

Method. A general framework based on the single-site Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM) to determine optimal probe states and readout observables, combined with Discrete Truncated Wigner Approximation (DTWA) for numerical simulations.

Model / system. A single ensemble of N identical d-level qudit sensors, specifically implemented as a trapped-ion qutrit chain with power-law interactions and a collective twisting-like interacting Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Multiparameter squeezing parameter (xi^2), collective spin operators (S_alpha), Symmetric Logarithmic Derivatives (SLD), and transduction-rate operator matrix (G_alpha_beta).

Important parameters / regimes. Local dimension (d), number of sensors (N), power-law interaction exponent (gamma), and the Standard Quantum Limit (SQL).

Assumptions / limitations. The ensemble is initially in a direct product state; the optimization assumes the ability to implement optimal SLD measurements; the framework is subject to the Matsumoto weak-commutativity theorem.

Figures summary. Figure 1(a) provides a schematic of qudits and their phase space using SU(d) generators, illustrating how multiple independent response channels can coexist.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the limitations of qubit-based sensing, proposes a qudit-based solution, develops a mathematical framework using QFIM, demonstrates a concrete qutrit implementation, and provides numerical evidence using a trapped-ion model.

Abstract

Quantum-enhanced multiparameter sensing is often associated with distributed architectures or 2-anticoherent states, whereas squeezing in a single collective ensemble is typically limited to single-parameter metrology. Here, we show that a single ensemble can support simultaneous multiparameter squeezing when each sensor is promoted from a qubit to a qudit (i.e., spin with $d$ energy levels). We develop a general framework in which the optimal product probe state, the corresponding global readout observables, and the associated squeezing parameters are all determined from the single-site quantum Fisher information matrix. We then present a minimal qudit construction for two-parameter vector magnetic field sensing with local dimension $d=3$. We further identify a collective twisting-like interacting Hamiltonian that generates such multiparameter-squeezed states and numerically demonstrate scalable metrological gain. In particular, for a trapped-ion qutrit chain with power-law interactions, we obtain up to 12 dB enhancement in two-parameter sensing for $N=256$ sensors. Our results establish qudit-enabled multiparameter squeezing in a single ensemble as a distinct route to multiparameter quantum metrology with global readout, and highlight its potential advantage over distributed multi-ensemble strategies in the fixed-sensor-budget regime.

Summary. This paper presents an analytical model and a practical algorithm to synchronize clocks in quantum key distribution without needing extra communication channels. Abstract

Clock synchronization is critical for maintaining low error rates in quantum key distribution. Here, we describe how a frequency mismatch between the transmitter and receiver clocks affects the quantum bit error rate in quantum key distribution, and derive from this model a simple synchronization algorithm together with clock stability requirements for practical operation. Our algorithm continuously compensates for both frequency mismatch and time-offset fluctuations directly from detection timestamps. It does not require a dedicated synchronization channel or auxiliary qubit sequence, converges from a large frequency mismatch within approximately one second of photon acquisition, and remains effective in low-photon-count regimes (more than 30 dB of channel loss) using standard hardware. We validate our approach by demonstrating successful key exchange over 100 km of fiber and continuous operation over 24 hours in a 16 km metropolitan network using commercial systems, with performance equivalent to using a service channel for clock synchronization.

Summary. The study demonstrates how the non-coplanar geometry of interlocked quantum rings can protect Bell-state entanglement from Coulomb-driven decay. However, it also establishes a fundamental limit where high-order orbital transitions eventually trigger entanglement collapse. This defines the necessary conditions for achieving robust topological protection in semiconductor-based quantum architectures.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a theoretical framework for using geometric topology and spatial symmetry to protect entanglement against many-body interactions, which is highly relevant for designing robust qubits in mesoscopic or synthetic platforms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the stability of bipartite entanglement (specifically Bell states) in Hopf-linked quantum rings against interaction-driven localization and parity mixing.

Main result. Identified a geometric protection mechanism via non-coplanar tilt that isolates the positive-parity Bell state, but discovered a critical interaction threshold (lambda_crit) beyond which higher-order transitions cause irreversible entanglement collapse.

Method. Exact diagonalization of the unexpanded 3D Coulomb interaction combined with a higher-order Schrieffer-Wolff transformation and symmetry analysis using a magnetic point group.

Model / system. Two interacting electrons confined to spatially separated, non-coplanar 1D quantum rings forming a Hopf-linked topology, subject to a local Rashba spin-orbit field and 3D Coulomb repulsion.

Key observables. Concurrence (C), exchange gap (Delta E+), and the interaction strength parameter (lambda).

Important parameters / regimes. Geometric tilt angle (alpha), interlocking parameter (beta), interaction ratio (lambda), and the dimensionless bounding constant (u_cross).

Assumptions / limitations. Negligible intra-band energy splittings compared to kinetic separation; use of the Heitler-London approximation for wavefunction overlap; assumes a hard-wall vacuum barrier to suppress direct tunneling.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the Hopf-linked geometry and parameters; Fig 2 illustrates the Coulomb landscape; Fig 4 shows 1D parameter slices of concurrence demonstrating entanglement protection vs. decay.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Hopf-linked system, defines the Hamiltonian and geometric parameters, proves the spatial symmetry for protection, applies Schrieffer-Wolff to find the collapse threshold, and discusses implementation constraints in semiconductor architectures.

Abstract

We determine the bipartite entanglement bounds of two interacting electrons in deeply interlocked Hopf-linked quantum rings via exact diagonalization of the unexpanded 3D Coulomb interaction. This identifies an exact continuous spatial symmetry that geometrically isolates the positive-parity Bell state, preventing classical interaction-driven localization. A non-coplanar geometric tilt ($α> 0$) is essential to lift the exchange degeneracy and maintain this maximally entangled manifold as a state of frozen entanglement. However, a higher-order Schrieffer-Wolff transformation demonstrates this geometric protection is fundamentally bounded; uncancelled inter-orbital momentum transitions inevitably induce dynamical parity mixing. This defines a critical interaction threshold ($λ_{crit}$) for irreversible entanglement collapse. Our analysis shows that the resulting bounding conditions reveal scaling limitations in mesoscopic semiconductor architectures, dictating the necessity of synthetic macroscopic platforms to achieve robust topological protection.

Abstract

We develop a Schwinger--Keldysh effective theory for quantum-interference corrections in a two-dimensional electron system in the hydrodynamic regime. Starting from the clean hydrodynamic fixed point, we introduce a minimal random-friction disorder model that generates a finite momentum-relaxation time within the self-consistent Born approximation. The disorder-averaged theory then allows us to construct a hydrodynamic Cooperon and to compute the associated self-energy corrections to the collective modes. Conservation laws protect the density and momentum sectors, so that the leading quantum-coherence correction is forced into the spin-two stress sector. The associated stress self-energy renormalizes the shear viscosity and modifies both the Gurzhi response and its low-field magnetohydrodynamic signatures.

Kubo-Ando Means and Rigidity of Quantum Positivity Cones

Authors: Mohsen Kian
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26272v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that only standard convex mixing (arithmetic means) preserves the essential entanglement-related properties of quantum states and channels. It shows that any other type of Kubo-Ando averaging can destroy separability or increase the complexity of entanglement (Schmidt number).

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum information, as it establishes fundamental limits on how non-linear averaging processes can affect entanglement and the structural integrity of quantum channels.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the stability and rigidity of quantum positivity cones (separable, PPT, and Schmidt-number cones) under the action of nonlinear Kubo-Ando operator means.

Main result. The authors prove that weighted arithmetic means are the only Kubo-Ando means that preserve the separable cone and the class of entanglement-breaking channels; all other means can increase Schmidt numbers or violate the PPT condition.

Method. The study uses curvature analysis of operator monotone functions, Taylor expansions, and the construction of explicit two-qubit counterexamples, extended to higher dimensions via canonical isometries and the Choi-Jamiołkowski correspondence.

Model / system. The mathematical framework involves bipartite tensor product spaces (C^m tensor C^n) and the study of operator means acting on positive operators and quantum channels.

Key observables. Schmidt number, PPT (positive partial transpose) property, and separability.

Important parameters / regimes. The curvature of the operator monotone function (kappa_sigma), where kappa_sigma = 0 corresponds to arithmetic means and kappa_sigma > 0 corresponds to non-arithmetic means.

Assumptions / limitations. The results specifically address the behavior of non-arithmetic Kubo-Ando means and assume the use of the Choi-Jamiołkowski correspondence to relate operators to maps.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the curvature of Kubo-Ando means, moves to proving rigidity theorems for separable and PPT cones using two-qubit counterexamples, extends these results to Schmidt-number cones, and concludes by translating these findings to the theory of quantum channels.

Abstract

We investigate the stability of quantum positivity cones under nonlinear operator means. Specifically, we examine how Kubo--Ando means interact with the separable, positive partial transpose (PPT), and Schmidt-number cones. By analyzing the curvature of operator monotone functions at the identity, we give a strict rigidity phenomenon: weighted arithmetic means are the only Kubo--Ando means that preserve the separable cone in all dimensions. We show that the strictly positive curvature of any non-arithmetic mean explicitly forces a violation of the PPT condition, even in the foundational two-qubit setting, and can strictly increase the Schmidt number of the resulting operator. Finally, using the Choi--Jamiołkowski correspondence, we translate these geometric obstructions to the map-theoretic setting, concluding that convex mixing is the uniquely permissible Kubo--Ando operation for preserving entanglement-breaking quantum channels.

Abstract

Narrow-linewidth lasers with absolute frequency anchoring are essential for precision metrology, coherent sensing, and emerging quantum technologies beyond laboratory environments. Optical cavities and interferometers provide exceptional short-term spectral purity but lack intrinsic absolute frequency references. Atomic transitions, in contrast, provide stable frequency anchors but offer limited discrimination sensitivity. Recent hybrid approaches have demonstrated the combination of compact optical resonators with atomic references, yet achieving the Hz-level regime remains challenging. Here, we present a hybrid architecture that enables simultaneous realization of Hz-level linewidth and atomic-referenced frequency stability. An external-cavity diode laser is first stabilized to a fiber interferometer to achieve Hz-level spectral purity, while the interferometer is subsequently anchored to an 87Rb D2 transition via modulation transfer spectroscopy to suppress long-term drift and define the laser frequency relative to the atomic transition. This dual-stabilization scheme realizes a compact atomic-referenced laser with a 3.4-Hz linewidth (1-rad integrated-phase method), a minimum fractional frequency stability of 3.4x10-14 at 0.56 s, and 9x10-13 at 100 s. This architecture establishes a practical and scalable route toward compact and field-deployable atomic-referenced narrow-linewidth lasers for precision metrology and quantum technologies.

Quantum Resistance Paradox of Low-Dimensional Superfluids

Summary. The researchers used ultracold Fermi gases to observe a paradox where increasing the width of a superfluid channel actually increases resistance during the transition from 1D to 2D. They demonstrate that this occurs because the system moves from a regime of phase slips to one of vortex motion, with a minimum in dissipation occurring where both are suppressed. This work provides a benchmark for understanding dimensional crossovers in both quantum gases and superconductors.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a clean, tunable platform to study the fundamental transition between different topological dissipation mechanisms (phase slips vs. vortices) without the confounding effects of disorder found in solid-state systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how resistance and dissipation evolve during the dimensional crossover from 1D to 2D in low-dimensional superfluids.

Main result. Discovery of a 'quantum resistance paradox' where widening the transport channel increases resistance in the 1D-to-2D crossover, caused by a transition between phase-slip and vortex-driven dissipation.

Method. Using a defect-free, programmable transport geometry of ultracold Fermi gases to measure atom-pair imbalance relaxation and underdamped oscillations.

Model / system. A transport system of two superfluid reservoirs of a unitary Fermi gas of 6Li atoms connected by a programmable channel of varying width, modeled as a Topological Nonlinear Resistor-Inductor-Capacitor (TNRLC) circuit.

Key observables. Atom-pair imbalance (delta N/N), atom transport traces, oscillation frequency, oscillation amplitude, and current (I).

Important parameters / regimes. Channel width (w), temperature (T/TF), coherence length (xi), and the 1D-to-2D crossover regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The gas is in the unitary limit and local thermodynamic equilibrium; compressibility and temperature are assumed identical for both hyperfine states.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup and dissipation mechanisms; Figure 2 illustrates the breakdown of scaling laws (LAMH and vortex models) in the crossover regime; Figure I shows inductance scaling with channel width.

Paper structure. Introduction of the resistance paradox and dimensional crossover; description of the experimental platform using 6Li atoms; presentation of the TNRLC circuit model; analysis of the 1D phase-slip regime; analysis of the 2D vortex regime; discussion of the crossover and the resistance minimum; conclusion on implications for superconducting devices.

Abstract

Resistance in standard conductors decreases with increasing cross-section. Yet, in low-dimensional superconductors and superfluids residual resistance arises from topological fluctuations of the order parameter manifesting as phase slips in one-dimensional (1D) and vortices in two-dimensional (2D) systems. How resistance and dissipation evolve as geometry interpolates between these regimes remains an open question. This evolution is masked in solid-state experiments by disorder, impurities, and geometric imperfections, and poses theoretical challenges due to competing dissipative processes and pronounced finite-size effects. Here, we use a defect-free unitary Fermi gas in a digitally programmable transport geometry to isolate geometric effects on superfluid dissipation and discover a paradox: in the crossover from 1D to 2D, the resistance reaches a minimum. There, widening a channel increases its resistance. Narrower, quasi-1D channels show dissipation described by Langer-Ambegaokar-McCumber-Halperin theory of phase slips. In this regime, varying the channel width yields the predicted exponential scaling of the activation factor over more than ten orders of magnitude. Wider, quasi-2D channels show dissipation consistent with a finite-size vortex model. The minimal dissipation in the dimensional crossover reflects a transition in the dominant dissipative mechanism, with both phase slips and vortices simultaneously suppressed. Our measurements suggest a route to minimizing dissipation in superconducting devices and provide a benchmark for theoretical efforts aimed at describing the dimensional crossover.
