Programming nanomechanical computation with light

Authors: Xiaofei Guo, Jonne Drost, Fons van der Laan, Jesse J. Slim, Marc Serra-Garcia, Ewold Verhagen
Type: experiment · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26319v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 3/5 · quantum optics experiment 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. The researchers have created a programmable computing platform using nanomechanical resonators where the logic functions and connections are controlled by light. By tuning lasers, they can switch between different Boolean gates and even connect different mechanical modes. This work shows that optomechanical nonlinearities can be harnessed to create reconfigurable, hardware-agnostic physical computers.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a way to use classical nonlinear dynamics and optomechanical backaction to implement programmable logic, which provides a bridge to understanding how controlled nonlinearities can be used for more complex tasks like reservoir computing or probabilistic computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a programmable physical computing platform where nonlinearity and connectivity can be reconfigured in situ, rather than being fixed by material properties.

Main result. Demonstration of all six fundamental Boolean logic gates (AND, NAND, OR, NOR, XOR, XNOR) and programmable connectivity between mechanical modes using laser-controlled optomechanical interactions.

Method. Using cavity optomechanical backaction to induce Duffing nonlinearity and an optical spring effect, controlled via laser wavelength, power, and phase, with readout via intensity modulation detection.

Model / system. A pair of silicon nanobeams acting as mechanical oscillators coupled to a photonic-crystal nanocavity, modeled as a Duffing oscillator with radiation-pressure-induced nonlinearity.

Key observables. Mechanical oscillation amplitude, frequency response (Lorentzian vs. shark-fin), power spectral density (PSD), and gate fidelity/error rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Mechanical frequency (5-18 MHz), optical detuning, Duffing nonlinearity coefficient (beta), damping rate (Gamma), and thermal amplitude (x_th).

Assumptions / limitations. Use of the Rotating-Wave Approximation (RWA) for single-mode treatment and neglecting higher-order harmonics and intermodulation products through optimized DC bias.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup and the transition from linear to nonlinear frequency response. Figure 2 demonstrates the realization of all six logic gates. Figure 3 shows the implementation of cascadable gates using multiple eigenmodes. Figure 4 shows the time trace and noise analysis of an OR gate.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of programmable physical computing, describes the optomechanical platform and the Duffing model, demonstrates the implementation of various logic gates, shows the extension to multi-mode cascading, and concludes with a detailed noise and error rate analysis.

Abstract

Looking at physical systems as computers allows us to regard physical properties, such as thermal noise, symmetry or topology, as unconventional resources for computation. However, harnessing these resources requires programming computational functionality through strong, controllable nonlinearities in the system. Here, we show that cavity optomechanical interactions allow laser-controlled computation with nanomechanical degrees of freedom. We demonstrate a set of basic digital logic gates with level restoration and controlled mechanical couplings as essential ingredients for arbitrary computing networks. Owing to the strong optomechanical nonlinearity and precise readout, the system operates close to thermal amplitudes, in the regime where thermodynamic stochasticity governs its behavior. This opens a new path for the realization of physical computing with controlled nonlinear resonators.

High-mobility inertial domain walls driven by spin-transfer torque in a ferrimagnetic spinel oxide

Summary. This paper demonstrates exceptionally fast and efficient domain wall motion in a ferrimagnetic spinel oxide using spin-transfer torque. The researchers observed high mobility and a significant inertial effect, positioning this material as a strong candidate for next-generation ultrafast spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of achieving high-velocity, energy-efficient domain wall motion in magnetic devices for spintronic applications like racetrack memories.

Main result. Demonstrated domain wall velocities exceeding 1 km/s in NiCo2O4 with high mobility and a pronounced inertial effect due to large nonadiabatic spin-transfer torque.

Method. Used epitaxial growth of NiCo2O4 thin films, scanning NV magnetometry for domain wall structure, and MOKE microscopy for tracking dynamics under current pulses.

Model / system. Single-layer ferrimagnetic spinel oxide NiCo2O4 (NCO) thin films grown on MgAl2O4 substrates, utilizing a 1D domain wall model to describe dynamics.

Key observables. Domain wall velocity, domain wall width, chirality, nonadiabatic torque parameter (beta/alpha), and energy consumption per volume.

Important parameters / regimes. Current density (2 x 10^11 A/m2), characteristic relaxation time (~1 ns), and spin polarization (-0.73).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes spin-transfer torque is the sole driving mechanism and that the system operates in a regime without Walker breakdown.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Structural and magnetic characterization (XRD, STEM, SQUID). Fig 2: NV microscopy of domain wall structure. Fig 3: Current-driven domain wall displacement and velocity. Fig 4: Evidence of domain wall inertia. Fig 5: Comparative performance of NCO against other magnetic materials.

Paper structure. The paper begins with material characterization, moves to structural analysis of the domain wall, presents dynamic measurements of velocity and inertia, and concludes with a comparative performance analysis against other magnetic systems.

Abstract

Efficient electrical manipulation of domain walls is key to developing magnetic devices with fast switching capabilities and low energy consumption. Here we demonstrate Bloch-type domain wall velocities exceeding 1 km s$^{-1}$ in the single-layer ferrimagnetic spinel oxide NiCo$_2$O$_4$ induced by spin-transfer torque at a current density of $2 \times 10^{11}$ A m$^{-2}$. This exceptional domain wall mobility is attributed to the combination of giant nonadiabatic spin-transfer torque, low magnetization, and high spin polarization. Additionally, we report a pronounced domain wall inertia effect in this ferrimagnet due to the large nonadiabaticity of the torque. The characteristic time for domain wall acceleration and deceleration is $\sim 1$ ns, shorter than that reported for typical ferromagnets. Our findings highlight the potential of spinel oxides as a promising platform for engineering high-performance domain wall devices that take advantage of ultrafast ferrimagnetic dynamics.

Anharmonic Quantum Transport Analysis of Thermal Transport Anomalies in Ultrathin Silicon Nanowires

Authors: Lokanath Patra, Mayur Pratap Singh, Satish Kumar
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26529v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper uses a quantum-mechanical NEGF framework with machine-learning potentials to explain why thermal conductivity in ultrathin silicon nanowires does not decrease linearly with diameter. It identifies a critical diameter where thermal transport transitions between hydrodynamic-like phonon flow and a regime dominated by quantum confinement and boundary scattering.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the origin of the nonmonotonic dependence of thermal conductivity on the diameter in ultrathin silicon nanowires and resolving discrepancies between classical MD and quantum-mechanical reality.

Main result. Confirmed a nonmonotonic thermal conductivity behavior where a minimum occurs at critical diameters (6.24 nm for [001] and 5.50 nm for [110]), driven by the competition between boundary scattering and hydrodynamic-like phonon flow at room temperature, and radial quantum confinement at cryogenic temperatures.

Method. Anharmonic non-equilibrium Green's function (NEGF) simulations combined with neuroevolution potentials (NEP) trained on density-functional-theory (DFT) data.

Model / system. Ultrathin hydrogen-passivated silicon nanowires (Si NWs) with diameters ranging from 0.5 to 6 nm, oriented along [001] and [110] crystallographic directions.

Key observables. Thermal conductivity (k), spectral thermal conductance, phonon scattering rates (Normal vs. Umklapp), and phonon dispersion/band structures.

Important parameters / regimes. Nanowire diameter (d), temperature (10 K to 300 K), and crystallographic orientation ([001] and [110]).

Assumptions / limitations. The NW length is fixed at 10 nm; the Cauchy principal integral in the retarded self-energy calculation is neglected to reduce complexity; Gaussian broadening is used for numerical smoothing.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the NW structures and phonon band structure agreement; Figure 2 plots thermal conductivity vs. diameter at 10 K and 300 K; Figure 5 displays frequency-resolved spectral thermal conductance for various diameters and temperatures.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the thermal transport problem, describes the ML-NEGF/NEP methodology, presents results for thermal conductivity across different diameters and orientations, analyzes the underlying phonon scattering mechanisms at different temperature regimes, and provides supplementary details on potential training and spectral conductance.

Abstract

Thermal transport in low-dimensional semiconductors is crucial for advancing thermal management in nanoelectronics, quantum devices, and thermoelectric devices. Recent molecular dynamics (MD) studies have identified a nonmonotonic dependence of thermal conductivity (k) on diameter in ultrathin silicon nanowires (NWs). However, classical MD methods are limited at low temperatures and in strongly confined regimes. This work introduces a fully quantum-mechanical perspective on this anomaly by employing anharmonic non-equilibrium Green's function (NEGF) simulations combined with density-functional-theory-trained neuroevolution potentials. For [001]- and [110]-oriented NWs, k decreases with diameter d to a minimum at d_c = 6.24 nm and 5.50 nm, respectively, then rises with d, for a temperature range of 10-300 K. At room temperature, this behavior arises from dominant momentum-conserving normal scattering relative to Umklapp processes in confined regimes, thereby enabling Poiseuille-like hydrodynamic phonon flow that competes with boundary scattering. At cryogenic temperatures, strong radial confinement quantizes the phonon spectrum, and only low-frequency phonons (< 2 THz) significantly contribute to heat transport through quasi-ballistic propagation of long-wavelength modes, as demonstrated by the spectral thermal conductance. In contrast to classical MD, which is inaccurate at low temperatures due to overexcitation of high-frequency vibrations by Boltzmann statistics, neglect of quantum suppression, and overestimation of thermal conductivity in thinner NWs with stronger quantum confinement, the NEGF framework provides quantitative accuracy even at low temperatures, such as 10 K.

Anomaly-Induced Hybrid Bulk Electromagnetic Mode in Weyl Semimetals

Authors: Subrahmanyam D, Suhas Gangadharaiah, E. G. Mishchenko
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27031v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5

Summary. This paper predicts a new type of bulk electromagnetic excitation in Weyl semimetals that occurs due to the chiral anomaly. This hybrid mode is distinct from known excitations and can be identified through its unique dispersion and avoided crossing with the bulk plasmon. The discovery offers a new way to probe the chiral magnetic effect using electron energy-loss spectroscopy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The identification and characterization of a previously unknown hybrid bulk electromagnetic mode in Weyl semimetals arising from the interplay between the chiral anomaly and the orientation of the chiral magnetic response.

Main result. The authors predict a linearly dispersing hybrid mode that undergoes an avoided crossing with the bulk plasmon when the chiral magnetic current has a component along the wave-vector, providing a direct signature of the chiral anomaly.

Method. A semiclassical kinetic-Maxwell framework using the Boltzmann formalism and the continuity equation to solve coupled kinetic and Maxwell equations.

Model / system. Doped 3D Weyl semimetals with broken time-reversal symmetry, characterized by a node-separation vector and a weak static external magnetic field.

Key observables. Loss function spectra L(omega, q), dispersion relations, and damping rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Orientation angle theta between the magnetic field and wave-vector, wave-vector q, frequency scale omega_anom, and the ratio of internode to intra-node scattering rates.

Assumptions / limitations. Long-wavelength regime, collisionless limit, negligible Landau quantization, and neglecting interband effects due to low Fermi energies.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the system; Figure 2 shows loss-function spectra L(omega, q) for different magnetic field configurations and angles, highlighting the presence or absence of the hybrid mode.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of anomaly-induced modes, describes the semiclassical kinetic-Maxwell model, analyzes the electromagnetic response in different magnetic field orientations (parallel vs. perpendicular), and concludes with the predicted experimental signatures in EELS.

Abstract

Collective modes provide direct fingerprints of quantum matter. We predict a previously unidentified hybrid bulk electromagnetic mode in Weyl semimetals arising from the interplay between the chiral anomaly and the orientation of its associated chiral magnetic response relative to the direction of the wave-vector. When the anomaly-induced chiral magnetic current has a component along the propagation direction, oscillations of valley imbalance hybridize with plasmonic charge oscillations, producing a linearly dispersing mode that undergoes avoided crossing with the bulk plasmon, producing a hybrid bulk excitation absent in ordinary metals. The hybrid mode provides a direct signature of Weyl semimetals and a probe of the chiral anomaly and its associated chiral magnetic effect, with observable features in electron energy-loss spectroscopy. Studying this interplay can uncover various optical and electronic properties of Weyl semimetals.

Interaction-Phase Dynamics and Spectral Organization in Damped Higher-Order Nonlinear Schrödinger Models

Authors: C. M. Schober
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26811v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explains how different types of dissipation fundamentally alter the stability of nonlinear wave structures. It shows that while viscous damping destroys spectral organization, nonlinear mean-flow damping allows for complex phase restructuring without breaking the underlying coherent Floquet-band structure.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates why two different dissipative mechanisms—viscous damping and nonlinear mean-flow damping—lead to fundamentally different long-time Floquet spectral evolutions and modulation dynamics in higher-order nonlinear Schrödinger (HONLS) systems.

Main result. The author demonstrates that nonlinear mean-flow damping maintains organized, recurrent Floquet-band structures through interaction-dependent dissipative feedback, whereas viscous damping causes spectral reconnection, diffusion, and the breakdown of coherent modulation.

Method. The research employs a reduced five-mode Fourier truncation and an amplitude-phase reformulation to isolate interaction phases, alongside numerical simulations and comparisons with integrable NLS finite-gap benchmark solutions.

Model / system. The system consists of Higher-Order Nonlinear Schrödinger (HONLS) models subject to two distinct dissipative extensions: Viscous-HONLS (V-HONLS) and Nonlinear Mean-Flow Damped-HONLS (NLD-HONLS).

Key observables. Nonlinear Floquet spectrum (band structure, critical points, and reconnection), interaction phases (psi_j), wave amplitude evolution, and peak wavenumber (k_peak).

Important parameters / regimes. Viscous damping strength (Gamma), nonlinear mean-flow damping parameter (beta), higher-order dispersive correction (epsilon), and wavenumber (mu).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on a five-mode Fourier truncation and assumes a carrier-sideband hierarchy (r_0 >> r_plus/minus_1 >> r_plus/minus_2) for asymptotic reductions.

Figures summary. Figures show the evolution of Floquet spectra over time, comparison of surface amplitude and wavenumber dynamics, and the evolution of interaction phases correlated with rogue-wave events and spectral restructuring.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the HONLS models and Floquet diagnostic framework, moves to the derivation of a reduced five-mode interaction-phase model, presents numerical comparisons between viscous and mean-flow damping, and concludes with benchmark analyses using integrable NLS solutions.

Abstract

We investigate the dynamical mechanisms underlying the contrasting nonlinear Floquet spectral evolutions observed in viscous and nonlinear mean-flow damped higher-order nonlinear Schr"odinger systems. Motivated by the persistent organized Floquet-band structure under nonlinear mean-flow damping and the repeated Floquet band reconnection observed under viscous damping, we derive a reduced five-mode carrier-sideband truncation and reformulate the dynamics in amplitude-phase variables to isolate the dominant interaction phases associated with the principal four-wave interaction products. Within this framework, viscous damping acts primarily modewise and does not directly modify the leading interaction-phase dynamics. By contrast, nonlinear mean-flow damping contributes directly to the interaction-phase evolution through interaction-dependent dissipative corrections. In the carrier-sideband regime, these corrections generate terms of the form $-κ_j \sin(ψ_j)$, introducing dissipative feedback into the dominant carrier-sideband interaction dynamics. To interpret the resulting interaction-phase evolution, we examine recurrent finite-gap NLS benchmark solutions whose modulation dynamics are independently understood. These benchmarks show that substantial interaction-phase evolution and localized restructuring may occur even within organized quasiperiodic dynamics possessing invariant Floquet spectral structure. Numerical diagnostics show that the nonlinear mean-flow damped system exhibits persistent recurrent carrier-sideband focusing dynamics together with organized Floquet evolution despite substantial interaction-phase restructuring, whereas the viscous system exhibits progressively diffuse modulation dynamics together with repeated Floquet reconfiguration and weakening persistence of the recurrent carrier-sideband interaction structure.

Amorphous vs. Short-Range-Ordered Complexions: Consequences for Grain-Boundary-Mediated Plasticity in Nanocrystalline Al-Ni Alloys

Authors: Frederic Sansoz, Eve-Audrey Picard
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27218v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the structural state of grain boundaries—either purely amorphous or containing short-range order—dictates the mechanical properties of nanocrystalline Al-Ni alloys. It demonstrates that while SRO increases material strength, it also triggers detrimental shear localization. These findings suggest that controlling the structural order at interfaces can be used to engineer the plasticity of nanostructured materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how local short-range ordering (SRO) within amorphous grain-boundary complexions influences the plasticity and mechanical response of nanocrystalline Al-Ni alloys.

Main result. Amorphous grain boundaries promote homogeneous plasticity via shear transformation zones but lower strength, whereas SRO-containing boundaries increase strength but induce intense shear localization due to stress heterogeneity.

Method. Large-scale hybrid Monte Carlo and Molecular Dynamics (MC/MD) simulations using the LAMMPS framework and an EAM interatomic potential.

Model / system. Nanocrystalline Al-Ni alloys (Al-2Ni and Al-4Ni) consisting of an FCC Al polycrystal with approximately 13 million atoms and an average grain size of 24 nm.

Key observables. Stress-strain curves, grain-boundary thickness, radial distribution functions (RDF), polyhedral template matching (PTM) for structural ordering, and atomic-scale von Mises stress and strain distributions.

Important parameters / regimes. Ni concentrations (2 at.% and 4 at.%), annealing temperatures (378 K and 913 K), and engineering strain rate (10^8 s^-1).

Assumptions / limitations. The interatomic interactions are accurately captured by the EAM potential, and the grain boundary complexions reach equilibrium via the simulated annealing regimes.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the equilibrium microstructure and Ni segregation; Figure 2 presents the Al-Ni phase diagram; Figure 3 compares Ni-Ni RDF curves; Figure 4 visualizes the structural difference between amorphous and SRO complexions; Figures 5-6 show tensile behavior and shear strain; Figures 7-8 compare atomic-scale von Mises stress distributions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of GB-mediated plasticity, describes the MC/MD simulation setup and structural analysis tools, presents the structural evolution of GBs under different annealing temperatures, compares the mechanical response (strength vs. localization) of amorphous and SRO regimes, and concludes with implications for complexion engineering.

Abstract

Amorphous grain-boundary (GB) complexions in thermally stable nanocrystalline alloys are commonly assumed to be structurally homogeneous, yet their disordered nature makes them susceptible to local short-range ordering (SRO). The influence of local SRO on GB-mediated plasticity mechanisms in such complexions remains poorly understood. This article employs large-scale Monte Carlo and molecular dynamics simulation to address this gap through simulations of nanocrystalline Al-Ni alloys at two Ni concentrations, 2 at.% and 4 at.%. Annealing at 913 K produces thick uniform amorphous intergranular film complexions, while annealing at 378 K produces semi-amorphous complexions containing FCC-type and BCC-type SRO. These two complexion states produce fundamentally different mechanical responses. Amorphous complexions act as dislocation sinks, suppressing shear localization and promoting homogeneous plasticity through shear transformation zones, but at the cost of lower strength. SRO complexions generate higher strength but also promote heterogeneous stress concentrations across the GB network, leading to intense shear localization regardless of Ni concentration. This contrast reflects a fundamental shift in governing mechanism, from shear-transformation-zone-controlled behavior in amorphous complexion alloys to GB-stress-heterogeneity-controlled behavior in SRO complexion alloys. These findings highlight the potential of complexion engineering to tailor the mechanical properties of nanocrystalline materials.

Abstract

We investigate magnetoresistance phenomena associated with the magnetization hard-axis collapse in polycrystalline Co thin films. Transport measurements reveal that, for specific orientations of the applied magnetic field, the system exhibits distinct remanent resistance levels in both the in-plane longitudinal and transverse voltage responses. In particular, the planar Hall resistance shows multiple stable and reproducible levels at room temperature, enabling the identification of at least three remanent states that can be distinguished and used for information storage. The observation of such behavior in polycrystalline Co thin films grown on Si substrates suggests a simple and low-cost platform for spintronic memory and sensing devices based on the remanent planar Hall effect.

Summary. This paper identifies that controlling domain wall energy is key to achieving stable, domain-free negative capacitance in ferroelectric heterostructures. This finding suggests that engineering high domain wall energy is a viable path for optimizing negative capacitance devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the specific conditions, particularly regarding domain wall energy, required to achieve an ideal, domain-free negative capacitance state in ferroelectric-dielectric heterostructures.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that a stable, domain-free negative capacitance state is achieved if the domain wall energy parameter (D) exceeds a critical value (Dc), effectively suppressing domain formation.

Figures summary. Figure 2(b) shows the variation of T'C with D for different dielectric thicknesses, and Figure 3 illustrates the variation of the critical domain wall energy parameter Dc with ferroelectric thickness.

Paper structure. Abstract

While negative capacitance has been demonstrated in ferroelectric-dielectric heterostructures in the form of capacitance enhancement, all experimental evidence, to date, suggests the existence of domains therein. Here, we address the question: what are the conditions to achieve ideal, domain-free negative capacitance in ferroelectric-dielectric heterostructures? Our main claim is that for given thicknesses of the ferroelectric and the dielectric layers, there is a critical value of domain wall energy parameter -- above which the system would be stabilized in an ideal and robust domain-free negative capacitance state and would be robust against domain formation. Our analyses suggest that to achieve ideal negative capacitance, efforts should lie in understanding the means to control the domain wall energy on all fronts, both theory and experiments via high throughput design, discovery, and engineering of ferroelectrics.

Detailed structure

Abstract

Carbon possesses remarkable physicochemical properties, enabling main group metals to react with it under high pressure and produce superconducting compounds. However, the research on high-pressure potassium carbides remains still unsystematic and inadequate. Using first-principles calculations and state-of-the-art swarm intelligence structure prediction techniques, we comprehensively explore the binary novel potassium-carbon system and characterize their structural, electronic, and superconducting properties under compression. Impressively, Imma KC reaches a maximum Tc of 21.4 K at 25 GPa, which is highly desirable for low-pressure metallic carbide superconductors. This work provides valuable insights into K-C compounds and broadens the diversity of metal carbide superconductors.

Abstract

We theoretically investigate the properties of phonon angular momentum in the superconducting state, using fulleride compounds in an external magnetic field as a model system. The electron orbital angular momentum injected by an external field is transferred to the phonon subsystem via electron--phonon coupling. We show that this field-induced phonon angular momentum is significantly enhanced and undergoes a sign reversal upon entering the superconducting state. In the normal state, the dominant energy scale governing the response function is the electronic bandwidth $D$. In the superconducting state, the phonon energy scale $ω_1$ enters the denominator, leading to an enhancement of order $D/ω_1$. The observed sign change in the response can be explained by the competition between Fermi surface and Fermi volume contributions.

Abstract

Spin transport across interfaces is critical for spintronic devices, yet remains difficult to probe on ultrafast timescales. We use terahertz emission spectroscopy on Co|Pt heterostructures whose interface roughness is tuned through the thickness of an underlying Au buffer layer, while leaving other growth parameters unchanged. From the measured THz electric field, we extract the interface spin-current transparency ts after correcting for the changes in sample impedance and optical absorption of the stack. Surprisingly, we find that ts decreases by only approximately 30% as the interface root-mean-square roughness and the lateral grain size both increase by a factor of three, with no measurable change in the THz spectrum. These results demonstrate that interfacial spin transport is relatively robust against morphological variations on ultrafast timescales, establishing terahertz emission spectroscopy as a reliable probe of spin dynamics across imperfect interfaces.
