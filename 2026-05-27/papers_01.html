⭐ Intermittency and metastable dark states as a resource for continuous sensing

Highlighted author(s): Igor Lesanovsky
Authors: Robert Mattes, Igor Lesanovsky, Albert Cabot
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26923v1

Summary. This paper compares two types of quantum emission dynamics—intermittency and metastable dark states—as resources for continuous parameter sensing. It reveals that while dark states offer superior sensitivity in ideal conditions, intermittent 'blinking' dynamics are far more resilient to the detection losses and dephasing common in real-world experiments.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a critical trade-off analysis between high-sensitivity/high-fragility (dark states) and low-sensitivity/high-robustness (intermittency) regimes, which is essential for designing practical continuous-monitoring quantum sensors.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Evaluating how different emission dynamics, specifically intermittency (blinking) and metastable dark states, impact the sensitivity of continuous quantum sensing under detection inefficiency and dephasing.

Main result. Metastable dark states provide much higher sensitivity at unit detection efficiency but are highly sensitive to losses, whereas intermittent emission is less sensitive but much more robust to low detection efficiency and dephasing.

Method. The study uses the Lindblad master equation, classical and quantum Fisher information (FI and QFI) to quantify sensitivity, and Maximum-Likelihood Estimators (MLE) to evaluate parameter estimation performance.

Model / system. A four-level driven-dissipative system (applicable to trapped ions like Sr+ and Ca+) featuring a strong fluorescent transition and a weak metastable transition, modeled via a Lindblad master equation.

Key observables. Photon-counting records, waiting time distributions (WTD), and the no-jump probability.

Important parameters / regimes. Detection efficiency (eta_g), Rabi frequencies (Omega_e, Omega_d, Omega_u), detuning (Delta_u), and dephasing rate (gamma_D).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is treated as a renewal process where every detected jump returns the system to the ground state; the analysis focuses on the asymptotic regime of long observation times.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the level scheme and WTD for different setups; Figure 2 compares WTD parameters and stationary populations; Figure 3 and 4 analyze FI rates and WTD oscillations; Figure 12 shows the damping of WTD modulations due to dephasing.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the sensing problem and two dynamical setups, analyzes the waiting time distributions and sensitivity (FI/QFI) for both regimes, evaluates the impact of experimental imperfections like detection loss and dephasing, and demonstrates the optimality of MLE-based estimation.

Abstract

Quanta emitted by an open quantum system carry information about intrinsic parameters, enabling their estimation via continuous monitoring. In practice, however, only a fraction of the emitted quanta is detected, reducing the achievable sensitivity. Here, we consider few-level systems in which coherent couplings and dissipative processes compete, producing metastable dynamics characterized by emission intermittency or by the emergence of a dark state. We show that both phenomena can be beneficial for sensing but their relative performance depends strongly on the achievable detection efficiencies. Intermittent emission, marked by long alternating bright and dark periods, allows to achieve robustness with respect to inefficient detection and dephasing, whereas dark states yield significantly higher sensitivity at unit detection efficiency. Yet the latter are highly susceptible to losses. We quantify the impact of inefficient detection through the classical Fisher information of the emission record and benchmark it against the ultimate sensitivity encoded in the joint system-environment state. Finally, we demonstrate that maximum-likelihood estimators based on the observed emission record can effectively approach this sensitivity. We focus here on trapped-ion systems, however, the results extend to other quantum platforms in which similar emission dynamics can be observed.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that quantum fluctuations can regularize chaotic dynamics in a fully connected SU(3) spin model. By using the 2PI effective action formalism, the authors show that higher-order correlations are essential to capture the relaxation and smoothing of chaotic trajectories that standard mean-field or cumulant methods fail to describe.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a robust theoretical framework (2PI) for understanding how quantum effects can suppress classical chaos, which is crucial for controlling many-body dynamics in platforms like cavity-QED and trapped ions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how quantum fluctuations regularize chaotic dynamics in the macroscopic observables of many-body systems and addressing the failure of standard cumulant expansions in strong-correlation regimes.

Main result. The 2PI effective action formalism successfully captures the regularization of chaos and relaxation processes that are missed by second-order cumulant expansions, which instead predict unphysical persistent oscillations.

Method. The authors use the 2PI effective action formalism within the Schwinger-Keldysh framework, employing a 1/L expansion and Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to derive self-consistent nonequilibrium Dyson equations.

Model / system. A fully connected SU(3) spin-exchange model, which serves as an effective description for N-level systems coupled to a common bosonic mediator, such as in cavity-QED or trapped-ion systems.

Key observables. Macroscopic collective spins, two-time correlation functions, bosonic level populations, and the Frobenius norm of trajectory divergence (to diagnose chaos).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strengths (g+, g-), magnetic field (h), system size (L), and the inverse system size (1/L) acting as an effective Planck constant.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be homogeneous and the study focuses on U(1)-symmetric states; the 1/L correction due to operator-ordering subtleties is assumed to be negligible.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a dynamical phase diagram of regular vs. chaotic regimes; Figure 2 and 3 present diagrammatic notation for the 2PI formalism; Figure 4 shows the divergence of trajectories (Lyapunov-like growth); Figure 6 compares the relaxation of coherences in 2PI vs. cumulant expansion.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SU(3) model and the problem of chaos regularization, details the 2PI and cumulant methodologies, presents numerical results comparing relaxation and chaos suppression, and concludes with a discussion on the superiority of the 2PI framework.

Abstract

We investigate beyond-mean-field dynamics in a fully connected $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ spin-exchange model, focusing on the interplay between chaotic dynamics and quantum fluctuations. Using the two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action formalism, we derive equations of motion that systematically account for higher-order correlations generated by interactions, and demonstrate how quantum fluctuations can regularize chaotic dynamics displayed by macroscopic observables. Our results show that an accurate treatment of fluctuations is essential for describing macroscopic dynamics in quantum many-body systems and promote 2PI as a robust framework for connecting microscopic correlations to macroscopic nonequilibrium phenomena.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that monitoring a spin chain induces both an entanglement phase transition and a transition from ballistic to diffusive transport. Crucially, the transport transition can be observed through linear observables without the need for the complex postselection required for entanglement entropy. This provides a practical pathway for studying measurement-induced phenomena in quantum simulators.

Why it may be interesting. It identifies a way to observe measurement-induced phase transitions using linear, postselection-free observables, providing an experimentally accessible manifestation of the many-body Zeno effect.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the connection between measurement-induced entanglement phase transitions and transitions in transport dynamics (ballistic-to-diffusive) in monitored spin chains.

Main result. The study discovers a postselection-free ballistic-to-diffusive transition in the transient dynamics of spin chains that is closely interlinked with the steady-state entanglement phase transition.

Method. Numerical simulations using Matrix Product State (MPS) methods with the ITensorMPS package, involving trajectory averaging and single-parameter scaling analysis.

Model / system. One-dimensional spin-1/2 XXZ chains in the gapless regime subject to periodic monitoring (measurements) in the high-frequency regime.

Key observables. Von Neumann entanglement entropy, domain-wall width (spatial variance of magnetization), and local magnetization density.

Important parameters / regimes. Measurement rate (p), anisotropy parameter (Delta), and system size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The unmonitored limit derivation assumes the light cone remains far from system boundaries; the high-frequency monitoring is treated in the discrete-time limit.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the domain wall melting process; Figure 2 illustrates the Trotter-decomposed dynamics; Figure 3 displays entanglement entropy growth and transitions; Figure 4 shows the evolution of the spatial variance and its derivatives; Figure 5 presents the scaling analysis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of measurement-induced transitions, describes the XXZ model and monitoring protocol, presents numerical results for entanglement and transport dynamics, provides scaling analysis and theoretical arguments for the link between transitions, and concludes with experimental implications.

Abstract

We study spin and entanglement dynamics in spin-1/2 XXZ chains under periodic monitoring and show that this system exhibits two measurement-induced phase transitions: a steady-state entanglement phase transition similar to those in monitored quantum circuits and a ballistic-to-diffusive transition in transient dynamics. Specifically, we discover that at low monitoring rate, an initial configuration containing a domain wall $|\uparrow\uparrow\uparrow\ldots \downarrow\downarrow\downarrow\ldots\rangle$ spreads ballistically while, at large monitoring rates, the domain melting is diffusive. Extensive numerical simulations, supported by theoretical arguments, indicate that the ballistic-diffusive transition is intimately interlinked with the entanglement phase transition. In contrast to the entanglement phase transitions, which require exponentially complex postselection, the ballistic-diffusive transition can be observed without postselection and constitutes an experimentally accessible manifestation of the many-body Zeno effect.

Summary. The paper investigates how Krylov complexity and operator growth behave in periodically driven conformal field theories and their lattice fermion counterparts. It identifies distinct signatures in the heating and non-heating phases and demonstrates that the type of driving protocol significantly impacts the spectral and graph-theoretic properties of the system.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it provides a precise diagnostic (Krylov complexity) for distinguishing between thermalizing (heating) and non-thermalizing (non-heating) regimes in driven quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Characterizing the transition between heating and non-heating dynamical phases in periodically driven (Floquet) systems using Krylov complexity.

Main result. In the heating phase, Arnoldi coefficients approach unity exponentially, while in the non-heating phase, they exhibit oscillatory behavior; additionally, lattice realizations of continuous drives show different spectral and graph signatures compared to square-wave drives.

Method. The authors use the Arnoldi and Lanczos algorithms to construct Krylov bases, apply conformal mapping techniques for CFT analysis, and employ graph-theoretic analysis of the Floquet operator's transition matrix.

Model / system. The study focuses on (1+1)-dimensional Conformal Field Theories (CFTs) and their lattice realizations via 1D critical fermions under two driving protocols: a square-wave drive and a continuous sinusoidal drive.

Key observables. Krylov complexity (Ck), Arnoldi coefficients (h_{n, n-1}), return amplitude/autocorrelation (Gn), level-spacing ratio (r-factor), and Fiedler value.

Important parameters / regimes. Driving protocols (square-wave vs. continuous), heating vs. non-heating phases, and the driving strength parameter (delta_f).

Assumptions / limitations. The CFT analysis is restricted to the SL(2, R) sector and (1+1)-dimensional systems.

Figures summary. Figures include schematics of Krylov vector transitions, conformal mapping visualizations, driving protocol diagrams, and plots of Arnoldi coefficients, return probability, and graph connectivity/clustering.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Floquet dynamics, analyzes the CFT limit using analytical methods, compares this to lattice fermion simulations, explores different driving protocols (square-wave vs. continuous), and concludes with graph-theoretic and spectral analysis of the transition.

Abstract

We study Krylov construction in periodically driven conformal field theories and their lattice realisations via critical fermions. Two types of driving are considered: a square-wave drive and a continuous sinusoidal drive. Using the Arnoldi construction, we examine Arnoldi coefficients and return amplitudes in periodically driven conformal field theories in the heating and non-heating phases. In the heating phase, the Arnoldi coefficients approach unity exponentially; in contrast, in the non-heating phase, they exhibit oscillatory behaviour. For the lattice realisations, we further analyse the Krylov complexity of the correlation matrix, quasi energy level statistics, and the graph structure induced by the Floquet operator. Although the two drives exhibit similar Krylov growth on the CFT side, their lattice realisations exhibit markedly different spectral and graph signatures, indicating distinct mechanisms governing the transition between the heating and non-heating phases.

Scar Full Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis

Authors: Ning Sun, Yanting Cheng
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.26389v1

Summary. The paper introduces the Scar Full ETH (SFETH) to describe the dynamics of quantum many-body scars. It proves that multi-point correlation functions in these systems can be decomposed into fundamental thermal and scar-specific building blocks. This framework allows for a precise characterization of non-thermal correlations that traditional statistical mechanics cannot capture.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a systematic, unified framework for understanding how non-thermal, persistent dynamics and higher-order correlations emerge and organize in weakly ergodicity-breaking systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The breakdown of the full Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis (ETH) in systems with quantum many-body scars, which prevents traditional ETH from describing non-thermal correlations.

Main result. The formulation of the 'scar full ETH' (SFETH) framework, which successfully decomposes multi-point correlation functions into thermal and scar free cumulants.

Method. Theoretical derivation using typicality arguments, Weingarten calculus, and free probability theory, with numerical validation via exact diagonalization of the PXP model.

Model / system. The spin-1/2 PXP model, a paradigmatic model for quantum many-body scars representing Rydberg atom arrays with blockade effects.

Key observables. Local observables (sigma-z), matrix elements in the energy eigenbasis, and multi-time/multi-point correlation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Thermodynamic limit (N to infinity), energy density, and the scaling of contributions with the Hilbert space dimension (e^-S).

Assumptions / limitations. Thermal energy eigenstates behave as orthogonal Haar-random states, and the operator being measured is local.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows entanglement entropy and matrix element sectors; Figure 2 illustrates the diagrammatic decomposition of correlation functions into scar and thermal cumulants; Figure 3 provides numerical verification of the SFETH sum rule using the PXP model.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of ETH breakdown, proposes the SFETH ansatz, establishes scaling and factorization properties using mathematical tools, and concludes with numerical validation in the PXP model.

Abstract

The eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) provides a fundamental mechanism for emergent statistical mechanics in isolated chaotic quantum systems, asserting that individual energy eigenstates behave as pseudorandom vectors within an energy window. This enables a complete characterization of nontrivial correlations among matrix elements in the energy eigenbasis, as described by the full ETH ansatz. Nevertheless, this description breaks down in systems exhibiting quantum many-body scars, which host non-thermal eigenstates with extensive energy. In this Letter, we address this problem by formulating the \textit{scar full ETH}, which captures correlations among matrix elements involving scar states. The corresponding scaling forms and factorization properties are established using typicality arguments. Multi-time correlation functions for scar states are then organized in terms of both thermal and scar cumulants, providing a nontrivial reorganization of higher-order correlations. We numerically demonstrate the validity of this framework in the paradigmatic model of quantum scars, the PXP model. Our results pave the way for a systematic understanding of intriguing correlations in systems with quantum many-body scars.

Mapping twist fields to local operators via tensor networks

Summary. This paper develops a method to map non-local twist fields used in calculating Renyi entropies to local, physically accessible operators in tensor networks. By doing so, it enables the estimation of entanglement in quantum many-body systems using only local measurements. The approach is shown to be efficient, scalable, and transferable across different system sizes.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a direct bridge between abstract tensor network formalisms and practical measurements in quantum simulators like Rydberg atom arrays or trapped ions, enabling the measurement of complex entanglement properties using only local observables.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The conventional twist field formalism for computing Renyi entropies in tensor networks relies on inaccessible virtual degrees of freedom, making it difficult to implement in physical experiments.

Main result. The authors construct explicit local physical operators that reproduce the action of twist fields in Matrix Product States, providing a scalable and experimentally accessible way to probe entanglement.

Method. The method uses the injectivity of MPS and the center of orthogonality to lift virtual swap operators to the physical Hilbert space via SVD and coarse-graining.

Model / system. The framework is applied to 1D Matrix Product States (MPS) and 2D Projected Entangled Pair States (PEPS), with numerical validation performed on the transverse-field Ising model.

Key observables. Renyi entropies (Sn), entanglement entropy, and expectation values of local Pauli strings.

Important parameters / regimes. Injectivity scale (l), correlation length (xi), bond dimension (chi), and central charge (c).

Assumptions / limitations. The construction is exact in the injectivity limit or at the center of orthogonality, and the accuracy of operator transferability depends on the reference system exceeding the correlation length.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the swap operator at virtual vs. physical levels; Figure 2 shows error decay in Renyi entropy estimates; Figure 3 displays twist field correlation functions; Figure 5 demonstrates the transferability of operators from small to large systems.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-local twist fields, presents a construction for mapping them to local physical operators in 1D MPS, validates the method using the transverse-field Ising model, discusses the transferability of these operators across different system sizes, and proposes a generalization to 2D PEPS.

Abstract

Twist fields are a powerful formal tool to compute Rényi entropies in quantum many-body systems, but their conventional formulation in tensor network states involves operations acting on virtual degrees of freedom, which are not directly accessible in experiments. In this work, we construct explicit local operators acting on the physical Hilbert space whose expectation values reproduce the action of twist fields in matrix product states. Our construction is exact in the injectivity limit and when the tensor is chosen at the center of orthogonality, and provides a direct operational method to evaluate Rényi entropies without accessing auxiliary tensor indices. We test our formulation numerically in the transverse-field Ising model, demonstrating rapid convergence to the exact entanglement entropy as the injectivity scale is reached. Furthermore, we show that twist operators determined from relatively small reference systems can be reliably transferred to larger systems, once the reference size exceeds a characteristic scale set by the correlation length. Since the resulting operators admit a decomposition in terms of a finite number of local observables, our results provide a scalable and experimentally accessible framework to probe entanglement in quantum simulators.

Spatiotemporal Structures of Parametrically Driven Nonlinear Lattices

Summary. This paper explores how parametric driving creates complex spatiotemporal patterns in a nonlinear 1D lattice. It identifies a transition between different resonant states and explains how nonlinear effects and parametric amplification work together to select specific spatial wavenumbers.

Why it may be interesting. The study of pattern formation and stability in driven nonlinear lattices is highly relevant to understanding non-equilibrium phase transitions and collective dynamics in many-body systems, which are central themes in both classical and quantum many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the formation mechanisms and stability of spatiotemporal structures, specifically nontrivial k-resonance and pi-resonance states, in parametrically driven nonlinear many-body lattices.

Main result. The study finds that parametric driving induces spatiotemporal ordering with a subharmonic frequency, where the wavenumber k increases with driving strength before jumping discontinuously to pi-resonance. The k-resonance is driven by the interplay between parametric amplification and nonlinear collective fluctuations.

Method. Numerical simulations using the fourth-order Runge-Kutta method combined with analytical extended linear stability analysis and the method of multiple scales.

Model / system. A one-dimensional Frenkel-Kontorova (FK) model consisting of elastically-coupled classical oscillators with an on-site potential and periodic parametric driving.

Key observables. Fourier spectrum S(k, omega), spatiotemporal patterns of phase, wavenumber k, and oscillation amplitude.

Important parameters / regimes. Parametric driving strength (Pac), driving frequency (omega_ex), coupling constant (K), damping (gamma), and noise strength (T).

Assumptions / limitations. Small parameter epsilon for the method of multiple scales; single-mode approximation for the evolution of Fourier components; nonlinear approximation of the sine potential.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the schematic of pi-resonance and a phase diagram of wavenumber k; Fig 2 displays real-space spatiotemporal structures and Fourier spectra for different driving regimes.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the FK model and parametric driving, presents numerical results on wavenumber selection and phase transitions, provides an analytical derivation of the resonance mechanism via stability analysis, and concludes with an investigation of domain wall dynamics in the supplemental material.

Abstract

We theoretically investigate the effects of parametric driving on the one-dimensional Frenkel-Kontorova model, a nonlinear many-body lattice system. It is numerically found that a parametric vibration induces spatiotemporal ordering characterized by the subharmonic frequency $ω_{\mathrm{ex}}/2$ and wavenumber $k$ which takes nontrivial values depending on the vibration strength $P_{\mathrm{ac}}$ and frequency $ω_{\mathrm{ex}}$. With increasing $P_{\mathrm{ac}}$, $k$ gradually increases and discontinuously jumps up to $k=π$. Based on an extended linear stability analysis, we show that the former $k$ resonance (the latter $π$ resonance) corresponds to the unstable (stable) mode and that the $k$ resonance is made possible by the interplay between the parametric amplification and the collective fluctuation developing through the nonlinear effect.

Summary. This review paper proposes a new classification framework for time crystals based on how they are stabilized and their physical properties. Why it may be interesting. It provides a unified perspective on non-equilibrium phase transitions and the competition between ergodicity-breaking mechanisms like localization and prethermalization in driven quantum systems.

Main result. The authors propose a new classification framework for time crystals (discrete, continuous, closed, open, topological, etc.) and review recent experimental implementations on various quantum platforms.

Method. A review of recent experimental implementations combined with a proposed theoretical classification based on stabilization mechanisms (MBL, prethermalization, dissipation) and physical character.

Model / system. The review covers a wide range of platforms including superconducting qubits, trapped ions, neutral atoms, NV centers, and BECs, focusing on periodically driven (Floquet) many-body systems.

Abstract

Time crystals are nonequilibrium phases of matter characterized by the emergence of temporal ordering, in which an interacting many-body system develops robust structure in its time evolution that is not trivially dictated by the external driving or environment. While related phenomena have long been studied in classical nonlinear systems, their realization in entangled quantum matter represents a distinct frontier. The theoretical understanding of discrete time crystals has substantially advanced, yet recent experiments using modern quantum devices and quantum processors reveal regimes beyond established paradigms. These developments call for an extended classification of time-crystalline phases according to both their stabilization mechanisms and their physical character, including discrete and continuous, closed and open, critical, topological, quasiperiodic, and controlled realizations. We review recent implementations of time crystals on quantum platforms and propose such a classification framework, identifying promising directions for the discovery of novel time-crystalline phases of matter.

Summary. This paper introduces a waveguide model to show that variations in light-matter coupling strength can be harnessed to suppress unwanted noise in quantum memories. It identifies the physical origin of echo suppression during adiabatic pulses and proposes a new pulse sequence to maintain entanglement. The findings have direct implications for improving the performance of both microwave and optical quantum network protocols.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel perspective on treating disorder (coupling inhomogeneity) as a functional resource rather than a nuisance, which is highly relevant for designing robust quantum networks and high-fidelity quantum memories.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to explain the unexpected suppression of echoes during Adiabatic Rapid Passage (ARP) pulses in quantum memories and to address the degradation of entanglement in RASE protocols caused by coupling inhomogeneity.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that coupling inhomogeneity can be used as a resource to suppress unwanted echoes (echo-silencing) and propose a new composite-pulse protocol to restore entanglement in the presence of such inhomogeneity.

Method. The study uses a waveguide model that maps subradiant modes to an effective waveguide, combined with second-order cumulant expansion simulations and the Holstein-Primakoff approximation.

Model / system. An ensemble of N two-level systems (spins) interacting with a single resonator mode, described by the Tavis-Cummings Hamiltonian with both frequency and coupling strength distributions.

Key observables. Superradiant emission, dephasing timescales, silencing factor (F), output noise, and the Duan-Simon criterion for entanglement.

Important parameters / regimes. Collective cooperativity (C), adiabaticity factor (Q), coupling distribution width (sigma_g), and frequency distribution width (Gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The model relies on the Holstein-Primakoff approximation (valid for low excitation regimes) and assumes a second-order truncation of the cumulant expansion.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the physical system of spins in a resonator and the simplified waveguide model; Figure 3 illustrates the ROSE protocol, the suppression of ACE by coupling inhomogeneity, and the silencing factor versus coupling width.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of inhomogeneity, develops a new analytical waveguide model, applies this model to explain echo suppression in ROSE/RASE protocols, proposes a mitigation strategy for entanglement, and validates the results against experimental platforms like microwave and optical systems.

Abstract

Inhomogeneous ensembles of two-level systems are important for both fundamental light-matter physics and quantum-network applications. Here we develop a general waveguide model that provides an intuitive and analytical description of collective dynamics in such systems by mapping the subradiant modes to an effective waveguide with well-defined input-output relations. For echo-based quantum memories, the model reveals the physical origin of noisy-echo suppression by adiabatic pulses and shows that inhomogeneous light-matter coupling can be harnessed as a resource for noiseless storage. For entanglement generation, the same mechanism exposes a previously unexplored limitation of robust control pulses and leads to a new composite-pulse protocol that overcomes it. These results establish the waveguide model as a practical bridge between fundamental collective physics and quantum-network protocol design, with direct implications for high-fidelity memories and robust entanglement generation in inhomogeneous ensembles.

Abstract

We address the problem of identifying non-Markovian quantum time evolutions of an open quantum system by only performing measurements of the system's energy. We demonstrate that violations of CP-divisibility are always witnessed by non-positive values of the energy-change Kirkwood-Dirac quasiprobability distribution associated with the system's Hamiltonian, evaluated at consecutive times. The link between non CP-divisibility and non-positivity of the system's energy-change distribution is stronger when the system-environment interactions are energy-preserving. The witness works whenever anomalous energy fluxes, due to non-Markovianity, are realized. Anomalous fluxes are also detected by the non-Markovianity measure built over the quantum mutual information between the states of the open system and of a quantum correlated reference.

Summary. This study demonstrates that combining microwave-driven Floquet engineering with Fano interference in a ring-chord quantum dot structure can significantly boost thermoelectric efficiency and spin-dependent transport. By using geometry to suppress thermal conductance while using microwaves to tune energy-selective channels, the authors achieve remarkably high figures of merit. This provides a strategy for designing high-performance nanoscale heat engines and spin filters.

Why it may be interesting. The paper explores the dynamic control of quantum interference and energy-selective transport using periodic driving, which is highly relevant to the study of open quantum systems and Floquet engineering of many-body states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the interplay between microwave-driven Floquet sidebands and geometry-driven Fano interference can be used to enhance the thermoelectric and spin-caloritronic performance of multi-quantum-dot nanostructures.

Abstract

We investigate photon-assisted thermoelectric transport in four--quantum-dot nanostructures featuring ring and ring--chord geometries coupled to ferromagnetic leads. Focusing on the interplay between microwave-induced Floquet sidebands and geometry-driven Fano interference, we employ the nonequilibrium Green's function formalism combined with Floquet theory and a self-consistent Hartree treatment of electron-electron interactions within the linear response regime. The inclusion of a longitudinal interdot chord bridging the lead-coupled dots introduces a discrete interference pathway that competes with the continuum of ring-mediated states, giving rise to pronounced Fano resonances. Microwave irradiation dynamically reshapes these resonances through photon absorption and emission processes, enabling tunable control of electrical conductance, thermopower, and electronic thermal conductance. Quantitatively, at an intermediate temperature of $T=0.3Γ_0$ (where $Γ_0$ denotes the dot-lead coupling strength), the microwave-driven ring-chord geometry exhibits an exceptional thermoelectric figure of merit of ZT $\approx$ 12 and an optimal efficiency--power trade-off, reaching nearly $62\%$ of the Carnot efficiency with an output power of $6.24~\mathrm{fW}$. Crucially, the combination of spin-polarized injection from the leads and Zeeman splitting within the dots induces a robust spin-dependence within this Floquet--Fano interference. This cooperative interplay results in an enhanced spin Seebeck response and a maximum spin thermoelectric figure of merit of nearly $Z_sT \approx 18$. Our findings establish microwave-driven engineering of Fano interference as an effective strategy for modulating spin-caloritronic behavior in multi-quantum-dot devices.
