Cumulant dynamics in finite-memory diffusion

Authors: Navid Abbasi, Xin An, Shanjin Wu
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02446v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 1/5 · non-equilibrium universality 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work develops a theoretical framework to describe how charge fluctuations evolve in the rapidly expanding QGP, moving beyond simple Fickian diffusion. By incorporating finite current relaxation time (Maxwell-Cattaneo diffusion), the authors show that memory effects significantly alter the measurable cumulants. This provides a more accurate diagnostic tool for probing the QCD critical point.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical treatment of memory effects in transport coefficients, leading to modified evolution equations for correlation functions, provides a generalizable framework for non-equilibrium dynamics in complex systems, relevant to open quantum systems modeling.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the complex, non-equilibrium evolution of fluctuation correlators for conserved charges in the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) fireball, especially when current relaxation effects are significant.

Main result. Finite current memory, modeled by Maxwell-Cattaneo diffusion, introduces an additional memory effect beyond standard diffusive lag, which can suppress, shift, and reshape the non-monotonic behavior of higher-order cumulants.

Method. The authors derive closed evolution equations for multi-point Wigner functions, extending the standard Fickian diffusion baseline to include finite current relaxation time scales.

Model / system. The system is the QGP undergoing rapid expansion, analyzed using theoretical frameworks that extend standard hydrodynamics to incorporate memory effects via the Maxwell-Cattaneo constitutive relation.

Key observables. Acceptance-dependent cumulants ($C_N(\Delta)$) of conserved charges, and the evolution of multi-point Wigner functions ($W_N$).

Important parameters / regimes. Current relaxation time ($ au$), diffusive lag time, and the proximity to the QCD critical point.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the ability to derive closed deterministic evolution equations for multi-point correlators and compares results against the $ au o 0$ (Fickian) limit.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the comparison of cumulant evolution under different diffusion models and potentially show background trajectories in the QCD phase diagram.

Paper structure. The paper builds from establishing the link between cumulants and correlators, deriving the Maxwell-Cattaneo evolution equations for the correlators, and finally numerically comparing the resulting cumulant dynamics against the standard Fickian baseline.

Abstract

Fluctuations of conserved charges are among the main proposed signatures of the quantum chromodynamics (QCD) critical point, but their interpretation requires a dynamical description of how fluctuation correlators evolve during the finite lifetime of the quark--gluon plasma (QGP) fireball. The standard baseline for this evolution is Fickian diffusion, in which the diffusive current follows the local density gradient instantaneously. This instantaneous-current limit can miss delayed-response effects when the current-relaxation time becomes comparable to the relaxation time of the relevant fluctuation modes. In this work we extend this baseline to Maxwell--Cattaneo diffusion, where the current relaxes on a finite time scale and therefore retains memory. We derive closed evolution equations for multi-point Wigner functions and convert the freezeout correlators into acceptance-dependent cumulants along representative trajectories in the QCD phase diagram. While Fickian diffusion already causes the correlators to lag behind their instantaneous equilibrium values, finite current relaxation introduces an additional memory effect beyond this diffusive lag. As a result, current memory can suppress, shift, and reshape the non-monotonic behavior of the cumulants relative to both instantaneous equilibrium and Fickian diffusion, with the most visible effects appearing in higher-order cumulants and their ratios.

Summary. This work investigates the phase transitions of the 2D ANNNI model using sophisticated simulation methods. The authors introduce and validate a powerful combination of Population Annealing and a two-replica cluster algorithm. This new technique successfully resolves the complex physics of the incommensurate floating phase, demonstrating superior equilibration capabilities over existing simulation tools.

Why it may be interesting. The development and rigorous testing of the two-replica cluster algorithm combined with PA represent a significant advancement in computational techniques for simulating highly frustrated magnetic systems, providing insights applicable to other complex lattice models.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study focuses on resolving the sequence of sharp specific heat peaks that characterize the finite-size incommensurate floating (IC) phase in the 2D ANNNI model.

Main result. The combination of population annealing with a two-replica cluster algorithm proves significantly more effective at equilibrating the system than single-replica or Metropolis methods in the strongly frustrated regime.

Method. The authors employ advanced Monte Carlo techniques, specifically Population Annealing (PA) coupled with a novel two-replica cluster algorithm, to simulate the system's thermal properties.

Model / system. The system is the 2D Axial Next-Nearest-Neighbor Ising (ANNNI) model, described by a Hamiltonian involving nearest-neighbor and competing next-nearest-neighbor interactions. Abstract

The axial next-nearest-neighbor Ising (ANNNI) model in two dimensions is studied using population annealing combined with a two-replica cluster algorithm. We are able to fully resolve the sequence of sharp specific heat peaks that characterize the finite-size incommensurate floating phase. We also show that the two-replica cluster algorithm is much more effective in equilibrating the system than either single-replica cluster methods or the Metropolis algorithm when these are combined with population annealing. We argue that effectiveness of the new algorithm is due to its ability to move groups of defect lines between replicas combined with resampling in population annealing, which removes replicas from the population that have larger numbers of defect lines.

Magnon bandstructure and topology in a periodically deformed Kagome lattice with DM interaction

Summary. This work theoretically examines how periodic deformations on a Kagome lattice modify the magnon band structure when DM interactions are present. By comparing anti-ferromagnetic and ferromagnetic couplings, the authors show that deformation can induce rich topological features, evidenced by large Chern numbers. This highlights lattice engineering as a key tool for realizing novel topological physics in magnetic materials.

Why it may be interesting. The explicit connection between geometric deformation, magnetic interactions, and the emergence of non-trivial topology (quantized Chern numbers) provides a powerful mechanism for engineering exotic quantum states in condensed matter magnets.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the magnon band structure and topological properties of a magnetic system subjected to periodic lattice deformations and Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya (DM) interactions.

Main result. The lattice deformation enriches the band structure, leading to rich topological features, particularly in the ferromagnetic coupling case where Chern numbers can reach $\pm 2$.

Method. The analysis employs linear spin-wave theory via the Holstein-Primakoff transformation, followed by diagonalizing the resulting bosonic Hamiltonian in momentum space to calculate topological invariants like the Chern number.

Model / system. The system is a Heisenberg model with DM interaction defined on a Kagome lattice. The model is analyzed on a periodically deformed lattice, which modifies the exchange and DM couplings.

Key observables. Magnon band structure, Chern numbers ($C_n$), and the presence/closing of band gaps.

Important parameters / regimes. The nature of coupling (anti-ferromagnetic vs. ferromagnetic), the strength of the DM interaction ($D_z/J$), and the strength of the lattice deformation ($C$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on linear spin-wave theory and a $1/S$ expansion, which are approximations valid under certain conditions.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate band structures comparing undeformed vs. deformed lattices, and show the dependence of Chern numbers and band gaps on the deformation strength and DM interaction strength.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the Hamiltonian, first establishing the theoretical framework using spin-wave theory. It then compares the topological properties in the anti-ferromagnetic (trivial) versus ferromagnetic (rich) regimes, demonstrating how lattice deformation drives topological phase transitions.

Abstract

We study the band structure and topology of magnons for a Heisenberg model with DM interaction on a deformed Kagome lattice. For simplicity, we focus on a periodically deformed lattice with hexagonal symmetry and an enlarged unit cell. This enlarged unit cell gives rise to a richer band structure than in the undeformed case. Analyzing band topology, there is a distinction between the topologically trivial case with anti-ferromagnetic coupling and the topologically rich case with ferromagnetic coupling. In the anti-ferromagnetic case, a spin-space symmetry, also present in the classical ground state, enforces these topologically trivial states. In the ferromagnetic case, this symmetry is spontaneously broken by the classical ground state. Consequently, the band structure also hosts rich topological features. Specifically, we observe many topological transitions and bands with Chern numbers ranging from $+2$ to $-2$, which makes the system richer than in the undeformed case. This emergent rich topological structure demonstrates that deformed magnets can host new and exciting physics.

Quantum-inspired Topographic Stereovision

Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02197v1

Summary. This work re-evaluates stereovision, arguing that topography is more fundamental than distance for distant objects. The authors propose a quantum-inspired topographic interferometer that uses quantum correlations to measure topography with precision surpassing classical limits. This establishes a new quantum framework for heat-assisted detection in fields like astronomy.

Why it may be interesting. It applies advanced quantum estimation theory (Quantum Fisher Information) to a complex, non-equilibrium sensing problem (thermal imaging), providing a quantum-enhanced framework for remote sensing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper challenges the traditional reliance on triangulation for stereovision, arguing that for distant objects, topography is the more relevant observable than absolute distance.

Main result. The proposed topographic interferometer enables quantum-enhanced stereovision of thermal sources beyond the Rayleigh limit by exploiting cross-detector correlations.

Method. It introduces stereo regularization and proposes a topographic interferometer that uses Mach-Zehnder arms and balanced homodyne detection to probe topography.

Model / system. The system models the optical field from a thermal source in the X-Z plane, analyzing the superposition of photons across stereo spatial modes (L, R) fed into a beam splitter.

Key observables. Topography ($ heta$), absolute distance ($Z$), disparity ($\delta_q$), and the quantum Fisher information ($K_h$).

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis focuses on the long-range and weak-source limits ($\epsilon \ll 1$) and the incompatibility quantified by $\Delta^2 Z + \Delta^2 heta \ge ext{constant}$.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the sub-Rayleigh regime and addresses stereo anisotropies violating emitter-number conservation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares traditional stereovision (measuring Z) versus topographic stereovision.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the observable-measurement mismatch, then introduces stereo regularization, and finally details the construction and analysis of the topographic interferometer to achieve quantum-enhanced ranging.

Abstract

We challenge the long-unquestioned triangulation in distant stereovision, where shape rather than distance is the relevant observable. Our information-regret analysis reveals that the optimal measurements for absolute distance and distance gradient are unexpectedly different and incompatible. To resolve this observable-measurement mismatch, we introduce stereo regularization to address stereo anisotropies that violate prevailing emitter-number conservation, and propose the topographic interferometer, which exploits cross-detector correlations to probe topography without measuring the distance profile. Our interferometer turns parallaxing paths into Mach-Zehnder arms and incorporates a central path as the local oscillator for balanced homodyne detection, saturating the quantum Fisher information with improved topographic error scaling. Our work enables topographic stereovision of thermal sources beyond the Rayleigh limit, thereby establishing a quantum-inspired framework for heat-assisted detection and ranging in remote sensing and astronomy.

Locality-Induced Hierarchical Backflow Wavefunctions for Correlated Fermions

Summary. This work introduces the Hierarchical Backflow (HB) wavefunction, a novel, systematically improvable ansatz for correlated fermions, by leveraging the principle of locality. By extending this to the Residual HB (RHB) ansatz, the authors achieve high accuracy in simulating the Hubbard model, demonstrating a powerful, scalable framework for strongly correlated electron physics.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on condensed matter, the development of a structured, systematically improvable ansatz (HB/RHB) that bridges determinantal forms with neural network representations is highly relevant for developing general-purpose quantum state representations applicable across different physical domains.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of simulating and representing the ground state of strongly correlated fermionic systems, such as those described by the Hubbard model, due to the exponentially large Hilbert space.

Main result. The proposed Hierarchical Backflow (HB) and Residual Hierarchical Backflow (RHB) wavefunctions provide a systematically improvable and highly accurate variational ansatz, achieving results comparable to advanced quantum Monte Carlo methods.

Method. The core method involves constructing a variational wavefunction based on the backflow concept, which is systematically refined by introducing a path depth parameter K, and further enhanced by a nonlocal neural network factor (RHB).

Model / system. The study focuses on the two-dimensional Hubbard model, characterized by hopping ($t_{ij}$) and on-site repulsion ($U_{ik}$), applied to various doping levels and system sizes.

Key observables. Ground-state energy per site, staggered spin correlation function, and local spin/hole density distributions.

Important parameters / regimes. Path depth K (controlling correlation range), doping level ($n_h$), and interaction strength ($U$).

Assumptions / limitations. The method relies on the locality principle to organize backflow correlations, and the RHB ansatz uses a two-stage optimization scheme to overcome trapping in the optimization landscape.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the orbital structure dependence on K, energy convergence curves showing systematic improvement with K, and spatial maps revealing the formation of stripe phases at finite doping.

Paper structure. The paper develops the HB ansatz by proposing locality as the organizing principle, systematically improving its expressive power via K. It then extends this to the RHB ansatz, which incorporates nonlocal correlations via a neural network, and validates this structure using advanced optimization techniques and comparisons to established benchmarks.

Abstract

We show that locality provides a natural principle to hierarchically organize backflow wavefunctions. This leads us to propose a family of variational fermionic states, termed hierarchical backflow (HB) wavefunctions. The expressive power of HB is systematically improvable, controlled by a path depth $K$ which reflects the range of backflow correlations. At half-filling, the HB with $K=1$ already achieves high energy precision, with an accuracy around $0.5\%$ for system sizes from $4\times 4$ to $10\times 10$. At hole doping $n_h=0.125$, the method scales efficiently to $12\times16$ and $16\times16$ systems, and the energy systematically achieves higher accuracy with $K$ increasing, yielding a clear stripe phase. The HB further enables a local-nonlocal decomposition, naturally bridging to neural quantum states, while featuring compact representations and efficient optimization. Our work reveals locality as a natural organizing principle of backflow wavefunctions, opening a new framework with systematic improvability and interpretability for large-scale simulations of correlated fermion systems.

Quenching of Nonrelativistic p-Wave Spin Splitting by c-f Decoupling in CeNiAsO

Authors: Xinnuo Zhang, Zhicheng Jiang, Shibo Shen, Jian Yuan, Junseo Yoo, Changyoung Kim, Mao Ye, Jishan Liu, Zhengtai Liu, Yanfeng Guo, Yilin Wang, Dawei Shen
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02420v1

Summary. This work experimentally investigates the predicted $p$-wave spin splitting in the correlated material CeNiAsO, which is theoretically expected based on magnetic symmetry. The authors report that ARPES measurements fail to detect this splitting, demonstrating that the strong electronic correlations force the Ce 4f electrons into a localized regime. This establishes a key many-body constraint: symmetry alone is insufficient to guarantee spin splitting if electron localization dominates the magnetic coupling.

Why it may be interesting. The core finding—that symmetry is necessary but not sufficient—provides a crucial lesson for modeling correlated electron systems, particularly those where local moments interact with itinerant carriers, which is relevant to understanding exotic quantum states in condensed matter.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the theoretical prediction versus experimental observation of nonrelativistic p-wave spin splitting in the heavy-fermion oxypnictide CeNiAsO, challenging symmetry-based predictions for magnetism.

Main result. Experimental ARPES data show no evidence of the expected spin splitting or band folding across magnetic transitions, suggesting that the Ce 4f electrons remain localized and decouple from the conduction bands.

Method. The study combines ultra-low-temperature, high-resolution ARPES measurements with theoretical calculations (DFT) comparing different treatments of the f-electrons.

Model / system. The system is the layered heavy-fermion oxypnictide CeNiAsO, which is studied across magnetic transitions into SDW and coplanar antiferromagnetic states. The physics involves the interplay between itinerant conduction electrons and localized Ce 4f moments.

Key observables. ARPES band dispersions, Fermi surface mapping, observation of $p$-wave band splitting, and the spectral weight of the Ce 4f electrons.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic transition temperatures ($T_{N1} \approx 9 ext{ K}$, $T_{N2} \approx 6 ext{ K}$), the ratio of Kondo coherence temperature ($T^*$) to Néel temperature ($T_N$), and the localized vs. itinerant limit.

Assumptions / limitations. The initial theoretical framework assumes that extending spin-space group symmetries predicts the splitting, but the experiment tests the validity of this assumption under strong correlations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate magnetic transitions via transport measurements, ARPES intensity maps showing the absence of predicted band folding or spin splitting, and theoretical comparisons between different electronic structure treatments.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the theoretical prediction based on symmetry, presents the experimental ARPES measurements across magnetic phases, analyzes the failure to observe the predicted splitting, and concludes by revising the theoretical understanding to incorporate the many-body constraint imposed by electron localization.

Abstract

The extending of spin-space group symmetries to coplanar antiferromagnets has predicted the emergence of odd-parity nonrelativistic spin splittings, making the identification of a practical $p$-wave magnet a central pursuit in spintronics. The layered heavy-fermion oxypnictide CeNiAsO has been widely regarded as the prototypical platform to verify this paradigm, as its commensurate coplanar magnetic configuration is theoretically expected to induce a robust $p$-wave band splitting. Here, we investigate the electronic structure of single-crystal CeNiAsO using ultra-low-temperature, high-resolution, and resonant angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES). Across the consecutive magnetic transitions into the ordered phases, our spectroscopic data reveal neither the expected band folding associated with a spin density wave nor any observable $p$-wave band splitting, demonstrating that the conduction bands retain full Kramers degeneracy. By tracking the temperature dependence of the Ce 4$f$ spectral weight via resonant ARPES, we find no evidence of coherent $c-f$ hybridization near the Fermi level within the magnetically ordered states, confirming that the Ce 4$f$ electrons operate in the localized limit. Our findings establish a clear many-body constraint on projecting real-space magnetic symmetries onto momentum-space electronic bands, demonstrating that geometric symmetry classifications constitute a necessary framework but are not a sufficient condition for nonrelativistic spin splittings in the presence of strong electronic correlations.

Abstract

The phase diagram of the two-dimensional quantum $J_1$-$J_3$ Heisenberg antiferromagnet on a square lattice is a long-standing open problem. Interplay between Quantum Metric and Hybridized Collective Modes in Flat-Band Superfluids

Authors: Yi Liu, Mingyan Wang, Penghui Hu, Yao Lu
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01235v1

Summary. This theoretical work investigates collective excitations in flat-band superfluids, showing that the mode dispersion is not linear (like conventional superfluids) but quadratic ($\omega \propto q^2$). The key finding is that the curvature of this parabolic dispersion is universally determined by the normal-state quantum metric of the underlying flat band.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a rigorous, geometric understanding of superfluid dynamics in highly constrained systems (flat bands), linking macroscopic excitation properties directly to fundamental quantum geometric invariants (the quantum metric).

Detailed structure

Main problem. To determine the low-energy collective excitation dispersion relation in flat-band superfluids by analyzing the coupled dynamics of pairing, amplitude, and density fluctuations.

Main result. The collective mode exhibits a quadratic dispersion ($\omega \propto q^2$) at small momenta, and the coefficient governing this curvature is determined by the normal-state quantum metric of the flat band.

Method. The authors employ a path integral formalism with a Generalized Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, analyzing the full dynamic response matrix via a multi-channel fluctuation expansion.

Model / system. The system is a time-reversal symmetric superfluid characterized by an isolated flat band, modeled using a mean-field Hamiltonian projected onto the flat-band subspace.

Key observables. Collective excitation dispersion relation $\omega(q)$, the quadratic curvature coefficient $\kappa$, and the quantum metric $g_{qq}(k)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Zero temperature ($T=0$), long-wavelength limit ($q o 0$), and the existence of an isolated flat band.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the flat band is energetically well separated from other bands, and that the low-energy dynamics can be captured by projecting onto the degenerate null space of the static response matrix.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the schematic band structure, the parabolic nature of the low-energy dispersion ($\omega \propto q^2$), and a direct comparison showing the fitted curvature tracking the theoretical quantum metric.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the coupled fluctuation dynamics (GHS transformation) to constructing the full dynamic response matrix, solving for the zero-frequency modes, and finally calculating the second-order spatial derivative of the matrix to extract the curvature coefficient.

Abstract

We investigate collective excitations in flat-band superfluids by incorporating the coupled dynamics of pairing (phase and amplitude) and density fluctuations. We demonstrate that for any time-reversal symmetric superfluid system with an isolated flat band, only a single low-energy collective mode emerges in the long-wavelength limit. In contrast to the linearly dispersive Goldstone mode in conventional superfluids, this hybridized mode is gapless at zero momentum but exhibits a quadratic dispersion ($ω\propto q^2$) at small momenta. Analytically, we reveal that the dispersion coefficient of this collective mode is governed by the normal-state quantum metric of the flat band. These analytical predictions are in excellent agreement with numerical calculations. Our results are universally applicable to any generic $s$-wave flat-band superfluid, provided the flat band is energetically well separated from other dispersive bands.

Abstract

Exact tensor network contraction underpins quantum circuit simulation, quantum error correction, combinatorial optimization, and many-body dynamics. The dominant parallelization strategy, slicing, scales exponentially and incurs redundant computation. We present a multi-GPU framework that instead distributes intermediate tensors across devices with explicit communication, converting a fixed contraction path into a communication-efficient schedule via GEMM-oriented mode reordering and communication-aware mode distribution planning. Within a single DGX H100 node (8 GPUs, NVLink), distribution delivers $7$--$173\times$ extra speedup beyond embarrassingly parallel slicing, capturing nearly all of the available compute reduction (87--101%) because NVLink's high bandwidth keeps communication small relative to compute. Scaling the same four workloads to 1024 H100 GPUs over InfiniBand, the extra speedup beyond slicing ranges from $42\times$ to $67{,}869\times$, demonstrating that communication-aware distributed contraction far surpasses slicing-based scaling limits for frontier tensor networks.

Abstract

Training Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) under Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) constraints introduces severe computational limitations: classical statevector simulation memory scales exponentially ($\mathcal{O}(2^n)$), and global cost functions suffer from barren plateaus where gradient variance decays exponentially ($\mathcal{O}(1/2^n)$). This paper introduces and evaluates the Quantum Algorithm for Distributed Reduction of Entanglements (QADR), a hybrid quantum-classical machine learning framework that decomposes a global $n$-qubit VQC into localized sub-circuits operating approximately within the causal light cones of individual target qubits. QADR reduces classical simulation memory scaling from $\mathcal{O}(2^n)$ to $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot 2^{2d+1})$ for a light cone radius $d$, while naturally mitigating global barren plateaus. We benchmark QADR against standard global VQCs, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and two customized classical parameter-matched neural networks (CANN and PMNN) on the MNIST dataset and the high-dimensional NASA IMS wind turbine drivetrain diagnostic task. QADR demonstrates excellent scalability, operating successfully at $n_{\text{features}}=2000$ where standard global VQCs crash due to memory exhaustion, while matching or exceeding the performance of optimized classical architectures.
