No-Go Theorem for Ancilla-Assisted Gaussian Enhancement in Passive-Unitary Estimation

Authors: Zihao Gong, Saikat Guha
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00256v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5 · quantum measurements 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper derives a no-go theorem for quantum parameter estimation in lossless passive-unitary channels. It proves that constraining the energy only in the signal modes, while allowing infinite energy in ancilla modes, does not improve the ultimate estimation precision (QFI). The result shows that the optimal measurement is entirely determined by the energy budget allocated to the signal modes.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides fundamental limits on quantum estimation precision in passive quantum channels, which is crucial for understanding the ultimate performance of quantum sensors and quantum communication protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the maximum Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) achievable for estimating a parameter encoded in a passive-unitary transformation applied to a multimode Gaussian state.

Main result. It establishes a no-go theorem proving that allowing arbitrarily large energy in locally retained ancilla modes does not enhance the maximum QFI beyond the limit set by the signal-energy constraint.

Method. The authors calculate the QFI by deriving and bounding contributions from the covariance matrix and displacement vector, utilizing matrix inequalities and analyzing the structure of optimal probe states.

Model / system. The system models a bipartite sensing setup involving $m_A$ ancilla modes and $m_B$ signal modes, where the probe state is a pure Gaussian state evolving under a passive Gaussian unitary. The parameter is encoded via a passive Gaussian unitary acting only on the signal modes.

Key observables. Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), $ ext{F}_\varphi$; covariance matrix ($\Gamma$); mean photon number ($ar{n}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Mean photon number constraint ($ar{n}$) on the signal modes; the structure of the passive Gaussian unitary transformation.

Assumptions / limitations. The estimation process is restricted to lossless passive-unitary transformations, and the probe state is a pure Gaussian state.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the general setup involving ancilla and signal modes for the estimation process.

Paper structure. The paper analyzes the QFI by decomposing it into parts related to the covariance matrix and displacement vector, deriving upper bounds using matrix inequalities, and ultimately proving the no-go theorem by showing the optimal state requires zero signal displacement energy.

Abstract

We study the maximum quantum Fisher information (QFI) for estimating a single parameter embedded in a generic multimode lossless passive Gaussian unitary using general Gaussian probes under a signal-energy constraint. Unlike previous work, which imposed a total energy constraint on the full probe, we constrain only the transmitted signal modes while allowing an arbitrary number of locally retained ancilla modes with arbitrarily large energy. We prove that this additional freedom does not increase the maximum achievable QFI; the optimum remains identical to that attainable without extra ancilla energy. The same conclusion also extends to the sequential setting under a total energy constraint. We also characterize the family of optimal probe states and show that entanglement is not necessary to attain the optimum in the lossless setting. This extends the result of Matsubara et al. to the physically motivated signal-energy-constrained scenario and establishes a no-go theorem for ancilla-assisted Gaussian enhancement in noiseless passive-unitary estimation.

Significance of time-convolutionless mode-coupling theory in capturing the dynamics of glass-forming liquids

Authors: Michio Tokuyama

Summary. This paper rigorously compares Time-Convolutionless Mode-Coupling Theory (TMCT) against standard MCT for glass-forming liquids. It argues that the choice between time-convolution (TC) and time-convolutionless (TCL) formalisms is crucial, showing that TMCT correctly captures the dynamics across different time scales. The results indicate that TMCT yields more physically accurate predictions for the glass transition compared to ideal MCT.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous comparison of different theoretical frameworks (MCT vs. TMCT) for non-equilibrium dynamics, especially concerning the choice of mathematical formalism (TC vs. TCL), provides deep insights into the limitations of standard theoretical tools when applied to complex many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to demonstrate the superiority of the Time-Convolutionless Mode-Coupling Theory (TMCT) over conventional Mode-Coupling Theory (MCT) in accurately describing the complex dynamics of glass-forming liquids.

Main result. TMCT provides a fundamentally different and more physically consistent description of the dynamics, particularly in the intermediate and long-time regimes, leading to critical parameters that align better with experimental expectations than ideal MCT.

Method. The authors compare two projection operator methods (Time-Convolution vs. Time-Convolutionless) and apply them to derive and solve coupled equations for correlation functions, utilizing Laplace transforms and asymptotic analysis.

Model / system. The system studied is a three-dimensional equilibrium glass-forming liquid, modeled using density-density correlation functions and analyzed across different space-time scales (microscopic, kinetic, hydrodynamic).

Key observables. Intermediate scattering function $f_\alpha(q, t)$, non-Gaussian parameter $\alpha_2(t)$, self-diffusion coefficient $D_L^s$, and the non-ergodicity parameter $f_\alpha(q)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Critical volume fraction ($\phi_c$ or $\phi_g$), temperature $T$, and wavevector $q$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on comparing the full TMCT against ideal MCT, noting that approximations like (A2) lead to unphysical results ($\alpha_2(t) < 0$) in both formalisms.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the classification of basic equations into three distinct space-time scale stages: microscopic [N], kinetic [K], and hydrodynamic [H].

Paper structure. The paper reviews the theoretical basis by comparing TC and TCL projection operators, applies these to the glass-forming liquid dynamics, derives the full TMCT equation, simplifies it into a recursion equation, and finally compares its predictions against ideal MCT across various physical observables and asymptotic limits.

Abstract

This paper demonstrates the significance of the recently proposed time-convolutionless mode-coupling theory (TMCT) in capturing the dynamics of glass-forming liquids. The origin of the primary differences between ideal MCT and TMCT is comprehensively explored from a unified perspective. First, we review two distinct projection operator methods in the Heisenberg picture: the time-convolution (TC) formulation proposed by Mori and the time-convolutionless (TCL) formulation proposed by Tokuyama and Mori. We show that the appropriate choice between these frameworks fundamentally depends on the space-time scales of the relevant variables. In TMCT, the TC formulation is applied to the current density, whereas the TCL formulation is applied to the number density because the latter operates on a significantly longer space-time scale. In contrast, ideal MCT applies the TC formulation to both densities. Consequently, the governing equation in TMCT is timeconvolutionless, whereas the ideal MCT equation features a time-convolution form. This fundamental difference significantly affects various physical quantities near the glass transition. Finally, the full TMCT equation is transformed into a simplified recursion equation to facilitate numerical analysis, and the key predictions of TMCT are compared with those of ideal MCT

Surface Excitations, Energy Loss, and Decoherence in Electron Interferometry

Authors: David Kordahl
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01004v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 1/5 · interference shaping light 1/5

Summary. This paper models decoherence in electron interferometry near surfaces by linking the loss of fringe visibility to the substrate's electronic excitation spectrum (EELS). The authors show that the decoherence rate depends on the EELS spectrum weighted by the path separation, concluding that both finite temperature and retardation effects are crucial for accurate quantitative predictions. This suggests that fringe visibility can be used as a sensitive, near-field thermometer for the substrate.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a concrete, measurable link between quantum interference phenomena (visibility loss) and the continuum of excitations in a material (EELS), offering a novel, near-field thermometric probe.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper develops a mode-resolved description of decoherence in split-beam electron interferometry near dielectric surfaces and connects this process to Electron Energy-Loss Spectroscopy (EELS).

Main result. The decoherence rate is found to be a weighted integral over the differential EELS spectrum, and both retardation and finite temperature effects are necessary for quantitative agreement with experiments.

Method. The analysis involves deriving a rate equation for fringe visibility decay, relating the decoherence rate to the EELS spectrum via a path distinguishability factor.

Model / system. The system is electron interferometry using two electron trajectories separated by distance $d$ above a dielectric substrate. The substrate response is modeled using its dielectric function $\epsilon(\omega)$.

Key observables. Fringe visibility ($V$), decoherence rate ($\gamma(b, d)$), and the differential EELS spectrum.

Important parameters / regimes. Path separation ($d$), electron height ($b$), interaction length ($L$), temperature ($T$), and the substrate's dielectric function $\epsilon(\omega)$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis uses the Markovian approximation and initially considers first-order coupling to the substrate excitations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the geometry of the interaction, compare predicted visibility vs. beam height for different models (thermal vs. retarded), and show temperature dependence of visibility.

Paper structure. The paper builds from the fundamental decoherence rate calculation, derives the rate equation for visibility decay, and then compares the resulting model against established experimental results, emphasizing the necessity of thermal and retardation corrections.

Abstract

We develop a mode-resolved description of decoherence in split-beam electron interferometry near dielectric surfaces, and show how it connects to models from electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS). The central result is that the decoherence rate is a weighted integral over the differential EELS spectrum, with a factor $1-\cos(k_x d)$ encoding the path distinguishability of the two electron trajectories. Incorporating retardation and thermal occupation reproduces the macroscopic QED model of Scheel and Buhmann. We compare non-retarded vs. retarded and non-thermal vs. thermally weighted variants of the model against published results from Kerker et al., finding that both retardation and finite temperature are required for reasonable quantitative agreement. The thermal weighting suggests an application: fringe visibility as a near-field thermometric probe of the substrate, sensitive to the evanescent regime inaccessible to far-field methods.

Chutes and Ladders: Dynamical Automorphisms via the ZX-Calculus

Authors: Alexander Frei, Sascha Zakaib-Bernier, Zachary Mann, Michael Vasmer, Victor V. Albert
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02542v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5

Summary. This paper introduces a powerful graphical method using the ZX-calculus to study dynamical automorphisms in quantum error correction codes. By modeling code switching as a path in the space of stabilizer codes, the authors provide a systematic way to construct these automorphisms. This machinery allows for the theoretical design and search for implementations of complex logical quantum gates.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical framework connecting gauge fixing, graph theory, and code structure provides powerful tools for understanding the underlying symmetries and computational capabilities of quantum error-correcting codes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a machine-interpretable method for constructing dynamical automorphisms for quantum error correction (QEC) codes, particularly for handling dynamical stabilizer codes.

Main result. The authors show that dynamical automorphisms can be constructed by viewing code switching as a closed loop in the space of stabilizer codes, which they term 'chutes and ladders' relative to local Clifford operations.

Method. The core method extends the ZX-calculus to model gauge fixing, which is used to implement these paths in the space of stabilizer codes by combining gauge-fixing steps.

Model / system. The work focuses on stabilizer codes, represented by their ZX-encoding graphs, and extends this framework to subsystem codes and dynamical codes. The mathematical tools involve the ZX-calculus, graph theory, and symplectic representations.

Key observables. Dynamical automorphisms, logical gates (e.g., logical S or phase gate), and the structure of the Local Clifford (LC) orbit graph.

Important parameters / regimes. The code parameters $[[n, k, d]]$ (e.g., the 7-qubit code), and the local Clifford group elements.

Assumptions / limitations. The method relies on the connection between measurement-based code-switching and gauge fixing, and the ability to define a unique normal form for ZX-encoding graphs.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate ZX-encoding graphs, showing how gauge fixing creates closed loops (dynamical automorphisms) in the space of stabilizer codes, and demonstrating the implementation of specific logical gates.

Paper structure. The paper systematically develops the ZX-calculus formalism for subsystem codes, establishes a unique normal form for these graphs, and then applies this framework to show that gauge fixing generates dynamical automorphisms that can implement desired logical gates.

Abstract

The ZX-calculus is a powerful graphical language for manipulating quantum circuits, which has recently found many applications in quantum error correction. We extend this language to handle Floquet and other dynamical stabilizer codes via the connection between measurement-based code switching and gauge fixing (arXiv:1810.10037). We combine gauge-fixing steps to implement a closed loop in the space of stabilizer codes, returning to the original codespace up to a logical Clifford gate. These measurement-based paths in the space of stabilizer codes can be viewed as shortcuts, or "chutes and ladders", relative to single-qubit Clifford operations and qubit permutations. This yields a machine-interpretable method for constructing dynamical automorphisms and facilitates the search for implementations of desired logical gates. As an example, we implement a logical phase gate via distance-preserving code switching for the seven-qubit code bare code (arXiv:1702.01155), which has no non-trivial logical Clifford gates based on single-qubit Clifford operations and qubit permutations (arXiv:2409.18175).

Computational Phase Transitions in Binary Compressed Sensing: Quantum Annealing Inside the Relaxation Gap

Authors: William Hahn, Natalia Romero
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00806v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores quantum annealing for solving sparse signal recovery problems in compressed sensing, focusing on a regime called the 'relaxation gap'. The authors report that quantum annealing successfully recovers signals in this gap where even the best classical algorithm, AMP, fails. While the advantage is shown empirically for small systems, the work suggests a potential role for quantum tunneling in solving classically intractable optimization barriers.

Why it may be interesting. The work directly applies quantum computation (quantum annealing) to a hard optimization problem (sparse recovery), providing empirical evidence of quantum advantage in a regime where classical methods are theoretically optimal, which is highly relevant to quantum algorithms for optimization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the computational phase transition boundary in binary compressed sensing, specifically identifying a regime where quantum annealing outperforms all tested classical recovery methods.

Main result. At a specific point in the 'relaxation gap' ($n=32$), quantum annealing achieved exact signal recovery (7%) where the best classical solver (AMP) failed entirely (0%), suggesting a quantum advantage in this finite-size setting.

Method. The authors compared the performance of D-Wave quantum annealing against established classical algorithms like AMP, using energy landscape analysis to hypothesize that quantum tunneling is responsible for the observed advantage.

Model / system. The problem is formulated as recovering sparse binary signals ($\mathbf{x} \in \{0, 1\}^n$) from measurements ($\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}$), mapped onto a QUBO Hamiltonian solved on a D-Wave quantum annealer.

Key observables. Exact recovery rate (%), QUBO ground state energy, and comparison against the Bayes-optimal classical threshold.

Important parameters / regimes. The critical regime is the 'relaxation gap' (below the Donoho-Tanner $\ell_1$ threshold), with specific tested parameters being $n=32, k=5, m/n=0.19$.

Assumptions / limitations. The observed advantage is preliminary, finite-size evidence, and the mechanism relies on the hypothesis that quantum tunneling overcomes local minima trapping classical search.

Figures summary. Figures map the phase transition boundaries and compare recovery rates across different system sizes ($n=32$ vs $n=64$), highlighting the quantum advantage in the gap region.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the problem in compressed sensing, details the comparison between quantum and classical solvers, presents empirical results showing quantum advantage in the gap, analyzes the energy landscape, and concludes by outlining the limitations and need for larger-scale confirmation.

Abstract

We map the computational phase transition boundary in binary compressed sensing and identify a regime where D-Wave's quantum annealer recovers signals in a region where all tested classical methods fail, including Approximate Message Passing (AMP), which achieves the Bayes-optimal recovery threshold asymptotically for Gaussian matrices. In 19,775 experiments (n in {32, 64}, nine classical solvers, two D-Wave modes), we find that quantum annealing recovers sparse binary signals in the relaxation gap -- the regime below the Donoho-Tanner l1 phase transition where the l0 solution exists but convex relaxations fail. At n=32, k=5, m/n=0.19, D-Wave achieves 7% exact recovery while AMP and eight other solvers score 0% across 250 combined trials (Fisher exact p=0.018). At n=64, embedding overhead limits the QPU, but D-Wave's hybrid solver remains competitive with AMP. Energy landscape analysis reveals that the QUBO ground state contains the true signal, but incorrect solutions occupy shallower local basins that trap classical search -- a structure consistent with quantum tunneling dynamics. To our knowledge, this constitutes preliminary finite-size evidence that quantum annealing succeeds in a narrow regime where all tested classical methods, including the Bayes-optimal AMP, fail within a well-characterized combinatorial inference problem. Confirmation at larger n, higher trial counts, and with stronger classical controls remains an open problem.

Defect Holonomy Near Rank-Deficient Mixed States

Authors: Yu-Huan Huang, Xu-Yang Hou, Hao Guo
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02343v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work analyzes the geometry of mixed quantum states near rank-changing singularities, identifying these points as geometric defects. The authors show that while local curvature diverges path-dependently, the global topological invariant—the Uhlmann holonomy—remains robust. This holonomy provides a continuous classification of the defect, generalizing concepts from Berry phase physics to mixed systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful geometric tool (holonomy) to characterize singularities in the space of quantum states that are robust against local perturbations, which is crucial for understanding quantum phase transitions or decoherence effects.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the geometric structure of mixed quantum states near points where the rank of the density matrix changes, treating these singularities as effective geometric defects.

Main result. The robust geometric information is encoded not in the path-dependent Uhlmann curvature, but in the global, asymptotically robust Uhlmann holonomy around the defect, which is classified by a continuous, non-quantized variable.

Method. The authors use the Uhlmann connection and holonomy, generalizing Berry phase concepts to mixed states, by analyzing the topology of the punctured state manifold.

Model / system. The analysis is performed on the manifold of mixed quantum states ($ ho$), using an exactly solvable qutrit model ($ ext{SU}(2)$ sector) as a primary test case.

Key observables. Uhlmann holonomy, Wilson loop variable $W(C) = ext{Tr} U(C)$, and the Uhlmann curvature $\lVert F_U Vert$.

Important parameters / regimes. Spectral ratios of vanishing eigenvalues ($\zeta$), and the scaling exponents ($\alpha_a$) governing eigenvalue approach to the singularity.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis requires restricting to the full-rank sector and working on the punctured manifold $M^ imes$ to define the holonomy.

Figures summary. Figures schematically illustrate the punctured manifold $M^ imes$ and the non-trivial fundamental group $\pi_1(M^ imes) \simeq \mathbb{Z}$. They also plot the continuous dependence of the Wilson loop variable $W(C)$ on spectral ratios and the power-law scaling of the curvature magnitude.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the singularity at rank-deficient points, contrasts the path-dependent curvature singularity (Theorem 2) with the robust asymptotic holonomy (Theorem 1), and derives the continuous classification of the defect via the Wilson loop variable.

Abstract

We investigate the geometry of mixed quantum states near rank-changing points, showing that these singularities function as effective geometric defects. The Uhlmann connection is well-defined only on the full-rank sector of the density-matrix manifold, while rank-deficient states form singular boundary strata where the bundle structure degenerates. By restricting to a punctured state manifold that excludes the singular set, we obtain a well-defined gauge structure and identify an asymptotically robust invariant: the Uhlmann holonomy around noncontractible loops encircling the defect. In an exactly solvable qutrit model, a restricted submanifold emerges on which the connection is locally flat yet carries nontrivial monodromy, analogous to flat connections with Aharonov--Bohm-type transport. The holonomy depends only on the ratios of the vanishing eigenvalues under frozen radial dependence of the eigenbasis geometry and a fixed angular loop. In contrast, the Uhlmann curvature may diverge path-dependently when eigenvalues shrink with distinct powers, with a leading spectral-prefactor scaling law, establishing that the holonomy survives as a universal asymptotic invariant while the curvature remains non-universal. Within the effective SU(2) defect sector, the conjugacy class of the holonomy, equivalently the Wilson loop variable, provides a continuous, non-quantized classification of the asymptotic monodromy surrounding the rank-deficient defect. This non-quantization does not imply a lack of robustness: the asymptotic holonomy is protected by the topology of the punctured manifold and is insensitive to smooth deformations of the loop or the radial profile.

Dynamics of the Density Cube

Summary. This theoretical paper introduces 'Density Cube Theory' to model quantum dynamics beyond standard pairwise interference by using a three-index density cube. The authors derive the evolution equation from quantizing ternary Nambu dynamics, predicting that triple-path interference terms exhibit oscillatory behavior. This work suggests a new framework for understanding higher-order quantum correlations.

Why it may be interesting. This work proposes a fundamental extension to quantum dynamics that models higher-order interference effects, which is highly relevant for theoretical investigations into quantum gravity or non-standard quantum foundations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to extend standard quantum mechanics formalism beyond pairwise path interference to incorporate and describe triple-path interference.

Main result. The authors derive an equation of motion for the density cube based on quantizing ternary Nambu dynamics, finding that pairs of triple-path interferences oscillate into each other.

Method. The formalism involves extending the density matrix to a three-index 'density cube' ($ ho_{ijk}$) and deriving its dynamics using a quantum analog of the Nambu bracket.

Model / system. The model is the 'Density Cube Theory,' which describes quantum states using a three-index tensor ($ ho_{ijk}$). The dynamics are governed by a structure derived from ternary Nambu dynamics, requiring the use of a specific 'Kawamura Product' to maintain algebraic consistency.

Key observables. Triple-path interference elements ($ ho_{ijk}$ with distinct indices) and the conservation of the trace ($ ext{Tr} ho = 1$).

Important parameters / regimes. The system is analyzed for $N$-level systems, with specific calculations shown for $N=3$ and $N=4$.

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics are assumed to follow the form $d ho/dt = i[ ho, H_1, H_2]$, and the authors adopt the Kawamura Product to bypass full associativity requirements.

Figures summary. Not specified (The notes only mention equations and structural results, not figure content).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the density cube formalism, derives the equation of motion from quantized ternary Nambu dynamics, calculates the structure constants for the resulting ternary algebra, and finally demonstrates the oscillatory behavior of the triple-path interference terms.

Abstract

Density cube theory extends the canonical quantum density matrix $ρ_{ij}$ with the addition of an extra index to $ρ_{ijk}$. The elements of the density cube with two different indices, $ρ_{iij}$ and $ρ_{ijj}$, correspond to the real and imaginary parts of the off-diagonal element $ρ_{ij}$ of the density matrix and describe double-path interference, while those with three different indices describe non-canonical triple-path interference. In this letter, we propose an equation of motion for the density cube, obtained from the quantization of ternary Nambu dynamics, and find that pairs of triple-path interferences oscillate into each other.

Excitonic-Superconducting Coexistence and Emergent Nematic Superconductivity Driven by Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking

Authors: Fei Yang, Ruigang Li, Junwei Liu, Binghai Yan
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01785v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical work demonstrates that the competition between excitonic and superconducting orders can lead to a novel coexistence regime driven by Fermi surface mismatch. This coexistence forces the superconducting state to inherit an anisotropic structure, resulting in intrinsic nematic superconductivity even without external symmetry-breaking fields. The findings suggest a general mechanism for generating electronic nematicity in correlated quantum materials.

Why it may be interesting. The mechanism of generating intrinsic nematicity purely from the competition between two competing many-body orders (EI and SC) is a general concept applicable to understanding anisotropic electronic states in correlated materials, which is highly relevant to modern condensed matter theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the competition and coexistence between Excitonic Insulating (EI) and Superconducting (SC) orders, which are generally considered mutually exclusive instabilities.

Main result. The intrinsic mismatch between electron and hole Fermi surfaces stabilizes a finite-momentum excitonic order, which reconstructs the pairing phase space to allow for the coexistence of EI and SC states, leading to emergent nematic superconductivity.

Method. The study employs a self-consistent mean-field theory, solving coupled gap equations for both SC and EI order parameters.

Model / system. The model considers correlated quantum materials, using a two-band Hamiltonian approach that incorporates both superconducting pairing and interband Coulomb interactions driving the excitonic instability.

Key observables. Superfluid density tensor components ($ ho_{s,xx}, ho_{s,yy}$), EI gap ($\Delta_{EI}$), SC gap ($\Delta_{SC}$), and the excitonic ordering wave vector ($\mathbf{q}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Carrier density ($n$), temperature ($T$), and the degree of Fermi surface mismatch.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on mean-field approximations and often assumes an isotropic parabolic band structure for initial simplicity, though extensions consider intervalley effects.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the schematic pairing on mismatched Fermi surfaces, the momentum-resolved SC correlation, and the superfluid density tensor components showing anisotropy ($ ho_{s,xx} > ho_{s,yy}$) in the coexistence regime.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by establishing the theoretical framework (Hamiltonian, gap equations), analyzing the role of Fermi surface mismatch in stabilizing finite-momentum excitonic order, demonstrating the resulting phase space reconstruction, and finally calculating the anisotropic superfluid response as the signature of nematic coexistence.

Abstract

Excitonic insulating (EI) and superconducting (SC) orders are generally regarded as mutually exclusive electronic instabilities. Within a self-consistent microscopic theory, we study electronic systems hosting an EI phase in the presence of SC pairing and show that an intrinsic mismatch between electron and hole Fermi surfaces fundamentally reshapes this competition. This mismatch stabilizes FFLO-like electron-hole pairing and drives spontaneous symmetry breaking of the EI state. The resulting symmetry breaking reconstructs the pairing phase space for SC and EI state, such that different regions of the Fermi surface complementarily support either EI or SC correlations, leading to a natural coexistence of the two orders. Notably, the emergent SC state consequently breaks rotational symmetry and develops intrinsic nematic superconductivity, even in the absence of explicit symmetry-breaking fields (such as magnetic fields, spin-orbit coupling, or bare band-structure anisotropy). Our results suggest that candidate materials such as monolayer 1T$'$-MoTe$_2$ and the square-net semimetal NaAlSi may provide promising platforms for observing this phenomenon. More broadly, these findings reveal a unique mechanism by which competing many-body orders generate electronic nematicity, suggesting a broader route toward spontaneous anisotropic electronic states in correlated quantum materials.

Abstract

This paper studies the Fourier spectral method (FSM) for the Schrödinger equation with singular potentials $V \in H^{s}$, where $s > \max\{d/2-2,-1\}$ and $d$ denotes the spatial dimension. This setting includes a broad class of singular potentials, such as the 3D Coulomb potential and the 1D Dirac-delta potential. First, we combine the Feshbach-Schur map with a refined perturbation argument to derive sharp convergence orders for FSM, yielding order $2s+2$ for eigenvalues and order $s+1$ for eigenfunctions in the $H^1$ norm. More importantly, the $H^1$ error with respect to the projected eigenfunction converges with a higher order $s+1+b$, where $b=\min\{s+2-d/2-\varepsilon,\; s+1,\; 2\}>0$ for arbitrarily small $\varepsilon>0$, revealing a super-convergence phenomenon. Second, in the presence of potentials with isolated point singularities, we develop an asymptotic-recovery (AR) technique to post-process the FSM solutions. The resulting method, dubbed AR-FSM, fully exploits the super-convergence property and achieves convergence orders $2s+2+2b$ for eigenvalues and $s+1+b$ for eigenfunctions in the $H^1$ norm, while the AR post-processing requires only a computational cost that is linear in the number of FSM degrees of freedom. The analysis introduces a rigorous definition of point singularities and develops a foundational framework for their study. It further establishes an asymptotic expansion of eigenfunctions consisting of a regular component in $H^{s+4}$ together with $d+1$ asymptotic functions associated with each singular point. Numerical experiments confirm the sharpness of these theoretical bounds.

Abstract

Compile-time optimization is important for improving the efficiency and reliability of quantum circuits on current noisy hardware. While many existing methods simplify circuits using structural patterns or quantum-state information, most of them target only unitary circuits and do not support dynamic circuits with mid-circuit measurements and classical feedforward. In this work, we present Branch-Aware Quantum Constant Propagation (BQCP), a compile-time analysis for dynamic circuits. BQCP extends Quantum Constant Propagation (QCP) by tracking the classical information produced by mid-circuit measurements together with the corresponding post-measurement quantum states across different execution branches. This enables path-sensitive reasoning inside conditional blocks and more precise information propagation than QCP. To keep the analysis scalable, we bound both the size of the quantum-state representation and the number of tracked branches. Using the information inferred by the analysis, we apply semantics-preserving simplifications to circuit operations. We prove the soundness of both the analysis and the simplifications. Experimental results on both application-driven and synthetic benchmarks show that, on dynamic circuits, our method consistently achieves larger reductions than other existing passes including QCP.
