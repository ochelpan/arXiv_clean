Abstract

We report light emission from the moire superlattice of a twisted bilayer of tungsten diselenide (WSe2/WSe2) encapsulated in insulating hexagonal boron nitride (hBN). The low-temperature photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy reveals signatures of moire-potential induced strong interlayer excitonic emission and phonon-assisted recombination, while the twisting significantly suppresses the emission from localized defect-bound excitons. The moire potential redistributes carriers into indirect valleys, thereby enhancing recombination efficiency and stabilizing the interlayer excitons. Our findings establish that precise control of twist angle and dielectric environment provides a new route for engineering excitonic systems for exploring exciton-phonon interactions and associated quantum phenomena in transition metal dichalcogenides.

Summary. This work presents a novel, Python-native, multi-GPU framework built on PyTorch Distributed to accelerate micromagnetic simulations. By efficiently distributing the computational mesh, the authors achieve substantial speedups in calculating critical effective fields like the demagnetization field. This advancement allows researchers to model larger and more complex spintronic devices previously out of reach due to computational limitations.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on classical micromagnetics, the core contribution is a highly optimized, portable, and scalable numerical framework using modern deep learning infrastructure (PyTorch Distributed) for solving complex partial differential equations, which is highly relevant for simulating quantum many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is to accelerate computationally intensive finite difference micromagnetic simulations, which are essential for studying nanomagnetism and spintronics.

Main result. The authors achieved significant speedups, reporting up to a 7.0x acceleration for demagnetization field calculations across 8 GPUs via NVLink, and a 6.8x speedup on CPU using NUMA pinning.

Method. They extended the Python-native FDM solver magnum.np by integrating PyTorch Distributed to enable efficient, multi-GPU computation across sharded simulation meshes.

Model / system. The simulations model micromagnetism using the Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation, applied to systems like permalloy magnets and Pt/Gd/Co/Ni stacks.

Key observables. Simulation time per iteration step, calculated effective fields (demagnetization, exchange), and magnetization components ($M_z$, in-plane components).

Important parameters / regimes. The simulation size can reach $23.6$ million cells; performance is benchmarked across various hardware, including H100 GPUs and multi-socket CPUs.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes the simulation mesh can be sharded along one axis, and performance scaling is limited by communication overheads like halo exchange latency.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the schematic of the distributed 3D FFT implementation, comparisons of simulated magnetization components for standard problems, and performance scaling plots comparing GPU vs. CPU execution times.

Paper structure. The paper details the extension of the solver using PyTorch Distributed, presents performance benchmarks across different effective field calculations (demagnetization, exchange), and validates the results against established solvers like Mumax3.

Abstract

Micromagnetic simulations are essential tools in nanomagnetism and spintronics research. Although widely adopted solvers like Mumax3 and the Python-native magnum.np use GPU acceleration to improve performance, these tools are limited to single-device computation. In this work, we present the first Python-native multi-GPU micromagnetic framework by extending magnum.np with PyTorch Distributed. This leverages high-speed communication and computation across multiple GPUs while retaining the benefits of ease of installation, platform-agnostic design, and compatibility with Python. For computationally intensive demagnetisation effective-field calculations, we achieve a 7.0x speedup across 8 GPUs connected via NVLink, whereas Halo exchange required for Heisenberg exchange shows limited scaling due to kernel dispatch latency. We also demonstrated the framework's versatility by achieving a 6.8x speedup in demagnetisation field computation on CPU with NUMA pinning via the MPI backend of PyTorch Distributed. Faster turnaround times will enable researchers to explore larger, more complex systems and accelerate the design cycle for novel spintronic devices.

Rogue waves from noise-induced modulational instability of a plane wave

Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01406v1

Summary. This paper numerically studies rogue wave formation in the 1D-NLSE under noise-induced modulational instability. It systematically analyzes these extreme events by comparing them against known exact solutions, finding that the dynamics are best described by the collision of a few nonlinear modes. The results suggest that RWs arise from the synchronization of these underlying nonlinear structures.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to the study of open quantum systems and quantum optics because the 1D-NLSE is a canonical model for nonlinear wave propagation, and the study of extreme events like rogue waves provides insights into nonlinear energy localization and wave-wave interactions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the formation and characteristics of rogue waves (RWs) that emerge during the nonlinear stage of noise-induced modulational instability (MI) in a plane wave.

Main result. The study shows that RWs are significantly more numerous than in linear systems, and their dynamics are best approximated by the collision of two nonlinear modes, such as solitons or breathers.

Method. The analysis combines numerical simulations of the 1D-NLSE with rigorous comparisons of the observed RWs against known exact solutions derived via the Inverse Scattering Transform (IST).

Model / system. The primary model is the one-dimensional nonlinear Schrödinger equation (1D-NLSE) of the focusing type. The analysis compares RWs generated from noise-induced MI against various exact solutions, including multi-soliton and multi-breather collision models.

Key observables. Frequency of RW occurrence (monitored via the fourth-order moment $\kappa_4$), the intensity distribution of RWs, and the deviation measure $D_\Lambda$ used to quantify the fit quality between observed RWs and theoretical models.

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis depends on the initial noise spectrum (Gaussian vs. MI spectrum) and the parameters defining the best-fit models, such as soliton/breather eigenvalues ($\lambda_n$) and collision parameters.

Assumptions / limitations. The core hypothesis tested is that RWs emerge due to the synchronization of only a few nonlinear modes (solitons or breathers) within the complex wavefield, although this is noted as potentially naive.

Figures summary. Figures compare the Probability Density Functions (PDFs) of intensity and phase slope across different spectral regimes (MI vs. Gaussian) and show nested rectangular regions used for calculating the deviation measure $D_\Lambda$ during model fitting.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the physical model (1D-NLSE) and the phenomenon (RWs from MI) to developing advanced mathematical tools (IST, dressing method) and finally comparing the numerically observed RWs against a comprehensive database of known exact solutions (soliton/breather collisions).

Abstract

In the framework of the one-dimensional nonlinear Schrodinger equation (1D-NLSE) of the focusing type, the present paper studies numerically rogue waves (RWs) that emerge in the nonlinear stage of noise-induced modulational instability of a plane wave. For a large ensemble of simulations, all sufficiently large local maximums of the wavefield (i.e., RWs) are systematically collected and analyzed. It is shown that the frequency of occurrence of such maximums begins to increase from zero at the time, when the fourth-order moment of amplitude reaches its first (largest) local maximum, and then grows in an oscillatory manner approaching its asymptotic value at long time. Near the statistically stationary state, the 1D-NLSE generates a much larger number of RWs than a comparable linear system, but, in average, one RW affects a much smaller spatiotemporal area. The distribution of these RWs by maximum intensity represents an exponential-like function without noticeable changes in behavior, indicating a similar origin for RWs of significantly different amplitudes. The collected RWs are compared within a sufficiently large spatiotemporal window with nine exact solutions, of which two models reproduce RW dynamics much better than the others: a general collision of two solitons and a general collision of two Tajiri-Watanabe breathers; RWs are fitted with these collisions using pre-compiled databases of such solutions. The parameters of the two models turn out to be quite similar to the direct scattering transform spectrum of the whole wavefield, supporting a hypothesis that RWs may emerge due to synchronization of a few nonlinear modes (solitons or breathers) in presence of many other nonlinear modes of the wavefield.

Summary. This work theoretically investigates heat conduction in 2D amorphous carbon by coupling structural disorder ($q_3$) with quantum statistics. Using the Landauer formalism, the authors show that quantum effects significantly suppress thermal conductivity compared to classical models. The findings establish that the overall degree of amorphization is the dominant factor controlling heat flow in these disordered quantum materials.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous quantum mechanical treatment of heat transport in amorphous solids, particularly the comparison between quantum and classical limits, provides insights into energy dissipation mechanisms in disordered quantum materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how structural topology, degree of amorphization, and quantum statistics govern the thermal transport in two-dimensional amorphous solids.

Main result. Quantum thermal conductivity is significantly lower than classical predictions, and the thermal transport is primarily governed by the global degree of amorphization ($q_3$) rather than specific microscopic structural details.

Method. The authors employ a fully quantum mechanical framework using the Landauer formalism and Green's function techniques to calculate thermal conductivity ($\kappa$).

Model / system. The physical system is two-dimensional monolayer amorphous carbon (MAC). Configurations are generated using various amorphization algorithms (e.g., Zachariasen-type networks) and characterized by the local bond order parameter, $q_3$.

Key observables. Quantum Thermal Conductivity ($ ext{TC}$), Participation Ratio ($ ext{PR}$), Local bond order parameter ($q_3$), and vibrational mode characteristics (propagons, diffusons, locons).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature ($T$), degree of amorphization ($q_3$), and the ratio of classical to quantum conductivity ($\kappa_{cl}/\kappa_q$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the Landauer formalism and assumes the system can be modeled by partitioning it into a central amorphous region and semi-infinite crystalline reservoirs.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate representative MAC structures, the dependence of $\kappa$ on $T$ and $q_3$, and comparisons between quantum and classical conductivity predictions.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes structural characterization (using $q_3$ and bond statistics), vibrational mode analysis (using PR and polarization spheres), and finally calculates $\kappa(T)$ by comparing quantum (Bose-Einstein) and classical limits.

Abstract

We investigate the quantum thermal conductivity (TC) of two-dimensional monolayer amorphous carbon (MAC). We employ three distinct amorphization algorithms to generate various possible MAC configurations, ranging from Zachariasen-type continuous random networks to nanocrystallites embedded in random networks. The local bond order parameter, q3, is used to quantify the amorphousness of the structures, and TC is computed as functions of q3 and temperature. This framework enables us to assess how structural topology, degree of amorphization, and quantum statistics contribute to heat conduction in a two-dimensional amorphous solid. At room temperature, TC values are predicted to range between 3.5 to 10 W/m/K, in agreement with recent experiments. Analysis of vibrational modes reveals that, while the modes of these 2D amorphous structures fall into the usual categories, namely, propagons, diffusons, and locons, their polarization characteristics display distinct behavior. Owing to the fully quantum mechanical framework, we examine both low- and high-temperature characteristics of this 2D amorphous system. By examining the classical limit, we show that classical treatments substantially overestimate the TC of MAC; namely, the quantum TC is less than half of the classical value at room temperature and up to nearly an order of magnitude lower at low temperatures.

Abstract

Ferroelectric materials have the unique ability to maintain an electric polarization which can be reversed under an external applied electric field. This property makes them valuable for applications such as non-volatile random-access memories, transducers, actuators and electro optic modulators. Recently, emergent unconventional ferroelectricity has been demonstrated in moiré superlattices of bilayer graphene and hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) hosting non centrosymmetric stacking order. Whether this phenomenon is also present in noncentrosymmetric single layer graphene (SLG)-hBN moiré superlattices is still under debate. Here we demonstrate a ferroelectric response in an SLG-hBN moiré superlattice. Through Hall measurements, we pinpoint the origin of the hysteretic behavior to abnormal charge screening due to the moiré superlattice band and estimate the spontaneous polarization magnitude in the moiré superlattice structure. Temperature dependent measurements confirm that the hysteretic behavior persists from 2K up to room temperature, opening opportunities for high-mobility, ultrathin non-volatile devices

Universal Right-Hand Chirality of Evanescent Vector Fields

Authors: Yilin Qing, Ji Zou, Yi-Pu Wang, Gerrit E. W. Bauer, Tao Yu
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01585v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: exotic spin models in light-matter 1/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 1/5

Summary. This work establishes a universal physical law stating that any source-free, evanescent vector field must possess a fixed, right-handed chirality defined by the relationship between its propagation direction, spin, and surface normal. The authors derive a fundamental upper bound for this chirality, demonstrating that the absence of left-handed configurations underscores a deep physical non-equivalence between mirror-image wave states.

Why it may be interesting. The concept of universal chirality locking in fundamental wave phenomena (like surface waves) provides a powerful, model-independent diagnostic tool for distinguishing between source-free physical regimes and those containing internal sources or higher-order multipoles.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To determine the general conditions under which an evanescent vector field possesses definite chirality and to prove a universal upper bound for this chirality.

Main result. The paper proves that any source-free, evanescent vector field must exhibit a rigid, right-handed chirality ($C_q > 0$), bounded by $C_q \le \sqrt{1 - \eta^2}$.

Method. The derivation uses the source-free condition ($ abla \cdot \mathbf{V} = 0$) applied to time-harmonic evanescent plane wave modes to constrain the relationship between propagation ($\hat{\mathbf{q}}$), spin ($\hat{\mathbf{S}}$), and surface normal ($\hat{\mathbf{n}}$).

Model / system. The analysis focuses on source-free, time-harmonic evanescent vector fields, applicable to various physical systems like surface plasmons, spin waves, and acoustic waves at interfaces.

Key observables. The chirality index, $C_q \equiv \hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot (\hat{\mathbf{S}} imes \hat{\mathbf{q}})$, and the spin-orbit parameter, $\eta = \hat{\mathbf{q}} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{S}} / |\mathbf{q}||\mathbf{S}|$.

Important parameters / regimes. The chirality index $C_q$ is bounded by $\sqrt{1 - \eta^2}$; the analysis is simplified under the approximation $eta \ll |\mathbf{q}|$.

Assumptions / limitations. The field must be source-free ($ abla \cdot \mathbf{V} = 0$) and time-harmonic ($e^{-i\Omega t}$).

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the universal right-handed rule and the general bound $0 \le C_q \le \sqrt{1 - \eta^2}$. Table I summarizes the chirality index for various prototypical evanescent waves, confirming the universal chirality.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the physical problem of chirality in evanescent waves, derives the mathematical constraints using the source-free condition, proves the universal right-handed nature and the upper bound, and discusses the physical implications of this chirality.

Abstract

A locking between propagation direction $\hat{\bf q}$, spin $\hat{\bf S}$, and surface or interface normal $\hat{\bf n}$ -- a phenomenon broadly termed \textit{chirality} -- pervades evanescent or surface waves in optics, magnetism, plasmonics, and acoustics. Yet, it is not known whether the phenomenon is universal or bound to conditions. Here, we unveil that any \textit{source-free} vector field that propagates along $\hat{\bf q}$ and is evanescent along $\hat{\bf n}$, the existence of a spin automatically enforces a rigid, right-handed locking among $\hat{\bf q}$, $\hat{\bf S}$, and $\hat{\bf n}$, that is characterized by a chirality index $C_{\bf q}\equiv \hat{\bf n}\cdot(\hat{\bf S}\times \hat{\bf q})>0$. For an arbitrary propagation direction defined by $\hat{\bf q}\cdot \hat{\bf S}\equiv η\in [-1,1]$, i.e., not necessarily transverse spin, we derive a fundamental upper bound $C_{\bf q}\le \sqrt{1-η^2}$. The universal absence of left-handed evanescent (source-less) vector fields underscores the fundamental physical non-equivalence of mirror-image configurations -- a principle echoed by parity violation, the natural excess of enantiomers in chiral molecules, and the fixed chirality of DNA.

Summary. This work develops a universal theoretical framework to calculate the domain-wall width in complex multi-sublattice magnets, unifying descriptions for ferro-, antiferro-, and ferrimagnetic orders. The key method links the DW width directly to the parameters of the long-wavelength spin-wave dispersion. The results provide quantitative agreement with atomistic simulations, offering a powerful tool for understanding magnetic textures across diverse spin systems.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on classical magnetism, the connection between macroscopic domain structure (DW width) and microscopic excitations (SW spectrum) is a fundamental problem in many-body dynamics, relevant to understanding thermal fluctuations in quantum magnetic materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to derive a universal expression for the domain-wall (DW) width in complex multi-sublattice Heisenberg magnets, applicable across different magnetic ordering types.

Main result. A universal relation, $\delta_{DW} = \sqrt{A_{eta}/\epsilon_{eta}^0}$, is proposed that successfully unifies the description of DW width across ferromagnetic, antiferromagnetic, and ferrimagnetic systems.

Method. The derivation relies on an exact theoretical connection between the DW profile and the long-wavelength spin-wave (SW) dispersion relation.

Model / system. The study focuses on multi-sublattice Heisenberg magnets, modeled using Hamiltonians incorporating Heisenberg exchange and uniaxial anisotropy, tested on lattices like rock-salt, honeycomb, and kagome.

Key observables. Domain-wall width ($\delta_{DW}$), spin-wave spectrum parameters (gaps $\epsilon_{\pm}^0$ and stiffnesses $A_{\pm}$), and the temperature dependence of $\delta_{DW}$.

Important parameters / regimes. Exchange coupling constants ($J$), anisotropy constants ($D$), temperature ($T$), and the ratio $T/T_c$.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach assumes the validity of connecting the DW profile to the long-wavelength spin-wave theory, and it analyzes the system up to the magnetic ordering transition ($T_c$).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the SW spectrum for various magnets, show the calculated DW width versus temperature, and display magnetization profiles confirming the theoretical predictions.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the problem by noting the failure of simple models, introduces the universal relation derived from SW theory, applies it to various lattice types (FM, AFM, FI), and validates the results against atomistic spin dynamics simulations, including temperature dependence.

Abstract

We propose a universal expression for the domain-wall width in generic multi-sublattice Heisenberg magnets, applicable to ferro-, antiferro-, and ferrimagnetic orders. The result follows from an exact connection between the domain-wall profile and the long-wavelength spin-wave dispersion, yielding a unified framework for describing magnetic textures across distinct ordering types. The predictions show excellent quantitative agreement with large-scale atomistic spin dynamics simulations over a broad range of exchange and anisotropy values and spin multi-sublattice structures, including three-dimensional rock-salt-type magnets and two-dimensional honeycomb and kagome ferromagnets. Moreover, we establish a general microscopic foundation for the temperature dependence of the domain-wall width. Our approach offers a powerful tool for understanding domain-wall profiles in complex spin systems.
