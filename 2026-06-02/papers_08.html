Electron vs. hole doping in infinite-layer nickelates: electronic structure, magnetism and correlations

Authors: Ezra Day-Roberts, Fabio Bernardini, Harrison LaBollita, Yi-Feng Zhao, Andres Cano, Antia S. Botana
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00223v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical study investigates the electronic structure and magnetism of infinite-layer nickelates across both electron- and hole-doped regimes. Using DFT+DMFT, the authors highlight a significant asymmetry, noting that electron doping preserves antiferromagnetism while hole doping suppresses it. The work suggests that the $ ext{Ni-}d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital governs low-energy physics in both regimes.

Why it may be interesting. The focus on strongly correlated transition metal oxides, particularly the comparison between electron and hole doping asymmetries, is central to understanding high-temperature superconductivity mechanisms, a key area in condensed matter theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study re-examines the electronic structure, magnetism, and correlations in infinite-layer nickelates ($ ext{RNiO}_2$) by extending the investigation from the well-studied hole-doped regime to the less explored electron-doped regime.

Main result. The research reveals a striking asymmetry between electron and hole doping, particularly in the self-doping of the $ ext{Ni-}d_{x^2-y^2}$ band and the magnetic ground state, which remains antiferromagnetic upon electron doping.

Method. The authors employed a combination of Density-Functional Theory (DFT) and Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (DMFT) calculations, utilizing supercell and Variable Cell Approximation (VCA) methods.

Model / system. The physical system is the infinite-layer nickelate $ ext{RNiO}2$. The calculations model the electronic structure and correlation effects arising from the $ ext{Ni-}d$ orbital, considering doping variations.

Key observables. Electronic structure evolution, magnetic response (AFM vs. FM), orbital occupations, self-doping effect, and orbital-resolved mass enhancement ($m^*/m$).

Important parameters / regimes. Doping level (hole vs. electron), Hubbard U parameter, and the nature of the rare earth $R(5d)$ states.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis suggests that despite the complexity, the low-energy correlation physics is dominated by the $ ext{Ni-}d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital, potentially allowing for a single-band description.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate band structures, Fermi surface evolution at specific momenta, energy differences between magnetic states ($ ext{AFM-C}$, $ ext{AFM-G}$, $ ext{FM}$), and orbital-resolved spectral functions and self-energies across doping levels.

Paper structure. The paper systematically compares the electronic and magnetic properties under hole and electron doping, detailing the observed asymmetries in band filling, magnetic stability, and correlation strength using advanced theoretical techniques.

Abstract

The observation of superconductivity in undoped infinite-layer nickelates $R$NiO$_2$ ($R$ = rare earth) challenges our current understanding and calls for a re-examination of the underlying electronic structure of this family of materials. In this context, it is particularly important to extend the investigation of $R$NiO$_2$ compounds from the intensively studied hole-doped regime to the almost unexplored electron-doped one. Here, we use a combination of density-functional theory and dynamical mean-field theory to study the evolution of the electronic structure of infinite-layer nickelates in these two doping regimes. We find a striking asymmetry in the self-doping of the Ni-$d_{x^2-y^2}$ band due to the $R(5d)$ states: while this effect is strongly suppressed upon hole doping, electron doping instead leads to an increase in the size of the $R(5d)$ electron pockets, but without effectively hole-doping the Ni-$d_{x^2-y^2}$ band. This asymmetry has an important impact on the magnetic response as antiferromagnetism is rapidly suppressed upon hole doping, whereas it remains the ground state upon electron doping. Despite these differences, electronic correlations on both sides of the phase diagram are dominated by the Ni $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital, suggesting that a single-band description may be appropriate for infinite-layer nickelates in both the electron- and hole-doped regimes.

Evolutionary Discovery of Bivariate Bicycle Codes with LLM-Guided Search

Authors: Juan Cruz-Benito, Andrew W. Cross, David Kremer, Ismael Faro
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02418v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces an LLM-guided evolutionary search method to discover new and high-performing quantum LDPC codes, specifically Bivariate Bicycle variants. By combining generative AI with rigorous mathematical verification (like MILP), the authors efficiently explore massive code design spaces. The results confirm that this AI-assisted approach is a powerful, practical tool for advancing quantum error correction theory.

Why it may be interesting. The integration of large language models into the highly structured, combinatorial search space of quantum error correction codes represents a novel computational paradigm for theoretical quantum information science.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is the systematic and reliable discovery of high-performing quantum Low-Density Parity-Check (qLDPC) codes within vast algebraic design spaces.

Main result. The LLM-guided evolutionary workflow successfully identified 465 distinct candidate codes, including new representatives and demonstrating that the method is a practical tool for structured quantum-code discovery.

Method. The authors employed an LLM-guided evolutionary workflow where language models mutate Python programs generating code ansätze, followed by a rigorous, multi-stage validation pipeline including MILP and graph theory checks.

Model / system. The focus is on Bivariate Bicycle (BB) and perturbed Bivariate Bicycle (PBB) quantum error-correcting codes, which are defined by polynomial structures over finite fields, suitable for near-term fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Key observables. Code parameters ($n, k, d$), Figure of Merit (FOM = $kd^2/n$), and error rates (LER, $p_L$) under various noise models.

Important parameters / regimes. Block length $n \le 360$, noise probability $p$ (e.g., up to 8%), and the computational cost ($\sim 140$ hours, $\sim$US\$400).

Assumptions / limitations. The reported LERs are lower bounds on the optimal depolarizing threshold, and the comparison between CSS and PBB codes is noted to be decoder-dependent.

Figures summary. The figures summarize code parameters, performance metrics (FOM, LER), and comparison of simulation bounds versus exact MILP distances across various noise channels.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the need for better code discovery methods, introduces the LLM-guided evolutionary framework, details the staged validation pipeline (from rank computation to MILP), and concludes with performance comparisons under noise models.

Abstract

Quantum LDPC code discovery requires searching large algebraic design spaces while reliably certifying the parameters and equivalence classes of any candidates found. We introduce an LLM-guided evolutionary workflow in which language models mutate Python programs that generate bivariate-bicycle and perturbed bivariate-bicycle code ansätze. Across five campaigns, the system performed approximately 1{,}650 evolutionary iterations, screened about $2 \times 10^5$ candidate codes, and required ${\sim}140$ hours of computation and ${\sim}$US\$400 in LLM inference cost. Candidate codes are evaluated through a staged validation pipeline combining $\mathrm{GF}(2)$ rank computation, distance estimation and certification, mixed-integer linear programming, BLISS Tanner-graph deduplication, decomposability analysis, and local-Clifford equivalence checks. At block length $n \leq 360$, the workflow identifies 465 distinct candidate codes: 97 CSS bivariate-bicycle codes and 368 non-CSS perturbed variants. The CSS search recovers known high-performing codes and finds new finite-length representatives, including an indecomposable [[288,16,12]] code and higher-weight codes with up to $k = 50$ at distance $d = 8$. The non-CSS search produces perturbed codes matching the gross-code figure of merit at [[144,12,12]], along with additional high-distance candidates reported as certified values or upper bounds according to MILP status. Overall, these results show that LLM-guided program evolution can serve as a practical tool for structured quantum-code discovery when paired with independent evaluation.

Hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ symmetry and Jordan chains in a resonant ghostly three-dimensional model

Authors: Andreas Fring, Ian Marquette
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02290v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical paper studies a highly resonant, higher-derivative physical model, revealing a hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ symmetry algebra alongside a multi-Hamiltonian structure. Despite the rich algebraic structure, the authors demonstrate that the system is genuinely singular, as the expected positive-definite conserved Hamiltonian fails to materialize.

Why it may be interesting. The deep connection between hidden symmetries ($\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$), non-standard integrability structures (multi-Hamiltonian systems), and the breakdown of standard quantization techniques (positive-definite Hamiltonians) is highly relevant for understanding complex, non-linear quantum dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the hidden algebraic structure ($\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ symmetry) and the complex Jordan chain behavior in a highly degenerate, resonant, higher-derivative physical system.

Main result. The system exhibits a hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ symmetry and a multi-Hamiltonian structure, but the analysis shows that the natural higher-order conserved charge is reducible, and the positive-definite Hamiltonian mechanism fails due to the resonance.

Method. The authors analyze the classical phase-space flow using matrix diagonalization (Jordan chains) and construct the quantum symmetry algebra using intertwining operators, followed by a multi-Hamiltonian analysis.

Model / system. The model is a three-dimensional ghostly Hamiltonian realization of the fully degenerate resonant sixth-order Pais-Uhlenbeck (PU) oscillator, governed by equations of motion derived from a sixth-order differential equation.

Key observables. Jordan chains (classical and quantum), hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$-algebra, tri-Hamiltonian family ($H_1, H_2, H_3$), and the failure of positive-definite conserved charges.

Important parameters / regimes. The resonance condition leading to the sixth-order PU equation; the structure constants defining the $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ algebra.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the existence of a hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ symmetry and relies on the specific parameter choice leading to the fully degenerate resonant point.

Figures summary. Not specified.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by analyzing the classical flow and its non-diagonalizable Jordan structure, then constructs the hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ algebra and its quantum representations, followed by deriving the tri-Hamiltonian formulation and concluding with the analysis of higher-order integrals and the failure of the positive-definite mechanism.

Abstract

We investigate a three-dimensional ghostly Hamiltonian realisation of the fully degenerate resonant sixth-order Pais-Uhlenbeck oscillator. On the classical level, the phase-space flow is non-diagonalisable and decomposes into two complex-conjugate Jordan chains of length three, explaining the appearance of oscillatory solutions with secular terms. Upon quantisation, we construct intertwining operators whose quadratic combinations generate a hidden spectrum-generating $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$-algebra. The associated descendant spaces are finite-dimensional invariant subspaces carrying non-trivial Jordan structure. Although these spaces admit a natural decomposition into irreducible modules of a distinguished $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-subalgebra, this decomposition does not in general coincide with the Jordan decomposition of the Hamiltonian. We further derive a tri-Hamiltonian formulation from Lie point symmetries of the classical flow and show that the corresponding Hamiltonians are naturally encoded by the same hidden algebra. Nevertheless, unlike in the non-resonant case, no positive-definite linear combination of them generates the same dynamics. Finally, we analyse the common centraliser of the tri-Hamiltonian family in $U(\mathfrak u(2,1))$, showing that the natural higher-order candidate $Q$ is reducible and yields no independent classical or quantum integral. The model thus provides a resonant higher-derivative system in which hidden $\mathfrak{u}(2,1)$ symmetry, classical and quantum Jordan structures, and multi-Hamiltonian geometry coexist.

Hybrid Clifford Codes via Operator Algebra Quantum Error Correction and Projective Representation Theory

Summary. This paper develops a highly generalized framework for quantum error correction by defining 'Hybrid Clifford Codes' using projective representation theory and operator algebras. It extends fundamental theorems to characterize these new codes and derives necessary and sufficient conditions for their error correctability. This advances the theoretical understanding of fault-tolerant quantum computation beyond standard stabilizer codes.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a deep mathematical generalization of QEC theory, connecting it to abstract algebra (representation theory) in a way that could inspire new code constructions beyond standard Pauli stabilizer models.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to formulate and prove error correction theorems for a generalized class of codes called "Hybrid Clifford Codes," integrating concepts from projective representation theory and operator algebra quantum error correction.

Main result. The core result is the extension of the fundamental quantum error correction theorem to these new hybrid, projective representation-based codes, providing explicit formulas for code space projections and correctability conditions.

Method. The methodology relies heavily on advanced mathematical tools, including projective representation theory (analyzing cocycles and intertwiners), operator algebras, and group theory (induction/restriction of representations).

Model / system. The physical system is a quantum error correction code defined on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space $H$, utilizing a projective error model $(G, \pi)$ derived from a finite group $G$. The codes are generalizations of stabilizer and Clifford codes.

Key observables. Code distance $d(G, \pi, L, G, T_0)$, the orthogonal projection operators $P_C$ and $P_t$, and the group structure of stabilizers $S(G, \pi)(C)$ and logical operators $L(G, \pi)(C)$.

Important parameters / regimes. The finite group $G$, the projective representation $\pi$, the associated cocycle function $\sigma$, and the structure of the normal subgroup $L$.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation of projections relies on $L$ being a normal subgroup of $G$, and the final correctability theorem depends on specific commutation relations involving the error operators.

Figures summary. Not specified in the notes, but the text describes deriving explicit formulas for projection operators ($P_C, P_t$) and establishing a necessary and sufficient condition (Theorem 2) for error correctability.

Paper structure. The paper systematically reviews the mathematical preliminaries (projective representations, character theory), introduces the generalized code types (Clifford subspace, subsystem, hybrid), derives the projection formulas, and culminates in proving the generalized error correction theorem.

Abstract

Clifford codes are a natural generalization of quantum stabilizer codes based primarily on representation theory. This class of codes has previously been extended to the setting of quantum subsystem codes. We formulate a two-fold generalization of Clifford codes, for both the hybrid classical and quantum information and projective representation theory settings. This leads to new classes of hybrid subspace and subsystem Clifford codes. We extend the fundamental representation theoretic quantum error correction theorem to include these codes, based on the operator algebra quantum error correction framework. We also discuss several examples throughout the presentation, of both stabilizer and non-stabilizer type.

Joint Optimization of Qubit Leasing and Quantum Circuit Distribution

Authors: Anoushka Dey, Gaurav S. Kasbekar
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00501v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper tackles the complex JQLQCD problem of scheduling quantum circuits across multiple quantum computers by jointly optimizing qubit leasing, storage, gate execution, and qubit movement. After proving the problem is NP-complete, the authors introduce a fast and effective greedy heuristic that achieves near-optimal results, making large-scale quantum circuit deployment more computationally feasible.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a crucial, computationally tractable framework for optimizing resource utilization in large-scale, distributed quantum computing architectures, which is a major bottleneck for near-term quantum applications.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the Joint Qubit Leasing and Quantum Circuit Distribution (JQLQCD) problem, which involves optimally scheduling and allocating qubits across multiple quantum computers connected by a network.

Main result. The problem is proven to be NP-complete, but the authors propose a highly efficient greedy heuristic that performs comparably to simulated annealing while being significantly faster.

Method. The core methodology involves formulating the problem as an Integer Linear Program (ILP) and then developing and analyzing a greedy algorithm with local search refinement to solve large instances.

Model / system. The model simulates a quantum circuit executed by an agent across a set of Quantum Computers (QCs) connected by a quantum network. The system must jointly manage qubit leasing, storage location, gate execution assignment, and qubit movement (migration vs. teleportation).

Key observables. Total system cost (minimization objective), circuit makespan ($T_{max}$), and computational execution time.

Important parameters / regimes. Leasing costs ($c_{s,p}, c_{e,p}$), gate execution costs ($c_{g,p}$), communication costs (migration $d$, teleportation $E$), and the weight $eta$ assigned to the makespan.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes passive QCs providing resources to a single agent, and the complexity analysis relies on bounding the circuit tree-width ($w_t$) for fixed-parameter tractability.

Figures summary. Figures compare the total cost and execution time of Optimal, Greedy, and Simulated Annealing algorithms across varying circuit sizes, number of QCs, and makespan weights, demonstrating the greedy algorithm's superior speed.

Paper structure. The paper reviews related work, presents the comprehensive ILP formulation, proves NP-completeness via reduction from $P| ext{prec}|C_{max}$, analyzes solvable special cases, proposes the greedy heuristic, and concludes with extensive numerical performance evaluations.

Abstract

We consider an agent, who would like to execute a given quantum circuit using resources leased from a set of quantum computers (QCs) connected by a quantum network. For this purpose, the agent needs to make the following four key decisions: (i) how many qubits to lease from each QC, (ii) at which QCs to store different circuit qubits in different time slots, (iii) at which QC to execute each gate in the circuit, and (iv) how to move qubits between QCs, choosing between migration and teleportation. We refer to this problem facing the agent as the joint qubit leasing and quantum circuit distribution (JQLQCD) problem, and provide a comprehensive integer linear programming (ILP) formulation for it. We show that the JQLQCD problem is NP-complete. Next, we identify several special cases in which the problem can be optimally solved in closed form or via polynomial-time algorithms. Also, we propose a greedy algorithm with local search refinement to solve large instances of the general JQLQCD problem. Finally, we evaluate the performance of the proposed greedy algorithm using extensive numerical computations.

Learning with Active Quantum Subspaces: Scalable Hybrid Advantage without Full Quantum Data-Encoding

Authors: Jeongho Bang, Wooyeong Song, Kyoungho Cho, Taewan Kim, Yongsoo Hwang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00932v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work proposes 'active quantum subspace data-encoding' to achieve quantum machine learning advantage by only encoding the most informative parts of a large classical input into a quantum state. The authors prove that this approach avoids the exponential resource scaling of full quantum encoding while maintaining statistically efficient learning guarantees. This offers a scalable, NISQ-compatible pathway for quantum advantage.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a concrete, theoretically bounded framework for achieving quantum advantage in machine learning that is directly relevant to near-term, resource-constrained quantum hardware (NISQ).

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether quantum learning advantage can be achieved scalably without fully encoding large classical inputs into highly superposed quantum states.

Main result. The authors prove that a scalable hybrid advantage can be maintained by using active quantum subspace data-encoding, which keeps the quantum contribution low-dimensional and polynomially costly.

Method. They introduce Active Quantum Subspace Data-Encoding (AQSE) and derive structural results concerning the projected hybrid kernel, the necessary conditions for quantum improvement, and PAC sample-complexity bounds.

Model / system. The model involves partitioning an input $\mathbf{x}$ into a classical part $\mathbf{x}_C$ and an active quantum part $\mathbf{x}_Q$, encoding the quantum information using a polynomial-cost gate sequence on a subset of qubits.

Key observables. Projected hybrid kernel, projected quantum feature map $\Phi_Q(x)$, oracle reliability $eta(x)$, and sample complexity $N$.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of projected observables ($M$), the oracle reliability ($eta$), and the polynomial scaling of the encoding gate complexity ($G_{enc}(n) = ext{poly}(n)$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the projected hybrid kernel being positive semidefinite and the existence of a direction in the quantum sector orthogonal to the classical span that correlates with the residual error.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the comparison between full data quantumization and active subspace encoding, showing the projected model's superior performance and controlled effective degrees of freedom compared to global kernels.

Paper structure. The paper builds theoretically by defining the AQSE model, proving structural properties of the resulting kernel (Theorem 1), establishing the necessary and sufficient condition for quantum advantage (Theorem 2), and finally deriving the PAC sample-complexity bounds under noise (Theorem 3).

Abstract

We study whether quantum learning advantage can persist without fully embedding a large classical input into a highly superposed quantum state. To address this question, we introduce active quantum subspace data-encoding, in which only an information-bearing subset of the input is lifted to a quantum representation while the remaining variables stay classical. For this model, we define a projected hybrid readout and prove three structural results. First, the projected hybrid kernel is positive semidefinite and its sample regularized dimension is bounded by the number of projected observables, so the dimension blow-up of naive global kernels is avoided. Second, we give a necessary and sufficient criterion for improvement over a purely classical predictor in squared loss: the projected quantum sector must contain a direction that lies outside the classical feature span and correlates with the classical residual. Third, in a realizable noisy-oracle setting, we derive a PAC sample-complexity bound proportional to the inverse square of the oracle reliability. We then show, for a canonical Clifford active-subspace family under local dephasing noise, that this reliability can remain inverse-polynomial even when the encoding gate complexity grows polynomially with system size. Hence, the polynomial encoding cost does not by itself destroy the hybrid learning advantage. A sixty-four-qubit family and a synthetic contextual classification task illustrate how one projected quantum feature can compress a useful high-order interaction into a low-dimensional hybrid model. Our results generalize QRAM-free hybrid learning and provide a scalable route toward NISQ-compatible quantum advantage without full quantum data-encoding.

Magnetic control of electron scattering in silicene quantum dots

Authors: Mohamed El Azar, Elmustapha Feddi, Pablo Díaz, David Laroze, Ahmed Jellal
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01938v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work theoretically investigates how magnetic fields and intrinsic spin-orbit coupling control electron scattering within silicene quantum dots. By exploiting the SOC-induced energy gap, the authors demonstrate that confinement is significantly enhanced, leading to robust, spin-selective electron trapping. This suggests silicene is a promising platform for spintronics and quantum information applications.

Why it may be interesting. The mechanism of using intrinsic material properties (SOC) to engineer quantum confinement and spin filtering in a solid-state nanostructure is highly relevant for designing next-generation quantum devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study analyzes the mechanism of electron diffusion and achieving robust, spin-selective confinement in silicene quantum dots (SQDs) subjected to a perpendicular magnetic field.

Main result. The intrinsic spin-orbit coupling (SOC) in silicene opens an energy gap, suppressing Klein tunneling and enabling superior carrier localization and spin-selective confinement when combined with an external magnetic field.

Method. The authors derive solutions to the Dirac equation at low energy and use continuity conditions at the dot interfaces to obtain exact expressions for diffusion coefficients and scattering efficiencies.

Model / system. The system is a silicene quantum dot (SQD) modeled using the low-energy Dirac equation, incorporating the effects of a perpendicular magnetic field and the intrinsic SOC.

Key observables. Diffusion coefficients, scattering efficiency (Q), spatial probability density (rho), and probability current density (j).

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic field strength (B), SOC strength (lambda_SO), and dot radius (R).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the Dirac fermion framework and assumes the SOC gap is the dominant mechanism for overcoming Klein tunneling limitations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the scattering efficiency (Q) vs. energy/B-field, showing sharp resonances in the SOC regime compared to the weak trapping in the massless (graphene) limit. Spatial plots show localized probability and current loops.

Paper structure. The paper follows a theoretical derivation (Dirac equation solutions), formal scattering analysis (matching coefficients), and numerical investigation demonstrating the synergistic effects of the B-field and SOC.

Abstract

The Klein tunnel effect phenomenon makes it impossible to permanently confine charge carriers in massless nanostructures. However, applying a constant magnetic field allows these electrons to be temporarily localized, thus forming quasi-bound states. In this study, we analyze the mechanism of electron diffusion through a silicene quantum dot (SQD) subjected to a perpendicular magnetic field. To enhance spatial localization, we exploit the spin-orbit coupling (SOC) specific to silicene, which generates a natural energy gap by acting as an effective mass. We first derive the solutions to the Dirac equation at low energy. Subsequently, by imposing the continuity conditions at the SQD interfaces, we obtain exact expressions for the diffusion coefficients. These results are then used to map the scattering efficiency together with the spatial distributions of probability and current densities. Our simulations demonstrate that the presence of this intrinsic gap significantly enhances electron trapping at the center of the SQD. Finally, we prove that the interplay between the external field and SOC breaks spin symmetry, thereby enabling robust and spin-selective confinement.

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous mathematical re-examination of geometric quantization for the relativistic electron, building on Souriau's work. It successfully derives the Dirac equation and proves the conservation of both probability and spin currents. The work is significant for its systematic use of advanced mathematical tools like coadjoint orbits and Kaluza-Klein theory to establish fundamental symmetries.

Why it may be interesting. While highly abstract, the rigorous development of conserved currents and symmetries ($C, P, T$) using advanced geometric quantization techniques provides a deep theoretical underpinning for relativistic quantum field theory, which informs many modern quantum theories.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper revisits and rigorously justifies Jean-Marie Souriau's geometric quantization procedure applied to the relativistic electron, addressing ambiguities in the original formulation.

Main result. The authors successfully derive the Dirac equation and establish the conservation laws for both the probability and spin currents using a systematic geometric quantization framework.

Method. The core method involves applying the coadjoint orbit method, equipping the prequantum manifold with symplectic and contact structures, and utilizing Kaluza-Klein theory to systematically construct symmetries.

Model / system. The system is the relativistic particle with spin 1/2 (the electron), analyzed using the Poincaré group and formulated via quaternionic matrices and spinor representations.

Key observables. Dirac equation, conservation of probability current, conservation of spin current, and the systematic construction of $C, P, T$ symmetries.

Important parameters / regimes. Rest mass ($m_0$), spin ($ ext{spin}$), and the quantization constant $\hbar$.

Assumptions / limitations. The existence of a prequantum manifold $Y$ and the applicability of the Sommerfeld quantization condition are key assumptions.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the prequantum manifold structure for the electron and positron, and tables summarize the action of symmetry transformations ($C, P, T$) on spinors and coordinates.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by reviewing the coadjoint orbit method, establishing keystone theorems for symplectic/contact structures, applying these to the spin-1/2 case to derive the Dirac equation, and finally using Kaluza-Klein theory for symmetry construction.

Abstract

The aim of this paper is to revisit, in Souriau's book "Structure of Dynamical Systems", the chapter devoted to the geometric quantization where the justifications of important results and formulae are not given and are difficult to prove. After recalling the coadjoint orbit method and its application to the relativistic particle with spin, we state and prove two keystone theorems that allow to equip its prequantum manifold with the symplectic and contact structures. We apply them to the relativistic particle with spin 1/2, leading to the Dirac equation and the conservation of the probability current. We propose also an identity of conservation of the spin current and, invoking the Kaluza-Klein theory, a systematic construction of the symmetries of charge conjugation, parity transformation and time reversal which seems to us more convenient and readable than that of the classical presentations.

Optimized Point Addition Circuits for Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithms

Authors: André Schrottenloher

Summary. This paper presents an optimized quantum logical circuit architecture for performing point addition on elliptic curves, a critical subroutine for breaking discrete logarithm cryptography via Shor's algorithm. By redesigning the underlying modular arithmetic using a space-efficient, two-stage EEA approach, the authors achieve resource costs comparable to the best published results. This work provides a detailed blueprint for implementing complex quantum algorithms on near-term or fault-tolerant quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on cryptography, the detailed quantum circuit optimization techniques—especially the space-saving arithmetic implementations—represent advanced quantum circuit design principles applicable to any complex quantum computation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is to reduce the quantum resource cost (qubit and gate count) required to compute elliptic curve discrete logarithms, which is a major threat posed by Shor's algorithm to modern cryptography.

Main result. The authors detail a quantum logical circuit architecture for point addition that achieves results comparable to recent state-of-the-art work, offering a slight reduction in Toffoli gates compared to previous methods.

Method. The optimization involves implementing the Extended Euclidean Algorithm (EEA) in a two-step process: an Euclidean algorithm circuit followed by a Bézout reconstruction circuit to enable in-place modular multiplication.

Model / system. The model is a quantum circuit designed for point addition on elliptic curves over prime fields, specifically targeting the secp256k1 curve. The circuits operate at the logical level, abstracting away error correction details.

Key observables. Qubit count, Toffoli gate count (CCX, CCZ, And gates), and the total resource cost for running Shor's algorithm.

Important parameters / regimes. The bit-size of the curve (e.g., 256 bits), the failure probability (stated to be at most $2^{-13.3}$), and the asymptotic space complexity ($\approx 4.355n + O(\sqrt{n})$ qubits).

Assumptions / limitations. The circuits are not exact and include a failure probability. The analysis often relies on approximations, such as using only the most significant bits (MSBs) for modular arithmetic steps.

Figures summary. Table 1 compares qubit and Toffoli gate counts for point addition circuits across different methods. Table 2 compares the total resource cost for running Shor's algorithm.

Paper structure. The paper builds upon prior work by first detailing the structure of point multiplication via windowed addition. It then focuses on optimizing the core modular arithmetic components by adapting the EEA into a space-saving, two-step process (Euclidean Algorithm $ o$ Bézout Reconstruction) to achieve in-place modular multiplication.

Abstract

Shor's algorithm represents the main threat of quantum computers to cryptography. In order to precisely understand its feasibility, many authors have worked towards reducing its costs, either at the logical level (assuming a fault-tolerant architecture), or at the physical level (taking into account the constraints of envisioned hardware). In particular, recent works by Chevignard et al. (CRYPTO 2024) and Gidney (arXiv 2025) used improved arithmetic to significantly reduce the qubit cost of factoring RSA public keys. Even more recently, Babbush et al. (arXiv 2026) improved the cost of computing elliptic curve discrete logarithms, with a reduction of a factor 2 to 3 in gate count and qubit count compared to a previous work by Litinski (arXiv 2023). Their result relies on optimized point addition circuits on elliptic curves over prime fields. However they did not reveal their logical quantum circuits, relying instead on a zero-knowledge proof. In this paper, we detail a quantum logical circuit architecture which gives similar results as Babbush et al., with a slightly higher number of qubits (around 1.5% increase) and a slightly smaller Toffoli gate count (between 6.5% and 10% reduction) for the curve secp256k1. We also give gate counts for a generic variant of the circuit, which is valid for any prime field.

Abstract

A central test case for quantum linear system algorithms (QLSA) is elliptic PDEs after a finite element discretization. Most existing analyses focus on preparing a normalized solution state. But an end-to-end quantum PDE solver must also extract physical quantities of interest, such as fluxes, currents, tractions, and energy. These outputs require quantum measurement, and their observable norms may grow like $h^{-χ} $ with mesh size $h $, creating a readout bottleneck even when a quantum preconditioner reduces the condition-number dependence on $h$. We present a multilevel framework for this readout problem, motivated by the variance-reduction mechanism of multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC), which is naturally compatible with a multi-level finite element discretization. Instead of estimating the full fine-grid observable directly, the method estimates a telescoping sum of interlevel corrections, so that the fine-coarse cancellation is exposed before quantum measurement. Our algorithm is based on Schur-complement factorization of the corrected Green's operator through a Ritz-complement map. For quantities of interest with readout order $χ\leq 2$, the multilevel estimator removes the polynomial $h$-dependent readout overhead. With amplitude estimation, the remaining statistical dependence is $ \widetilde{O}(1/\varepsilon)$, i.e., Heisenberg scaling in the inference precision up to logarithmic factors and with direct sampling, the complexity is reduced to standard Monte Carlo scaling $\widetilde{O}(1/ε^2)$.

Abstract

Weyl semimetals obey a global chirality constraint: the net chiral topological charge and any associated chiral spectral flow must vanish, as required by the Nielsen-Ninomiya theorem. Under magnetic fields, this constraint manifests through counter-propagating zeroth Landau levels associated with Weyl nodes of opposite chirality. Here, we experimentally demonstrate how non-Hermiticity can reshape this balance in a synthetic photonic Weyl semimetal. Using engineered gauge fields in one-dimensional multilayer structures, we realize both homogeneous and axial magnetic fields and directly probe the resulting Landau-level spectra. While a homogeneous field produces the expected chirality-balanced zeroth Landau levels, an axial field spatially separates the compensating chiral channels: co-propagating bulk pseudo-Landau levels carry one chirality, whereas the opposite chirality resides in boundary-localized surface states. We show that radiative boundary loss selectively suppresses these surface states, removing them from the long-lived observable spectrum and producing an experimentally accessible chirality imbalance. By reducing boundary loss, we recover the hidden chiral channel and reveal its surface-state origin. These results show that non-Hermiticity, present naturally in photonics, can control and relax fundamental chirality constraints in topological systems, enabling access to otherwise forbidden spectral responses.
