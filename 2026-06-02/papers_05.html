Anisotropic Moiré Fractional Chern Insulators and Their Phase Transitions

Authors: Bo Peng, Jin-Xin Hu
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02094v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 3/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work explores how lattice anisotropy destabilizes Fractional Chern Insulator (FCI) phases in moiré materials. By tuning anisotropy, the system undergoes quantum phase transitions from a topological insulator to competing charge-density-wave or Fermi-liquid states. This establishes anisotropy as a powerful tool for engineering and exploring diverse correlated phases in moiré heterostructures.

Why it may be interesting. The mechanism of anisotropy-driven phase transitions from topological to symmetry-broken/compressible states provides a general framework for engineering correlated phases in moiré systems, relevant to understanding emergent quantum states in artificial lattices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the stability and nature of Fractional Chern Insulator (FCI) phases in moiré heterostructures when subjected to varying degrees of lattice anisotropy.

Main result. Sufficiently strong lattice anisotropy drives quantum phase transitions, destabilizing the incompressible FCI state into competing charge-density-wave (CDW) or Fermi-liquid (FL) phases.

Method. The research employs theoretical modeling using continuum Hamiltonians and analyzing key many-body observables like the structure factor and topological gap.

Model / system. The system is based on moiré heterostructures, such as twisted TMDs ($ ext{MoTe}_2$), modeled via a continuum Hamiltonian incorporating interlayer tunneling and electron-electron interactions.

Key observables. Hall conductivity ($\sigma_{xy}$), topological gap size, structure factor $S(\mathbf{q})$, and Bloch state occupation numbers ($n_k$).

Important parameters / regimes. Lattice anisotropy, controlled by interlayer momentum shift ($\gamma_q b(t)$) induced by heterostrain, or lattice stretching ($eta$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis often utilizes the single Landau Level (LL) approximation and treats the interaction via projection onto the Chern band.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the phase transition from FCI (topological gap) to CDW/FL as anisotropy increases, showing changes in $S(\mathbf{q})$ and $n_k$.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the effect of anisotropy on the FCI state, first showing robustness to moderate strain, then detailing the transition mechanism to CDW/FL states, and finally comparing these findings to anisotropic FQHE models.

Abstract

Recently, moiré heterostructures have been observed to host fractional Chern insulator (FCI) phases at zero magnetic field. In this work, we show that the FCI phases are robust against moderate lattice anisotropy, while sufficiently strong anisotropy drives quantum phase transitions from incompressible topological phases to competing charge-density-wave (CDW) and Fermi-liquid (FL) phases. Specifically, in the case of twisted transition metal dichalcogenides (such as MoTe$_2$), the anisotropy arises from an interlayer momentum shift induced by heterostrain. We find that increasing anisotropy suppresses the many-body topological gap, eventually destabilizing the fractional topological phase. Beyond a critical anisotropy value, the system gradually transitions to a symmetry-broken state characterized by stripe-like CDW order. It has a Landau Level counterpart, where effective mass anisotropy provides an additional tuning knob for the fractional quantum Hall state; sufficiently strong anisotropy drives a transition to a charge-ordered phase. Moreover, in the anisotropic ideal Chern band model with lattice stretching, a Fermi liquid phase emerges as anisotropy increases. Our results establish that anisotropy provides a direct route for engineering and exploring competing correlated phases in FCI states based on moiré materials.

Bounds on Nonlocality and Random Access Codes from Extended Information Causality Principle

Authors: Prabhav Jain, Nikolai Miklin, Mariami Gachechiladze
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.02416v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5

Summary. This paper develops an extended version of the Information Causality principle to place stronger constraints on quantum correlations and nonlocality. It applies these tools to analyze the performance limits of entanglement-assisted random access codes. While the extension yields stronger bounds in some Bell tests, it ultimately shows that for the specific RACs analyzed, the original IC bounds remain optimal.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous derivation of new, tighter bounds on nonlocality using information-theoretic principles provides fundamental constraints on physical theories beyond quantum mechanics, which is crucial for understanding the limits of quantum correlations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to derive tighter theoretical bounds on quantum correlations (nonlocality) and the success probabilities of Random Access Codes (RACs) by extending the Information Causality (IC) principle.

Main result. The extended IC principle yields new, tighter quantum Bell inequalities in certain scenarios, although for entanglement-assisted random access codes, the extended principle does not improve the bounds over the original IC principle.

Method. The authors use advanced information-theoretic techniques, including deriving quantum Bell inequalities from the extended IC statement and applying optimization methods (like KKT conditions) to bound specific expressions.

Model / system. The analysis is conducted on non-signaling correlations (Bell scenarios) and applied to the theoretical framework of entanglement-assisted random access codes (EARACs) and general communication channels.

Key observables. Quantum Bell inequalities (e.g., $ ext{Inn}_{22}$ expression), winning probabilities for RACs, and Shannon mutual information.

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis explores limits such as zero classical channel capacity ($\gamma o 0$) and varying input correlation parameters ($\epsilon$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on extending the IC principle to allow correlations among Alice's inputs, and in some parts, assumes uniform marginals or specific error channel structures.

Figures summary. Figures compare the bounds derived from the original IC, the extended IC, and other known bounds (ML, QM) across various dimensions ($d^2d$ scenario).

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the extended IC principle and deriving new Bell inequalities for general scenarios, to applying these inequalities to derive bounds on EARACs, and finally confirming the optimality of the original IC bounds for the specific EARAC case.

Abstract

Information Causality was introduced as a physical principle for constraining the set of nonlocal correlations. In recent work, we proposed an extension of Information Causality that allows correlations among Alice's inputs. This extended principle yields tighter constraints than the original formulation and recovers part of the quantum boundary in certain Bell scenarios. In this work, we further investigate the implications of extended Information Causality and apply it to scenarios beyond binary inputs and outputs. We derive a family of quantum Bell inequalities that strengthen previously known constraints on quantum correlations. Using these inequalities, we obtain an improved analytical bound for the Collins-Gisin family of Bell inequalities. We also apply Information Causality to entanglement-assisted random access codes and derive new theory-independent analytical bounds on the winning probability. For this latter task, we prove that, despite being stronger in general, the extended principle does not improve the bounds obtained from the original Information Causality principle. This suggests that the existing Information Causality bounds are optimal for this class of random access codes.

Extracting central charge from ground-state overlaps of spatially deformed Hamiltonians

Summary. This work introduces a novel technique to measure the central charge of a CFT by analyzing the ground-state overlap of a system subjected to a $q$-Möbius spatial deformation. By applying this method to critical 1D chains and 2D topological insulators, the authors show that the overlap provides a simple, robust, and universal probe of fundamental CFT data using only wave-function properties.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a powerful, purely wave-function-based diagnostic tool to probe universal CFT properties (like $c$) in complex, interacting quantum many-body systems, which is highly relevant for understanding critical points in condensed matter.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The central problem is developing a method to extract the central charge ($c$) of a $(1+1)$-dimensional Conformal Field Theory (CFT) using only ground-state overlaps derived from spatially deformed Hamiltonians.

Main result. The ground-state overlap under $q$-Möbius deformations provides a simple and robust, wave-function-based probe for the central charge in critical quantum chains and topological edge modes.

Method. The authors derive an exact overlap formula using CFT techniques applied to the $q$-Möbius deformation, and then construct a lattice estimator based on this overlap.

Model / system. The study applies to gapless 1D quantum systems, including critical chains (Ising, Potts, Heisenberg) and the edge modes of 2D Chern insulators (QWZ model). The Hamiltonian is subjected to a spatially modulated $q$-Möbius deformation.

Key observables. Central charge ($c$), ground-state overlaps ($\langle 0|0_q( heta) angle$), entanglement spectrum, and entanglement entropy ($S_A$).

Important parameters / regimes. Deformation parameters $q$ (modulation wave number) and $ heta$ (deformation strength); system size $L$.

Assumptions / limitations. The method relies on the deformation being generated by a finite conformal transformation to maintain analytical tractability.

Figures summary. Figures demonstrate the finite-size extrapolation of the effective central charge ($c_{ ext{eff}}$) for various critical chains, and show the entanglement spectrum and entropy for deformed Ising and Potts chains, as well as the 2D QWZ model.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from the CFT derivation of the overlap formula, to its lattice realization, and finally applies this estimator to calculate $c$ and analyze entanglement structures for both 1D and 2D topological models.

Abstract

We show that the conformal anomaly of a $(1+1)$-dimensional conformal field theory can be extracted directly from a ground-state wave-function overlap associated with a spatial conformal deformation. Focusing on the $q$-Möbius deformation, we derive an exact overlap formula between the deformed and undeformed ground states, whose exponent depends only on the central charge. Motivated by this result, we construct a lattice estimator based solely on ground-state overlaps and apply it to representative critical quantum chains and the gapless edge modes of a two-dimensional Chern insulator. Numerical results demonstrate that the resulting overlaps provide a simple and robust probe of the central charge in microscopic models. We further demonstrate that the deformed ground states retain universal geometric structures in their entanglement spectra and entanglement entropies. These results provide a simple wave-function-based route to probing conformal data in critical systems and topological edge modes.

Finite-Sample Selected Covariance Spectra in Classical Shadows

Authors: Masahito Hayashi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.00527v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper develops a rigorous finite-sample theory for estimating the covariance spectrum of quantum measurements reconstructed via classical shadows. It proves that for local measurement settings, the required sample size to estimate the selected covariance is independent of the total system dimension. This is a significant result for quantum tomography, as it shows that local structure can overcome the curse of dimensionality.

Why it may be interesting. It provides rigorous, quantitative tools for characterizing the statistical information contained in quantum measurements (shadows), which is crucial for understanding the limits of quantum state reconstruction.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the finite-sample estimation of selected covariance matrices derived from classical-shadow outputs in quantum measurements.

Main result. It establishes dimension-independent sample size bounds for selected covariance estimation when the measurement protocol is local (e.g., local Pauli shadows), contrasting this with protocols where the required sample size scales with system dimension.

Method. The analysis combines matrix Bernstein concentration inequalities with exact rank-one centering identities to control the error in the sample-centered empirical covariance matrix.

Model / system. The study uses the framework of classical shadows, where quantum state tomography is approximated by measuring a set of observables (Pauli strings) and reconstructing a shadow snapshot. The system is analyzed in terms of local product measurements versus global measurements.

Key observables. Selected covariance matrix ($\Sigma_l( ho)$), shadow-output vector ($\hat{x}$), and the selected one-shot radius ($B_l$).

Important parameters / regimes. The selected dimension ($l$), the local support sizes, and the local reconstruction coefficients are critical parameters determining the sample complexity.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption for the dimension-independent result is that the selected set of observables (or their associated coefficients) is bounded and local in nature (e.g., local product shadows).

Figures summary. The notes mention TABLE I comparing the work to other literature strands and TABLE II/III defining notation for local/global measurements, but no specific figure content is detailed.

Paper structure. The paper develops the theory by first defining the shadow-output vector and covariance, then proving the general finite-sample theorem, specializing to local product shadows, and finally deriving explicit, dimension-independent covariance formulas for biased local Pauli shadows.

Abstract

We study finite-sample estimation of selected covariance matrices of classical-shadow outputs. For a general shadow-output vector, we consider its covariance matrix and a fixed selected compression. Our main theorem applies to arbitrary shadow protocols and gives an operator-norm error bound for the selected sample-centered empirical covariance. When the protocol-dependent constants appearing in this bound remain independent of the ambient system size, the required sample size is also independent of the ambient dimension. The proof combines matrix Bernstein concentration, an exact rank-one centering identity, and Weyl and Davis--Kahan perturbation bounds. We verify this bounded-output condition for local measurement settings. For general local product shadow protocols with fixed local dimension, finite-weight product observables lead to bounds controlled by support sizes and local reconstruction coefficients, not by the total number of tensor factors. Hence uniform bounds on selected set size, observable weight, and local reconstruction coefficients imply dimension-independent selected covariance estimation. For biased local Pauli shadows, we evaluate the relevant bound in closed form from the selected Pauli supports and local basis-selection probabilities. We also derive an exact covariance formula governed by Pauli compatibility and inverse-probability overlap factors, showing how measurement bias affects both diagonal variances and off-diagonal statistical couplings. A comparison with global Clifford shadows shows that this dimension-independent local behavior is not automatic for every shadow protocol.

Abstract

The nitrogen vacancy (NV) center has emerged as a powerful quantum sensor in high-pressure research, with the observation of optically detected magnetic resonance at megabar pressures. However, some aspects of NV physics require further investigation to optimize the development of NV-based sensing under pressure. Here, we study both experimentally and theoretically the optical properties of the NV center under hydrostatic pressure. We also provide spectroscopic guidelines for performing high-pressure optical experiments. Our results confirm that the NV center remains a robust quantum sensor under extreme hydrostatic pressures, especially for magnetic characterization.

Summary. This paper introduces Quantum Resonance Encryption (QRE), a novel protocol for securing data in shared quantum computers. It leverages the unique, non-classical dynamics of the quantum kicked top system to scramble information. The method ensures that only authorized parties, knowing the precise resonance parameters, can recover the original data, offering robust security against eavesdropping.

Why it may be interesting. The application of quantum chaos and resonance dynamics from a physical model (kicked top) to solve a fundamental problem in quantum information security (QKD/data storage) is highly relevant for theoretical physicists studying open quantum systems and quantum computation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the critical need for ensuring data privacy and secure communication within shared quantum computing environments, protecting data even from the service provider.

Main result. A Quantum Resonance Encryption (QRE) protocol is proposed that uses quantum kicked top dynamics to scramble data, ensuring perfect recovery for authorized users while rendering intercepted states mixed to eavesdroppers.

Method. The protocol involves encoding the secret state, scrambling it using the unitary evolution of the quantum kicked top ($U_{QKT}$), and then requiring a specific recovery unitary based on the system's quantum resonance period.

Model / system. The core physical model is the quantum kicked top, whose dynamics are governed by a Floquet operator. This system is used to generate the necessary unitary transformations for encryption and decryption.

Key observables. Fidelity recurrence (comparing perfect recovery vs. wrong time/wrong parameters), and the measurement outcome of ancillary qubits for tampering detection.

Important parameters / regimes. The resonance condition $eta = 4\pi j r / s$ is crucial for ensuring periodic dynamics ($U_{QKT}^T = I$).

Assumptions / limitations. The protocol assumes the availability of quantum memory and functioning quantum networks, though it can be demonstrated on current platforms.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the schematic flow of the QRE protocol (Encode $ ightarrow$ Conceal $ ightarrow$ Store $ ightarrow$ Retrieve), security demonstrations via fidelity recurrence, and the extension to long-distance communication using multi-qubit teleportation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem, details the QRE protocol using the quantum kicked top, demonstrates its security against eavesdropping, and extends the concept to multi-qubit teleportation for long-distance communication and QKD.

Abstract

In a shared quantum computer, how to ensure data privacy and protection from access by unauthorized parties? We propose a genuine quantum protocol for protecting user's data which is not accessible even to the service provider. The protocol is based on quantum kicked top -- the dynamics of a spin system --operating at quantum resonance regime. This protocol ensures perfect recovery for authorized users while making intercepted states appear mixed to eavesdroppers, with built-in tampering detection. This protocol can also be used for secure communication between two parties in geographically different locations, and also for quantum key distribution. The effectiveness of this protocol is demonstrated by assuming a quantum computer with quantum memory and functioning quantum networks. In the absence of the latter, at present, the protocol can be demonstrated in laboratory using currently available quantum computing platforms.

Summary. This paper introduces a highly efficient, matrix-free algorithm to compute the action of a spin Hamiltonian on a quantum state vector. By using mixed-radix indexing, it avoids the massive memory overhead of explicitly assembling the Hamiltonian matrix. This makes simulating large, complex quantum spin systems feasible for workflows requiring frequent Hamiltonian updates.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant as it provides a general, scalable computational backbone for simulating complex Hamiltonians in AMO physics, especially when the Hamiltonian structure changes frequently (e.g., parameter sweeps).

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge is overcoming the memory limitations associated with explicitly assembling the Hamiltonian matrix for simulating large quantum many-body systems.

Main result. The authors present a generic, memory-conserving algorithm to compute the action of the Hamiltonian on a state vector entirely on the fly, avoiding explicit matrix storage.

Method. The method utilizes a mixed-radix indexing scheme for the full tensor-product basis, translating local spin operations into simple integer offsets to perform the matrix-vector product.

Model / system. The framework applies to quantum many-body systems on arbitrary spin lattices, handling local spin operators ($\hat{S}$) and allowing for mixed-spin or bosonic degrees of freedom.

Key observables. The core operation is the matrix-vector product $\hat{H}|\mathbf{x} angle$; key diagnostics include computational time and operator memory scaling.

Important parameters / regimes. The method's efficiency is measured against the system dimension $D$ and the number of local interactions ($N+|P|$).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the state vector itself can be stored (the state vector bottleneck) and that the required index arithmetic is feasible within hardware word limits.

Figures summary. The notes reference a comparison (Table I) showing the superior storage scaling of the matrix-free method versus explicit sparse assembly as system dimension grows.

Paper structure. The paper develops the mixed-radix indexing logic, details the target-driven approach for calculating matrix elements, and provides pseudocode for the matrix-free matrix-vector product ($ ext{MATVEC!}$).

Abstract

We present an algorithm that computes the action of a generic spin Hamiltonian on a state vector on the fly, entirely avoiding explicit matrix assembly. This is achieved through mixed-radix indexing of the full tensor-product basis, which translates local spin operations into simple integer offsets. The result is an explicit framework for evaluating single- and two-site terms across arbitrary spin lattices, including mixed-spin systems. Our construction bridges the basis-indexing logic familiar from exact diagonalization with the matrix-free state-update philosophy of address-based frameworks. By writing the indexing logic in closed form, a single uniform loop applies to every site regardless of its local Hilbert-space dimension. The method is parallelizable and memory-conserving, and can be extended to restricted basis or truncated bosonic levels.

Summary. This paper quantifies the threat posed by radon progeny contamination to superconducting qubits. By developing an enhanced experimental setup, the authors measure the accumulation of radioactive daughters on surfaces. Why it may be interesting. The work directly addresses environmental noise sources that limit the scalability of quantum processors, providing critical dosimetry data for hardware engineers designing radiation-hard quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how long-lived, unstable daughters of Radon-222, which plate out on device surfaces, induce decoherence in superconducting qubits.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that radon progeny plate-out can create a significant, localized source of ionizing radiation, potentially dominating the background noise, especially in shielded or underground environments.

Key observables. Surface activity density of Po-210 ($A_{3y}$), event rates from $ ext{Rn}$ progeny vs. cosmic rays, and the resulting crystalline defects (Frenkel defects).

Important parameters / regimes. Plate-out enhancement factor ($7 imes 10^4$), ambient radon concentrations ($ ext{Bq/m}^3$), and the time delay for measurement (3 years).

Paper structure. Abstract

Ionizing radiation in the form of $α$, $β$, $γ$, and additional high-energy particles can induce decoherence via phonon and quasiparticle poisoning in superconducting qubits. Recent studies have explored this effect using cosmic rays or controlled radioactive sources held in the proximity of a qubit package, and have concluded that reductions in such external'' environmental radiation may benefit stable operation of qubit devices. However, the effect of long-lived, unstable daughters of $^{222}$Rn thatplate out'' directly on device and packaging surfaces has not been as extensively explored. This plate-out process, well-known to the dark matter direct detection field, occurs throughout the fabrication and testing lifecycle of a device and (separately) its packaging, and produces a local source of $α$-decays which can remain active for decades. As this scales with chip area, understanding and managing this source of ionizing radiation is relevant for successfully scaling quantum computing architectures to larger numbers of qubits in a radiation-robust way. We present a setup capable of accelerating and enhancing radon daughter plateout by a factor of $7\times10^4$ over ambient, in order to study, \textit{in situ}, the impact of these events on superconducting qubits. We also provide outlook on the potential impact of this source of ionizing radiation on current and future qubit arrays.

An Explicit Scott-Type Bound for Absolutely Maximally Entangled States with Arbitrary Defect

Authors: Shixuan Zeng, Xiande Zhang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01943v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 3/5

Summary. This paper establishes a comprehensive and explicit Scott-type upper bound for the existence of highly entangled quantum states (AME states) with an arbitrary defect $l$. By employing advanced techniques involving linear programming on quantum enumerators, the authors provide a general nonexistence criterion, significantly improving previous results and yielding explicit asymptotic bounds on the entanglement ratio $k/n$.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides deep mathematical constraints on the structure of highly entangled quantum states, which are fundamental resources in quantum information theory, particularly for quantum error correction and secret sharing protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to establish explicit Scott-type upper bounds for the existence of Absolutely Maximally Entangled (AME) states and generalized $k$-uniform states with an arbitrary defect $l$. This is equivalent to finding nonexistence bounds for specific one-dimensional pure quantum error-correcting codes.

Main result. The authors derive a fully explicit Scott-type upper bound for AME states with arbitrary defect $l$, providing a general result that encompasses previous bounds for $l=0, 1, 2$. They also establish explicit asymptotic upper bounds on the ratio $k/n$.

Method. The proof utilizes a truncated MacWilliams linear-programming system derived from quantum weight and unitary enumerators. The core technique involves solving linear systems and deriving contradictions based on the non-negativity of solution vectors.

Model / system. The system under study is pure quantum states $|\psi angle$ within the Hilbert space $(\mathbb{C}^q)^{\otimes n}$, where $q$ is the local dimension and $n$ is the number of parties. These states are characterized by their $k$-uniformity, relating them directly to quantum error-correcting codes.

Key observables. The existence or nonexistence of $k$-uniform states; the ratio $k/n$; and the values of the quantum weight and unitary enumerators.

Important parameters / regimes. Local dimension $q$, number of parties $n$, and the defect parameter $l$ (where $k = \lfloor n/2 floor - l$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the existence of a $k$-uniform state and proceeds by contradiction using linear programming constraints derived from the enumerator formalism. The bounds are strongest for $q \ge 4$.

Figures summary. A table is mentioned comparing the derived explicit bound with previous results for small local dimensions $q \in \{4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9\}$.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem by reviewing existing bounds (Scott's, Ning et al.'s). It then develops the general theory using MacWilliams identities, translates the problem into a linear-programming feasibility problem, and solves the system to derive the explicit upper bounds for both finite $n$ and the asymptotic ratio $k/n$.

Abstract

Absolutely maximally entangled (AME) states and, more generally, $k$-uniform states in $(\C^q)^{\otimes n}$ are central objects in multipartite entanglement theory, with applications to quantum secret sharing, quantum masking, and quantum error correction. In the extremal case $k=\lfloor n/2\rfloor$, Scott (2004) proved a sharp nonexistence bound showing that AME states cannot exist once the number of parties $n$ exceeds a threshold of order $2q^{2}$ (with a parity dependence on $n$), where $q$ is the local dimension. Recently, Ning et al.\ studied \emph{defective} AME states (i.e., $k=\lfloor n/2\rfloor-l$ with $l>0$), gave explicit Scott-type bounds for defects $l=1,2$ and conjectured a general $(2l+2)q^{2}+o(q^{2})$ behavior. In this paper, we solve this conjecture and establish a fully explicit Scott-type upper bound for AME states with arbitrary defect $l\ge 0$, yielding Scott's bound for $l=0$ and Ning et al.'s bounds for $l=1,2$ as special cases. Equivalently, this gives nonexistence bounds for one-dimensional pure quantum error-correcting codes near the quantum Singleton regime. The proof uses a truncated MacWilliams linear-programming system and an explicit infeasibility certificate. As a direct application, we derive explicit asymptotic upper bounds on $k/n$ for fixed local dimension $q$, improving the implicit upper bounds given by Ning et al.

Why it may be interesting. The study provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding how microscopic discrete symmetries constrain and modify emergent quantum critical behavior, which is highly relevant for designing and interpreting measurements in novel quantum materials.

Key observables. Critical exponents ($ u, u'$), the ratio $ u'/ u$, and the emergence of two characteristic length scales ($\xi, \xi'$).

Abstract

Supersymmetry, originally proposed in high-energy physics, can emerge as a remarkable low-energy structure in condensed matter systems. While emergent supersymmetry at quantum critical points is widely discussed in models with continuous symmetries, real materials are constrained by microscopic discrete symmetries. To address this, we investigate (2+1)-dimensional Gross-Neveu-Yukawa theories coupling Dirac fermions to a complex order parameter with discrete $Z_n$ anisotropy. Using the functional renormalization group, we find that for $n>3$, the anisotropic perturbations are irrelevant at the fixed point, yielding a $\mathcal{N}=2$ Wess-Zumino supersymmetric critical point. In the ordered phase, this dangerously irrelevant anisotropy gives rise to a second characteristic length scale, $ξ'$, alongside the usual correlation length, $ξ$. By tracking mass thresholds along symmetry-broken renormalization group trajectories, we extract the exponents $ν'$ and $ν$ without imposing prior scaling assumptions. For the $Z_4$, $Z_5$, and $Z_6$ models, our results support the scaling relation $ν'/ν= 1+|y_n|/p$ with $p=2$ in the isotropic framework used here.

Summary. This work uses the Spin Seebeck Effect (SSE) to electrically probe the exotic magnetic excitations, specifically magnetic monopoles, within the insulating magnet Dy2Ti2O7. By measuring the voltage response as a function of magnetic field and frequency, the authors confirm that the observed anomalies are signatures of fractionalized magnetic dynamics. This demonstrates a powerful spintronic tool for studying emergent quantum phenomena in insulators.

Why it may be interesting. The use of spintronics (SSE) to probe charge-neutral, emergent excitations like monopoles provides a novel, electrical handle on complex many-body physics, bridging condensed matter magnetism with quantum transport.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is the electrical observation and characterization of fractionalized excitations, such as magnetic monopoles, in insulating quantum magnets.

Main result. The Spin Seebeck Effect (SSE) successfully detected characteristic peaks in the voltage signal corresponding to monopole proliferation in the 3D fractionalized magnet Dy2Ti2O7.

Method. The SSE technique is used, where a temperature gradient generated by an AC current in a Pt layer drives magnetic excitations in the insulator, generating a measurable voltage via the Inverse Spin Hall Effect.

Model / system. The study focuses on the non-collinear Ising magnet Dy2Ti2O7, a pyrochlore oxide exhibiting spin-ice behavior, which hosts fractionalized excitations like magnetic monopoles.

Key observables. Spin Seebeck Voltage (V_SSE) dependence on magnetic field (B), frequency (f), and in-plane angle (theta).

Important parameters / regimes. Low temperatures (down to 1.4 K), magnetic fields near 1.1 T, and characteristic frequencies in the 0.5–1 kHz range.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the interfacial spin current conversion is instantaneous within the measurement frequency range, and that the SSE signal is dominated by the magnetic excitation dynamics.

Figures summary. Figures show V_SSE vs B, V_SSE vs f, and V_SSE vs theta, demonstrating field- and frequency-dependent anomalies linked to magnetic transitions and monopole physics.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the SSE measurement protocol on Dy2Ti2O7, presents measurements showing field- and frequency-dependent peaks, and interprets these features as evidence for the dynamics of fractionalized magnetic excitations.

Abstract

Fractionalized excitations are among the most striking signatures of emergence in quantum matter. While widely sought in frustrated magnets, their detection and characterization remain challenging, motivating the exploration of new probes. Meanwhile, Spintronics offers versatile tools for probing spin-related phenomena. In particular, the spin Seebeck effect (SSE) converts thermally driven magnetic excitations into a voltage in an adjacent metal, providing electrical access to the underlying dynamics and transport properties. Here we employ the SSE to probe emergent magnetic monopoles in the non-collinear Ising magnet Dy$_2$Ti$_2$O$_7$, a rare instance of a three-dimensional fractionalized magnet. We observe an SSE signal featuring a pronounced peak at monopole proliferation, accompanied by characteristic frequency and angular dependence. Our results broaden the scope of spintronic methods for detecting exotic excitations, provide new insights into magnetic insulators generally and monopole physics specifically, and suggest the potential of quantum materials as functional interfaces.
