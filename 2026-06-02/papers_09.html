Summary. This theoretical work examines how coupling an artificial graphene structure to a cavity field, while simultaneously including Rashba spin-orbit interaction, modifies its electronic band structure. The key finding is that this interplay allows for the precise engineering of topological features, such as opening gaps at specific Dirac points, which manifests as strong, tunable oscillations in the spin-Hall conductivity.

Why it may be interesting. The explicit coupling between spin-orbit coupling (a key ingredient in topological physics) and cavity photons (a core concept in cavity QED) to engineer transport properties like $\sigma_{SH}$ is highly relevant for designing novel quantum devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the combined influence of a far-infrared (FIR) cavity field and Rashba spin-orbit interaction (SOI) on the electronic band structure and transport properties of artificial graphene.

Main result. The interplay between Rashba SOI and cavity coupling governs Dirac-point physics, allowing for the opening of gaps at type-II Dirac points and producing pronounced, oscillatory modifications in the spin-Hall conductivity.

Method. The authors employ a theoretical Hamiltonian approach, constructing a basis that is a tensor product of the electronic Hilbert space and the photon Fock space, followed by exact diagonalization and the use of the Kubo formalism.

Model / system. The system is artificial graphene (AG) made of InAs/GaAs quantum dots, modeled as a honeycomb lattice, coupled to a quantized cavity photon mode. The Hamiltonian includes terms for kinetic energy, periodic potential, Rashba SOI, and electron-photon interaction.

Key observables. Spin-Hall conductivity ($\sigma_{SH}$), band structure dispersion, mean photon number ($\langle \hat{N}_{ph} angle$), and the existence/nature of Dirac points (Type-I vs. Type-II).

Important parameters / regimes. Cavity geometry (cylindrical vs. linear), Rashba coupling strength, and cavity coupling strength ($g$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis uses the long-wavelength approximation for the vector potential and treats the electron-photon interaction perturbatively in some derivations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate energy miniband dispersion for different cavity types and coupling constants, and show the evolution of $\sigma_{SH}$ as a function of chemical potential, highlighting oscillatory behavior.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining the full Hamiltonian incorporating all interactions, deriving matrix elements for the coupling terms, solving the effective Hamiltonian via unitary transformations, and finally calculating the transport observable ($\sigma_{SH}$) using the Kubo formula.

Abstract

We investigate the combined effect of a far-infrared cavity field and Rashba spin-orbit interac- tion on the band structure and transport properties of artificial graphene composed of quazi-2D InAs/GaAs quantum dots. The coupling to cavity photons is modeled by constructing a complete basis as the tensor product of the electronic Hilbert space and the Fock space. Our calculations for the system embedded in a linear cavity predict the existence of both type-I and type-II Dirac points which can be distinguished by their response to Rashba interaction. Namely, Rashba coupling opens a gap at type-II Dirac points, while type-I Dirac points remain gapless. For both cylindrical and linear cavities, we demonstrate the formation of electron-photon hybrid states and Rabi oscillations between energy minibands. Multiple splittings, crossings, and anticrossings between Dirac-band replicas produce pronounced modifications of the spin-Hall conductivity, including strong anisotropy and oscillatory behavior controlled by cavity geometry and polarization. Our results show that the interplay between Rashba and cavity couplings governs Dirac-point physics and provides a route toward tunable polaritonic transport and topological phases in engineered nanostructures.

Abstract

Integer quantum Hall effect, which is the Hall effect quantized into integer times $e^2/h$ ($e$: elementary charge, $h$: Planck's constant) observed first in two-dimensional electron gases in strong magnetic fields, is reviewed from both theoretical and experimental standpoints. Basic physics underlying the phenomenon is explained. Specifically in this new edition we have a fresh look at how the quantum Hall effect is captured in a perspective of topological systems, since, while the quantum Hall effect is historically the first realization of the topological systems, the field has been delved into a much wider realm of physics of topological systems. We also mention diverse advances such as the quantum Hall effect (QHE) in various materials and contexts that include graphene, oxides and narrow-gap semiconductors, a relation with the fractional quantum Hall effect, and the quantum Hall effect as the resistance standard and further roles in the new SI system. Abstract

We investigate the electrically driven metal-to-insulator transition (MIT) in nanoscale vanadium dioxide (VO$_2$) Mott memristor through noise spectroscopy and two-dimensional resistor network simulations. Our experiments focus on both the insulating phase as the applied voltage approaches the threshold voltage (set transition) and the metallic phase as the voltage is reduced toward the reset voltage (reset transition). In both regimes, we observe an order of magnitude increase in relative current noise near the transition points. To analyze the origin of this noise enhancement, we use simulations that capture the stochastic dynamics of the phase transition. The simulations indicate that the increased noise stems from amplified phase fluctuations near the percolation threshold, where competing metallic and insulating domains lead to dynamic reconfiguration of the conduction paths. In addition, we show that the precursor current fluctuations observed near the switching threshold are consistent with the threshold voltage variability measured in repeated switching cycles, indicating that the noise sets a lower bound on the achievable variance. These findings offer key insights into the non-equilibrium processes governing phase transitions in nanoscale VO$_2$ devices under electrical stimuli.

Abstract

Transport of optically excited carriers in semiconductors is typically described within a quasi-equilibrium picture, where energy is carried by a single thermalized quasiparticle population characterized by well-defined transport coefficients. Here, we demonstrate that in epitaxial CsPbBr3 perovskite single crystals, this picture holds near room temperature - but breaks down dramatically at low temperature. Using transient microscopy, we show that optically measured carrier mobilities match device-scale Hall-effect and field-effect transistor measurements across a broad temperature range, resolving reported discrepancies and validating the equilibrium framework in the free-carrier regime. Below ~60 K, however, when excitonic effects become significant, equilibrium models begin to fail. We observe two coupled populations: a transient (<100 ps) hot-exciton gas with a diffusivity ~25-30 cm2/s - greatly exceeding the diffusivity expected for thermalized excitons - and a quasi-localized state that is fed by the cooling of the hot-exciton gas. These results reveal that in CsPbBr3, transport and thermalization are not separable processes: carriers move while still redistributing among internal degrees of freedom, breaking the timescale separation that underpins equilibrium transport theory in conventional semiconductors. By resolving transport at the population level, we can directly access the competing kinetics of exciton formation, interconversion, and cooling, offering a new space for controlling energy flow in perovskite materials and their photonic applications.

Abstract

We have studied the finite-temperature topology of higher-order topological insulators (HOTIs) based on the Uhlmann phase, which is a phase angle of the Uhlmann overlap. As an example of HOTIs, the Hamiltonian of the Benalcazar-Bernevig-Hughes (BBH) model is constructed from Gamma matrices satisfying the Clifford algebra. This specific algebraic structure underpins the model's higher-order topological properties, including the quantization of the Uhlmann phase to $0$ or $π$. This quantization enables us to treat the abrupt jumps of the Uhlmann phase as an indication of the nontrivial topological phase of the BBH model at finite temperature. From the disappearance of these jumps, we determine the critical temperature at which the topological transition occurs. For a special choice of parameters, the Uhlmann overlap and the critical temperature can be computed analytically.

Summary. This work theoretically explains how phonon interactions, combined with Coulomb repulsion, can induce superconductivity exclusively on the topological Fermi arcs of Weyl semimetals. The key finding is that weak surface screening favors an inter-arc pairing channel, which results in the observationally important nodal superconducting gap structure. This suggests that surface engineering of the dielectric environment can control the superconducting properties.

Why it may be interesting. The detailed theoretical framework for deriving pairing mechanisms from lattice vibrations and surface topology is highly relevant for understanding emergent superconducting states in topological materials, bridging concepts from lattice dynamics and superconductivity theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the mechanism driving the formation of a superconducting gap specifically on the topological surface states (Fermi arcs) of Weyl semimetals, while the bulk remains non-superconducting.

Main result. Nodal superconductivity arises generically from the combination of phonon-mediated attraction and weakly screened long-range Coulomb repulsion acting on disconnected Fermi-arc segments.

Method. The authors use a continuum description, deriving effective electron-electron interactions (Coulomb and electron-phonon) and solving the resulting gap equation in the mean-field approximation.

Model / system. The system is time-reversal-invariant Weyl semimetals, modeled using a tight-binding approach that yields surface Fermi arcs. The theory incorporates coupling to both surface and bulk phonons and long-range Coulomb interactions.

Key observables. Superconducting gap structure (nodal vs. nodeless), critical temperature ($T_c$), and the relative strengths of intra-arc and inter-arc pairing channels.

Important parameters / regimes. Coulomb screening strength ($q_s$), phonon frequencies ($\omega$), and the geometry of the Fermi arcs.

Assumptions / limitations. The bulk density of states is negligible compared to the surface DOS near the chemical potential, and the analysis relies on linearizing the gap equation near $T_c$.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Fermi arcs in the surface Brillouin zone, the competition between intra-arc and inter-arc scattering processes, and the dependence of $T_c$ on the screening momentum.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first deriving the electronic structure and the electron-phonon coupling Hamiltonian, then formulating the effective electron-electron interaction, and finally solving the gap equation by distinguishing between intra-arc and inter-arc pairing channels to determine the superconducting gap symmetry and $T_c$.

Abstract

According to recent observations, the topological surface states of Weyl semimetals may develop a superconducting gap, while bulk superconductivity remains absent. What drives the formation of this novel superconducting state is an open question. Here, we show that this phenomenon can arise from the interaction of Fermi arc electrons with both surface and bulk phonons in time-reversal-invariant Weyl semimetals. We identify two competing pairing channels, intra-arc and inter-arc, whose relative strength is governed by the efficiency of Coulomb screening at the surface. The combined effect of the Fermi arcs being disconnected and the weak screening of the Coulomb repulsion at the system's surface causes nodes to appear in the superconducting gap, as observed recently by photoelectron spectroscopy experiments on PtBi2. This suggests manipulation of the Coulomb screening, e.g. by a surface layer coating, as a pathway to engineer the critical temperature, as well as size and symmetry of the surface superconducting gap.

Summary. This work demonstrates a novel method to enhance the detection of coherent acoustic phonons in semiconductors by generating a propagating acoustic grating using a high-repetition-rate laser pulse train. By exploiting Bragg reflection, the Brillouin light scattering signal is resonantly enhanced by factors up to 30 times compared to single-pulse excitation. This technique provides a highly sensitive tool for studying high-frequency acoustic dynamics in solid-state materials.

Why it may be interesting. The use of periodic excitation to engineer a macroscopic optical/acoustic resonance (Bragg enhancement) is a powerful analogy to engineered quantum systems, particularly in cavity QED or photonic structures, demonstrating controlled enhancement of a fundamental physical signal.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to enhance the detection of Brillouin light scattering (BLS) from coherent acoustic phonons in semiconductors by utilizing a propagating acoustic grating.

Main result. The multi-pulse approach generates a propagating acoustic grating, leading to a significant resonant enhancement of the BLS signal, observed to be 10-30-fold in GaAs and (Cd,Zn)Te.

Method. The technique involves using a high-repetition-rate laser pulse train to create the grating, followed by time-domain Brillouin scattering measurements in a pump-probe configuration.

Model / system. The experiment is performed on bulk semiconductors (GaAs and (Cd,Zn)Te) at cryogenic temperatures, utilizing a two-color differential reflectivity pump-probe setup.

Key observables. Resonant peaks in the spectral dependence of the TDBS amplitude, the acoustic phonon frequency (f_ac), and the enhancement factor.

Important parameters / regimes. The enhancement factor is limited by the number of strain pulses (N) and the finite spectral width of the probe pulse; the resonance condition is governed by the Bragg reflection relation.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis models the signal using a geometric series sum for the total scattered field, assuming normal incidence of the probe beam.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the comparison between conventional single-pulse and multi-pulse TDBS approaches, and show the spectral dependence of the enhanced signal.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from introducing the concept of coherent phonon generation to detailing the multi-pulse grating formation, deriving the enhanced signal mathematically via geometric series summation, and finally presenting experimental results on GaAs and (Cd,Zn)Te.

Abstract

Generation and detection of coherent phonons in semiconductors by femtosecond optical pulses is a powerful tool for high-frequency acoustic control of their electronic properties. Here, we demonstrate a propagating one-dimensional acoustic grating in bulk semiconductors using above-band-gap excitation by a train of laser pulses with high repetition rate of 1 GHz. This approach enables shaping of the coherent acoustic phonon spectrum and leads to a significant enhancement of Brillouin light scattering at selected probe wavelengths in pump-probe configuration. We demonstrate this effect at a cryogenic temperature of 5 K in prototypical semiconductor systems, namely bulk crystalline GaAs and (Cd,Zn)Te, which serve as benchmark materials for the proposed method. The spectral dependence of the time-domain Brillouin light scattering amplitude exhibits resonant peaks at discrete probe wavelengths arising from Bragg reflection of the probe light by the propagating acoustic grating. A 10-30-fold resonant enhancement of the signal amplitude is observed for GaAs and (Cd,Zn)Te, determined by the finite spectral width of the probe pulse. With further spectral narrowing, enhancements of the order ~ 100-150 are expected, set by the number N of strain pulses in the acoustic grating within the sample and ultimately limited by the material parameters and sample thickness.

Summary. This work introduces a node-resolved framework to analyze collective dynamics in complex networks by exploiting spectral localization in the graph Laplacian. By showing that structural disorder concentrates spectral modes onto specific nodes, the authors allow stability analysis to be performed directly in physical node space rather than abstract spectral space. This restores physical interpretability and predicts the onset and nature of emergent collective behaviors.

Why it may be interesting. The concept of spectral localization and mapping abstract spectral modes back to physical nodes is a powerful tool for interpreting complex, high-dimensional dynamics, which has parallels in understanding collective excitations in quantum many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses how to restore physical interpretability to collective dynamics in complex, heterogeneous systems whose stability is traditionally analyzed only in abstract spectral space.

Main result. Structural disorder in networks induces spectral localization, allowing stability properties to be interpreted directly in node space and enabling the identification of specific nodes driving collective transitions.

Method. The authors develop a node-resolved framework by exploiting spectral localization of the graph Laplacian eigenvectors to decouple the full system Jacobian into localized, node-dependent components.

Model / system. The analysis uses general networked systems, exemplified by reaction-diffusion models (like the Brusselator) and SIS dynamics, coupled via the network Laplacian structure.

Key observables. Localized Master Stability Function (MSF), Mean Normalized Eigenvalue Error (MNE), and the identification of dominant nodes responsible for instability.

Important parameters / regimes. Network size (N), parameter heterogeneity (s), and the coupling structure (Adjacency vs. Laplacian).

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is the existence of strong spectral localization, which allows the complex dynamics to be accurately approximated by local dynamics governed by a 'host node.'

Figures summary. Figures illustrate mode-node mapping via heatmaps of Laplacian eigenvectors, compare MNE across different network types (ER, BA, real-world), and show time series demonstrating various collective states like amplitude chimera and oscillon patterns.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the failure of classical spectral theory to proposing the localization-based node-resolved framework. It then applies this framework to analyze stability, predict transition onsets, and characterize various nonlinear collective states (e.g., chimera states) based on the localized spectrum.

Abstract

Real complex systems often exhibit collective transitions emerging from interactions across many components. Classical stability theory describes such transitions in spectral space, where dynamics is organized by spatially extended global eigenmodes whose collective nature obscures direct association with individual physical components. Here, we show that structural disorder in empirical random networks can fundamentally alter this picture. These properties induce spectral localization, causing Laplacian modes to concentrate on small subsets of nodes and producing a mode--node correspondence in which collective dynamics becomes governed predominantly by the local behavior of a dominant node together with their effective coupling to the surrounding network. As a consequence, stability properties can be interpreted directly in node space rather than purely in spectral space. Exploiting this principle, we develop a node-resolved framework that predicts transition onsets, identifies the nodes responsible for emergent collective behavior, and restores interpretability in systems where classical modal theories fail. In heterogeneous reaction networks, the same mechanism gives rise to exotic collective states where different subsets of nodes develop distinct dynamical behaviors beyond those associated with homogeneous assumptions. Our results show that complex network structures naturally generate spectral localization, revealing the microscopic drivers of macroscopic dynamics.
