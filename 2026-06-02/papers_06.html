Microscopic Theory of the Phonon Thermal Hall Effect in Chiral Mott Insulators

Authors: Junha Kang, Taekoo Oh
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01876v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · analog quantum simulation 1/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work establishes a fully microscopic theory for the Phonon Thermal Hall Effect in chiral Mott insulators. By deriving the effect from an emergent gauge field linked to spin chirality, the authors propose a definitive experimental protocol using isotopic substitution. This allows researchers to cleanly separate the phonon contribution to thermal transport from other background electronic signals.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous derivation of the PTHE links lattice vibrations (phonons) directly to electronic magnetic order (spin chirality) via emergent gauge fields, providing a powerful new diagnostic tool for strongly correlated materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper develops a microscopic theory for the Phonon Thermal Hall Effect (PTHE) in chiral Mott insulators, aiming to provide a definitive method to isolate this phonon contribution.

Main result. The PTHE is shown to be governed by an emergent gauge field, which is proportional to the scalar spin chirality, and its signal can be quantitatively separated using isotopic substitution and a derived scaling law.

Method. The theory employs advanced many-body techniques, including inverse Schrieffer-Wolff perturbation theory, Born-Oppenheimer approximations, and the calculation of Abelian phonon Berry curvature using projector formalisms.

Model / system. The study focuses on chiral Mott insulators, modeled using the double exchange Hamiltonian at half-filling, and specifically investigates the 2D kagome lattice.

Key observables. Phonon thermal Hall conductivity ($\kappa_{ph}^{\mu u}$), emergent gauge field ($F_{em}^{ab}$), and the temperature/mass dependence of $\kappa_{ph}^{\mu u}$.

Important parameters / regimes. Scalar spin chirality ($\chi_{ijk}$), temperature ($T$), and isotopic mass ratio ($M_r$).

Assumptions / limitations. The calculation assumes hopping amplitudes depend only on inter-atomic displacement, and the method relies on the Born-Oppenheimer approximation for separating electron and nuclear dynamics.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the intrinsic PTHE in the kagome model, showing band structure changes and the temperature dependence of $\kappa_{ph}^{xy}(T)$, and demonstrate the isotope effect where $\kappa_{ph}^{xy}$ changes dependence on $M_r$ at low vs. high temperatures.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from deriving the effective lattice Hamiltonian via BO approximation, calculating the emergent gauge field via perturbation theory, and finally establishing the experimental protocol using isotopic substitution and scaling laws to isolate the pure PTHE signal.

Abstract

The thermal Hall effect (THE) probes charge-neutral excitations in insulators, where the charge gap blocks electronic transport. Recently, phonons have been shown to induce a THE comparable in magnitude to the spin contribution, underscoring their critical role in thermal transport. Here, we develop a microscopic theory of the phonon thermal Hall effect (PTHE) in chiral Mott insulators. First, we derive the exact analytic form of the effective Raman interaction in half-filled Mott insulators, showing that its strength is directly proportional to the scalar spin chirality. Next, we demonstrate the intrinsic PTHE explicitly on the kagome lattice. Crucially, our formulation reveals a temperature-dependent crossover in the transport behavior under isotopic substitution. Using this result, we establish a scaling law that quantitatively separates the phonon contribution to the THE from other background signals. Our results not only provide the first fully microscopic derivation of the PTHE, but also establish a definitive experimental standard for isolating microscopic heat carriers in chiral Mott insulators.

Wilson Holonomy and Spectral Monodromy in Spin-Orbit Rings: Effective Gauge Connections and Loop Observables

Authors: N. Bolivar
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01029v1

Summary. This work develops a rigorous gauge-theoretic framework to analyze spin-orbit coupling in quantum rings. It achieves a crucial separation between the energy-independent Wilson holonomy, which governs interference, and the energy-dependent monodromy, which dictates spectral quantization. The methodology successfully maps complex SOC Hamiltonians onto computable loop observables, providing deep insights into spin-dependent transport.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful, general mathematical machinery (loop/holonomy algebra) to analyze complex quantum transport phenomena in condensed matter systems, linking topology to measurable spectral properties.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To construct a precise, computable bridge between the loop/holonomy representation of gauge theories and condensed-matter spin-orbit transport in quantum rings.

Main result. The paper successfully separates the energy-independent Wilson holonomy (governing interference) from the energy-dependent monodromy (quantizing the spectrum), providing a unified framework for SOC systems.

Method. The analysis involves mapping the SOC Hamiltonian to an effective $U(1) \oplus A_{ ext{int}}$ connection, reducing the problem to a first-order transport equation, and analyzing the resulting loop operators.

Model / system. The primary models are spin-orbit coupled quantum rings, specifically analyzing Dirac (graphene) rings with Aharonov-Bohm flux and Rashba-Dresselhaus rings exhibiting non-Abelian $SU(2)$ transport.

Key observables. Wilson holonomy, spectral monodromy, curvature, and eigenphases.

Important parameters / regimes. Rashba coupling ($\alpha$), Dresselhaus coupling ($eta$), Aharonov-Bohm flux ($\Phi$), and the pure-gauge locus ($\alpha = \pm eta$).

Assumptions / limitations. The non-Abelian Stokes theorem and Magnus expansion are used as diagnostic tools, and the spectral quantization condition is derived from the monodromy operator, distinct from the holonomy.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the factorization of the total holonomy into Abelian and internal parts for the graphene ring, and show the non-vanishing curvature of the internal connection for generic SOC parameters.

Paper structure. The paper systematically develops the gauge theory formalism, first establishing the separation of holonomy and monodromy, then applying this to the Dirac ring (factorization), and finally tackling the non-Abelian case (Rashba-Dresselhaus) using first-order reduction and curvature diagnostics.

Abstract

A spin-orbit Hamiltonian with an effective gauge structure carries two distinct loop objects that are routinely conflated: an energy-independent Wilson holonomy, which organizes interference and internal spin transport, and an energy-dependent monodromy, which quantizes the spectrum. We show that cleanly separating these objects supplies a precise, computable bridge between the loop/holonomy representation of gauge theories and condensed-matter spin-orbit transport. The construction maps a spin-orbit Hamiltonian to an effective $U(1)$ plus internal non-Abelian connection, reduces it to a first-order transport problem, and reads physical predictions from holonomy, monodromy, curvature, and eigenphase data. Two rings make the separation explicit. For a Dirac (graphene) ring with Rashba coupling and Aharonov-Bohm flux, the total holonomy factorizes exactly into a commuting $U(1)$ flux phase times an internal spin/pseudospin holonomy, and the spectrum follows from a holonomy-eigenvalue condition. For a Rashba-Dresselhaus ring, the internal $SU(2)$ transport is genuinely non-Abelian away from the $α=\pmβ$ pure-gauge locus, where curvature controls path ordering; spectral quantization then requires an explicit first-order reduction obtained by phase-space doubling of the second-order Schrödinger problem. A non-Abelian Stokes formulation and Magnus expansion serve as ordering diagnostics rather than spectral tools. Spin-network ideas enter only as historical geometric motivation, not as a dynamical import into spintronics.

Summary. This paper develops a rigorous Effective Field Theory framework to study three- and four-boson systems near the unitarity limit, applying it to $^4$He. By systematically calculating corrections beyond the leading order, the authors show that the resulting binding energies and radii match established phenomenological results, confirming the underlying physics is robustly described by small deviations from scale invariance.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a highly systematic, quantum-field-theoretic approach to few-body physics, which is crucial for understanding ultracold atomic gases tuned near Feshbach resonances (unitarity limit).

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates three- and four-boson systems near the unitarity limit, applying the theoretical framework to the Helium-4 ($^4$He) system.

Main result. The binding energies and radii of $^4$He three- and four-atom systems calculated using the EFT approach converge well with sophisticated phenomenological potentials, suggesting the physics is governed by small deviations from discrete scale invariance.

Method. The authors employ Short-Range Effective Field Theory (SREFT) combined with the Faddeev-Yakubovsky formalism and diagrammatic techniques to systematically calculate corrections beyond the leading-order unitarity limit.

Model / system. The system consists of three- and four-boson interactions, modeled using an EFT Lagrangian. The calculations are performed by systematically including corrections from finite scattering lengths and effective ranges, necessitating the introduction of four-body forces at next-to-leading order.

Key observables. Binding energies ($B_3, B_4$) and radii ($r_3, r_4$) of the few-boson clusters.

Important parameters / regimes. The unitarity limit, the three-body scale parameter ($\Lambda_{\star}$), the two-body scattering length ($a$), and the effective range ($r_2$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that the physics is dominated by small deviations from discrete scale invariance, allowing for a systematic perturbative expansion around the unitarity limit.

Figures summary. Figures compare the tetramer ground-state binding energy and radius against the regulator cutoff ($\Lambda/\kappa_{3,0}$) using different angular momentum cutoffs ($l_{max}$) and theoretical orders (LO/NLO), demonstrating convergence.

Paper structure. The paper systematically builds the theory from the two-body sector (Lippmann-Schwinger) to the three-body (Faddeev) and four-body (FY) equations, detailing the necessary renormalization procedures and the removal of deep trimer states via pseudopotential projection.

Abstract

The three- and four-boson systems with a large scattering length and a short effective range in the two-body sector are studied in the framework of Short-Range Effective Field Theory. The starting point (leading order) of the EFT is taken to be the universal unitarity limit, where the two-body sector is parameter-free and only one three-body parameter enters. In this limit, physical systems manifests discrete scale invariance. Deviations from universality arising from finite scattering-length and effective-range corrections, as well as a four-body force required by renormalization, are included perturbatively at next-to-leading order. The three-body ground state and its associated four-body ground and first-excited states are studied using the Faddeev-Yakubovsky formalism and a complementary diagrammatic approach. By employing techniques to remove contributions from deep trimers in tetramer calculations, we extend our analysis to larger cutoffs than previously accessible within the FY approach. Our results for binding energies and radii of $^4$He three- and four-atom systems converge well to results obtained with sophisticated phenomenological potentials. These successes suggest that the physics of $^4$He atomic clusters is governed by only small deviations from discrete scale invariance.

The proposed couplers, based on quarton and fluxonium circuits, overcome limitations in coupling strength and capacitance loading inherent to small-footprint qubits. This design successfully enables strong, tunable interactions while actively suppressing error sources to support practical quantum processors.

Abstract

Fluxonium qubits have demonstrated high-fidelity operations and long coherence times in small-scale systems, highlighting their promise for quantum computing. However, large-scale integration into a high-performance two-dimensional (2D) qubit array remains the central challenge for practical applications. In this work, we introduce an extensible architecture for scaling up fluxonium qubits in 2D grids. To address the key challenges, namely achieving controllable strong interaction and high connectivity for qubits featuring small shunting capacitors (footprints), we propose using low-shunt-capacitance couplers to enable tunable interactions between fluxonium qubits. When embedded into 2D square lattices, large couplings can be achieved even with relatively small coupling capacitances, thus enabling multiple connections with sufficient capacitance budget. We further propose coupler realizations based on generalized flux qubit circuits, specifically the quarton and the fluxonium, and demonstrate that both enable fast, high-fidelity gates with low spectator errors, while supporting multiple connections on 2D grids.

Half the Interference, Most of the Answer: Approximate Quantum Simulation via Path-Sum Pruning

Authors: Sinan Pehlivanoglu, Srinivasan Iyengar, Amr Sabry
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.01922v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work proposes a novel approximation technique called statistical interference sampling to tackle the computational bottleneck of quantum interference in circuit simulation. By modeling interference explicitly and introducing a threshold rule, the authors demonstrate that significant portions of the interference calculation can be safely pruned. This suggests that interference arithmetic is a structured resource amenable to targeted approximation strategies.

Why it may be interesting. The explicit separation and approximation of interference arithmetic provide a novel, structured resource that can be pruned, offering insights applicable to path-sum and tensor-network simulation techniques.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Classical simulation of quantum circuits is computationally expensive due to the exponential state-space size and the high cost associated with coherently combining many computational histories (interference).

Main result. The proposed method can omit nearly 50% of the endpoint interference work while retaining over 90% output accuracy for benchmark quantum algorithms.

Method. It introduces statistical interference sampling, treating endpoint interference as a separately schedulable computation using a Chemical Abstract Machine (ChAM) model and employing a threshold rule for early termination.

Model / system. The simulation models quantum circuits using a path-integral formulation, where weighted path contributions evolve like concurrent molecular species undergoing time evolution, tagging, and interference reactions.

Key observables. Output distribution, output accuracy, and the fraction of omitted endpoint interference work.

Important parameters / regimes. The threshold parameter T, which dictates when the simulation halts based on accumulated amplitude.

Assumptions / limitations. The method does not improve worst-case complexity, and the formal error analysis assumes a uniform random arrival order for path contributions.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Deutsch-Jozsa circuit, the ChAM model structure, and concrete execution traces showing the benefit and error associated with early termination.

Paper structure. The paper first identifies the two bottlenecks (state-space and interference), introduces the ChAM model and statistical interference sampling, demonstrates its application to benchmark circuits (Deutsch-Jozsa, Grover, Simon, Shor), and concludes with a formal analysis of error bounds.

Abstract

Classical simulation of quantum circuits is expensive for two distinct reasons. The obvious one is state-space size: an n-qubit system requires exponentially many amplitudes. The less obvious one is interference: useful output distributions emerge only after many computational histories have been coherently combined at common endpoints, and this aggregation step is itself a substantial source of cost. We introduce statistical interference sampling, a framework that makes this second bottleneck explicit by treating endpoint interference as a separately schedulable computation. Using the Chemical Abstract Machine (ChAM) as our model, weighted path contributions evolve as concurrent molecular species, and interference reactions combine contributions that share a common output state. A threshold rule terminates the process once an endpoint accumulates sufficient amplitude, discarding the remaining reactions. The method does not improve worst-case complexity and is not intended as a general-purpose simulator. Its purpose is to ask a more targeted question: how much of the interference calculation can be skipped while still recovering a useful output distribution? On benchmark circuits for Deutsch-Jozsa, Grover search, Simon's problem, and small Shor period-finding instances, we find that nearly 50% of endpoint interference reactions can be omitted while maintaining over 90% output accuracy for most algorithms tested. These results suggest that interference arithmetic is a structured resource that admits meaningful approximation, and that exposing it explicitly opens new opportunities for pruning strategies across path-sum, Pauli-path, and tensor-network simulation methods.

Summary. This paper provides a comprehensive review of multidimensional reconciliation for CV-QKD, a critical technique for long-distance quantum key distribution. It details how the physical quantum channel is mapped to a virtual BIAWGN channel, enabling the use of powerful classical error-correcting codes. The work provides practical guidance by analyzing the trade-offs between reconciliation dimension, coding strategy, and achievable key rate.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on quantum communication, the mathematical machinery—transforming a continuous physical process into a discrete, coded virtual channel—shares conceptual links with advanced quantum error correction codes and information bounding techniques relevant to open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is developing highly efficient reconciliation techniques for Continuous-Variable Quantum Key Distribution (CV-QKD) to maintain a positive secret key rate over long distances and low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).

Main result. Multidimensional reconciliation successfully transforms the physical Gaussian quantum channel into a virtual BIAWGN channel, allowing the application of modern error-correcting codes like LDPC codes to achieve high reconciliation efficiency.

Method. The authors review the theory by mapping the physical channel to a virtual channel in dimension $d$, and then apply advanced classical coding techniques (e.g., Coset Coding) for error correction.

Model / system. The system is CV-QKD, which encodes information in the quadratures ($\hat{x}, \hat{p}$) of coherent states. The analysis models the channel as linear and lossy, requiring reconciliation to extract the secret key.

Key observables. Secret Key Rate (SKR), Reconciliation Efficiency ($eta$), Frame Error Rate (FER), and Mutual Information ($I_d$).

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension ($d$), Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), Code Rate ($R$), and the security parameter ($\epsilon$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the physical channel can be modeled by an additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) process, and the security analysis relies on bounding Eve's information.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the trade-offs between FER and reconciliation efficiency ($eta$) for different code rates and dimensions, and compare the throughput of different coding schemes (Coset vs. Syndrome-Concatenation).

Paper structure. The paper progresses from reviewing the necessity of multidimensional reconciliation, detailing the mathematical transformation to the virtual BIAWGN channel, comparing various coding schemes (Coset vs. Syndrome-Concatenation), and finally presenting simulation results using the HDirac framework to guide system design.

Abstract

Continuous-variable quantum key distribution (CV-QKD) requires highly efficient reconciliation techniques to operate at low signal-to-noise ratios and long distances. Multidimensional reconciliation addresses this challenge by transforming the physical Gaussian quantum channel into a virtual binary-input additive white Gaussian noise (BIAWGN) channel, enabling the use of modern errorcorrecting codes. In this work, we review the principles of multidimensional reconciliation, with a particular focus on high-dimensional constructions beyond the algebraic dimensions 1, 2, 4, 8. We describe the construction of the virtual channel, discuss practical coding schemes for reverse reconciliation, and analyse their integration with linear error-correcting codes. We also present an opensource simulation framework, HDirac, implementing multidimensional reconciliation for arbitrary dimensions, and use it to evaluate state-of-the-art LDPC codes. The results highlight key trade-offs between dimension, reconciliation efficiency, and frame error rate, providing practical guidance for CV-QKD system design.

Reduce dimensional quantum criticality for Non-Fermi liquids

Summary. This work introduces a reduced-dimension theoretical framework to study Non-Fermi liquid physics near quantum critical points. By spatially separating bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom, the authors significantly simplify the Renormalization Group analysis. This approach successfully eliminates problematic higher-order divergences, allowing for a controlled perturbative study of strongly correlated electron behavior.

Why it may be interesting. The technique of dimensional separation to regularize divergences is a powerful theoretical tool applicable to other strongly coupled systems where standard perturbative methods fail.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the theoretical challenge of understanding Non-Fermi liquid (NFL) behavior near quantum critical points (QCPs) in strongly correlated electronic systems.

Main result. By employing a reduced-dimension framework where bosons and fermions occupy different spatial dimensions, the authors successfully tame problematic higher-order divergences ($\log^2, \log^3$) found in conventional Renormalization Group (RG) analyses.

Method. The core method involves dimensional separation of degrees of freedom (bosons in bulk, fermions on a boundary) to simplify the RG analysis of boson-fermion coupling and calculate loop corrections.

Model / system. The study analyzes quantum field theories at finite density, modeling the breakdown of Landau Fermi liquid theory near a QCP, involving massless bosons coupled to a fermion Fermi surface.

Key observables. The finiteness of the tree-level boson exchange propagator ($\delta V_0$) and the reduction of 1-loop divergences from $\log^2/\log^3$ to simple logarithms are key diagnostics.

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis is performed using a perturbative $\epsilon$-expansion in $d = 3 - \epsilon$, focusing on the flow of coupling constants and anomalous dimensions.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a weakly coupled theory and focuses on marginal interactions; it also notes limitations regarding UV-IR mixing and the need for higher-loop calculations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the tree-level boson-mediated scattering process and 1-loop four-fermion coupling diagrams, comparing the conventional approach with the reduced-dimension scheme.

Paper structure. The paper builds by first identifying the divergence issues in standard treatments, then introducing the reduced-dimension framework, and finally demonstrating through explicit calculations (e.g., for $\delta V_0$ and $\Gamma_4$) that the divergences are controlled, leading to a tractable RG flow analysis.

Abstract

We present a reduced dimension theoretical framework for studying quantum field theories at finite density, providing a tractable model for investigating non-Fermi liquid (NFL) behavior near quantum phase transitions. Our approach departs from the standard paradigm by placing bosons and fermions in different spatial dimensions: bosonic fields reside in a $(d+1)$-dimensional bulk, while fermionic fields are confined on a $d$-dimensional boundary. This dimensional separation significantly simplifies the renormalization group (RG) analysis of gapless boson-fermion coupling. We demonstrate that the tree-level boson exchange contributions, which typically exhibit logarithmic divergences, become finite in our reduced-dimension scheme. Furthermore, the $\log^2$ and $\log^3$ divergences that characterize 1-loop four-fermion interactions in conventional treatments are reduced to logarithmic divergences within this framework, substantially improving the convergence properties of the perturbative expansion and allowing controlled theoretical analysis of NFL physics.

Summary. This theoretical work investigates how the spectral properties of synaptic matrices dictate the stability and dynamics of neural networks. By comparing various sparsity models, the authors show that local structural correlations can induce instabilities (positive outliers) that are robust even when global homeostatic balance is imposed. This highlights that local topology, rather than global constraints, governs the network's functional state.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical framework used to analyze spectral stability in complex, non-equilibrium systems like the brain is highly relevant to understanding the dynamics of open quantum systems or many-body systems near criticality.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper analyzes the spectral behavior of the synaptic matrix to understand the stability, dynamics, and memory capacity of complex, sparsely connected neural networks.

Main result. The spectral properties are highly sensitive to the type of connectivity structure (e.g., Power-Law vs. Uniform) and show that global homeostatic constraints (like row-sum zero) are insufficient to stabilize the network against instabilities arising from local spatial correlations.

Method. The authors use spectral analysis of the synaptic matrix eigenvalues, comparing ensemble averages across various connectivity models (UCE, PCE, ECE, SCE) and testing the impact of balance constraints.

Model / system. The system is a neural network whose connectivity is defined by a synaptic matrix J. The analysis moves from dense to highly sparse models, incorporating biological constraints like Dale's Law and spatial decay functions.

Abstract

The spectral behaviour of the synaptic matrix, representing the neuronal connection strengths, is an important tool to analyze the stability and transient dynamics of a typical brain as well as its learning process and memory capacity. The complexity of the brain due to large number of neurons as well as underlying transient mechanisms e.g. homeostasis, seizure or synaptic plasticity can lead to networks with time-varying degree and type of sparsity. This renders an exact determination of the synaptic matrix not only technically difficult but also meaningless, leaving its statistical analysis as the best available theoretical approach. This motivates us to pursue a spectral analysis of the synaptic matrix models with different type of sparsity and thereby analyze latter's role on various aspects of network dynamics and stability. Our results have potential relevance for detemining the type of synaptic sparsity required to induce a specific brain function or desired transient mechanism e.g for pharmacological effects or physiological modulators.

Summary. This work investigates the non-phononic flat band in amorphous solids by developing a resonant-coupling model. The model posits that the flat band arises from the resonant interaction between acoustic phonons and localized vibrational modes (QLVs). This framework successfully explains the observed spectral features and links the flat band's energy directly to the characteristic Boson Peak frequency.

Why it may be interesting. The concept of resonant coupling between propagating modes (phonons) and localized excitations (QLVs) is relevant to understanding energy dissipation and mode hybridization in complex, disordered quantum systems, which has parallels in open quantum systems theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the physical origin and nature of the non-phononic flat band observed in the dynamical structure factor of amorphous solids.

Main result. A resonant-coupling model successfully reproduces the key features of the flat band, establishing its connection to the boson peak via coupling between acoustic phonons and quasi-localized vibrations.

Method. The authors employ a resonant-coupling model derived from a soft-potential scenario, analyzing the renormalized phonon Green's function.

Model / system. The system is two- and three-dimensional amorphous solids. The model involves coupling acoustic phonons (phononic modes) with single-frequency quasi-localized vibrations (QLVs) via a resonant interaction term.

Key observables. Dynamical structure factor $S(q, \omega)$, the non-phononic flat band signal, and the Boson Peak (BP) in the vibrational density of states.

Important parameters / regimes. The critical wave vector $q^*$ (related to the first diffraction peak) and the coupling strength $g(q)$ which is hypothesized to scale with the static structure factor $S(q)$.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the flat band signal relative to the phononic peak, show the correlation between flat-band intensity and $S(q)$, and demonstrate the emergence of the flat band at the QLV frequency.

Abstract

Recent experiments and simulations provide compelling evidence for the emergence of a non-phononic flat band in the dynamical structure factor of two- and three-dimensional amorphous solids. This feature has been suggested to be connected to the excess in the reduced vibrational density of states of glasses, commonly known as the boson peak, and displays several apparently universal characteristics. First, it is nearly dispersionless, with an energy close to the boson-peak frequency. Second, its intensity is negligible below a critical wave vector of the order of the first diffraction peak. Third, its reduced intensity exhibits a strong correlation with the static structure factor. Here, we revisit the resonant-coupling model, a single-mode harmonic realization of the soft-potential scenario in which acoustic phonons interact with single frequency quasi-localized vibrations. We show that this minimal framework naturally reproduces the main features of the observed flat band and clarifies its connection to the boson peak.

Abstract

We consider the task of distinguishing between a single weak incoherent optical point source and two weak incoherent optical point sources located symmetrically about the first source. $θ$ is the separation between the two point sources scaled to the Point Spread Function (PSF) width in the image plane. Using an ideal focal plane array of intensity detectors (ideal direct imaging), we quantify the performance using the Bhattacharyya distance and find the scaling of its leading order term in terms of $θ$ in the sub-Rayleigh regime. A suite of previous analyses of this problem lacked a comprehensive analysis for when the amplitude spread function (ASF) of the imaging system has zeros and reported a scaling that we find to be incorrect. We complete this analysis by explicitly calculating the leading order term of the Bhattacharyya distance for ideal direct imaging with any ASF, for small $θ$ and show the difference in scaling based on the presence or absence of zeros in the ASF. This is similar to the ASF dependent performance in the task of estimating the separation between the two point sources and the task of detecting a change to an object. We then apply our results to the specific example of a Gaussian and a Sinc ASF and show good agreement with numerical calculations. Our results allow the accurate comparison of other measurement schemes with ideal direct imaging, and to the quantum limit.

Abstract

The strong coupling between spin, charge, and lattice degrees of freedom in perovskite oxides leads to an array of exotic phenomena, giving these materials rich phase diagrams that can include coupled orders. This is exemplified by the A-site doped ferrite La$_{1/3}$Sr$_{2/3}$FeO$_{3}$ (LSFO), which exhibits a coupled paramagnetic-antiferromagnetic and charge ordering phase transition at $\sim$190 K that has been well studied in thin films, bulk, and polycrystalline samples. However, the low temperature behavior of LSFO thin films below $\sim$100 K has not been thoroughly explored. This work uses several X-ray scattering and spectroscopy techniques to directly probe LSFO's magnetic and charge order down to low temperature. Using resonant X-ray scattering, we observe a complete suppression of LSFO's known antiferromagnetic and charge order below $\sim$25 K. Further spectroscopy and coherent scattering measurements provide insight into LSFO's electronic structure and domain dynamics in this new low temperature phase, and we propose possible explanations for the observed order suppression based on reduced dimensionality of domains in our thin films. Our findings provide insight into the effects of competing interactions in strongly correlated materials, particularly those with coupled orders.
