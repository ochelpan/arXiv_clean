High-rate and computationally-efficient seedless extractors for device-independent quantum cryptography

Authors: Simone Lin, Cameron Foreman, Lluis Masanes
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.31525v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a novel, efficient proof technique for constructing seedless randomness extractors essential for device-independent quantum cryptography. By employing a truncation method, the authors overcome previous limitations regarding the number of required experimental rounds. This results in a protocol that achieves the optimal key generation rate, advancing the practical security guarantees of quantum key distribution.

Why it may be interesting. The work provides rigorous, advanced theoretical tools (truncation methods, spectral analysis) for quantifying security in quantum communication protocols, which is fundamental to quantum information theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the need for high-rate and computationally-efficient seedless randomness extractors for device-independent (DI) quantum cryptography.

Main result. The authors present a new proof technique using a truncation method that achieves the optimal key rate of one bit per singlet for one-party DI randomness generation.

Method. The core method involves developing a new proof technique based on truncation to estimate protocol parameters, circumventing the need for many rounds previously required when bounding Bell inequality expectations.

Model / system. The protocol is set in the context of DI quantum cryptography, utilizing two non-communicating quantum devices (Alice and Bob) whose security relies on violating a Bell inequality (like CHSH). The analysis involves bounding the trace distance between actual and ideal key states.

Key observables. Bell Violation (CHSH inequality), Key Rate (bits per singlet), Trace Distance ($\epsilon$), and the raw key vector $\mathbf{a}_r$.

Important parameters / regimes. The asymptotic key rate, the error parameter $\epsilon$, and the threshold winning frequency $w_e$ for the CHSH game.

Assumptions / limitations. The security proof relies on bounding the trace distance using spectral decomposition and a truncation method, which is shown to be effective for the CHSH setting.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the positive region for the key rate $R$ as a function of the CHSH violation ($8w_e - 4$).

Paper structure. The paper develops proofs for several lemmas, including those related to state dilation (Lemma 1), spectral decomposition of measurement operators (Lemma 2), bounding the trace distance via truncation (Lemma 3), and finally establishing the key rate bound using linear algebra and probability tools (Lemma 7).

Abstract

Device-independent (DI) quantum cryptography provides secure cryptography with minimal trust in, or characterisation of, the used quantum devices. An essential component of DI protocols is the use of randomness extractors for privacy amplification, but these typically require an initial seed of randomness that introduces a potential vulnerability. To solve this problem, the security of seedless extractors was proven in Quantum 9, 1654 (2025). The core idea was to use the Bell violation of the raw data, rather than its min-entropy, as the extractor promise. However, the large fluctuations in the Bell inequality used required many rounds to precisely estimate the Bell violation, consuming substantial randomness and making the protocol very inefficient. In this work, we present a new proof technique based on a truncation method that allows the user to estimate the protocol parameters with an asymptotically vanishing fraction of rounds and, as a consequence, achieves the optimal rate of one key bit per singlet. Notably, we prove this result using seedless extractors that can be implemented efficiently.

Intrinsic locality dimension of quantum codes

Authors: Yimin Lu, Esther Xiaozhen Fu, Zi-Wen Liu
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.31441v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work introduces the intrinsic locality dimension, based on the Assouad dimension, as a unifying geometric parameter for quantum error-correcting codes. It demonstrates that this dimension characterizes code properties, generalizing existing bounds for topological and lattice codes. The theory provides a versatile mathematical foundation for understanding the fundamental geometric limits of fault tolerance.

Why it may be interesting. The geometric framework provides a powerful, abstract tool to constrain the fundamental capabilities of quantum error correction, linking algebraic structure to geometric measure theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a general, intrinsic geometric characterization of quantum error-correcting codes that is independent of the background geometry.

Main result. The intrinsic locality dimension, defined via the Assouad dimension, serves as a fundamental parameter unifying code properties and generalizing known bounds for code parameters and fault-tolerant gates.

Method. The authors employ concepts from fractal geometry, specifically the Assouad dimension, to analyze the connectivity graphs associated with stabilizer codes.

Model / system. The study focuses on stabilizer quantum codes, analyzing their connectivity graphs, which can be derived from topological structures, fractal sets, or lattice constructions.

Key observables. Intrinsic locality dimension ($eta$), code parameters ($k, d, n$), and the Assouad dimension of the connectivity graph.

Important parameters / regimes. The intrinsic dimension $eta$ can take non-integer values; the analysis depends on the structure of the underlying manifold or lattice.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the existence of good triangulations for topological codes and quasi-convexity for fractal constructions to establish dimension equivalences.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Assouad dimension concept using covering numbers and show examples of fractal structures (like the Sierpiński gasket) whose dimensions are calculated.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining the intrinsic locality dimension using the Assouad dimension, applies this to specific code types (topological, fractal, lattice), derives general bounds, and concludes with implications for thermal stability.

Abstract

Quantum error-correcting codes are a cornerstone of quantum computing, with broad and profound connections to physics and mathematics. In this work, we introduce the notion of intrinsic locality dimension of stabilizer codes that is independent of any background geometry and naturally incorporates flexible architectures and accommodates noninteger values, drawing on mathematical machinery from fractal geometry and geometric measure theory. Important scenarios include topological codes and algebraic codes such as bivariate-bicycle-type codes. We show how the intrinsic dimension serves as a fundamental organizing parameter that unifies code properties. In particular, we prove general limitations on code parameters and compatible fault-tolerant logical gates induced by the intrinsic dimension, generalizing the Bravyi--Poulin--Terhal and Bravyi--König bounds for regular topological codes, respectively. Furthermore, we discuss implications on thermal properties, presenting a conditional no-go result for self-correcting quantum memories in dimension $3-ε$ for any $ε>0$. Our theory lays a versatile and unifying mathematical foundation for studying the fundamental capabilities and geometric implementations of quantum error correction and fault tolerance.

Optimization of multisite reactions in complex compartmentalized media

Authors: T. V. Mendes, T. Guérin
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.31418v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical work analyzes how confining a reaction target within a compartment characterized by sub-diffusion can optimize the rate of multisite chemical reactions. By deriving scaling laws for mean first passage times, the authors show that the resulting reaction time can be minimized at an optimal compartment size, demonstrating kinetic acceleration despite local transport slowing.

Why it may be interesting. The core concept of kinetic optimization via geometric confinement is relevant to understanding reaction rates in complex biological environments, which often exhibit fractal or disordered structures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how the geometry and dynamics of a complex, compartmentalized medium influence the kinetics of multisite chemical reactions.

Main result. The presence of a crowded compartment can accelerate the reaction rate compared to an unconfined system, provided the target is weakly reactive, by optimizing the search process.

Method. The authors derive analytical expressions for mean first passage times and reaction times by adapting arguments from random walk theory in fractal and diffusive media.

Model / system. The model involves a random walker searching for two or more activation events (dual-site reactions) within a compartment exhibiting fractal sub-diffusion, contrasting this with classical 3D diffusion outside.

Key observables. Mean reaction time ($\langle T angle$), mean first passage time ($\langle au^*_r angle$), and the speed-up factor relative to the no-compartment case.

Important parameters / regimes. Compartment size ($R$), reaction probability ($p$), time window ($\Delta$), and diffusion exponents ($d_w, d_f$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the weakly reactive limit ($p \ll 1$) and the regime where the time window $\Delta$ is much smaller than the characteristic escape time ($\Delta \ll \langle au_\infty angle$).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the optimization of $\langle T angle$ with respect to compartment radius ($R$) and the comparison of the acceleration factor ($\langle T angle / \langle T angle_{n.c.}$) versus reactivity ($p$).

Paper structure. The paper builds from calculating mean first passage times in confined/fractal media to deriving the reaction time for multisite events, culminating in showing the optimal size $R_m$ that minimizes $\langle T angle$.

Abstract

In complex media, transport and geometric properties deeply influence the kinetics of random encounters between reactants. Here, we consider the situation where a random walker, moving in a regularly diffusing medium, has to reach and activate a target located inside a compartment characterized by fractal (obstructed) sub-diffusion. We focus on dual-site reactions, which end when two activation events occur within a given time window. Each activation event happens with a finite probability whenever the random walker visits the target. For weakly reactive targets, we demonstrate that the reaction time can be minimized for an optimal compartment size and can even be accelerated when compared to the same system without compartment. Our analytical predictions are validated through simulations of a random walker on a cubic lattice, where some sites inside the compartment are obstructed at the critical percolation threshold. Our theory illustrates the fact that adding a crowded compartment around a target, even if it slows down the motion in its vicinity, can accelerate the kinetics of complex reactions, especially for weakly reactive targets.

Real-Time Quantum Error Correction System Stack: Architecture, Algorithms, and Engineering Practice

Authors: Yaojian Chen, Chun-Yang Luan, Peilin Zheng, Xianghong Zeng, Jia-Yi Hou, Zhuo Fu, Yirong Jin, Fei Wang, Guangwen Yang, Dingshun Lv

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 1/5

Summary. This white paper addresses the engineering gap preventing scalable quantum computation by analyzing the real-time constraints of Quantum Error Correction (QEC). It proposes a detailed, multi-layer system architecture and benchmarks decoders to show that system latency, not just algorithmic speed, is the primary bottleneck. The work is vital for guiding the development of fault-tolerant quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a crucial systems-level roadmap for quantum computing hardware, detailing the necessary classical control stack and timing budgets required to make fault-tolerant computation practical.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is transitioning Quantum Error Correction (QEC) from theoretical feasibility to a scalable, real-time systems engineering challenge for Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computing (FTQC).

Main result. The bottleneck for scalable QEC is not purely algorithmic but resides in system-level engineering constraints, particularly managing tail latency and end-to-end data path coordination.

Method. The authors propose a six-layer reference architecture and benchmark major decoder algorithms (e.g., MWPM, BP+OSD) across different code types (Surface, qLDPC) to quantify real-time readiness.

Model / system. The system is modeled as a multi-stage data path: syndrome acquisition, classical decoding, error inference, and correction application, which must operate within strict time budgets.

Key observables. QEC round time, tail latency (p99), sustained decode throughput ($\mu$), and the accumulated decoding backlog $B(t)$.

Important parameters / regimes. The operational regime shifts between streaming (Clifford gates) and synchronization barriers (non-Clifford gates like T gates), which dictate the required feedforward latency.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis often assumes a uniform depolarizing noise model, though the paper notes that real-device effects like leakage and crosstalk are omitted in current benchmarks.

Figures summary. The notes reference a multi-stage data path diagram (Figure 1) and tables comparing various decoders and platform-specific timing constraints.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the QEC cycle, identifies the shift from algorithmic to engineering bottlenecks, proposes a layered reference architecture, and benchmarks performance across different physical platforms and code families.

Abstract

Quantum error correction (QEC) is transitioning from physical feasibility demonstrations to systems engineering challenges. Google has achieved below-threshold performance on distance-5/7 surface codes, while Riverlane and Rigetti have demonstrated hardware-integrated low-latency feedback loops. These milestones indicate that the core challenge of real-time decoding has shifted from algorithmic capability to system-level engineering. However, a substantial engineering gap remains between laboratory demonstrations and scalable fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC). This white paper addresses three questions: (1) Where are the real bottlenecks in real-time QEC: beyond average decoder speed, the constraints lie in QEC round time, tail latency, and end-to-end data path coordination; (2) How mature are mainstream decoder algorithms: we benchmark the major decoders for both surface codes and quantum low-density parity-check (qLDPC) codes, evaluating their real-time readiness; (3) What system stack do we propose: a six-layer reference architecture from syndrome acquisition to logical operations, with interface definitions and latency budget models. Our results quantify the gap between current decoder performance and real-time requirements, and identify the architectural choices needed to close it.

Summary. This paper introduces a novel Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) pre-screening technique to construct shallow, hardware-efficient quantum circuits for molecular electronic structure calculations. By using Givens rotations, the method significantly reduces circuit depth compared to standard UCC ansätze, enabling chemical accuracy on current noisy quantum devices. This provides a crucial, tunable trade-off between computational power and hardware feasibility for quantum chemistry.

Why it may be interesting. This work directly addresses the gap between theoretical quantum chemistry requirements and the physical limitations of near-term quantum hardware by proposing a resource-aware ansatz construction technique.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is developing quantum algorithms for molecular electronic structure simulation that are shallow enough to run reliably on current, noisy quantum hardware.

Main result. The proposed QMC-Givens ansatz provides a significantly shallower and more hardware-efficient alternative to conventional Unitary Coupled Cluster (UCC) methods, especially in the presence of noise.

Method. The authors combine Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) pre-screening to identify crucial wavefunction components with the use of Givens rotation ansätze within the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) framework.

Model / system. The work focuses on simulating the electronic structure of small molecules ($ ext{LiH}$, $ ext{BeH}_2$, $ ext{N}_2$) using fermionic operators mapped to qubits. Benchmarking was performed on the Quantinuum System Model H1.

Key observables. Ground state energy, circuit depth, Hardware Quantum Credits (HQC) cost, and the Spearman rank correlation coefficient ($ ho$) quantifying parameter importance.

Important parameters / regimes. The trade-off between circuit expressivity (depth) and circuit depth is a key parameter space explored, tailored for current high-noise devices.

Assumptions / limitations. The controlled Givens ansatz is used for its relative ease of classical implementation; furthermore, the final analysis suggests that zero-initialization might outperform QMC-initialization for certain systems.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the correlation between early determinant spawning and final amplitudes (Fig. 1, 2), compare energy error vs. parameters (Fig. 3), and compare initialization strategies (Fig. 4).

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QMC-Givens method as an improvement over UCC for quantum chemistry. It details the QMC pre-screening procedure, benchmarks the ansatz on molecular systems using hardware metrics (HQC), and concludes by demonstrating the practical advantages of shallow circuits on noisy hardware.

Abstract

Quantum computers hold great promise for molecular simulation, but noise remains a fundamental obstacle. We introduce a Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) pre-screening procedure that constructs compact, physically motivated Givens rotation ansätze tailored to realistic quantum hardware. By identifying the most important wavefunction contributions early in a QMC simulation, we build circuits that are shallower that conventional alternatives while preserving number symmetry. Benchmarked on Quantinuum System Model H1, QMC-prescreened circuits outperform more complex ansätze under realistic noise conditions. The method offers a practical path toward chemical accuracy on quantum devices, by providing an adjustable trade-off between expressivity and circuit depth to generate shallow circuits suited to current high-noise devices, as well as deeper, more expressive circuits that can be deployed on future lower-noise devices.

Symmetry-Resolved Second Harmonic Generation in Quantum and Functional Materials

Authors: Xiaoyu Guo, Chang Jae Roh, Youngjun Ahn
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.30584v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: interference shaping light 1/5

Summary. This review details how Second Harmonic Generation (SHG) acts as a symmetry-resolved optical technique to probe hidden order parameters in quantum and functional materials. By analyzing polarization-resolved rotational anisotropy (RA-SHG), researchers can map out the symmetry-allowed components of the nonlinear susceptibility tensor. This methodology provides a crucial link between fundamental group theory and the measurable electronic and magnetic states of complex condensed matter systems.

Why it may be interesting. The connection between symmetry group theory, nonlinear optics, and emergent electronic order parameters provides a powerful, non-perturbative diagnostic tool for studying symmetry breaking in strongly correlated systems, which is highly relevant to modern condensed matter theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The central problem is identifying the symmetry of ordered phases in quantum and functional materials, as these phases are defined by the symmetries they break.

Main result. The paper establishes that Second Harmonic Generation (SHG) spectroscopy, particularly polarization-resolved rotational anisotropy (RA-SHG), serves as a powerful, symmetry-resolved optical probe to map out these hidden order parameters.

Method. The methodology combines rigorous quantum mechanical derivations of nonlinear susceptibility tensors ($\chi$) with group-theoretical constraints (Landau theory) to predict symmetry-allowed SHG responses.

Model / system. The analysis focuses on condensed matter systems, including polar, magnetic, and electronic materials, using the nonlinear optical process of SHG ($\omega o 2\omega$). The theoretical framework involves multipole expansions (ED, MD, EQ) and symmetry analysis within point groups like $C_{4v}$.

Key observables. Nonlinear susceptibility tensor components ($\chi_{ijk}$), SHG intensity as a function of rotation angle ($\varphi$), and the symmetry-allowed coupling terms related to order parameters ($\phi$).

Important parameters / regimes. Crystallographic and magnetic point group symmetries, polarization states (P/C, SS/SP/PS/PP), and the order parameter magnitude ($\phi$).

Assumptions / limitations. The quantum mechanical formalism is rigorous but may struggle with materials exhibiting strong electronic correlations or complex orbital hybridization; the symmetry analysis relies on the invariance principles of the parent point group.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the multipole sources (ED, MD, EQ) for SHG radiation and schematically depict the various geometries for RA-SHG measurements (normal vs. oblique incidence).

Paper structure. The paper progresses from the basic theoretical background of SHG sources (multipole expansion) to the quantum mechanical derivation of susceptibility tensors, followed by a comprehensive review of RA-SHG applications across various symmetry-breaking phenomena, and concluding with future research directions.

Abstract

Second harmonic generation (SHG) has evolved from a probe of noncentrosymmetric crystals into a symmetry-resolved optical method for identifying order parameters in quantum and functional materials. In particular, polarization-resolved rotational anisotropy (RA) measurements of SHG can connect nonlinear susceptibility tensors to the crystallographic and magnetic point groups of the underlying materials. This capability is especially powerful when the ordered state is weak, spatially confined, multipolar, magnetic, or hidden from conventional linear probe techniques. In this review article, we provide a comprehensive overview of RA-SHG studies across a broad range of condensed matter systems. We begin with basic theoretical background for the multipole origins of SHG radiation, the construction of nonlinear susceptibility tensors, and group-theoretical framework connecting tensor components to order parameters. We then review the applications of RA-SHG to polar materials, magnetic orders, and other hidden electronic materials. Finally, we outline challenges and future research directions for using SHG to reveal, image, and control hidden, intertwined, and nonequilibrium phases in quantum and functional materials.

Summary. This paper introduces a novel, denser planar surface code architecture by efficiently packing twist defects into a 2D hex grid. Abstract

We present a quantum code implementable on a regular $2$D hex grid with an estimated encoding rate up to $4.5\times$ of that of a rotated surface code patch using circuit-level noise in a one- and two-qubit $10^{-3}$ error uniform depolarizing model. Our approach is based on yoking a dense packing of surface code twist defects, enabled by new stabilizer measurement cycles with an optimal four layers of nearest-neighbor two-qubit gates, almost no distance-reducing hook errors, and efficient decoding. We demonstrate a space-efficient architecture for computing on densely packed logical qubits, including new padding-free lattice surgery protocols in an optimal bounding box of $2d^2$ data and measurement qubits per patch. Assuming a $1μ$s surface code cycle time and a $10μ$s reaction time, these developments enable chemically accurate ground state phase estimation of a broad class of `utility-scale' electronic structure simulation problems such as the $108$ spin-orbital FeMoco-based nitrogen fixation catalyst in under a month with $89$k noisy superconducting qubits. We elucidate a Pareto frontier of space-time trade-offs and find a minimum physical quantum volume of $1.3$ mega-qubit-hours. These correspond to a $36\times$ space and $6.6\times$ spacetime improvement, respectively, over our previous state-of-the-art minimum-Toffoli resource estimates (Phys. Rev. X 15, 041016).

Summary. This paper investigates the ambiguity problem inherent in applying the bootstrap method to quantum Hamiltonians with mixed potentials. The authors demonstrate that the method fails for such potentials but propose and test several resolutions, including imposing additional positivity constraints. These resolutions successfully recover the correct energy spectrum for specific cases, providing a more robust theoretical framework.

Why it may be interesting. The work tackles a fundamental theoretical limitation of a powerful mathematical tool (the bootstrap method) used to determine quantum spectra, offering systematic ways to resolve ambiguities arising from operator structure.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The bootstrap method for quantum mechanics suffers from an ambiguity problem, failing to yield the correct energy spectrum when the potential contains mixed functional forms (e.g., polynomial and exponential terms).

Main result. The ambiguity can be resolved by imposing additional positivity conditions, such as $M_{\cosh} \succeq 0$, which successfully reproduce the true energy spectrum for certain potentials.

Method. The authors use the bootstrap method, which constrains energy eigenvalues by enforcing the positive semi-definite condition ($M \succeq 0$) on a constructed bootstrap matrix derived from expectation values.

Model / system. The analysis focuses on quantum mechanical Hamiltonians, specifically comparing the anharmonic oscillator ($H_{AHO}$) with a mixed potential $H_{mixed} = rac{1}{2} \hat{p}^2 + (\hat{x} - L)^2 + \cosh(\hat{x})$.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption is that the energy spectrum must satisfy the positivity condition on the bootstrap matrix; resolutions involve imposing additional constraints like parity or specific positivity inequalities.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the allowed regions in the $(E, \langle \hat{x} angle)$-plane for both the anharmonic oscillator (convergence shown) and the mixed potential (failure/resolution shown using different constraints).

Paper structure. Abstract

The bootstrap method for quantum mechanics is a powerful tool for computing the energy eigenvalues of a Hamiltonian. However, we point out that this method suffers from an ambiguity problem: it fails to yield the correct spectrum when the potential contains different types of functions, such as polynomial and exponential terms. Similarly, the bootstrap method may break down when evaluating the expectation values of operators of different types. This issue can arise in a wide range of systems, including statistical models and matrix models. We propose three possible resolutions to this problem.

Competing heterogeneities shape ordering via higher-order interactions

Summary. This work analyzes the phase transitions of the Simplicial Ising Model on hypergraphs with varying structural disorder. By developing a cavity method framework, the authors show that size and degree heterogeneities fundamentally alter the transition type, leading to complex double transitions. The findings highlight the intricate interplay between these structural features in collective ordering phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. The detailed analysis of phase transitions driven by structural disorder (heterogeneity) in interacting systems is a core topic in statistical mechanics, providing theoretical tools applicable to complex network dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how structural heterogeneity, specifically in group size and node degree, influences the collective phase transitions in systems governed by higher-order interactions.

Main result. Size heterogeneity sharpens the transition via large-group unanimity, while degree heterogeneity softens it; the coexistence of both can lead to complex double transitions and hysteretic regions modulated by cross-order degree correlations.

Model / system. The system is the Simplicial Ising Model (SIM) defined on heterogeneous hypergraphs. The Hamiltonian involves higher-order interactions ($\delta_{\{S_i\}_{i\in\partial_a}}$) that enforce that all spins within a hyperedge must be identical.

Abstract

Higher-order interactions admit richer structural heterogeneity than pairwise networks. To understand how heterogeneity impacts collective phenomena we develop a framework based on the cavity method and apply it to the simplicial Ising model on heterogeneous hypergraphs. Unlike in homogeneous structures, group size and node degree play fundamentally different roles: size heterogeneity sharpens the transition via large-group unanimity, while degree heterogeneity softens it as hubs cooperatively seed ordering with non-hubs. Under either type of heterogeneity, continuous--discontinuous double transitions can arise, where the symmetry-breaking continuous transition is driven by pairs or by hubs, respectively. When both heterogeneities coexist, cross-order degree correlations further modulate the phase diagram, with anticorrelation delaying the group-driven discontinuous jump and broadening the hysteretic region. Our results reveal the intricate interplay between size and degree heterogeneities in collective phenomena beyond pairwise interactions.

Abstract

Strongly correlated electrons can be treated with a configuration interaction of Slater determinants grouped by number of unpaired electrons with exponential cost. The first two papers in this series demonstrated that single reference methods built from Richardson-Gaudin states gave results of similar quality at polynomial cost. In this contribution, the states are simplified substantially yielding the perfect-pairing state as a reference along with its low-lying excitations. The states are much simpler, the computational cost is substantially reduced, and there is no sacrifice in numerical accuracy. Second-order Epstein-Nesbet perturbative corrections for the valence electrons are similar in quality to the complete active space self-consistent field.

Summary. This work introduces a software platform to systematically analyze and benchmark various random number generators, including LFSRs and Mersenne Twister. It demonstrates that these classical generators are fundamentally predictable by reconstructing their internal states using advanced statistical and machine learning techniques. The findings strongly advocate for the use of quantum random number generators due to their inherent, non-algorithmic source of true randomness.

Why it may be interesting. While not directly focused on quantum dynamics, the paper's rigorous comparison between deterministic algorithmic randomness and non-deterministic quantum randomness provides a strong theoretical justification for the necessity of quantum sources in high-security quantum information protocols.

Main problem. The paper addresses the fundamental problem of generating and analyzing the predictability of random bit sequences, comparing classical pseudo-random generators (LFSR, MT) against truly random quantum sources.

Main result. Classical PRNGs are shown to be deterministic and predictable using statistical and AI-based reconstruction techniques, whereas quantum random sequences offer superior, inherent unpredictability for security applications.

Method. The authors developed a software platform to generate, analyze, and attempt to 'hack' the internal states and parameters of LFSRs and MTs, contrasting these methods with testing quantum random number streams.

Important parameters / regimes. LFSR feedback polynomial coefficients, MT state vector size (e.g., 624 states), and the required input bit length for successful state reconstruction.

Assumptions / limitations. The predictability of PRNGs relies on exploiting underlying algorithmic patterns, and the analysis assumes quantum randomness is fundamentally non-deterministic.

Paper structure. The paper is structured around developing a comprehensive software platform with two modes: Generation Mode (implementing LFSR, MT, and hybrid combinations) and Hacking Mode (applying statistical, algebraic, and deep learning techniques to estimate internal states).

Abstract

Generating reliable random and pseudo-random sequences is important in many electronic and signal processing systems, such as secure communications, radar, spread-spectrum methods, and autonomous platforms. Although true and quantum random number generators provide stronger unpredictability, classical pseudo-random number generators, including Linear Feedback Shift Registers (LFSRs) and the Mersenne Twister (MT), are still widely used because they are efficient and easy to implement. This work introduces a user-friendly software platform for generating, analyzing, and evaluating the predictability of pseudo-random bit sequences. The software supports two main functions: generating sequences using classical PRNGs and hybrid combinations, and analyzing input sequences through statistical measures and data-driven methods. In particular, hybrid LFSR-MT structures are studied to examine how they affect sequence complexity and resistance to prediction. The platform also includes machine-learning and deep-learning tools to investigate when deterministic PRNGs may remain partially predictable, even when their structure becomes more complex. The results show that algorithmic random sequence generators have inherent limitations in terms of unpredictability, which supports the use of quantum random sequences in security-critical applications. A comparative study between classical LFSR-MT sequences and quantum random sequences shows that quantum randomness offers higher unpredictability due to its non-deterministic physical origin. The potential use of quantum random sequences in jamming applications is also discussed, highlighting their improved robustness against prediction-based attacks. Overall, the proposed software provides a practical tool for analyzing, comparing, and benchmarking random sequence generators in modern electronic, sensing, and quantum-enabled communication systems.
