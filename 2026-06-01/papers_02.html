A mathematical framework for dynamic emergent constraints in climate science

Authors: Francesco Ragone, Valerio Lucarini

Summary. This work establishes a mathematical framework for dynamic emergent constraints in climate science by generalizing linear response theory. It uses proxy Green's functions and causality indices to determine how relationships between observables (like temperature and precipitation) are constrained by the observation time scale. This provides a powerful, mathematically grounded protocol for analyzing complex, non-equilibrium physical systems.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical machinery developed—using linear response theory, Green's functions, and causality indices—is highly transferable to analyzing non-equilibrium dynamics in open quantum systems, such as those involving dissipation or environmental coupling.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a rigorous mathematical framework for quantifying 'dynamic emergent constraints' between different physical observables in complex systems like the climate, aiming to reduce projection uncertainties.

Main result. The framework generalizes traditional constraints to 'integral dynamic emergent constraints,' showing that the existence and form of these constraints depend critically on the causality of the system's proxy Green's function and the coarse-graining time scale.

Method. The study employs linear response theory, utilizing susceptibility ratios and proxy Green's functions derived from climate model simulations to establish integral relationships between observable responses to external forcing.

Model / system. The analysis is applied to global warming simulations using the MPI-ESM climate model, focusing on observables such as global surface temperature, precipitation, and AMOC intensity.

Key observables. Global surface temperature (T_s), Precipitation (P), Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (M), and their respective responses ($\delta\Phi$).

Important parameters / regimes. Coarse-graining time scale ($ au$), which dictates whether the relationship is instantaneous, directional, or reciprocal.

Assumptions / limitations. The theory relies on the assumption that the system dynamics can be described by a stochastic dynamical system amenable to linear response theory, and that the relationship can be captured by the proxy Green's function.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the causality index ($C_{\Phi_1\Phi_2}$) as a function of the time scale $ au$, showing how the predictive power and directionality of constraints change with the observation window.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining the problem using linear response theory to generalizing constraints into integral forms. It then applies this theory to climate model data, analyzing how the causality index and proxy Green's functions reveal the dependence of emergent constraints on the coarse-graining time scale $ au$.

Abstract

Emergent constraints in climate science are empirical relations that link the response to a forcing of a physical observable to the properties of other observables, with the aim of reducing climate change projection uncertainties. Here we use recent results in linear response theory to develop a mathematical framework for dynamic emergent constraints, a class of emergent constraints linking the response of different observables to the same forcing. We show how traditional dynamic emergent constraints are a special case of more general relations, that we call integral dynamic emergent constraints. These relations allow to compute the response of a predictand as the convolution of the response of a predictor and the proxy Green's function of the predictand-predictor pair. The conditions for the existence of integral emergent constraints are related to the causality of the proxy Green's function and the time scales at which the system is observed. We apply this framework to global warming simulations with the MPI-ESM climate model, to study dynamic emergent constraints between different observables. These results allow to put the theory of dynamic emergent constraints on firm mathematical ground, and suggest a protocol to identify necessary conditions for the existence of such relations in climate data.

Summary. This work analyzes how active noise modifies fluctuation-driven first-order phase transitions in condensed matter systems. The authors show that increasing activity systematically suppresses the fluctuation corrections, thereby stabilizing the ordered phase at higher temperatures. This establishes activity as a powerful, generic control parameter for tuning the transition strength.

Why it may be interesting. The study provides a general theoretical framework for understanding how non-equilibrium driving forces (activity) can tune the nature of phase transitions, moving them away from purely equilibrium predictions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates how introducing activity, modeled by correlated noise, modifies fluctuation-induced first-order phase transitions, particularly those governed by the Brazovskii mechanism.

Main result. Activity systematically suppresses the fluctuation effects driving the first-order transition, shifting the transition to higher temperatures and enhancing ordering without causing spinodal instability.

Method. The analysis employs response field formalism, Dyson equations, and functional integral methods to calculate renormalized propagators and effective potentials in the presence of colored noise.

Model / system. The system is modeled by a scalar order parameter $\phi(x, t)$ governed by a relaxational dynamics equation derived from a Swift–Hohenberg-like free energy functional. Activity is introduced via non-equilibrium, spatiotemporally correlated noise $\zeta(x, t)$.

Key observables. Renormalized mass parameter ($r$), static structure factor $S(q)$, and the Binder cumulant $U_Q^4$ for the nematic order parameter $Q$.

Important parameters / regimes. Activity strength (controlled by noise correlation time $ au$ and length $\xi$), and the bare parameters of the free energy functional ($r_0, u, \kappa$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on perturbative expansions (e.g., decoupling interaction terms) and assumes the system dynamics are well-described by the given relaxational equation.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the suppression of fluctuation effects (e.g., Fig. 2 showing $r$ vs. $ au$) and the enhancement of ordering, with the Binder cumulant (Fig. 4) confirming the transition shift with activity.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the theoretical framework using response field methods to analyze the effect of activity on the transition character. It then develops the necessary equations of state and compares results across different activity limits ($ au o 0$ vs. $ au o \infty$), culminating in numerical simulations confirming the suppression of fluctuations.

Abstract

Fluctuations can drive otherwise continuous phase transitions to first order through the Brazovskii mechanism. We study how these fluctuation-induced transitions are modified in active systems by introducing nonequilibrium spatiotemporally correlated noise. We show that, while the transition remains fluctuation-induced first order, activity systematically suppresses these fluctuation effects, shifting the transition to higher temperatures and rendering it increasingly weakly first order. As a result, ordering is enhanced without inducing a spinodal instability of the isotropic phase, as confirmed by direct numerical simulations. In the strong-activity limit, fluctuation effects disappear and mean-field behavior is recovered. Our results identify activity as a generic control parameter for tuning the strength of fluctuation-induced first-order transitions.

Summary. This theoretical work analyzes the diffusion and mean-squared displacements of rough particles in a granular gas. By incorporating particle roughness via restitution coefficients, the authors show that particle motion is highly sensitive to these mechanical properties. The key finding is the emergence of ultraslow, logarithmic diffusion at long times, providing insights into non-equilibrium transport phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on classical granular dynamics, the study of non-equilibrium energy dissipation, cooling laws (Haff's law), and anomalous diffusion ($\log t$ scaling) provides theoretical tools relevant to understanding energy relaxation in complex many-body systems, which has parallels in quantum many-body physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the diffusion coefficients and mean-squared displacements (MSDs) of rough particles within a polydisperse granular gas undergoing a homogeneous cooling state.

Main result. The MSD exhibits ultraslow motion characterized by a logarithmic time-dependence ($\langle R^2(t) angle \propto \log t$) at long times, and the particle motility is strongly influenced by surface roughness.

Method. The analysis uses kinetic theory approaches, deriving MSDs from velocity correlation functions by solving coupled differential equations for granular temperatures.

Model / system. The system is a polydisperse granular gas modeled by binary collisions, incorporating particle roughness via normal ($\epsilon$) and tangential ($eta$) restitution coefficients. The dynamics are analyzed in the homogeneous cooling state.

Key observables. Diffusion coefficients ($D_k(t)$), Mean-Squared Displacements ($\langle \mathbf{R}^2(t) angle$), and partial granular temperatures ($T_{tr,k}, T_{rot,k}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Normal and tangential restitution coefficients ($\epsilon, eta$), particle size/mass polydispersity, and time scales ($ au_0$).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be in a rarefied gas regime dominated by binary collisions, and approximations like the adiabatic limit and molecular chaos hypothesis are employed.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the time dependence of partial MSDs for ternary mixtures, showing dependence on parameters like $eta$ and particle mass/density.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the system dynamics and collision rules to deriving the MSD in terms of velocity correlation functions, applying approximations like the adiabatic limit, and finally presenting the long-time asymptotic behavior of the MSD.

Abstract

We investigate the diffusion coefficients and mean-squared displacements in a polydisperse granular gas in a homogeneous cooling state by considering the roughness of the particles. We study their dependence on the normal and tangential restitution coefficients. We show that the motility of particles is strongly affected by their mechanical properties and surface characteristics.

Summary. This work introduces 'diabolical textures' as a new source of topological phenomena in inhomogeneous quantum systems, modeled using fermions on a lattice. The authors show that these textures create distinct gapped phases separated by unique 'trap-scaling' critical points. By combining continuum field theory with homotopy methods, they establish a general classification scheme for these robust topological features.

Why it may be interesting. The concept of 'diabolical textures' provides a powerful, geometrically motivated framework for classifying topological phases beyond standard symmetry protection, which is highly relevant for designing novel quantum materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates a novel class of topological phenomena arising from the adiabatic spatial variation of quantum states, termed 'diabolical textures,' in inhomogeneous electronic systems.

Main result. These distinct topological textures lead to gapped states separated by unique 'trap-scaling' critical points, which are stable against weak perturbations and can be systematically classified using homotopy theory.

Method. The analysis combines continuum field theory (bosonization) with finite-size scaling arguments, mapping the low-energy physics near criticality to a 2D Dirac fermion in a magnetic field.

Model / system. The primary model is a non-interacting fermion system described by a lattice Hamiltonian, exemplified by the spatially embedded Thouless pump (Rice-Mele model). The theory is generalized to arbitrary spatial dimensions and symmetries.

Key observables. Spectral gap ($\Delta$), entanglement entropy ($S(b)$), and the critical scaling exponent ($\vartheta$).

Important parameters / regimes. The parameters defining the texture, such as $\delta$ and $J$, and the critical point characterized by $\alpha^* = \pm 1$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes slow spatial variation of parameters and relies on bosonization techniques near the critical points.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Thouless pump's spatial embedding, the finite-size scaling of the spectral gap ($\Delta \sim L^{-1/2}$), and the comparison of single-particle energy spectra between lattice and continuum descriptions.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from introducing the physical model (Thouless pump/Rice-Mele), deriving the low-energy continuum theory, establishing the unique trap-scaling critical behavior, and finally generalizing the classification framework using homotopy theory.

Abstract

We present a new class of topological phenomena in inhomogeneous systems arising from the adiabatic spatial embedding of parametrized families of quantum states such as charge pumps and their generalizations. We demonstrate that each topologically distinct class of these "diabolical textures" gives rise to distinct gapped states that are separated by "trap-scaling" critical points. When the texture varies sufficiently rapidly in space, the critical line terminates abruptly, producing an "unnecessary critical" surface. We demonstrate our results using a microscopic model of non-interacting fermions with a spatially embedded Thouless pump. We study its phase diagram comprehensively and establish its stability to arbitrary perturbations, including interactions, in the vicinity of the critical regions. For systems in arbitrary spatial dimensions and global symmetries, we present a framework to systematically classify diabolical textures using Kitaev's $Ω$ spectrum conjecture.

Summary. This work advances Functional Renormalization Group Density Functional Theory (FRG-DFT) to quantum thermodynamics by benchmarking it against the exactly solvable single-site Bose-Hubbard model. The authors identify that a self-interaction correction term is necessary for formal consistency, and they demonstrate that the maximum-entropy closure scheme provides the most accurate description of thermodynamic observables and density correlations. This establishes a controlled foundation for deriving quantum many-body functionals.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous development of FRG-DFT provides a powerful, non-perturbative tool for calculating quantum thermodynamic properties, offering a systematic path to derive ab initio functionals applicable across condensed matter, ultracold atoms, and nuclear physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to develop a systematic and controllable functional route using Functional Renormalization Group (FRG) to derive energy functionals for quantum many-body systems in the context of quantum thermodynamics.

Main result. The maximum-entropy closure scheme (Scheme IV) is shown to be the most accurate, successfully reproducing exact thermodynamic quantities and the low-temperature oscillatory structure of density correlators.

Method. The core method is FRG-DFT, which involves deriving flow equations for the effective action by systematically truncating an infinite hierarchy of equations derived from the imaginary-time path integral.

Model / system. The primary testbed is the single-site Bose-Hubbard model, which is analytically solvable and allows for rigorous benchmarking against exact finite-temperature thermodynamics.

Key observables. Free energy, chemical potential, and connected density correlators ($ ilde{G}^{(2)}, ilde{G}^{(3)}, ilde{G}^{(4)}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature ($T$), interaction strength ($g$), and particle density ($ ho$ or $N$). The low-temperature (strong-coupling) regime is a key diagnostic test.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation requires a careful Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation and the inclusion of a Self-Interaction Correction (SIC) term to maintain formal consistency. The analysis relies on comparing results across various closure approximations.

Figures summary. Figures compare the flow evolution of $ ilde{G}^{(2)}$ and other correlators across different closure schemes (I, II, III, IV), demonstrating that Scheme IV best reproduces exact results, especially the oscillatory structure at low temperatures.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the general FRG-DFT formalism, then applies it to the single-site Bose-Hubbard model, deriving the necessary SIC term. It then systematically compares four different truncation/closure schemes (I-IV) by benchmarking their predictions for thermodynamic observables against exact solutions.

Abstract

The functional renormalization group provides a nonperturbative and systematically improvable route to constructing density functionals for quantum many-body systems from microscopic Hamiltonians. Here we advance this program by benchmarking functional-renormalization-group density functional theory (FRG-DFT) against the exact thermodynamics of the single-site Bose-Hubbard model. This model provides an ideal testing ground because it is analytically solvable, yet remains subtle in the imaginary-time coherent-state path integral, where a naive continuum treatment generates a spurious self-interaction. We show that a careful Hubbard-Stratonovich derivation identifies the self-interaction correction term that must be included in the FRG-DFT flow to recover the exact thermodynamics. We then systematically compare several closures of the resulting hierarchy of flow equations for the free energy, chemical potential, and connected density correlators over broad ranges of density, temperature, and interaction strength. The benchmark shows that the free energy is comparatively robust, whereas the chemical potential and fluctuation observables provide much sharper diagnostics of the hierarchy closure. A maximum-entropy closure gives the most accurate overall description and reproduces even the low-temperature oscillatory structure of the connected two-density correlator. These results identify two general requirements for functional approaches to quantum thermodynamics: the renormalization group flow equation must retain the equal-time contact subtraction to avoid spurious self-interactions, and any closure of the hierarchy must preserve the statistical consistency of density correlators. This work provides a controlled foundation for deriving ab initio density functionals for quantum many-body systems across condensed-matter, ultracold-atom, and nuclear physics, as well as quantum chemistry.

Summary. This paper develops a global thermodynamic framework to analyze liquid-gas coexistence in fluids subjected to both heat conduction and weak gravity. It constructs a variational free-energy function, decomposing it to show that while the effective gravity dictates the primary phase ordering, a residual term is crucial for correctly calculating macroscopic thermodynamic observables. This work provides a general methodology for incorporating non-equilibrium driving forces into phase coexistence theory.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous application of global thermodynamics to non-equilibrium systems, particularly separating the ordering principle from residual non-equilibrium corrections, provides a powerful theoretical template for analyzing complex phase transitions in open quantum or classical systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the global thermodynamics of liquid-gas coexistence in heat-conducting fluids subjected to weak gravity.

Main result. The free-energy landscape is decomposed into an effective-gravity part determining the primary phase ordering, and a residual excess-latent-heat contribution necessary for deriving fundamental thermodynamic relations and observables.

Method. The authors employ a global variational free-energy function approach, extending equilibrium thermodynamics to incorporate non-equilibrium effects from heat conduction and gravity.

Model / system. The system is a fluid undergoing liquid-gas coexistence in a container under weak gravity and imposed temperature gradients. The analysis uses the van der Waals model for numerical illustration.

Key observables. Spatially averaged pressure, global chemical potential, and the variational free-energy landscape structure.

Important parameters / regimes. Weak gravity ($mgL$), temperature difference ($\Xi$), and the dimensionless parameter $\varepsilon$ characterizing the weak coupling regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a sharp interface and operates in the linear response regime, neglecting explicit interfacial transport processes.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate variational free-energy landscapes in $(\xi_1, \xi_2)$ space, showing how temperature differences ($\Xi$) deform the barrier shape and how the ordering changes across different physical regimes.

Paper structure. The paper builds from setting up the global thermodynamic framework, deriving steady-state conditions via variational minimization, decomposing the free energy into effective-gravity and residual parts, and finally analyzing the resulting free-energy landscape structure.

Abstract

We study liquid-gas coexistence under gravity and heat conduction from the viewpoint of global thermodynamics. We construct a variational free-energy function for the fixed-global-temperature description and decompose it into two parts. The first has the same configurational form as the equilibrium weak-gravity free energy with gravity replaced by the effective gravity, and it determines the first-order configurational transition between the two separated liquid-gas arrangements. The second is a residual excess-latent-heat contribution that vanishes without heat conduction. Although it does not decide which separated liquid-gas arrangement is thermodynamically favored, this residual part is needed to derive the fundamental relation in the laboratory variables and to recover thermodynamic observables such as the spatially averaged pressure. The same residual contribution reshapes the barrier geometry, ridge/valley structure, and interfacial anomalies of the fixed-global-temperature free-energy landscape. Numerical examples based on the van der Waals model illustrate the resulting landscape structure, and estimates of experimental scales suggest a setup for detecting the effective-gravity inversion.

Summary. The researchers used high-bandwidth spin noise spectroscopy on warm rubidium vapor to measure how spin noise changes with atomic density. They found a non-linear, quadratic increase in noise at high densities, which they attribute to resonant dipole-dipole interactions between atoms. By experimentally quenching these interactions, they confirmed that this non-linear scaling is indeed a signature of atomic cross-correlations.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a direct, measurable spectroscopic signature (non-linear noise scaling) for many-body quantum correlations in a simple, controllable atomic system, extending SNS beyond simple magnetization measurements.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To experimentally characterize non-linear atomic correlations in warm alkali vapors by measuring the density dependence of spin noise.

Main result. The spin noise variance exhibits a quadratic, superlinear dependence on atomic density at high concentrations, which is attributed to resonant dipole-dipole interactions (DDI).

Method. High-bandwidth Spin Noise Spectroscopy (SNS) is employed to measure Faraday rotation fluctuations in a warm rubidium vapor.

Model / system. The system is a warm alkali vapor (rubidium) probed by a detuned laser, where the noise measurement probes spin correlations arising from resonant dipole-dipole interactions (DDI).

Key observables. Spin noise variance ($\langle \delta heta^2 angle$) and the spin noise power spectral density (PSD).

Important parameters / regimes. Vapor density ($n$), detuning ($\Delta$), and input probe power ($P_p$).

Assumptions / limitations. The non-linear scaling is assumed to arise from DDI, which is tested by quenching the interaction using an auxiliary resonant beam.

Figures summary. Figures show the non-linear (quadratic) scaling of spin noise variance vs. density, and demonstrate the suppression of this non-linearity and spectral distortions when the DDI is quenched.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the linear regime (non-interacting) and then presents evidence for a superlinear, quadratic scaling at high densities. The core argument is built by introducing and then experimentally suppressing the DDI via an auxiliary beam, confirming the correlation origin.

Abstract

We experimentally demonstrate a non-linear dependence of the spin noise variance on atomic density in a warm alkali vapor. Implementing high-bandwidth spin noise spectroscopy (SNS) near the D2 transition of rubidium, a quadratic spin noise contribution is shown to arise at high densities, in contrast with the linear dependence valid in non-interacting ensembles. This non-linear scaling is shown to crucially depend on the residual optical excitation of the vapor by the probe beam, suggesting it stems from atomic cross-correlations due to resonant dipole-dipole interaction (DDI) in the vapor. We support this claim by introducing an additional experimental protocol to quench the ddi, resulting in a suppression of both the quadratic scaling of the spin variance and the distortions of the spin noise spectrum induced by the interaction. These results extend the applications of SNS to the characterization of many-body correlations in complex quantum systems.

Asymptotic distinguishability of Haar-averaged measurement models

Summary. This work rigorously analyzes how distinguishable two measurement models are when the underlying randomness is either collective (shared unitary) or local (independent unitaries). By calculating the Total Variation Distance in various asymptotic limits, the authors show that the level of observation—whether aggregate or block-resolved—fundamentally changes the measurable distinguishability.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical tools developed for quantifying distinguishability between structured vs. unstructured quantum processes (shared vs. independent unitaries) are highly relevant for understanding resource quantification in quantum information theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper studies the asymptotic distinguishability between measurement models generated by a single collective unitary versus those generated by independent local unitaries.

Main result. The distinguishability, quantified by the Total Variation Distance (TVD), depends critically on the observation level: the aggregate statistics yield a nontrivial limit, while the block-resolved statistics show perfect distinguishability in certain limits.

Method. The authors use Weingarten calculus and combinatorial analysis to derive closed-form expressions and asymptotic limits for the TVD in various scaling regimes.

Model / system. The model involves Haar-random measurement-and-prepare channels acting on a composite system of $N=n_1+n_2$ subsystems of local dimension $d$. The comparison is between the collective channel $Q(N)_U$ and the block-separated channel $Q(n_1, n_2)_{U,V}$.

Key observables. Type-II error probability, Total Variation Distance (TVD) between aggregate and block-resolved histogram laws, and the block-resolved pair-of-histograms law.

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis covers multiple asymptotic regimes: fixed-$N$, large-$d$; fixed-$d$, large-$N$; sparse joint-scaling ($N = o(\sqrt{d})$); and critical scaling ($N/\sqrt{d} o c$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis distinguishes between the aggregate (block-unresolved) and block-resolved statistics, and relies on asymptotic expansions derived from Haar measure integration.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the discrimination protocol using entangled testers and show the relationship between the collective and multipartite measurement models.

Paper structure. The paper first addresses channel discrimination (Type-II error) and then focuses on comparing the aggregate vs. block-resolved histogram laws using the TVD, deriving specific asymptotic limits for different scaling regimes.

Abstract

We study discrimination problems generated by the same basic Haar-random measurement mechanism at two observational levels. First, we derive an explicit expression for the type-II error in the task of discriminating a Haar-random measure-and-prepare channel from the identity channel $\mathbb{I}$, using a coherence-sensitive entangled tester. Second, after passing to the induced classical measurement records, we compare two random measurement models: one induced by a single collective unitary of the form $U^{\otimes (n_1+n_2)}$ with $U\in U(d)$, and another induced by independent local unitaries $U_1^{\otimes n_1}\otimes U_2^{\otimes n_2}$. For the associated Haar-averaged aggregate histogram laws, in which the block of origin of each count is not retained, we obtain closed-form formulas and quantify their discrepancy through the total variation distance. We derive asymptotic expressions in the fixed-$N$, large-$d$ regime, the fixed-$d$, large-$N$ regime, the sparse joint-scaling regime $N=o(\sqrt d)$, and the critical scaling regime $N/\sqrt d\to c$. We also identify the block-resolved pair-of-histograms law, showing that the aggregate total variation distance is a coarse-grained lower bound on the distinguishability available when block labels are retained.

Abstract

The two-dimensional metallic quantum critical problem is a long-standing puzzle that is widely believed to hold the key to resolving ubiquitous non-Fermi liquid behavior in strongly correlated electronic systems. In this study, we present a non-perturbative renormalization group (RG) analysis of the metallic Ising-nematic quantum critical point in two dimensions, formulated directly around an intrinsically nonlocal infrared (IR) boson propagator. Rather than treating the anomalous dynamical critical exponent $a$ as a fixed phenomenological parameter, we regard it as an intrinsic component of the fixed-point data to be determined from the internal consistency of the low-energy patch field theory under highly anisotropic scaling dimensions ($[k_0]=a+1$, $[k_x]=2$, $[k_y]=1$). While the leading two-loop diagrammatics vanish identically due to kinematic pole configurations, our three-loop evaluation reveals a profound structural asymmetry between the sectors: the fermion self-energy and Yukawa vertex receive non-vanishing logarithmic corrections, whereas the corresponding bosonic counter-term remains strictly zero. Consequently, we find that no self-consistent, intersecting fixed-point solution for the exponent $a$ exists within the three-loop truncation, failing to reproduce the physical value of $a \approx 1.85$ observed in quantum Monte Carlo simulations. We conjecture that the cross-linked topology of the four-loop boson self-energy diagrams is exactly marginal and yields the minimal, mandatory bosonic counter-term required to restore multi-sector self-consistency. Our framework establishes a rigid multi-loop matching scheme necessary to uniquely pin down the critical exponent, and uncovers a stable phase space for field anomalous dimensions.
