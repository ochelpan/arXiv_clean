Summary. This paper shows that the process of Above-Threshold Ionization (ATI) driven by squeezed light can serve as a powerful, scalable mechanism analogous to photon subtraction in quantum optics. By detecting photoelectrons, the system filters the quantum state to generate large-amplitude optical Schrödinger cat states. These states exhibit clear non-classical features, allowing for the violation of Bell inequalities, thus opening strong-field physics for quantum state engineering.

Why it may be interesting. This work bridges fundamental quantum optics concepts (cat states, photon subtraction) with high-intensity, non-linear strong-field physics (ATI), proposing a novel, scalable platform for generating macroscopic quantum states.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge is generating large-amplitude optical Schrödinger cat states, as standard photon subtraction protocols are limited in scalability and achievable macroscopicity.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that Above-Threshold Ionization (ATI) driven by bright squeezed light acts as a scalable, strong-field analogue of photon subtraction, enabling the generation of large-amplitude cat states that can violate a Bell inequality.

Method. The work employs a combination of quantum optics theory (photon subtraction formalism) and strong-field physics (ATI path integrals) to establish an analogy between the two processes.

Model / system. The system involves bright squeezed vacuum light interacting with atoms undergoing Above-Threshold Ionization (ATI). The theoretical framework models the post-selected optical state $|\Phi(\mathbf{p}_f, t) angle$ resulting from photoelectron detection.

Key observables. Wigner function negativity, violation of Bell inequalities (CHSH value), and the structure of the photoelectron probability amplitude $ ilde{c}(\alpha)$.

Important parameters / regimes. The squeezing strength ($r$), the detected photoelectron momentum ($\mathbf{p}_f$), and the finite momentum resolution ($\sigma_\mu$) are key parameters analyzed.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on semiclassical approximations (saddle-point methods) and assumes that the photoelectron detection acts as a highly selective, non-linear filter on the quantum state.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the comparison between photon subtraction and photoelectron heralding, show the transformation of probability amplitudes, and display Wigner functions confirming cat-like features and Bell inequality violations.

Paper structure. The paper builds by first establishing the theoretical analogy between photon subtraction and ATI heralding. It then uses path integrals and semiclassical methods to derive the post-selected state, characterizes its non-Gaussian features, and finally tests its non-classicality by violating a Bell inequality while analyzing the impact of experimental noise.

Abstract

While the interface between strong-field physics and quantum optics offers a unique regime for combining extreme nonlinearity with quantum optical resources, its potential for generating non-classical states of light remains largely unexplored. Standard protocols for generating optical Schrödinger cat states, such as photon subtraction from squeezed light, are inherently limited in the achievable macroscopicity of the state and its scalability. In this work, we bridge this gap by demonstrating that above-threshold ionization driven by bright squeezed light provides a strong-field analogue of photon subtraction, where photoelectron detection acts as a high-intensity heralding mechanism, enabling the generation of large amplitude optical Schrödinger cat states. We characterize the resulting non-Gaussian features and show that they can be tuned via the detected photoelectron momentum, and study their robustness against the experimental imperfections arising from finite momentum resolution at the heralding step. Despite the noise, we show that the generated states can be manipulated to violate a Bell inequality, thereby highlighting their potential for foundational and practical applications. Our results establish strong-field processes as a scalable platform for macroscopic quantum state engineering, opening a route to quantum optics in previously inaccessible regimes.

Summary. This paper develops rigorous mathematical results for estimating the time required for a search process to find a target when searchers arrive continuously (immigrate). It extends existing theory to account for searchers moving at a bounded speed, deriving exact distributions and moments in the fast immigration limit. The results are applied to canonical models like Brownian motion and run-and-tumble dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. While the core topic is stochastic search, the mathematical machinery involving convergence theorems, moment calculations, and the comparison between different physical diffusion models (BM vs. run-and-tumble) is highly relevant to non-equilibrium statistical physics and transport phenomena.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the search times for searchers that progressively enter or 'immigrate' into a domain at a constant Poissonian rate, specifically addressing the case where searchers move at a bounded speed.

Main result. In the fast immigration limit, the probability distribution and moments of the $k$-th search time ($T_k$) are determined, showing convergence to Erlang distributions.

Method. The analysis uses properties of inhomogeneous Poisson point processes and derives survival probabilities based on the short-time behavior of the single-searcher first passage time distribution.

Model / system. The models analyzed include 1D, 2D, and 3D run-and-tumble processes, Brownian motion, and linear PDMPs, all subjected to a constant immigration rate ($\lambda$).

Key observables. The $k$-th First Passage Time ($T_k$) and its moments ($E[T_k^m]$).

Important parameters / regimes. Immigration rate ($\lambda$), system size ($L$), searcher speed ($v$), and the characteristic time scale ($t_{\min} = L/v$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is rigorously performed in the fast immigration limit ($\lambda o \infty$) and assumes the underlying single-searcher process ($ au$) has a specific short-time distribution.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares the relative error of theoretical approximations against stochastic simulations for four different search models. Figure 2 compares the mean first passage time across three processes (BM, discrete, run-and-tumble) showing convergence/divergence regimes.

Paper structure. The paper develops general theorems for the fast immigration limit, applies these theorems to various physical models (run-and-tumble, BM), and compares the theoretical predictions against numerical simulations, paying close attention to the breakdown of approximations in different parameter regimes.

Abstract

Many biophysical search processes employ searchers which enter or "immigrate" into the domain progressively over time. Existing search time estimates can become unphysical for fast immigration since they imply that searchers move at infinite speed. In this paper, we investigate search times of immigrating searchers that move at bounded speed. In the fast immigration limit, we determine the full probability distribution and all the moments of the $k$th search time in terms of the early time probability distribution of a single searcher. We apply these rigorous mathematical results to several canonical models of stochastic search. We further analyze different models of "diffusion" and use these results to investigate when and how the minutiae of searcher dynamics affect search times. We compare our theory to numerical simulations.

(Non-)Traversable Quantum Phase Transitions

Summary. This paper introduces a novel geometric criterion to classify Quantum Phase Transitions (QPTs) as either 'traversable' or 'nontraversable'. Traversability is determined by whether the ground-state manifold has a finite geometric distance across the transition point, which is tested using counterdiabatic driving techniques. This classification offers a fundamental, dynamical understanding of QPTs, independent of local order parameters.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful, geometry-based diagnostic tool that transcends traditional classifications (like symmetry breaking or RG fixed points) for understanding the dynamical feasibility of quantum state preparation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to develop a geometric classification of Quantum Phase Transitions (QPTs) based on whether the transition can be dynamically connected (traversed) between the two phases.

Main result. A QPT is classified as 'traversable' if the geometric distance between the ground states in the thermodynamic limit is finite, allowing for exact counterdiabatic driving; otherwise, it is 'nontraversable'.

Method. The authors use the framework of counterdiabatic (CD) driving and analyze the geometry of the ground-state manifold using the fidelity susceptibility ($\chi_F$) to define a finite geometric length ($\ell$).

Model / system. The analysis applies to general many-body systems described by a tunable Hamiltonian $H(\lambda)$, with specific examples including the Transverse Field Ising Chain (TFIC) and the Ising Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) model.

Key observables. Geometric distance ($\ell$), Fidelity Susceptibility ($\chi_F$), and the constant-speed CD driving schedule.

Important parameters / regimes. The thermodynamic limit ($L o \infty$), the divergence exponent $\alpha_F$, and the control parameter $\lambda$ near the critical point $\lambda_c$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on constructing a constant-speed parametrization $\lambda_{cs}(t)$ to tame the divergence of the adiabatic gauge potential in the thermodynamic limit.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the constant-speed CD driving protocol, showing how it maintains unit fidelity across the transition, and provide a genealogy summarizing examples of traversable (blue) and non-traversable (red) QPTs.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the geometric concept of traversability, develops the mathematical machinery using CD driving and the fidelity susceptibility, derives the constant-speed parametrization, and applies this criterion to classify known QPTs.

Abstract

Quantum phase transitions manifest as an abrupt change in the ground state of a many-body system; yet it is an open question whether this sudden change necessarily precludes a continuous dynamical connection between the two phases. We introduce a classification of quantum phase transitions based on this geometric aspect of the ground-state manifold, that differs from known classifications. By leveraging the framework of counterdiabatic driving, we explicitly construct schedules that dynamically connect one phase to another. This strategy allows us to uncover a large class of quantum phase transitions, where the states on both sides are separated only by a finite geometric distance in the thermodynamic limit. We term such transitions traversable, since exact counterdiabatic driving links the two phases via a finite dynamical protocol in the thermodynamic limit. We show that multiple known transitions fall into this class -- e.g., symmetry-breaking transitions obeying hyperscaling and discontinuous transitions with an enhanced continuous symmetry. We further show the existence of quantum phase transitions that cannot be crossed dynamically even with the help of nonlocal counterdiabatic driving, as they would require divergent amplitudes and frequencies. Geometrically, these nontraversable transitions correspond to an infinite distance separating the two phases of matter; we show that the class comprises continuous transitions exhibiting mean-field universality, and discontinuous transitions arising from the competition between metastable minima. Our geometric classification goes beyond the known taxonomy, is independent of local order parameters and renormalization group fixed points, and has direct implications for the complexity of state preparation and adiabatic quantum computation.

How To Track Qubits Through Space and Time (Or: Sailing in a Quantum Boat)

Summary. This paper advances quantum cryptography by proposing 'quantum localization' and 'trajectory verification' to overcome security flaws in existing position verification schemes. It constructs protocols using quantum anchor states to verifiably track quantum information through space and time. This work establishes stronger theoretical foundations for position-based cryptography by localizing both quantum states and computational functions.

Why it may be interesting. While highly abstract, the concepts of localizing quantum information and computational capabilities in space and time provide novel theoretical tools for understanding the physical constraints on quantum information processing and security.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing quantum position verification security definitions are insufficient because they fail to prevent distributed adversarial simulation across space and time.

Main result. The authors introduce and construct protocols for 'quantum localization' and 'trajectory verification,' providing stronger foundations for position-based cryptography.

Method. The work develops protocols using 'quantum anchor states' and generalizes concepts from unclonable cryptography to track quantum information through spacetime.

Model / system. The framework relies on quantum information theory, specifically utilizing bipartite entangled states and the concept of tracking quantum states or computations across a specified spacetime trajectory.

Key observables. Fidelity of the joint state between anchor and vessel registers, and the success probability ($\eta$) of the extraction process.

Important parameters / regimes. The security proofs are established in the classical oracle (ideal obfuscation) model, which is heuristically instantiated using post-quantum indistinguishability obfuscation.

Assumptions / limitations. The security proofs rely on the existence of one-way functions and the ideal obfuscation model.

Figures summary. Figure 2 illustrates a non-localizability attack on the f-BB84 protocol involving three provers, demonstrating how entanglement can be nonlocally distributed.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the weakness in existing QPV to introducing stronger notions (quantum, state, functionality localization). It then constructs protocols for trajectory verification, details the mathematical machinery using quantum anchor states, and concludes by addressing public verifiability.

Abstract

While quantum position verification aims to certify a prover's location using quantum information, existing security definitions only guarantee that part of the successful adversarial party is in the claimed location. This leaves open the possibility that a distributed team of adversaries can jointly simulate a prover in a way that defeats the intended meaning of ``being at a location'' in position-based cryptography. We introduce stronger notions of position verification that we call quantum localization, which requires that there is a specified, unclonable state at the verified spacetime point -- and that this state can be found nowhere else. We show that quantum localization leads naturally to a meaningful notion of trajectory verification, in which quantum information is verifiably tracked through space and time. We construct quantum localization and trajectory verification protocols using quantum anchor states, which generalize coset states from unclonable cryptography. The security of our schemes is proven in the classical oracle (i.e. ideal obfuscation) model, which can be heuristically instantiated in the plain model using post-quantum indistinguishability obfuscation. We also introduce and instantiate the concept of functionality localization, which guarantees that the adversary has the ability to compute a secret function at the verified spacetime point, and this function cannot be computed anywhere else. This raises the intriguing possibility of localizing computational capabilities in space and time. More broadly, we believe our notions of quantum localization and our feasibility results provide stronger foundations for position-based cryptography.

Summary. This paper introduces the Progressive Swapping to the Middle (PSM) protocol for quantum networks to improve long-distance qubit transmission. By comparing PSM against other established protocols, the authors demonstrate that PSM offers superior resource efficiency and link probability while maintaining high fidelity, especially when quantum memories are imperfect. This advances the practical feasibility of quantum networking.

Why it may be interesting. This work directly tackles the scalability and resource limitations of quantum communication networks, which is a core area of quantum information theory relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of efficiently transferring quantum bits (qubits) over long distances in quantum networks by optimizing entanglement distribution and swapping protocols.

Main result. The proposed Progressive Swapping to the Middle (PSM) protocol is shown to be highly resource-efficient, yielding better link probabilities and lower resource consumption compared to existing protocols like PS and BS, especially under imperfect memory conditions.

Method. The authors compare PSM against several established protocols (PS, BS, HD) by calculating key performance metrics like link probability, link fidelity, and minimum required resource consumption.

Model / system. The system modeled is a quantum network utilizing entangled photon pairs distributed over links, requiring Bell state measurements and teleportation for end-to-end qubit transfer. The analysis incorporates decoherence from imperfect quantum memories and fiber loss.

Key observables. Link probability (success rate), Link Fidelity ($F$), and Resource Consumption (minimum number of generated pairs required).

Important parameters / regimes. Memory decay time ($ au$), time-slot ($\Delta$), and the swap success probability ($p_{swap} \approx 0.9$).

Abstract

The distribution of entangled pairs of photons on the links composing a quantum network, combined with Bell state measurements and teleportation, is the basic apparatus to transfer quantum bits (qubits) over long distances. Entanglement distribution establishes an end-to-end entangled pair while consuming intermediate pairs on links and holding them for a certain time period. The technical literature identifies two main kinds of protocols, parallel and sequential ones, the latter having an advantage in resource consumption over the former. In this paper, we introduce an efficient swapping protocol called Progressive Swapping to the Middle (PSM) as it combines the existing Progressive Swapping (PS) protocol from both extremities of a path that meet in the middle where the received pairs are swapped. We compare PSM with two parallel protocols and PS; in our evaluation, we take into account imperfect memories and fidelity degradation. We demonstrate that PSM yields a much better link probability than PS while keeping a reasonable link fidelity, and shows an advantage in resource consumption over other protocols.

Summary. This paper introduces the Hamming quantum kernel (HQK) to overcome the exponential concentration problem plaguing standard quantum kernels in Support Vector Machines. By utilizing the full measurement statistics weighted by Hamming weight, the HQK maintains high classification accuracy even as the number of qubits increases significantly. This demonstrates a scalable and robust method for quantum feature mapping.

Why it may be interesting. While the focus is on machine learning kernels, the underlying challenge—extracting robust, scalable information from quantum measurements—is central to quantum information processing and quantum characterization.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The standard fidelity quantum kernel suffers from exponential concentration when scaling to large quantum systems, limiting its practical use in Support Vector Machines (QSVMs).

Main result. The proposed Hamming quantum kernel (HQK) successfully mitigates this concentration issue, showing superior and stable performance compared to the standard fidelity kernel and classical kernels for systems with 15 or more qubits.

Method. The HQK is a classical post-processing method that utilizes the full measurement statistics of quantum circuits, weighted by the Hamming weight, rather than relying on a single fidelity value.

Model / system. The work uses QSVMs trained on two types of data: classical MNIST digits (reduced via PCA) and synthetic data generated from quantum circuits. The core model involves comparing the performance of three kernels: RBF (classical), FQK, and HQK.

Key observables. Test accuracy, kernel matrix values, and the theoretical distinguishability $D(P_0, P_1)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits ($n$, ranging from 2 to 27), the HQK weighting parameter ($\lambda$), and the RBF bandwidth ($\gamma$).

Assumptions / limitations. The HQK assumes that similar data points lead to measurable shifts in the Hamming distributions, and the analysis relies on the ability to perform full measurement statistics.

Figures summary. Figures compare test accuracy vs. dimensionality ($n$) for MNIST and quantum data, consistently showing HQK outperforming FQK and RBF at large $n$. One figure illustrates the concentration issue in FQK versus the informative kernel from HQK.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of FQK concentration, details the HQK formulation using full measurement statistics, and then evaluates the method by comparing its performance against RBF and FQK across multiple classification tasks (MNIST, synthetic quantum data) as the system size increases.

Abstract

Quantum support vector machines are classification algorithms that rely on quantum-generated kernels. The fidelity quantum kernel commonly used in quantum support vector machines suffers from exponential concentration as system size increases, preventing an efficient scaling beyond fewqubit systems. We introduce the Hamming quantum kernel, a classical post-processing method that is based on the same measurement outcomes as the fidelity quantum kernel. However, it avoids the exponential concentration problem by using the full measurement statistics rather than a single fidelity value. We evaluate the approach on both classical data (MNIST) and synthetic data generated from quantum circuits, using systems ranging from 2 to 27 qubits. Throughout the simulations, the Hamming quantum kernel outperforms the fidelity quantum kernel whenever 15 or more qubits are used. Furthermore, for synthetic quantum data, our method consistently outperforms the classical Gaussian kernel. This demonstrates that the Hamming quantum kernel improves the expressivity and robustness at larger qubit scales without requiring any additional quantum ressources.

Summary. This work explores the limits of entanglement in quantum channel discrimination, proving that entanglement is not always beneficial. By introducing the MEWC/MEBC framework, the authors demonstrate that for certain channel pairs, using maximally entangled states actively reduces the maximum achievable success probability compared to using separable input states.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding when entanglement, typically viewed as a resource, fails to provide an advantage in quantum information tasks, which is crucial for designing practical quantum protocols.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates quantum channel discrimination, specifically identifying scenarios where the use of maximally entangled input states is suboptimal or actively detrimental to the discrimination success probability.

Main result. It establishes that for certain pairs of channels (MEWC pairs), the optimal input state must be separable, and excessive entanglement can significantly reduce the maximum achievable discrimination probability.

Method. The analysis employs concepts like the Diamond Norm ($\|\cdot\|_\diamond$) and the ME-norm ($\|\cdot\|_{ME}$), introducing the Maximal Entanglement Worst Case (MEWC) and Best Case (MEBC) classifications to systematically characterize the role of entanglement.

Model / system. The study models the task of distinguishing between two or more quantum channels ($\Phi_i$), using the Choi-Jamiołkowski isomorphism to translate the problem into analyzing the difference map $\Delta\Phi$. The analysis compares performance using separable vs. maximally entangled input states.

Key observables. Success probability of discrimination ($P^*_s$), the M-operator ($M(\Delta\Phi)$), and the relationship between the diamond norm and the ME-norm.

Important parameters / regimes. The entanglement entropy of the input state, the dimension of the Hilbert space ($d$), and the structure of the difference map $\Delta\Phi$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis often focuses on the uniform prior probability case and compares the optimal strategy (using the diamond norm) against the maximally entangled strategy (using the ME-norm).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the success probability for discriminating Pauli channels, showing an increase with entanglement, but also show cases where the probability decreases with increasing entanglement, favoring separable states.

Paper structure. The paper develops from general channel discrimination theory, introduces the MEWC/MEBC concepts, derives mathematical bounds relating different norms, and applies these concepts to specific examples like Pauli and measurement channels to prove when entanglement is detrimental.

Abstract

Entanglement is known to be a powerful resource that improves performance in various quantum information and computational tasks. A standard example of such a phenomenon is the possibility of perfectly discriminating all four Pauli operations in a single shot via the superdense coding protocol. While entanglement is often a powerful resource for quantum channel discrimination, this is not necessarily the case. In this work, we identify scenarios in which the maximally entangled state is a bad choice of input state and, more generally, show that excessive entanglement can reduce channel discriminability dramatically. To do so, we present an explicit pair of unitary channels which are perfectly discriminable without entanglement, but for which any strategy with maximally entangled input states is $ε$-close to a blind uniform guessing strategy. To develop a systematic approach, we introduce the concepts of Maximal Entanglement Worst Case (MEWC) and Maximal Entanglement Best Case (MEBC) pairs of channels, and present conditions for a pair of channels to be MEWC or MEBC. With these conditions, we show that the optimal input states for discriminating MEWC pairs of channels are necessarily separable, and provide non-trivial examples of measurement channels for which entanglement necessarily reduces the maximum probability of discrimination.

Summary. This paper develops a resource theory framework for 'block coherence' by analyzing pure state transformations under three generalized incoherent operations: PBIO, SBIO, and BDCO. The resulting entropy-maximizing distribution changes from the exponential (Boltzmann-Gibbs) to the so-called q-exponential one. Since maximizing Shannon entropy may produce an unjustified independence between degrees of freedom, here we investigate how the most popular ERGs with independent edges (namely, the Erdos-Renyi and configuration models) generalize to higher-order q-Exponential Random Graphs with dependent edges in the non-Shannon case, while keeping their defining constraints (number of links and degree sequence, respectively) unchanged. We find features, such as a phase transition between sparse and dense regimes, that are absent in the original ERGs but typical of higher-order networks, plus novel phenomena such as richer assortativity and clustering profiles, which allow for the coexistence of link sparsity and triadic closure. These results show that higher-order networks do not necessarily require higher-order constraints, as they naturally arise from simpler ones in a framework that is even more agnostic than Shannon's.

Abstract

Roaming reactions occur when a molecule enters a near-dissociation region, avoids immediate separation, and later forms products by a pathway not controlled by the conventional tight transition-state bottleneck. Classical studies have shown that roaming is best understood in phase space: inner and outer transition-state structures, together with their invariant manifolds, organize trapping, return, and dissociation. The corresponding quantum question is less settled. Can a single quantum resonance carry a recognizable signature of the classical roaming region? We address this question in Chesnavich's two-degree-of-freedom model for the ion--molecule reaction $\mathrm{CH}_4^+\rightarrow\mathrm{CH}_3^+ + \mathrm{H}$. Resonance states are computed with a complex absorbing potential and analyzed using diagnostics designed to mirror the classical phase-space picture: radial probability weights derived from the tight and outer transition-state structures, radial Husimi projections, angular-momentum channel weights, and coherent-state probes of the classical periodic orbits. One resonance is distinguished from the rest of the computed resonance ensemble. Its wavefunction is concentrated in the projected region between the inner and outer transition-state structures, its radial phase-space distribution is centered at intermediate radius with nearly zero radial momentum, and its angular structure is consistent with a standing rather than a directed rotating component. We interpret this state as a phase-space-localized quantum analogue of classical roaming. The result provides a controlled example in which quantum roaming is identified directly from a resonance wavefunction and its phase-space diagnostics, rather than only from product-state or scattering signatures.

Abstract

Finding symmetries is crucial for understanding physical models. In this work, we present an optimization framework that searches Pauli symmetries of Hamiltonians, merging the fields of machine learning with automated symmetry finding. Built on a Set-Transformer architecture, our framework uses self-attention to encode the pairwise and higher-order correlations among the Pauli-Strings. The relations are then decoded as a candidate, which is further optimized with a custom commutation-based objective, and mapped to a symmetry of the input Hamiltonian. We apply our method to random Pauli Hamiltonians, periodic one and two dimensional transverse-field Ising model and the Toric code. We show that for physical Hamiltonians (Ising and Toric), our framework succeeds with near-deterministic probability while providing substantial advantage compared to state-of-the-art strategies. For random Pauli Hamiltonians, we estimate the required computational resources, specifically the number of parallel starts and the number of GPUs, to find a symmetry with high success probability under fixed design specifications.
