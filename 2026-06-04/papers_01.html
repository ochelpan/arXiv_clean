Summary. This theoretical paper proves that superradiant neutrino emission from atomic condensates is fundamentally impossible. The authors demonstrate that fermionic anticommutators cause destructive interference, preventing the emission rate from scaling as $N^2$. This result places a strict theoretical limit on collective decay processes in quantum gases.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the theoretical proposal of superradiant neutrino emission from atomic Bose-Einstein condensates (BECs).

Main result. The authors prove that superradiant neutrino emission is fundamentally impossible due to the nature of fermionic anticommutators, which cause interference terms to cancel.

Method. The analysis involves deriving fundamental limits on the emission rate using quantum mechanical operators (jump operators) and analyzing the scaling of the emission rate with the number of particles, N.

Model / system. The system under study is neutrino emission from atomic BECs, considering various models including bosonic-bosonic, bosonic-fermionic, and fermionic-to-bosonic transitions.

Key observables. Neutrino emission rate, superradiant enhancement factor, and the scaling of the emission rate with particle number (N).

Important parameters / regimes. The rate is found to scale as $\propto N$ rather than the superradiant $\propto N^2$, and kinetic effects suppress any enhancement by factors like $10^{17}$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the fundamental fermionic nature of the amplitudes and accounts for the rapid decay and recoil of reaction products.

Abstract

We show that the recent proposal for superradiant emission of neutrinos cannot evade our proof that superradiant neutrino emission is fundamentally impossible. Pairing two fermions in a molecule does not remove the cancellation of interference terms in neutrino emission due to fermionic anticommutators.

Summary. This work examines how non-equilibrium dynamics differ between ergodic (ETH) and Many-Body Localized (MBL) quantum phases in a disordered spin chain. By adiabatically ramping the system parameters, the authors use entropy measures to show that the rate of excitation generation is strongly suppressed in the localized phase compared to the thermalizing phase. This confirms that entropy production serves as a powerful diagnostic tool for probing quantum thermalization dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. The use of entropy measures to diagnose non-equilibrium dynamics across a fundamental quantum phase transition (ETH-MBL) is highly relevant for understanding how quantum information spreads or is suppressed in interacting many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates non-equilibrium dynamics across the transition between ergodic (ETH) and Many-Body Localized (MBL) quantum phases.

Main result. The entropy production (diagonal and entanglement) is significantly higher and more dependent on the driving rate in the ergodic phase compared to the MBL phase, confirming the dynamical distinction between the two regimes.

Method. The authors employed exact diagonalization and real-time numerical evolution to simulate the system's time evolution under time-dependent Hamiltonian ramps.

Model / system. The model is a disordered spin-1/2 XXZ chain, described by a Hamiltonian with disorder terms ($h_j$) and a time-dependent interaction coupling ($J_{zz}(t)$). The system is analyzed in the zero-magnetization sector.

Key observables. Diagonal entropy density, entanglement entropy density, and the adjacent-gap ratio (used to characterize static spectral statistics).

Important parameters / regimes. Disorder strength ($h_0$), system size ($L \le 14$), and the ramp speed ($v$) characterizing the adiabaticity of the time-dependent drive.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is limited by finite-size effects, though the results show robustness across multiple system sizes. The study assumes the validity of the ETH/MBL framework for characterizing thermalization.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the disorder-averaged adjacent-gap ratio transitioning from GOE (ergodic) to Poisson (localized) statistics, and show entropy measures ($ ext{sd}, ext{sent}$) varying with ramp speed for different protocols.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the static ETH-MBL crossover using spectral statistics. It then analyzes the non-equilibrium dynamics by applying three different time-dependent driving protocols (linear, quadratic, exponential) and comparing the resulting entropy growth in the ergodic versus localized regimes.

Abstract

Many-body localization(MBL) provides a mechanism by which isolated interacting quantum systems with disorder can avoid thermalization unlike ergodic systems satisfying the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis(ETH). Many-body localized systems retain signatures of their initial conditions at long times, whereas systems obeying ETH lose such information as they approach thermal equilibrium. Studying Non-equilibrium dynamics across ETH-MBL crossover is an important problem in condensed matter physics. Adiabatic control of parameters in interacting disordered systems provides a powerful framework to investigate MBL phases and their dynamical robustness. Using exact diagonalization and time-dependent numerical methods we study the effects of adiabatically ramped interactions in a disordered spin-1/2 XXZ chain, a paradigmatic model for exploring the many-body localization transition. By monitoring diagonal entropy density and entanglement entropy density growth across various ramp speeds, and system sizes. Our study incorporates Finite-size effects of spectral observables to probe the transition between ergodic and localized phases. The numerical results show that localized dynamical behavior remains largely intact under sufficiently slow ramp evolution, while increasing the driving rate promotes stronger excitation generation and larger entropy growth. This trend highlights the strong dependence of nonequilibrium adiabatic dynamics in disordered interacting quantum many-body systems.

Abstract

Stimulated Raman transitions in Faraday geometry allow for simultaneous single-shot qubit readout and qubit control. It involves driving an unbalanced $Λ$ system via an auxiliary excited state. We identify a distinct regime in which repeated passages through avoided crossings lead to step-like population inversion arising from Landau-Zener-Stückelberg interference. By changing the detuning beatnote, we demonstrate a controlled continuous crossover from Rabi-like oscillations to adiabatic population switching. These findings establish the oscillating Stark shift as a mechanism for engineering and controlling spin dynamics in Faraday geometry.

Summary. This paper develops a theoretical framework to study quantum transport in chaotic scattering systems when the system is continuously monitored by an external measurement. It derives a quantum master equation governing the evolution and uses Random Matrix Theory to predict the statistics of the transferred charge. The key finding is that monitoring breaks transport reciprocity, providing new diagnostics for open quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to AMO and open quantum systems as it provides a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing non-unitary, measurement-induced dynamics in mesoscopic conductors, extending concepts from quantum chaos to open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper extends random-matrix theory to analyze quantum transport through chaotic scattering systems when the dynamics are subjected to continuous, time-resolved monitoring.

Main result. Monitoring breaks the reciprocity of transport measurements, and the resulting charge transfer statistics can be predicted by comparing numerical solutions of a quantum master equation against Random Matrix Theory conjectures.

Method. The analysis involves deriving and solving a discrete-time quantum master equation for the density matrix, followed by applying Random Matrix Theory techniques (CUE/COE ensembles) to calculate probability distributions for transferred charge.

Model / system. The model is a quantum dot coupled to leads, where the system's evolution is governed by a quantum channel defined by Kraus operators due to the monitoring process. The scattering is statistically modeled using the Circular Unitary Ensemble (CUE).

Important parameters / regimes. Weak measurement strength ($w_m$), the ratio of lead modes ($M$ vs $N$), and the fixed parameter $\gamma = Mw^2$ in the multi-mode limit.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the setup (open/closed leads), two-terminal transport configurations, and the probability density functions $P(T)$ and $P(T')$ for both single-mode and multi-mode monitoring under CUE and COE statistics.

Abstract

We extend the random-matrix theory of chaotic scattering to quantum dots whose dynamics is monitored by time-resolved measurements. Starting from a scattering matrix drawn from a circular ensemble, we construct the corresponding ensemble of Kraus operators for the monitored evolution of the many-body density matrix. In the single-particle sector the sum over measurement outcomes can be carried out algebraically, giving a discrete-time quantum master equation for the transferred charge. We solve this equation numerically and compare the resulting charge-transfer statistics with closed-form random-matrix predictions. The latter rely on an equipartition rule for monitored particles, which we formulate as a conjecture and test against the master equation.

Abstract

When a quantum system passes through an unstable fixed point the local dynamics reduces to the inverted harmonic oscillator (IHO). It exponentially amplifies along one quadrature while squeezing the other, producing macroscopically extended quantum states from microscopic zero-point fluctuations. We realize this dynamics with a Bose-Einstein condensate on an AtomChip. Radio-frequency dressing flips the transverse harmonic confinement into an IHO. Through phase-space tomography we follow the full Wigner function of the evolving quantum state, observe sub-vacuum squeezing of 10.6(1.3) dB, and test coherent reversibility by time-reversing the IHO evolution. Matter-wave interference between the two daughter clouds confirms quantum coherence over timescales far beyond the initial expansion. Our experiment establishes ultra-cold atoms as a clean, controlled, many-body platform for unstable quantum dynamics opening a route to force sensing with time-reversal-based coherence certification and to analog studies of the amplification of quantum fluctuations in inflationary field dynamics.

Abstract

Dispersive readout of the transmon qubit in circuit QED is known to lose its quantum non-demolition character at small to moderate measurement drive amplitudes. This phenomenon is understood to originate from Laundau-Zener transitions at accidental multi-photon resonances, where $n$ drive photons can promote the transmon by $m$ levels. The impact of these measurement-induced state transition (MIST) of a transmon embedded in a multi-qubit chip, however, remains largely unexplored. Here, we show that the presence of other components, such as qubits and couplers, can affect the MIST threshold of a measured transmon. As an example, we consider the case of two transmon qubits, and we show that the spectator qubit can be impacted by the measurement-induced transition of the readout qubit and, conversely, that the presence of the spectator qubit can lower the MIST threshold of the readout qubit. Finally, we explore how adding a coupler mode between the two qubits further modifies these effects.

Abstract

Establishing quantum advantage requires comparison against the best achievable classical simulation. The Q-CTRL team recently simulated quench dynamics of the one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard model on an IBM processor, completing a $L=60$ evolution to time $t=6$ in under three minutes and claiming a $3000\times$ speedup over classical Time-Dependent Variational Principle (TDVP) simulation at bond dimension $χ=4096$. At the bond dimension comparable to Q-CTRL's best classical run, our GPU implementation completes in $\sim\!100$ minutes, directly reducing the claimed $3000\times$ quantum advantage to $\sim\!36\times$. These results substantially narrow the quantum-classical performance gap and establish a new standard for tensor-network benchmarking of large-scale quantum simulations.

Abstract

A single quantum pulse undergoing parametric amplification feeds into at most two pulses in the output. In this work, we present an efficient, analytical method for finding the quantum state of these output modes. Our method applies the amplification to the vacuum rather than to the input state, and subsequently applies a transformed version of the operator that creates the input state from vacuum.
