QPredSGG: Hybrid Quantum Predicate Learning for Long-Tailed Scene Graph Generation

Authors: Prerana Ramkumar, Nouhaila Innan, Muhammad Shafique
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04689v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a Quantum Predicate Head (QP-Head) to enhance Scene Graph Generation by addressing the long-tail imbalance of predicate labels. By replacing a large classical classifier with a compact, hybrid quantum module, the authors achieve state-of-the-art mean recall scores while drastically reducing the number of trainable parameters. This validates the use of quantum circuits for parameter-efficient relational reasoning in complex vision tasks.

Why it may be interesting. The work demonstrates a concrete application of quantum circuits (PQC) to solve a challenging, classically difficult machine learning problem (long-tail classification), providing quantitative metrics on parameter efficiency and performance gains.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Scene Graph Generation (SGG) performance is hampered by severe long-tail predicate imbalance, causing models to over-rely on frequent relations.

Main result. The proposed 4-qubit hybrid quantum head achieved an mR@100 of 57.25%, significantly outperforming the classical baseline (41.1%) while remaining highly parameter-efficient.

Method. The authors replace the classical predicate head in an existing network with a Quantum Predicate Head (QP-Head), which processes compressed relational features through a trainable Variational Quantum Circuit (PQC).

Model / system. The system is a hybrid quantum-classical model applied to visual scene graph reasoning. The QP-Head uses quantum encoding (Amplitude/Angle) and trainable entangling layers (e.g., SEL) to map high-dimensional features into a quantum state for classification.

Key observables. Mean Recall ($ ext{mR}@K$), Global Recall ($ ext{R}@K$), trainable quantum parameters, KL divergence from Haar-random distribution, and inference latency.

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit count ($n \in \{4, 8\}$), circuit depth ($L$), and the use of Weighted Cross-Entropy (WCE) loss.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that compact hybrid quantum heads can effectively capture the complex, long-tail relational structure in visual data while maintaining computational feasibility.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the general SGG framework, the four-stage experimental pipeline (baseline $ ightarrow$ quantum architecture $ ightarrow$ training $ ightarrow$ analysis), and representative quantum circuit structures.

Paper structure. The paper follows a structured comparison: first establishing a classical baseline (CFEN); second, defining the quantum architecture search space (qubits, embedding, depth); third, training using a bias-aware loss (WCE); and finally, analyzing the trade-off between semantic performance, quantum expressibility, and computational overhead.

Abstract

Scene Graph Generation (SGG) requires relational reasoning over objects and their interactions, but performance is often limited by severe long-tail predicate imbalance. Classical SGG models frequently rely on dataset statistics, leading to biased predictions toward frequent relations rather than fine-grained semantic predicates. Although existing debiasing strategies improve mean recall, predicate classification in current frameworks still often depends on large classical decision modules with high parameter cost. This work introduces a hybrid quantum predicate classifier for SGG by replacing the classical predicate head in Causal Feature Enhancement Network (CFEN) with a Quantum Predicate Head (QP-Head) trained using weighted cross-entropy. To the best of our knowledge, this is among the first studies to evaluate a hybrid quantum architecture for scene graph predicate classification on Visual Genome 150. We study the effect of qubit count, encoding strategy, entangling structure, and circuit depth on relational prediction. The best 4-qubit QP-Head uses Amplitude Embedding and Strongly Entangling Layers to compress 4096-dimensional pair features into a 16-dimensional quantum-compatible representation, corresponding to a 256$\times$ reduction. It achieves an mR@100 of 57.25%, compared with 41.1% for the classical CFEN reference, while using only 96 trainable quantum parameters. Scaling to 8 qubits maintains strong long-tail performance, reaching an mR@100 of 55.38% with 384 quantum parameters, while the depth analysis shows a trade-off between expressibility and runtime overhead. These results suggest that compact hybrid quantum predicate heads can support parameter-efficient long-tail relational classification in complex visual reasoning tasks.

Summary. This paper introduces the Quantum Flow (QFlow) algorithm, a resource-efficient method for simulating complex correlated electronic structures on hybrid quantum-classical hardware. By decomposing the problem into smaller active-space subproblems, QFlow achieves high accuracy, recovering over 95% of the correlation energy benchmarked against CCSD. This demonstrates a scalable pathway for quantum chemistry simulations on near-term quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a concrete, scalable quantum algorithm that bypasses the exponential scaling bottleneck of full quantum simulations by intelligently decomposing the problem into smaller, manageable quantum subroutines.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is accurately simulating correlated many-body systems, which suffer from exponential computational complexity.

Main result. The Quantum Flow (QFlow) algorithm successfully recovers over 95% of the total correlation energy from CCSD benchmarks for large systems, even when the underlying qubit requirements are manageable.

Method. QFlow uses a resource-efficient, parallel quantum-classical framework that decomposes the full problem into smaller, coupled active-space subproblems solved iteratively.

Model / system. The method is applied to electronic Hamiltonians describing molecular systems (e.g., $ ext{H}_2 ext{O}$, Propane) in the context of quantum chemistry.

Key observables. Total correlation energy (compared against CCSD), convergence rate, and the percentage of correlation energy recovered.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of orbitals (up to 114), the number of active electrons/orbitals (e.g., 6 active electrons in 6 active orbitals), and the required qubit count (determined by the largest active space).

Assumptions / limitations. The dominant correlation effects can be captured by averaging the results across a collection of reduced-dimensional active spaces, and the Trotter approximation is used for the energy functional.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the coverage-driven active-space sampling algorithm, energy profiles showing rapid convergence for $ ext{H}_2 ext{O}$, and the scaling of active spaces required for propane.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the QFlow formalism, details the coverage-driven active-space sampling algorithm, presents the HPC implementation results on molecular systems, and compares the recovered correlation energy against established methods like CCSD.

Abstract

The Quantum Flow (QFlow) algorithm provides a resource-efficient framework for describing correlated many-body systems on hybrid quantum-classical architectures. By enabling parallel utilization of quantum and classical resources, QFlow offers a scalable pathway toward simulations of realistic systems. In this Letter, we report a high-performance computing (HPC) implementation of the QFlow formalism based on a singles-and-doubles model. We demonstrate its performance for target spaces comprising 82 and 114 orbitals, where the flow includes all 6 active electrons in 6 active orbitals type active spaces. In the largest QFlow simulations, we optimize 1.17 million wave function parameters using the equivalent of 12 qubits. Despite the modest qubit requirements of the underlying active-space problems, the method recovers over $95\%$ of the total correlation energy obtained with the coupled cluster singles and doubles (CCSD) approach for systems dominated by dynamical correlation effects, which remain challenging for existing quantum algorithms. We further show that the QFlow formalism retains high accuracy in extended basis sets with diffuse functions, highlighting its potential for realistic large-scale quantum chemistry simulations.

Resource-efficient energy-based operator selection in fermionic ADAPT-VQE via exact Hamiltonian transformation

Summary. This paper improves the resource efficiency of energy-based operator selection in fermionic ADAPT-VQE by introducing an exact Hamiltonian transformation. This technique significantly reduces the measurement overhead required to score potential operators, making the selection process competitive with gradient-based methods. The results demonstrate that this efficient approach helps achieve accurate quantum chemical results for molecules even in highly correlated regimes.

Why it may be interesting. This work directly tackles the resource scaling of quantum chemistry simulations, which is crucial for applying VQE to complex, strongly correlated molecular systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge addressed is the computational bottleneck associated with reconstructing the one-parameter energy landscape during energy-based operator selection in fermionic ADAPT-VQE.

Main result. The authors introduce an exact Hamiltonian transformation that reformulates the energy landscape, reducing the measurement overhead of energy-based selection by about a factor of two, bringing it close to gradient-based methods.

Method. The method involves using an exact Hamiltonian transformation to efficiently calculate the energy cost function $E^{(n+1)}_g( heta_g)$ for operator selection, which is then used within the ADAPT-VQE framework.

Model / system. The study is applied to fermionic quantum chemistry calculations for molecules like LiH, BeH2, and H2O, using the electronic Hamiltonian in second quantization.

Key observables. Energy convergence relative to the FCI reference, selection cost (measured in circuit evaluations), and overall resource cost.

Important parameters / regimes. The comparison spans weakly correlated (equilibrium) and highly correlated (stretched) molecular geometries.

Assumptions / limitations. The efficiency gain relies on the mathematical identity derived from the exact Hamiltonian transformation, which simplifies the calculation of the energy cost function.

Figures summary. Figures compare the energy convergence and the relative cost (selection vs. optimization) of various ADAPT-VQE strategies (Gradient vs. Energy-based) across different molecular geometries.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the ADAPT-VQE framework and the cost of energy-based selection. It then details the exact Hamiltonian transformation to derive a resource-efficient energy cost function. Finally, it benchmarks this improved method against gradient-based approaches across various molecular systems and optimization strategies.

Abstract

The energy-based approach to operator selection in ADAPT-VQE relies on reconstructing the one-parameter energy landscape for each operator in the pool. In fermionic implementations, the cost of reconstructing this energy landscape often becomes a bottleneck. We address this issue through an exact Hamiltonian transformation that reformulates the one-parameter energy landscape according to a generator-dependent fragmentation of the transformed Hamiltonian. While our method is mathematically identical to standard fermionic Rotoselect, it effectively reduces its cost by about a factor of two, bringing it close to that of gradient-based ADAPT-VQE. We use this formulation to benchmark the gradient-based and energy-based selection approaches in combination with two ansatz-optimization strategies -- last', where only the appended operator is optimized, orfull', where the full ansatz is re-optimized -- and with both fixed-orbital and orbital-optimized formulations. The benchmark comprises $\text{LiH}$, $\text{BeH}_2$, and $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ at both equilibrium and stretched geometries. In the weakly correlated regime, the `last' optimization strategy combined with energy-based selection enables the efficient construction of an accurate ansatz, while avoiding any VQE optimization. As correlation increases, full ansatz re-optimization and orbital optimization become the main factors governing convergence and overall resource cost. These results show that exact Hamiltonian transformations provide an effective route to reducing the measurement overhead of fermionic energy-based ADAPT-VQE. Moreover, the benchmark clarifies the relative role of operator scoring approach, re-optimization strategy, and orbital treatment in the performance of ADAPT-VQE.

Spectral criteria for generalization in unsupervised Hebbian nets

Authors: Elena Agliari, Paulo Duarte Mourão, Alberto Fachechi, Pierpaolo Vivo
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04651v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work develops a theoretical framework using random matrix theory to analyze unsupervised Hebbian networks. It shows that the network's ability to generalize is directly encoded in the spectral structure of its interaction matrix. A clear spectral separation between signal and noise directions is predicted to be the necessary condition for successful reconstruction of underlying patterns.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical framework connecting spectral properties (eigenvalue distribution) to functional performance (generalization) provides a powerful, generalizable diagnostic tool applicable to complex quantum or classical learning systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To derive the asymptotic spectral properties of the interaction matrix in unsupervised Hebbian networks and establish spectral criteria that predict the network's ability to generalize beyond training data.

Main result. Generalization capability is predicted by the spectral transition: the existence of two separated spectral bulks (signal/noise separation) indicates successful generalization, while a merged single-bulk phase implies failure.

Method. The analysis combines random matrix theory and replica methods to compute the quenched free energy and derive the limiting spectral density of the interaction matrix.

Model / system. The model is an unsupervised Hebbian network trained on noisy realizations of hidden ground-truth vectors, extending classical Hopfield models from simple memory retrieval to latent structure extraction.

Key observables. The asymptotic spectrum ($ ilde{ ho}'(\lambda)$), the spectral gap (separation into two bulks), and the overlap measures (archetype and example overlaps).

Important parameters / regimes. Network load ($\alpha$), noise parameter ($r$), regularization parameter ($t$), and the ratio $\alpha M$ which dictates the spectral regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the thermodynamic limit ($N o \infty$) and the use of the replica-symmetric ansatz for order parameters.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the theoretical spectral predictions versus numerical simulations, showing the spectral evolution under regularization and comparing spectral analysis results with overlap measurements across different load regimes.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first deriving the spectral density using random matrix theory and replica methods, then analyzing the effect of regularization ($t$) on this spectrum, and finally establishing the spectral gap as the necessary and sufficient condition for successful generalization.

Abstract

We consider an unsupervised Hebbian network where the pairwise interactions among neurons are built on noisy realizations of hidden ground-truth vectors. Unlike classical Hopfield models, designed as memory devices, this class of networks can be employed to extract latent structure and generalize beyond the "training" set. By combining random matrix theory and replica methods, we derive the asymptotic spectrum of the corresponding interaction matrix and show that the onset of generalization is controlled by a sharp spectral transition. Depending on the quality and the size of the accessible dataset, the spectrum displays either two separated bulks, encoding informative and noisy directions, or a merged single-bulk phase where such distinction is lost. We show that, when coupled with regularization, the emergence of such a spectral split predicts the network's capability to reconstruct the ground-truth vectors from corrupted samples.

Variational low-energy subspaces for chemically accurate excited states

Authors: Clemens Giuliani, Rocco Martinazzo, Giuseppe Carleo, Riccardo Rossi
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.05148v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces EXIDOS, a novel, low-scaling variational method for calculating electronic excited states. It reformulates the complex excited-state optimization into an iterative, ground-state-like problem solvable via generalized eigenvalue equations. The method achieves chemical accuracy across diverse molecular systems, making it a powerful tool for photochemistry and spectroscopy.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a highly scalable, fully variational route to excited states that bypasses the need for explicit symmetry assumptions or state-specific knowledge, which is a major bottleneck in non-adiabatic dynamics simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Accurately calculating electronic excited states is crucial for photochemistry and spectroscopy, but high-level methods scale steeply and require prior knowledge of the state's character.

Main result. The developed EXIDOS method achieves chemical accuracy for numerous excited states in various molecules by reformulating the optimization as an iterated ground-state-like problem.

Method. The method reformulates the variational excited-state optimization as an iterated ground-state problem, solving a generalized eigenvalue problem within a low-energy subspace spanned by non-orthogonal Slater determinants.

Model / system. The work applies to general molecular systems, benchmarked on molecules like N2, CO, HCl, C2, and NH3, using the electronic molecular Hamiltonian.

Key observables. Excitation energies, oscillator strengths, dipole moments, and the ability to characterize different state types (charge-transfer, Rydberg, double excitations).

Important parameters / regimes. The method is controlled only by the number of states ($K$) and the number of determinants per state ($ ext{ND}$); accuracy is benchmarked against FCI and high-level CC methods.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the overlap matrix $\mathbf{S}$ is non-singular and uses an unconstrained Slater determinant basis, which is noted to not be strictly size-consistent.

Figures summary. Figures compare EXIDOS results against FCI/CCSDTQ for spectra, show potential energy curves for C2, and illustrate the characterization of different excited states (charge-transfer, double excitations, Rydberg) in various molecules.

Paper structure. The paper systematically develops the theoretical framework by reformulating the excited-state optimization into a generalized eigenvalue problem, detailing the EXIDOS algorithm, and validating its performance across diverse chemical benchmarks.

Abstract

Accurate electronic excited states are essential for photochemistry, spectroscopy and non-adiabatic molecular dynamics, but high-level calculations often scale steeply and require prior knowledge of the target state's character or symmetry. Here we show that variational excited-state optimization can be reformulated as an iterated ground-state-like problem for a low-energy subspace of the electronic Hamiltonian. Applying this variational principle to non-orthogonal Slater determinants leads to EXIDOS, an automatic method for excited state calculations controlled only by the number of states and determinants per state. EXIDOS optimizes multiple excited states simultaneously, without explicit orthogonality constraints or imposed spin and point-group symmetries. Benchmarks against FCI and state-of-the-art quantum chemistry methods show chemical accuracy for a multitude of states in N$_2$ and CO, charge-transfer states in HCl, Rydberg states in NH$_3$, double excitations and extended potential-energy curves in C$_2$, and avoided crossings and conical intersections in ethylene. These results establish EXIDOS as a low-scaling, fully variational route to chemically accurate excited states.

Abstract

A vibrational paradigm of atomic dynamic in dense fluids is known to provide useful insight on the transport and thermodynamic properties of fluids in three dimensions. In this paper, a vibrational model is generalized to describe the excess entropy of two-dimensional (2D) fluids. A simple practical implementation of this model is demonstrated to deliver accurate results for various systems, such as one-component plasmas with Coulomb and logarithmic interactions, a 2D fluid of dipole particles, and a 2D Yukawa fluid. The applicability limits, relevance to three-dimensional fluids, relations to other 2D phenomena, and potential practical applications are briefly discussed.

Summary. This paper analyzes how disorder and high-frequency driving control the non-equilibrium dynamics of quantum spin chains. By studying entanglement entropy and energy absorption, the authors distinguish between thermalization, prethermalization, and a robust Floquet-MBL phase. The findings highlight that disorder is crucial for stabilizing non-equilibrium, localized states against external driving.

Why it may be interesting. This work directly addresses non-equilibrium dynamics in disordered quantum systems, which is central to understanding thermalization, localization, and the stability of quantum states under external driving fields, highly relevant to AMO physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the non-equilibrium dynamics of a disordered, periodically driven quantum spin chain, focusing on the competition between interaction, disorder, and external driving.

Main result. The research establishes that strong disorder and high driving frequencies can suppress energy absorption and lead to a stable Floquet-Many-Body Localized (Floquet-MBL) regime, preventing thermalization.

Method. The analysis employs Floquet theory, utilizing the van Vleck inverse-frequency expansion to derive effective Hamiltonians, alongside numerical simulations to track energy and entanglement entropy.

Model / system. The system is a disordered, periodically driven quantum spin chain (e.g., transverse-field Ising model) governed by a time-periodic Hamiltonian incorporating nearest-neighbor interactions, disorder fields, and a time-dependent drive.

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (J), disorder strengths (W), driving frequency ($\Omega$), and the ratio of disorder to interaction scales.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate stroboscopic dynamics showing bounded energy density oscillations and slow, saturating entanglement growth under strong disorder and high driving frequencies, contrasting with thermalization signatures at low frequencies.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the system's evolution by comparing different parameter regimes: low frequency (thermalization), high frequency without disorder (prethermalization), and high frequency with strong disorder (Floquet-MBL).

Abstract

We study the non-equilibrium dynamics of a disordered periodically driven quantum spin chain, with the competition between the interaction, disorder, and Floquet driving being of particular interest. We study dynamics of entanglement entropy, energy absorption to characterize dynamical regimes of the system whether it stays in the Floquet-MBL(many-body localization) region or thermalized region. Starting with a product state in the computational basis, followed by reduced density matrix which in turn gives rise to the entanglement entropy density. With the strength of the interaction, the transverse field, the parallel field, the disorder strength W and the driving frequency, we discover the distinct behaviors of fast delocalization and logarithmic entanglement growth and long-lasting memory of the initial state, indicative of localized or prethermal Floquet regimes. We observe that strong disorder arrests transport and enables slow entanglement dynamics, whereas strong driving frequency arrests energy absorption and creates a long-lived non-equilibrium state. Conversely, weak disorder or low driving frequency leads to delocalization. The outcomes show strong support for non-equilibrium phases in driven many-body systems.

Abstract

We investigate the non-equilibrium dynamics of the disordered one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard model with a focus on many-body localization. The system is initialized in a charge-density-wave-state, and its time evolution is analyzed through sublattice imbalance (spin and charge), and bipartite entanglement entropy. A clear crossover from ergodic to non-ergodic behavior is observed with increasing disorder strength. In the weak disorder regime, rapid decay of imbalance and the fast growth of entanglement indicate efficient thermalization. In contrast, a strong disorder leads to persistent imbalance and slow dynamics, signaling the breakdown of ergodicity. The charge and spin sectors exhibit distinct relaxation behavior, providing evidence for partial decoupling between these degrees of freedom. Furthermore, in the interacting regime, the entanglement entropy shows slow logarithmic growth, reflecting the dephasing-driven dynamics characteristic of the many-body localized phase. These results highlight the interplay between disorder and interactions in determining the dynamical properties of the system and establish robust signatures of many-body localization in the Fermi-Hubbard model.

Summary. This work establishes a comprehensive two-scale analytical framework to model heat transfer in nanomagnet chains under alternating magnetic fields. By rigorously coarse-graining the nanoscale physics, the authors predict that realistic systems exhibit collective heating, with temperature fluctuations at the microkelvin level. The methodology is valuable for understanding energy dissipation in complex, coupled mesoscopic systems.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous development of a multi-scale, non-equilibrium thermal transport model, especially one that quantifies the crossover between local and global energy dissipation, provides advanced tools applicable to open quantum systems where environmental coupling and spatial averaging are crucial.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper develops a two-scale framework to study heat generation and thermal transport in chains of nanomagnets subjected to an alternating magnetic field, focusing on the crossover between localized and global heating regimes.

Main result. For realistic magnetic hyperthermia systems, the model predicts the system operates in a collective heating regime, with local temperature variations at the $\sim\mu ext{K}$ level, which is currently experimentally unresolvable.

Method. The authors employ a rigorous two-scale analytical formalism involving spatial and temporal coarse-graining, solving the resulting coupled equations using modal decomposition.

Model / system. The system consists of one-dimensional chains of nanomagnets (e.g., magnetite) embedded in a matrix, where heat generation is modeled as a localized, temperature-dependent source term governed by magnetic losses.

Key observables. Spatial correlation length, temperature variance, and the crossover condition governed by the competition between heat generation, diffusion, and dipolar coupling.

Important parameters / regimes. Nanoscale volumetric loss coefficient ($L_m$), macroscopic loss coefficient ($L_N$), Knudsen number ($Kn$), and the dimensionless coupling strength ($\gamma$).

Assumptions / limitations. The continuum Fourier description is valid because the Knudsen number is much less than one ($Kn \ll 1$) for the matrices considered.

Abstract

We develop a two-scale analytical formalism to study heat generation and thermal transport in one-dimensional systems of nanomagnets subjected to a uniform alternating magnetic field. At the nanoscale, each nanomagnet acts as a localized, temperature-dependent heat source governed by its magnetic response, dipolar interactions, and interfacial coupling to the matrix, characterized by a nanoscale volumetric loss coefficient $L_m$. After spatial and temporal averaging, we obtain a coarse-grained assembly-scale equation with effective heating terms and a macroscopic loss coefficient $L_N$. Using modal decomposition, we solve both equations exactly under Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions and establish explicit conditions for a local-to-global heating crossover; this is governed by the competition between heat generation, diffusion, dipolar coupling, and hierarchical losses. The crossover is quantified through the spatial correlation length and temperature variance, with stability criteria incorporating both diffusion and nanoscale losses. The coarse-graining procedure is derived rigorously, and its systematic approximation errors are quantified. For prototypical magnetic hyperthermia systems, such as magnetite nanomagnets in water, our formalism reveals that realistic parameters place these systems firmly in the collective heating regime, with local temperature variations at the $\simμ$K level, which is currently unresolvable experimentally. The continuum Fourier description used here is validated by a Knudsen-number analysis ($\mathrm{Kn} \ll 1$ for amorphous polymer and aqueous matrices).

Abstract

Tip-induced switching in ferroelectrics is commonly framed as a field-driven process controlled by domain-wall kinetics. Here we argue for a more universal viewpoint: under a scanning probe, polarization reversal is fundamentally constrained by how fast screening charge can be supplied and redistributed. By combining local switching experiments with finite-element simulations, we identify a screening-current-limited mechanism in which charge injection and its space-charge-limited relaxation set the pace of switching and the scaling of domain growth. This framework naturally explains regime changes and the apparent breakdown of intrinsic domain-wall laws often inferred in the nanoscale piezoelectric hysteresis measurements. Beyond a reinterpretation of tip switching, the results position screening currents as a hidden control parameter of ferroelectric reversal across materials, turning current regulation into a practical handle for deterministic nanoscale domain engineering.

Abstract

Recent experiments in van der Waals multi-layer systems have demonstrated that superconductivity can emerge from symmetry-reduced, chiral normal states. We here provide a theory for quasiparticle interference (QPI) of superconductors with chiral Bloch bands. Our analysis reveals how the non-trivial quantum geometry of the Bloch states crucially affects the interference pattern even in the normal state, inducing significant sublattice dependence. In the superconducting state, the behavior becomes more complex due to the interplay of the quantum geometry of the Bogoliubov quasiparticles with the momentum-dependent phase of the order parameter. We reveal how the spatial dependence of the local spectral function around impurities can be used to distinguish between different candidate pairing states, both with zero and finite center-of-mass momentum. Our work thus provides guidance to interpreting QPI patterns in materials with chiral bands, which may be useful when probing the rich physics of pairing in such systems.
