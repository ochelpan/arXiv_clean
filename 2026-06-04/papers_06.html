A unified theory of thin film and bulk bilayer nickelates

Authors: Jiangfan Wang, Yi-feng Yang
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04821v1

Summary. This work develops a unified theoretical model to explain the complex superconducting behavior in bilayer nickelates across different crystal structures and doping levels. By treating the interplay between $d_{z^2}$ and $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbitals, the theory successfully accounts for multiple superconducting domes, valence bond states, and Kondo scattering. It predicts pathways to achieve high-$T_c$ superconductivity even at ambient pressure.

Why it may be interesting. The detailed treatment of orbital physics, strong correlations, and phase transitions (SC $\leftrightarrow$ VBS $\leftrightarrow$ Kondo) provides a rich theoretical playground relevant to understanding emergent quantum phases in strongly correlated materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To provide a unified explanation for the similarities and distinctions in bilayer nickelate superconductivity observed in both pressurized bulk and thin films.

Main result. The theory predicts two superconducting domes upon doping, separated by a valence bond state near half filling, and suggests ambient pressure superconductivity can be achieved by tuning interlayer coupling.

Method. The authors employ a two-component theoretical model using slave-boson representations and calculate superconducting properties via the Bethe-Salpeter equation.

Model / system. The system is bilayer nickelates ($ ext{R}_3 ext{Ni}_2 ext{O}_7$), modeled using a two-orbital $t-V-U$ Hamiltonian that incorporates interlayer superexchange coupling ($J$) between $d_{z^2}$ and $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbitals.

Key observables. Superconducting critical temperature ($T_c$), superconducting gap structure ($\Delta$), resistivity ($ ho(T)$), and self-energy ($\Sigma$).

Important parameters / regimes. Interlayer superexchange coupling ($J$), doping level ($\delta_d$), and on-site Coulomb repulsion ($U$).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of a two-component scenario and slave-boson representation to treat strong magnetic correlations.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate $T_c$ dependence on doping and coupling, the evolution of the $d_{x^2-y^2}$ spectral function across different doping regimes, and the unified phase diagram mapping SC, VBS, Kondo, FL, and WI regions.

Paper structure. The paper develops a unified theoretical framework by analyzing the interplay between $d_{z^2}$ and $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbitals, predicting phase transitions between superconducting, valence bond, and normal metallic states based on doping and interlayer coupling strength.

Abstract

The discovery of bilayer nickelate superconductivity in both pressurized bulk and thin films has drawn enormous attention on their similarity and distinction. Here we provide a unified explanation based on the two-component scenario for a number of key experimental observations reported recently. Our theory predicts two superconducting domes upon electron or hole doping, separated by a valence bond state near $d_{z^2}$ half filling for strong interlayer superexchange coupling $J$, and a single dome across half filling with a lower $T_c$ for weak or moderate $J$. Increasing doping drives the normal state from a Fermi liquid to non-Fermi liquid or weak insulating behaviors, with quasi-linear-in-$T$ scattering rate near optimal $T_c$, while breaking the interlayer valence bonds by oxygen vacancies or chemical substitution simultaneously suppresses the superconductivity and causes local Kondo scattering of $d_{x^2-y^2}$ electrons. These explain the different superconducting transitions and normal states in bulk and thin films, the effect of $d_{z^2}$ hole or electron doping, and the Kondo effect in non-superconducting samples. We propose bulk superconductivity at ambient pressure by doping or reducing the interlayer magnetic coupling and predict even higher $T_c$ upon electron doping.

Summary. This paper introduces and analyzes Exponential Time Differencing (ETD) schemes to simulate the complex dynamics of multicomponent membranes. The authors first prove the stability of these schemes for the simplified case and then apply them to the full, coupled system governing membrane mechanics and protein segregation. This provides a robust and efficient numerical tool for studying pattern formation in biological membranes.

Why it may be interesting. While the physics is condensed matter/soft matter, the focus on developing and proving the stability of advanced time integration schemes (ETD) for stiff, coupled partial differential equations is highly relevant to computational physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing stable, accurate, and efficient numerical schemes to simulate the complex, coupled dynamics of multicomponent membranes.

Main result. The authors rigorously prove that the developed Exponential Time Differencing (ETD) schemes satisfy the discrete maximum-bound principle and unconditional energy stability for the fixed-membrane case.

Method. The core method involves applying ETD schemes (ETD1 and ETDRK2) combined with operator splitting and spectral collocation to solve the coupled phase-field and Ohta-Kawasaki dynamics.

Model / system. The system models multicomponent membranes using a force-balance phase-field equation coupled with membrane-associated Ohta-Kawasaki (OK) dynamics for protein segregation.

Key observables. Discrete energy decay, preservation of the discrete maximum-bound principle (MBP), protein-driven pattern formation, and membrane deformation.

Important parameters / regimes. Repulsion strength ($\gamma$), biochemical strength ($\alpha$), and numerical parameters like time step ($ au$) and spatial resolution ($h$).

Assumptions / limitations. The initial rigorous analysis assumes a fixed phase-field membrane geometry; extending to the fully coupled, evolving system requires careful stabilization and splitting.

Figures summary. Numerical experiments confirm predicted temporal accuracy and energy decay for the fixed-membrane OK problem, and demonstrate stable 3D simulations of protein-driven pattern formation.

Paper structure. The work first establishes the ETD schemes and proves stability for the simplified, fixed-geometry OK problem. It then extends this framework to the full, coupled system, employing spectral methods and narrow-band techniques for efficiency.

Abstract

In this paper, we develop and analyze exponential time differencing (ETD) schemes for a phase-field model of multicomponent membranes proposed in our previous work \cite{luo2025ohta}, in which membrane deformation is governed by a force-balance phase-field equation and protein segregation is described by a membrane-associated Ohta-Kawasaki (OK) dynamics. For a fixed phase-field membrane, we introduce a geometry-adapted operator splitting method based on the localization function, which reformulates the surface OK dynamics into a form suitable for ETD integration. The resulting first- and second-order ETD schemes, combined with finite-difference spatial discretization, are rigorously proved to satisfy a discrete maximum-bound principle and unconditional energy stability. For the coupled system, we construct stabilized ETD schemes in an FFT-based spectral framework, treating stiff linear terms exactly and nonlinear mechanochemical couplings explicitly. A narrow-band implementation further reduces the computational cost by restricting surface calculations to the diffuse membrane region. Numerical experiments confirm the predicted temporal accuracy, maximum-bound preservation, and energy decay for the fixed-membrane OK problem, and demonstrate stable and efficient three-dimensional simulations of protein-driven pattern formation and membrane deformation.

Abstract

Summary. This work reports on achieving high-quality quantum Hall transport in $\beta$-Ag$_2$Te topological insulators using a novel, simple synthesis technique. By controlling stoichiometry in-situ via Te capping, the researchers suppress bulk conduction and tune the carrier density without external gates. This allowed for the clear observation of quantized Hall plateaus and confirmation of massless Dirac fermion behavior, establishing a powerful, accessible platform for studying topological quantum phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. The self-tuning, gate-free nature of the carrier control mechanism is highly relevant for realizing clean quantum states in novel materials, bypassing complex lithography or doping requirements common in quantum physics research.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Accessing clean surface quantum transport in topological insulators is difficult due to residual bulk conduction from lattice defects.

Main result. The authors demonstrated that stoichiometry-driven vacancy engineering in $\beta$-Ag$_2$Te allows for the observation of a fully developed $\nu=1$ Quantum Hall state, confirming massless Dirac dispersion.

Method. The study uses Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) combined with an in-situ Te capping step to control stoichiometry and suppress bulk defects, enabling gate-free tuning of carrier density.

Model / system. The physical system is $\beta$-Ag$_2$Te, a topological insulator exhibiting Dirac surface states. The method relies on controlling the Ag/Te stoichiometry via Te capping time to tune the Fermi level across the bulk gap.

Key observables. Quantized Hall plateaus ($\nu=1, 3, 5, 7$), Landau-level energy collapse onto $E_N \propto \sqrt{N}$, and the dependence of mobility on carrier density ($\mu$ vs. $n_{2D}$).

Important parameters / regimes. The ability to tune carrier density over an order of magnitude without external gates; the observation of odd-integer filling factors ($\nu=2N+1$).

Assumptions / limitations. The surface-state penetration depth is much smaller than the film thickness, ensuring surface state dominance; the Te capping process effectively controls donor-type defects.

Figures summary. Figures show magnetotransport measurements ($R_{xx}$ and $R_{yx}$ vs. $B$) demonstrating QHE plateaus, and plots showing the dependence of quantum parameters ($\mu$, $m_{eff}$) on carrier density ($n_{2D}$).

Paper structure. The paper establishes the problem (bulk conduction in TIs), introduces the novel MBE/vacancy engineering technique, presents magnetotransport data showing QHE and Dirac signatures, and concludes by validating this method as a versatile, gate-free approach.

Abstract

Accessing surface quantum transport in topological insulators is hampered by residual bulk conduction arising from lattice defects. Here, we demonstrate a novel synthesis pathway for realizing high mobility $β$-Ag$_2$Te thin films where surface transport is dominant. An \textit{in-situ} vacancy engineering step as part of the molecular beam epitaxy growth process acts to modify the stoichiometry and suppress donor-type defects, enabling continuous tuning of the sheet carrier density over more than an order of magnitude through the charge-neutrality point without an external gate electrode. In the lower-carrier-density films, a fully developed $ν=1$ quantum Hall state is observed, and Landau-level energies extracted across samples collapse onto the $E_N=v_\mathrm{F}\sqrt{2e\hbar NB}$ relation, providing evidence for the massless Dirac dispersion of the top and bottom surface states. These results establish stoichiometry-driven vacancy engineering as a versatile lithography- and gate-free approach to accessing quantum Hall transport in epitaxial topological-insulator thin films.

Abstract

State-of-the-art transistors have been successfully scaled the gate lengths and channel thicknesses down to 5 nm for high-performance and energy-efficient information processing. However, reducing channel width below 40-50 nm remains a bottleneck, as dangling bonds, edge disorder, and lateral depletion suppress drive current and degrade device performance. Here, we break this width-scaling wall using ultra-scaled two-dimensional semiconductor (2DSC) nanoribbon transistors down to 15 nm. In contrast to the conventional scaling rule of degradation of current density upon width scaling, our atomically-thin monolayer and bilayer molybdenum disulfide nanoribbon transistors exhibit enhancement of on-current density of up to 230% and 170%,respectively, followed by a saturation for the narrowest channels down to 15 nm. The ultra-narrow nanoribbon transistors maintain the highest on/off ratios reported so far for similar device dimensions, with improved mobility and threshold-voltage stability, indicating reduced edge scattering and depletion with a stronger electrostatic control. These findings lead to a breakthrough in width scaling rules using 2DSC nanoribbons with enhanced performance at narrower channel widths, which is promising for the ultimate scaling of transistors.

Abstract

Abstract

Hydrogenation is an effective strategy for enhancing electron--phonon coupling (EPC) and superconductivity in two-dimensional materials. However, excessively strong EPC can also induce lattice instabilities, leading to charge-density-wave (CDW) formation and structural phase transitions. Here, using first-principles calculations, we investigate CDW order in the Janus transition-metal chalcogenide hydrides 1T-WSH and 1T-WSeH. We find that the high-symmetry phases exhibit pronounced phonon softening at the M point, driving a transition to a commensurate $2\times2$ distorted structure characterized by an emergent triangular charge-density-wave (T-CDW) pattern. Analysis of the electronic structure, susceptibility, and phonon spectrum reveals that the instability is not driven by conventional Fermi-surface nesting but originates from strong momentum-dependent EPC. The T-CDW transition reconstructs the electronic structure and reduces the density of states at the Fermi level, leading to a substantial renormalization of the EPC strength. Consequently, the electron--phonon coupling constants decrease from $λ=2.04$ to $1.50$ in 1T-WSH and from $λ=3.94$ to $1.06$ in 1T-WSeH, while superconductivity remains robust in CDW phase with predicted transition temperatures of $T_c=12.28$ K and $7.75$ K, respectively. Together with previous results for MoSH and MoSeH, our findings establish a universal mechanism in the 1T-$MCH$ family ($M=\mathrm{Mo},\mathrm{W}$ and $C=\mathrm{S},\mathrm{Se}$), where the primary role of the T-CDW phase is not to eliminate superconductivity but to stabilize the lattice through EPC renormalization. The T-CDW phase therefore acts as an intrinsic self-stabilizing response that relieves excessively strong EPC while preserving phonon-mediated superconductivity.
