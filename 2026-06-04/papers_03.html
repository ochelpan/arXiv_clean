Atom Interferometry with Transverse Optical Modes

Authors: Ryan Husband, Ryan J. Thomas, Yosri Ben-Aïcha, Rhys H. Eagle, Jessica Eastman, John E. Debs, Patrick J. Everitt, Michael Larsen, Eric Imhof, Charles A. Sackett, John D. Close, Simon A. Haine, Samuel Legge
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04430v1

Summary. This paper demonstrates atom interferometry using the transverse phase structure of optical beams, specifically utilizing the helical phase windings of HyGG beams. The authors successfully measured rotations and provided a detailed theoretical and experimental characterization of how thermal motion causes spatially-dependent decoherence in the interferometer contrast. This advances the field by establishing a robust method for high-precision sensing using structured light.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant as it pushes the metrological capabilities of atom interferometry by systematically incorporating and quantifying the effects of transverse optical structure (OAM) and thermal decoherence, providing a roadmap for next-generation quantum sensors.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop and experimentally demonstrate atom interferometry utilizing the transverse phase profile (orbital angular momentum) of structured optical modes, moving beyond traditional longitudinal phase measurements.

Main result. The interferometer successfully measures rotations with a sensitivity scaling linearly with the orbital angular momentum quantum number ($\ell$) and interrogation time ($t_{ ext{int}}$). Furthermore, the study derived and confirmed an analytical expression for the spatially-varying thermal decoherence of the fringe visibility.

Method. The authors employed Ramsey interferometry with cold $^{87} ext{Rb}$ atoms, using Hypergeometric Gaussian (HyGG) beams to imprint helical phase windings. They measured rotation by inducing physical rotations of the optical fields and characterized decoherence by measuring fringe contrast reduction.

Model / system. The physical system is atom interferometry using cold $^{87} ext{Rb}$ atoms and structured light fields (HyGG beams). The theoretical analysis models the phase accumulation ($\Delta\phi$) and contrast ($C$) by considering the dependence on atomic velocities ($\dot{r}, \dot{ heta}$) in the presence of a helical phase front.

Key observables. Rotational phase shift ($\phi$), interferometer fringe contrast, and the measured dephasing radius ($R$).

Important parameters / regimes. Orbital Angular Momentum quantum number ($\ell$), interrogation time ($t_{ ext{int}}$), and the atomic temperature (which dictates decoherence).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on approximations for the change in angle ($\Delta heta$) during ballistic expansion and assumes that the primary limitation for sensitivity is thermal phase diffusion.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate measured Ramsey fringes showing dependence on $\ell$ and $\Omega$, and plots confirm the linear scaling of the measured dephasing radius ($R$) with the predicted analytical scaling law derived from the thermal motion.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from demonstrating the basic rotation measurement using transverse modes to quantitatively characterizing the thermal decoherence. It develops the theoretical framework by modeling the phase accumulation in polar coordinates, leading to the final analytical expression for the contrast reduction due to velocity spread.

Abstract

We experimentally demonstrate atom interferometry using the transverse phase profile of an optical mode. As proof-of-principle, we use the helical phase windings of Hypergeometric Gaussian beams for Ramsey interferometry with ensembles of ballistically-expanding cold Rb87 atoms, and we show that the interferometer can measure rotations induced by a motor with a sensitivity that scales linearly with orbital angular momentum and interferometer time. We characterize the thermal decoherence of the interferometer, deriving and experimentally confirming a closed-form expression for the spatially-varying interferometer visibility arising near the singularity of the helical phase winding, motivating the use of condensed atoms in ring-shaped traps.

High-fidelity neutral atom gates leveraging low-rank Hessian optimization

Authors: Genyue Liu, Guillaume Bornet, Deniz Kurdak, Mingxuan Xiao, Chenyuan Li, Bichen Zhang, Jeff D. Thompson
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.05060v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 3/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a novel, efficient calibration technique for quantum gates by exploiting the low-rank structure of the gate fidelity Hessian. By restricting optimization to the principal subspace defined by this Hessian, the authors successfully demonstrate high-fidelity controlled-Z gates on neutral atoms. This method offers a general, robust framework for improving quantum gate performance in complex atomic platforms.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful, physically motivated mathematical tool (Hessian analysis) to simplify the notoriously difficult problem of optimizing complex quantum control pulses, directly applicable to state-of-the-art neutral atom quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of calibrating high-dimensional waveforms required for optimal quantum control to achieve high-fidelity multi-qubit gates.

Main result. A low-rank Hessian optimization method is established as an efficient calibration strategy, achieving high gate fidelities (e.g., 0.99902) with demonstrated robustness to environmental variations.

Method. The core method involves analyzing the fidelity Hessian of the gate error, exploiting its low-rank structure to restrict optimization to a small, physically relevant principal subspace.

Model / system. The work uses neutral atom qubits, specifically $^{171} ext{Yb}$ nuclear-spin qubits, to implement an amplitude-robust controlled-Z (CZ) gate. The dynamics are modeled using optimal control theory applied to a multi-level atomic system.

Key observables. Gate fidelity ($F$), raw and post-selected error rates ($\epsilon$), and the eigenvalues/eigenvectors of the fidelity Hessian.

Important parameters / regimes. The rank ($r$) of the Hessian, which is determined by the number of accessible leakage and coherent error channels; laser power variation (up to 20%).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on a second-order expansion of the infidelity in the control parameter error ($\delta\vec{\Omega}^T H \delta\vec{\Omega}$), and the low-rank structure is assumed to capture the dominant error channels.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the CZ gate setup, the eigenvalue spectrum showing the few nonzero eigenvalues, the decomposition of the error into parallel and perpendicular components, and comparisons of optimization strategies.

Paper structure. The paper develops the theory by analyzing the fidelity Hessian, applies it to the CZ gate, demonstrates the optimization protocol using experimental feedback, and verifies the robustness and error correction capabilities.

Abstract

Quantum optimal control can produce fast and robust multi-qubit gates, but experimentally calibrating the resulting high-dimensional waveforms remains challenging because direct searches over large parameter spaces converge slowly. Building on the low-rank structure of quantum-control landscapes, we develop and benchmark a Hessian-based calibration method for optimal-control gates. The method identifies the few waveform directions that affect fidelity to leading order, with the number of directions set by the accessible leakage and coherent error channels, and optimizes only within this principal space using closed-loop experimental feedback. We apply this approach to an amplitude-robust controlled-Z gate on metastable-state 171Yb nuclear-spin qubits. Experimentally, we verify the predicted Hessian-sensitive directions and demonstrate rapid convergence of the optimization protocol. The optimized gate reaches a raw fidelity of 0.9959(2), increasing to 0.99902(7) after postselection on no detected loss, and the performance is essentially unchanged under laser-power variations of up to 20%. We further show that the same fidelity Hessian directions can correct certain Hamiltonian parameter errors. These results establish low-rank Hessian optimization as an efficient and physically motivated calibration strategy for high-dimensional optimal-control gates, which is broadly applicable to many qubit types.

The findings establish a pathway for developing high-temperature, spin-driven magnetoelectric memory and logic devices.

Main problem. The research aims to demonstrate and understand room-temperature multiferroic ordering in simple, collinear magnetoelectric antiferromagnets.

Main result. The study experimentally and theoretically confirms that domain walls in $ ext{Cr}_2 ext{O}_3$ exhibit multiferroic behavior driven by the flexo-Zeeman interaction, leading to measurable electric polarization and magnetization.

Abstract

Spin-driven multiferroicity attracts significant interest due to its tunability and inherently strong magnetoelectric coupling. While this mechanism induces sizeable electric polarization, it typically occurs at low temperatures and in complex materials. In the simple oxide, the magnetoelectric antiferromagnet Cr$_2$O$_3$, we experimentally demonstrate the existence of specific domain walls that act as room-temperature multiferroic regions. This behavior stems from an anisotropic crystal-symmetry-dependent mechanism of exchange origin, which is applicable to a broad class of bipartite antiferromagnets. The key signature is the magnetization occurring at antiferromagnetic textures, driven by the flexo-Zeeman interaction. These findings establish a foundation for exploring high-temperature spin-driven multiferroicity for magnetoelectric spin-orbit memory and logic applications.

Magnetic structure of diamond EuTi2Al20

Authors: S. W. Lovesey, D. D. Khalyavin
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04802v1

Summary. This work uses resonant X-ray diffraction theory to distinguish between two possible low-temperature magnetic symmetries of EuTi2Al20. By calculating how magnetic multipoles affect the diffraction pattern based on symmetry constraints, the authors provide a detailed diagnostic tool to resolve the ambiguity left by current experimental data.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on solid-state magnetism, the use of advanced X-ray scattering techniques to probe subtle symmetry breaking via multipole interactions is relevant to understanding complex electronic correlations in materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to definitively determine the magnetic symmetry of the compound EuTi2Al20 that emerges below 3.3 K, distinguishing between two competing orthorhombic candidates.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the predicted resonant X-ray diffraction patterns, particularly those involving parity-odd multipoles, are distinct for the two candidate symmetries, offering a path for experimental discrimination.

Method. The study combines theoretical calculations of electronic structure factors with the analysis of selection rules derived from parity mixing in the parent cubic structure.

Model / system. The physical system is EuTi2Al20, which undergoes magnetic ordering below 3.3 K. The analysis focuses on the magnetic symmetries and the resulting magnetic multipoles formed by Eu ions.

Key observables. Diffraction intensities measured via resonant X-ray diffraction, specifically analyzing polarization ($\sigma$ vs. $\pi$) and azimuthal angle ($\psi$) dependence.

Important parameters / regimes. The critical temperature (3.3 K), the specific magnetic space groups ($ ext{PInna}$ and $ ext{PInn2}$), and the parity-odd multipole contributions.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that the selection rule from parity mixing in the parent cubic structure is preserved, and that the local axes align with the crystal axes in the nominal setting.

Figures summary. The notes mention the use of X-ray polarization states ($\sigma, \pi, \sigma', \pi'$) and the fulfillment of the Bragg condition, suggesting figures illustrate these polarization dependencies.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by establishing the context of magnetic symmetry determination, detailing the theoretical framework involving parity mixing and multipole calculations for the two candidate symmetries, and concluding with a comparison of the predicted, symmetry-dependent diffraction patterns.

Abstract

We study magnetic symmetries of EuTi2Al20 that appear in the ordering of Eu ions below a temperature at 3.3 K, according to an analysis of substantial experimental evidence [M. Kawamata et al., J. Phys. Soc. Japan. 95, 024701 (2026)]. They descend from the cubic diamond structure that depends explicitly on mixing of orbitals with different spatial parities, e.g., diamond s- and p-atomic states. The lattice (2, 2, 2) Bragg spot, for example, is lattice forbidden in the absence of parity mixing. Our work is motivated by a first proposal that low-temperature magnetic symmetries of diamond EuTi2Al20 are orthorhombic and belong to magnetic crystal classes that include time reversal. Body-centre anti-translation in diffraction patterns is a corollary. Current experimental data does not distinguish between two orthorhombic candidates, even though one is centrosymmetric and the other one is polar. We demonstrate that magnetic parity-odd entities (multipoles) formed by mixing d-and f-atomic states at europium positions, with discreet symmetries that match those of a Dirac monopole, are different for the two candidates. Moreover, the multipoles are visible in resonant x-ray diffraction patterns. The selection rule from parity mixing in the parent cubic diamond structure is preserved, and permitted Bragg spots are different for the two candidate magnetic symmetries. Our symmetry informed diffraction patterns include rotation of the crystal about the reflection vector (an azimuthal angle scan) and a full account of x-ray polarization to spur more revealing experiments.

Summary. This theoretical work tests the limits of quantum mechanics by examining the violation of the Leggett-Garg inequality in neutrino oscillations. The study models both flavour and spin transitions under the influence of a strong magnetic field. The main finding is that the LGI is violated in specific spatial regions, providing a quantum signature potentially observable in extreme astrophysical environments like magnetars.

Why it may be interesting. While the core topic is particle astrophysics, the methodology—testing fundamental quantum limits (LGI) using time/space correlations derived from quantum evolution equations—is relevant to open quantum systems theory and quantum foundations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the violation of the Leggett-Garg inequality (LGI) when applied to neutrino flavour and spin oscillations in the presence of a constant magnetic field.

Main result. The LGI is shown to be violated for both flavour and spin neutrino oscillations in regions defined by the oscillation length $\Delta L$, particularly when strong magnetic fields are present.

Model / system. The system models neutrino flavour ($ u_L^e \leftrightarrow u_L^\mu$) and spin ($ u_L^e \leftrightarrow u_R^e$) oscillations governed by the Dirac equation, incorporating the effects of a constant transverse magnetic field ($B_\perp$).

Key observables. The LGI violation is quantified by the correlator functions $K_3$ and $K_4$, and the primary observables are the flavour and spin oscillation probabilities $P(t)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Key parameters include the mass squared difference ($\Delta m^2$), mixing angle ($ heta$), neutrino energy ($p$), and the magnetic field strength ($B \sim 10^{12}$ T).

Abstract

The Leggett-Garg inequality (LGI), an analogue of Bellś inequality involving correlations of measurements of one observable on a system at different times, stands as one of the hallmark tests of quantum mechanics against classical predictions. In this work, we investigate its implications in the context of neutrino flavour ($ν_{e}^{L}\leftrightarrowν_μ^{L}$) and spin ($ν_{e}^{L}\leftrightarrowν_{e}^{R}$) oscillations in the presence of a constant transverse magnetic field. For systems with strong magnetic fields, we show that, for both cases, there are length regions $ΔL$ where the LGI is violated, as quantified by the correlator functions $K_{3}$ and $K_{4}$.

Summary. This theoretical work develops a framework showing that the inherent chaos in recurrent neural networks does not preclude stable computation. By analyzing the network's kernel structure using advanced mean-field techniques, the authors prove that this chaos acts as a beneficial intrinsic regularizer, enabling smooth, generalizable coding despite microscopic unpredictability.

Why it may be interesting. The mathematical machinery developed (DMFT, replica method) for analyzing correlations in complex, non-equilibrium systems like RNNs shares conceptual overlap with techniques used in analyzing disordered quantum systems, such as spin glasses or spin-boson models.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the fundamental conflict between the intrinsic chaotic nature of recurrent neural networks and the necessity for their population codes to exhibit smooth, stable representations for functional computation.

Main result. Chaotic dynamics induce a structural property—local roughness while maintaining global smoothness—that acts as an intrinsic regularizer, allowing chaotic systems to sustain coherent, differentiable coding.

Method. The authors combine kernel methods with Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (DMFT) and the replica method to derive the effective kernel describing the network's representation geometry.

Model / system. The study analyzes Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) operating in regimes of intrinsic chaos, comparing the theoretical predictions derived from continuous-rate models against those from discrete, spiking (Glauber) dynamics.

Key observables. Representation geometry (smoothness/roughness), power-law spectral signatures, and the two-replica kernel function $k_{ts}(x_1 \cdot x_2)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Synaptic strength ($g$), network size ($N$), and the ratio of recurrent to feedforward coupling ($g/g_V$).

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the comparison of classification accuracy and kernel functions between chaotic discrete networks and regular/feedforward models, demonstrating the regularization effect of local chaos.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the theoretical link between chaos and representation geometry, then deriving the mean-field equations for the two-replica kernel using DMFT, and finally contrasting the resulting kernel structure for continuous versus discrete signaling dynamics.

Abstract

Cortical circuits operate in a regime of intrinsic chaos, where even tiny changes in input can lead to divergent neural responses. Yet, remarkably, population codes in the brain vary smoothly with sensory stimuli, forming coherent representational manifolds. How can chaotic networks sustain such stable coding? Here, we develop a theoretical framework that links the microscopic chaos of recurrent networks to the macroscopic geometry of neural representations. Combining kernel methods with dynamical mean-field theory, we show that chaotic dynamics induce local roughness (introducing sharp distortions at small scales) while preserving global smoothness across larger stimulus variations. This structural property acts as an intrinsic regularizer, enhancing generalization while maintaining expressivity. Moreover, we show how chaotic networks naturally produce power-law spectral signatures, closely matching experimental observations in cortical recordings. These results explain how chaotic spiking networks can sustain smooth, differentiable population codes and establish a theoretical framework linking network dynamics, computational structure, and recorded neural activity.

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5 · entanglement & information structure 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

Summary. This paper provides a rigorous statistical mechanics treatment of maximizing the minimum spacing among randomly placed points. It reformulates the problem using a threshold-resetting random walk, yielding exact formulas for the tail probability $P(S^* \ge s)$. The results are crucial for understanding optimal resource dispersion in various physical models.

Why it may be interesting. The mathematical techniques developed—relating optimal resource allocation (spacing maximization) to random walk statistics and large deviation theory—are highly relevant for analyzing resource constraints in quantum information or many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the problem of maximizing the minimal spacing ($S^*$) when selecting $M+1$ points from an initial set of $N+1$ random points on a line.

Main result. It derives exact distributional identities and asymptotic formulas for $P(S^* \ge s)$ for general i.i.d. gap distributions, relating it to first-passage functionals of a threshold-resetting random walk.

Method. The core methodology involves mapping the optimization problem to a random walk process, utilizing generating functions, and employing saddle-point approximation techniques for large $N$ and $M$.

Model / system. The model consists of $N+1$ points on a line with successive spacings ($T_j$) assumed to be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). The analysis covers general, exponential, and geometric gap distributions.

Key observables. The maximal minimal spacing ($S^*$), the tail probability $P(S^* \ge s)$, and the relative spacing $eS$.

Important parameters / regimes. The ratio $\alpha = M/N$, the total number of gaps $N$, and the number of selected blocks $M$.

Assumptions / limitations. The initial spacings $T_j$ are assumed to be i.i.d., and the analysis often focuses on the large $N$ limit.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the max-min spacing problem for small $N, M$, sketch the threshold-resetting random walk cycles, and show numerical comparisons of $S^*$ for uniform and exponential gaps.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining the max-min spacing problem, reformulating it via a threshold-resetting random walk, deriving exact distributional identities using generating functions, and finally establishing asymptotic results using saddle-point methods for different gap types.

Abstract

From $N+1$ random points on a line we wish to select $M+1$ points so as to maximize the minimal spacing between them. We consider an initial configuration with independent and identically distributed spacings. The problem is equivalent to optimally grouping consecutive gaps into $M$ blocks and maximizing the smallest block sum. For general gap distributions, and for all $M\leq N$, we derive exact distributional identities for the optimal spacing and obtain its asymptotic behavior. The problem admits a reformulation in terms of a threshold-resetting random walk. The walk advances by successive random increments and is reset to the origin upon exceeding a fixed threshold. The probability that the optimal spacing exceeds a given value coincides with the probability that the walk completes at least $M$ reset cycles within $N$ steps. This yields an exact representation in terms of first-passage functionals of the walk. The same mapping suggests a numerical scheme for the max-min spacing problem in the regime of large $N$ and $M$, whose accuracy is tested against the exact results obtained here.

Summary. This paper analyzes the spectral conditions governing quantum state transfer in 1D spin chains, focusing on the phenomenon of Early State Exclusion (ESE). By introducing the spectral fusion (SF) process, the authors construct infinite families of Hamiltonians that exhibit both perfect state transfer and ESE. This deepens the understanding of how the spectral structure dictates the time evolution and information transport properties of quantum many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides deep spectral insights into quantum transport phenomena in 1D systems, connecting the algebraic structure of the Hamiltonian (Jacobi matrix) directly to observable quantum dynamics like state transfer and its early failure modes.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the phenomenon of Early State Exclusion (ESE) in quantum spin chains, which is the vanishing of the time-evolved state overlap with the initial state at a time earlier than the first Perfect State Transfer (PST).

Main result. The authors provide explicit constructions, using a process called spectral fusion (SF), to build infinite families of Jacobi matrices exhibiting both PST and ESE, thereby broadening the known class of such spin chains.

Method. The analysis heavily relies on the spectral properties of the governing Jacobi matrix, utilizing polynomial sequences defined by three-term recurrence relations and analyzing the time evolution amplitude function $A(t)$ in terms of the matrix eigenvalues.

Model / system. The system is a one-dimensional quantum spin chain with nearest-neighbor interactions, whose dynamics in the single-excitation subspace are modeled by a real symmetric tridiagonal Jacobi matrix $J$.

Key observables. Perfect State Transfer (PST) time $T_0$, the amplitude function $A(t) = \langle e^{-iJt} e_0, e_0 angle$, and the occurrence of ESE at time $ au < T_0$.

Important parameters / regimes. The length of the chain (parity of $N+1$), the structure of the spectrum $\sigma(J)$, and the convergence parameter $m$ used in spectral fusion.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the single-excitation subspace, and several results assume the spectrum $\sigma(J)$ is symmetric or 'reduced' for simplification.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the quantum spin-chain model, examples of ESE occurrences relative to PST times, and comparisons between different spectral structures (e.g., T-Rex vs. Krawtchouk chains) regarding ESE.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by defining the problem (ESE vs. PST), deriving amplitude formulas based on spectral properties, introducing the spectral fusion (SF) process as an inverse operation to spectral surgery, and finally proving convergence theorems to construct infinite families of matrices exhibiting these behaviors.

Abstract

Perfect state transfer (PST) in one-dimensional quantum spin chains provides a natural setting in which quantum information transport can be analyzed using spectral methods. In the single-excitation subspace, the dynamics of a chain with nearest-neighbor interactions are governed by a Jacobi matrix, allowing questions of state transfer to be formulated in terms of eigenvalue distributions and symmetry properties of eigenvectors. In this work, we investigate the phenomenon of \emph{early state exclusion} (ESE), whereby the overlap of the time-evolved state with the initial state vanishes at a time strictly earlier than the first occurrence of perfect state transfer. Building on earlier constructions of Hamiltonians exhibiting PST with and without ESE, we provide explicit Jacobi matrix realizations for arbitrary odd-length chains and establish general conditions under which ESE occurs or does not occur. We propose the process of \emph{spectral fusion} (SF) to build infinite families of such Hamiltonians. These results broaden the known class of spin chains displaying early state exclusion and further clarify the role of spectral structure of the Hamiltonians.

Abstract

We formulate the gravitationally induced quantum decoherence of a massive object prepared in a spatial superposition. Starting from linearized gravity coupled to a massive system particle and an environmental scalar field, we derive a closed-time-path influence functional governing the reduced system dynamics. In the nonrelativistic and quasi-static regime, the decoherence exponent can be written as a bilinear functional of the difference of the system stress-energy tensors and an effective noise kernel obtained by dressing the environmental stress-energy tensor correlator with graviton propagators. We then apply this framework to the Newtonian long-range gravitational interaction and evaluate the resulting decoherence function for a dilute nonrelativistic gas modeled by finite wave packets and coarse-grained in time and space. By performing controlled approximations, we obtain analytic expressions for the cumulative decoherence function and show that the dominant contribution is accumulated logarithmically over a broad range of distances, while remaining subdominant to conventional collisional decoherence under realistic conditions.
