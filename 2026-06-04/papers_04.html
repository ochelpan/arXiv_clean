Protecting Quantum States via the Super-Zeno Effect and Anticoherence

Authors: C. Chryssomalakos, A. G. Flores-Delgado, E. Guzmán-González, L. Hanotel
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04681v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 1/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a robust method for protecting quantum states by combining the Super-Zeno Effect with the concept of anticoherence in spin systems. By confining the state to a specially defined anticoherent subspace and applying periodic unitary pulses, the authors demonstrate that quantum evolution can be effectively 'frozen' against environmental noise to arbitrarily high orders in time. This offers a powerful theoretical framework for building more resilient quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful theoretical toolkit for quantum control, showing how combining geometric constraints (anticoherence) with dynamical control (SZE) can achieve superior noise resilience compared to standard error correction methods.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is to develop and analyze methods for protecting fragile quantum states from environmental noise and decoherence.

Main result. The combination of the Super-Zeno Effect (SZE) and exploiting spin anticoherent subspaces proves highly effective, allowing for the theoretical freezing of state evolution to arbitrarily high orders in time.

Method. The authors combine the SZE, which uses periodic unitary pulses, with the concept of anticoherence, which identifies subspaces where the system's evolution is naturally constrained, leading to high-order protection.

Model / system. The system modeled is a spin system subjected to noise characterized by an $n$-magnetic interaction Hamiltonian, $H \propto (B \cdot S)^n$. The protection mechanism relies on identifying and confining the state to a $q$-anticoherent subspace $P$.

Key observables. Fidelity ($F$) and Freezing Fidelity ($F_P$) are the key observables used to quantify the degree of state protection against noise.

Important parameters / regimes. The order of anticoherence ($q$), the degree of the Hamiltonian ($n$), and the number of super-Zeno pulses ($K$) are critical parameters governing the protection level.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the initial state resides within the protected anticoherent subspace $P$, and the protocol relies on the ability to apply precise, periodic unitary pulses.

Figures summary. Figures compare the fidelity ($ar{F}$) achieved using standard Zeno vs. super-Zeno effects, and demonstrate that increasing the order of anticoherence ($q=1, 2, 3$) significantly improves the protection time for a given Hamiltonian.

Paper structure. The paper systematically introduces the problem of noise protection, defines the mathematical tools (SZE, anticoherence), derives the conditions for protection, and then analyzes the performance using fidelity measures for various orders of protection.

Abstract

We consider a spin system coupled to a noisy environment via an interaction Hamiltonian $H$ which is a polynomial of degree $n$ in the spin operators $S_i$ ($n$-magnetic Hamiltonian), and show that the use of the quantum super-Zeno effect in conjunction with a spin-anticoherent subspace, is highly effective in protecting the spin from the noise. When $n=1$, as in the case of unwanted rotations induced by stray magnetic fields, our scheme provides a quantum gyroscope, a concept that generalizes naturally to higher $n$ values (a quantum $n$-gyroscope). The gist of the proposed protocol is to use the quantum super-Zeno effect to confine a state to the subspace, and then exploit anticoherence to freeze its evolution within it.

Surface Charge Doping for Ion-Pairing Criticality in Confined Electrolytes

Authors: Na Shen, Yabei Wu, Wenqing Zhang
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04791v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work analyzes how imposing a weak surface charge on confined electrolytes modifies their ionic stability. Using advanced statistical mechanics theory, the authors show that the surface charge acts as a doping agent, preventing complete ion depletion and suppressing the critical temperature for ion pairing. This reveals a universal scaling law governing the interplay between surface chemistry, confinement, and correlated ionic behavior.

Why it may be interesting. The concept of surface charge acting as a tunable 'thermodynamic doping field' to control phase transitions and transport in confined ionic systems is highly relevant to understanding interfacial phenomena in quantum materials and nanoscale devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how weak surface charge doping modifies the ion-pairing criticality in quasi-two-dimensional electrolytes, specifically examining the decoupling between microscopic pairing and macroscopic ionicity.

Main result. Surface charge doping suppresses the association-driven thermodynamic instability by maintaining a finite screening floor via mobile counterions, causing the critical temperature to collapse onto a single scaled perturbation variable.

Method. The authors employ a generalized Debye-Huckel-Bjerrum theory, supplemented by Brownian-dynamics simulations, to analyze the free energy and stability criteria.

Model / system. The system is a monovalent 1:1 electrolyte confined in an angstrom-scale slit with planar walls carrying a uniform surface charge. The theory treats surface charge as a thermodynamic doping field influencing the Coulomb correlations.

Key observables. Screening strength ($\kappa_{ ext{eff}}^2$), ionic fractions ($I_{\pm}, I$), critical temperature ($T_c^*$), and in-plane diffusion coefficient ($D_{xy}$).

Important parameters / regimes. Surface charge density ($\sigma$), slit width ($h$), intrinsic salt density ($ ho_0$), and the dimensionless ratio $\chi = ho_0 / \sigma$.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis primarily focuses on the weak surface-coupling regime ($h/\lambda_{ ext{GC}} \ll 1$) and assumes the counterions are part of the quasi-2D Coulomb fluid.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the difference between neutral and charged slits regarding ionicity, show the suppression of $T_c^*$ vs. $\sigma$, and demonstrate the collapse of this suppression onto a scaled perturbation variable $X_{\xi}$.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the physical problem (surface doping effects) to developing the generalized theory, analyzing the resulting decoupling of ion species, and finally validating the theoretical predictions using both scaling laws and explicit Brownian dynamics simulations.

Abstract

Dielectric confinement strengthens Coulomb correlations in quasi-two-dimensional electrolytes and can promote Bjerrum pairing in charge-neutral slits. Here we use a generalized Debye-Huckel-Bjerrum theory to show that weak surface charge changes this picture by stoichiometrically doping the slit with mobile counterions. These counterions maintain a finite screening floor, decouple microscopic pairing from macroscopic ionicity, and shift association-driven criticality to lower temperatures. The critical-temperature suppression collapses onto a single scaled perturbation variable, revealing how surface charge and dielectric confinement jointly control charged nanofluidic slits. Brownian-dynamics tests further show that the same counterions are not always fully bulk-like diffusive: at low intrinsic salt density, explicit wall charge slows in-plane diffusion, whereas at higher intrinsic density the wall-induced diffusion penalty decreases and the mobile-counterion description becomes dynamically accurate. These results identify surface charge as a thermodynamic doping field that tunes both correlated ionic stability and the diffusion mechanism in nanofluidic confinement.

Coherent State Path Integral Monte Carlo

Authors: Riccardo Fantoni
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.05013v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces the Coherent State Path Integral Monte Carlo (CSPIMC) method, a new technique for simulating quantum many-body liquids at finite temperature. It replaces the standard plane-wave basis with coherent states of a harmonic oscillator, offering a tunable parameter that enhances computational efficiency. The method successfully reproduces known thermodynamic limits for kinetic energy estimators.

Why it may be interesting. The ability to tune the harmonic oscillator stiffness ($\xi$) to improve computational efficiency by allowing larger imaginary time steps is a significant algorithmic advancement for simulating quantum dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a novel and computationally efficient quantum simulation method for studying many-body quantum liquids at finite temperature.

Main result. The proposed Coherent State Path Integral Monte Carlo (CSPIMC) method successfully reconstructs the thermal density matrix, showing convergence to known results while offering a tunable parameter ($\xi$) that allows for larger imaginary time steps.

Method. The method expands the high-temperature density matrix using the overcomplete set of single-particle coherent states of a Harmonic Oscillator, contrasting with conventional plane-wave path integral methods.

Model / system. The system is a many-body quantum liquid of identical particles (bosons/fermions) governed by a thermal density matrix $\hat{ ho} = \exp(-eta \hat{H})$. The method is tested on free particles and extended to systems like liquid Helium.

Key observables. Kinetic energy ($\langle\hat{T} angle$), potential energy ($\langle\hat{V} angle$), and the factor $\phi$ used in the kinetic energy estimator.

Important parameters / regimes. Inverse temperature ($eta$), imaginary time step ($ au$), and the stiffness parameter ($\xi$) of the harmonic oscillator, which can be tuned.

Assumptions / limitations. The method does not solve the Feynman sign problem for fermions, and the kinetic action approximation relies on the properties of coherent states.

Figures summary. Figures compare path snapshots between CSPIMC and standard PIMC for free particles, and tables detail the dependence of the factor $\phi$ on simulation parameters.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the CSPIMC formalism by replacing plane waves with coherent states, derives the necessary path integral elements, analyzes the limiting cases (stiff HO, thermodynamic limit), and compares results against standard PIMC methods.

Abstract

We propose a new quantum simulation method for a many body quantum liquid of identical particles at finite (non-zero) temperature. The new scheme expands the high temperature density matrix on the overcomplete set of single particles coherent states of John Rider Klauder instead of the usual plane waves as in conventional path integral methods. One is free to tune the elastic constant and/or the mass of the harmonic oscillator subtending the coherent states so as to maximize the computational efficiency of the algorithm. We prove that in the limit of an extremely stiff harmonic oscillator the results for the internal energy tends towards the correct expected values. Moreover we suggest that a stiff harmonic oscillator could allow the use of larger (imaginary) timesteps. This additional degree of freedom is the characteristic feature of our new algorithm and is not available in more conventional path integral methods.

High-density, high-mobility ultrathin spin-polarized two-dimensional electron gas at the polar/polar LaVO$_3$/KTaO$_3$ interface: Insights from first-principles calculations

Authors: Arpan Das
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04252v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 1/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical study uses DFT+U calculations to investigate the LaVO3/KTaO3 interface, finding that the metallic 2DEG arises from charge transfer driven by polar discontinuity. The resulting 2DEG is highly confined, spin-polarized, and possesses an exceptionally small effective mass, suggesting potential for high-mobility quantum electronic applications.

Why it may be interesting. The study models charge transfer and electronic reconstruction in a strongly correlated oxide system, which is a key area for understanding emergent quantum phenomena in solid-state devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To uncover the microscopic origin and characterize the properties of the two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) formed at the polar/polar LaVO3/KTaO3 interface.

Main result. The 2DEG is highly confined, spin-polarized, and originates from an electronic reconstruction driven by charge transfer across the interface, exhibiting an exceptionally small effective mass.

Method. First-principles density functional theory (DFT) calculations, incorporating DFT+U methods to treat strong electronic correlations in the Mott insulator component.

Model / system. The physical system is the LaVO3/KTaO3 heterostructure, modeled as a slab. The study investigates the electronic reconstruction mechanism that stabilizes the metallic state at the interface.

Key observables. Spin-up parabolic band effective mass ($m^*$), interfacial electron density, layer-resolved charge density, and polarization vectors.

Important parameters / regimes. Hubbard U and J parameters for V 3d and Ta 5d orbitals; comparison to the LaAlO3/SrTiO3 interface.

Assumptions / limitations. The metallic state arises from an electronic reconstruction to avoid the polar catastrophe; the A-AFM magnetic ordering is assumed to be the ground state.

Figures summary. Figures show layer-resolved PDOS confirming Ta 5d orbital contribution, band structures illustrating the parabolic 2DEG band, and charge density maps confirming 2D confinement at the interface.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the physical context (polar/polar interface) and computational methods (DFT+U). It then analyzes the magnetic and polarization states, leading to the key finding of electronic reconstruction, which finally characterizes the resulting high-mobility, spin-polarized 2DEG.

Abstract

The emergence of high-mobility two-dimensional electron gases (2DEGs) at oxide interfaces provides a fertile platform for exploring emergent quantum phenomena and next-generation oxide electronics. Here, using first-principles density functional theory (DFT) calculations, we investigate the microscopic origin of the 2DEG formed at the interface between the band insulator KTaO$_3$ (KTO) and the Mott insulator LaVO$_3$ (LVO). Although both constituents are insulating in bulk, the LVO/KTO heterostructure develops robust metallicity at the interface, consistent with experimental observations. Our calculations show that this metallic state originates from an electronic reconstruction driven by the polar discontinuity across the interface. To avoid the polar catastrophe on both the polar LVO film and the polar KTO substrate, electrons are transferred from the outer surfaces toward the interface, leading to hole accumulation in the surface VO$_2$ layer and electron accumulation in the interfacial TaO$_2$ layer. This charge redistribution stabilizes a highly confined and spin-polarized 2DEG localized at the interface. The electronic states forming the 2DEG are predominantly derived from interfacial Ta $5d_{xy}$ orbitals, confining carrier motion to the interfacial plane. Remarkably, the spin-up parabolic band hosting the 2DEG exhibits an exceptionally small effective mass, substantially lower than that of the prototypical LaAlO$_3$/SrTiO$_3$ interface, indicating the potential for enhanced carrier mobility. Furthermore, the calculated interfacial electron density is nearly an order of magnitude larger than that of LaAlO$_3$/SrTiO$_3$, consistent with experiment. These findings identify the LVO/KTO heterostructure as a promising platform for realizing high-density, high-mobility spin-polarized 2DEGs and for engineering correlated oxide interfaces for quantum electronic applications.

Convergence rates of Sum-of-Hermitian-Squares Hierarchies for the Pauli algebra

Authors: Ali Almasi, Dávid Bugár, Cambyse Rouzé, Peter Brown
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.04940v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper rigorously analyzes the convergence rate of Sum-of-Hermitian-Squares (SOHS) relaxations used to find the ground state energy of $n$-qubit systems. By extending techniques from commutative optimization to the non-commutative Pauli algebra, the authors derive an explicit error bound dependent on Krawtchouk polynomials. This provides a major quantitative advance in the mathematical analysis of quantum many-body problems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides rigorous mathematical tools to analyze the convergence of quantum many-body approximations, which is vital for developing reliable quantum simulation algorithms and understanding the limits of current optimization techniques for quantum chemistry and materials science.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper develops explicit convergence rates for Sum-of-Hermitian-Squares (SOHS) relaxations applied to noncommutative polynomial optimization problems (NPO). This is crucial for accurately estimating ground state energies in quantum systems.

Main result. The authors provide a quantitative upper bound on the error, showing the convergence rate is governed by the smallest roots of Krawtchouk polynomials, representing the first such analysis for this class of problems.

Method. The technique extends the polynomial kernel method from commutative SOS hierarchies to the non-commutative setting of the Pauli algebra, utilizing specialized linear maps $\Phi_\eta$ and properties derived from Krawtchouk polynomials.

Model / system. The focus is on the ground state energy problem for $n$-qubit systems, formulated as an NPO problem over the Pauli algebra. The analysis uses the SOHS hierarchy, which generates a sequence of lower bounds ($H_r$) to the true minimum energy ($H_{\min}$).

Key observables. The primary observable is the error bound, $H_{\min} - H_r$, which is quantified in terms of the smallest root $\zeta_{n,r+1}$ of the Krawtchouk polynomial $K_{n,r+1}^{(4)}(x)$.

Important parameters / regimes. The parameters include the number of qubits ($n$), the level of the hierarchy ($r$), the maximum Pauli weight ($d$), and the Krawtchouk polynomial roots $\zeta_{n,r+1}$.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation relies on constructing a linear map $\Phi_\eta$ satisfying specific algebraic properties (P0)-(P3) within the Pauli algebra structure.

Paper structure. The paper builds by first establishing the necessary algebraic framework (Pauli algebra, diagonal maps $A_k, E_k$) using Krawtchouk polynomials, then deriving the required linear map $\Phi_\eta$ that satisfies the necessary properties, and finally applying this structure to derive the explicit convergence rate bound for the SOHS hierarchy.

Abstract

Moment/Sum-of-Hermitian-Squares relaxations for noncommutative polynomial optimization problems have become an important tool for analyzing problems within quantum theory. Despite their widespread success, little is known about their rate of convergence and, consequently, their accuracy. In this work, we develop explicit convergence rates for relaxations of noncommutative polynomial optimization problems generated from the Pauli algebra -- covering applications to the ground state energy problem for n-qubit systems. In particular, we show that the rate of convergence can be bounded in terms of the smallest roots of a family of orthogonal polynomials known as Krawtchouk polynomials. Our result represents the first quantitative analysis of the rate of convergence for relaxations of noncommutative polynomial optimization problems.

Summary. This paper explores using digital Quantum Reservoir Computing (QRC) to forecast ATM cash demand time series on near-term quantum hardware. The methodology encodes time data into quantum rotations and uses measurements to feed a classical regression model. Although QRC underperforms classical benchmarks on point-wise errors, it shows promise in capturing temporal structure via the DTW metric.

Why it may be interesting. The work provides an empirical assessment of applying quantum machine learning techniques, specifically QRC, to complex, real-world time series data, which is relevant for understanding how quantum circuits can encode and process temporal correlations.

Main problem. The paper investigates using a digital quantum reservoir computing (QRC) framework to forecast multi-step time series data, specifically ATM cash demand.

Main result. While QRC models did not outperform the classical Prophet benchmark on standard error metrics (MAE, NMSE), they showed more competitive results using the Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) metric, suggesting partial ability to capture temporal structure.

Abstract

We investigate a digital quantum reservoir computing (QRC) framework for multi-step forecasting of automated teller machine (ATM) cash demand time series on near-term quantum devices. The proposed approach uses parametrized four-qubit reservoirs with a fixed structure exploiting partial measurement and reset, where temporal data is encoded in rotation angles. Training is restricted to a classical Ridge-regression readout. We systematically analyze the impact of the circuit ansatzë, reservoir memory, measurement-derived observables, and the execution backend on the forecasting performance. Experiments are performed with noiseless simulation, noise-aware emulation, and a real IQM Spark quantum processor. Although the QRC models do not outperform the classical Prophet benchmark in terms of Mean Absolute Error and Normalized Mean Squared Error metrics, they achieve more competitive results in Dynamic Time Warping metric, indicating a partial ability to capture temporal structure. These findings provide an empirical assessment of digital QRC for realistic financial forecasting and highlight both its current limitations and its potential on near-term quantum hardware.

Distributed Variational Quantum Optimisation by Entanglement-Selective Transport

Authors: Edric Matwiejew, Pascal Elahi, Ugo Varetto

Summary. This paper introduces QESTO, a novel variational ansatz designed for distributed quantum optimization on multiple QPUs. It leverages pre-shared Bell pairs to encode and transport constraint information locally, avoiding costly non-local gates. Benchmarking shows QESTO significantly outperforms standard QAOA methods by efficiently concentrating probability amplitude on low-cost solutions.

Why it may be interesting. The focus on utilizing persistent entanglement to simulate long-range interactions while minimizing gate overhead is highly relevant for near-term quantum hardware architectures that struggle with high-fidelity, deep non-local gate sequences.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of performing distributed quantum optimization for graph-based discrete problems when computing the cost function requires non-local gates across multiple quantum processors.

Main result. The proposed QESTO ansatz achieves stronger convergence to low-cost solutions than equivalent partitioned QAOA and outperforms monolithic QAOA on benchmark tile-matching problems, suggesting persistent entanglement is key to efficient distributed computation.

Method. QESTO introduces a variational ansatz that uses persistent, pre-shared Bell pairs to encode local constraint information and facilitate amplitude transfer across distributed edges, minimizing the need for non-local gates during the optimization layers.

Model / system. The model involves quantum computation distributed over multiple Quantum Processing Units (QPUs) solving graph-based discrete optimization problems, such as weighted Wang tile-matching. The core mechanism relies on local operations and shared entanglement (Bell pairs) to simulate long-range interactions.

Key observables. Mean normalized optimality gap (g), mean feasible probability of finding low-cost tilings, and the concentration of probability mass near the optimum solution.

Important parameters / regimes. Ansatz depth (p), the number of distributed edges ($|E_{dist}|$), and variational parameters $\{\gamma_r, eta_r, ext{tr}, \eta_r\}$.

Assumptions / limitations. The approach assumes the ability to utilize persistent Bell pairs, which reduces the overhead associated with repeatedly executing non-local gates compared to gate-teleported methods.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the QESTO circuit structure for two QPUs sharing edges, and comparative plots show the mean normalized optimality gap and feasible probability versus ansatz depth for QESTO versus reference QAOA methods.

Paper structure. The paper first introduces the problem and the QESTO ansatz, detailing its local and entanglement-based layers. It then benchmarks QESTO against subgraph-QAOA and monolithic QAOA on specific tile-matching ensembles, presenting quantitative results on convergence and probability distribution.

Abstract

Distributed quantum optimisation is challenging because computing the problem cost function across multiple quantum processors requires non-local gates, which can incur overhead in latency and fidelity. Here we introduce QESTO, a distributed variational ansatz for graph-based discrete optimisation that requires only persistent pre-shared Bell pairs for remote operations. Using local operations, it encodes local constraint information in the Bell pairs that is leveraged to produce amplitude transfer towards globally valid distributed solution states. QESTO requires one Bell pair per distributed edge of the problem graph and, after initialisation of the Bell states, uses no non-local gates. On two bounded weighted Wang tile-matching problem ensembles, QESTO achieves stronger convergence to low-cost tilings than equivalently partitioned QAOA with no distributed gates at ansatz depths of two or higher, and exceeds the mean performance of monolithic QAOA at the deepest studied depth in both ensembles. These results suggest that persistent entanglement can support useful variational communication while reducing per-layer non-local gate overhead.

Effect of cations on van der Waals interactions between particles in aqueous alkali nitrate electrolytes

Authors: Micah P. Prange, Jaehun Chun, Gregory K. Schenter, Elias Nakouzi, Yihui Wei, Aurora E. Clark, Kevin M. Rosso, Carolyn I. Pearce

Summary. This theoretical study calculates how van der Waals interactions between nanoparticles are modified by dissolved alkali nitrate electrolytes using an extended Lifshitz theory. By employing advanced electronic structure calculations, the authors find that increasing salt concentration can actually increase the Hamaker constant for certain ions, contradicting older assumptions and highlighting the persistent role of these forces in colloidal stability.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on classical colloidal forces, the rigorous application of advanced quantum mechanical methods (TDDFT) to model the dielectric response of complex ionic solutions provides a sophisticated theoretical framework for understanding many-body interactions in condensed matter systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to resolve the incomplete understanding of how electrolytes affect van der Waals interactions between particles, especially at high concentrations.

Main result. Contrary to prior assumptions, increasing concentrations of $ ext{NaNO}_3$, $ ext{KNO}_3$, and $ ext{RbNO}_3$ cause appreciable increases in the Hamaker constants, suggesting van der Waals interactions remain significant for colloidal stability.

Method. The authors extended Lifshitz theory by developing a dielectric response model for alkali nitrate solutions, calculated using electronic structure methods like LR-TDDFT.

Model / system. The system involves nanoparticles (rutile, boehmite, alumina) suspended in aqueous alkali nitrate electrolytes ($ ext{MNO}_3$). The theory models the interaction potential via the Hamaker constant, which depends on the dielectric response of the solvent and ions.

Key observables. Hamaker constants ($A$), dielectric functions ($\epsilon(\omega)$ and $\epsilon(i\xi)$), and the concentration dependence of these constants.

Important parameters / regimes. Intermediate and high electrolyte concentrations, the cation identity ($ ext{Na}^+, ext{K}^+, ext{Rb}^+, ext{Cs}^+$), and the temperature ($T=300 ext{K}$).

Assumptions / limitations. The model neglects Debye-type relaxation ($\hbar_7 = 0$) and assumes the dielectric response can be modeled via linear combinations of molecular/ionic oscillator strengths.

Figures summary. Figures compare dielectric functions using TDDFT vs. pole models, plot the Hamaker constant vs. cation species and concentration, and show photoabsorption cross-sections for various species.

Paper structure. The paper builds from a literature review of interparticle forces and solution chemistry, develops a theoretical framework based on extending Lifshitz theory using TDDFT to calculate the dielectric response, and presents calculations showing the concentration dependence of the Hamaker constant.

Abstract

The van der Waals interaction has been extensively studied for colloidal forces and resultant emergent phenomena such as colloidal stability, aggregation, and suspension rheology, but the effect of electrolytes on this interaction, especially at intermediate and high electrolyte concentrations, remains incompletely understood. We have extended the Lifshitz theory for van der Waals interactions in pure water to alkali nitrate solutions at arbitrary concentrations by developing a dielectric response model for alkali nitrate solutions that is based on electronic structure calculations of the molecular constituents. Due to their importance in catalysis, ceramics, and coating technologies, the Hamaker constants for rutile, boehmite, and alumina nanoparticles suspended in alkali nitrate solutions are calculated as a function of salt concentration. Contrary to prevailing assumptions, increasing the concentration of sodium (Na), potassium (K), and rubidium (Rb) nitrate solutions causes appreciable increases of the Hamaker constants relative to pure water instead of decreases, whereas cesium nitrate (CsNO3) has almost no effect on the Hamaker constant. We discussed the influence of the solution molar volume, the polarizability of the dissolved ions, and optical properties of the interacting particles in the context of previously published work. Our study indicates a non-vanishing role of van der Waals interactions on colloidal stability at intermediate and high electrolyte concentrations, leading to physical insights on emergent phenomena associated with nanoparticles.

Abstract

The variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) is a promising algorithm for near-term quantum chemistry applications, but selecting optimal ansatz circuits remains challenging. Expressibility, a metric quantifying a circuit's ability to explore the Hilbert space, has been proposed as a guide for ansatz selection, but recent work showed it inconsistently predicts VQE performance under realistic noise for $H_2$. We extend this investigation to cover both $H_2$ and $H_3^+$ under four execution scenarios: ideal, noisy, and noisy with zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) or probabilistic error cancellation (PEC). We find that error mitigation does not reliably restore expressibility's predictive power. ZNE reduces error for only 4 of 12 $H_2$ circuits and 4 of 6 $H_3^+$ circuits, while PEC actually increases error in 11 of 12 $H_2$ circuits and all 6 $H_3^+$ circuits. We reproduce and extend Saib et al.'s key finding that circuit rankings scramble under noise (Spearman $ρ\approx -0.1$ between ideal and noisy rankings), and identify a new result: ZNE largely preserves noisy rankings ($ρ= +0.80$ for $H_2$) while PEC actively reorders them ($ρ= -0.22$). Noisy expressibility, computed from density matrix simulations, strongly predicts unmitigated performance for $H_3^+$ (Pearson $r = +0.91$, $p = 0.01$), but this metric is computationally intractable at scale. We demonstrate that zero-cost circuit topology metrics such as two-qubit gate count provide comparable or superior predictive power for PEC degradation ($r = +0.96$ for $H_3^+$), while standard expressibility best predicts noisy and ZNE performance for $H_2$ ($r = +0.74$ and $r = +0.77$).

Abstract

Automorphisms of quantum codes, when they exist, offer a pathway toward fault-tolerant gate implementation via qubit relabeling. Although useful, the conditions under which automorphisms appear in a given code remain poorly understood. In this paper, we develop an algebraic framework for systematically analyzing and engineering automorphisms in Generalized Bicycle (GB) codes. Central to our approach is the derivation of a three-space dependency between the polynomial ring space, the parity check matrix space, and the $\mathbb{F}_2^{2\ell}$ qubit space, similar to the structure found in the study of classical cyclic codes. By expressing GB codes as a pair of cyclic submodules of $R_\ell^2$, where $R_\ell \cong \mathbb{F}_2[x]/\langle x^\ell-1\rangle$, we reduce the search for code automorphisms to a deterministic algebraic problem, deriving necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of block-separable automorphisms built from cyclic shifts, ring automorphisms and block-swaps. We connect these conditions to the fold-transversal gate framework, providing explicit criteria for the existence of $H$-, $S$-, and $CX$-type fold-transversal gates. We further discuss structured bases for logical operators in order to determine the logical action of a given automorphism. Finally, we introduce the Maximal Cube Root (MCR) code family, a family of GB codes constructed around the principle of maximizing automorphism flexibility and fold-CX gates. We demonstrate a collection of $k=2$ MCR codes up to $d=13$ generating the 2-qubit Clifford group via automorphism and fold-transversal gates, with stabilizer weight ranging from 8 to 16, and $k>2$ MCR codes with a minimum of 20 distinct logical gates achievable from automorphisms. This serves as a first demonstration of inverse design: using these methods to build codes around a rich automorphism structure from the ground up.
