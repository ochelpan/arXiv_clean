Feature Encoding in Quantum Machine Learning: A Survey and Practical Guidelines

Authors: Vincenzo Sammartino
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.05387v1

Summary. This survey systematically reviews quantum feature encoding techniques for QML on noisy hardware. It develops a comprehensive taxonomy classifying encodings based on cost, expressivity, and robustness. The central finding is that practical performance favors shallow angle encodings over exponentially powerful amplitude encodings when device error rates are non-negligible.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides crucial, hardware-aware guidelines for applying QML theory to real-world NISQ devices by quantifying the trade-offs between theoretical power and physical noise limitations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The encoding of classical data into quantum states is identified as the primary performance bottleneck limiting Quantum Machine Learning (QML) on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices.

Main result. For current noise levels ($p \gtrsim 10^{-3}$), shallow angle-based encodings consistently outperform theoretically superior but noise-sensitive amplitude encoding in practice.

Method. The authors conducted a systematic literature review and developed a multi-faceted decision framework that jointly characterizes resource cost, expressivity (via Fourier analysis), and noise robustness for various encoding families.

Model / system. The study analyzes the mapping of classical feature vectors $x \in \mathbb{R}^D$ to quantum states $|\psi(x) angle$ using parameterized quantum circuits (PQCs) on NISQ hardware. The comparison spans multiple encoding types: basis, angle, amplitude, and IQP.

Key observables. State fidelity ($\mathcal{F}$), resource cost (qubit count $n$, gate count $g_2$, circuit depth $d$), Fourier expressivity (frequency spectrum $\Omega$), and the critical error rate ($p^*$).

Important parameters / regimes. Feature dimension ($D$), qubit budget ($n$), physical error rate ($p$), and task type ($ au$). Critical thresholds include $p^* \approx 10^{-3}$ for amplitude encoding viability.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on modeling decoherence using CPTP maps under NISQ conditions, and it assumes that the performance trade-offs can be captured by analyzing these measurable resource metrics.

Figures summary. A three-axis taxonomy visualizes the trade-off between Expressivity, Noise Robustness, and Qubit Efficiency. A five-regime decision framework guides practitioners based on input parameters $(D, n, p, au)$.

Paper structure. The paper systematically reviews existing literature to establish a comprehensive taxonomy of encoding families, derives theoretical bounds relating depth/fidelity to error rates, unifies the analysis across trainability metrics (Fourier spectrum, barren plateaus), and culminates in a practical five-regime decision framework.

Abstract

The encoding of classical data into quantum states constitutes the primary performance bottleneck in Quantum Machine Learning (qml) on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (nisq) devices. No existing framework jointly characterises resource cost, expressivity, and noise robustness, nor provides actionable selection guidelines for practitioners. This survey addresses that gap through a systematic review of 66 primary works (2017-2026) assembled via a PRISMA-adapted protocol across five academic databases. Four principal contributions are made. First, a three-axis cost-expressivity-robustness taxonomy classifies all major encoding families - basis, angle, dense-angle, amplitude, data re-uploading, and IQP - along independently measurable axes. Second, closed-form depth-fidelity bounds under nisq decoherence channels identify the critical gate-error rate p* ~ 10^-3 below which amplitude encoding is viable. Third, a unified treatment of Fourier expressivity, barren-plateau onset, and quantum kernel concentration as functions of the encoding circuit provides the first joint trainability analysis. Fourth, a five-regime decision framework maps (D, n, p, tau) - feature dimension, qubit budget, error rate, and task type - to a hardware-grounded encoding recommendation. The central finding is that for p >= 10^-3, shallow angle-based encodings consistently outperform amplitude encoding in practice, despite the latter's exponential qubit advantage.

Multi-Qubit Dyadic Phase Fixing for Fault-Tolerant Quantum Compilation

Summary. The paper introduces Dyadic Phase Fixing (DPF), a general synthesis tool that optimizes the compilation of quantum circuits into the T-gate restricted set required for fault tolerance. By extending phase kickback, DPF significantly reduces the necessary number of costly T gates compared to existing methods. Crucially, it also warns that while T-count is important, physical resource metrics like space-time volume must be considered alongside gate count minimization.

Why it may be interesting. This work directly addresses resource optimization in quantum computation, which is critical for realizing large-scale quantum processors. The detailed analysis of cost trade-offs (T-count vs. volume) provides crucial guidelines for compiler design relevant to any physical platform.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge is efficiently compiling arbitrary quantum circuits into the fault-tolerant Clifford+T gate set while minimizing the resource cost, dominated by T gates.

Main result. The proposed Dyadic Phase Fixing (DPF) synthesis achieves significant reductions (up to 70%) in T-gate count compared to established methods like gridsynth on diverse benchmarks.

Method. DPF extends phase kickback by using numerical unitary synthesis to greedily extract dyadic angle rotations from general circuits, optimizing the trade-off between T-count reduction and ancilla overhead.

Model / system. The work models fault-tolerant quantum computation implemented on architectures like the surface code. The compilation process translates high-level unitaries into a sequence of Clifford and $Rz$ gates, which are then mapped to physical operations.

Key observables. T-gate count (primary metric), space-time volume, approximation error ($\epsilon$), and ancilla qubit overhead.

Important parameters / regimes. The precision threshold $\epsilon$, the size of the phase gradient register ($k_{reg}$), and the choice between different Pauli basis computation schemes (LPBC vs. HPBC).

Assumptions / limitations. T-count is a useful but incomplete proxy for total fault-tolerant cost, as space-time volume can diverge from T-count savings due to CNOT overhead.

Figures summary. Figures compare T-gate counts across benchmarks (QFT, QAE, etc.) showing DPF's advantage over baseline methods; other figures detail the decision matrix comparing synthesis techniques and plot resource costs vs. register size.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of fault-tolerant compilation, details the DPF method using unitary synthesis and a decision matrix to select optimal phase kickback parameters, presents quantitative results showing T-count reduction, and concludes by analyzing the limitations regarding space-time volume overhead.

Abstract

Fault-tolerant quantum computing requires translating application-level quantum circuits into the Clifford+$T$ gate set, where the $T$ gate is the dominant resource cost. Phase kickback is an ancilla-based technique that can dramatically reduce $T$-count for rotations with dyadic angles, but has previously been limited to highly structured circuit families. We present Dyadic Phase Fixing (DPF), a general multi-qubit synthesis tool that extends phase kickback to general quantum circuits. DPF uses numerical unitary synthesis to greedily extract dyadic angle rotations from any input circuit. Combined with a decision matrix to automatically size the final phase gradient register, our end-to-end workflow achieves up to 70% reduction in $T$-count compared to \texttt{gridsynth} and up to 60% compared to Repeat-Until-Success synthesis on a diverse set of benchmarks. We map these compiled circuits to a surface-code architecture to evaluate space-time volume, demonstrating up to a 60\% reduction in this metric as well. However, for some circuits and mapping strategies the two metrics diverge significantly, demonstrating that $T$-count alone is a useful but incomplete proxy for fault-tolerant program costs.

Abstract

Semiconductor nanostructures are central to many developing technologies. Notably, double quantum dots are especially important for semiconductor spin-qubit architectures, quantum sensing applications, and quantum-dot solar cells. Accurate modelling is highly desirable but conventional methods can struggle when dynamics involve more than two interacting electrons. In this work, we present a quantum simulation framework capable of addressing multi-electron double quantum dots. We adopt an efficiently scaling 1$^\text{st}$ quantised representation of the system and develop algorithms based on both Trotterisation and qubitisation. Incorporating insights from classical simulations enables us to produce resource estimates that are more realistic than those obtained from theoretical error bounds. Using a standard surface code model with physical noise at $10^{-3}$, our results indicate that the ground-state energy of four electrons in a double quantum dot can be estimated in approximately 24 hours using 226k physical qubits, or an eight-electron system in 3.4 days with 314k qubits (with runtimes falling dramatically when more qubits are available). We anticipate that incorporating very recent advances including dense surface code architectures (Low et al. arXiv:2605.30455) may reduce these costs significantly further. We conclude that early fault tolerant computers may prove to be valuable tools for designing mature-era quantum technologies.

Periodic Symmetry-Adapted Encoding: Qubit Reduction in Crystalline Electronic Structure

Authors: Dario Picozzi
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.05777v1

Summary. The paper introduces Periodic Symmetry-Adapted Encoding (SAE) to drastically reduce the qubit count needed for simulating crystalline electronic structures on quantum computers. By incorporating crystal translation symmetries into the symmetry constraints, it achieves substantial resource savings—up to 309x in CNOT gates—while maintaining high energy accuracy compared to standard encodings.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a concrete, scalable method to reduce the exponential scaling of quantum resources required for solid-state physics simulations by systematically exploiting crystal symmetries, which is crucial for applying quantum computing to materials science.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary goal is to enable qubit-efficient quantum simulation of crystalline materials by adapting symmetry reduction techniques from molecular systems to periodic electronic structures.

Main result. The proposed Periodic Symmetry-Adapted Encoding (SAE) successfully reduces the required number of qubits and computational resources (CNOT counts, VQE evaluations) significantly compared to standard Jordan-Wigner encodings across ten benchmark crystals.

Method. The method extends Symmetry-Adapted Encoding by incorporating crystal translation symmetries alongside point-group and spin-parity generators. These symmetries are used to constrain the Hamiltonian via Boolean linear equations, projecting the system onto a smaller, symmetry-adapted qubit subspace.

Model / system. The model involves simulating the electronic structure of various crystalline materials (e.g., diamond, CsCl) using an active space derived from a supercell Hamiltonian constructed by folding $k$-point calculations to the $\Gamma$-point. The simulation is mapped onto qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation.

Key observables. Qubit count reduction (e.g., 14 to 6 for CsCl), CNOT gate counts, variational parameter counts, and preservation of target energies below chemical accuracy.

Important parameters / regimes. The number of independent Boolean generators ($n_g$), the size of the active space (CAS(6,7)), and the specific crystal structure symmetries ($\mathbb{Z}_2^8$ for CsCl).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes that the physical sector remains the global minimum when projected onto the symmetry-adapted subspace, and that all relevant symmetry generators can be identified and commute.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate resource savings: Qubit count drop (e.g., CsCl 14 $ o$ 6), reduction in VQE objective evaluations ($\approx 3 imes$ fewer), and significant CNOT gate compression, demonstrating the efficiency of periodic SAE.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem by extending molecular SAE to periodic systems. It details the Hamiltonian construction via supercells and symmetry identification (spin-parity, point-group, translation). Results are presented through benchmarking on ten crystals, comparing resource costs against JW encoding, and validating energy preservation using UCCSD-VQE benchmarks.

Abstract

We extend the symmetry-adapted encoding (SAE) framework to periodic electronic structure, enabling qubit-efficient quantum simulation of crystalline materials. By constructing a $Γ$-point supercell Hamiltonian from a folded $k$-point calculation and systematically identifying all applicable space-group symmetry generators -- including spin-parity, point-group, and crystal translation symmetries -- we obtain qubit Hamiltonians with fewer qubits than the Jordan--Wigner starting point. We benchmark diamond, silicon, 3C-SiC, MgO, NaCl, CsCl, h-BN, wurtzite AlN, $α$-quartz SiO$_2$, and MgF$_2$ using active spaces chosen to preserve complete near-degenerate frontier manifolds across cubic, hexagonal, trigonal, and tetragonal space groups. Across the suite the periodic SAE removes 4--8 qubits. The B2 CsCl benchmark realises eight independent Boolean generators, i.e. a symmetry group isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_2^8$, reducing CAS(6,7) from 14 to 6 qubits. This exceeds the $\mathbb{Z}_2^5$ maximum of molecular SAE, where only two spin parities and at most three independent Boolean point-group generators are available, because the folded crystal supplies three additional half-translation symmetries. Noiseless UCCSD-VQE benchmarks against exact diagonalisation in the active-space sector show that the reduced encodings preserve the target energies to well below chemical accuracy while reducing variational parameter counts by $3$--$8\times$ and CNOT counts by up to $309\times$. The largest circuit savings occur when translation and point-group generators act independently in the active space, demonstrating that periodic symmetry can be converted directly into both qubit and ansatz compression. The method is implemented in the open-source QuantumSymmetry package and requires no manual specification of symmetry generators.

Abstract

Triangles capture higher-order structures in graphs and are fundamental to applications such as clustering and network analysis. To enable efficient use of such structures at scale, we study the problem of \emph{triangle cut sparsification}, which aims to reduce the graph size while approximately preserving triangle counts across every cut. We investigate \emph{quantum algorithms} for this problem, using triangle listing as our main technical ingredient. In particular, we present a quantum algorithm for triangle listing that, for a graph with $n$ vertices, $m$ edges, and $t$ triangles, runs in time $T_{\mathrm{q\text{-}list}} =$ $\widetilde{O}\bigl(\min(n^{5/4}t^{7/12} + n^{7/6}t^{7/9}, m + m^{3/4}t^{1/2},$ $n^{3/2}t^{1/2})\bigr)$, improving upon the best known classical bounds over a broad range of parameters. Our algorithm is based on a heavy-light vertex partition and an extension of triangle detection via quantum walks and Grover search. Leveraging this result, we design a quantum algorithm for constructing $\varepsilon$-triangle cut sparsifiers of size $\widetilde{O}(n/\varepsilon^2)$ in time $\widetilde{O}(T_{\mathrm{q\text{-}list}} + \sqrt{mn}/\varepsilon)$. Finally, we demonstrate applications to clustering algorithms based on triangle-related measures and prove a lower bound of $Ω(n/\varepsilon^2)$ on the size of any $\varepsilon$-triangle cut sparsifiers.

Abstract

We present a multilevel embedding framework for quantum chemistry calculations on a quantum computer. In our framework, a quantum algorithm treats the strongly correlated active space, while a high-level wave-function method such as coupled cluster theory or multireference perturbation theory recovers the remaining correlation in the surrounding region. A sampling-based quantum algorithm, quantum-selected configuration interaction, bridges the quantum and classical treatments. The entire calculation is embedded in a low-cost density functional theory description of the surrounding environment using Manby's projection technique. We apply the framework to organic, metal-organic, and metallic systems, computing bond dissociation energies, adsorption energies, and reaction barriers using only the subset of qubits of a 144-qubit superconducting quantum computer at the University of Osaka and achieving $\sim$1 kcal/mol agreement with classical references for a Menshutkin $\mathrm{S_N2}$ reaction inside a carbon nanotube. Our results may open the way to quantitatively reliable quantum-classical hybrid calculations for large-scale chemical systems.

Abstract

Quantum algorithms for basic numerical linear algebraic tasks have proven essential for translating diverse problems to a unified quantum computational context. Many of these tasks -- e.g., applying a polynomial function to the spectrum of a matrix embedded in a unitary process (a so-called block encoding), or taking linear combinations of block encodings -- are well-addressed by techniques like quantum singular value transformation (QSVT) or linear combination of unitaries (LCU). We show the space required to compute quantum element-wise transforms can be reduced exponentially in the degree of the applied function compared to prior work, and raise and rectify errors in previous constructions. We present our algorithms alongside applications to machine learning, simulation, and signal processing.

Abstract

Financial crashes, cascading failures in infrastructure, and critical errors in AI systems are frequently triggered by events that occur with extremely small probability. Efficiently discovering and sampling events with probability below a threshold is therefore of critical interest. Yet this task is highly non-trivial using existing classical or quantum methods. Being rare, such events require an immense sampling overhead to collect sufficient data samples. Moreover, because the rare events are not known in advance, they cannot be flagged for amplification using standard techniques. Here, we introduce a quantum algorithm for rare-event discovery and sampling without first learning which events are rare. The algorithm achieves the optimal quantum scaling with the rarity threshold. We further demonstrate that this can achieve a quadratic speedup for heavy-tailed systems whose tail has nonvanishing total mass, and translates into a robust polynomial speedup for stationary stochastic processes, with the exponent determined by its entropy-rate structure.

Summary. This work theoretically predicts a novel 'ghost' interference pattern in superconductors caused by an impurity situated near a physical boundary. This effect combines standard Friedel oscillations with hyperbolic fringes, offering a powerful, first-order diagnostic tool for mapping the superconducting order parameter using STM measurements.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on superconductivity, the methodology of using local probes (STM/STS) to detect subtle quantum interference effects arising from boundaries is highly relevant to open quantum systems and mesoscopic physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates particle-hole interference, analogous to Young's double-slit experiment, occurring in superconductors due to a single impurity near a physical boundary or defect.

Main result. This 'ghost' interference pattern is parametrically stronger than previously studied effects because it appears at first order in the impurity potential. It provides a robust local probe sensitive to superconducting order parameter anisotropy.

Method. The analysis involves calculating the impurity-modified Green's function using boundary conditions and deriving the resulting Tunneling Density of States (TDOS).

Model / system. The system is a superconductor modeled with Bogoliubov quasiparticles, featuring an impurity scatterer near a line defect or phase boundary. The physics is governed by modifications to the Green's function due to this proximity.

Key observables. Tunneling Density of States (TDOS) measured via STM/STS; interference patterns combining $2k_F$ Friedel oscillations with hyperbolic fringes.

Important parameters / regimes. The superconducting gap ($\Delta$), Fermi momentum ($k_F$), and the bias voltage ($eV$) relative to $\Delta$.

Assumptions / limitations. Low temperature is assumed, leading to delta-function approximations; the reflection amplitude at the boundary is treated phenomenologically.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the TDOS showing both the impurity/mirror image interference pattern (a) and a Fourier-filtered view of hyperbolic Young's patterns for a d+id superconductor (b).

Paper structure. The paper develops the theory by modifying the Green's function to account for boundary scattering, derives the resulting spectral function, and analyzes the observable TDOS to isolate and interpret the ghost interference term.

Abstract

Mirror-assisted optical interference can improve the fidelity of Young's double-slit interference. Here we discuss an electron analogue: particle-hole interference in superconductors produced by a single impurity near a line defect, terrace edge, or phase boundary. Quasiparticle waves scattered directly from the impurity interfere with waves reflected by the boundary, generating a ``ghost'' interference pattern that combines conventional $2k_F$ Friedel oscillations with additional hyperbolic fringes. Compared to the recently studied two-impurity Young's interference, this effect appears already at first order in the impurity potential and is therefore parametrically stronger. The resulting spatial modulation extends beyond $λ_F/2$ and is directly sensitive to the quasiparticle structure of the paired state, including possible Fermi-surface anisotropy of the superconducting order parameter. These findings point to boundary-assisted impurity interference as a robust local probe of superconducting electronic order, with clear signatures accessible to STM/STS measurements.

Summary. This paper theoretically analyzes magnetochiral anisotropy (MCA) in 2D superconducting TMDs like $ ext{MoS}_2$. It shows that MCA, a nonreciprocal transport effect, is strongly enhanced near the critical temperature due to superconducting fluctuations. The work uses advanced symmetry and fluctuation theories to quantify these effects, highlighting the role of strain in modifying the response.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on condensed matter superconductivity, the rigorous application of symmetry constraints to derive non-linear transport coefficients ($\gamma_{ijkl}$) is a powerful theoretical tool applicable to understanding directional couplings in complex quantum materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates nonreciprocal charge transport, specifically magnetochiral anisotropy (MCA), in two-dimensional superconducting materials.

Main result. MCA is strongly enhanced near the superconducting transition temperature ($T_c$) due to fluctuation effects, with contributions arising from both order-parameter fluctuations (AL) and quasiparticle interference (MT).

Method. The analysis employs theoretical methods combining symmetry group theory, Ginzburg-Landau formalism, and diagrammatic techniques (e.g., Cooperon diagrams) to calculate nonlinear current response tensors.

Model / system. The system is modeled using superconducting transition metal dichalcogenides ($ ext{MoS}_2$) exhibiting noncentrosymmetric superconductivity in 2D. The theory incorporates symmetry-breaking perturbations like strain and magnetic fields.

Key observables. Nonreciprocal conductivity tensor components ($\gamma_{ijkl}$), the MCA current ($j_{MCA}$), and nonlinear conductivity scaling $\sigma(\mathbf{E})$.

Important parameters / regimes. $T_c$, correlation length ($\xi$), disorder scattering time ($ au$), magnetic field strength ($\mathbf{B}$), and strain tensor ($oldsymbol{\epsilon}$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is performed in the diffusive limit ($T_c au \ll 1$) and relies on separating contributions into AL (collective) and MT (quasiparticle) fluctuation terms.

Figures summary. Not specified by notes, but the structure implies showing derived tensor forms and scaling laws for conductivity components.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from general symmetry considerations of MCA to detailed calculations incorporating both Aslamazov-Larkin (AL) and Maki-Thompson (MT) fluctuation corrections near $T_c$, concluding with an analysis of nonlinear conductivity scaling in the superconducting state.

Abstract

We present a theoretical study of nonreciprocal charge transport in two-dimensional noncentrosymmetric superconductors, focusing on transition-metal dichalcogenide MoS$_2$ as a representative example. In the normal state, the electrical response of a material to the relative orientation of the current and magnetic field is suppressed to leading order in the symmetry-breaking perturbations. In the vicinity of the superconducting transition, magnetochiral anisotropy is strongly enhanced. We consider contributions to the nonreciprocal current originating from order-parameter fluctuations and quantum-interference processes. These terms are linked to higher-order Lifshitz invariants generically allowed in the Ginzburg-Landau free energy of superconductors with broken inversion and time-reversal symmetries. We further show that strain enables additional vector components in the nonlinear current response.

Summary. This work argues that realizing ambitious critical minerals supply chain goals requires moving beyond simple resource mapping. It champions Digital Rock Physics (DRP) as a necessary, integrated infrastructure combining 3D imaging, chemistry, and reactive modeling to accurately predict resource viability from complex feedstocks like tailings and e-waste.

Why it may be interesting. While not quantum physics, the paper's emphasis on complex, multi-scale modeling of reactive transport and data integration echoes challenges in simulating many-body systems or open quantum systems where boundary conditions and coupling are critical. The need for 'physics-informed ML' to bridge scales is conceptually relevant.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core challenge is transitioning from ambitious policy targets for critical minerals to practical, environmentally sound resource extraction by characterizing complex, heterogeneous feedstocks.

Main result. Digital Rock Physics (DRP) is proposed as an essential enabling infrastructure that integrates 3D imaging, chemistry, and reactive transport modeling to make resource assessment predictive and actionable.

Method. The approach combines advanced experimental techniques like 3D X-ray Computed Tomography ($\mu$CT), correlative chemistry, and AI analysis with computational pore-scale modeling of fluid flow and chemical reactions.

Model / system. The physical systems include hard-rock ores, mine tailings, brines, and spent battery black mass. The model is the integrated DRP workflow that links mineral texture and reactive pathways to industrial process decisions across the entire resource lifecycle.

Key observables. True liberation volumes (3D vs 2D), connected flow pathways, time-resolved changes in pore geometry/chemistry, dissolved product concentrations, and energy demand reduction potential.

Important parameters / regimes. Scale gap between nano-CT ($\sim 0.1 ext{ mm}^3$) and mine scale ($10^{10} ext{ m}^3$); the need for standardized data infrastructure across disciplines.

Assumptions / limitations. Conventional automated mineralogy based on 2D sections systematically underestimates true resource potential; policy implementation requires shared, integrated digital platforms rather than standalone instruments.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the integrated DRP workflow connecting characterization to decision-making, mapping DRP's contribution across the critical minerals value chain (Exploration through Recycling), and presenting a roadmap for infrastructure development.

Paper structure. The paper establishes the policy gap, details the technical limitations of current 2D methods, presents the integrated DRP methodology combining structure/chemistry/transport, outlines predictive applications in various resource types (ore, tailings, batteries), and concludes with a concrete policy agenda for infrastructure investment.

Abstract

The United Kingdom and Europe are moving rapidly from critical minerals target-setting to implementation. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act and the UK's Vision 2035 create ambitious benchmarks for domestic extraction, processing, recycling, circularity, and supply-chain resilience, but many prospective regional resources remain complex, under-explored, historically worked, or economically marginal. This paper argues that implementation will depend not only on permitting reform and project designation, but also on pre-competitive measurement, modelling, and data infrastructure capable of determining which ores, brines, waste streams, and recycling feedstocks can be processed viably and with lower environmental impact. Digital Rock Physics (DRP) should therefore be understood as enabling infrastructure for resource policy rather than as a specialist laboratory method alone. By combining three-dimensional imaging, correlative chemistry, AI-enabled image analysis, and pore-scale modelling, DRP can connect mineral texture and reactive pathways to decisions about ore characterisation, liberation prediction, leaching, Direct Lithium Extraction, mine-waste valorisation, and battery recycling. The paper sets out a UK-European policy agenda built around translational demonstrators, cross-disciplinary training, a Digital Ore Passport standard, a federated Digital Ore Database, and integrated geo-reactive end stations. Treated as shared implementation infrastructure, DRP could help turn critical minerals strategies into practical routes for supply security, resource efficiency, circularity, and more environmentally responsible development.
