Excitation-Energy-Selective Control of Hot-Carrier Cooling via a Resonant Optical-Phonon Bottleneck in Graphene

Authors: Sachin Sharm, Elliott Walker, Rachael Myers-Ward, Jenifer Hajzus, Yijing Liu, Paola Barbara, Ioannis Chatzakis
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20430v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The researchers demonstrated that the cooling rate of hot carriers in graphene can be reversibly controlled by tuning the energy of the excitation light. This is achieved by triggering a resonant optical-phonon bottleneck that traps energy in the phonon system, providing a new way to engineer ultrafast energy flow in 2D materials.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding and controlling the ultrafast hot-carrier relaxation dynamics in graphene, specifically identifying the mechanism behind an anomalous slowdown in carrier cooling at specific excitation energies.

Main result. The discovery of an energy-selective control mechanism where carrier lifetime increases by an order of magnitude (up to ~20 ps) within a narrow 0.42-0.48 eV window due to a resonant optical-phonon bottleneck.

Method. Mid-infrared pump-terahertz probe spectroscopy combined with an extended two-temperature model (TTM) to analyze energy exchange between electrons, optical phonons, and the lattice.

Model / system. The system consists of monolayer and bilayer graphene (CVD-grown on SiO2/Si and epitaxial on SiC). The theoretical framework is a three-subsystem energy-balance model involving the electronic system, a hot optical-phonon bath, and a thermal lattice reservoir.

Key observables. Carrier relaxation time (tau), photoinduced change in THz conductivity (delta sigma), and THz transmission change (delta T/T).

Important parameters / regimes. Excitation photon energy (0.22-0.73 eV), resonant window (0.42-0.48 eV), Fermi energy (0.06-0.39 eV), and optical-phonon lifetime.

Assumptions / limitations. The electronic system reaches a quasi-equilibrium Fermi-Dirac distribution on a sub-100 fs timescale before the probe pulse; the lattice temperature is assumed to be fixed at room temperature.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the THz pump-probe setup and waveforms; Fig 2 plots relaxation time vs. photon energy and time-resolved transmission; Fig 4 illustrates the three cooling regimes (low, resonant, and high energy); Fig 5 shows Raman and XPS characterization of the graphene samples.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of carrier cooling, describes the experimental setup and material characterization, presents the discovery of the energy-dependent relaxation peak, develops a theoretical energy-balance model to explain the phonon bottleneck, and concludes with the implications for optoelectronic control.

Abstract

Understanding and controlling hot-carrier relaxation in graphene is crucial for advancing ultrafast optoelectronic and terahertz technologies. Here, we investigate carrier cooling dynamics in monolayer and bilayer graphene using mid-infrared pump pulses (0.22-0.73 eV) and terahertz probe pulses. We uncover a pronounced, reproducible, and non-monotonic dependence of the carrier relaxation time on excitation photon energy. Remarkably, within a narrow spectral window (0.42 to 0.48 eV), the carrier lifetime increases by an order of magnitude compared to a few picosecond-scale cooling observed at other energies. We show that this anomalous slowdown originates from a resonant enhancement of the optical-phonon lifetime, causing accumulation and reabsorption of hot optical phonons that suppress energy transfer to the lattice. All observed behaviors are captured within a unified carrier-phonon energy-balance framework, where excitation-energy-dependent variations of the effective optical-phonon decay pathway govern the cooling dynamics. These findings demonstrate excitation-energy-selective control of hot-carrier relaxation in graphene and provide new insight into non-equilibrium carrier-phonon interactions near the optical-phonon bottleneck.

Fröhlich-type Polarons in Isotopically Enriched Hexagonal Boron Nitride

Authors: Ioannis Chatzakis, Timur Abdilov, Elliot Walker, Jaime Freitas, Song Liu, James H. Edgar
Type: experiment · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20428v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum optics experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that enriching hBN with the lighter boron-10 isotope modifies the lattice dynamics and dielectric screening, leading to enhanced exciton binding energy and the formation of large Fröhlich-type polarons. By using cathodoluminescence, the researchers provide a quantitative measurement of exciton-phonon coupling strengths. This work establishes a foundation for engineering the optical and quantum properties of hBN through isotopic control.

Why it may be interesting. The study provides a direct quantitative characterization of how lattice engineering (isotopic substitution) can be used to tailor phonon-polaritonic and quantum-optical properties in 2D materials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The quantitative determination of exciton-phonon interactions and the role of isotopic composition in defining the optical response of hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) remains incomplete.

Main result. The study reveals Fröhlich-type exciton-phonon coupling in boron-10-enriched hBN, demonstrating that isotopic engineering can increase exciton binding energy and enhance polaron formation.

Method. Low-temperature (6 K) cathodoluminescence (CL) spectroscopy was used to resolve exciton and phonon-replica peaks, complemented by Raman spectroscopy and AFM for structural characterization.

Model / system. The physical system is isotopically enriched (99.22 at% 10B) and natural-abundance hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), modeled using the Fröhlich interaction and Feynman's path-integral theory for polarons.

Key observables. Indirect exciton energy (5.95 eV), LO phonon replica detuning (184 meV), Fröhlich coupling constant (alpha), exciton binding energy, and phonon energy shifts.

Important parameters / regimes. Fröhlich coupling constant alpha = 0.159, exciton binding energy of 161 meV, polaron binding energy of ~48 meV, and exciton scattering time of ~97 fs.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the validity of the Fröhlich interaction model for long-range coupling and uses the Kanaya et al. diffusion model for electron penetration depth.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Raman spectra comparing natural and 10B-enriched hBN; Figure 3 shows AFM thickness profiles; Figure 4 shows CL spectra with defect series; Figure 5 shows high-energy CL features; Figure 6 illustrates the phonon-mediated transition mechanism.

Paper structure. The paper begins with material synthesis and structural verification (Raman/AFM), proceeds to the identification of excitonic and defect-related spectral features via CL, and concludes with a quantitative analysis of exciton-phonon coupling and isotopic effects.

Abstract

Exciton-phonon interactions play a central role in defining the optical response of hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), yet their quantitative determination has remained incomplete. Here, we reveal the Fröhlich-type exciton-phonon coupling in boron-10-enriched hBN using low-temperature cathodoluminescence. We resolve the indirect exciton 5.95$\pm$0.02 eV together with its longitudinal optical (LO) phonon replica detuned by 184$\pm$56 meV, enabling the extraction of a Fröhlich coupling constant $α$=0.159 and a larger exciton binding energy of 161 meV, larger than previously reported values for natural-abundance hBN, which is attributed to isotope enrichment. The inferred polaron radius exceeds the lattice constant, indicating large-polaron behavior. We deduced an exciton scattering time ~of 97 fs, corresponding to a homogeneous linewidth of ~6.76 meV. We further obtain a polaron binding energy of ~48 meV and an effective mass of 1.045 $m_0$. These results provide a direct quantitative characterization of exciton-phonon coupling in isotopically engineered hBN and establish a foundation for tailoring its phonon-polaritonic and quantum-optical properties.

Probing Lattice Dynamics in Real-Space and Real-Time

Authors: Navdeep Rana
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20631v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work explores using high-harmonic spectroscopy to sense the impact of lattice vibrations on electronic properties in solids like graphene. It also proposes a new method using inelastic scattering to image atomic-scale lattice dynamics in real-space and real-time.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The research aims to develop and validate methods for probing coherent lattice dynamics (phonons) in real-space and real-time, specifically investigating how these vibrations influence electronic responses in solids.

Main result. The study demonstrates that High-Harmonic Spectroscopy (HHS) can characterize phonon energy, polarization, and chirality via spectral sidebands, and shows that inelastic scattering techniques can serve as a viable alternative to time-resolved diffraction for imaging lattice dynamics.

Method. The work combines theoretical frameworks (tight-binding, DFT, and semiconductor-Bloch equations) with simulated and experimental comparisons involving High-Harmonic Spectroscopy (HHS) and inelastic scattering.

Model / system. The primary physical system is monolayer graphene, with secondary focus on silicon and germanium. Models include time-dependent tight-binding, dynamical matrix calculations, and Floquet Hamiltonians.

Key observables. High-harmonic spectra, sideband separation, phonon frequency, polarization, phase difference, chirality, dynamical structure factor, and response function.

Important parameters / regimes. Attosecond and femtosecond timescales, phonon frequency, atomic displacement amplitudes, and probe pulse polarization.

Assumptions / limitations. The study utilizes approximations such as the truncated expansion of band structure energy dispersion and the semiclassical three-step model for HHG.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the HHG recollision mechanism, graphene band structures, phonon dispersion, high-harmonic spectra with sidebands, and real-space/time visualizations of lattice vibrations in silicon.

Paper structure. The thesis begins with the fundamentals of HHG in gases and solids, moves to the use of HHS to probe coherent phonon modes in graphene, and concludes with the development of inelastic scattering methods for 4D imaging of lattice dynamics.

Abstract

The coherent lattice vibrations significantly impact physical and chemical processes in solids, such as heat transfer, displacive phase transitions, and thermal conductivity. Thus, probing lattice dynamics in real-space and real-time is essential for understanding ubiquitous phenomena in solids. High-harmonic spectroscopy (HHS) has emerged as a preferred technique for investigating static and dynamic properties of solids on ultrafast timescales. Yet, despite these accomplishments, the applicability of HHS to probe the influence of coherent lattice vibrations on electronic responses has remained unexplored. In this thesis, we explore the impact of coherent lattice dynamics on attosecond electronic responses in solids using HHS. We observe that coherent excitation of the in-plane phonon mode in graphene results in sidebands in the harmonic spectrum, separated by the frequency of the excited phonon mode. Additionally, we demonstrate the capability of HHS to characterize energy, polarization, phase difference, and the "chirality" of phonon modes. This thesis offers an avenue to probe phonon-driven processes in solids with sub-cycle temporal resolution. In the later segment, our focus shifts toward probing coherent lattice dynamics in real-space and real-time. We demonstrate that inelastic scattering techniques, combined with theoretical analysis, yield comparable results to those from time-resolved diffraction and imaging measurements within pump-probe configurations. Our findings exhibit excellent agreement with results from a time-resolved diffuse x-ray scattering experiment. Our proposed method serves as an alternative to time-resolved diffraction and imaging methods for probing lattice dynamics in real-space and real-time with atomic-scale spatiotemporal resolution.

Pulse-Driven Reconfiguration of Fractional Polar Topology in Zr-Substituted Barium Titanate

Authors: Florian Mayer
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21368v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates through simulations that ultrafast electric pulses can reconfigure the fractional topological charge within a ferroelectric nanodomain. By applying specific pulse patterns to vortex cores, researchers can program the system into 64 distinct, stable, and distinguishable metastable states. This provides a computational foundation for using topological textures as a multi-state degree of freedom in future nano-electronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the internal fractional polar topology of a ferroelectric nanodomain can be actively and locally reconfigured using ultrafast electric-field pulses to serve as a programmable multistate degree of freedom.

Main result. All 64 possible pulse-mask configurations lead to distinct, stable, and distinguishable metastable topological configurations that persist for at least 1 ns in a cryogenic regime.

Method. Effective-Hamiltonian molecular-dynamics (EHMD) simulations using a first-principles-parameterized framework and a binary pulse-mask protocol.

Model / system. An ordered 12.5% Zr-substituted barium titanate (BT) nanodomain featuring a coupled Q = -2 antiskyrmionic and Q = +4 skyrmionic texture.

Key observables. Sector-resolved topological fingerprints, real-space polarization fields, local topological charge density, and pairwise mean angular mismatch.

Important parameters / regimes. Cryogenic temperature (10 K), picosecond pulse duration (0.45 ps), electric field amplitude (6 MV/cm), and nanodomain diameter (2.6 nm).

Assumptions / limitations. The study is a computational proof of concept performed in an idealized cryogenic setting and uses a simplified effective Hamiltonian approach rather than full atomistic complexity.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the nanodomain structure and topological quarks; Figure 3 displays topological charge fingerprints and inter-slice charge transfer; Figure 4 presents matrices of angular mismatch and RMS vector difference to demonstrate state distinguishability; Supplemental Figure S1 shows the potential-energy surface and switching path.

Paper structure. The paper defines the topological nanodomain structure, describes the pulse-driven switching mechanism and simulation protocol, quantifies the resulting 64 metastable states via topological and polarization metrics, analyzes the energetic barriers and thermal stability, and concludes with a proof of concept for topological reconfiguration.

Abstract

Polar topological textures in ferroelectrics can host internal structure beyond a single integer topological charge. Here, effective-Hamiltonian molecular-dynamics simulations are used to examine whether such internal fractional topology can be reconfigured by local electric excitation in ordered 12.5% Zr-substituted barium titanate. Chemical doubling along the polar axis stabilizes a coupled nanodomain texture consisting of alternating Q = -2 antiskyrmionic and Q = +4 skyrmionic slices, in which the local topological charge fragments into six -1/3 and six +2/3 localized contributions, denoted here as topological quarks, separated by Bloch-point-like singular conversion regions. Picosecond local electric-field pulses applied to selected vortex-core columns drive reconfiguration of the internal dipolar texture of a 2.6 nm nanodomain. Under a binary pulse-mask protocol addressing the six vortex cores, all 64 masks lead, within the chosen low-temperature simulation protocol, to distinct relaxed metastable configurations. The switching calculations are performed in a cryogenic regime, and the programmed states remain stable over at least 1 ns of field-free evolution on the simulation timescale. The resulting configurations are distinguishable both by sector-resolved topological fingerprints and by their real-space polarization fields. These results provide a computational proof of concept that fractional polar topology in a ferroelectric nanodomain can be locally reconfigured by ultrafast electric excitation and used as a multistate configurational degree of freedom in an idealized low-temperature setting.

This technology enables high-throughput, scalable fabrication of moiré superlattices for studying emergent quantum phenomena.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The fabrication of 2D van der Waals twistronic heterostructures, such as twisted bilayer graphene, is currently a manual, low-throughput, and high-variability process that limits the exploration of moiré physics.

Paper structure. Abstract

Nanomaterials stacked on-demand, such as rotationally assembled two-dimensional (2D) van der Waals (vdW) layered compounds, provides a versatile platform for quantum simulation and the exploration of exotic electronic phases. Currently, however, such nanoassemblies remain largely confined to inefficiency, manually operated process, limiting their potential for probing emergent physical phenomena. There is a pressing need in the field for high-precision, automated assembling techniques, especially for the scalable fabrication of 2D twistronic heterostructures. Here, we present an intelligent automation system dedicated to the fabrication of van der Waals stacks, following the state-of-the-art protocol for dry transfer of exfoliated 2D materials. The system further employs metadata generated from each automated stacking procedure to perform reinforcement learning, thereby continuously bettering its performances. As a concrete demonstration, we fabricate twisted bilayer graphene (TBLG) -- known for its challenging preparation -- and exhibit its unconventional superconductivity near the magic angle. Our work may pave the way for high-throughput fabrication of low-dimensional nanomaterials including twistronic heterostructures, where integrating data mining and artificial intelligence can accelerate the discovery of novel physical phenomena.

Summary. This review article details how magnetic topological insulator multilayers can be used as building blocks to design custom quantum states. By controlling layer thickness and magnetic doping, researchers can engineer complex phases like high-Chern-number insulators and axion insulators. This platform provides a versatile toolkit for studying topological magnetoelectric effects and 3D topological physics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of engineering and controlling specific topological quantum states, such as high Chern number QAH states, axion insulators, and parity anomaly states, by manipulating the interplay between magnetism and topology.

Main result. The review demonstrates that magnetic TI multilayers act as 'topological Legos,' allowing for the precise engineering of designer quantum states, including high Chern number (C up to 5) states, axion insulators, and synthetic Weyl semimetals.

Method. The paper reviews experimental progress using Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) for atomic-layer precision growth and transport measurements (Hall resistance and longitudinal resistance) to identify quantized states.

Model / system. Magnetic Topological Insulator (TI) multilayers and heterostructures, specifically using Cr-doped or V-doped (Bi,Sb)2Te3 layers combined with undoped (Bi,Sb)2Te3 layers as building blocks.

Key observables. Chern number (C), Hall resistance (rho_yx), longitudinal resistance (rho_xx), Hall conductance (sigma_xy), and magnetic exchange gaps.

Important parameters / regimes. Layer thickness (in Quintuple Layers), magnetic doping concentration, magnetization alignment (parallel vs. antiparallel), and interlayer coupling.

Assumptions / limitations. The review focuses on the regime of low temperatures (down to 25 mK) and assumes high-quality growth via MBE to minimize disorder.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the C=1 QAH effect in doped films; Figure 2 illustrates the 'Topological Legos' concept; Figure 3 details QAH in trilayers; Figure 4 shows high Chern number (C=1 to 5) engineering; Figure 5/6 covers phase transitions and SOT switching.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the realization of C=1 QAH effect, progresses to modulation-doped trilayers and high-C states, moves to mesoscopic devices and electrical switching, and concludes with the realization of axion insulators, parity anomaly states, and 3D topological phases.

Abstract

Magnetic topological insulators (TIs) provide a highly tunable platform for engineering quantum states that emerge from the interplay between topology and magnetism. In this review article, we summarize experimental progress over the past decade in designing magnetic TI multilayers by molecular beam epitaxy (MBE). By treating magnetically doped and undoped TI layers as topological Legos, we discuss how layer thickness, magnetic doping, heterostructure architecture, and stacking sequence can be used to control magnetic exchange gaps, interlayer coupling, and the Chern number C with atomic-layer precision. We first briefly review the realization of the C = 1 quantum anomalous Hall (QAH) effect in uniformly Cr-doped (Bi,Sb)2Te3 films in 2013 and uniformly V-doped (Bi,Sb)2Te3 films in 2015. We then discuss how Cr-doped and undoped (Bi,Sb)2Te3 layers can be combined to realize the C = 1 QAH effect in magnetically modulation-doped trilayers, including its extension into the three-dimensional (3D) regime. Next, we review the development of high-C QAH states, engineered plateau phase transitions, mesoscopic QAH devices, and electrical switching of chiral edge-current chirality. Finally, we discuss the realizations of axion insulator and C = 1/2 parity anomaly states in asymmetric magnetic TI trilayers. These advances establish magnetic TI multilayers as a versatile materials platform for creating new designer quantum states, including synthetic Weyl semimetal and QAH metal phases, and for probing the topological magnetoelectric effect in thick axion insulators and 3D QAH insulators.

Designing Magnetic Topological Insulator Trilayers for Highly-Efficient Spin-Orbit Torque Switching

Authors: Ling-Jie Zhou, Deyi Zhuo, Han Tay, Zi-Jie Yan, Pu Xiao, Xiaoda Liu, Bomin Zhang, Cui-Zu Chang

Summary. This paper demonstrates how to achieve efficient magnetization switching in magnetic topological insulator trilayers by leveraging substrate-induced chemical potential asymmetry. By controlling the heterostructure design and gating, the researchers show that the switching of edge current chirality in the Quantum Anomalous Hall state can be effectively manipulated. This work provides a pathway for developing energy-efficient spintronic logic and memory devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the mechanism behind Spin-Orbit Torque (SOT)-driven magnetization switching and the associated switching of edge current chirality in magnetic topological insulator trilayers.

Main result. The researchers identified that a substrate-induced charging effect from SrTiO3 creates an asymmetric chemical potential between the top and bottom layers, preventing SOT cancellation and enabling efficient magnetization reversal.

Method. Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) was used to synthesize magnetic TI trilayers, followed by electrical transport measurements using DC current pulses and gate voltage tuning in a Physical Property Measurement System (PPMS).

Model / system. Magnetic topological insulator (mTI) trilayers consisting of Cr-doped (Bi,Sb)2Te3 layers separated by a 4 quintuple layer (QL) (Bi,Sb)2Te3 spacer, grown on heat-treated SrTiO3(111) substrates.

Key observables. Hall resistance (rho_yx), longitudinal resistance (rho_xx), Hall angle, and the normalized switching ratio (delta_rho_yx/rho_yx(0)).

Important parameters / regimes. Layer thicknesses (m, n), gate voltage (Vg), in-plane magnetic field (mu_0Hx), and chemical potential (Ef) asymmetry.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the trilayer/bilayer structure and transport results (rho_xx, rho_yx) vs magnetic field and gate voltage; Fig 2 illustrates the SOT switching process and the dependence of the switching ratio on pulse current.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SOT switching problem in magnetic TIs, describes the MBE growth and device fabrication, presents transport measurements characterizing the QAH state, details the SOT switching experiments, and concludes with a mechanistic explanation based on substrate-induced chemical potential asymmetry.

Abstract

Spin-orbit torque (SOT) enables efficient electrical control of magnetization, offering a pathway towards low-power spintronic devices. Magnetic topological insulators (TIs), with spin-momentum-locked surface states and intrinsic ferromagnetism, provide a unique platform for realizing SOT switching of edge current chirality in quantum anomalous Hall (QAH) insulators. In this work, we employ molecular beam epitaxy to synthesize a series of magnetic TI trilayers with controlled layer thicknesses on heat-treated SrTiO3(111) substrates. Electrical transport measurements reveal that SOT-driven magnetization reversal and the associated switching of edge current chirality are governed by the SrTiO3(111) substrate-induced charging effect, which generates an asymmetric chemical-potential alignment between the top and bottom magnetic TI layers. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the switching polarity and efficiency can be tuned through heterostructure design, gate voltage, and in-plane magnetic field, consistent with SOT symmetry. These findings identify chemical potential asymmetry as the origin of the large SOT switching ratio in magnetic TI trilayers and establish a route for electrical control of edge current chirality in QAH insulators. This work advances the understanding of SOT switching mechanism in magnetic topological materials and paves the way for next-generation, energy-efficient QAH-based logic and memory devices.

Abstract
