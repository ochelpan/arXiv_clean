Competing anisotropies and phase transitions in the $q$-state clock model with a $p$-fold crystalline field

Authors: Milan Žukovič
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20990v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how adding a p-fold symmetry-breaking field to the 2D q-state clock model reshapes its phase diagram. It demonstrates that these fields can destroy the topological BKT phase and replace it with conventional long-range order, with the specific transition type depending on the competition between the intrinsic and imposed symmetries.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how competing discrete anisotropies (intrinsic Zq symmetry vs. external Zp crystalline fields) affect the phase transitions and the stability of the BKT phase in the 2D q-state clock model.

Main result. Even weak crystalline fields suppress the BKT phase and induce transitions to long-range order, with the phase diagram being governed by the competition between locking mechanisms rather than symmetry alone. Specifically, for q=6, p=2, the sign of the field determines whether there is a single transition (h2 > 0) or a two-step ordering process (h2 < 0).

Method. Monte Carlo simulations using the Metropolis algorithm combined with finite-size scaling (FSS) analysis to determine critical exponents and universality classes.

Model / system. A two-dimensional q-state clock model on a square lattice with an additional p-fold crystalline field term, described by the Hamiltonian H = -J sum cos(theta_i - theta_j) - hp sum cos(p*theta_i).

Key observables. Specific heat, generalized magnetizations (m1, m3), magnetic susceptibilities, and logarithmic derivatives of magnetization moments.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T/J), crystalline field strength (hp), symmetry indices (p, q), and lattice size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the system is in the strong-anisotropy limit of generalized XY models and focuses on the interplay between discrete symmetries.

Figures summary. Figures show temperature dependence of specific heat, magnetization, and susceptibility for the isotropic model; phase diagrams and specific heat peaks for p=2 (showing single vs. double transitions); and FSS analysis of critical exponent ratios for p=3.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model and the problem of competing anisotropies, details the Monte Carlo methodology, presents results for the p=2 case (distinguishing between positive and negative field strengths), analyzes the p=3 case (identifying Potts criticality), and concludes with a discussion on the instability of the BKT phase and the role of finite-size effects.

Abstract

We study the two-dimensional $q$-state clock model in the presence of an additional $p$-fold symmetry-breaking crystalline field using Monte Carlo simulations. While the pure clock model exhibits Berezinskii--Kosterlitz--Thouless (BKT) transitions for sufficiently large $q$, the effect of competing discrete anisotropies on this topological phase remains nontrivial. We show that even weak crystalline fields qualitatively modify the phase diagram by suppressing the BKT phase and inducing transitions to states with true long-range order. The resulting behavior depends sensitively on the interplay between the intrinsic $\mathbb{Z}_q$ symmetry and the imposed $\mathbb{Z}_p$ anisotropy. In particular, in the six-state clock model for $p=2$ we observe qualitatively different scenarios depending on the sign of the field: a single transition for $h_2>0$ and a two-step ordering process for $h_2<0$ with an intermediate ordered phase. For $p=3$, the system exhibits a direct transition consistent with three-state Potts criticality. These results demonstrate that the phase structure cannot be inferred from symmetry considerations alone, but is governed by the competition between distinct locking mechanisms. Our findings provide a discrete counterpart to the multi-frequency sine-Gordon description of generalized $XY$ models and illustrate how additional anisotropies reshape topological phase transitions in two dimensions.

Discernible signatures of fractionally charged anyons in a Pfaffian-Laughlin state

Authors: Vadym Apalkov, Tapash Chakraborty
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21462v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a theoretical method to detect e/4 anyons in the Pfaffian state by coupling the state to a quantum dot. By analyzing the energy dispersion and charge density, the authors show that the interaction between the dot and the fluid provides clear signatures of these elusive quasiparticles. This approach could potentially be verified using photoluminescence spectroscopy.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to identify discernible signatures of e/4 fractionally charged anyons in a Pfaffian-Laughlin state by investigating their energy dispersion and charge density distribution.

Main result. The authors observe clearly gapped energy branches in the energy spectra, where branch splitting is driven by inter-quasihole interactions, and estimate the e/4 quasihole creation energy to be approximately 0.014 epsilon_C.

Method. The researchers use exact diagonalization of the interaction Hamiltonian within a spherical geometry to calculate many-electron energy spectra and electron density distributions.

Model / system. A bilayer system consisting of a 2D electron layer in an incompressible Pfaffian state at filling factor nu = 1/2, coupled via Coulomb interactions to a nearby 2D quantum dot with a parabolic confinement potential.

Key observables. Energy dispersion, charge density distribution, and quasihole creation energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Filling factor nu = 1/2, magnetic field B = 15 T, interlayer distance d (ranging from 1.5l0 to 4.5l0), and Haldane pseudopotentials.

Assumptions / limitations. The electrons are assumed to be spinless due to large Zeeman energy, and the system is treated as a finite electron system.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows energy spectra for a QD with one electron at various distances; Figure 2 shows electron density distribution for the one-electron case; Figure 3 shows energy spectra for a QD containing an exciton.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of anyon identification, describes the theoretical model of a QD-coupled Pfaffian state, presents numerical results for energy spectra and density distributions for both electron and exciton-filled dots, and concludes with implications for PL spectroscopy.

Abstract

Understanding the nature of quasihole excitations, i.e., anyons that have fractional charge and statistics, has been a challenging problem in condensed matter physics. Our theoretical approach to this problem has been to consider a quantum dot, containing a few charged particles, coupled to the incompressible fluid. It has provided important insights into the energetics of Laughlin quasiholes. Photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy studies of this system have been able to probe these quasiholes that have confirmed our expectations. Turning to the Pfaffian state, we now observe that such a system is also able to provide valuable information about the Pfaffian quasiholes, viz., the energy dispersion, the charge density distribution and the quasihole creation energy. The energy dispersion of e/4 quasiholes derived here, clearly reflect the interaction between the quantum dot and the incompressible Pfaffian state. PL spectroscopy experiments on the 5/2 Pfaffian-Laughlin state could perhaps shed light on the energetics of these elusive quasiparticles.

Distinct lattice and charge excitations in AV3Sb5 kagome superconductors

Authors: Dongjin Oh, Stefan Enzner, Lennart Klebl, Harley D. Scammell, Julian Ingham, Tim Wehling, Giorgio Sangiovanni, Ronny Thomale, Ahmet Kemal Demir, Connor Occhialini, Dirk Wulferding, Ziqiang Wang, Andrea C. Salinas, Stephen D. Wilson, Riccardo Comin
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20573v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

Summary. This paper investigates the difference between lattice and charge excitations in AV3Sb5 kagome superconductors using polarized Raman spectroscopy. It finds that while the splitting of certain phonon modes is a universal feature across Cs, Rb, and K variants, the charge density wave excitations are highly material-dependent. This distinction provides new clues into how symmetry breaking emerges in these correlated electronic systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The microscopic origin of the distinct electronic symmetry-breaking phenomena and the material-dependent charge density wave (CDW) orders in AV3Sb5 (A=Cs, Rb, K) kagome superconductors remains elusive.

Main result. The study reveals that while the lifting of the E2g phonon degeneracy is a ubiquitous lattice anomaly across all three compounds, the collective CDW amplitude modes exhibit markedly different polarization dependences depending on the alkali-metal species.

Method. Angle-resolved polarized Raman spectroscopy (ARPRS) combined with first-principles calculations (DFPT) and an effective Hamiltonian model.

Model / system. The physical system is the family of AV3Sb5 (A=Cs, Rb, K) kagome superconductors, which feature both superconductivity and charge density wave phases with complex symmetry breaking.

Key observables. Phonon modes (E2g doublet splitting and A1g singlet), CDW amplitude modes, and polarization-resolved Raman spectra (XX and XY geometries).

Important parameters / regimes. Polarization angle (theta), temperature (T), Raman shift (cm-1), and phonon frequency modulation.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the observed phonon shifts are due to spectral overlap of independent modes rather than experimental misalignment.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the experimental geometry and basic Raman spectra; Fig 2 demonstrates the fourfold modulation of the P1 phonon frequency across Cs, Rb, and K compounds; Fig 3 provides detailed Lorentzian fitting of the P1 phonon splitting.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of symmetry breaking in AV3Sb5, presents experimental Raman spectroscopy results showing universal lattice anomalies and distinct charge dynamics, and concludes with theoretical modeling and implications for charge order structures.

Abstract

The kagome superconductor family AV3Sb5 (A=Cs, Rb, K) provides a rich platform for exploring diverse electronic symmetry breaking phenomena, including superconductivity and various forms of density wave orders. Although these compounds share the identical lattice structure in the normal state, they exhibit distinct forms of symmetry breaking upon entering the charge density wave (CDW) phase, and the microscopic origin of which remain elusive. Here, we investigate the lattice and charge degrees of freedom in AV3Sb5 using angle-resolved polarized Raman spectroscopy. Our comprehensive polarization-resolved measurements reveal that the lifting of the twofold-degeneracy of the E2g phonon mode in the CDW phase-previously reported only in CsV3Sb5 with a 3 GHz splitting-also appears ubiquitously in the other two compounds. In contrast, the collective CDW excitations exhibit markedly different polarization dependences depending on the alkali-metal species. These distinct behaviors in the lattice and charge channels provide crucial insight into the enigmatic material-dependent symmetry breaking phenomena that appear in the CDW phase. Furthermore, our experiments, together with first-principles calculations and an effective Hamiltonian model, shed light on the nature of the charge order structure in AV3Sb5 kagome superconductors.

Enhanced Reinforcement Learning-based Process Synthesis via Quantum Computing

Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21213v1

Summary. This paper proposes a quantum reinforcement learning framework for optimizing chemical process flowsheets. By introducing new encoding strategies, it overcomes previous qubit scaling limitations, showing that quantum circuits can match classical performance with significantly fewer parameters.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the scalability bottleneck in using quantum reinforcement learning for combinatorial process synthesis, where previous qubit requirements scaled poorly with problem complexity.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that new state encoding algorithms decouple qubit requirements from problem size, allowing quantum-enhanced RL to achieve competitive performance and improved parameter efficiency compared to classical Deep Q-Networks.

Method. The work introduces a hybrid quantum-classical framework using Parameterized Quantum Circuits (PQCs) as value-function approximators, employing data re-uploading and multi-axis encoding strategies.

Model / system. The problem is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) for chemical process synthesis, specifically applied to a hydrodealkylation (HDA) reaction involving toluene, hydrogen, and methane.

Key observables. Performance metrics include the number of optimal, unique, and feasible solutions found, as well as the episode number of first discovery and total runtime.

Important parameters / regimes. Key parameters include qubit count, circuit depth, number of input features, rotation gate angles, discount factor, and learning rate.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the HDA reaction occurs in the vapor phase with ideal vapor-liquid equilibrium and that the process synthesis can be discretized into a sequential decision-making task.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares classical and quantum RL; Figure 2 shows RL nomenclature; Figures 3-6 illustrate the Bloch sphere, PQC architectures, and data re-uploading; Figures 8-10 show the RL workflow and specific encoding variants; Figure 11 shows an optimal flowsheet.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the process synthesis problem, defines the MDP framework, details the classical DQN baseline, presents the proposed quantum-enhanced RL variants and encoding strategies, evaluates performance across increasing design complexities, and concludes with a discussion on scalability and hardware limitations.

Abstract

In this work, we present quantum reinforcement learning (RL) as a solution strategy for process synthesis problems. Building on our prior work, we develop a generalized framework that formally poses process synthesis as a Markov decision process and introduces quantum-enhanced RL algorithms to solve it with improved scalability. Earlier implementations of quantum-based RL for process synthesis were limited by qubit requirements, which scaled poorly with problem complexity. This work overcomes this challenge by introducing state encoding algorithms to decouple qubit requirements from problem size. A classical RL-based solution strategy is used as a baseline to benchmark the quantum algorithms under identical training conditions. All algorithms are evaluated across a flowsheet synthesis problem of increasing unit counts to analyze their performance and scalability. Results show that all approaches are capable of identifying the optimal flowsheet designs in small design spaces. For moderate-scale unit counts, quantum approaches demonstrate competitive performance on a per-episode basis and improved efficiency on a per-parameter basis versus the classical RL benchmark. This work provides a foundation for future quantum computing applications within process systems engineering, establishes a controlled benchmark for comparing classical and quantum algorithms, and shows that the proposed quantum variants remain competitive for the process synthesis problem examined in this work.

Fermion condensation in a generalized Hatsugai-Kohmoto model with momentum-mixing Landau interactions

Authors: Jan Heinrich, Andreas Rückriegel, Peter Kopietz
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21277v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how momentum-dependent interactions modify the electronic structure of the Hatsugai-Kohmoto model. It demonstrates that these interactions can induce a partially flat energy band, providing a theoretical mechanism for fermion condensation. The study also provides a mapping between the electronic model and a generalized Ising model.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether momentum-mixing Landau interactions in a generalized Hatsugai-Kohmoto model lead to the emergence of a partially flat energy band (fermion condensation).

Main result. The study finds that momentum-mixing interactions smooth out magnetic domain walls in a pseudospin representation, resulting in a partially flat electronic band consistent with the fermion condensation scenario.

Method. The authors employ a self-consistent mean-field analysis and a pseudospin formulation that maps the model onto a generalized Ising model in reciprocal space.

Model / system. A generalized Hatsugai-Kohmoto (HKL) model describing electrons on a d-dimensional hypercubic lattice with kinetic energy, Hubbard-like interaction (U), and momentum-mixing Landau-type interactions.

Key observables. Momentum distribution (n_k), Fermi surfaces, energy dispersion, pseudomagnetization (m_k), and the excitation gap (Delta).

Important parameters / regimes. Interaction strength (U), bandwidth (W), particle density (rho), and momentum-mixing interaction parameters (f_kk').

Assumptions / limitations. The HKL model is not exactly solvable, necessitating a mean-field approximation; the interaction range is assumed to be finite in reciprocal space.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows ground state configurations and momentum distributions for different phases (HSD, HS, SD, S); Figure 6 displays the local magnetic moment, effective magnetic field, and the phase diagram in the U vs. h_k plane.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the generalized HK model, develops a pseudospin mapping to an Ising model, performs a mean-field analysis to identify the fermion condensation phase, and concludes by proposing an exactly solvable variant of the model.

Abstract

The Hatsugai-Kohmoto (HK) model is an exactly solvable electronic lattice model where the interaction between electrons with opposite spin is diagonal in momentum space. We generalize the HK model by introducing momentum-mixing Landau interactions. Within a self-consistent mean-field analysis we find that the ground state of this model exhibits a partially flat energy band, in agreement with the fermion condensation scenario proposed by Khodel and Shaginyan [JETP Lett. 51, 553 (1990)]. Inspired by Andersons pseudospin formulation of BCS theory, we show that the HK model with Landau interactions can be mapped onto a generalized Ising model where each site of the reciprocal lattice hosts two Ising spins. In the pseudospin picture the emergence of a partially flat electronic band corresponds to the smoothing of a magnetic domain wall. Moreover, guided by the pseudospin picture, we propose an exactly solvable variant of the HK model which has a unique ground state for all densities.

Forced Gap Post-Selection for Quantum LDPC Codes and their Operations

Authors: Adam Wills, Theodore J. Yoder, Isaac Chuang
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20346v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a new post-selection strategy for quantum LDPC codes that significantly reduces logical error rates with minimal computational overhead. By forcing the decoder to explore complementary logical outcomes, the method identifies and rejects unreliable decoding shots. This approach is particularly effective for high-rate codes and is compatible with efficient, FPGA-friendly decoders.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient and lightweight post-selection strategy for high-rate quantum LDPC codes to reduce logical error rates without the high computational overhead of existing methods.

Main result. The proposed 'forced gap' strategy improves logical error rates by over a factor of 4 compared to previous strategies in the low post-selection regime (up to 1%) and scales linearly with the number of logical observables.

Method. A decoder-agnostic strategy involving a baseline decoding run followed by K additional runs where the decoder is forced to find corrections with complementary outcomes for each logical observable, using the Relay-BP decoder.

Model / system. Quantum Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes, specifically the 72-qubit and 144-qubit bivariate bicycle codes, under a circuit depolarizing noise model.

Key observables. Logical error rate, post-selection rate, and the 'Gap' (difference in log-likelihoods between the most-likely and second-most-likely logical classes).

Important parameters / regimes. Physical noise rates (p = 10^-3 and 2.5 x 10^-3), number of logical observables (K), and user-defined gap threshold (T).

Assumptions / limitations. The decoder is assumed to be able to operate on modified decoding instances; faults are assumed to occur independently; the 'Gap' is an approximation of the exact difference between logical class probabilities.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the flowchart of the forced gap strategy; Figure 2 plots logical error rates vs. post-selection rate for idling codes; Figure 3 shows logical error rates for various surgery instructions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of post-selection in high-rate LDPC codes, details the proposed forced gap algorithm, describes the Relay-BP decoder implementation and parameter optimization, presents benchmarking results on bivariate bicycle codes for both idling and surgery operations, and discusses future work regarding error floors and scaling.

Abstract

We develop a simple and general post-selection strategy for high-rate quantum codes that is transferrable across decoders. After an initial baseline run, the decoder is re-run once per logical observable, and forced in these latter runs to provide a solution where the given observable has the complementary outcome. Shots are rejected that find logically complementary solutions with similar likelihoods compared to the baseline. Using the Relay-BP decoder, we benchmark the strategy on the $72$-qubit and $144$-qubit bivariate bicycle codes, as well as surgery gadgets for the latter. In comparison to previous post-selection strategies, our results offer an improved logical error rate by over a factor of $4$ on the same circuit and physical error rate, and at the same rate of post-selection. Our strategies are also lightweight, relying only on FPGA-friendly belief propagation, whereas the previous best used repeated rounds of a high-latency BP-OSD decoder.

Giant nonlinear conductivity in 2D electron gas from substrate-induced dipolar scattering

Summary. This paper proposes that the unexpectedly large nonlinear conductivity in 2D materials arises from electrons scattering off periodic electric dipoles in the substrate. The mechanism relies on a mathematical singularity in the scattering gradients, which naturally predicts the observed scale of 1 micro-ohm/volt.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing theoretical frameworks cannot quantitatively explain the massive second-order nonlinear conductivities observed in 2D van der Waals heterostructures.

Main result. The authors identify a mechanism where electron scattering from a substrate-induced periodic dipole array leads to a singular enhancement of the nonlinear response due to logarithmic divergences in scattering probability gradients.

Method. The study uses a Boltzmann equation approach, T-matrix calculations to leading order in the dipole moment, and symmetry analysis to determine the conductivity tensor structure.

Model / system. A 2D isotropic Fermi gas in the xy-plane subject to both conventional short-range impurity scattering and a static periodic array of electric dipoles originating from a substrate.

Key observables. Linear conductivity (sigma) and nonlinear conductivity tensor components (specifically longitudinal response B and transverse Hall response C).

Important parameters / regimes. Dipole moment (d), impurity concentration (n_imp), Fermi momentum (kF), and the screening parameter (xi).

Assumptions / limitations. The 2D electron gas is assumed to be isotropic with a circular Fermi surface, and the dipole array is assumed to be periodic with uniform dipole moments and orientations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of electrons in a 2D gas scattering from substrate dipoles. Figure 2 displays linear conductivity scaling with d^2, nonlinear tensor components as a function of d, and the logarithmic enhancement of nonlinear conductivity with the screening parameter.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the puzzle of giant nonlinearity, defines the physical model of dipole scattering, performs symmetry analysis, derives the nonlinear conductivity using the Boltzmann equation and T-matrix, and concludes with the identification of the singular enhancement mechanism.

Abstract

Despite a surge of interest in the nonlinear transport in 2D materials, a fundamental puzzle remains: existing theoretical frameworks are unable to quantitatively account for the giant nonlinear conductivities ($\gtrsim 1 \frac{μ\text{m}}{Ω\text{V}}$) recently reported in 2D van der Waals heterostructures. Here, we introduce a mechanism based on electron scattering from a substrate-induced periodic dipole array. We show that the strict kinematic constraints, inherent to 2D scattering, lead to a singular enhancement of the nonlinear response, fundamentally dictating a natural scale of $1 \frac{μ\text{m}}{Ω\text{V}}$.

Halving the cost of QROM

Authors: Danial Motlagh, Matthew Pocrnic
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20334v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a way to significantly reduce the gate complexity of the QROM subroutine, which is a major bottleneck in many quantum algorithms. By replacing the standard SelectSwap architecture with a SelectCopy approach, the authors effectively halve the Toffoli cost in certain regimes. This allows for much more efficient data loading using available dirty ancilla qubits.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The goal is to reduce the computational overhead, specifically the Toffoli gate cost, of the QROM (Quantum Read-Only Memory) subroutine in fault-tolerant quantum algorithms.

Main result. The authors achieve approximately a 50% reduction in the dominant Toffoli cost in the qubit-constrained regime, effectively matching the performance of clean-qubit QROM using dirty qubits.

Method. The paper introduces a 'SelectCopy' architecture to replace 'SelectSwap' and presents a parametric family of methods that treats a b-bit QROM as sequential smaller QROMs to optimize the cost based on available ancilla qubits.

Model / system. The work focuses on the algorithmic implementation of coherent table lookup (QROM) for loading N bitstrings of length b in superposition, utilizing both clean and dirty ancilla qubits.

Key observables. Toffoli gate count (non-Clifford cost) and the number of required ancilla qubits.

Important parameters / regimes. N (number of elements), b (bitstring length), lambda (sub-register parameter), and the availability of dirty vs. clean qubits.

Assumptions / limitations. Initial derivations assume N, b, and alpha are powers of 2; the efficiency is tied to the availability of dirty ancilla qubits.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares the cost improvement of the new QROM against the previous state-of-the-art; Figure 2 shows circuit diagrams for the SelectCopy and SelectSwap constructions; Figure 4 illustrates the circuit for the Copy operation in the Restore process.

Paper structure. The paper identifies the QROM bottleneck, introduces the SelectCopy architecture, presents an optimization for the qubit-constrained regime, provides a parametric family of methods for cost interpolation, and concludes with a generalized theorem for non-power-of-2 parameters.

Abstract

Table lookup, often referred to as quantum read only memory (QROM), is one of the most widely used subroutines in quantum algorithms, and constitutes the majority share of algorithmic overheads in most practical applications of quantum computers. It involves the coherent loading of $N$ bitstrings of length $b$ in superposition, and naively has a non-Clifford cost of $N$ Toffolis. It is known that given access to $b\, λ$ dirty qubits, one can reduce the Toffoli cost of QROM to $2\frac{N}λ + 4b(λ- 1)$. In this work, we first present an optimization to reduce this cost to $2\frac{N}λ + 2b(λ- 1) + 2λ-6$ by replacing the SelectSwap" architecture withSelectCopy". We then provide a further optimization for the qubit-constrained regime where the Toffoli cost is typically $\sim 2\frac{N}λ$, and reduce it to $\sim (1+\frac{1}{b})\frac{N}λ$, cutting the cost by approximately $50\%$ and effectively matching the performance of clean-qubit QROM using dirty qubits for practical values of $b$. Lastly, we provide a parametric family of methods that allow the interpolation of the prefactor of the $\frac{N}λ $ term from $2$ to ($\, 1+\frac{1}{b}\,$) to obtain the best cost for different qubit availability regimes.

Hubbard-$U$-corrected electron-phonon interactions in strongly correlated materials via the finite-displacement method

Authors: Jiale Chen, Youyou Tu, Chengliang Xia, Jin Zhao, Hanghui Chen
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20985v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a new algorithm that consistently applies Hubbard-U corrections to both electronic and phonon properties in strongly correlated materials. By applying this to nickelates and ruthenates, the authors show that such corrections are vital for accurately predicting structural stability and electron-phonon coupling strengths. This work helps resolve discrepancies between previous theoretical predictions and experimental observations of superconductivity.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study addresses the need for a consistent method to include Hubbard-U corrections in both electronic and lattice degrees of freedom to accurately model electron-phonon interactions in correlated materials.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that applying Hubbard-U corrections to the entire calculation (phonon spectrum and e-ph matrix elements) is essential to stabilize the structure of strained RuO2 and to reconcile theoretical e-ph coupling with experimental superconductivity temperatures in both nickelates and ruthenates.

Method. The researchers implemented an algorithm integrating the DFT+U method with the finite-displacement method using Numerical Atomic Orbital (NAO) basis sets to calculate phonons and electron-phonon g matrices.

Model / system. The study focuses on two prototypical correlated materials: 20% hole-doped infinite-layer nickelates (LaNiO2) and strained ruthenium dioxide (RuO2) thin films on TiO2 substrates.

Key observables. Electron-phonon coupling constant (lambda), phonon dispersion/spectrum, electron-phonon g matrices, superconducting transition temperature (Tc), and electronic self-energy.

Important parameters / regimes. Hubbard U parameters (U_Ni and U_Ru), hole-doping concentration, and substrate-induced strain.

Assumptions / limitations. The calculation of Tc assumes the Coulomb pseudopotential (mu*) is zero due to the explicit inclusion of the Hubbard U; the study also focuses on bands close to the Fermi level.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the evolution of electronic properties and Fermi surface for LaNiO2 with varying U; other figures illustrate the evolution of e-ph coupling, phonon DOS, and electronic self-energy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational challenge, describes the implementation of the integrated DFT+U and finite-displacement algorithm, applies the method to LaNiO2 and RuO2 to analyze electronic and phonon-channel effects, and concludes with the necessity of self-consistent U-corrections.

Abstract

Although the density functional theory plus Hubbard $U$ correction method (DFT+U) is broadly used to study electronic structure of strongly correlated materials, the extension of this method to electron-phonon $g$ matrices has received limited attention. Here, we implement an algorithm that integrates DFT+U method with the finite-displacement method for the calculations of phonons and electron-phonon $g$ matrices. The Hubbard $U$ corrections are applied not only to electronic and phonon structures, but, more importantly, also to electron-phonon $g$ matrices. We demonstrate our algorithm in two prototypical correlated materials: infinite-layer nickelates LaNiO$_2$ and ruthenium dioxide RuO$_2$. We find that: i) While the Hubbard $U$ corrections weakly increase the electron-phonon interaction of 20% hole-doped LaNiO$_2$, its total electron-phonon coupling remains small and is insufficient to account for the observed superconducting transition temperature of about 10-30 K. Our results contrast with the recent work showing that the full GW corrections yield an elevated electron-phonon coupling of 20% hole-doped LaNiO$_2$ five times larger than its DFT value. We attribute this discrepancy to the differences in the Fermi surface topology between DFT+$U$ and GW methods. ii) The inclusion of Hubbard $U$ corrections eliminates the imaginary phonon modes of RuO$_2$ under strain on the TiO$_2$ substrate and substantially reduces the electron-phonon coupling. Our results alleviate the discrepancy between the reported large theoretical electron-phonon coupling and the low superconducting transition temperature observed experimentally. Our work provides an algorithm that fully includes the Hubbard $U$ corrections on electron-phonon properties of correlated materials, and highlights the importance of Fermi surface shape and correlation effects on phonon spectrum and electron-phonon $g$ matrices.

Interpreting Bohm quantum potentials in Computing quantum waves exactly from classical action

Summary. This paper provides a mathematical clarification for a method used to compute exact quantum waves from classical action. It proves that while the Bohm quantum potential is present in standard Madelung equations, it vanishes in this specific computational framework without affecting the final quantum wave. This resolves a technical dispute regarding the completeness of the underlying mathematical derivation.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses a technical critique claiming that a previous proof (Lemma 3.1) omitted the Bohm quantum potential term in the Madelung hydrodynamic equations.

Important parameters / regimes. The ratio of phi/hbar (noted as approximately 5 in a double-slit example); the regime is explicitly noted as being far from the quasi-classical limit.

We propose a quantum-inspired approach for prototype selection in OPF classifiers by reformulating the MST problem as a Polynomial Unconstrained Binary Optimization (PUBO) task and further employing the Feedback-Based Quantum Optimization (FALQON) algorithm for Hamiltonian minimization. The PUBO formulation reduces the need for qubits and eliminates the need for auxiliary variables, thereby addressing scalability constraints in current quantum hardware. Experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that the FALQON-optimized MST achieves accuracies comparable to those of the classical Prim's algorithm while maintaining prototype quality. While FALQON occasionally reached local minima, it did not significantly impact the accuracy of the prototype selection process.

Abstract

Credit card fraud detection is fundamentally challenged by extreme class imbalance, where fraudulent transactions are rare yet operationally critical. This imbalance often biases supervised learners toward the legitimate class, leading to high overall accuracy but weaker fraud-class recall and F1-score. This paper introduces Q-SYNTH, a hybrid classical--quantum generative adversarial framework in which a parameterized quantum circuit serves as the generator and a classical neural network serves as the discriminator. Q-SYNTH is designed for minority-class fraud synthesis in tabular data and is evaluated along two dimensions: statistical fidelity to real fraud samples and downstream performance for fraud detection. To this end, generated samples are assessed using distributional similarity measures based on Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistics and Wasserstein distances, real-vs-synthetic detectability measured by AUC-ROC, and downstream classification performance across both quantum and classical classifiers. Under the reported protocol, Q-SYNTH reduces marginal distribution mismatch relative to a classical GAN baseline while maintaining competitive downstream fraud-detection performance. Although SMOTE achieves the strongest feature-wise similarity and the classical GAN attains the highest downstream performance in several settings, Q-SYNTH offers a favorable compromise between distributional fidelity and downstream performance, supporting the feasibility of hybrid quantum augmentation for imbalanced fraud detection.
