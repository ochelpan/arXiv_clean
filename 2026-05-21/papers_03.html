Generalized quantum Stein's lemma for mixed sources

Authors: Haruka Kanazawa, Hayata Yamasaki
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20776v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper extends the quantum Stein's lemma to scenarios where the null hypothesis is a mixture of different IID quantum sources. It proves that while the error rate is determined by the hardest-to-distinguish component when the false alarm rate is zero, the specific weights of the mixture matter significantly when a fixed false alarm threshold is allowed.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to generalize the quantum Stein's lemma to the case where the null hypothesis is a mixed source, specifically a finite probabilistic mixture of IID quantum states, and to determine the optimal asymptotic error exponents.

Main result. For vanishing type-I error, the optimal type-II error exponent is determined by the worst-case (least distinguishable) IID component in the mixture. However, for a fixed non-zero type-I error, this 'worst-component' characterization fails as the exponent becomes dependent on the mixture's probability distribution.

Method. The authors utilize quantum information-spectrum analysis, operator inequalities derived from the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, and pinching maps to reduce non-commutative problems to commuting settings.

Model / system. The model involves quantum hypothesis testing between a null hypothesis consisting of a finite mixture of IID quantum states (rho_n = sum p_i * rho_i^n) and a composite alternative hypothesis consisting of sequences of compact convex sets of quantum states.

Key observables. Type-I error (alpha), Type-II error (beta), and the spectral inf-divergence rate.

Important parameters / regimes. The vanishing type-I error regime (epsilon = 0) versus the fixed non-zero type-I error regime (epsilon in (0, 1)), and the number of components in the mixture.

Assumptions / limitations. The results assume a finite number of components in the mixture and that the number of distinct eigenvalues in the alternative hypothesis grows subexponentially.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of mixed-source hypothesis testing, proves the characterization of the error exponent for vanishing type-I error using spectral tests, provides a counterexample for the fixed type-I error regime, and uses pinching maps to analyze the non-commutative setting.

Abstract

The generalized quantum Stein's lemma characterizes the optimal asymptotic exponent of the type-II error in quantum hypothesis testing for an independent and identically distributed (IID) null hypothesis against a composite alternative hypothesis. Classically, a probabilistic mixture of IID sources arises as a natural generalization of IID sources, and, in the non-composite setting, the optimal type-II error exponent in hypothesis testing for such classical mixed sources is known to be characterized concisely by the worst-case component of the mixture. In this work, we extend these foundational results to composite quantum hypothesis testing where the null hypothesis is a mixed source, i.e., a probabilistic mixture of IID quantum states, and the alternative hypothesis is composite as in the generalized quantum Stein's lemma. When the type-I error vanishes asymptotically, we characterize the optimal type-II error exponent of this composite quantum hypothesis testing problem in terms of the worst-case component of the mixture, by developing techniques for the non-commutative quantum setting inspired by the classical information-spectrum analysis. We also show that the analogous characterization does not hold in general for a fixed nonzero type-I error threshold, by providing a counterexample beyond the vanishing type-I error regime. These results clarify the applicability of the generalized quantum Stein's lemma to highly non-IID null hypotheses arising from arbitrary finite probabilistic mixtures of IID quantum states.

Orbital-Angular-Momentum Entangled Photon Emission from Circular Currents in Semiconductor-Superconductor Structures

Authors: Avi Koriat, Ankit Kumar, Alex Hayat
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20329v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · interference shaping light 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper theoretically demonstrates a method to generate OAM-entangled photon pairs from a superconducting LED by utilizing the spatial phase of a circular supercurrent. This mechanism allows for the direct mapping of superconducting qubit states onto photonic OAM modes, providing a potential interface for hybrid quantum networks.

Why it may be interesting. It proposes a novel mechanism for high-dimensional quantum state transfer between solid-state qubits and photonic modes, bridging the gap between superconducting circuits and quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The need for a quantum interface to transfer quantum information from superconducting qubits (limited by local environment) to photonic channels (ideal for long-distance communication) using high-dimensional OAM encoding.

Main result. Demonstrated that a superconducting circular current can imprint its spatial phase onto emitted photon pairs, creating OAM-entangled photons that faithfully map the qubit's superposition state.

Method. Theoretical modeling using a combination of Ginzburg-Landau theory for the superconducting phase and BCS theory for the semiconductor recombination, utilizing Bogoliubov transformations and perturbation theory.

Model / system. A superconductor-semiconductor-superconductor (S-N-P-S) structure acting as a Superconducting LED (SLED), featuring a ring-shaped superconducting cathode and a disk-shaped anode with a p-n quantum well.

Key observables. Two-photon emission rate, OAM quantum numbers, photon-energy detuning, state purity, and Bell-state fidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. Winding number (ls), voltage bias (V), temperature (T/Tc), and the enhancement factor (L/Lphi) representing the ratio of device circumference to coherence length.

Assumptions / limitations. The model assumes a well-defined superconducting gap and relies on the ability to detect specific OAM modes; fidelity is sensitive to thermal excitations and gap narrowing.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the device architecture, side view with optical cavity, and band diagrams. Fig 2 displays the two-photon emission rate vs. detuning and temperature for CP and BQP processes. Fig 5 shows Bell-state fidelity as a function of temperature for different enhancement factors.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific problem of platform bridging, describes the SLED device architecture and theoretical framework (GL and BCS), details the mathematical derivation of photon emission and OAM conservation, evaluates the impact of thermal quasiparticles on fidelity, and concludes with the implications for quantum communication.

Abstract

We theoretically demonstrate that a superconducting circular current induced in a semiconductor results in emission of orbital-angular-momentum (OAM) entangled photon pairs upon carrier recombination. Combining the macroscopic Ginzburg-Landau theory and the microscopic Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory, we investigate the emission of a superconducting light-emitting diode (SLED) with a spatially varying phase profile in the superconducting order parameter. We show that in the active region of the SLED with a circular supercurrent, radiative recombination processes inherit the order parameter phase and result in photon pairs emitted into modes of different OAM quantum numbers. We demonstrate that coherent superposition of superconducting qubit eigenstates can also be mapped onto a coherent superposition of emitted photon states. We also show that other recombination processes due to thermally excited quasi particles do not significantly degrade the state purity. Our results introduce an original scheme for generating OAM-entangled photons enabling a new method of transmitting superconducting qubit information to photonic channels thereby bridging the gap between solid-state and photon-based platforms for quantum communications and information processing.

Decoy Statebased Time Synchronization

Summary. This paper proposes a way to synchronize clocks in QKD systems by using the inherent randomness of the decoy states already present in the protocol. This eliminates the need for an extra classical channel, reducing the cost and complexity of quantum networks, especially for satellite-to-ground links.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Achieving precise clock synchronization between transmitter and receiver in QKD systems without the need for a dedicated classical physical channel, which is costly and increases system complexity.

Main result. The authors demonstrate a protocol-native synchronization method using existing decoy states that requires no protocol changes, and an enhanced method using bright pulses that extends synchronization capability to much higher channel losses (up to 59 dB).

Method. The method uses FFT-based cross-correlation of the random pattern of signal and decoy state intensities with the detected photon arrival times at the receiver.

Model / system. A simulated fiber-based and free-space QKD setup using the decoy-state BB84 protocol with time-bin encoding and weak coherent pulses at high repetition rates (up to 2.5 GHz).

Key observables. Photon arrival times, photon statistics, clock offset, Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER), and synchronization performance score.

Important parameters / regimes. Mean photon numbers (signal vs. decoy), channel loss (up to 70 dB), repetition rate (up to 2.5 GHz), background rate, and detector dead time.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes a 1-decoy or 2-decoy BB84 protocol where state intensity information is eventually made public; assumes frequency recovery (syntonization) is already addressed.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows state encoding; Fig 2 illustrates the pulse sequence; Fig 6 shows cross-correlation performance vs. loss; Fig 7 shows the modified protocol with bright pulses; Fig 8-10 show the impact of bright pulse brightness and probability on performance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the synchronization problem, proposes a protocol-native method using existing decoy states, introduces an enhanced method using bright pulses for high-loss scenarios, provides simulation results for both, and compares the performance against existing synchronization techniques.

Abstract

Time synchronization is a crucial requirement in quantum key distribution (QKD)8 protocols, ensuring accurate key generation via the correct assignment of bits of raw key and9 enabling eavesdropping detection via the precise recording of photon statistics. State-of-the-art10 experiments typically use an extra channel to synchronize the clocks of the transmitter and receiver11 via classical signals. In this work, we study the possibility of performing clock synchronization12 via the signals used for the key generation, which are already present in decoy-state-based BB8413 protocols.14 Without altering the protocol in any way, we use the different mean photon numbers of the15 signal and decoy states for time synchronization without a dedicated physical channel capable of16 clock synchronization. The proposed method relies only on the photons sent and received for17 key generation and does not require any change to the QKD protocol. The only change in the18 experiment is on the software level, thus making it very simple to implement.19 We demonstrate clock synchronization method in a simulation of a specific fiber-based QKD20 experiment. Like other decoy-state-based BB84 protocols, it is based on weak coherent pulses.21 In this simulation, we investigate the parameter space to find limits and optimal choices of our22 proposed method.23 In addition to the non-protocol-altering clock synchronization method, we also discuss an24 approach that significantly improves performance in lossy channels by introducing an additional25 decoy state with a very high mean photon number.26 By eliminating the need for an extra channel capable of clock synchronization, both methods27 proposed potentially reduce the complexity and cost of QKD systems and improve their agility

A Simple GPU-Accelerated Solver for the Schrödinger Operator with Applications to Ground States and Hamiltonian Simulation

Authors: Xinyu Liu, Xiangxiong Zhang
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20491v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a highly efficient GPU-accelerated solver for the Schrödinger operator that scales exceptionally well to billions of degrees of freedom. By using a tensor-product preconditioner, the authors enable rapid ground state computation and Hamiltonian simulation for complex, non-separable, and high-dimensional quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a highly scalable computational framework for simulating many-body quantum dynamics and Bose-Einstein condensates, enabling the study of high-dimensional systems (up to 9D) that were previously computationally inaccessible.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing an efficient, GPU-accelerated solver for the Schrödinger operator, specifically addressing the loss of tensor-product structure when dealing with non-separable potentials.

Main result. The authors present a solver that achieves $O(N^{1+1/d})$ complexity and can invert operators with $10^9$ degrees of freedom in under one second on an NVIDIA A100. They also prove that the proposed preconditioner ensures grid-independent convergence and a bounded condition number.

Method. A tensor-product direct solver using per-axis eigendecomposition for separable potentials, combined with a Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient (PCG) method using the separable part as a preconditioner for non-separable potentials. The implementation uses the Spectral Element Method (SEM) and JAX on NVIDIA GPUs.

Model / system. The Schrödinger operator $H = -\Delta + V$, including separable and non-separable potentials, the Gross-Pitaevskii equation (GPE), and multi-body Coulomb Hamiltonians in 4D, 6D, and 9D.

Key observables. Ground state energy, eigenvalues, wavefunction profiles, and inter-particle correlations.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimension $d$, nonlinearity strength $eta$, mesh size $h$, domain size $L$, and the Coulomb singularity regularization parameter $\delta$.

Assumptions / limitations. The potential $V$ can be split into a separable part $V_1$ and a bounded non-separable part $V_2$; domains are primarily rectangular with homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions.

Figures summary. Figures show PCG convergence for various potentials, the evolution of ground state profiles as nonlinearity increases, and visual slices of particle densities in 6D and 9D systems.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the extension of the tensor-product solver to the Schrödinger operator, provides mathematical proofs for spectral clustering and preconditioner effectiveness, demonstrates high-performance GPU implementation, and applies the method to ground state computation (GPE) and high-dimensional Hamiltonian simulation.

Abstract

We extend the tensor-product direct solver from the Laplacian to the Schrödinger operator $-Δ+ V$. When the potential $V_1$ is separable, the operator $-Δ+ V_1$ is inverted or exponentiated at cost $O(N^{1+1/d})$ in $d$ dimensions via per-axis eigendecomposition. On a single NVIDIA A100 GPU, this costs less than one second for $10^9$ degrees of freedom in 3D. For non-separable potentials $V = V_1 + V_2$, the same solver provides a preconditioner $(-Δ+ V_1)^{-1}$ for the preconditioned conjugate gradient (PCG) method and a propagator for operator-splitting time integrators. For bounded $V_2$, we prove that the preconditioned operator has a bounded condition number and a clustered spectrum with at most finitely many outlier eigenvalues, independently of the mesh size, and also independently of the domain size when $V_1$ is a confining potential. This explains the mesh- and domain-independent PCG iteration counts observed in practice. We apply this method to ground state computation via inverse iteration for linear problems and via the $a_u$ gradient flow for Gross--Pitaevskii energy in 3D, and also Hamiltonian simulation via the approximated qHOP and Magnus-2 splitting methods from 3D to 9D on a single NVIDIA GH200 GPU.

Carrier-doping effect and anomalous transport properties in Ni-doped CeCoIn5 investigated by Hall resistivity measurements

Authors: Ryosuke Koizumi, Hayao Fujimoto, Teppei Takahashi, Azumi Yashiro, Haruna Kawakami, Kazuki Ishii, Hinako Kosaka, Takeshi Hasegawa, Yusei Shimizu, Ai Nakamura, Dai Aoki, Kenichi Tenya, Makoto Yokoyama

Summary. This paper demonstrates that Ni-doping in the heavy-fermion superconductor CeCoIn5 increases the electron carrier density and suppresses anomalous transport properties. The findings suggest that Ni modifies the electronic band structure rather than acting as a simple impurity, and that the suppression of Hall anomalies is decoupled from the quantum critical point at the upper critical field.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how Ni-doping affects carrier density and the anomalous Hall resistivity features in the heavy-fermion superconductor CeCoIn5.

Main result. Ni-doping acts as an electron dopant that increases carrier density and suppresses the anomalous enhancement of the Hall coefficient near the upper critical field and in the low-field region.

Method. Hall resistivity and electrical resistivity measurements were performed on CeCo1-xNixIn5 single crystals using a PPMS across various temperatures and magnetic fields.

Model / system. The physical system is the heavy-fermion superconductor CeCo1-xNixIn5 (x <= 0.3), a compensated multi-band metal with a tetragonal structure exhibiting superconducting, non-Fermi liquid, and Fermi liquid phases.

Key observables. Hall coefficient (RH), Hall resistivity, carrier density (n), electrical resistivity (rho), and temperature derivatives of resistivity.

Important parameters / regimes. Ni concentration (x), temperature (T), magnetic field (H), and the crossover temperatures between NFL and FL states.

Assumptions / limitations. The estimation of carrier density assumes the high-field, low-temperature limit (0.5 K, 9 T) isolates the normal electronic contribution from anomalous terms.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows Ni concentration agreement and the H-T-x phase diagram; Fig 2 displays the temperature dependence of RH and phase diagrams; Fig 3 shows RH field dependence and carrier density vs. Ni concentration; Fig 4 shows the suppression of RH anomalies with increasing x.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Ni-doped CeCoIn5 system, details the experimental methods and sample characterization, presents the temperature and field dependence of Hall and resistivity data, analyzes the evolution of carrier density, and discusses the physical mechanisms behind the suppression of transport anomalies.

Abstract

We investigated the effects of Ni doping on carrier density and anomalous electrical transport properties in CeCo$_{1-x}$Ni$_x$In$_5$ ($x \leq 0.3$) by performing Hall resistivity measurements. The carrier density, estimated from the Hall coefficient $R_{\rm H}$ at a temperature of 0.5 K in high magnetic fields, increases linearly with $x$, indicating that the doped Ni ions act as electron dopants. In CeCoIn$_5$, the magnitude of $-R_{\rm H}$ is strongly enhanced at magnetic fields near the superconducting upper critical field $H_{c2}$ and in the low-field region above the superconducting transition temperature $T_c$. However, these anomalies are found to be significantly suppressed by Ni doping. Possible origins of this suppression in $-R_{\rm H}$ are discussed.

Combining non-parametric quantum states and MERA tensor networks for ground-state optimization

Authors: Julian Schuhmacher, Alberto Baiardi, Francesco Tacchino, Ivano Tavernelli

Summary. The paper introduces a new hybrid tensor network method that uses classical shadows to connect a fixed quantum state from quantum annealing to a variationally optimized MERA network. This approach improves ground-state energy accuracy for the transverse-field Ising model without increasing quantum circuit depth. The method is shown to be robust against both shot noise and simulated hardware noise, offering a practical path toward quantum utility.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and numerical methods as it provides a scalable strategy to bridge quantum hardware outputs with classical tensor network power, specifically addressing the bottleneck of state tomography and the barren plateau problem in hybrid algorithms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The difficulty of efficiently representing many-body quantum wavefunctions in higher dimensions and the high resource overhead/trainability issues associated with optimizing the quantum components of hybrid tensor networks.

Main result. The proposed hybrid approach, combining non-parametric quantum annealing states with classical MERA, improves ground-state energy accuracy without increasing circuit depth and remains robust against both statistical and hardware noise.

Method. A hybrid architecture using classical shadows to interface a fixed quantum state (prepared via digital quantum annealing) with a variationally optimized classical MERA tensor network, optimized via Riemannian gradient descent.

Model / system. The transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) on 1D spin chains of up to 24 sites, implemented via second-order Trotterized quantum annealing.

Key observables. Ground-state energy approximation (relative energy error), energy variance, and operator locality/weight.

Important parameters / regimes. Annealing time (t_final), Trotter step size (delta t), MERA bond dimension (chi), number of MERA layers (l), and hardware noise scaling factor (eta).

Assumptions / limitations. The quantum annealing process assumes the system remains in the instantaneous ground state (adiabatic approximation), and the accuracy is inherently limited by the quality of the initial non-parametric state.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows energy error vs. Trotter step; Figure 2 depicts the hybrid MERA architecture; Figure 3 compares energy reduction across system sizes; Figure 4 analyzes energy estimator variance; Figure 5 shows optimization trajectories; Figure 6 demonstrates robustness to hardware noise; Figure 7 shows the quantum circuit; Figure 8 shows operator locality transitions; Figure 9 shows variance by Pauli string weight; Figure 10 compares optimization protocols and bias.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the hybrid architecture, describes the quantum annealing and MERA components, details the classical shadow interface and Riemannian optimization, presents numerical results on energy accuracy and noise robustness, analyzes the impact of MERA on operator locality and variance, and concludes with a discussion on resource estimation and optimization bias.

Abstract

Hybrid tensor networks offer a promising route to enhance the expressivity of classical tensor network methods by incorporating quantum states prepared on a quantum computer. Existing approaches are limited by the variational optimization of the quantum component of the tensor network. In this work, we introduce an alternative strategy that combines a non-parametric quantum state prepared through quantum annealing and a classical isometric tensor network. The latter is variationally optimized while the former is used as a fixed, boundary tensor resource in the form of classical shadows. We demonstrate the feasibility of this approach through extensive numerical simulations on the transverse-field Ising model, showing that the optimization procedure remains robust under statistical and hardware noise. Moreover, our results indicate that our newly proposed setup improves the accuracy of the obtained ground state approximation compared to the original quantum simulation, without increasing the depth of the applied quantum circuits. Therefore, this setup offers a practical route to scale variational quantum algorithms towards the quantum utility scale.

Dynamical systems on ultra small-world networks

Authors: Nirbhay Patil, Ada Altieri, Fabian Aguirre-Lopez
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21364v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new theoretical framework to study dynamics on ultra small-world networks by accounting for the degree correlations caused by structural cut-offs. By mapping observed degrees to hidden degrees, the authors successfully predict species survival and stability in highly heterogeneous networks, showing much better agreement with simulations and real-world data than previous models.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Standard Heterogeneous Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (HDMFT) fails in 'ultra small-world' networks because it cannot account for the structural cut-offs and degree correlations (disassortativity) that arise in highly heterogeneous, finite-size networks.

Main result. The authors developed a framework using a Soft Configurational Model to map observed degrees to 'hidden degrees,' allowing for accurate prediction of species survival rates and stability in highly heterogeneous networks.

Method. The study employs Heterogeneous Dynamical Mean-Field Theory (HDMFT) combined with a maximum entropy ensemble (Soft Configurational Model) and generating functional analysis to derive effective single-species stochastic dynamics.

Model / system. The system is a Generalized Lotka-Volterra (GLV) model on highly heterogeneous, power-law distributed networks, where species interactions are stochastic and include competition or mutualism.

Key observables. Species survival rates, species abundance distributions, and linear stability of the equilibrium solutions.

Important parameters / regimes. Power-law exponent (alpha), mean interaction strength (mu), disorder strength (sigma), reciprocity (gamma), and average connectivity (C).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes a sufficiently large average degree (C) to neglect higher-order terms in 1/C and uses a mapping between hidden and observed degrees to handle structural constraints.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows how structural constraints force negative assortativity; Figure 2 compares survival rate predictions across different joint distribution assumptions; Figure 3 validates the theory against real-world networks; Figure 4 identifies critical system size for stability; Figure 5 demonstrates numerical convergence of the theory.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the failure of existing HDMFT in ultra small-world regimes, proposes a new framework using hidden degrees via the Soft Configurational Model, derives the effective single-species dynamics and stability conditions, and validates the approach using both simulated power-law networks and empirical real-world networks.

Abstract

Despite the knowledge that social, economical, and ecological networks are often of a small-world nature with inter-nodal distance growing even slower than logarithmically with system size, we often assume theoretical systems to be outside of this regime, to make them easier to treat analytically. Here we derive a framework to apply the powerful dynamical mean-field theory on highly heterogeneous networks that is able to account for more of the degree correlations naturally arising from network constraints, known as structural cut-offs. We apply this framework to the well-studied and understood disordered Lotka-Volterra model, and show typically reported observables such as survival rates and stability for these systems on ultra small-world networks. We find much better agreement for these variables for all ranges of exponents for simulated power-law networks as well as empirically sourced networks.

Equilibrium and dynamics of a three-state opinion model on a network of networks

Authors: Irene Ferri, Albert Díaz-Guilera, Hiroki Sayama

Summary. This paper explores a multi-scale opinion model where agents possess their own internal networks of beliefs. It demonstrates that the internal arrangement of these beliefs (e.g., star vs. ring) fundamentally changes how social consensus or polarization emerges at different temperatures and neutrality levels.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the internal topology of an individual's belief system (the organization of interconnected beliefs) influences collective opinion formation, social consensus, and phase transitions in a network of networks.

Main result. The critical temperature for consensus destabilization increases with the number of beliefs in star-like agents but saturates in ring and clique topologies; additionally, mixing different agent topologies leads to regime-dependent dominance where rings act as catalysts for extremist convergence.

Method. The study employs Mean-Field Approximation (MFA) to analytically derive order parameters and free energy, complemented by Metropolis Monte Carlo simulations to study zero-temperature dynamics and finite-temperature phase transitions.

Model / system. A three-state (positive, negative, neutral) opinion model on a multilayer graph where each agent has an internal belief graph (clique, ring, or star) and interacts with others via an external social graph. The Hamiltonian includes terms for social homophily and internal belief coherence, incorporating temperature to represent social agitation and cognitive dissonance.

Key observables. Net polarization (magnetization m), neutral fraction (n0), internal magnetization (M_int), internal energy (E_int), and critical temperature (Tc).

Important parameters / regimes. Neutrality parameter (alpha) controlling the weight of the neutral state, temperature (T) representing social agitation/dissonance, and the number of internal beliefs (c).

Assumptions / limitations. The external social network is assumed to be a fixed, fully connected graph; for star-like agents, the identity of the central hub belief is assumed to be the same across all agents.

Figures summary. Figure 1 visualizes agent belief topologies; Figure 2 shows the three internal network structures; Figure 3 illustrates energy contributions; Figure 4 shows order parameters vs. temperature; Figure 6 compares magnetization curves between MC and MFA; Figure 10 shows magnetization and neutrality distributions in mixed populations.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the network-of-networks model, defines the Hamiltonian and topologies, presents analytical Mean-Field results, validates them with Monte Carlo simulations, explores the effects of increasing belief count, analyzes zero-temperature dynamics/steady states, and concludes with the study of mixed-topology populations.

Abstract

Opinion formation models typically represent each individual as a single variable. However, in practice each individual holds interconnected beliefs whose internal organization may influence collective outcomes. To explore this dependence, we study a three-state opinion model on a network of networks in which each agent has an internal belief graph and interacts with other agents through an external social graph. Each belief can take two opposite polarized states or a neutral one and a neutrality parameter tunes the relative conviction of the neutral stance. We incorporate temperature into the model to account for external social agitation and for the tolerance of internal cognitive dissonance. We explore the stationary state and dynamics of the model using analytical approaches and Monte Carlo simulations on a fully connected external social graph, with internal belief topologies given by one-dimensional chains, cliques, and star-like structures, where there is a central core belief to which all other beliefs are connected. We find that the critical temperature at which the polarized consensus destabilizes increases with the addition of more beliefs to star-like agents but saturates in the case of ring- and clique-like internal topologies. We also consider binary mixtures of agents with different internal topologies in equal proportions, showing that the interplay between agents is regime-dependent, with the dominant topology depending on the value of the neutrality parameter.

Essentially singular limits of Jacobi operators and applications to higher-order squeezing

Authors: Felix Fischer, Daniel Burgarth, Davide Lonigro
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.21355v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper studies how Jacobi operators behave in a singular limit where the limiting operator is not essentially self-adjoint. It demonstrates that the specific way the limit is approached determines which self-adjoint extension is realized, providing a mathematical foundation for understanding higher-order squeezing in quantum optics.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for quantum optics and open quantum systems because it addresses the fundamental ambiguity in defining the dynamics of non-self-adjoint squeezing operators when the regularization term vanishes, showing that symmetry plays a key role in selecting physical extensions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the 'essentially singular' limit of a family of Jacobi operators as a coupling parameter approaches zero, specifically when the limiting operator admits multiple self-adjoint extensions.

Main result. The authors prove that the strong resolvent limit depends on the chosen sequence of the coupling parameter, meaning different extensions can be reached via different sequences; in the context of quantum optics, this implies the limit does not pick a unique extension but identifies a subclass compatible with symmetry.

Method. The study employs discrete WKB methods, Airy-function asymptotics, and the analysis of the Weyl m-function to derive uniform bounds for eigenvectors and establish strong resolvent convergence.

Model / system. The mathematical model is a family of Jacobi operators on l2(N) with a scaling parameter lambda. The physical application involves higher-order squeezing operators in quantum optics, specifically regularized Hamiltonians of the form Ak,h(K) = (a_dagger)^k + K(a_dagger a)^h + a^k.

Key observables. Wigner functions of the squeezed vacuum state and the spectral properties (eigenvalues and resolvents) of the operators.

Important parameters / regimes. The coupling parameter lambda (or K) representing the strength of the diagonal/free-field term, the order of squeezing k, and the power of the number operator h.

Assumptions / limitations. The results rely on specific growth conditions for the Jacobi coefficients (a_n and b_n) and assume smoothness and analyticity for the construction of approximate solutions via the Langer transform.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a numerical plot of the Weyl m-function at a complex point, illustrating how the values spiral around a limit circle as lambda approaches zero.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the mathematical framework for the essentially singular limit of Jacobi operators, provides technical proofs for eigenvector bounds using WKB and Airy asymptotics, and then applies these results to the selection of self-adjoint extensions in higher-order squeezing Hamiltonians.

Abstract

We study a family of Jacobi operators in which the diagonal entries are multiplied by a coupling parameter $λ\geq0$. Under suitable conditions, the operator is self-adjoint for every $λ>0$, while the formal limit at $λ=0$ is a symmetric Jacobi operator admitting a one-parameter family of self-adjoint extensions. A central ingredient of our analysis is the derivation of uniform bounds for square-summable generalized eigenvectors in the small-$λ$ regime, which combines discrete WKB methods with Airy-function asymptotics. Using these estimates, we analyze the limiting behavior $λ\to0$ in the strong resolvent sense, proving that for every sequence $λ_j\to0$ one can extract a subsequence along which the corresponding Jacobi operators converge to some self-adjoint extension of the limiting operator; conversely, every such extension can be obtained in this way. We call this behavior an essentially singular limit, by analogy with essential singularities in complex analysis. As an application, we study higher-order squeezing operators arising in quantum optics. Using the connection with Jacobi operators, we show that when the relative strength of the free-field term tends to zero, different self-adjoint extensions of the squeezing operator are selected along different sequences. In particular, this limit does not single out a physically distinguished self-adjoint extension, but instead identifies a distinguished subclass of extensions compatible with the underlying symmetry.

Universal approximation theorems provide a mathematical explanation for the expressive power of neural networks. They assert that, under mild conditions on the activation function, feedforward neural networks are dense in broad function classes, such as continuous functions on compact subsets of $\mathbb{R}^d$, $L^p$ spaces, or Sobolev spaces. Over the past four decades, these qualitative universality results have evolved into a rich quantitative theory addressing approximation rates, parameter efficiency, and the role of architectural features such as depth and width. This survey presents several glimpses into this theory. We review classical density results for single-hidden-layer networks, as well as quantitative bounds that relate approximation error to network size and smoothness assumptions on target functions. Particular emphasis is placed on depth--width trade-offs and on results demonstrating that deeper architectures can achieve superior parameter efficiency for structured function classes. In addition to standard feedforward neural networks, we also review recent developments on Kolmogorov--Arnold Networks (KANs), which offer an alternative architectural paradigm and whose approximation-theoretic properties have begun to attract significant theoretical attention.

Circuits of Quantum Hashing and Quantum Fourier Transform for a Cactus as a Qubit Connectivity Graph

Summary. This paper provides an efficient, polynomial-time method for building optimized quantum circuits for Quantum Hashing and QFT on cactus-structured quantum hardware. By solving a specific graph-theoretic path problem, the authors significantly reduce the number of CNOT gates required compared to previous methods.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Optimizing quantum circuit construction, specifically minimizing CNOT gate counts for Quantum Hashing and Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT), under restricted qubit connectivity constraints.

Main result. The authors present an O(n^3) algorithm to construct shallow circuits for cactus graph architectures, significantly reducing CNOT complexity compared to exponential-time algorithms for arbitrary graphs.

Method. The approach uses a solution to the 'Shortest non-simple 1-covering path' (1-SNSCPP) problem via dynamic programming on a tree-transformed cactus graph to guide qubit movement via SWAP gates.

Model / system. A quantum device where two-qubit gates are restricted by a 'cactus' connectivity graph, where any two simple cycles share at most one vertex.

Key observables. CNOT gate count (cost) and circuit depth.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of qubits (n), number of edges (m), path length (k), and the length of the input word (l).

Assumptions / limitations. The optimization results are specifically tailored to the cactus graph topology and assume the ability to implement SWAP and controlled rotation gates.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate shallow circuit architectures, gate decompositions (CRy, SWAP, CRd), comparisons between standard and optimized paths on cactus graphs, and the construction of auxiliary trees for the DP algorithm.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of connectivity-constrained circuit optimization, defines the cactus graph model, presents a polynomial-time solution for the 1-SNSCPP problem using dynamic programming, applies this to Quantum Hashing and QFT, and provides complexity analysis and CNOT cost bounds.

Abstract

We present a quantum circuit implementation of the quantum hashing algorithm (quantum fingerprinting) for a quantum device with restrictions on the application of two-qubit gates by a qubit connectivity graph. We present an optimization technique for the shallow circuit for quantum hashing in the case of a cactus as a qubit connectivity graph. The algorithm has $O(n^3)$ complexity to build the circuit, where $n$ is the number of qubits and $m$ is the number of connections (edges) in the graph. It is improvement compared to the existing exponential-time algorithm in the case of arbitrary graphs. The algorithm uses solution for the shortest non-simple 1-covering path problem as a subroutine. We present an $O(n^3)$-time solution for this graph-theory problem in the case of a cactus. This result can be interesting independently. The algorithm also used for improving of the quantum circuit for Quantum Fourier Transform.
