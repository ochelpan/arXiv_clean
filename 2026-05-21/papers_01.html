⭐ Benchmarking a machine-learning differential equations solver on a neutral-atom logical processor

Highlighted author(s): Antoine Browaeys
Authors: Pauline Mathiot, Elio Garnaoui, Axel-Ugo Leriche, Evan Philip, Boris Albrecht, Clémence Briosne-Fréjaville, Lorenzo Cardarelli, Antoine Cornillot, Gwennolé Cournez, Luc Couturier, Julius De Hond, Rebecca El Koussaifi, Thomas Eritzpokoff, Florian Fasola, Antonio Andrea Gentile, Casper Gyurik, Clotilde Hamot, Loïc Henriet, Gaétan Hercé, Michael Kaicher, Lucas Lassablière, François-Marie Le Régent, Edgar Leroux, Yohann Machu, Hadriel Mamann, Luis Ortiz, Annie Paine, Thomas Pansiot, Arnaud Peloquin, Francisco Ponciano, Julien Ripoll, Raja Selvarajan, Adrien Signoles, Henrique Silvério, Siddhy Tan, Marie Taouzinet, Selim Touati, Louis Vignoli, Antoine Browaeys, Pascal Scholl

Summary. This paper demonstrates that a logical quantum processor can solve differential equations more accurately than a physical processor by using error detection to suppress kernel distortions. By implementing a [[4, 2, 2]] code on a neutral-atom platform, the researchers showed a significant reduction in the error of the final application-level solution. This highlights the importance of fault-tolerant architectures for practical quantum machine learning tasks.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates a practical application of error detection in a neutral-atom platform, showing how logical encoding can specifically target and suppress coherent errors that degrade algorithmic performance in quantum machine learning.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Benchmarking the performance of a machine-learning differential equation solver on a neutral-atom logical processor to determine if logical qubits can mitigate kernel distortions caused by hardware noise.

Main result. The logical implementation using a [[4, 2, 2]] error-detecting code systematically outperforms the physical implementation, significantly reducing kernel shape distortion and achieving much higher accuracy in solving non-linear differential equations.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical workflow using quantum kernel methods and mixed-model regression to solve differential equations, compared across physical and logical qubit encodings.

Model / system. A neutral-atom quantum processor based on Rubidium-87 atoms in an optical tweezer array, utilizing Rydberg blockade for two-qubit gates and a zoning architecture for atom transport.

Key observables. Quantum kernel quality (probability p00), kernel distortion (shape and amplitude alterations), and the residual (NRMSE) of the differential equation solution.

Important parameters / regimes. Coherence times (T1 approx 5s, T2 approx 1.49s), gate fidelities (single-qubit approx 99.96%, CZ approx 98.7%), and the [[4, 2, 2]] error-detecting code.

Assumptions / limitations. The benchmark differential equations are chosen such that their solutions are expressible within the kernel's feature space; error models assume errors can be decomposed into Pauli and atom loss channels.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the end-to-end benchmark workflow; Figure 2 details the quantum kernel circuit and regression process; Figure 3 illustrates the atomic hardware, imaging, and error-corrected circuits; Figure 4 compares the distribution of residuals for linear and non-linear DEs; Supplementary figures detail error models, hardware stability, and proxy-phasing.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the benchmarking goal, describes the hardware platform and error-correcting encoding, details the machine-learning algorithm for solving DEs, presents the comparative results between physical and logical kernels, and concludes with an error analysis and discussion of future scaling.

Abstract

We report on a performance comparison between physical and logical computations on a prototypical machine-learning application: solving differential equations using quantum kernel methods. The algorithm is implemented on an atom-based logical quantum processor, both at the physical and logical levels. We show that the kernel estimated from the logical implementation performs better than its physical counterpart on relevant metrics. We observe how such performance improvement can be traced back to specific noise-induced errors detected by the chosen encoding. We apply the computed quantum kernel to the task of solving differential equations, confirming how the superior performance of a logical quantum kernel is retained also at an end-to-end applicative level. Our findings show that experimental validation of end-to-end protocols can already highlight the positive impact of fault-tolerant implementations despite their higher quantum resource count, and guide application-informed architectural choices.

Summary. This paper demonstrates a four-qubit SiMOS quantum processor that successfully generates and verifies three-qubit GHZ and cluster states. It achieves this by implementing parallelized readout and a specialized gate sequence that protects entanglement from dephasing. This work provides a significant step toward scaling silicon-based quantum computing using industry-standard CMOS techniques.

Why it may be interesting. The use of a laddered decoupling sequence to extend entanglement lifetime toward the Hahn-echo limit and the demonstration of multipartite entanglement in a scalable semiconductor architecture are highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum network research.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Scaling SiMOS quantum dot processors beyond isolated double quantum dot unit cells by achieving high-fidelity coupling between unit cells and implementing efficient, parallelized initialization and readout.

Main result. Demonstrated a four-qubit SiMOS processor capable of generating maximally entangled three-qubit GHZ and cluster states, with entanglement lifetimes extended toward the Hahn-echo limit using a laddered decoupling sequence.

Method. The researchers used a decoupled-CZ (dCZ) gate set, FPGA-based heralded initialization, and parallelized Pauli spin blockade readout, while employing Gate Set Tomography and Mermin-witness operators for state verification.

Model / system. A four-qubit SiMOS (Silicon Metal-Oxide Semiconductor) processor consisting of a linear array of quantum dots in two coupled double quantum dot (DQD) unit cells, utilizing isotopically enriched silicon.

Key observables. Single- and two-qubit gate fidelities, Mermin witness values, coherence times (T2* and THahn), Rabi frequencies, and readout fidelity.

Important parameters / regimes. Larmor frequencies ~11.4 GHz, T2* between 4.8-6.2 us, THahn between 76.3-87.2 us, and two-qubit dCZ fidelities ~95.5-95.8%.

Assumptions / limitations. The dephasing is primarily driven by charge noise rather than hyperfine coupling; SPAM errors can be modeled and corrected via a uniform depolarizing channel.

Figures summary. Figures demonstrate the comparison of sequential vs. simultaneous readout, the calibration of detuning for parallel operation, the implementation of the laddered gate sequence, and the decay of Mermin values as a function of idle and exchange time.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SiMOS platform, describes the architecture and control/readout hardware, details the gate implementation and decoupling strategies, presents the generation and verification of multi-qubit entanglement, and concludes with a discussion on scalability and noise sources.

Abstract

Spin qubits in silicon-MOS (SiMOS) quantum dots have recently demonstrated compatibility with existing industry standard CMOS fabrication techniques. These devices have routinely achieved single- and two-qubit gate fidelities above 99% and demonstrated highly entangled two-qubit Bell states in isolated double quantum dot (DQD) unit cells, however coupling between unit cells has remained challenging. In this work, we present a two unit cell, four-qubit SiMOS processor with universal controllability and fully parallelised state initialisation and readout. We use this processor to generate maximally entangled three-qubit states, including the Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) state, and certify multipartite entanglement through violation of the classical Mermin-witness bound. By using a fully symmetric dynamically decoupled gate sequence to create our entangled states, we are able to preserve the lifetime of the entanglement beyond $T_2^*$, to a time limited instead by $T_2^\textrm{Hahn}$. These demonstrations pave a road to the scalable operation of larger SiMOS processors, and achieving high purity, long-lived multi-qubit entangled states in them.

Summary. This paper identifies a mechanism where SU(2) symmetry protects a fast-decaying channel in a long-range XXZ spin chain. This protection causes a strong quantum Mpemba effect, where highly symmetric states relax much faster than less symmetric thermal states. The findings suggest that symmetry can be used to engineer and control relaxation dynamics in open quantum many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental mechanism for controlling nonequilibrium pathways and many-body lifetimes in open quantum systems using symmetry as a control knob, which is highly relevant to the stability of states in trapped ions or Rydberg arrays.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how symmetry constrains dissipative relaxation in open many-body systems and identifying the microscopic origin of the strong quantum Mpemba effect.

Main result. The authors discover a symmetry-protected fast-decay channel in an XXZ spin chain where SU(2) symmetry allows certain states to bypass slow decay modes, leading to an anomalous relaxation speed where states farther from equilibrium relax faster than closer ones.

Method. Liouvillian spectral decomposition, symmetry analysis using SU(2) and permutation groups, and analytical derivation of exact Liouvillian eigenmodes.

Model / system. A long-range spin-1/2 XXZ spin chain subject to local dephasing noise, described by a Lindblad master equation with an interaction exponent alpha.

Key observables. Trace distance (D(t)) from the steady state and the spectral weight/overlap of initial states with Liouvillian eigenmodes.

Important parameters / regimes. Anisotropy parameter (Delta), interaction range (alpha), and temperature (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is governed by a Markovian Lindblad master equation with local dephasing jump operators.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the mechanism showing how symmetric states couple to a single fast mode while symmetry-broken states project onto slow modes; Figure 2 shows the ground state relaxation dynamics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of symmetry-constrained relaxation, presents the analytical identification of a fast-decay mode at the SU(2) point, explores the effect of symmetry breaking and interaction range, and concludes with the emergence of the strong quantum Mpemba effect.

Abstract

Understanding how symmetry constrains dissipative relaxation in open quantum many-body systems remains a central challenge in nonequilibrium physics. Here we uncover a symmetry-selective Liouvillian mechanism that protects an isolated fast-decay channel in a long-range XXZ spin chain subject to dephasing noise. At the \(SU(2)\)-symmetric point, highly symmetric initial states couple exclusively to an exact Liouvillian eigenmode with decay rate \(λ=-2\), producing universal exponential relaxation independent of system size and interaction range. Breaking the symmetry restores overlap with slow Liouvillian modes and substantially suppresses the relaxation dynamics. This symmetry-filtered mode accessibility naturally gives rise to a strong quantum Mpemba effect, where a state farther from the steady state relaxes anomalously faster than closer thermal states. Our results establish symmetry-protected fast relaxation as a mechanism for controlling nonequilibrium pathways in open quantum systems.

Summary. This paper presents an analytical approximation for the dynamics of a two-level system interacting with two off-resonant electromagnetic modes. By separating mean-field dynamics from quantum fluctuations, the method captures essential entanglement and back-action effects that standard semiclassical models miss. This allows for efficient study of complex multi-mode light-matter interactions.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a computationally efficient way to study complex atom-field interactions and entanglement generation in multi-mode cavity QED and circuit QED without requiring full quantum simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The two-mode Jaynes-Cummings model lacks an exact analytical solution because its dynamical Lie algebra is infinite-dimensional, unlike the single-mode case.

Main result. The authors develop an approximation method that decomposes the dynamics into a solvable semiclassical component and a residual part treating quantum fluctuations, maintaining high fidelity even during entanglement.

Method. The approach uses a semiclassical reduction (replacing field operators with c-numbers) combined with a sequence of unitary transformations and the Magnus expansion to capture quantum correlations and back-action.

Model / system. A two-level system (atom) coupled to two independent quantized electromagnetic modes within the Jaynes-Cummings framework under the Rotating Wave Approximation.

Key observables. Atomic inversion, average photon number, quantum fidelity, atomic coherence, and linear entropy.

Important parameters / regimes. Off-resonant regime (large detunings), large field amplitudes (coherent states), and coupling strengths (g1, g2).

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes sufficiently large photon numbers to justify the semiclassical substitution and is specifically optimized for the off-resonant regime.

Figures summary. Figure 2 compares different analytical approximations against numerical simulations; Figure 3 shows how two modes cooperatively achieve atomic inversion; Figure 4 and 5 illustrate the evolution of fidelity, linear entropy, and photon numbers.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the algebraic complexity of the two-mode JCM, introduces a decomposition strategy into semiclassical and fluctuation components, applies the Magnus expansion and Wei-Norman theorem for solutions, and validates the method via numerical comparison with the full quantum model.

Abstract

We study a two-level system coupled to two quantized electromagnetic modes within the Jaynes-Cummings framework. While the single-mode model is exactly solvable due to its conserved excitation number, yielding finite-dimensional invariant subspaces, the two-mode model extension presents a fundamental challenge: although the total excitation number remains conserved, each invariant subspace is infinite-dimensional, preventing a closed-form analytical solution. Our scheme separates the dynamics into a dominant, exactly solvable semiclassical component, the atom interacting with the mean fields of both modes, and treats the remaining quantum fluctuations through a sequence of unitary transformations that preserve essential quantum features. We validate our approach through direct comparison with numerical solutions, focusing on the non-resonant regime where multiple detunings give rise to rich interference effects and multi-timescale dynamics inaccessible to standard approximations. The method accurately reproduces atomic inversion, field observables, and fidelity over relevant timescales, while remaining computationally efficient.

Resource generation and dynamical complexities in open random quantum circuits

Summary. This paper explores how environmental memory influences the growth of quantum resources and complexity in random circuits. It finds that while memoryless environments destroy entanglement, memoryful environments can sustain non-classical features and even enhance pseudorandomness compared to closed systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides deep insights into how controlled environmental interactions (memory) can be used to manipulate the generation of non-classicality and chaos in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how environmental memory affects the generation of quantum resources (entanglement, magic) and dynamical complexity (Krylov complexity, k-designs) in open random quantum circuits.

Main result. Environmental memory (MFORC) preserves entanglement and complexity, even outperforming closed systems in generating low-order k-designs, whereas memoryless environments (MLORC) cause entanglement to decay to zero while non-stabilizerness persists.

Method. Numerical simulations of random circuits using the Lanczos algorithm for Krylov complexity, trace distance for k-designs, and Rényi stabilizer entropy for non-stabilizerness.

Model / system. Three classes of random circuits: Random Unitary Circuits (RUC) for closed systems, Memoryless Open Random Circuits (MLORCO) with fresh auxiliary qubits, and Memoryful Open Random Circuits (MFORC) with retained auxiliary qubits.

Key observables. Logarithmic negativity, non-stabilizerness (magic), Krylov complexity, quantum state k-designs, mutual information, and fluctuations.

Important parameters / regimes. System size (Ns), number of auxiliary qubits, and the presence/absence of environmental memory.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is performed on relatively small system sizes (up to Ns=8) and focuses on accessible timescales for randomness generation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares circuit architectures and metrics; Figure 2 shows circuit diagrams; Figure 3 displays growth of entanglement and mutual information; Figure 5 shows SRE growth; Figure 7 shows Krylov complexity growth; Figure 8 shows trace distance for k-designs; Figure 9 illustrates GHZ-like vs W-state-like entanglement structures.

Paper structure. The paper introduces three circuit models, analyzes resource generation (entanglement and magic) from a resource-based viewpoint, evaluates dynamical complexity (Krylov and k-designs) from a statistical viewpoint, and concludes with the impact of memory as a control parameter.

Abstract

Realistic quantum devices are inherently open and often involve environments with memory. Here, we investigate quantum resource generation in two classes of random circuits, namely, memoryless open and memoryful open random circuits, and compare their behavior with the well-explored random unitary circuit model. We show that environmental memory qualitatively alters the dynamics: while unitary and memoryful circuits exhibit sustained growth and saturation of entanglement and non-stabilizerness (magic); memoryless dynamics leads to a distinct behavior where entanglement decays to zero after transient growth, even though non-stabilizerness remains non-zero, indicating the persistence of nonclassical features beyond entanglement. Consistently, Krylov complexity reveals suppressed spreading of quantum states in memoryless circuits, in contrast to strong growth in unitary and memoryful dynamics, which saturates at the maximum value. Finally, we show that memoryful circuits more effectively approach low-order quantum-state k-designs than the other two circuits. Closed dynamics are therefore usually the most resource-generating, but are ideal; realistic dynamics are open and seem to generate less, but if they possess memory, they can sometimes even outdo closed dynamics.

Summary. This paper studies how a disordered, liquid-like environment modifies high-order harmonic generation by inducing decoherence. It demonstrates that scattering from the environment leads to a secondary spectral plateau and the emergence of 'dynamic scars' where the electron density localizes around classical periodic orbits.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a mechanistic link between structural disorder and decoherence in a time-dependent framework, extending the concept of quantum scars from static systems to driven, open-system dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how a stochastically structured, disordered environment (simulating a liquid phase) affects high-order harmonic generation (HHG) and drives the transition from quantum to classical behavior.

Main result. The disordered environment induces global decoherence and a secondary HHG plateau due to off-site recombination, causing the electron probability density to localize around unstable classical periodic orbits (dynamic scars).

Method. Numerical solution of the 1D time-dependent Schrödinger equation using a symplectic integrator, combined with an ensemble average over stochastic configurations to represent the density matrix.

Model / system. A single atom with a soft-Coulomb potential in a 1D environment containing a stochastic distribution of Gaussian-well perturbers, driven by a strong laser field.

Key observables. HHG intensity spectra, harmonic amplitudes/cutoff, system purity, and photoelectron probability density.

Important parameters / regimes. Laser intensity (7.896e14 W/cm2), wavelength (1030 nm), mean inter-scatterer distance (10 a.u.), and ionization potential (0.90 a.u.).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is modeled in one dimension to isolate elastic scattering, and the environment is treated via ensemble averaging rather than a Markovian reservoir.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the pair-correlation function of the scatterers; Figure 6 illustrates the decay of purity over time; Figure 9 shows the spatial component of the flow for classical trajectories.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of HHG in liquids, describes the 1D stochastic model and numerical methods, analyzes the resulting HHG spectra and decoherence dynamics, and concludes by linking the results to the concept of quantum scars.

Abstract

Using one-dimensional simulations analyzed through the lens of open quantum systems, we study the photoelectron's strong-field dynamics from an atom surrounded by a scattering environment stochastically structured. We theoretically investigate high-order harmonic generation from this situation. We show that local dephasing of the photoelectron wavepacket induced by elastic scattering leads to global decoherence. This drives a transition from quantum to classical behavior, as witnessed by the photoelectron probability density localizing around specific trajectories of the classical analog system: unstable periodic orbits. This phenomenon mirrors quantum scars traditionally observed in the eigenfunctions of time-independent systems, such as quantum billiards. Here, it emerges in-situ within a time-dependent framework, manifesting directly in the real-time dynamics from the ground state rather than solely through spectral analysis.

Summary. This paper reports the experimental observation of a tripartite quantum phase where extended, localized, and critical states coexist in an orbital optical lattice. By using a two-stage Floquet protocol, the authors successfully prepare and distinguish these states using momentum-space and real-space diagnostics. The work demonstrates how inter-orbital coupling can be used to engineer complex localization physics.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a versatile platform for studying unconventional critical phenomena, localization physics, and the transition between different types of quantum states using Floquet engineering.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The observation and characterization of a tripartite quantum phase where extended, localized, and critical states coexist over finite spectral windows in a quasiperiodic system.

Main result. The researchers experimentally realized and identified a tripartite phase in an orbital optical lattice, demonstrating that inter-orbital coupling creates incommensurately distributed zeros that drive the emergence of critical states.

Method. The study uses a two-stage Floquet engineering protocol (frequency and amplitude ramps) for state preparation, combined with Renormalization Group (RG) analysis for theoretical validation and expansion dynamics for transport characterization.

Model / system. Ultracold 87Rb atoms in a 1D quasiperiodically driven s-p orbital optical lattice, where s and p orbitals are coupled via Floquet modulation.

Key observables. Momentum distributions (via TOF), Fractal Dimension (FD), Spatial Coherence Length (L_Coh), Momentum-space Correlation Length (k_Cor), and expansion exponents (alpha).

Introduction to the tripartite phase problem, experimental setup and Floquet engineering description, development of the two-stage preparation protocol, identification of the three phases using dual-space correlation lengths, verification via expansion dynamics and RG theory, and discussion of micromotion and robustness.

Abstract

The disordered quantum world hosts three fundamental types of states: extended, localized, and critical, of which the critical states are confined to fine-tuned critical points or mobility edges in randomly disordered systems. The tripartite phase, with all three types of states coexisting over finite spectral windows, represents a hallmark distinction between quasiperiodic and truly random systems in the localization physics. Here, we report the realization of this exotic phase in a quasi-periodically driven orbital optical lattice with ultracold atoms. The optical lattice with a quasiperiodic Floquet modulation coupling s and p orbitals is realized in experiment and shown to host the tripartite phase from exact theory. We develop a two-stage protocol to precisely prepare and detect the three types of quantum states. The characteristic exponents of these states are determined from expansion dynamics, showing their distinct universal transport properties. Our study marks a significant advancement in exploring unconventional critical phenomena and localization physics with ultracold atoms.

Entangling Power: A Probe of Symmetry and Integrability in Quantum Many-Body Systems

Authors: Ian Low, Pallab Goswami
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.20661v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5 · scars & prethermalization 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the ability of a quantum operator to generate entanglement can act as a probe for symmetry and integrability. By studying Heisenberg spin chains, the authors show that higher symmetry leads to a suppression of entangling power. The results bridge the gap between small-scale quantum gates and the thermodynamic limit of many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a new operator-based diagnostic for detecting symmetry and integrability, which is highly relevant for characterizing the dynamics of programmable quantum simulators.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the entangling power of a unitary operator can serve as a dynamical diagnostic for symmetry and integrability in quantum many-body systems.

Main result. The study establishes that increasing symmetry suppresses entangling power, showing sharp dips at SU(2) and free-fermion points in finite chains, while the two-magnon S-matrix shows vanishing entangling power at SU(2) points.

Method. The authors use an efficient algorithm for computing time-averaged entangling power via the diagonal ensemble, alongside analytical derivations using the operator-state correspondence and the Bethe Ansatz.

Model / system. Anisotropic Heisenberg (XXZ, XXX, XYZ) spin chains of various sizes (two-site, finite-size, and thermodynamic limit) and the two-magnon S-matrix.

Key observables. Time-averaged entangling power (e_p), linear entropy, and the S-matrix scattering amplitudes.

Important parameters / regimes. Anisotropy parameter (Delta), spin magnitude (s=1/2, 1), system size (L), and rapidity (theta).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes Haar-random product states for the definition of entangling power and focuses on integrable sectors of the XXZ model.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows instantaneous entangling power oscillations; Figure 3 illustrates the non-monotonic dependence of e_p on the isospectral parameter q; Figure 4 shows the scaling of the SU(2) dip with system size.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the entangling power concept, analyzes two-site models to establish a symmetry hierarchy, extends the study to finite-size XXZ chains, and concludes with the thermodynamic limit via the two-magnon S-matrix.

Abstract

The entangling power of a unitary operator quantifies its ability to generate entanglement from product states and provides a natural probe of quantum many-body dynamics. Entanglement extremization at points of enhanced symmetry has previously been observed in high-energy scattering. In this work we compute the time-averaged entangling power of anisotropic Heisenberg spin chains across two-site models and finite-size systems, as well as the entangling power of the two-magnon $S$-matrix in the thermodynamic limit. For two-site models we establish a monotonic hierarchy: the entangling power decreases as the symmetry group grows, reaching its minimum at the $SU(2)$ XXX point. Finite-size XXZ chains exhibit sharp dips at the $SU(2)$ points $Δ=\pm 1$ and the free-fermion point $Δ=0$, with the free-fermion dip decaying much more slowly with system size. In the thermodynamic limit, we decompose the two-magnon $S$-matrix into quantum logic gates -- Identity, SWAP, and $σ_z\otimesσ_z$ -- and show that the entangling power vanishes for all scattering energies at the $SU(2)$ points, where the $S$-matrix reduces to the Identity gate, while the free-fermion point achieves the maximum -- the opposite of the finite-size many-body behavior. The entangling power can serve as an {\em operator} diagnostic for symmetry and selected aspects of integrability in quantum simulations of spin-chain dynamics.

Summary. This paper introduces the 'input-output Hierarchical Equations of Motion' (io-HEOM) to simulate strong-coupling dynamics in waveguide QED. It demonstrates how the method captures non-Markovian effects from both spatial non-locality and non-linear dispersion. The work is significant for its ability to model the dynamics of virtual photon release and persistent oscillations in structured environments.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful, unified numerical tool (io-HEOM) for studying non-Markovian waveguide QED in regimes where standard approximations fail, specifically addressing complex phenomena like photon-atom hybridization and Van Hove singularities.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Modeling a single qubit in a 1D waveguide beyond the standard Markovian and perturbative approximations, specifically addressing non-Markovianity arising from non-local coupling and non-linear dispersion.

Main result. The developed io-HEOM method accurately captures non-Markovian dynamics, including the release of virtual bound photons during a qubit frequency quench and persistent oscillations due to Van Hove singularities in cavity arrays.

Method. The input-output Hierarchical Equations of Motion (io-HEOM) framework, supplemented by the Prony method for correlation function fitting and Silbey-Harris polaron theory for analytical validation.

Model / system. A single qubit interacting with a 1D waveguide, including both continuous waveguides with linear dispersion and 1D cavity arrays with non-linear dispersion.

Abstract

Quantum criticality is a resource for quantum-enhanced metrology, but existing schemes face intrinsic limitations. These arise because using criticality directly in the encoding dynamics restricts the accessible parameters to those explicitly supported by the critical Hamiltonian, and the requirement for critical conditions narrows the effective estimation range. To solve this, we introduce a general framework termed criticality-assisted noncommutative preparation (CANP). In this approach, critical evolution is employed as a state-preparation resource. We establish the underlying algebraic conditions and show that the intrinsic noncommutativity between the preparation and encoding operations leads to a genuine enhancement of the quantum Fisher information (QFI). Remarkably, this enhancement may be achieved at fixed total sensing time and energy cost. The effect is quantified by the Wigner-Yanase skew information, which measures noncommutativity and exhibits the same critical scaling as the QFI. We demonstrate effective use of CANP in the quantum Rabi and Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick models. Our results establish CANP as a robust technique to effectively implement criticality-enhanced quantum metrology.

Abstract

We identify anomalous scale-free localization and the associated impurity-induced loss bursts in a non-Hermitian dissipative cross-stitch lattice. By a local basis rotation, the model is mapped onto an effective non-Hermitian Su-Schrieffer-Heeger lattice, where local impurities act as tunable effective boundaries. For the parameter choice considered here, tuning the impurity strength $η$ connects two effective open-boundary-condition-like limits, reached for $η\to0$ and $η\to\infty$, through generalized-boundary-condition regimes and the impurity-free periodic-boundary-condition point at $η=1$. For finite $η\notin\{0,1\}$, the spectral loops remain separated from the real-energy axis, while the eigenstates exhibit scale-free localization pinned by the impurity. Unlike conventional impurity-induced scale-free localization, the Lyapunov exponent depends explicitly on the eigenenergy, making the localization strength eigenstate dependent. We further show that this anomalous eigenmode structure produces an impurity-induced loss burst: the long-time integrated dissipation probability is strongly enhanced near an impurity-generated effective boundary even when the initial wave packet is far away. In the single-impurity case, the burst region consists of the impurity site and its adjacent effective-boundary site, and the effect occurs without imaginary-gap closing. For multiple impurities, local burst regions emerge around all impurities, while the dominant burst boundary is selected by the initial wave-packet position and the nonreciprocal drift direction. These results connect anomalous scale-free localization with controllable dissipation dynamics in non-Hermitian lattices.

Abstract

There is a growing consensus that large-scale, fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC) necessitates high-fidelity photonic interconnects to overcome the scaling limits of monolithic architectures. However, most current platforms were not originally designed for native photonic connectivity and require significant engineering overhead. To overcome these fundamental hardware limitations, we recently introduced a rationally designed organic molecule that serves as an ideal quantum node, featuring a robust qubit-photon interface (QPI) and a long-lived nuclear-spin register. In this work, we present PIQC (Photonic Integrated Quantum Circuits), a distributed architecture designed to scale these molecular nodes into a functional quantum computer. The PIQC framework integrates five mutually reinforcing innovations: (i) Designer molecular qubits, i.e. carbene molecules in an isosteric host that provide millisecond-coherence electron spins with high spectral stability and spin-dependent optical emission, (ii) deterministic nuclear registers made of synthetically placed $^{13}$C or $^{14}$N labels that enable fast ($\sim 1~μ$s), high-fidelity electron-nuclear gates, (iii) hybrid photonic integration, which allows molecular films to seamlessly integrate with existing mature fabrication technologies, e.g. thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN), (iv) heralded entanglement protocols that can tolerate up to 70% photon loss, and (v) stairway Floquetification, i.e. high-rate quantum low-density parity-check (qLDPC) codes that are converted into Floquet codes, reducing syndrome extraction to weight-two Bell-pair measurements that match PIQC's networked hardware. PIQC offers a hardware-efficient, commercially viable pathway toward a utility-scale quantum computer based on distributed FTQC.
