Full Quantum and Mixed Quantum--Classical Dynamics of Hot Exciton Cooling in Semiconductor Nanocrystals

Authors: Bokang Hou, Johan E. Runeson, Samuel L. Rudge, Salvatore Gatto, Hans-Dieter Meyer, Michael Thoss, Eran Rabani
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27708v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This study benchmarks various quantum and mixed quantum-classical methods against exact dynamics to study hot-exciton cooling in nanocrystals. It reveals that the initial ultrafast cooling is caused by rapid state mixing from low-frequency phonons rather than traditional energy relaxation. The results identify MASH as a highly reliable approximation for these complex vibronic systems.

Why it may be interesting. The paper provides a rigorous benchmark for non-adiabatic dynamics in the intermediate-coupling regime, specifically addressing how thermal fluctuations of couplings drive rapid dephasing/mixing, which is highly relevant to open quantum systems and many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Assessing the accuracy of various perturbative and mixed quantum-classical dynamical methods for describing hot-exciton relaxation and cooling in semiconductor nanocrystals.

Main result. The MASH (Mapping Approach to Surface Hopping) method provides the most consistent agreement with exact quantum benchmarks, while the ultrafast cooling component is identified as rapid diabatic state mixing driven by low-frequency phonons.

Method. Benchmarking various approximations (QME, Ehrenfest, Spin-LSC, FSSH, MASH) against numerically exact ML-MCTDH dynamics and PIMC equilibrium populations.

Model / system. Atomistically parameterized models of CdSe core and CdSe/CdS core-shell nanocrystals using a linear vibronic coupling Hamiltonian and semi-empirical pseudopotentials.

Key observables. Time-dependent average exciton energy, diabatic and adiabatic populations, and exciton-phonon spectral densities.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (300 K), nanocrystal size (3 nm), and the separation of timescales between ultrafast (~10 fs) and slow (~100 fs) relaxation.

Assumptions / limitations. Linear vibronic coupling (first-order expansion), static screening approximation for the Bethe-Salpeter equation, and use of the harmonic approximation for the nuclear Hamiltonian.

Figures summary. Fig 1: Exciton energies and spectral densities for CdSe and CdSe/CdS. Fig 2: Real-time diabatic population dynamics comparison. Fig 3: State-resolved diabatic equilibrium populations. Fig 5: Exciton energy relaxation over time.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the physical problem of exciton cooling, defines the atomistic models and Hamiltonian, details the benchmark and approximate dynamical methods, presents comparative results for population dynamics and energy relaxation, and concludes with mechanistic insights.

Abstract

Hot-exciton relaxation in semiconductor nanocrystals (NCs) is often described using perturbative theories, but their accuracy is difficult to assess for realistic exciton--phonon Hamiltonians. Here, we benchmark the perturbative quantum master equation (QME) and several mixed quantum--classical (MQC) methods against fully quantum mechanical dynamics. Using atomistically parameterized models for CdSe core and CdSe/CdS core--shell NCs, we find that bare CdSe exhibits an ultrafast initial decay followed by slower cooling, whereas the core--shell system is dominated by the slower component. Analysis of reduced models shows that the ultrafast component arises from rapid diabatic state mixing driven by thermal fluctuations of low-frequency phonons, rather than from nuclear-assisted energy relaxation. The QME captures the initial fast decay but can fail for the slower relaxation in the diabatic representation, while the mapping approach to surface hopping (MASH) gives the most consistent agreement with both benchmark dynamics and equilibrium populations. These results establish a benchmark for exciton-cooling dynamics in NCs and clarify the physical regimes in which widely used approximate methods are reliable.

Substrate-driven topological engineering in plasmonic Su-Schrieffer-Heeger chains

Authors: Florian Herz, Alireza Naeimi, Svend-Age Biehs
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28463v1

Summary. The paper demonstrates that the topological properties of a plasmonic SSH chain can be controlled by its interaction with a substrate. By tuning the distance to the substrate or the substrate's doping, one can induce topological phase transitions and create protected edge modes. These findings also show that such topological features can be used to modulate spectral radiative heat transfer.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems as it demonstrates how controlling the external electromagnetic environment (the substrate) can be used to manipulate topological edge states and near-field energy transport.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how the electromagnetic environment of a substrate can be used to engineer the topological band structure and induce topological phase transitions in a plasmonic Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) chain.

Main result. The substrate interaction introduces two mechanisms—long-range band hybridization and short-range band touching—that can trigger topological phase transitions and enable the emergence of protected edge modes even in previously trivial regimes.

Method. The study uses a Green's function approach within the dipole approximation and the quasistatic limit, applying Bloch's theorem and calculating the Zak phase and Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR).

Model / system. A bipartite array of N identical InSb nanoparticles arranged in an SSH chain, placed at a distance z from a semi-infinite InSb planar substrate.

Key observables. Band structure, Zak phase, Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR), polarization (in-plane vs. out-of-plane), and spectral radiative heat flux.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimerization parameter (beta), chain-substrate distance (z), substrate carrier density (n_sub), and disorder strength (rho).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is modeled using the dipole approximation in the quasistatic limit, and higher-order multipole moments are neglected.

Figures summary. Fig 1: System schematic; Fig 2: Eigenfrequencies and IPR; Fig 3: Infinite chain band structure and SPP interaction; Fig 4: Unit cell polarization; Fig 5: Nearest-neighbor approximation comparison; Fig 8: Eigenfrequencies/IPR vs distance; Fig 9: IP band structure; Fig 10-11: Band structure under disorder; Fig 12-14: Spectral radiative heat flux and robustness analysis.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the plasmonic SSH system, details the mathematical framework for substrate-induced interactions, analyzes the two distinct topological engineering mechanisms (hybridization and band touching), evaluates the robustness of edge modes against structural disorder, and concludes with the implications for near-field radiative heat transfer.

Abstract

We demonstrate the possibility of engineering the topological band structure of a plasmonic Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) chain through the interaction with its electromagnetic environment. We find that the long-range interaction of the in-plane modes of the SSH chain with the surface plasmon polaritons of a planar substrate introduces a band hybridization connected to a change of the Zak phase. On the other hand, the short-range interaction with the substrate introduces a band touching, again with a change in the Zak phase. Surprisingly, this second mechanism enables the emergence of topologically protected edge modes for parameters which correspond to the topologically trivial phase for an isolated plasmonic SSH chain. We study these mechanisms by changing the chain-substrate distance and the dimerization parameter. Finally, we discuss the robustness against disorder and, as one example, the impact of the observed effects on the near-field radiative heat transfer along the chain. Our findings pave the way to the engineering of edge modes in plasmonic topological configurations via the coupling to a plasmonic environment.

Unity-order coupling between free electrons and multiphoton waveguided Fock states

Authors: L. Prelat, S. Abdullah, C. I. Velasco, F. J. García de Abajo
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28383v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · interference shaping light 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes using electrostatic fields to steer electron beams into grazing trajectories near silicon waveguides. This technique allows for tunable control over the interaction, significantly increasing the number of photons generated per electron while suppressing unwanted energy losses.

Why it may be interesting. The work demonstrates a method for generating tunable, high-yield multiphoton Fock states and entangled electron-photon states, which is highly relevant for quantum nanophotonics and quantum light-matter interfaces.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The coupling between free electron beams and waveguided optical modes is typically limited by short interaction times and a lack of spectral selectivity due to fixed straight-line trajectories.

Main result. Electrostatic steering of electron trajectories creates a tunable turning point that enhances coupling strength, enables modal selectivity, and can produce high photon yields exceeding ten photons per electron.

Method. The authors use the Boundary Element Method (BEM) to calculate electrostatic potentials and the local-response approximation to determine momentum-resolved Electron Energy-Loss Spectroscopy (EELS) probabilities.

Model / system. A system consisting of a 100-200 keV electron beam interacting with a rectangular silicon waveguide, utilizing either vertical or lateral electrostatic gating to deflect the electron trajectory.

Key observables. EELS probability (energy-loss probability), photon yield per electron, and modal selectivity (TE vs. TM modes).

Important parameters / regimes. Electron kinetic energy (100-200 keV), impact parameter (b), electron incidence angle, and applied electrostatic bias voltage.

Assumptions / limitations. The silicon waveguide is modeled as a Perfect Electric Conductor (PEC) for DC potential calculations, and the electron velocity is assumed to be nearly constant during the interaction.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the electron-waveguide geometry and EELS probability for different impact parameters; Figure 3 illustrates the lateral gating configuration and photon yield vs. incidence angle; Figures 5-7 detail the EELS spectrum, decay of probability with distance, and the electrostatic potential distributions for various gating geometries.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of limited electron-waveguide coupling, proposes electrostatic steering as a solution, details the theoretical framework and numerical methods (BEM/EELS), compares different gating configurations (planar vs. lateral), and quantifies the resulting photon yield and tunability.

Abstract

Electron beams enable highly localized near-field excitation of waveguided optical modes, yet their coupling is typically limited by short interaction times along straight-line trajectories with fixed impact parameters. Here, we theoretically demonstrate that electrostatic steering overcomes this limitation by introducing a tunable turning point in grazing electron trajectories, thus controlling the minimum electron--waveguide separation and producing strong coupling to waveguided modes. Specifically, we consider a biased rectangular silicon waveguide, where a repulsive static field deflects a grazing electron. In this configuration, the electron turning point governs both the coupling strength and the modal selectivity, which can be dynamically tuned through the electron incidence angle and the applied bias. In addition, the aloof electron--waveguide interaction suppresses lossy high-energy channels (e.g., above the silicon band gap) while preserving substantial excitation of the targeted waveguided modes. Using a practical biasing configuration and 100~keV electrons, we predict an average yield exceeding ten photons per electron, with voltage-tunable control of the interaction. Our results establish electrostatic steering as a practical route for engineering and enhancing free-electron coupling to waveguided photonic modes.

A supersymmetric study of charge and spin transport in Weyl semimetals under axionic electrodynamic response

Authors: J. C. Pérez-Pedraza, A. Martín-Ruiz, L. F. Urrutia
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27657v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper explores how the interplay between axionic electrodynamics and supersymmetry governs transport in Weyl semimetals. By using supersymmetric quantum mechanics, the authors solve the Dirac equation to reveal how magnetic and electric fields induce chiral imbalances and specific spin currents. The results provide a theoretical framework for understanding the effective field theory of Weyl semimetals under external magnetic influence.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the charge and spin transport properties of Weyl semimetals under an external magnetic field, specifically focusing on the effects of axionic electrodynamic response.

Main result. The study demonstrates that the system's dynamics separate into a magnetic sector with supersymmetric factorization and an electric sector with PT-supersymmetric structure, leading to a chiral imbalance and specific spin-current patterns.

Method. The authors use a low-energy effective Dirac-Weyl theory, applying chiral transformations, supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM), and solving the resulting equations using tri-confluent Heun functions and numerical methods.

Model / system. A Weyl semimetal (WSM) modeled as a plane slab of thickness 2L in a uniform external magnetic field, characterized by axion electrodynamics where the axial term is replaced by an effective electromagnetic response.

Key observables. Chiral projections of probability and current densities, spin density, spin currents, and axial probability density.

Important parameters / regimes. Fermi velocity (vF), axial 4-vector (b), magnetic field (B0), slab thickness (L), and the axion-like field theta(x).

Assumptions / limitations. Low-energy approximation near Weyl nodes, neglect of lattice regularization and band curvature, and a one-particle domain where the axial anomaly is absent.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of the WSM slab; Figures 5-6 show energy eigenvalues as a function of quantum numbers and magnetic field; Figures 7-10 display probability and current densities for different chiralities.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the WSM Hamiltonian and applying a chiral transformation to derive axion electrodynamics, then proceeds to decouple the Dirac equation into magnetic and electric sectors, solves these sectors using SUSY and PT-SUSY techniques (including Heun functions), and concludes with the calculation of transport observables and conservation laws.

Abstract

We investigate the charge and spin transport properties of a Weyl semimetal under an external magnetic field using a low-energy effective theory. By performing a chiral transformation, we remove the axial term from the fermionic Lagrangian, while its physical effects are retained through the system's electromagnetic response. This response, derived from integrating out the fermionic degrees of freedom, takes the form of axionic electrodynamics and enters the Dirac equation via minimal coupling. The resulting dynamics separate naturally into magnetic and electric components: the magnetic sector admits a supersymmetric factorization, while the electric part exhibits a PT-supersymmetric structure. Robin boundary conditions are applied to the spinor, setting the energy spectrum and defining exact spinor solutions. We obtain the chiral projections of probability and current densities, exhibiting a chiral imbalance in the material. We show that in the x-direction, only one chirality contributes to the chiral current ($j^x_l = 0$). Spin density and currents where also computed. Our results demonstrate how the interplay between axionic response and supersymmetry governs transport phenomena in our specific setup, offering novel insights into the effective field theory description of Weyl semimetals.

Magneto-Optical Detection of Anisotropic Spin Currents in Altermagnetic RuO2

Summary. This paper provides experimental evidence for the altermagnetic spin-splitter effect in RuO2 thin films using magneto-optical probes. By demonstrating directionally anisotropic spin accumulation, the study establishes RuO2 as a promising material for field-free spintronic devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to determine if metallic RuO2 sustains robust altermagnetic order in films thicker than the ultrathin limit and to distinguish the intrinsic altermagnetic spin-splitter effect (ASSE) from extrinsic mechanisms like the Spin Hall Effect or Rashba-type splitting.

Main result. The researchers experimentally demonstrate directionally anisotropic spin accumulation in 12-nm RuO2 films, confirming the presence of the altermagnetic spin-splitter effect and establishing RuO2 as an efficient, symmetry-controlled spin source.

Method. The study combines polarization-resolved Second-Harmonic Generation (SHG) and high-sensitivity polar Magneto-Optical Kerr Effect (MOKE) with 4D-STEM structural analysis and Kubo-formalism-based electronic structure calculations.

Model / system. The physical system is a 12-nm-thick (1-01)-oriented RuO2 thin film grown on r-plane sapphire via molecular beam epitaxy, characterized by altermagnetic spin-split electronic bands and Néel order.

Key observables. SHG intensity and asymmetry, MOKE rotation (Kerr rotation), spin Hall angle, and local lattice strain.

Important parameters / regimes. 12-nm film thickness, Néel temperature (T_N ~ 500 K), current densities up to 6 x 10^6 A/cm^2, and MOKE sensitivity of ~10 nrad.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that the observed optical signals are intrinsic to the altermagnetic spin accumulation and not artifacts of Joule heating or thermal gradients.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the crystallographic/magnetic structure, spin-split Fermi surfaces, and optical probing geometry; Figure 3 involves 4D-STEM analysis of structural clusters and strain mapping.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the altermagnetic properties of RuO2, details the experimental setup for optical and structural characterization, presents the symmetry-driven anisotropic spin response, discusses the role of strain and structural heterogeneity, and concludes with the implications for spintronics.

Abstract

Altermagnets are a recently identified class of collinear antiferromagnets that host large spin-split electronic bands, offering a promising platform for efficient spin-current generation. Among proposed candidates, the metallic oxide RuO2 is predicted to exhibit strong altermagnetic spin splitting; however, whether it sustains robust magnetic order beyond the ultrathin thickness limit remains unresolved. Here, we employ optical probes to investigate charge-to-spin conversion in a 12-nm-thick (101)-oriented RuO2 film grown on sapphire. Polarization-resolved second-harmonic generation reveals nonlinear optical responses consistent with the surface symmetry and Néel order of RuO2. Under an applied current, both second-harmonic generation and polar magneto-optical Kerr effect measurements detect a pronounced, directionally anisotropic spin polarization, exhibiting enhanced signals for current along [010] and strongly suppressed responses for current along [-101], in agreement with the symmetry of the altermagnetic spin-splitter effect. Non-magnetic or Rashba-type mechanisms cannot explain this symmetry-selective response. Scanning transmission electron microscopy further reveals that substantial strain persists even in relatively thick films, providing a possible explanation for the observed behavior. Therefore, these results establish RuO2 as an efficient spin source and demonstrate the potential of altermagnets for field-free spintronic devices.

Quantum anomalous Hall effect in chiral semimetals

Authors: Peng-Yi Liu, Yu-Hao Wan, Qing-Feng Sun
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27826v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates that the Quantum Anomalous Hall effect is not restricted to insulators and can exist in chiral semimetals. It shows that while the Hall response remains quantized, the longitudinal conductivity stays finite, creating a unique 'dual-channel' transport regime. This discovery extends the fundamental understanding of topological transport in gapless systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether the Quantum Anomalous Hall (QAH) effect can persist in chiral semimetals where conduction and valence bands touch at zero energy, and identifying its unique transport signatures.

Main result. The Hall conductivity remains quantized even in the semimetallic regime due to the cancellation of Berry curvature at specific valleys, while the longitudinal conductivity remains finite and exhibits semimetallic behavior.

Method. Nonequilibrium Green's function (NEGF) calculations using a six-terminal Hall bar geometry and Büttiker virtual leads to simulate dephasing.

Model / system. A 2D chiral semimetal described by a two-band tight-binding model on a square lattice, featuring massive Dirac fermions at Gamma and M points and gapless cones at X and Y points.

Key observables. Hall conductivity (sigma_xy), longitudinal conductivity (sigma_xx), Hall resistivity (rho_xy), and equilibrium current density (Jy).

Important parameters / regimes. Fermi energy (Ef), dephasing strength (Gamma_d), mass term (m), and device dimensions (Lx, Ly).

Assumptions / limitations. The system is modeled using a clean tight-binding Hamiltonian with dephasing introduced via virtual leads to simulate realistic experimental conditions.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the 2D band structure and nanoribbon spatial distribution; Fig 2 displays the Hall bar setup and transport results (sigma_xx, sigma_xy vs Ef); Fig 4 shows the Hall response and current decay; Fig 5 analyzes the dependence of conductivities on dephasing, device size, and probe distance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of QAH in semimetals, presents the tight-binding model and band structure, details the NEGF transport simulation setup, analyzes the quantization and semimetallic signatures, explores the semimetal-to-insulator transition, and discusses the role of dephasing and device geometry.

Abstract

The quantum anomalous Hall (QAH) effect is conventionally understood to exist only in Chern insulators, while a recent study has shown that ferromagnetic metals can also host the QAH effect. Between insulators and metals, we demonstrate that QAH can persist even in a chiral semimetal, where conduction and valence bands touch at zero energy. Transport calculations demonstrate that the Hall conductivity of such a system can be quantized in the presence of dephasing. Interestingly, its longitudinal conductivity remains finite and exhibits semimetallic behavior, in contrast to Chern insulators. This unusual transport behavior originates from the quantization of the Berry curvature integral over occupied states and the semimetallic band structure. This chiral semimetal can transition into a Chern insulator, accompanied by the vanishing of longitudinal conductivity and a reduction of the intrinsic length scale of the Hall response. Our results extend the concept of QAH and uncover the semimetallic QAH transport signatures.

Taming quantum interference: a route to high electrical conductance in carbon nanotube assemblies

Authors: Teresa Kulka, Agnieszka E. Lekawa-Raus, John S. Bulmer, Krzysztof Koziol, Fedor F. Balakirev, Irina V. Lebedeva, Jacek A. Majewski, Magdalena Marganska, Karolina Z. Milowska
Type: both · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28250v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper investigates how quantum interference at the junctions between carbon nanotubes affects the overall electrical conductivity of nanotube networks. By combining atomistic simulations with high-field experiments, the authors show that complex multi-walled structures can suffer from interference-induced conductance suppression, providing a blueprint for designing more stable and conductive CNT-based electronics.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding and controlling quantum interference at inter-nanotube junctions to achieve high electrical conductance and field stability in carbon nanotube (CNT) assemblies.

Main result. The study identifies that multi-walled CNT architectures introduce complex, field-sensitive interference that reduces conductance, whereas SWCNT junctions can be tuned via overlap length and magnetic fields to achieve high-transmission 'gateway' states.

Method. The work combines atomistic tight-binding non-equilibrium Green's-function (NEGF) simulations and analytical waveguide models with experimental magnetotransport measurements on CNT fibers using pulsed magnetic fields up to 60 T.

Model / system. The physical systems include SWCNT, DWCNT, and TWCNT junctions and fibers. Theoretical models include an atomistic tight-binding Hamiltonian with Peierls substitution for magnetic fields and a minimal 1D atomic chain model.

Key observables. Electrical conductance (G), specific conductivity (normalized by linear density), magnetoresistance (MR), and transmission amplitude (T).

Important parameters / regimes. Nanotube diameter, chirality, overlap length, doping, perpendicular magnetic field strength, and inter-tube coupling.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes current injection is primarily through outer shells in MWCNTs; neglects inelastic processes like electron-phonon scattering; assumes magnetic fields are present only in the scattering region.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate junction architectures (SWCNT, DWCNT, TWCNT), transport regimes in atomic chains (standing waves, gateway states), and transmission maps/spectra as a function of energy and magnetic field.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of junction-governed transport, presents theoretical models (atomic chains and waveguide analogy), details the atomistic NEGF simulations, compares these to experimental magnetotransport data in CNT fibers, and concludes with design principles for high-conductance conductors.

Abstract

Miniaturized electronics require lightweight conductors that maintain high conductance under demanding conditions. CNT networks are promising candidates, but their transport is governed by inter-nanotube junctions where electron waves interfere. Controlling this interference requires understanding how junction architecture shapes transmission. We explore coherent transport through experimentally relevant junctions, from single and multiple single-walled CNT (SWCNT) contacts to double-walled CNT (DWCNT) and triple-walled CNT (TWCNT) junctions, with atomistic tight-binding non-equilibrium Green's-function calculations, also under a perpendicular magnetic field. We use analytically solvable minimal models to identify transport regimes expected for quasi-1D nanoscale junctions, and an electron-waveguide picture to interpret their CNT-specific manifestations. For single SWCNT--SWCNT junctions, high-transmission windows are set mainly by overlap length, doping and magnetic field. Gateway states can enhance conductance when some CNT subbands are gapped, and in some cases a magnetic field can restore transmission by lifting an interference blockade. In more complex architectures, added paths become selective: multi-junctions generate resonant filtering, while additional walls redistribute transmission instead of acting as independent channels. DWCNT junctions remain outer-wall dominated and SWCNT-like, whereas TWCNT junctions become genuinely multi-channel and more field-sensitive. This explains the lower, more field-sensitive conductance of multi-walled CNT (MWCNT) fibres, in accord with our ultrahigh-field measurements on SWCNT and MWCNT fibres. Ultimately, this work turns microscopic interference mechanisms into design principles for high-conductance, field-stable CNT conductors.

A GPU-based Solver for Polarization Dynamics in Ferroelectric Materials

Authors: Ali Hasan, Edoardo Piccolo, Anna Giordano, Natalya Fedorova, Jorge Íñiguez-González, Davi Rodrigues, Giovanni Finocchio
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28800v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces a new GPU-accelerated numerical solver, PETASPIN_microelectrics, designed for large-scale, high-fidelity simulations of 3D polarization dynamics in ferroelectric materials. It overcomes the limitations of previous CPU-based and uniaxial models, enabling the study of complex topological textures like skyrmions and frequency-dependent hysteresis. This tool is essential for the predictive design of next-generation ferroelectric devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing CPU-based ferroelectric solvers are computationally expensive for large-scale 3D simulations and often rely on simplified uniaxial models that cannot capture complex 3D structures like polarization rotation or topological textures.

Main result. The development of PETASPIN_microelectrics, a fully GPU-acceler_rated, scalable solver that accurately reproduces temperature-driven phase transitions in BaTiO3 and demonstrates the stabilization of 3D hybrid skyrmions in PbTiO3/SrTiO3 bilayers.

Method. A GPU-native phase-field approach using CUDA C/C++ and the Thrust library, employing the finite-difference method and NVIDIA's CUDA FFT library to solve the time-dependent Ginzburg-Landau equations.

Model / system. The model uses the Time-Dependent Ginzburg-Landau (TDGL) formalism applied to perovskite oxides like BaTiO3, PbTiO3, and SrTiO3, incorporating Landau, gradient, and electrostatic energy contributions.

Key observables. Polarization vector field (magnitude and rotation), domain wall profiles, hysteresis loops, electrostatic fields, and topological textures (skyrmions/vortices).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (0-360 K), external electric field frequency, epitaxial strain parameter (rs), Landau and gradient coefficients, and background permittivity.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is modeled using the TDGL formalism; finite samples are assumed to be embedded in vacuum (P=0 outside); the Landau expansion is applied to prototypical perovskites.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the effect of Landau coefficients on 180-degree domain wall profiles; Figure 3(b) illustrates the transition from vortex tubes to stabilized skyrmions via strain tuning.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational bottleneck of existing solvers, presents the architecture and implementation of the new GPU solver, validates the solver against analytical domain wall profiles and phase transitions, and demonstrates advanced applications in strain-controlled skyrmion stabilization.

Abstract

Ferroelectric materials can be used for the development of multiple device concepts combining non-volatility, small dimensions, low-power actuation, and electrical tunability. Such development demands efficient and precise design of simulation tools describing the polarization texture. However, most existing ferroelectric solvers are CPU-based and rely on simplified electrostatic treatments and reduced-dimensional representations of the polarization field. These approximations limit their ability to capture finite-size and boundary effects and restrict the range of domain structures and domain walls that can be realistically simulated. Here, we present a fully GPU (graphics processing units)-accelerated and scalable numerical solver, named PETASPIN_microelectrics, for computing the full polarization vector field of ferroelectric systems using the Ginzburg-Landau formalism. Our solver incorporates an optimized and validated calculation of the full electrostatic field and enables the parallel execution of multiple simulations. We systematically validated the solver with several benchmark problems, including phase transitions in BaTiO3 and ferroelectric domain wall profiles. Our simulations reproduce temperature-driven hysteretic phase transitions in BaTiO3. We also reproduce hysteresis loops and demonstrate stabilization of a three-dimensional hybrid skyrmion in a PbTiO3/SrTiO3 bilayer system. Our results show quantitative agreement with predictions from an analytical theory and prior experimental studies. The proposed solver provides an efficient, accurate platform for large-scale simulations of ferroelectric materials including stabilization of topological textures supporting predictive modeling for next-generation of ferroelectric device design.
