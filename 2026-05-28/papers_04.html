Odd spin symmetry and anisotropy switching in p-wave magnet CeNiAsO

Authors: Fayuan Zhang, Huaxun Li, Xingkai Cheng, Yibo Fan, Yifan Yin, Yifan Gao, Zhanfeng Liu, Shengtao Cui, Zhouyi Yin, Yue Zhao, Junhao Lin, Zhengtai Liu, Mao Ye, Yaobo Huang, Shan Qiao, Wu Xie, Ping Miao, Hao Wu, Junwei Liu, Guanghan Cao, Chaoyu Chen
Type: both · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28701v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides the first experimental evidence of p-wave spin symmetry in the odd-parity magnet CeNiAsO. By using potassium deposition to reveal intrinsic bulk bands, the authors demonstrate that magnetic domains can be manipulated to switch resistance anisotropy, suggesting high potential for antiferromagnetic spintronic memory devices.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The experimental verification of intrinsic p-wave spin symmetry in odd-parity magnets and the connection between this symmetry and observed anisotropic transport properties.

Main result. The authors experimentally demonstrate p-wave spin symmetry in CeNiAsO using spin-resolved ARPES and show that magnetic domain selection can switch the material between high- and low-resistance states.

Method. The study combines spin-resolved ARPES, STM, and transport measurements on single crystals and FIB-fabricated devices, supported by first-principles DFT calculations and in situ potassium deposition to remove surface states.

Model / system. The system is CeNiAsO, an antiferromagnetic Kondo metal with a layered tetragonal structure and p-wave magnetic order characterized by a non-relativistic spin group symmetry C4z^t.

Key observables. Spin polarization (asymmetry in EDCs), resistance anisotropy, magnetoresistance ratio, and Fermi surface topology.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic transition temperatures (TN1 approx 7.4 K, TN2 approx 9.4 K), magnetic field up to 9 T, and temperature regimes down to 2 K.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that potassium deposition effectively compensates for polar surface states to reveal the intrinsic bulk band structure.

Figures summary. Figures show XRD and EDS for composition, STM for surface termination identification, ARPES for Fermi surface and band spectra, and transport data showing resistance anisotropy and domain switching.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the importance of odd-parity magnets, details the structural and magnetic characterization of CeNiAsO, presents ARPES results showing p-wave symmetry after surface modification, demonstrates resistance anisotropy switching via magnetic field, and concludes with the implications for spintronics and superconductivity.

Abstract

Odd-parity magnets, complementary to altermagnets, exhibit unique properties such as high efficiency in charge-spin conversion and compatibility with conventional superconductivity, of critical importance in the pursuit of energy-efficient spintronics and topological superconductors for quantum computation. For even-parity d-wave and g-wave altermagnets, the magnetic structure, spin-split band structure and physical properties are currently under intensive study. On the contrary, while hundreds of odd-parity magnets and the promising properties have been predicted in theory, experimental studies are scarce. Specifically, the magnetic structure and transport properties of candidates NiI2 and Ga3Ru4Al12 have been reported, yet the characteristic band structure and particularly the odd-parity spin symmetry remain elusive. Here we demonstrate experimentally the deterministic p-wave spin symmetry and resistance anisotropy switching for the prototype odd-parity magnet, CeNiAsO. Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) reveals two cleaved terminations with distinct surface band structure. By compensating the polar surface, we achieve intrinsic bulk band structure, for which the spin splitting can be well described by the p-wave magnetic structure through first-principles calculation. The bulk spin polarization measured by spin-resolved ARPES exhibits symmetry with only one degenerate plane, fingerprint of p-wave magnetism. We further demonstrate giant resistance anisotropy and switching between high-resistance and low-resistance states through modest field-induced domain selection, highlighting its potential for antiferromagnetic spin memory devices. The structural similarity between CeNiAsO and 1111-type Fe-based superconductors stimulates further exploration on the interplay between p-wave magnetism, superconductivity and band topology.

Problem-Specific Basis Quantum State Readout via Proper Orthogonal Decomposition

Authors: Kota Ichiki, Xinchi Huang, Gekko Budiutama, Masari Watanabe, Yoshifumi Kawada, Ryunosuke Terasawa, Hirofumi Nishi, Takayuki Suzuki, Nagai Ryutaro, Yu-ichiro Matsushita

Summary. This paper proposes a problem-specific readout method for quantum PDE solvers that uses classical precomputation to create an efficient basis for reconstruction. By projecting quantum states onto a reduced POD basis, the method overcomes the scaling bottlenecks of standard readout techniques in fluid dynamics simulations.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The readout and reconstruction of classical solutions from quantum states in PDE solvers is a significant bottleneck, as conventional methods like Real-Space Readout scale poorly with grid size.

Main result. The proposed PODR method significantly reduces the number of measurements and computational resources in the online stage, achieving superior shot efficiency and reconstruction fidelity compared to RSR and FSR.

Method. A two-stage approach (offline/online) using Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) to precompute a reduced basis from classical snapshots, which is then encoded into quantum circuits using Matrix Product States (MPS) and measured via the Hadamard test.

Model / system. The method is applied to Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) benchmark problems, specifically 2D steady lid-driven cavity flow and 2D Kármán vortex street.

Key observables. Velocity fields (u_x, u_y), stream functions, and projection/encoding error estimators.

Important parameters / regimes. Grid size (N), number of POD bases (n_b), bond dimension (chi), Reynolds number (Re), and target error tolerance (epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. The solution state is well-represented by the classical snapshot ensemble, and the offline stage is performed only once for a specific problem class.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the PODR workflow and comparison to RSR; Figure 2 plots readout error vs. shots; Figure 3 shows circuit depth scaling; Figures 4-5 visualize reconstructed velocity fields; Figures 8-9 validate error estimators; Figure 11 compares shot efficiency across methods.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the readout bottleneck, details the PODR offline/online workflow, provides a mathematical error decomposition, presents complexity analysis, validates the method using CFD benchmarks, and concludes with error estimator validation.

Abstract

Quantum computing is a promising technology for accelerating partial differential equation solvers applied to large-scale real-world problems. However, reconstructing a classical representation of the solution from the quantum state remains a significant bottleneck. We propose a problem-specific method, called proper orthogonal decomposition-based readout (PODR), to improve readout efficiency by precomputing characteristic features of the solution. The present method consists of an offline stage and an online stage. In the offline stage, a set of basis functions representing the dominant features of the target problem is constructed from representative solution data using classical computations. In the online stage, the quantum state is projected onto this reduced basis, and only the minimal set of weight coefficients is extracted to reconstruct the solution. Since the offline stage is carried out only once, the proposed PODR method is especially advantageous for simulations with varying parameters, which are common in computational fluid dynamics (CFD). Futhermore, we apply the proposed method to benchmark problems in fluid dynamics and demonstrate that PODR significantly reduces both the number of measurements and the computational resources in the online stage compared with conventional readout methods.

Quantum geometry of connected state manifolds: When diabolic points act as bridges between eigenstate manifolds

Authors: Jan Střeleček, Jakub Novotný, Pavel Cejnar
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28351v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. This paper introduces a new geometric framework called the Connected State Manifold to treat energy level crossings as bridges rather than boundaries. By regularizing the metric at these points, the authors show that one can find shorter, more stable paths (geodesics) and compute Berry phases even when passing directly through degeneracies.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum control and adiabatic computing, as it provides a way to navigate through degeneracies and compute geometric phases for paths that traverse diabolic points.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The divergence of the Provost-Vallee metric and numerical instabilities at diabolic points (DPs) prevent a continuous geometric description of eigenstate manifolds.

Main result. The authors develop a 'Connected State Manifold' (CSM) formalism where DPs act as bridges to glue adjacent manifolds, regularizing the metric and enabling stable computation of geodesics and Berry phases.

Method. The study uses coordinate transformations (stretched coordinates) to regularize singularities, analytic continuation across DP horizons, and second-order perturbation theory to analyze multi-level systems.

Model / system. The framework applies to N-level parametric Hermitian Hamiltonians, specifically focusing on 3-level systems with effective PT-symmetry, such as those inspired by NV centers in diamond.

Key observables. Quantum Geometric Tensor (QGT), Provost-Vallee metric, Berry phase, and geodesics.

Important parameters / regimes. Diabolic points (DPs), spectral gap, radial and angular coordinates (r, phi), and the DP horizon.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes DPs have codimension 2, focuses on systems with effective PT-symmetry (antiunitary symmetry), and assumes no three levels cross at the same point.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows 3D spectra and metric divergence/regularization; Figure 2 illustrates coordinate transformations and path continuation; Figure 4 displays the topological genus and graph representation of CSMs; Figure 9 compares unstable single-manifold geodesics with stable CSM geodesics.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of metric singularities at DPs, proposes the CSM formalism via coordinate regularization, analyzes the local geometry of multi-level systems using perturbation theory, characterizes the topology and nodal lines of the CSM, and demonstrates the stability and efficiency of geodesics within this new framework.

Abstract

Parametric Hamiltonians often exhibit point-like spectral degeneracies (diabolic points, or conical intersections), which can lead to singularities in the Provost-Vallee metric of eigenstate manifolds. We regularise the metric by a coordinate transformation and develop a formalism in which diabolic points act as bridges between adjacent eigenstate manifolds, glueing them into a single connected state manifold. We characterise the topology of this structure and refine the rules for nodal lines governing the Berry phase. The connected state manifold restores the numerical stability near diabolic points, enlarges the class of geodesics allowing for new geodesic shortcuts, and provides a new mechanism for Berry phase computation, even along paths traversing diabolic points.

Quantum principal component analysis without eigenvector recovery

Authors: Yewei Yuan, Michele Minervini, Mark M. Wilde, Nana Liu
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27942v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a new way to perform Quantum PCA by treating it as a measurement-based scoring task rather than an eigenvector recovery task. By using a smooth Fermi-Dirac filter, the authors bypass the need for expensive iterative computations and enable efficient, training-free processing of quantum data for tasks like anomaly detection.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Traditional Quantum Principal Component Analysis (qPCA) is computationally expensive due to the need for explicit eigenvector recovery and is difficult to implement for quantum-native data that requires centering. The authors aim to develop a framework that performs PCA-like tasks (scoring and filtering) without needing to reconstruct individual eigenvectors.

Main result. The authors introduce a measurement-based 'soft PCA' framework using an entropy-regularized Fermi-Dirac filter that achieves dimension-independent sample complexity for scoring tasks. This framework allows for training-free calibration, where a single fixed quantum circuit can be used to identify optimal thresholds for various rank budgets or variance levels.

Method. The method replaces hard rank-k projectors with a smooth Fermi-Dirac filter derived from an entropy-regularized variational formulation. It utilizes a continuous-variable quantum measurement protocol involving Hamiltonian simulation of the covariance operator and a controlled thermal measurement using a non-Gaussian wavepacket.

Model / system. The system involves quantum feature states representing centered covariance operators. The mathematical model uses a controlled Hamiltonian evolution $U_C = e^{-i\hat{p} \otimes C/T_2}$, where $\hat{p}$ is the momentum quadrature of a control qumode and $C$ is the covariance operator.

Key observables. Principal-subspace scores, spectral-energy profiles, postselected filtered states, and the position quadrature $q$ of the control qumode.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature ($T$) controlling spectral smoothness, rank budget ($k$), retained variance, chemical potential ($\mu$), and the accuracy parameter ($\eta$).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the existence of a centered covariance operator represented by quantum feature states and requires a non-zero eigengap for the zero-temperature limit to recover hard PCA.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of the implementation of the Hamiltonian evolution using a two-substep product formula involving empirical and mean-subtraction steps.

Paper structure. The paper identifies limitations of traditional PCA, proposes an entropy-regularized soft PCA framework, derives the Fermi-Dirac filter, details the quantum implementation via thermal measurement, provides a calibration protocol for various ranks, and performs error and complexity analysis.

Abstract

Principal component analysis (PCA) is traditionally implemented through a covariance or kernel matrix, leading-eigenvector extraction, and hard rank-$k$ projection. These steps can be computationally costly in high-dimensional and quantum-data settings, sensitive to small eigengaps, and unnecessary when downstream tasks only require principal-subspace scores. Such score-based objectives are important in applications such as anomaly detection, spectral-energy profiling, and other postselection tasks. To address these needs, we introduce a measurement-based soft PCA framework replacing the hard top-$k$ projector with an entropy-regularized Fermi--Dirac filter. This filter is the unique optimizer of an entropy-regularized variational formulation of PCA and converges to the classical PCA projector in the zero-temperature limit. This filter has a direct interpretation as a quantum measurement, which naturally suggests a quantum approach. For centered covariance operators represented by quantum feature states, a single fixed circuit, together with threshold calibration, accesses all optimal filters for different rank budgets or retained-variance levels without rank-dependent circuit updates or eigenvector recovery. For new inputs, the same calibrated quantum circuit yields soft principal subspace scores, spectral energy profiles, and postselected filtered states. The required centering of both training and test data is performed coherently inside the quantum protocol, which is particularly important for quantum data where no classical feature vectors or centered Gram matrix are directly available. By reframing PCA as a calibrated measurement task, this framework bypasses the need for iterative eigenvector extraction and achieves a dimension-independent sample complexity $O(η^{-2})$ for normalized fractional-rank or retained variance scoring at additive accuracy $η$.

Spontaneous breaking of non-invertible symmetries and duality to beyond-Landau transitions

Authors: Xie Chen, Shang Liu, Da-chuan Lu, Nathanan Tantivasadakarn
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27672v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper explores how spontaneous symmetry breaking works when the symmetry cannot be inverted, such as in certain fusion categories. It demonstrates that these transitions can be dual to well-known deconfined quantum critical points, providing a new way to study phase transitions beyond the Landau paradigm.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the nature of spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB) when the symmetry involved is non-invertible, specifically looking for differences between these phases and conventional invertible symmetry-breaking phases.

Main result. The authors establish that non-invertible SSB phases can still be characterized by long-range correlations of local order parameters and prove a duality between certain non-invertible symmetry-breaking transitions and deconfined quantum critical points (DQCP) of invertible symmetries.

Method. The study utilizes the Symmetry Topological Field Theory (SymTFT) formalism, anyon condensation, and generalized gauging techniques, supported by exact diagonalization of 1D qubit lattice models.

Model / system. The primary models are 1+1D gapped phases with Rep(H8) symmetry, realized via 1D qubit lattice models (coupled chains) and a 'sandwich' construction involving a 2+1D topological bulk.

Key observables. Local order parameters, ground-state degeneracy, correlation functions, and topological spin.

Important parameters / regimes. The non-invertible Rep(H8) symmetry, the 3-cocycle parameter gamma for the dual D8 symmetry, and the thermodynamic limit of the 1D lattice size.

Assumptions / limitations. The study focuses on 1+1D systems and considers only untwisted local order parameters.

Figures summary. The notes do not provide specific descriptions of figures, though they mention the use of numerical verification for energy gaps and ground-state degeneracy.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of non-invertible SSB, develops the SymTFT and anyon condensation framework, presents concrete 1D lattice models and their Hamiltonian constructions, establishes the duality to DQCP via gauging, and generalizes the results to group-theoretical Hopf algebras.

Abstract

Spontaneous symmetry breaking is a well-understood mechanism for generating distinct phases of matter. Recently, the notion of symmetry has been broadened to include operations without inverses, leading to the concept of non-invertible symmetries. How do symmetry-breaking phases associated with non-invertible symmetries differ from those arising from conventional invertible symmetries? We address this question using concrete lattice models of the gapped phases with non-invertible Rep($H_8$) symmetry as an example. We find that, despite the symmetry being non-invertible, the symmetry-breaking phases can still be characterized by the long-range correlation of local order parameters, which obey a more general algebraic structure than in the invertible setting. Furthermore, via generalized gauging, certain non-invertible symmetry-breaking transitions can be mapped to deconfined quantum critical points of invertible symmetries, and vice versa. We establish precise conditions under which this duality holds and illustrate them with several families of examples, providing a systematic route to studying beyond-Landau phase transitions.

Stabilizer rank bounds for magic-state orbits

Authors: Farrokh Labib, Vincent Russo
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28586v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides tighter bounds on the stabilizer rank of various magic-state orbits for both qubits and qutrits, which is crucial for understanding the complexity of simulating quantum circuits. It introduces a new software library and uses formal verification to ensure the accuracy of the discovered algebraic decompositions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Determining the stabilizer rank and the per-copy asymptotic stabilizer-rank exponent for different Clifford orbits of magic states in qubit and qutrit systems.

Main result. Established new, lower upper bounds for several qutrit magic-state orbits (Strange, Norrell, and Hadamard-eigenstate) and provided a closed-form qubit T-type decomposition. The authors also proved the first nontrivial asymptotic lower bounds for the Hadamard-eigenstate and Norrell orbits.

Method. The authors use simulated-annealing searches via the 'stabrank' library to find stabilizer decompositions, apply a subset-sum argument for lower bounds, and utilize Lean 4 for formal machine-checked verification of the identities.

Model / system. The study focuses on the Clifford + magic-state model of quantum computation, specifically analyzing the stabilizer rank of tensor powers of magic states in qubit (p=2) and qutrit (p=3) systems.

Key observables. Stabilizer rank (chi) and the per-copy asymptotic stabilizer-rank exponent (gamma).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of copies (m), dimension of the system (p=2 or p=3), and the specific Clifford orbits (Strange, Norrell, Hadamard-eigenstate, and T-state).

Assumptions / limitations. The results assume the validity of the Clifford + magic-state model and rely on the algebraic structure of the Clifford group in prime dimensions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the 'stabrank' decomposition pipeline, showing the flow from simulated annealing to numerical, symbolic, and formal verification.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of orbit-dependent stabilizer ranks, presents new upper bounds for qutrit orbits through explicit decompositions, establishes asymptotic lower bounds using subset-sum techniques, provides a new qubit T-type decomposition, and concludes with the introduction of the 'stabrank' software and formal verification results.

Abstract

Distinct Clifford orbits of magic states can exhibit different stabilizer ranks at small tensor powers. We establish this for qutrits, where the single-qutrit Clifford group has four inequivalent orbits of magic states: Strange, Norrell, Hadamard-eigenstate, and the qutrit T-state, but a nontrivial upper bound on the asymptotic exponent had been pinned down for only the qutrit T-state. For the other three orbits we give explicit stabilizer decompositions, yielding upper bounds on the per-copy asymptotic stabilizer-rank exponent: $γ_S \le \log_3(2)/2 \approx 0.316$ for the Strange state, and $γ_{H_3}, γ_N \le \log_3(4)/3 \approx 0.421$ for the Hadamard-eigenstate and Norrell orbits, all strictly below the prior $γ_{T_3} \le 1/2$ baseline. We also prove the first nontrivial $Ω(m / \log m)$ asymptotic lower bounds for the Hadamard-eigenstate and Norrell orbits, and exhibit two-qutrit Clifford circuits that convert two copies of these states into an injectable phase state with constant success probability, enabling constant-overhead injection of one non-Clifford diagonal gate per orbit. In the case of qubits, we give a closed-form decomposition of the qubit T-type orbit at four copies matching the existing $γ_T \le \log_2(3)/4 \approx 0.396$ exponent via a direct algebraic identity rather than an entangled cat-state construction. An open-source library stabrank accompanies the paper, with Lean 4 proof formalizations of all the decompositions.

Summary. This paper develops a detailed noise model for multi-qubit gates in trapped-ion systems to determine their compatibility with scalable quantum error correction. It finds that despite the all-to-all connectivity, errors remain sufficiently localized to allow for a finite QEC threshold in the rotated surface code.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous bridge between device-level physics (motional heating, photon scattering) and high-level QEC performance, which is crucial for designing scalable ion-trap architectures.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether multi-qubit (MQ) gate operations in trapped-ion architectures are compatible with scalable quantum error correction (QEC) by analyzing noise propagation and error rates.

Main result. The study demonstrates that MQ-gate-induced errors are compatible with a finite QEC threshold for the rotated surface code, as error propagation is largely localized to coupled qubits and certain error types are suppressed.

Method. The authors use a Lindblad master equation formalism and Magnus expansion to derive a microscopic noise model, followed by quantum Monte-Carlo and Stim-based simulations of the rotated surface code.

Model / system. A trapped-ion quantum computing platform with all-to-all connectivity, utilizing generalized multi-qubit Ising gates implemented via engineered spin-motion interactions in the Lamb-Dicke regime.

Key observables. Single-qubit and two-qubit error rates, bit-flip probabilities, entangling phase trajectories, and logical error rates as a function of code distance.

Important parameters / regimes. Gate duration (tau ~ 450 us), heating rate (50-100 Hz), dephasing rate (10-100 Hz), and code distances (d=5 to d=17).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the Lamb-Dicke regime, neglects higher-order multi-qubit errors beyond two-qubit, and assumes Pauli twirling can treat coherent errors as stochastic Pauli channels.

Figures summary. Heatmaps of bit-flip probabilities, trajectories of entangling phases, comparison of analytical vs. numerical heating-induced errors, and performance plots of the surface code under different error models.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of MQ gate noise, develops a microscopic noise model using Lindblad dynamics, analyzes error propagation and locality, evaluates QEC performance using surface code simulations, and provides analytical derivations in the appendix.

Abstract

We construct a detailed microscopic noise model for multi-qubit (MQ) gate operations in the context of trapped ion architecture with all-to-all connectivity. We find that phonon heating and motional dephasing are well captured by effective single- and two-qubit error channels that can, in principle, act between arbitrary pairs of qubits. Nevertheless, the median magnitude of two-qubit errors between uncoupled qubits is substantially smaller than that of errors between gate-coupled qubits. Errors associated with photon scattering are shown to solely propagate to qubits participating in gate operations. Lastly, we combine all noise sources, assigned with experimentally relevant parameters, and explore the scalability of a quantum error correction (QEC) scheme based on the rotated surface code, as a function of error rates and code size. Our analysis bridges device-level physics and QEC performance for MQ gates in trapped-ion architectures.

Wetting of quantum fluids: a route to free-standing shell-shaped quantum droplets

Authors: Francesco Ancilotto
Type: theory · Category: quantum gases · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28469v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: analog quantum simulation 2/5

Summary. This paper demonstrates that tuning interspecies interactions in a three-component Bose mixture can drive a wetting transition, leading to the formation of self-bound, shell-shaped quantum droplets. These structures consist of a core-shell geometry where the outer component can host quantized vortices. This approach offers a new way to engineer and study the topology and capillarity of quantum fluids.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel mechanism (wetting) to create complex, self-bound topological structures (shell-shaped droplets) in ultracold gases, offering a platform to study superfluidity in curved, multiply connected geometries.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how wetting phenomena between self-bound quantum fluids can be used to engineer free-standing, shell-shaped quantum droplets.

Main result. The study identifies a transition from partial to complete wetting in a three-component Bose mixture, where the (1,2) liquid wraps around a (2,3) core to form a self-bound core-shell droplet that can sustain quantized vortices.

Method. A density-functional approach using the Mean-Field + Lee-Huang-Yang (MF+LHY) framework, solved via 3D imaginary-time evolution on a Cartesian grid.

Model / system. A three-component Bose mixture of 23Na, 39K, and 41K atoms, modeled as two interacting binary quantum liquids where one acts as a substrate for the other.

Key observables. Surface tension, contact angle, density profiles (column density), and critical rotation frequency for quantized vortices.

Important parameters / regimes. The interspecies scattering length a12, which drives the wetting transition, and the critical value a12c = -42 a0.

Assumptions / limitations. Zero temperature, absence of three-body recombination, and the validity of the MF+LHY approximation.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a dimer structure in the non-wetting regime; Figure 2 shows density profiles; Figure 6 shows interfacial energy vs a12; Figure 7 shows 2D column density projections illustrating the shell structure.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of droplet engineering, describes the MF+LHY theoretical framework, details the numerical implementation, analyzes the wetting transition and contact angle scaling, demonstrates the formation of core-shell structures, and explores vortex excitations in these geometries.

Abstract

We investigate wetting phenomena between self-bound quantum fluids in a three-component Bose mixture of $^{23}$Na, $^{39}$K, and $^{41}$K atoms. Within a density-functional approach including mean-field interactions and Lee-Huang-Yang quantum-fluctuation corrections, we consider two binary quantum liquids, formed by components $(1,2)$ and $(2,3)$, and study the adsorption of the softer $(1,2)$ liquid on a stiffer $(2,3)$ substrate. By tuning the interspecies scattering length $a_{12}$, we show that the surface tension of the $(1,2)$ liquid can be strongly varied, driving a transition from partial wetting to complete wetting of the $(2,3)$ phase. The contact angle extracted from cylindrical-cap geometries decreases continuously with increasing $a_{12}$ and vanishes near a critical value $a_{12}^{c}= -42\,a_0$. In the complete-wetting regime, a finite amount of $(1,2)$ liquid wraps around a spherical $(2,3)$ droplet, producing a self-bound core-shell droplet without external confinement, whose component-1 density has a shell-like, hollow projection. We further show that such shell-shaped quantum droplets can sustain quantized vortical excitations. These results identify wetting as a route to engineering free-standing shell-shaped quantum liquids and suggest new possibilities for studying capillarity, topology, and superfluidity in multicomponent quantum droplets.

A Demonstration of Quantum Circuit Implementation for Obstacle Flow Using Carleman-Linearized Lattice Boltzmann Method

Summary. This paper presents a quantum algorithm for simulating 2D fluid flow around obstacles by linearizing the Lattice Boltzmann Method via Carleman linearization. It provides a detailed gate-level implementation of boundary conditions and demonstrates that the quantum computational cost scales logarithmically with spatial resolution.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a practical quantum-circuit implementation for Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) that incorporates non-periodic boundary conditions and obstacle geometries.

Main result. The authors demonstrated a gate-level implementation of the Carleman-linearized Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) showing logarithmic scaling of qubits and gates with respect to spatial resolution.

Important parameters / regimes. Reynolds number (Re), Mach number (Ma), relaxation time (tau), number of lattice points (N), and Carleman truncation order (Nc).

Abstract

Fluid simulations, especially at high Reynolds numbers, are computationally expensive on classical computers, making them promising application targets for quantum computing. Recent studies have combined the lattice Boltzmann method (LBM) with Carleman linearization to design quantum algorithms for computational fluid dynamics (CFD). However, practical quantum-circuit implementations of these algorithms that incorporate non-periodic boundary conditions have not been fully explored. In this work, we implement a quantum algorithm for two-dimensional linearized fluid flow around an obstacle, using block-encoding of the linear-system matrix and quantum singular value transformation (QSVT) to solve it. Abstract

The rapid growth of computer vision and increasingly complex image recognition tasks has exposed fundamental computational limitations of classical machine learning models, motivating the exploration of quantum computing as an emerging new paradigm. This paper presents a comprehensive benchmarking study of classical and quantum machine learning models for image recognition on the MNIST handwritten digit dataset, evaluating both traditional models, a Classical Support Vector Machine (CSVM) and a Quantum Support Vector Machine (QSVM), and deep neural network models, a Classical Convolutional Neural Network (CCNN) and a Quantum Convolutional Neural Network (QCNN), across four performance dimensions: classification accuracy, computational runtime, parameter count, and memory requirements. Experiments are conducted as functions of both feature dimensionality and sample size, and across CPU and GPU execution environments, providing a controlled, multidimensional comparison to address gaps in prior work. For the SVM-based models, QSVM consistently outperforms CSVM in accuracy, reaching $\sim$ 0.90 versus $\sim$ 0.85 at 1,000 samples, with a higher computational cost. A feature count of 10 qubits and a sample size in the range of 200 -- 500 emerge as practical operating points that balance accuracy and runtime. For the neural network models, CCNN and QCNN achieve comparable classification accuracy, both exceeding 0.96 at 64 features and 60,000 samples, yet QCNN offers substantially superior parameter and memory efficiency, requiring $\sim$ 94\% fewer parameters and $\sim$ 75\% less memory than CCNN at higher feature counts, while incurring higher runtime. Across both model families, quantum models consistently outperform classical models by greater margins in accuracy as feature dimensionality or sample size increases.

Summary. This paper proposes a dynamic scheduling algorithm for quantum networks to replace inefficient static schedules. Abstract

Sharing entanglement among multiple users remains a central challenge for scalable quantum networks. Recent work proposed an on-demand entanglement packet architecture in which a controller uses a Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA) approach to allocate network resources. Quantum nodes are assigned a periodic schedule that probabilistically fulfills application requests for end-to-end entanglements. The schedule is recomputed periodically using well-known algorithms, such as Earliest Deadline First (EDF). However, a static schedule offers limited flexibility when outcomes are stochastic and arrivals are asynchronous. To overcome this limitation, we propose an online scheduler that dynamically schedules, defers, retries, or drops entanglement distribution reservations. In our simulations, the dynamic scheduler achieves lower completion time, higher completion ratio, and higher throughput than the static baseline. Furthermore, when the network is overloaded, the dynamic scheduler continues to construct deadline-feasible schedules and degrades gracefully.
