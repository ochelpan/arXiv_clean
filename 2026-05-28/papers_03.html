Noise adaptive two-way secure deterministic quantum key distribution

Authors: Abinash Kar, Ayan Patra, Aditi Sen De, Tamoghna Das
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27902v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper introduces a framework for noise-adaptive two-way quantum key distribution where encoding and decoding are dynamically optimized to match channel noise. It demonstrates that this approach can increase secret key rates and noise tolerance for specific channel types like bit-phase flip and amplitude damping.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to researchers in open quantum systems and quantum optics as it provides a framework for optimizing quantum communication protocols by leveraging the specific dynamics of the environment-induced noise.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Existing two-way quantum key distribution (QKD) protocols use fixed encoding and decoding strategies that do not account for specific channel noise characteristics. The authors aim to develop and analyze noise-adaptive QKD (NAQKD) protocols that optimize these operations based on the noise model.

Main result. The study identifies specific noise channels, such as bit-phase flip and amplitude damping, where adaptive encoding and decoding significantly enhance secret key rates and noise thresholds. Conversely, it proves that for certain unital channels like depolarizing and bit-flip channels, adaptive strategies provide no advantage.

Method. The authors use entropic uncertainty relations and the Devetak-Winter bound to derive closed-form expressions for secret key rates. They optimize the parameters of SU(2) unitary operators for encoding and measurement bases to maximize these rates under collective attacks.

Model / system. A two-way quantum communication framework involving two honest parties (Alice and Bob) and an eavesdropper (Eve). The system involves a qubit traveling through forward and backward quantum channels subject to various noise models, including Pauli, amplitude damping, and non-Markovian environments.

Key observables. Secret key rate, critical noise threshold, and measurement probabilities.

Important parameters / regimes. Encoding unitaries (SU(2) parameters theta, chi, phi), noise strength (p), and the overlap parameter (gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the eavesdropper performs collective attacks and that the honest parties can accurately estimate the noise model of the channel.

Figures summary. Plots of secret key rate versus noise strength for different noise models (Bit Flip, Phase Flip, Bit-Phase Flip, and Amplitude Damping), demonstrating where adaptive strategies outperform non-adaptive ones.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the NAQKD framework, defines the mathematical optimization of encoding/decoding unitaries, provides security proofs using entropic uncertainty relations, compares performance across various noise models (Pauli, amplitude damping), and includes detailed mathematical appendices for protocol verification.

Abstract

We introduce noise-adaptive quantum key distribution (QKD) protocols, in which the honest parties optimize the encoding (state preparation) and decoding (measurement basis) operations according to the noise models affecting the honest subsystems induced by an eavesdropper. This extends conventional QKD schemes that employ fixed encoding and decoding strategies independent of the noise characteristics of the communication channel. We investigate three representative protocols: entanglement-based secure dense coding (SDC), the entanglement-free Lucamarini and Mancini (LM05), and a two-way prepare-and-measure Bennett Brassard (BB84) protocols. Using entropic uncertainty relations, we derive the corresponding secret key rates for both adaptive and conventional non-adaptive scenarios under collective attacks. For independent but identical noise acting on the forward and backward transmission channels, as well as for correlated and non-Markovian environments, we identify classes of channels for which adaptive schemes yield enhanced secret key rates for the considered protocols. In contrast, we also determine Pauli channels, including depolarizing and bit flip channels, for which adaptive strategies provide no benefit. We further show that these optimal sets are generally non-unique and can differ substantially from the unitaries that maximize dense-coding capacity in the absence of security constraints. Our results establish noise-adaptive encoding and decoding as a powerful framework for improving secure communication over realistic noisy quantum channels.

Complex abelian varieties and quantum error correction: a mathematical framework for GKP codes

Authors: Maxence Mayrand, Baptiste Royer
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28784v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. This paper provides a formal mathematical framework that maps GKP quantum error-correcting codes to the theory of polarized complex abelian varieties. It establishes that the robustness of these codes against Gaussian noise is determined by the geometric systolic invariants of the associated varieties. This connection allows for the translation of quantum error correction optimization problems into problems within the moduli space of abelian varieties.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a powerful new geometric toolkit for optimizing GKP codes, specifically relating error-correction performance to well-studied invariants in algebraic geometry, which is highly relevant for continuous-variable quantum computing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Establishing a rigorous mathematical foundation for Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) quantum error-correcting codes by linking them to the geometry of complex abelian varieties.

Main result. The authors prove a dictionary between GKP code structures and abelian varieties, showing that logical gates correspond to automorphisms and error robustness is governed by the systolic invariant of the underlying polarization.

Method. The paper utilizes the Bargmann transform to relate Schrödinger and Fock representations, applying the theory of Heisenberg groups, theta functions, and polarized complex abelian varieties.

Model / system. The system consists of continuous-variable GKP codes modeled via symplectically integral lattices in a multi-mode harmonic oscillator (represented by the space of tempered distributions or L2 functions).

Key observables. Logical Pauli gates, logical Clifford gates, decoding success probability (robustness), and the syndrome of displacement errors.

Important parameters / regimes. Small Gaussian displacement noise variance (sigma), the type of the lattice (d1, ..., dn), and the systolic invariant (shortest nontrivial displacement).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the small-noise limit (sigma to 0) and assumes Gaussian displacement noise and a symplectically integral lattice.

Paper structure. The paper begins by establishing a geometric dictionary between GKP codes and abelian varieties, moves to the representation theory of the Heisenberg group and the Bargmann transform, then analyzes the decoding problem and error dynamics in the small-noise limit, and concludes with optimization problems on the moduli space.

Abstract

We study a class of quantum error-correcting codes through the geometry of complex abelian varieties. These codes, introduced by Gottesman--Kitaev--Preskill, are built from symplectically integral lattices and therefore naturally define polarized complex abelian varieties. We give a precise mathematical formulation of this relationship and extend it to a dictionary between the main structures of GKP code theory and classical objects in the theory of abelian varieties. For instance, under this dictionary, the finite-dimensional code space becomes the space of theta functions $H^0(X, L)$, logical Pauli gates arise from the theta group, passive logical Clifford gates correspond to automorphisms of the polarized abelian variety, and concatenation with stabilizer codes corresponds to isogeny. We also prove several key results that give precise mathematical formulations of statements about these codes that often appear in heuristic form in the physics literature. In particular, we prove that the encoding is asymptotically isometric, that every logical Clifford gate is realized by a Gaussian unitary, and that, for noise of small variance, the failure probability is governed to first order by the shortest nontrivial displacement in the kernel of the polarization isogeny, a systolic invariant of the underlying polarization. This leads naturally to optimization problems on the moduli space of polarized abelian varieties.

Summary. This paper introduces Latent-Conditioned Parameterized Quantum Circuits (LPQCs), a framework that uses classical neural networks to map latent variables to quantum circuit parameters. It proves that this approach can universally approximate any distribution of quantum states and shows that it effectively mitigates the vanishing gradient problem (barren plateaus). The method is successfully applied to generate complex molecular structures from the QM9 dataset.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for quantum generative modeling and introduces a practical method to bypass the barren plateau problem, which is a central bottleneck in variational quantum algorithms and quantum machine learning.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of efficiently generating complex, multimodal distributions of quantum states (ensembles) and mitigating the barren plateau problem in parameterized quantum circuits (PQCs).

Main result. The authors prove that Latent-Conditioned Parameterized Quantum Circuits (LPQCs) are universal approximators for distributions over density operators and demonstrate that they significantly alleviate barren plateaus while outperforming quantum generative baselines on molecular datasets.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical framework where a classical neural network maps a low-dimensional latent variable to the parameters of a PQC, utilizing a Mixture-of-Experts architecture and a 1-Wasserstein distance loss function.

Model / system. A hybrid generative model consisting of a classical neural network (the decoder) and a parameterized quantum circuit (the generator) acting on a system of n data qubits and m ancilla qubits.

Key observables. 1-Wasserstein distance, gradient norm (to measure barren plateaus), trace distance, and molecular metrics (validity, uniqueness, novelty).

Important parameters / regimes. Latent dimension (d), number of qubits (n), circuit depth (L), number of experts (E), and number of prior modes (M).

Assumptions / limitations. The latent space is compact, the prior density is positive and continuous, and the primitive gate set is dense in the unitary group.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the LPQC framework schematic; Figure 2 compares performance across different expert/mode configurations; Figure 3 uses PCA/t-SNE to visualize density matrix ensembles; Figure 4 shows test loss scaling; Figure 5 and 6 illustrate gradient norm decay/stability; Figure 7 compares Wasserstein loss; Figure 8 compares LPQC to IMPE; Figure 9 showcases generated novel molecules.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the LPQC framework, provides a mathematical proof of its universal approximation property, details architectural enhancements like Mixture-of-Experts, presents numerical experiments on synthetic ensembles, and demonstrates application to molecular generation using the QM9 dataset.

Abstract

Many applications in quantum simulation, quantum chemistry, and quantum machine learning require not a single quantum state but an ensemble of states characterizing the heterogeneity of a target system. Preparing such ensembles state-by-state is prohibitive in both variational and fault-tolerant settings, motivating a generative-modeling approach. We introduce latent-conditioned parameterized quantum circuits (LPQCs), a hybrid quantum-classical framework in which classical neural networks map a latent variable sampled from a prior distribution to the parameters of a parameterized quantum circuit. We prove that LPQCs are universal approximators for probability measures over density operators in the $1$-Wasserstein distance, extending classical universal approximation theorems to the quantum-distribution setting. We additionally introduce a multimodal latent prior and a mixture-of-experts circuit architecture, and show that it empirically alleviates the barren plateau problem during optimization. Numerical experiments validate the framework on a synthetic multi-cluster ensemble of mixed quantum states and on a QM9-derived ensemble of 3-D molecular structures. In these tasks, LPQC outperforms recent quantum generative baselines while remaining competitive with typical classical baselines at substantially reduced output dimensionality. By leveraging classical expressivity in the latent space, LPQCs offer a tractable route to quantum generative modeling.

Photon-Conditioned Squeezed States for Directional Displacement Response in Continuous-Variable Photonics

Authors: Boris Kiefer, Olivier Pfister

Summary. The paper compares different non-Gaussian quantum states to see how they perform under displacement noise. It finds that while cat states are better for overall non-Gaussianity, photon-conditioned squeezed states are better for protecting against noise in specific, known directions.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to quantum optics and continuous-variable quantum information, as it provides a task-oriented framework for selecting non-Gaussian resources for noise-resilient quantum communication and sensing.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to establish a common, energy-matched resource map to compare non-Gaussian state families (squeezed Fock, photon-subtracted, and cat states) based on their utility for displacement-noise mitigation and directional sensing.

Main result. While cat states provide superior scalar Wigner negativity, photon-conditioned squeezed states offer a tunable, anisotropic displacement-fidelity response, making them superior for tasks where the noise geometry is known.

Method. The authors use numerical simulations with a Fock-space cutoff of 80 and a uniform phase-space grid to benchmark states at a matched mean photon number.

Model / system. The study is set in the framework of continuous-variable (CV) quantum optics, utilizing single-mode bosonic operators and operations including squeezing, photon subtraction, and displacement.

Key observables. Integrated Wigner negativity, energy-normalized negativity, displacement-fidelity radii (Rx, Rp), and the anisotropy ratio.

Important parameters / regimes. Matched mean photon number (n), squeezing level (up to 12.5 dB), and fidelity threshold (Fth = 0.90).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a weak-tap limit for photon subtraction and does not account for experimental imperfections like optical loss or finite detector efficiency.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Wigner functions of various states; Figure 3 displays matched-energy displacement-fidelity radii and anisotropy ratios; Figure 4 presents a polar plot of the angular displacement-fidelity radius.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of resource comparison, defines the state families and metrics, presents the benchmarking results for scalar and directional metrics, and concludes with the implications for noise mitigation and sensing.

Abstract

Squeezed Fock states, photon-subtracted squeezed states, and optical cat states are established non-Gaussian resources in continuous-variable quantum optics. Here we compare these known state families from a task-oriented perspective: matched mean photon number, scalar Wigner negativity, and directional displacement-fidelity response. Starting from squeezed vacuum, single-photon subtraction prepares a state proportional to \(S(r,θ)\ket{1}\), while two-photon subtraction prepares an even-parity squeezed Fock superposition rather than a pure \(S(r,θ)\ket{2}\). We benchmark photon-conditioned squeezed states against Fock and coherent-cat references using the integrated Wigner negativity \(δ\), the energy-normalized metric \(δ/\langle n\rangle\), and fidelity-threshold displacement radii \(R_F(φ)\). Cat benchmarks remain strong scalar Wigner-negativity resources, whereas photon-conditioned squeezed states provide an origin-centered alternative with tunable anisotropic displacement response. In particular, the two-photon-subtracted squeezed state shows favorable displacement-fidelity radii over selected quadrature directions at matched \(\langle n\rangle\). These results identify a regime relevant to homodyne-aligned continuous-variable control and anisotropic displacement-noise mitigation, with directional sensing as a natural dual application.

Finite-size occupancy scaling of apparent fractal dimensions in stochastic trajectories

Authors: Bon A. Koo, Edward Ju
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27925v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: non-equilibrium universality 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 1/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper addresses the systematic error in calculating fractal dimensions from finite datasets. By modeling the error as a finite-size scaling problem using a balls-in-boxes approach, the authors achieve a universal correction that works across different types of random processes.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Estimating the fractal dimension from finite stochastic trajectories leads to a systematic bias in the box-counting exponent due to an occupancy crossover between resolved scales and finite sample points.

Main result. The authors demonstrate a universal scaling collapse for the box-counting bias and local slope across diverse processes (random walks, fBm, Levy flights) and provide an invertible occupancy model that corrects dimension estimates.

Method. A 'balls-in-boxes' occupancy model is used to treat the crossover as a finite-size scaling problem, validated through large-scale simulations and application to DNA walk data.

Model / system. The study analyzes various stochastic trajectories, including Simple Random Walks, fractional Brownian motion graphs, and Levy flights, using a mean-field balls-in-boxes occupancy framework.

Key observables. Fractal dimension (Df), box-count curve, normalized local slope, and windowed box-counting bias.

Important parameters / regimes. Saturation scale (k*), number of effective sampled sites (Neff), Hurst exponent (H), and the scaling variables (xi and x).

Assumptions / limitations. The model uses a mean-field approximation treating trajectory points as independently distributed into cells, which may fail for multifractal or strongly anisotropic systems.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the predicted box-count curve; Figure 2 shows the collapse of windowed bias against a scaling variable; Figure 3 shows the collapse of normalized local slopes; Figure 4 demonstrates the effectiveness of the bias correction on held-out models.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the finite-size bias problem, presents the balls-in-boxes occupancy model, demonstrates universal scaling collapse across different trajectory classes, validates the model with a bias correction, and applies the workflow to E. coli DNA walks.

Abstract

Estimating a fractal dimension from a finite stochastic trajectory is a finite-size scaling problem: the apparent box-counting exponent is shaped by an occupancy crossover between the resolved range of scales and the finite number of sampled points, and need not equal the dimension of the limiting process. We model this crossover with a balls-in-boxes occupancy law, which predicts the box-count curve, the finite-size saturation scale, and a scaling function for the normalized local slope. Across random-walk traces, fractional Brownian graphs, and Levy flights, the normalized local slope collapses onto a single crossover curve, while the windowed box-counting bias collapses when the regression window is positioned relative to the saturation scale. Inverting the occupancy model gives a finite-size bias correction that reduces error on controlled stochastic trajectories and transfers across held-out model classes. Comparisons with correlation dimension, detrended fluctuation analysis, the variogram, and Higuchi's method show that the dominant bias is specific to point-sampled box-counting over finite scale windows, and that local-slope stability alone is not a reliable diagnostic. A DNA-walk example illustrates the workflow on measured data, and all figures, tables, and in-text numbers are regenerated from released single-seed code.

Summary. This paper presents a new quantum generative model designed to produce high-resolution calorimeter images for high-energy physics. By using a specialized correlation-sensitive loss function and a compilation technique, the authors achieve high-fidelity generation at 64 qubits without encountering barren plateaus.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a scalable quantum generative model for high-resolution (64-qubit) calorimeter image generation while overcoming the limitations of existing IQP models in capturing spatial correlations and avoiding barren plateaus.

Main result. The PSCK-MoIQP model significantly outperforms the Liu-Wang baseline in correlation reconstruction and demonstrates that the compiled cIQP circuit can be deployed on quantum hardware with sub-Monte-Carlo noise precision.

Method. A 'train-on-classical, deploy-on-quantum' workflow using a Pearson-Stabilized Correlation Kernel (PSCK) for training and a Walsh-Hadamard-based deferred-measurement compilation (cIQP) for deployment.

Model / system. A Mixture-of-IQP (MoIQP) architecture consisting of L independent IQP circuits sharing an Erdős-Rényi gate graph, which is compiled into a single larger IQP circuit (cIQP) using ancilla qubits.

Key observables. Pauli-Z words/correlators, Pearson correlation matrices, Mean Absolute Error of correlations (MAE_rho), and Z-marginals.

Important parameters / regimes. 64 qubits (n=64), 8 mixture components (L=8), 8 bits per feature (B=8), and an Erdős-Rényi gate graph with average degree 6.

Assumptions / limitations. The PSCK loss uses a Gauss-Newton linearization which is exact at the optimum; the compilation assumes the ability to implement deferred measurement via ancilla qubits.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the MoIQP and cIQP architectures, compare Pearson correlation residuals between models, show per-feature distributional agreement, and plot training trajectories and gradient variance scaling.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific challenge of HEP image simulation, describes the MoIQP and cIQP architectures, details the PSCK loss function and training algorithm, presents numerical results on 64-qubit scaling and correlation accuracy, and concludes with a verification of the compilation fidelity.

Abstract

We train an instantaneous quantum polynomial-time (IQP) Born machine on real high-energy-physics calorimeter shower images at $64$ qubits and compile the trained model into a single sampling-hard IQP circuit for quantum deployment. The pipeline has three components: a Mixture-of-IQP (\moiqp{}) architecture, whose Walsh-diagonal MMD$^{2}$ loss is classically trainable by Van den Nest Fourier Monte Carlo; the Pearson-Stabilized Correlation Kernel (\psck{}), a positive-definite MMD kernel that biases descent toward correlation-sensitive directions through a data-evaluated Jacobian of the empirical Pearson matrix; and an exact deferred-measurement compilation of \moiqp{} into a single IQP circuit on $\nfeat + \lceil \log_2 \Lcomp \rceil$ qubits (\ciqp{}). Across five seeds at $\Lcomp = 8$, $1500$ epochs, the model reaches $\maerho = 0.069 \pm 0.008$ against a $0.052$ encoding-fidelity floor on the training split and $0.071 \pm 0.008$ on a held-out test split, versus a Liu--Wang baseline at $\maerho = 0.100$. The compiled \ciqp{} reproduces the \moiqp{} marginal to $0.591 \pm 0.012$ times the Monte Carlo noise floor.

Summary. This paper benchmarks classical, hybrid, and direct quantum annealing solvers on a practical optimization task involving greenhouse climate control. It finds that while quantum-inspired and classical methods are highly reliable, current D-Wave hybrid and direct QPU workflows do not yet demonstrate quantum advantage for this structured control problem.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Benchmarking the performance, reliability, and feasibility of various quantum and classical optimization workflows on a structured QUBO problem derived from greenhouse climate control.

Main result. Classical simulated annealing and path-integral simulated quantum annealing outperform the D-Wave Leap Hybrid BQM and direct QPU execution for the tested H=24 instance, with the QPU exact-hit rate dropping significantly as problem size increases.

Method. Comparison of exact enumeration, classical Simulated Annealing (SA), Path-Integral Simulated Quantum Annealing (PISA), D-Wave Leap Hybrid BQM, and direct D-Wave QPU execution.

Model / system. A greenhouse thermal model featuring air and canopy temperature dynamics, driven by solar radiation, outdoor temperature, and electricity tariffs, formulated as a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem for heater scheduling.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a greenhouse-inspired control problem, formulates it as a QUBO, describes the various classical and quantum solvers used, presents a benchmark comparison of solution quality and feasibility, and discusses the limitations of current quantum annealing workflows.

Abstract

We benchmark current annealing-based optimization workflows on a greenhouse-inspired quadratic unconstrained binary optimization problem for binary heater scheduling, where the horizon H denotes the number of hourly control decisions. For the main one-day instance (H=24), all solver outputs are decoded back into heater schedules and evaluated in the original greenhouse simulator using the same physical objective and feasibility criterion. Classical simulated annealing and path-integral simulated quantum annealing produce feasible near-optimal solutions in all repetitions, with best objectives close to the exact optimum. In contrast, the tested D-Wave Leap Hybrid BQM workflow is less reliable and does not outperform the classical baselines under 15--60~s requested time limits. Direct D-Wave QPU execution on reduced instances remains feasible in all runs and recovers the exact optimum for H=10 and H=12, but the exact-hit rate drops from 5/10 to 2/10 and then to 0/10 at H=14, with substantially higher variance than the classical baselines. The results do not indicate quantum advantage, but provide a reproducible, physically decoded benchmark that exposes the current strengths and limitations of classical, hybrid, and direct quantum annealing workflows on structured control QUBOs.

Change in charge density wave order beyond the Lifshitz transition in 2H-Ta\textsubscript{1$\pmδ$}S\textsubscript{2}

Summary. This paper investigates how tuning the electron density and lattice structure in TaS2 can engineer different charge density wave phases. It reveals a hidden lock-in transition in standard TaS2 and identifies a new, novel CDW order in self-intercalated samples. This work provides a roadmap for controlling electronic instabilities in layered materials via chemical doping.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Model / system. The physical system is the transition metal dichalcogenide 2H-TaS2 and its self-intercalated version 2H†-Ta1+δS2, characterized by quasi-2D layers and electronic instabilities driven by electron-phonon coupling.

Figures summary. The figures likely illustrate ARPES spectra showing spectral gaps and backfolded features, Fermi surface reconstructions, and theoretical phonon dispersion curves showing instabilities at specific q-vectors.

The paper introduces the problem of CDW ordering, presents experimental ARPES evidence for the lock-in transition in conventional TaS2, describes the discovery of the new CDW phase in the self-intercalated phase, and concludes with ab initio theoretical support for the role of doping and lattice spacing.

Abstract

We investigate electronic instabilities in 2H-TaS\textsubscript{2} and a self-intercalated variant, 2H$^\dagger$-Ta\textsubscript{1+$δ$}S\textsubscript{2}. In conventional samples, which we determine to be slightly hole-doped, spectral gaps and backfolded features are found as fingerprints of the $3\times3$ charge density wave (CDW). Notably, the backfolded features emerge only at a temperatures below $T\approx$~65~K, substantially lower than the established CDW temperature of 78~K, suggesting an incommensurate-commensurate lock-in transition analogous to the phenomenology of the 2H-TaSe\textsubscript{2}. In contrast, the self-intercalated 2H$^\dagger$ sample exhibits substantial electron doping and signatures of a novel \tworootthree CDW. Using \textit{ab initio} calculations of the phonon spectrum, we demonstrate that the \threebythree instability ($\mathbf{q}=\sfrac{2}{3}\mathbf{ΓM}$) is highly sensitive to band filling. Furthermore, with increased interlayer spacing, a competing soft phonon mode emerges near $\mathbf{q}=\sfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{ΓK}$, corresponding to the superstructure observed in the 2H$^\dagger$ phase, although in our calculations this instability arises under hole doping rather than the electron doping inferred experimentally. These results establish band filling and interlayer spacing as key control parameters for CDW ordering vectors in 2H-TaS\textsubscript{2}, and highlight a route to engineering electronic instabilities in a prototypical layered material.

Compile-Time Simplification of Classically Controlled Operations in Dynamic Circuits

Summary. This paper presents a new compilation framework designed to reduce the runtime overhead of dynamic quantum circuits. By using symbolic execution at compile-time, the method simplifies or removes classically controlled operations, effectively shifting the computational burden from the quantum hardware's controller to the classical compiler.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Dynamic quantum circuits introduce significant hardware latency and overhead due to the feedback loop between mid-circuit measurements and classical controllers. Method. A two-phase optimization scheme: Phase I uses symbolic execution and Classical Constant Propagation (CCP) to rewrite dynamic circuits into a probabilistic representation; Phase II samples this representation to generate executable circuit instances.

Model / system. Dynamic quantum circuits consisting of a quantum register of n qubits and a classical register of m bits, involving mid-circuit measurements, resets, and classically controlled operations.

Key observables. Number of classically controlled operations, reduction in classical feedforward, and circuit width/depth scaling.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the two-phase compilation overview; Figure 2 illustrates the structure of classically controlled operations; Figure 3 shows the scaling of operation reduction relative to circuit width and depth.

Abstract

Dynamic circuits use real-time outcomes of mid-circuit measurements, processed by a classical controller, to adapt subsequent operations during circuit execution. This additional flexibility over static circuits comes at a price. Mid-circuit measurements are typically slower and noisier than unitary gates. Abstract

In traditional quantum computing, it has been established that real quantum computation augmented with non-Clifford gates is as powerful as universal quantum computation. Here we investigate this phenomenon in the non-Hermitian setting. We show that a non-Hermitian quantum computer equipped with the real gate set ${H, \text{CCNOT}, G}$, where $G = \operatorname{diag}(g^{-1}, g)$ with $g > 0$ and $g \neq 1$, can solve problems in $\text{P}^{\sharp\text{P}}$ in polynomial time, matching the capability of its universal non-Hermitian counterpart ${H, T, \text{CNOT}, G}$. This demonstrates that non-unitarity, rather than universality, is the essential resource, and that complex numbers are unnecessary.

Abstract

We propose a definition of a non-invertible symmetry enriched topological order (NI-SETO), and we implement our definition for string net models. We do so in two ways, using full inclusions of unitary fusion categories (UFCs), as well as anyon condensation. In both cases, the NI-SETO is a relative center of UFCs. All NI-SETOs can be realized in either model, where we can use enriched UFCs to get chiral examples on the boundary of a 3D Walker-Wang model representing the anomaly. We describe several examples of NI-SETOs and compute the qualitative symmetry action on anyons and symmetry defects using tube algebra techniques.
