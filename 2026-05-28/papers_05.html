EFaaS: A Quantum-Classical Serverless Entangled Scheduler for Hybrid Variational Algorithms

Authors: Abolfazl Younesi, Nouhaila Innan, Alberto Marchisio, Muhammad Shafique
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27540v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper proposes EFaaS, a serverless middleware designed to optimize the execution of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms. By treating classical optimization and quantum execution as entangled, session-aware events, it minimizes the latency and hardware drift that typically plague cloud-based quantum computing.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. High latency and 'quantum drift' in hybrid variational quantum algorithms (VQAs) caused by decoupled, batch-queue cloud architectures that break the iterative loop between classical optimizers and QPUs.

Main result. The EFaaS middleware achieves convergence speedups of 83.2%-98.3% and significantly reduces Time-to-Next-Shot (TTNS) by up to 94.3% while maintaining near-zero hardware drift penalties.

Method. A serverless middleware architecture using a Calibration-Aware placement strategy, a Dual-Resource Fair Queuing (DRFQ) scheduler, and an 'EF-QuantumFuture' programming primitive for speculative classical execution.

Model / system. A distributed hybrid quantum-classical environment consisting of a pool of QPUs (superconducting and ion-trap) and classical FaaS nodes, simulating VQE and QAOA workloads on Hamiltonians like LiH and the transverse-field Ising chain.

Key observables. Time-to-Next-Shot (TTNS), Quantum Duty Cycle (QDC), end-to-end convergence time, and drift penalty events.

Important parameters / regimes. Drift threshold (tau_drift), calibration validity window, and scheduling weights (alpha, beta, gamma) for session awareness, drift urgency, and fair-share.

Assumptions / limitations. The effectiveness of stochastic optimizers relies on the assumption of i.i.d. sampling, which is violated by hardware drift; the system assumes a discrete-event simulation model for traffic.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the hybrid iterative workflow; Figure 2 details the EFaaS architecture; Figure 3 depicts the Session Awareness Engine state machine; Figure 4 illustrates the overlapping execution of classical and quantum tasks; Figure 8 and 9 show the reduction in drift events and calibration overhead.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the latency/drift problem, proposes the EFaaS architecture and its three technical innovations, provides a mathematical model for latency and scheduling, presents experimental results via a discrete-event simulator, and performs ablation and sensitivity studies.

Abstract

As quantum computing enters the Utility Era, realizing near-term advantage relies heavily on Hybrid Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs). These algorithms require a tightly coupled, iterative loop between a classical CPU optimizer and a Quantum Processing Unit (QPU). However, current quantum cloud access models are bottlenecked by decoupled batch-queues that sever this loop, introducing massive Time-to-Next-Shot (TTNS) latency. This delay inflates convergence time from minutes to hours and exposes the computation to quantum hardware drift, degrading algorithmic fidelity. Unlike prior works that rely on resource-wasting static hardware reservations or state-oblivious stateless functions, we propose EFaaS, a novel serverless middleware designed specifically for hybrid quantum workflows. EFaaS fundamentally departs from existing architectures by treating classical parameter optimization and quantum circuit execution as entangled, session-aware events. Our main technical innovations are threefold: (1) a Calibration-Aware placement strategy that dynamically routes circuits to QPUs with warm calibration caches, circumventing cold-start penalties, (2) a Dual-Resource Fair Queuing scheduler that maximizes quantum utilization by strictly prioritizing active iterative loops, and (3) the "EF-QuantumFuture" programming abstraction, a novel primitive enabling classical speculative execution to mask compute latency. Across the evaluated baselines, EFaaS achieves TTNS reductions of 11.4%-94.3%, QDC gains of 2.02%-15.78% points, and convergence speedups of 83.2%-98.3%, while eliminating drift penalties.

Emergence of an antiferromagnetic topological Anderson insulator in the interacting Haldane model

Summary. This paper explores how disorder can induce new topological phases in interacting systems. It specifically demonstrates the emergence of an antiferromagnetic topological Anderson insulator in the Haldane model, showing that disorder can effectively replace a staggered mass to create a C=1 phase. The study also showcases the utility of neural networks in identifying topological phase boundaries in disordered many-body systems.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether an antiferromagnetic (AFM) C=1 topological phase can be induced by Anderson disorder in the absence of a staggered mass in the interacting Haldane model.

Main result. The study identifies an antiferromagnetic topological Anderson insulator phase, demonstrating that Anderson disorder can generate the charge imbalance necessary to induce the C=1 phase.

Method. The authors use Exact Diagonalization (ED) on a 12A cluster and a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on the single-particle density matrix to predict Chern numbers and resolve phase boundaries.

Model / system. A spinful Haldane model on a honeycomb lattice including Hubbard (U) and nearest-neighbor density-density (V) interactions, subject to onsite Anderson disorder (W) and a staggered potential (Delta).

Key observables. Many-body Chern number (C), Spin Density Wave (SDW) structure factor, and Charge Density Wave (CDW) structure factor.

Important parameters / regimes. Hubbard interaction (U), density-density interaction (V), Anderson disorder strength (W), and staggered mass (Delta).

Assumptions / limitations. The ground state is assumed to be non-degenerate for the use of the Abelian Berry connection; results are limited by finite-size effects of the 12A cluster.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows clean model phase diagrams; Figure 2 shows CDW/SDW diagrams; Figure 3 shows phase diagrams at specific disorder strengths; Figure 4 shows CNN-predicted phase diagrams; Figure 5 compares ED and CNN results; Figure 6 shows SDW structure factor and phase classification percentages.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the clean model phase diagrams, moves to the introduction of disorder and the identification of the AFM topological phase, presents the machine learning methodology for phase prediction, and concludes with a comparison of ED and CNN results and the analysis of phase competition.

Abstract

We examine the emergence of topological Anderson insulating phases in the spinful Haldane model with Hubbard and next-neighbor density-density interactions, subject to Anderson disorder. Using finite-size exact diagonalization, we characterize the phases that arise from the interplay between topology, interactions, and disorder. In addition to standard $C=2$ topological Anderson phases, we observe an antiferromagnetic $C=1$ topological Anderson phase, consistent with the antiferromagnetic quantum anomalous Hall insulator previously identified in the clean model at finite staggered mass. We further analyze these phases using a neural network trained on the exact diagonalization data. Our results support the hypothesis that an explicit charge imbalance is required to induce the $C=1$ phase, generated by Anderson disorder rather than by a staggered mass.

Abstract

Gauge Geometry of Hodge Zero-Mode Transport in Parameter-Dependent Topological Data Analysis

Authors: Satoshi Kanno, Rei Nishimura, Hiroshi Yamauchi, Yoshi-aki Shimada
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28326v1

Summary. This paper introduces a new way to track how topological features in data evolve over time or external parameters. By treating homology as a geometric bundle, it uses concepts like curvature and holonomy to detect structural changes that traditional methods miss.

Why it may be interesting. The use of Berry connection, curvature, and holonomy to describe the evolution of topological features is directly analogous to the study of geometric phases and topological invariants in many-body systems and driven quantum dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Standard Topological Data Analysis (TDA) tools like persistence diagrams fail to capture how homological feature spaces reorganize, mix, or transport when data depends on external parameters like time or control variables.

Main result. The authors develop a gauge-geometric framework using the Hodge Laplacian to describe the transport of homology, proving that curvature and holonomy are stable descriptors of structural reorganization in regular parameter regions.

Method. The framework uses the combinatorial Hodge Laplacian to represent homology as a zero-mode bundle, applying Berry-type connection, curvature, and holonomy to track feature evolution.

Model / system. A parameter-dependent family of simplicial complexes (e.g., Vietoris-Rips filtrations) where the topology is studied as a vector bundle over a parameter space (time, temperature, or external fields).

Key observables. Curvature (local reorganization), Holonomy (accumulated memory/monodromy), and zero-mode projections.

Important parameters / regimes. Filtration scale (a), external parameter (lambda), spectral gap (gamma), and smoothing parameter (epsilon).

Assumptions / limitations. The theory assumes 'regular regions' where the zero-mode multiplicity is constant and a spectral gap exists; it also uses a smooth approximation for the discrete Laplacian.

Figures summary. The figures likely include curvature heatmaps identifying reorganization regions, comparisons between vineyard-style tracking and holonomy-guided tracking, and stability plots showing error bounds under noise.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the limitations of existing TDA, constructs a smooth Hodge-type operator and a zero-mode vector bundle, defines geometric quantities (connection, curvature, holonomy), provides stability proofs via Riesz projection, and validates the method through numerical experiments on time-dependent point clouds.

Abstract

We propose a practical computational framework for detecting structural changes in parameter-dependent topological data. In many applications, such as time-series data analysis, anomaly detection, and monitoring of systems under changing control parameters, persistence diagrams describe the birth and death of topological features at each parameter value, but they do not fully capture how these features are reorganized over time. To address this limitation, we represent homological features by zero modes of the ordinary combinatorial Hodge Laplacian and track the corresponding feature spaces in a common ambient chain space. This allows us to compute curvature and holonomy as descriptors of local reorganization and accumulated memory in evolving topological structures. Curvature highlights parameter regions where homological features mix or change rapidly, while holonomy summarizes the net effect of such changes after a closed cycle. We also establish stability estimates showing that these descriptors are robust under perturbations of the Hodge Laplacian on regular regions. Numerical experiments on controlled time-dependent point-cloud data show that the proposed method detects tracking instability, distinguishes systems with nearly identical persistence diagrams, and captures cycle-level memory invisible to pointwise feature matching. These results suggest that zero-mode transport geometry can serve as a useful computational tool for analyzing dynamic topological data.

Geometric Analysis of Variational Quantum Eigensolver

Authors: Zhen Qin
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27795v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper provides a unified geometric analysis of the VQE optimization landscape, proving that the landscape is free of spurious local minima. It establishes convergence guarantees for Riemannian Gradient Descent and provides a theoretical link between circuit depth and the emergence of barren plateaus.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous geometric foundation for understanding why VQE can be trained and how circuit depth and measurement noise fundamentally impact the convergence of variational algorithms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The lack of a unified geometric characterization of the VQE optimization landscape, specifically addressing the fragmentation between fixed-ansatz and adaptive-circuit formulations and the challenges of barren plateaus and noise robustness.

Main result. The paper establishes that the VQE landscape is geometrically benign (no spurious local minima, only global minima or strict saddles) and proves that Riemannian Gradient Descent (RGD) achieves linear convergence, though the rate deteriorates polynomially with circuit depth.

Method. The authors use Riemannian geometry on the unitary group, analyzing the landscape via the Riemannian Hessian and applying Riemannian Gradient Descent (RGD) with a polar-based retraction.

Model / system. The study focuses on the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) framework using parameterized quantum circuits (product-unitary ansatz) to approximate the ground-state energy of a Hamiltonian H.

Key observables. Ground-state energy expectation value, Riemannian gradient, and Riemannian Hessian.

Important parameters / regimes. Circuit depth (N), spectral gap (delta_1), Hamiltonian norm (||H||), and measurement budget (M).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes smooth parametrization of the ansatz layers, use of Pauli-decomposed Hamiltonians for noise analysis, and an initial state within a specific energy error threshold.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a unified ansatz-free product-unitary formulation, analyzes the landscape geometry (global minima and strict saddles), investigates initialization feasibility via random Pauli rotations, and concludes with a study of robustness under finite-shot measurement noise.

Abstract

The Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) is a fundamental algorithm in quantum computing, yet a coherent geometric characterization of VQE remains missing due to fragmented analyses across fixed-ansatz and adaptive-circuit formulations. In this paper, we establish a geometric analysis of VQE in terms of optimization landscape, initialization guarantee, and noise robustness. First, we study the optimization landscape via an ansatz-free product-unitary formulation over the unitary group, unifying both paradigms. For the single-unitary case, we establish linear convergence of Riemannian gradient descent (RGD) and prove the strict saddle property. For the product-unitary case, we show the convergence rate deteriorates polynomially with circuit depth, providing a geometric explanation of the barren plateau phenomenon. Second, we prove that small-angle random Pauli-rotation circuits satisfy the required initialization conditions with high probability. Third, we show that RGD retains linear convergence under finite-shot measurements, and that coefficient-adaptive allocation achieves strictly lower statistical error than uniform sampling under a fixed measurement budget.

Cobalt-based quantum magnets forming layered honeycomb arrangements have attracted much attention recently, as they are considered as a potential platform for materials with exotic Kitaev spin exchange. Amongst the discussed candidate materials are Na$_3$Co$_2$SbO$_6$ and Na$_2$Co$_2$TeO$_6$, both possessing a low-temperature ground state with magnetic zigzag ordering, similar to Na$_2$IrO$_3$ and $α$-RuCl$_3$. Our analysis is complemented by magnetic field measurements and simulations.

Abstract

Precision interferometry with atomic wavepackets confined in a one-dimensional optical lattice is an emergent paradigm in quantum sensing of forces and fields, with applications in gravimetry, accelerometry, geophysics, and fundamental physics tests. Summary. This paper introduces a variational framework called VarEFTQC for learning the physical implementation of logical gates in any quantum error-correcting code. Abstract

Logical operations are essential for quantum computation within quantum error-correcting codes. However, discovering their physical realizations is challenging, especially for non-additive codes that lack a stabilizer description. We present a general learning-based framework that, given only an encoding circuit, constructs physical implementations of logical operations while enforcing structural properties such as transversality or shallow depth. Our approach is validated by rediscovering known logical operations of standard stabilizer codes. A software library implements the complete learning pipeline, including loss-function variants, ansatz families, and optimization routines. Together, these results position VarEFTQC as a practical tool for discovering hardware-adapted logical gadgets for early fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Abstract

Quantum error correction (QEC) is often implemented on hardware that experiences biased noise, where dephasing errors occur more frequently than other errors. This has motivated many recent efforts to develop bias-tailored QEC codes, such as the Clifford-deformed compass codes: a family of codes that achieve high thresholds under biased dephasing noise. Our results demonstrate that correlated decoding leads to a higher relative gain in thresholds compared to standard MWPM when applied to codes with asymmetric stabilizers under biased noise.

Using an edge-state model that includes both the localized levels and the continuum states outside the magnetic-field-induced gap, we calculate the electric-dipole matrix elements and solve the time-dependent problem under resonant driving. The resulting dynamics exhibits Rabi oscillations with linear frequencies of $20$--$40$~GHz for realistic parameters. We find that the continuum states provide a leakage channel whose strength is strongly controlled by the driving amplitude: reducing the electric field suppresses leakage below the percent level while preserving GHz-scale coherent oscillations. These results establish a route from magnetic-field-induced corner-state physics to electrically driven two-level dynamics in a realistic two-dimensional topological-insulator edge geometry.
