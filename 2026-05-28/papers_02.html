On the existence of fully inseparable biseparable Gaussian states

Authors: Olga Leskovjanová, Klára Baksová, Jan Provazník, Ladislav Mišta,, Nicolai Friis
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28404v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. The paper explores whether Gaussian states can be fully inseparable without being genuinely multipartite entangled. By analyzing various three-mode Gaussian families using local projections to finite-dimensional subspaces, the authors find no evidence for such states and conjecture that all fully inseparable Gaussian states are genuinely multipartite entangled.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant to quantum optics and continuous-variable quantum information as it addresses a fundamental question about the structure of entanglement in Gaussian states and suggests that standard covariance matrix-based tests might be sufficient for GME detection.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether 'fully inseparable biseparable' (FIB) Gaussian states exist, specifically whether there are Gaussian states that are entangled across every bipartition but are still expressible as a convex mixture of states separable with respect to different partitions.

Main result. The authors find that for all tested families of three-mode Gaussian states, states appearing to be FIB are actually genuinely multipartite entangled (GME), leading to the conjecture that all fully inseparable Gaussian states are GME.

Method. The study uses the PPT criterion for entanglement detection, projections of Gaussian density matrices onto finite-dimensional Fock subspaces, and semidefinite programming (SDP) to construct fully decomposable witnesses.

Model / system. The study focuses on three-mode continuous-variable Gaussian states, including families constructed from mixtures of two-mode squeezed vacuum states with single-mode squeezed, thermal, or coherent states, as well as noisy GHZ-like Gaussian states.

Key observables. Squeezing parameter (r), mean photon number (n), displacement (alpha), and noise/mixing parameter (eta).

Important parameters / regimes. The dimension of the local projection subspaces (d=2, 3, 4) and the squeezing parameter (r).

Assumptions / limitations. The study is restricted to three-mode Gaussian states and relies on the observation that the gap between full inseparability and GME shrinks with increasing projection dimension to form a conjecture.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the hierarchy of multipartite entanglement; Figures 2-6 show detection ranges for various Gaussian state families (SMSV, thermal, coherent, and noisy GHZ) across different detection methods and subspace dimensions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the distinction between full inseparability and GME, defines the Gaussian state models, describes the numerical methods for Fock-basis projection, presents results for various state families, and concludes with a conjecture and discussion of implications.

Abstract

Genuine multipartite entanglement and full inseparability are two inequivalent quantum resources. Even though all genuinely multipartite entangled states are also fully inseparable, not all fully inseparable states are genuinely multipartite entangled. There exist fully inseparable states that can be prepared as convex mixtures of states separable with respect to different bipartite splits. Here, we are interested in examples of Gaussian states that possess this type of entanglement, so-called fully inseparable biseparable states. We show for several archetypical families of multimode Gaussian states that fully inseparable biseparable candidate states are actually genuinely multipartite entangled. Using projections to finite-dimensional subspaces and fully decomposable witnesses, we observe a shrinking of the regions of potentially fully inseparable biseparable Gaussian states with growing dimension of the projection subspaces. We therefore conjecture that all fully inseparable Gaussian states are genuinely multipartite entangled.

Simulation of additive binding energies in asphalt using quantum-selected configuration interaction (QSCI)

Authors: Karim Elgammal, Marc Maußner
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.27640v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 QC/QI experiment 4/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper demonstrates a hybrid quantum-classical workflow called QuantumPave that uses a quantum processor to sample electronic configurations for calculating binding energies. By simulating a pyridine-phenol complex, the authors show that current NISQ-era hardware can achieve chemically relevant results for industrial materials science problems. The approach highlights the potential of quantum-centric supercomputing to solve complex molecular recognition tasks.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to demonstrate the feasibility of using a quantum-centric supercomputing workflow to predict additive binding energies in asphalt, a critical factor in road infrastructure durability.

Main result. The QSCI/SQD algorithm on the 54-qubit IQM Emerald processor exactly reproduced the active-space CASCI reference binding energy of -3.52 kcal/mol, proving that chemically meaningful results are attainable on current NISQ hardware.

Method. A hybrid quantum-classical workflow (QuantumPave) using Quantum-Selected Configuration Interaction (QSCI) where a quantum processor samples electronic configurations and classical HPC performs diagonalization, integrated with machine-learning interatomic potentials (ORB v3) for geometry optimization.

Model / system. A 24-atom pyridine-phenol hydrogen-bonded complex (C11H11NO) serving as a representative model for asphalt binder interactions, mapped onto a 54-qubit IQM Emerald superconducting processor using a (10e, 10o) active space.

Key observables. Additive binding energy (in kcal/mol and eV).

Important parameters / regimes. Active space of 10 electrons in 10 orbitals; 5000 measurement shots; 10^-6 Ha energy tolerance; NISQ regime.

Assumptions / limitations. The discrepancy between calculated and experimental values is attributed to the use of a compact/limited active space; the study assumes the pyridine-phenol complex is a sufficient proxy for asphalt binder interactions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the molecular structure of the pyridine-phenol complex; Figure 2 displays the 3D optimized structure from ORB v3; Figure 3 illustrates the QSCI workflow loop.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the industrial problem of asphalt aging, presents the QuantumPave hybrid workflow, details the QSCI algorithm and hardware implementation, compares results against classical (CASCI, DFT) and experimental benchmarks, and concludes with future scaling directions.

Abstract

Quantum-centric supercomputing (in which a quantum processor samples the dominant electronic configurations and classical high-performance computing resources perform the diagonalisation) is emerging as a practical route to correlated electronic-structure calculations. We present QuantumPave, a hybrid quantum-classical workflow for computing additive binding energies in asphalt binder, a quantity central to the oxidative ageing of road infrastructure. Using a 24-atom pyridine-phenol hydrogen-bonded complex as a representative model, we couple machine-learning interatomic potentials (ORB v3) for geometry optimisation with quantum-selected configuration interaction (QSCI), also referred to as sample based quantum diagonalisation (SQD), in a (10e, 10o) active space run on the 54-qubit IQM Emerald processor. On hardware, SQD reproduces the active-space CASCI reference exactly, giving a binding energy of -3.52 kcal/mol (-0.153 eV); the device noise broadens the sampling to span the active space, so no zero-noise extrapolation is required. This active-space value describes a physical hydrogen bond that underbinds the calorimetric enthalpy of about -6.25 kcal/mol, the difference reflecting the compact active space. We show that chemically meaningful binding energies for an industrially relevant materials problem are attainable on current quantum hardware within a quantum-centric supercomputing workflow.

Dissipative Spectral Form Factor of the Complex Elliptic Ginibre Ensemble across Various Non-Hermiticity Regimes

Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5

Summary. This paper provides a complete asymptotic characterization of the Dissipative Spectral Form Factor for the complex elliptic Ginibre ensemble. It maps out a phase diagram showing how the spectral statistics transition between Hermitian and non-Hermitian behaviors depending on the scaling of time and non-Hermiticity. The results rigorously define the scaling of key features like the Thouless and Heisenberg times.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers of open quantum systems as it provides a rigorous description of how dissipation and non-Hermiticity affect spectral correlations and the emergence of universal features like the ramp and plateau in quantum chaotic dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to derive the mathematically rigorous large-N asymptotic behavior of the Dissipative Spectral Form Factor (DSFF) for the complex elliptic Ginibre ensemble across various non-Hermiticity regimes.

Main result. The authors identify a complete phase diagram for the DSFF, characterizing the dip-ramp-plateau structure and the transition between GUE-type (linear ramp) and GinUE-type (quadratic ramp) statistics, including the scaling of Thouless and Heisenberg times.

Method. The study employs the framework of determinantal point processes, using orthogonal polynomial techniques, Christoffel-Darboux formulas, and uniform asymptotic expansions (Bessel, Airy, and exponential regimes) to analyze the large-N limit.

Model / system. The model is the Complex Elliptic Ginibre Ensemble (eGinUE), a non-Hermitian random matrix model that interpolates between the Hermitian GUE limit and the strongly non-Hermitian Ginibre limit, representing open, dissipative quantum chaotic systems.

Key observables. Dissipative Spectral Form Factor (DSFF), split into connected and disconnected components; Thouless time (T_Th); Heisenberg time (T_H).

Important parameters / regimes. Non-Hermiticity parameter (tau); scaling parameters (alpha and gamma) for the non-Hermiticity and time variables; matrix dimension (N).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis is restricted to the large-N (asymptotic) limit and focuses on the simplest ensemble without additional symmetries.

Figures summary. Figure 1(a) shows the dip-ramp-plateau structure of the DSFF with characteristic time scales, and Figure 1(b) illustrates the universality crossover in the (alpha, gamma) phase plane; Figure 2 provides numerical illustration in the strong non-Hermiticity regime.

Paper structure. The paper begins with the definition of the eGinUE and the DSFF, followed by the decomposition into connected and disconnected components. It then proceeds through a detailed asymptotic analysis across four regimes (Bessel, Oscillatory, Airy, and Exponential) to identify the phase diagram and characteristic time scales, concluding with mathematical appendices on expansion coefficients.

Abstract

We study the dissipative spectral form factor (DSFF) at complex time $T e^{iθ}$ for the complex elliptic Ginibre ensemble with non-Hermiticity parameter $τ\in [0,1)$. As the matrix dimension $N \to \infty$, we consider the natural scalings in both the time variable and the non-Hermiticity parameter, namely $T = O(N^γ)$ and $1 - τ= O(N^{-α})$. For all regimes $γ\ge 0$ and $α\ge 0$, we derive the precise asymptotic behaviour of both the disconnected and connected components of the DSFF. In particular, we explicitly characterise the dip--ramp--plateau structure, including the dip time and the Heisenberg time. In addition, we identify the mesoscopic regime $α\in (0,1)$, which interpolates between the behaviour of the DSFF of non-Hermitian random matrices and the spectral form factor (SFF) of Hermitian ensembles. We further provide an explicit description of the phase diagram, in which the ramp exhibits quadratic, linear, or intermediate behaviour depending on the scaling parameters.

Superradiant LIDAR

Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28378v1

Summary. The paper proposes a new LIDAR technique called Superradiant LIDAR that uses multiple independent thermal light sources and higher-order intensity correlations. This method significantly improves distance measurement sensitivity, outperforming traditional LIDAR by a factor proportional to the number of light sources used.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates how exploiting collective effects (superradiance) and higher-order correlations can significantly enhance parameter estimation precision, which is a core theme in quantum metrology and quantum optics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Enhancing the sensitivity of Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR) for measuring the distance of remote objects beyond the limits of traditional and coherent LIDAR.

Main result. The proposed Superradiant LIDAR achieves a Cramér-Rao bound that is undercut by a factor of N compared to traditional LIDAR, with sensitivity further improved by increasing the correlation order m.

Method. The authors use analytical derivations and numerical evaluations of the m-th order photon correlation function and Fisher information, developing a high-precision fit function to describe the scaling behavior.

Model / system. A system of N statistically independent thermal light sources (TLS) placed equidistantly along an axis, where light is reflected by a remote object and detected using m-th order intensity correlations.

Key observables. m-th order correlation function (G(m)N) and Fisher information (F)}^{(sup)

Important parameters / regimes. Number of light sources (N), correlation order (m), unknown distance (z2), and phase differences (delta_i)

Assumptions / limitations. Measurements follow Poisson statistics, light sources are identical and uncorrelated, and pixel width is small enough for the phase approximation to hold.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental schematic; Figure 2 compares correlation functions and shows the steeper slopes for higher N; Figure 3 highlights the significant gains in Fisher information for larger N and m; Figure 4 compares the accuracy of the analytical lower bound versus the proposed fit function.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the LIDAR problem, proposes the Superradiant LIDAR scheme, provides the mathematical framework for correlation functions, derives analytical bounds and fits for Fisher information, and validates these with numerical simulations.

Abstract

In recent years, light detection and ranging (LIDAR) has seen a steep rise in the sensitivity of measuring the distances of remote objects. Here, we propose to enhance the sensitivity of LIDAR even further by exploiting Dicke's concept of superradiance, i.e., the collective light emission of statistically independent light sources. By using $N$ thermal light sources (TLS) and measuring intensity correlations of order $m \geq 2$ instead of $m=1$, i.e., the intensity, we show that the Cramér-Rao bound on the measurement of the distance of a remote object undercuts that of traditional LIDAR by a factor of $N$, and can be reduced further with increasing correlation order $m$. Our numerical calculations are supported by analytical expressions for the special cases of two and three TLS and a general approximate expression for any number of TLS.

Barrier crossing in a two-state system: Effect of bias and stochastic fields

Summary. This paper examines how steady bias and random magnetic fields influence the rate of transitions between states in a kinetic Ising model. It finds that while moderate noise helps overcome energy barriers, excessive noise leads to a loss of directional control. The results highlight how stochastic driving can effectively minimize finite-size effects in the system's dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. The study of noise-induced transitions and the modulation of energy barriers by stochastic and bias fields is highly relevant to understanding decoherence and switching dynamics in open quantum systems and driven many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how a combination of a weak steady bias field and time-dependent Gaussian random fields affects barrier crossing and switching dynamics in a two-state system.

Main result. Moderate stochastic fields lower the effective energy barrier and facilitate transitions, while strong noise induces backflow and reduces directional selectivity; additionally, stochastic driving suppresses finite-size effects.

Method. Monte Carlo simulations using single-spin heat-bath dynamics on a 2D square lattice.

Model / system. A kinetic Ising model on a 2D square lattice with periodic boundary conditions, subject to a Hamiltonian with ferromagnetic coupling, a constant bias field H, and a time-dependent Gaussian random field h_r(t).

Key observables. Probability distribution of magnetization P(m), time-averaged magnetization Q, and average first-passage time .

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature (T), noise intensity (D), bias field (H), and system size (L).

Assumptions / limitations. The stochastic magnetic field is assumed to be spatially homogeneous (uniform across the lattice).

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows a phase diagram in the (D, T) plane; Fig 2 displays P(m) for various parameters; Fig 3 compares P(m) for different system sizes; Fig 4 shows vs D for various H and T; Fig 5 demonstrates suppression of finite-size effects; Fig 6 shows vs H.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model and its phase diagram, explores the effect of noise and bias on the magnetization distribution, analyzes first-passage times and their dependence on noise and bias, and concludes with a discussion on system-size dependence and optimal transition conditions.

Abstract

We study barrier crossing in a two-state system, namely the kinetic Ising model, in the presence of a weak bias field and spatially homogeneous, but time-dependent, Gaussian random fields. We find that the bias field determines the location of the dominant maxima of the probability distribution function of the magnetization, whereas the noise intensity controls their sharpness and stability of the distribution. A moderate stochastic field lowers the effective energy barrier and facilitates transitions between ordered states, while strong noise induces broad distributions and significant backflow, which reduces directional selectivity. Our results suggest that efficient barrier crossing requires a balanced combination of moderate stochastic driving and controlled bias.

The Resetting Heat Engine: A Thermodynamic Cycle of Thermal Expansion and Compression

Authors: Oded Farago
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28445v1

Summary. The paper treats stochastic resetting as a thermodynamic cycle of thermal expansion and compression. It demonstrates that in harmonic potentials, the entropy production of this cycle can be simplified to a state-function-like expression, and shows that the resulting thermodynamic features are robust even in anharmonic systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and non-equilibrium dynamics, as it provides a thermodynamic framework for understanding the irreversibility and entropy production in systems subject to non-unitary, resetting-like dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to develop a thermodynamic characterization of stochastic resetting processes, specifically addressing whether entropy production can be reduced from complex trajectory-based measures to simpler state-function-like expressions.

Main result. The author proves that for a harmonic potential, entropy production reduces exactly to an expression determined by endpoint distributions, and shows that the observed thermodynamic features are qualitatively generic for anharmonic potentials.

Method. The study uses the Kullback-Leibler divergence between forward and reversed trajectory ensembles and employs Langevin simulations for anharmonic cases.

Model / system. A Brownian particle subject to overdamped Langevin dynamics in a one-dimensional potential (primarily harmonic, but also tested with a quartic potential) that undergoes stochastic resetting to the origin at periodic or Poissonian intervals.

Key observables. Entropy production (total, rate, and cycle-based), variance, probability current, and effective temperature.

Important parameters / regimes. Resetting rate (r), relaxation time (tau), damping coefficient (gamma), and the distinction between periodic and Poissonian resetting regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes the reset does not produce a perfect delta-function (finite width) and that the endpoint-based description serves as a lower bound for the full trajectory-level entropy production.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows Langevin simulation results for an anharmonic potential, plotting entropy production per cycle and the entropy production rate as functions of cycle time.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the thermodynamic cycle analogy, develops the analytical framework for harmonic potentials using KL divergence, extends the analysis to entropy production rates for different resetting protocols, and validates the findings via simulations of anharmonic potentials.

Abstract

We consider a Brownian particle confined by an external potential and subject to stochastic resetting to the origin. Motivated by the repetitive nature of the dynamics, we describe the process as a thermodynamic cycle of thermal expansion and collapse, analyzed via a framework based on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between forward and reversed trajectory ensembles. While the entropy production generally depends on the full trajectory ensemble and cannot be reduced to thermodynamic state variables alone, we show that the harmonic potential constitute a special case, where the entropy production reduces exactly to a state-function-like expression determined solely by the distributions before and after resetting. Explicit analytical results are derived for periodic and Poissonian resetting. At low resetting rates $r$, the entropy production rate grows linearly with $r$ and is proportional to the symmetric KL divergence between the reset and equilibrium distributions. At very high rates, the resetting process becomes effectively perpetual and the entropy production vanishes. Langevin simulations for an anharmonic quartic potential display the same generic behavior, indicating that these features are not restricted to harmonic confinement. Our results establish a direct connection between stochastic resetting, thermodynamic cycles, and information-theoretic measures of irreversibility.

Exceptional points in diamond optomechanics

Summary. This paper reports the first experimental access to an exceptional point in a diamond optomechanical crystal. By using engineered structural asymmetry, the authors demonstrate how to tune mechanical mode hybridization and redistribute optomechanical damping. This provides a new platform for non-Hermitian physics and topological dynamics in diamond-based quantum technologies.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates experimental control over non-Hermitian singularities and eigenvector rotation, which is highly relevant for developing direction-dependent quantum state transfer and topological protection in hybrid quantum networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The experimental achievement and utilization of exceptional points (EPs) in diamond optomechanical systems to leverage non-Hermitian phenomena like chiral dynamics and topological state transfer.

Main result. The researchers successfully reached an exceptional point in a diamond optomechanical crystal by using structural symmetry breaking to couple two mechanical modes, observing asymmetric redistribution of damping and branch-switching topology.

Method. The study uses structural asymmetry (sidewall angles) to mix mechanical modes and employs a fiber taper for evanescent coupling, with data analysis involving coupled-mode model fitting and COMSOL simulations.

Model / system. A diamond optomechanical crystal (OMC) nanobeam where a single optical cavity mode is coupled to two high-frequency mechanical resonances (breathing and flexural modes) via radiation pressure and structural symmetry breaking.

Key observables. Mechanical power spectral density (PSD), mechanical eigenfrequencies, damping rates, and optical transmission spectra.

Important parameters / regimes. Optical wavelength ~1565 nm, mechanical frequencies ~10.1 GHz, optomechanical coupling rates ~290 kHz, and the transition from weak coupling to the exceptional point and phonon-lasing threshold.

Assumptions / limitations. The derivation assumes a strong coherent intracarray field, neglects small radiation-pressure-induced cavity resonance shifts, and approximates mechanical frequency shifts as linear with photon number.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the experimental setup and conceptual EP evolution; Figure 2 displays the SEM image and simulated mode profiles; Figure 3 illustrates Bloch-sphere trajectories and stability maps; Figure 4 shows the 2D map of eigenfrequencies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of EPs in optomechanics, describes the diamond platform and fabrication-induced symmetry breaking, presents the experimental observation of the EP and its topological signatures, and discusses the implications for tunable spin-mechanical coupling.

Abstract

Multimode cavity optomechanical systems allow light to couple otherwise non-interacting mechanical resonators, enabling non-Hermitian phenomena such as exceptional points, where eigenfrequencies and eigenvectors of coupled modes coalesce. Accessing an exceptional point and its nearby parameter space is a first step towards chiral mode dynamics and topological state transfer. Diamond optomechanical devices support strong coherent optomechanical coupling required to tune resonances to an exceptional point, as well as strain-coupling to spin-defects for hybrid quantum technologies, but have not yet been used for multimode non-Hermitian physics. Here we tune to an exceptional point in a diamond optomechanical crystal, which uses structural symmetry breaking to produce two high-frequency mechanical resonances coupled to an optical cavity. The exceptional point is reached within a stable operating window below the phonon-lasing threshold, and we observe asymmetric redistribution of optomechanical damping and anti-damping between hybridized modes. These results establish diamond optomechanical crystals as a platform for non-Hermitian optomechanics, opening routes to topological mechanical dynamics in hybrid spin-phonon interfaces.

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical generalization of the Hall effect in multi-leg bosonic ladders using bosonization. It establishes a universal connection between Hall resistance and the density derivative of charge stiffness and characterizes how temperature affects the Hall response in Meissner and Vortex phases.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for many-body dynamics and AMO physics as it provides analytical predictions for transport properties (Hall effect) in synthetic gauge fields, which are a key component of ultracold atom experiments in optical lattices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to generalize the Hall effect and Hall resistance from two-leg to N-leg bosonic ladders and to understand the temperature dependence of the Hall response in different magnetic flux regimes.

Main result. The Hall resistance is found to be proportional to the derivative of the logarithm of the charge stiffness with respect to density, a result that is robust for N-leg ladders. Additionally, Hall imbalance corrections are exponentially small in the Meissner phase at low temperatures but follow a power law in the Vortex phase.

Method. The authors use bosonization and perturbation theory in the band curvature, employing a basis transformation with Hahn/Chebyshev polynomials to decouple modes and Green's function techniques for temperature-dependent analysis.

Model / system. The system consists of interacting bosonic N-leg ladders threaded by a magnetic flux (artificial gauge fields), modeled using a bosonized Hamiltonian including Tomonaga-Luttinger Liquid terms, transverse interchain hopping, and band curvature.

Key observables. Hall resistance (Rh), Hall voltage (Vh), Hall imbalance (Ph), and charge stiffness (uK).

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic flux (B), temperature (T), particle density (rho0), transverse hopping (t_perp), and Tomonaga-Luttinger parameter (K).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a perturbative expansion in band curvature and focuses on the low-field limit and the validity of the bosonization approach for the ladder geometries.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the temperature dependence of the Hall imbalance in the Meissner phase, Vortex phase, and at the commensurate-incommensurate transition for various magnetic flux values.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of N-leg ladders, applies bosonization and basis transformations to derive Hall resistance formulas, analyzes the temperature dependence of the Hall imbalance in different flux regimes, and discusses the robustness of the results.

Abstract

We use bosonization to analyze the ground state Hall response of interacting bosonic N-leg ladders threaded by a flux. We derive an explicit expression of the Hall imbalance in a perturbative expansion in the band curvature, retaining fully the interactions. For small magnetic field the Hall resistance is proportional to the derivative of the logarithm of the charge stiffness with respect to density, generalizing the result obtained in the two leg case. We also consider the effect of temperature, and establish that at low temperature, corrections to the Hall resistance are exponentially small in the Meissner phase.

Mechanical Squeezed-Fock Gravimeter

Authors: Rozhin Yousefjani, Saif Al-Kuwari
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.28289v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

Summary. This paper proposes a new type of quantum gravimeter using squeezed-Fock states in a levitated mechanical system. By using a two-phonon pump to create squeezing, the gravitational signal is exponentially amplified. The study highlights a fundamental trade-off where squeezing enhances the signal but also converts mechanical damping into anisotropic noise.

Why it may be interesting. It presents a novel application of squeezed-state engineering and open quantum system dynamics (anisotropic noise) to enhance the precision of a quantum sensor.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing high-sensitivity, compact quantum gravimeters that overcome the mass-independent sensitivity bottleneck and the weak intrinsic nonlinearity of nanomechanical resonators.

Main result. The authors propose a mechanical squeezed-Fock qubit gravimeter that provides exponential signal amplification via the anti-squeezed quadrature while preserving mass scaling, though it introduces a trade-off with anisotropic decoherence.

Method. The study uses Bogoliubov transformations, the Rotating-Wave Approximation (RWA), and Quantum Estimation Theory (calculating QFI and CFI) to analyze sensitivity and optimal readout strategies.

Model / system. A levitated mesoscopic mechanical particle modeled as a Duffing oscillator driven by a detuned two-phonon pump, described by an effective Hamiltonian in a squeezed-Fock basis and a master equation for zero-temperature damping.

Key observables. Gravity-induced transition rate, excited squeezed-Fock population, Bloch vector components, and Quantum/Classical Fisher Information.

Important parameters / regimes. Squeezing parameter (r), Duffing nonlinearity (D), mechanical damping rate (gamma_0), detuning (delta), and pump amplitude (Ap).

Assumptions / limitations. The use of the Rotating-Wave Approximation (RWA) and the assumption of a weak-force regime where the gravity-induced coupling is much smaller than the qubit gap.

Figures summary. Figures include a map of the RWA validity in the (r, D/D_crit) plane and a demonstration of the locally optimal projective readout for the decoherent gravimeter.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific problem of gravimetry, defines the mechanical squeezed-Fock model and Hamiltonian, analyzes the signal enhancement via squeezing, evaluates the impact of decoherence on sensitivity, and determines the optimal measurement readout strategy.

Abstract

Levitated mechanical systems are promising candidates for quantum gravimetry, as gravity couples directly to their center-of-mass motion, enabling the large mass of a mesoscopic particle to serve as a sensing resource. In this paper, we propose a mechanical squeezed-Fock qubit gravimeter using a Duffing oscillator that is driven by a detuned two-phonon pump. In the squeezed-Fock basis, the gravitational force couples to the anti-squeezed quadrature, which enhances the gravity-induced transition rate while preserving the direct mass scaling of the mechanical force coupling. We show that sensitivity improves with reduced effective qubit splitting that is controlled by the squeezing parameter and the Duffing nonlinearity. We further analyze mechanical damping and show that squeezing converts ordinary dissipation into anisotropic qubit noise, setting a practical trade-off between signal amplification and decoherence rate. These results identify the mechanical squeezed-Fock qubit as a new platform for quantum-enhanced gravimetry.

Abstract

Due to their exceptional isolation from the environment, magnetically levitated particles are explored as extremely sensitive mechanical sensors. For future gravity experiments on quantum superpositions, such systems need to be cooled close to their ground state. To demonstrate the combination of state of the art vibration isolation, milligram levitated high Q mechanical resonators and position detection with low noise, we present linear feedback cooling of a magnetically levitated gravity sensor to below 2 picometer amplitude and below 10 millikelvin mode temperature for two translational modes (the x- and y-mode) simultaneously. The sensor is a levitating permanent magnet in a type I superconducting trap, where its six resonance frequencies are measured with a superconducting coil coupled to a DC SQUID. This signal is measured with a lock-in amplifier and a feedback signal is sent to a piezoelectric actuator, allowing the cooling of resonant modes at 50.6 and 68.0 Hz simultaneously. These two translational modes have Q factors of $3.8 \cdot 10^6$ and $5.5 \cdot 10^6$ respectively. The experiment is mounted inside a dry dilution refrigerator where it is vibrationally attenuated with 110-130 dB at these frequencies. In this work, we discuss future improvements on the setup which may enable quantum ground state cooling on a magnetically levitated particle, that has previously been shown to be a gravitational sensor.

Summary. The paper reveals that while the Bures metric appears singular near pure states in qubits, higher-dimensional quantum systems possess intrinsic conical singularities. It classifies how different Lindblad-driven processes, such as purification and decoherence, interact with this complex geometry.

Why it may be interesting. It provides fundamental insights into the geometric landscape of open quantum systems, specifically how the dimensionality of the Hilbert space fundamentally changes the nature of quantum state space singularities.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating the geometric structure and curvature singularities of the Bures metric on the mixed-state manifold near rank-changing points.

Main result. The paper proves that for N=2 systems, the Bures metric divergence is a coordinate artifact, whereas for N>=3 systems, the geometry forms a metric cone with genuine curvature singularities (Dirac delta or power-law divergence).

Method. Geometric analysis using Bloch vector parameterization, coordinate transformations, and the calculation of the Riemann curvature tensor and scalar curvature for conical metrics.

Model / system. N-level quantum systems (specifically N=2 and N=3) undergoing Lindblad master equation dynamics, including scenarios of asymptotic purification, finite-time purification, and decoherence.

Key observables. Bures metric components, scalar curvature, and the Quantum Geometric Tensor.

Important parameters / regimes. Rank of the density matrix, dimensionality of the system (N), spectral ratio (zeta), and the radial distance to the pure-state boundary.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis focuses on the transition between full-rank and rank-1 states; the Hamiltonian is assumed to be zero for simplicity in the Lindblad constructions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows time evolution of the Bloch vector and metric components; Figure 5 illustrates the metric cone structure and radial geodesics; Figure 6 shows 2D and 3D conical singularities; Figure 8 shows the angular deficit in an unfolded cone.

Paper structure. The paper first compares N=2 and N>=3 geometries, then analyzes Lindblad evolution trajectories (geodesic vs non-geodesic), followed by a detailed construction of the metric cone and its curvature singularities, and concludes with experimental implications.

Abstract

We elucidate the Bures metric in quantum state space near a rank-changing point of the density matrix and show contrasting behavior for two-level ($N=2$) systems versus higher-level systems. Due to the smooth pure-state boundary for $N=2$, we prove the apparent metric divergences to be merely coordinate artifacts and present three Lindblad processes exhibiting qualitatively different evolution near rank-changing points, showing geodesic approach, power-law scaling, and pure-state escape law. For higher-dimensional ($N\ge 3$) systems, the geometry near a rank-changing point differs fundamentally. Under suitable restrictions of the density matrix and its approach towards a pure state, the Bures metric reduces to a conical metric with the pure state at the cone tip. Such a conic geometry leads to genuine curvature singularities: A two-dimensional cone exhibits a Dirac delta-function curvature near the tip while a higher-dimensional cone shows a power-law divergence of the curvature towards the cone tip. A construction of Lindblad evolution for $N=3$ systems with conic singularities is presented, along with possible implications for future experimental and theoretical research.
