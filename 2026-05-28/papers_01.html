Highlighted author(s): Helmut Ritsch
Authors: Eric Sánchez-Llorente, Helmut Ritsch, Maria Moreno-Cardoner

Summary. This paper shows that instead of being purely detrimental, environmental dephasing can enhance light absorption in a ring of quantum emitters by moving population into long-lived subradiant modes. This mechanism is particularly effective with thermal dephasing and can lead to absorption efficiencies exceeding the sum of independent atoms.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a counter-intuitive insight into open quantum systems, showing that environmental noise can be a constructive resource for enhancing light-matter interaction, which is highly relevant to quantum optics and light-harvesting research.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating whether environmental decoherence, such as dephasing and phonon coupling, can be utilized as a resource to enhance single-photon absorption in subwavelength nano-rings of quantum emitters.

Main result. The study demonstrates that decoherence can enhance absorption by redistributing population from superradiant (bright) modes into subradiant (dark) modes, with thermal dephasing potentially allowing collective absorption to surpass that of independent emitters.

Method. The authors use a Lindblad master equation approach within the single-excitation manifold, employing the Born-Markov approximation and analyzing steady-state populations and absorption cross-sections.

Model / system. A nano-ring of N dipole-coupled two-level quantum emitters in the deep subwavelength regime, modeled with a non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian including dipole-dipole interactions and collective radiative decay.

Key observables. Absorption cross-section, scattering cross-section, and excited state population.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of emitters (N), interparticle spacing (d/lambda_0), dephasing rates (pure and thermal), trapping rate (Gamma_T), and temperature (beta).

Assumptions / limitations. Single-excitation manifold approximation, weak-field/weak-driving limit, Born-Markov approximation, and fixed emitter positions.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the nano-ring geometry and the level scheme of collective modes; Figure 2 illustrates the absorption cross-section as a function of dephasing and emitter number, showing the emergence of enhancement.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of decoherence as a resource, defines the nano-ring model and master equation, analyzes single-atom vs. collective behavior under local dephasing, extends the study to thermal dephasing and temperature effects, and concludes with finite-size and incoherent illumination analysis.

Abstract

Decoherence is mostly detrimental in quantum information and quantum optics applications. However, the interplay between environment-induced incoherent dynamics and unitary evolution can give rise to novel quantum many-body phenomena that can be harnessed as a useful resource. As is well known, in dense subwavelength atomic arrays only a single collective eigenmode in the single-excitation manifold couples strongly to free-space radiation, exhibiting superradiant spontaneous emission. Most of the remaining eigenstates form a manifold of weakly radiative modes, giving rise to long-lived subradiant excitations. Here we demonstrate that populating these subradiant modes via additional decoherence mechanisms, such as dephasing or coupling to phonons, can significantly enhance single-photon absorption in a nanoring of quantum emitters. Such nanoring geometry is particularly appealing due to its unique optical properties and its resemblance to natural light-harvesting complexes, which serve as efficient antennas in photosynthesis. Our findings may shed light on fundamental aspects of energy absorption in nature; despite the much greater complexity of biological systems, they may nonetheless operate according to similar underlying optical principles.

Summary. This paper introduces Photon Correlation Microscopy to study many-body correlations in 1D dipolar excitons. By measuring photon statistics, the authors observe a transition from photon bunching to antibunching, signaling a crossover from a compressible thermal gas to an incompressible, number-stabilized state. This technique provides a new way to probe correlated electronic phases using optical tools.

Why it may be interesting. It establishes a powerful new bridge between quantum optics and many-body physics, showing how photon statistics can serve as a non-invasive probe for complex phases like Wigner crystals or Mott insulators in semiconductor systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Bridging the gap between quantum optics and many-body physics by developing a method to probe real-space many-body correlations and compressibility in quantum matter at mesoscopic scales.

Main result. The authors demonstrate Photon Correlation Microscopy (PCM) to observe a density-driven crossover in a 1D dipolar exciton gas from a compressible thermal phase (photon bunching) to an incompressible, number-stabilized quasi-crystal phase (photon antibunching).

Method. The study combines experimental Hanbury Brown-Twiss interferometry and power-dependent spectroscopy with numerical many-body simulations using the Multi-Configuration Time-Dependent Hartree method for bosons (MCTDH-B).

Model / system. A 1D ensemble of dipolar excitons confined in mesoscopic, gate-defined traps at a lateral MoSe2-WSe2 heterojunction, characterized by large permanent in-plane electric dipole moments and long radiative lifetimes.

Key observables. Second-order photon correlation function g2(0), photon bunching/antibunching, isothermal compressibility, particle number fluctuations, and photon recovery time.

Important parameters / regimes. Trap widths (50, 100, 150 nm), pump power, dipolar interaction strength, and the ratio of sampling length to coherence length.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to be in quasi-equilibrium (thermalization time is much shorter than radiative lifetime), and the photon statistics are assumed to track the local density fluctuations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 provides a schematic of PCM and the transition from thermal to incompressible phases; Figure 4 shows power-dependent photon correlation measurements for different trap sizes; Supplemental Figure S6 shows chemical potential and density distributions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the PCM technique, describes the experimental platform and confinement method, presents the observation of the phase crossover via photon statistics, provides a theoretical validation of the link between photon and matter correlations, and concludes with scaling and dynamical analysis.

Abstract

Light and matter share fundamental statistical properties, yet the experimental probes of quantum optics and many-body physics have largely evolved along separate trajectories. While many-body physics explores emergent collective phenomena, quantum optics has refined the measurement of correlations between individual photons. Here, we introduce photon correlation microscopy (PCM) - which bridges the two domains by leveraging correlations of emitted light to probe the correlations in quantum matter at mesoscopic scales. We demonstrate this approach using a one-dimensional (1D) ensemble of dipolar excitons confined at a lateral monolayer MoSe$_2$-WSe$_2$ heterojunction. We use gate-defined potentials to confine the 1D excitons to a mesoscopic lengthscale to enhance the visibility of matter correlations in the emitted photon field. Power-dependent spectroscopy reveals a transition from a compressible to an incompressible phase, signaled by the simultaneous saturation of the emission intensity and energy blueshift, which is supported by numerical simulations. Through this crossover, photon correlation measurements show a striking evolution from bunching at low densities to antibunching at high densities. This constitutes a many-body blockade of photon emission emerging directly from a number-stabilized state, driven by collective dipolar repulsion. Our results establish PCM as a powerful probe of many-body physics through the lens of quantum optics, extensible to a broad class of correlated electronic phases, while pointing toward a route to generating non-classical light through many-body correlations.

Summary. This paper reports the first experimental observation of non-equilibrium dynamics initiated from a many-body critical state using a trapped-ion quantum processor. By using MERA-based state preparation and a Floquet drive, the authors successfully measured the central charge of the Ising universality class. This establishes a versatile framework for studying complex, scale-invariant quantum dynamics on digital hardware.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant for many-body dynamics as it demonstrates a scalable way to explore Conformal Field Theory (CFT) predictions and non-equilibrium phase transitions using a digital quantum processor, moving beyond simple product-state initializations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Simulating non-equilibrium dynamics starting from many-body critical states is difficult due to bottlenecks in state preparation, hardware performance, and classical verification.

Main result. The researchers experimentally observed conformal Floquet dynamics on a trapped-ion processor, successfully extracting a central charge of c = 0.575 +/- 0.036, which is consistent with the Ising universality class prediction of c = 1/2.

Method. The study uses a hardware-tailored MERA circuit for critical state preparation and a deep Floquet drive implemented via Trotter-Suzuki decomposition, supplemented by error mitigation techniques like dynamical decoupling and parity post-selection.

Model / system. A 1D Transverse-Field Ising Model (TFIM) implemented on Quantinuum's Helios fully-connected trapped-ion quantum processor using Ba+ ions.

Key observables. Loschmidt echo, energy density, and central charge (c).

Important parameters / regimes. Heating and non-heating phases, driving periods (T0, T1), and the deformation amplitude/wavenumber.

Assumptions / limitations. The spatial profile of the deformation is simplified to a single harmonic sector, and the initial state is assumed to be the ground state of a uniform Hamiltonian.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the Floquet circuit setup and architecture; Figure 2 presents the phase diagram of the Loschmidt echo; Figure 4 shows the critical boundary in a perturbed Ising chain; Figure 5 compares experimental data to ED and CFT predictions; Figure 6 demonstrates the effectiveness of error mitigation.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the challenge of critical state dynamics, describes the experimental platform and MERA-based state preparation, details the Floquet driving protocol and error mitigation, presents the observation of heating/non-heating phases and central charge extraction, and concludes with the scalability and universality of the framework.

Abstract

Quantum simulations are traditionally confined to exploring dynamics starting from unentangled or low-entanglement states due to severe bottlenecks in protocol design, hardware performance, and classical verification. Here, we report the first experimental observation of non-equilibrium dynamics initiated directly from a many-body critical state. Using a fully-connected trapped-ion processor, we prepare the critical ground state of a transverse-field Ising model via a hardware-tailored, logarithmic-depth quantum circuit based on multi-scale entanglement renormalization. Following this initialization, we apply a deep Floquet drive that maintains emergent conformal symmetry, enabling us to benchmark the lattice dynamics against analytical results from continuum theory. In the resulting conformal heating phase, we extract a central charge consistent with the Ising universality class ($c=1/2$) from the universal decay of the Loschmidt echo and observe spatial energy localization predicted by field theory. Conversely, the non-heating phase exhibits global finite-time revivals. This work establishes a scalable and versatile framework for exploring critical quantum dynamics.

Summary. The researchers successfully created a large-scale array of 30 synchronized squeezed light beams using a single atomic vapor cell. By leveraging the movement of atoms between light beams, they achieved collective effects that improve the stability and purity of the quantum states. This provides a scalable pathway for generating multi-channel quantum light sources for imaging and computing.

Why it may be interesting. It demonstrates how dissipative coupling through a shared reservoir (flying atoms) can induce synchronization and collective enhancement in a many-body optical system, relevant to open quantum systems and quantum networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The challenge of scalable production of multiple beams of squeezed light for quantum information technologies and exploring the collective synchronization of these beams.

Main result. Demonstration of a 3-beam array of polarization-squeezed light with 2.03 dB of squeezing, showing that inter-channel coupling via atomic diffusion leads to synchronization and improved state purity.

Method. Experimental generation of squeezed light using polarization self-rotation (PSR) in a Rubidium vapor cell, with detection via balanced homodyne detection and characterization of array uniformity and synchronization.

Model / system. A 30-beam spatial array interacting with an 87-Rb atomic vapor in a paraffin-coated glass cell, modeled using a multi-level master equation describing atomic density matrix evolution and inter-channel coupling via a shared reservoir of atoms.

Key observables. Squeezing level (dB), squeezing angle, noise power vs. laser power, squeezing bandwidth, and time-resolved recovery dynamics after perturbations.

Important parameters / regimes. Number of channels (1 to 31), laser power per channel, cell temperature (55 to 66.7 C), beam size, and detection frequency.

Assumptions / limitations. Neglect of certain experimental imperfections like extreme inhomogeneities and optical losses in the numerical models; assumes atoms in the dark region act as a global reservoir.

Figures summary. Figures show atomic energy levels, experimental schematics, noise power vs. laser power for different array sizes, squeezing angle uniformity, and the impact of polarization defects and cell geometry on squeezing performance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scalability problem, describes the experimental setup and atomic mechanism, presents results on squeezing levels and array scaling, investigates synchronization and robustness to defects, and concludes with optimization strategies via numerical simulations.

Abstract

Quantum light sources such as squeezed light are essential for quantum information science and technologies, but the scalable production of multiple beams of them remains a challenge. Here,we experimentally demonstrate a novel approach to the generation of a large spatial array of polarization-squeezed light beams via atomic-coherence-enhanced nonlinear optical processes using a single atomic vapor cell. Unlike schemes based on independent squeezing generators, the squeezing dynamics of each channel here are governed by a common collective ground-state atomic coherence, produced by all input beams, homogenized by the thermal motion of the atoms, and protected against wall collisions by a paraffin coating. Consequently, the optical states of all channelsare coupled and regulated by each other via the moving atoms, leading to synchronization behavior.We realized a 30-beam array of polarization squeezed state with 2.03 dB of squeezing, experimentally verified the synchronization, and observed improved purity of the squeezed state as well as the system response to perturbations when the size of the array increases. This work provides a pathway towards scalable high-performance quantum light sources for applications in precision measurement, quantum imaging and quantum information processing.

A multilevel tensor network compression technique for simulating Lindblad dynamics in superconducting circuits

Summary. This paper presents a multilevel tensor network technique to simulate the dissipative dynamics of superconducting bosonic circuits. By leveraging state purity, low entanglement, and a quantized representation of Fock space, the method achieves massive reductions in memory and time complexity. This allows complex simulations of phenomena like transmon ionization to be performed on a laptop rather than a supercomputer.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a highly efficient numerical framework for simulating open quantum systems with large local Hilbert spaces, specifically bridging the gap between complex many-body dynamics and accessible desktop-level computation.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The exponential growth of the Hilbert space dimension (the curse of dimensionality) makes simulating the Lindblad dynamics of large-scale bosonic systems, such as superconducting circuits, computationally prohibitive.

Main result. The authors developed a multilevel tensor network compression technique that achieves orders-of-magnitude speed-ups, reducing the computational resources required for transmon ionization simulations from supercomputing scales to laptop level.

Method. A three-axis compression approach combining global purification of the density matrix, Matrix Product State (MPS) representation for low entanglement, and Quantics Tensor Train (QTT) for efficient Fock space discretization using Tensor Cross Interpolation (TCI).

Model / system. Superconducting quantum circuits, specifically focusing on bosonic modes in cat qubits and transmon-cavity systems subject to Lindblad master equation dynamics involving photon loss and dephasing.

Key observables. Purity, Wigner functions, expectation values of field amplitude, transmon and cavity populations, and error rates (phase-flip and bit-flip).

Important parameters / regimes. Bond dimensions (purity chi_mu, entanglement chi_e, and quantics chi_q), photon loss rate (kappa_2), dephasing rate (kappa_phi), and the number of bits (R) in the QTT.

Assumptions / limitations. The density matrix is relatively pure or not far from pure, and the entanglement between different modes is moderate (qubit-like).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows memory reduction across compression stages; Figure 2 illustrates the QTT construction process; Figure 3 shows error convergence of the annihilation operator; Figure 4 shows fidelity of basis conversion; Figure 12/13 show population evolution, purity loss, and Hamiltonian eigenstate branches.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the computational problem, details the three-axis compression ansatz (Purification, MPS, and QTT), describes the numerical integration schemes (Kraus maps, TDVP, Magnus expansion), and validates the method through simulations of cat qubits and transmon ionization.

Abstract

Designing superconducting quantum hardware requires simulation tools that can account for various deviations from ideal scenarios. This, in turn, requires approaches that automatically detect certain structures and leverage them to make the computation affordable. Here, we develop a tensor network based technique to simulate the Lindblad dynamics of a few interacting bosonic modes with a focus on superconducting quantum circuits. The technique detects and takes advantage of two very common situations: (i) the density matrix being pure or not far from pure and (ii) the entanglement between different modes being moderate (typically qubit-like). However, (iii) the occupation of the modes can be arbitrarily high (making naïve truncations inefficient). To leverage these features, we use three different nested levels of tensor network compression: (i) we work with a global purification of the density matrix, (ii) we compress the connection between different modes to account for the moderate entanglement and (iii) we use a quantics representation of the Fock occupation number. We showcase the technique for the simulation of large cat qubits as well as for the ionization of transmon qubits, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speed-up with respect to brute force approaches. In the latter example, it brings the simulation, previously reported on a large supercomputing infrastructure, to laptop level. The favorable scaling with system size should bring genuine computer assisted design of these systems within scope.

Summary. This paper demonstrates that Critical Exceptional Points in open many-body systems can significantly enhance quantum spin squeezing. It establishes specific power-law scaling for squeezed and anti-squeezed variances near the transition and identifies the conditions under which this effect is robust against decoherence.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a novel mechanism for engineering highly anisotropic quantum correlations and steady-state entanglement by leveraging non-Hermitian singularities (CEPs) in driven-dissipative platforms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates whether Critical Exceptional Points (CEPs) in open many-body systems can be used to parametrically enhance quantum spin squeezing and how these effects scale near the transition.

Main result. Near a CEP, the optimally squeezed variance scales linearly with the order parameter (|Z|), while the anti-squeezed variance diverges (|Z|^{-1}), creating a highly anisotropic fluctuation plane. This enhancement is robust against certain dephasing channels but is destroyed by z-axis dephasing.

Method. The study uses a mean-field approach, linearized dynamics around the PT-broken fixed point, and the solution of the steady-state Lyapunov equation within a Gaussian approximation in the thermodynamic limit.

Model / system. The model consists of dissipative collective-spin systems governed by the GKSL master equation, specifically focusing on L-PT symmetric models such as a spin-boson system where a collective spin is coupled to a lossy cavity mode.

Key observables. Quantum spin squeezing parameter (Kitagawa-Ueda), Wineland parameter, covariance matrix, and the Husimi-Q distribution.

Important parameters / regimes. Dimensionless order parameter (Z), spin size (S), collective decay rate (kappa), transverse field (g), and dephasing rates (gamma).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the thermodynamic limit (large S), a Gaussian description of fluctuations, and specific constraints on the diffusion matrix (e.g., Dz = 0 for the primary scaling result).

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a schematic of mode coalescence at the CEP and an illustration of the increased anisotropy of the spin distribution on the Bloch sphere. Figure 2 demonstrates the scaling collapse of the inverse squeezing using a logarithmically corrected finite-size scaling form.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the concept of CEPs and their role in fluctuations, develops the mathematical framework for the L-PT symmetric collective spin model, derives the scaling laws for squeezing and anti-squeezing near the CEP, analyzes the robustness against dephasing, and concludes with finite-size scaling and experimental realizations.

Abstract

Critical exceptional points (CEPs) are nonequilibrium critical points in open many-body systems at which multiple collective excitation modes coalesce. CEPs are known to amplify classical fluctuations, but their effect on genuinely \textit{quantum} fluctuations remains unclear. Here, we show that dissipative collective-spin systems hosting CEPs exhibit parametrically enhanced steady-state \textit{quantum} spin squeezing. Close to the CEP, the optimally squeezed variance scales as $|Z|$, whereas the anti-squeezed variance diverges as $|Z|^{-1}$, with $Z$ the dimensionless order parameter. Importantly, the anti-squeezed fluctuation direction asymptotically aligns with the coalescing eigenvector of the stability matrix, reflecting the defective nature of the CEP dynamics. These scalings are robust against dephasing channels generated by spin components orthogonal to the coalesced critical collective mode. Our results identify CEPs as a route to engineering steady-state anisotropic quantum fluctuations and correlations in driven-dissipative platforms.

Summary. This paper introduces a method to calculate the entropy production rate of a Markovian system without needing to know transition rates or dwell times. By using only the sequence of states and total elapsed time, the method allows for the estimation of thermodynamic properties from incomplete trajectory data. It also demonstrates that choosing specific cycle bases can optimize the convergence of this estimator.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics, as it provides a way to quantify non-equilibrium properties and dissipation in systems where the full microscopic dynamics are not experimentally resolvable.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Calculating the entropy production rate in non-equilibrium systems is difficult when underlying transition rates, dwell times, or transition times are unknown or unobservable.

Main result. The author presents a new method to infer the entropy production rate using only the sequence of visited states and the total trajectory time, bypassing the need for transition rates or dwell times.

Method. The method uses a fundamental-cycle decomposition (spanning tree approach) to express entropy production in terms of the log-ratio of forward to reverse cycle occurrences.

Model / system. The model consists of Markovian systems with discrete microstates and continuous-time dynamics, represented by a strongly connected, reversible directed graph governed by a master equation.

Key observables. Entropy production rate, net flux, cycle affinity, and the number of times a cycle occurs in a trajectory.

Important parameters / regimes. Total trajectory time, sequence of states, and the choice of cycle basis for convergence optimization.

Assumptions / limitations. The underlying dynamics must be Markovian, the system must have a finite number of discrete states, and the graph must be strongly connected and reversible.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates a biological active transport system; Figure 2 shows spanning trees and cycle decomposition via chords; Figure 3 compares full vs. coarse-grained systems; Figure 4 demonstrates the convergence of the estimator using different cycle bases.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the problem of calculating entropy production, introduces the mathematical framework of Markov graphs and master equations, presents the new inference method based on cycle affinities, validates the method via Gillespie simulations, and discusses the impact of cycle basis selection and coarse-graining.

Abstract

The entropy production rate is central to the study of non-equilibrium systems. This parameter is closely connected to violation of time-reversal symmetry, energy consumption, efficiency, and other properties of interest; in short, it quantifies how far a system is from thermodynamic equilibrium. Standard formulas for the entropy production require knowledge of the system's underlying dynamics, but this knowledge may be hard to acquire in practice. Here, I present a method for inferring the entropy production rate of a Markovian system from the sequence of states that the system occupies on a single long trajectory or many shorter trajectories, and the total time of the trajectory or trajectories. The method does not require knowledge of the dwell times in the various states, the times at which transitions occur, or of the transition rates characterizing the Markov process.

Summary. This paper investigates how the intrinsic spin and coherence dynamics of 2D perovskites can be used to generate secure quantum communication signals. By analyzing the Holevo bound and secret-bit rates, the authors show that controlling material parameters like population time can optimize information throughput. The work establishes a framework for using nonlinear spectroscopy to design new materials for quantum key distribution.

Why it may be interesting. It bridges nonlinear spectroscopy with quantum information theory, demonstrating how many-body dynamics (exciton-biexciton interactions) and spin relaxation can be engineered to optimize the security and throughput of photonic quantum networks.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Evaluating the security metrics (Holevo bound and secret-bit rates) of quantum communication using nonlinear optical light sources generated from material-intrinsic dynamics.

Main result. Incorporating coherence time into the parameter space reduces the Holevo bound by 2.6-5.8%, and population-time encoding is found to be significantly more efficient for high-throughput communication than coherence-time encoding.

Method. The researchers use polarization-resolved interferometric measurements and field reconstruction to extract polarization density matrices, combined with a microscopic nonlinear response model for the Bloch vector.

Model / system. The physical system is a 2D organic-inorganic hybrid perovskite ((PEA)2PbI4) utilizing four-wave-mixing (FWM) signal fields, modeled via a reduced-order response-function framework involving single-exciton and biexciton resonances.

Key observables. Polarization contrast (C_pol), relative phase (delta_phi), Holevo bound (chi), effective secret-bit yield (Y_eff), and Bloch vector components.

Important parameters / regimes. Coherence time (tau), population/spin-relaxation time (T), detection wavelength (lambda), and spectral overlap (Xi_X,XXB).

Assumptions / limitations. The calculation assumes reconstructed fields are pure by construction, the system operates in the slow-modulation limit, and the exciton fine-structure splittings are smaller than the spectroscopic linewidths.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the FWM communication approach and spin dynamics; Fig 2 details the experimental setup; Fig 3 displays energy levels and transient absorption; Fig 4 shows polarization components and contrast; Fig 5 shows polarization modulation; Fig 6 presents Bloch-sphere representations; Fig 7 compares encoding efficiencies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the quantum communication problem, describes the experimental FWM setup and material properties, presents the field reconstruction and security metric derivation, analyzes the results across different spectral regimes and encoding strategies, and concludes with implications for material-intrinsic quantum engineering.

Abstract

Nonlinear optical light sources enable the generation of photons with quantum states that are intrinsically linked to underlying material dynamics, rather than imposed through external modulation. Here we investigate fundamental quantum communication metrics of four-wave-mixing signal fields generated by the two-dimensional perovskite (PEA)2PbI4. Using polarization-resolved interferometric measurements together with a microscopic nonlinear response model for the Bloch vector, we reconstruct effective single-photon polarization density matrices inferred from the experimental signal fields and evaluate the corresponding Holevo bound and effective secret-bit rates as a function of the coherence time, population time, and detection wavelength. We find that incorporating the coherence-time degree of freedom systematically lowers the Holevo bound by approximately 2.6-5.8% across the various excitonic resonances, indicating reduced distinguishability of the polarization states when the full multidimensional parameter space is sampled. To connect the polarization-state indistinguishability with experimentally achievable throughput, we further introduce an effective secret-bits-per-pulse metric that enables rapid evaluation of secure information throughput for candidate materials without requiring photon-number-resolved detection. For the present system, control of the population time via spin-dependent evolution yields substantially higher secret-bit rates than manipulation of the coherence time, while spectral regions associated with single-exciton and biexciton resonances define complementary operating regimes for secure communication. More broadly, this work positions nonlinear spectroscopy as a framework for exploring how emergent optical materials can generate and structure quantum states in ways that are advantageous for established quantum communication schemes.

Abstract

Subspace diagonalization techniques based on quantum sampling, such as quantum selected configuration interaction (QSCI) and sample-based quantum diagonalization (SQD), have recently emerged as promising quantum-centric approaches for approximating ground-state energies of many-body systems. However, their performance is fundamentally limited by an intrinsic trade-off between sampling efficiency and the sparsity of the ground-state wavefunction, which becomes particularly severe in strongly correlated systems. Here, we introduce a filter-assisted SQD protocol that engineers wavefunction sparsity via a quantum filter, i.e., a unitary transformation of the Hamiltonian designed to concentrate the ground-state weight onto a small number of computational basis states. Using the Gini coefficient as a robust sparsity measure, we establish a quantitative relationship between wavefunction sparsity and the resource requirements of SQD, providing theoretical bounds on the required subspace dimension and sampling cost. To realize the quantum filter, we employ a tensor-network-based circuit-encoding algorithm that maps target states to quantum circuits with controllable fidelity. We benchmark our approach on the quantum Ising model with transverse and longitudinal fields using both numerical simulations and quantum hardware experiments. Our results demonstrate that, compared with standard SQD, the proposed protocol significantly enhances wavefunction sparsity, reduces ground-state energy estimation errors by orders of magnitude, and substantially lowers sampling overhead. These findings establish filter-assisted subspace diagonalization as a powerful and scalable framework for quantum many-body calculations in the strongly correlated regime.

Abstract

We study correlations between two Unruh-DeWitt detectors coupled to a scalar field in a cylindrical cavity. Boundary conditions strongly modify the detector-correlation dynamics relative to free space. The entanglement negativity is suppressed in the cavity and vanishes for smaller separation as compared to the free space. Increasing the cavity radius does not recover the free-space behavior of the negativity. In contrast, mutual information and quantum discord remain nonzero over much larger separations. While the mutual information decays monotonically with separation, the quantum discord is enhanced near the cavity boundary. Our results demonstrate that geometric confinement can selectively suppress distillable entanglement while preserving and even enhancing more general non-classical correlations, providing a controlled setting to probe the hierarchy of correlations in quantum field theory.

Abstract

We run a numerical linked-cluster expansion with a quantum algorithm (NLCE+QA), computing ground-state energies and one quasi-particle dispersions in the thermodynamic limit using a 20-qubit trapped-ion quantum processing unit (QPU). The NLCE+QA framework extracts thermodynamic-limit properties from small-cluster calculations, making it naturally suited for near-term quantum devices. Projector-based block-diagonalization schemes such as projective cluster-additive transformation (PCAT) are essential to NLCE+QA, and they involve matrix inversion and square root operations that amplify measurement noise. A central question is therefore whether current hardware can provide expectation values that are accurate enough to withstand non-linear classical post-processing. We explore this challenge for the transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) in one dimension, on a ladder geometry, as well as in a longitudinal field in one dimension. For the quantum algorithm, we consider adiabatic state preparation (ASP), as well as a variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) trained on a classical device. The final expectation values are obtained from the QPU, using a novel alternative to the Hadamard test that we name the CX-test. We explore the regimes currently attainable on quantum devices and comment on the improvements needed for quantum computers to achieve results beyond classical reach.
