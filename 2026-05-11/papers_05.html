Microscopic Magnetism of A(TiO)Cu4(PO4)4 (A = Ba, Pb, Sr): 31P and 63,65Cu NMR Study

Authors: Riho Rästa, Ivo Heinmaa, Joosep Link, Yusuke Kousaka, Tsuyoshi Kimura, Yoshihiko Ihara, Kenta Kimura, Raivo Stern
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07946v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper uses NMR spectroscopy to investigate the microscopic magnetic properties of a family of square-cupola cuprates. It demonstrates how changing the A-site cation (Ba, Pb, Sr) alters the magnetic stacking and local covalency, providing insight into the electronic structure of these charge-transfer insulators.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to characterize the microscopic magnetism and electronic structure of the chiral square-cupola antiferromagnet Pb(TiO)Cu4(PO4)4 and compare it with its Ba and Sr analogues.

Main result. The researchers found that PbTCPO exhibits enhanced 31P internal fields due to ferroic stacking that prevents interlayer field cancellation, and confirmed the materials are ligand-hole-dominated charge-transfer insulators.

Method. Single-crystal 31P NMR, zero-field 63,65Cu NMR, and crystal-rotation NMR were used alongside point-charge electric-field-gradient calculations.

Model / system. The physical system is the A(TiO)Cu4(PO4)4 family of tetragonal square-cupola cuprates, specifically focusing on the Pb, Ba, and Sr variants.

Key observables. 31P Knight shift, 31P internal field, 63,65Cu internal field, quadrupole frequency, and hyperfine coupling tensors.

Important parameters / regimes. Neel temperature (TN approx 6.7 K), exchange scale (J/kB approx 8.0 K), and Cu hole occupancy (nd approx 0.20).

Assumptions / limitations. Point-charge EFG calculations include Sternheimer corrections to account for the electronic environment.

Figures summary. The notes do not provide a specific description of the figures, but they imply data regarding Knight shift anisotropy, line splitting, and EFG-related parameters.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the square-cupola family, details the NMR and magnetometry experimental setup, presents the 31P NMR results regarding hyperfine coupling and criticality, discusses 63,65Cu ZFNMR results for local fields and EFG, and concludes with a comparison of the A-site substitution effects.

Abstract

We report a comprehensive NMR study of the chiral square-cupola antiferromagnet Pb(TiO)Cu$_4$(PO$_4$)$_4$ and compare its microscopic hyperfine and local-field parameters with the Ba/Sr analogues in the $A$(TiO)Cu$_4$(PO$_4$)$_4$ family. Above $T_{\rm N}\simeq 6.7$ K, the $^{31}$P Knight shift tracks the bulk susceptibility and yields nearly isotropic transferred hyperfine couplings $H_{\rm hf}^{[010]}=6.77(3)$ and $H_{\rm hf}^{[001]}=6.19(3)$ kOe/$μ_{\rm B}$. Below $T_{\rm N}$, the frequency-swept $^{31}$P spectrum splits into three lines, in contrast to the four-line pattern reported for BaTCPO. The line separation tracks the onset of the static $^{31}$P internal field with a power-law exponent $β\simeq 0.23$, consistent with quasi-two-dimensional criticality. Crystal-rotation $^{31}$P NMR in the ordered state resolves all eight symmetry-related P sites and their site-dependent anisotropy. In the ordered state, zero-field $^{63,65}$Cu NMR gives a Cu-site internal field $B_{\rm int}=14.50(6)$ T and a quadrupole frequency $ν_Q=32.72(5)$ MHz, while point-charge electric-field-gradient calculations including Sternheimer corrections yield an on-site Cu hole occupancy $n_d=0.20(4)$, consistent with a ligand-hole-dominated charge-transfer character. Comparing PbTCPO with BaTCPO and SrTCPO, we find that the transferred hyperfine coupling $H_{\rm hf}$ varies across the series, reflecting changes in local Cu-O-P covalency, whereas the ordered-state $^{31}$P internal field in PbTCPO is $69.5$ mT, considerably higher than in BaTCPO ($35.6$ mT) and SrTCPO ($34.6$ mT). This enhancement is not captured by dipolar terms alone and points to the combined effects of transferred contributions and stacking-dependent cancellation.

Summary. This paper provides the first experimental characterization of the magnetic and electronic properties of the kagome material CrRhAs. It reveals a noncollinear antiferromagnetic structure and strong electron correlations, specifically highlighting a discrepancy between experimental magnetic coupling and previous theoretical predictions.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to experimentally investigate the magnetic structure and physical properties of CrRhAs, a predicted kagome metal, to verify previous DFT predictions and understand its electronic correlations.

Main result. The researchers discovered a noncollinear antiferromagnetic structure with ferromagnetic second-nearest-neighbor coupling that contradicts previous DFT predictions, and identified CrRhAs as a strongly correlated kagome metal with a large Kadowaki-Woods ratio.

Method. The study uses powder neutron diffraction for magnetic structure determination, X-ray diffraction and XPS for structural characterization, and PPMS measurements for transport and thermal properties.

Model / system. The system is CrRhAs, a ZrNiAl-type intermetallic compound featuring a distorted Cr-based kagome lattice with noncentrosymmetric hexagonal space group P622m.

Key observables. Néel temperature (TN), magnetic propagation vector (k), Hall coefficient (RH), longitudinal resistivity (rho_xx), Kadowaki-Woods ratio (alpha), and magnetic susceptibility (chi).

Important parameters / regimes. TN = 149 K, theta_CW = -479 K, alpha = 33.9 micro-ohm cm mol^2 K^2/J^2, and propagation vector k = (1/3, 1/3, 1/2).

Paper structure. The paper begins with structural and chemical characterization, moves to the determination of the magnetic structure via neutron diffraction, analyzes magnetic exchange interactions, investigates electronic transport and thermal properties, and concludes with a discussion on electron correlations.

Abstract

CrRhAs was theoretically proposed to be a kagome metal with unusual magnetic ground states; however, little is known about its magnetic structure and physical properties experimentally. Here, we present an experimental investigation of CrRhAs with ZrNiAl-type structure and a distorted Cr kagome lattice. CrRhAs is an antiferromagnet with TN = 149 K. Powder neutron diffraction analysis reveals a noncollinear antiferromagnetic structure with propagation vector k = (1/3, 1/3, 1/2), which features a ferromagnetic second nearest neighbor coupling in the kagome plane that is different from the prediction in previous density functional theory calculations. Furthermore, CrRhAs exhibits anomalous electrical transport properties which are possibly related to multiband effects and strong spin fluctuations. For the temperature-dependent longitudinal resistivity \r{ho}xx, it is semiconductinglike above TN and becomes metallic below TN . The Hall coefficients exhibit two sign changes near 70 and 300 K. Combined with the results of heat capacity measurements, a large Kadowaki-Woods ratio α = 33.9 μΩ cm mol2 K2/J2 is obtained. The above results suggest CrRhAs is a strongly correlated kagome metal with multiband and noncollinear magnetic structure features.

On the single-Hessian Gaussian wavepacket dynamics

Authors: Davide Barbiero, Jiří J. L. Vaníček
Type: theory · Category: numerical methods · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07440v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable

Summary. This paper presents a more efficient version of Gaussian wavepacket dynamics (single-Hessian GWD) that maintains the accuracy of more expensive methods while being numerically more stable. By using symplectic integrators, the method avoids energy drift and preserves the underlying geometric structure of the wavepacket manifold. The approach is validated through successful simulations of complex molecular spectra.

Why it may be interesting. It introduces a more efficient, numerically stable, and symplectic-preserving alternative to standard Gaussian wavepacket dynamics, which is crucial for large-scale molecular quantum dynamics simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The computational cost of Heller's local harmonic Gaussian wavepacket dynamics (GWD) is high, and traditional methods often suffer from energy drift and lack of symplectic structure conservation.

Main result. The authors derive a symplectic formulation for single-Hessian GWD that conserves the non-canonical symplectic structure and avoids energy drift, achieving accuracy comparable to local harmonic GWD with significantly higher efficiency.

Method. A new symplectic derivation of the equations of motion for single-Hessian GWD, implemented using high-order time-stepping geometric integrators that are time-reversible and norm-conserving.

Model / system. The method is applied to molecular dynamics, specifically the first excited-state surface of ammonia, the photoelectron spectrum of the difluorocarbene anion, and the absorption spectrum of methylamine.

Key observables. Vibronic spectra, photoelectron spectrum, absorption spectrum, and the evolution of wavepacket width.

Important parameters / regimes. The asymptotic error scales as O(hbar); the choice of reference Hessian is identified as a key factor for spectral sensitivity.

Assumptions / limitations. The dynamics are assumed to be bounded in smooth potentials to ensure energy conservation.

Paper structure. The paper provides a symplectic derivation of single-Hessian GWD, introduces high-order geometric integrators, presents analytical expressions for wavepacket evolution, and validates the method through ab initio simulations of ammonia, difluorocarbene anion, and methylamine.

Abstract

Single-Hessian Gaussian wavepacket dynamics (GWD) significantly reduces the computational burden of Heller's local harmonic GWD, while maintaining comparable accuracy in approximating vibronic spectra. Here, we provide a new, symplectic derivation of the equations of motion of single-Hessian GWD and show that, unlike the local harmonic version, this method conserves the non-canonical symplectic structure on the manifold of Gaussian wavepackets and$-$for bounded dynamics in smooth potentials$-$avoids the drift of energy. Our numerical results suggest that, despite being much more efficient than the local harmonic variant, the single-Hessian GWD exhibits the same $\mathcal{O}(\hbar)$ asymptotic error in averages of observables. To further accelerate numerical simulations, we implement high-order time-stepping geometric integrators that are time-reversible and conserve the norm and symplectic structure exactly, regardless of the time step. In addition, we present explicit expressions for the exact evolution of the width of a single-Hessian Gaussian wavepacket in a general potential, as well as for the exact evolution of the whole wavepacket in a global harmonic potential. Using on-the-fly ab initio Gaussian wavepacket dynamics on the first excited-state surface of ammonia, we numerically confirm the conservation of geometric properties by these integrators and demonstrate that high-order integrators can enhance both accuracy and computational efficiency. We also compute the photoelectron spectrum of the difluorocarbene anion and the absorption spectrum of methylamine, and find that, in comparison with experiment, single-Hessian GWD outperforms global harmonic models and matches the accuracy of local harmonic GWD. Finally, we identify which spectral features are sensitive to the choice of reference Hessian.

Abstract

Phase-field simulations provide mechanistic descriptions of microstructure evolution, but repeated high-fidelity integration over long horizons and broad parameter spaces remains computationally expensive. We present PFNet, a physics-informed neural operator framework that advances microstructural states by learning conditional evolution operators rather than direct correlations. PFNet combines a diffusion-inspired U-Net with periodic padding, entropy-based state conditioning and thermodynamic-parameter modulation to encode boundary consistency, instantaneous ordering state and changes in the free-energy landscape. For Cahn-Hilliard coarsening, PFNet achieves accurate one-step prediction and stable autoregressive rollouts across composition, gradient-energy coefficient, coarsening stage and morphology class, with errors concentrated near diffuse interfaces and topology-changing regions. The same framework extends to a four-channel martensitic-transformation benchmark without martensite-specific redesign. These results indicate that physics-informed operator learning can provide transferable surrogates for phase-field dynamics and broader energy-dissipative dynamical systems.

Price and Payoff: Non-Determinism in Fault Tolerant Quantum Computation

Authors: Aditi Awasthi, Sayam Sethi, Sahil Khan, Gokul Subramanian Ravi, Jonathan Mark Baker
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07983v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable

Summary. This paper demonstrates that treating magic state production as a stochastic process rather than a deterministic one allows for much more efficient resource allocation in fault-tolerant quantum computing. By accounting for the 'demand smoothing' effect, architects can significantly reduce the number of required factories and the overall space-time volume of quantum programs.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Current deterministic methods for provisioning magic-state factories in fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC) either over-provision resources for worst-case scenarios or under-provision for average demand, leading to inefficient space-time volume usage.

Main result. Stochastic-aware provisioning can reduce space-time volume by up to 27% and factory requirements by up to 30% by accounting for the demand-smoothing effects of non-deterministic magic state production.

Method. The authors developed a simulation framework that couples circuit scheduling with various stochastic magic state production models (distillation, cultivation, and Rz synthesis) to quantify the impact of non-determinism.

Model / system. The study focuses on fault-tolerant quantum computing architectures utilizing quantum error correction (QEC) codes augmented with magic states produced via distillation, cultivation, or Rz synthesis.

Key observables. Space-time volume, peak per-cycle resource demand, total execution time, and factory allocation requirements.

Important parameters / regimes. Magic state production mechanisms (distillation, cultivation, Rz synthesis), stochasticity of production, and factory count.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes that magic state production is a stochastic process that impacts the availability of resources for circuit execution.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of deterministic vs. stochastic provisioning, presents a new simulation framework, analyzes the dual effect of non-determinism (price vs. payoff), compares different preparation mechanisms, and concludes with recommendations for standard resource planning.

Abstract

A promising approach to achieving scalable fault-tolerant quantum computation is the use of quantum error correction (QEC) codes augmented with magic states i.e. resource states produced via distillation, cultivation, or $R_z$ synthesis and teleported into the circuit as needed. Because magic-state production dominates the space-time volume of fault-tolerant programs, system architects must decide how many production units to allocate. Current approaches rely on deterministic analysis that either provisions for worst-case peak demand (wasting valuable qubit resources on factories that are never simultaneously utilized) or assumes average demand, which increases execution time. In this work, we build a simulation framework that couples circuit scheduling with different stochastic magic state production models, and use it to quantify the impact of non-determinism on circuit execution. We show that non-determinism has a dual effect that deterministic models cannot capture: it inflates total execution time (the price), while deflating peak per-cycle resource demand (the payoff). For distillation-based architectures, this demand smoothing shifts the space-time-optimal provisioning point: fewer factories are needed to minimize space-time volume than deterministic analysis predicts. Across benchmarks, stochastic-aware provisioning reduces space-time volume by up to 27% compared to the deterministic optimum for distillation, while requiring up to 30% fewer factories. We characterize these effects across each preparation mechanism, map the resulting design-space tradeoffs, and demonstrate that static resource estimation systematically mis-characterizes the cost of fault-tolerant execution. Our results establish that stochastic-aware analysis is necessary for right-sizing the factory allocations and should replace deterministic heuristics as the standard methodology for FTQC resource planning.

Abstract

Spectral Dynamics in Deep Networks: Feature Learning, Outlier Escape, and Learning Rate Transfer

Authors: Clarissa Lauditi, Cengiz Pehlevan, Blake Bordelon
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07870v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable

Summary. This paper provides a theoretical framework to track how the eigenvalues of neural network weights evolve during training. It shows that certain scaling regimes ($\mu$P) allow for stable learning dynamics across different network widths, whereas others are highly sensitive to width. The study also distinguishes between simple tasks and complex tasks like language modeling based on how the spectral bulk responds.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding the evolution of the spectral properties (bulk and outliers) of hidden weights in wide neural networks during training.

Main result. The authors develop a two-level dynamical mean-field theory that predicts how outliers evolve and demonstrate that the $\mu$P scaling allows for width-consistent hyperparameter transfer, whereas NTK parameterization does not.

Method. Development of a two-level dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT) to track both bulk and outlier spectral dynamics in spiked ensembles.

Model / system. Wide neural networks, specifically infinite-width nonlinear networks in mean-field/$\mu$P scaling and deep linear networks in the proportional high-dimensional limit.

Key observables. Hidden-weight spectra, bulk and outlier spectral dynamics, and the leading NTK mode growth.

Important parameters / regimes. Training time, network width, output scale, initialization variance, and the ratio of width, input dimension, and sample size.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes spiked ensembles where spike directions remain statistically dependent on the random bulk; it also considers the infinite-width and proportional high-dimensional limits.

Paper structure. The paper introduces a two-level DMFT, applies it to nonlinear $\mu$P scaling and deep linear networks, compares $\mu$P vs NTK parameterization, and extends the analysis to tasks with large output channels using a toy model.

Abstract

We study the evolution of hidden-weight spectra in wide neural networks trained by (stochastic) gradient descent. We develop a two-level dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT) that jointly tracks bulk and outlier spectral dynamics for spiked ensembles whose spike directions remain statistically dependent on the random bulk. We apply this framework to two settings: (1) infinite-width nonlinear networks in mean-field/$μ$P scaling and (2) deep linear networks in the proportional high-dimensional limit, where width, input dimension, and sample size diverge with fixed ratios. Our theory predicts how outliers evolve with training time, width, output scale, and initialization variance. In deep linear networks, $μ$P yields width-consistent outlier dynamics and hyperparameter transfer, including width-stable growth of the leading NTK mode toward the edge of stability (EoS). In contrast, NTK parameterization exhibits strongly width-dependent outlier dynamics, despite converging to a stable large-width limit. We show that this bulk+outlier picture is descriptive of simple tasks with small output channels, but that tasks involving large numbers of outputs (ImageNet classification or GPT language modeling) are better described by a restructuring of the spectral bulk. We develop a toy model with extensive output channels that recapitulates this phenomenon and show that edge of the spectrum still converges for sufficiently wide networks.

Susceptibilities and Patterning: A Primer on Linear Response in Bayesian Learning

Authors: Chris Elliott, Daniel Murfet
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07980v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable

Summary. This paper introduces a theory of susceptibilities to interpret how changes in input data affect the internal state of neural networks. By linking Bayesian inference to the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, it provides a way to map data perturbations to structural changes in the model. This offers a linearized approach to the 'patterning problem' in machine learning.

Why it may be interesting. not directly relevant

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to provide a unified framework for interpreting neural networks using the theory of susceptibilities to understand how data perturbations affect model observables.

Main result. The authors establish that the susceptibility of an observable to data perturbations is equivalent to a posterior covariance via the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, and show that the susceptibility matrix acts as a Jacobian for mapping data distributions to structural coordinates.

Method. The work uses statistical-mechanical foundations, specifically applying the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and linear response theory to Bayesian inference in neural networks.

Model / system. The system is a Bayesian neural network viewed through the lens of statistical mechanics, where model parameters are treated as degrees of freedom in a posterior distribution.

Key observables. Posterior expectation of observables (phi), per-sample losses (influence matrix), and component-localized observables (structural susceptibility matrix).

Important parameters / regimes. The inverse temperature beta (n*beta scaling) and the mapping between data distributions and structural coordinates.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes a Bayesian learning setting where the posterior distribution is well-defined and subject to small data perturbations.

Paper structure. The paper begins with a motivation from statistical-mechanical foundations, followed by a detailed exposition of susceptibility theory, empirical estimators, and finally discusses connections to the geometry of the loss landscape.

Abstract

These notes introduce the theory of susceptibilities as developed in [arXiv:2504.18274, arXiv:2601.12703] for interpreting neural networks. The susceptibility of an observable $φ$ to a data perturbation is defined as a derivative of a posterior expectation, which by the fluctuation--dissipation theorem equals a posterior covariance. Different choices of $φ$ yield different objects: per-sample losses give the influence matrix (the Bayesian influence function of [arXiv:2509.26544]), while component-localized observables give the structural susceptibility matrix that pairs model components with data patterns. The susceptibility matrix is (up to a factor of $nβ$) the Jacobian of the map from data distributions to structural coordinates; its pseudo-inverse provides a linearized solution to the patterning problem of [arXiv:2601.13548]: finding data perturbations that produce a desired structural change. We motivate the theory from its statistical-mechanical foundations, then give a detailed exposition of susceptibilities, their empirical estimators, and their connection to the geometry of the loss landscape.

Systematic frequency-collision analysis of the cross-resonance gate outside the straddling regime

Authors: Shinichi Inoue, Shotaro Shirai, Shuhei Tamate, Shu Watanabe, Kohei Matsuura, Rui Li, Yasunobu Nakamura
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07868v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable

Summary. The paper proposes moving the cross-resonance gate operation from the traditional straddling regime to a far-detuned regime to mitigate frequency collisions in transmon processors. By treating frequency allocation as a linear programming problem, the authors demonstrate that this approach allows for much tighter qubit-frequency spreads. This provides a scalable pathway for designing large-scale, fixed-frequency superconducting quantum processors.

Why it may be interesting. This is highly relevant for researchers in quantum computing and open quantum systems as it addresses a fundamental hardware scaling bottleneck (frequency crowding) using control-theory-inspired optimization and pulse-level analysis.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Frequency crowding in fixed-frequency transmon processors limits scalability because the conventional straddling regime for cross-resonance (CR) gates makes the system highly susceptible to frequency collisions.

Main result. The authors propose a 'far-detuned regime' for CR gates that significantly reduces frequency collisions and provide a linear programming optimization method for optimal frequency allocation in large lattices.

Method. The study uses a numerical method accurate under high-intensity, smoothly ramped microwave drives, combined with systematic parameter sweeps and Monte Carlo yield analysis.

Model / system. The system consists of fixed-frequency transmon qubits arranged in a square lattice, specifically analyzed up to a 1024-qubit scale.

Key observables. Two-qubit-gate error threshold and collision-free yield.

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit-frequency spread (sigma_f), frequency detuning (straddling vs. far-detuned regimes), and microwave drive intensity/ramping.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes a unit-cell lattice with periodic boundary conditions for the optimization framework.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of frequency crowding, proposes the far-detuned CR gate regime, presents a numerical method for collision evaluation, details a linear programming approach for frequency allocation, and concludes with a Monte Carlo yield analysis for large-scale lattices.

Abstract

Frequency crowding remains a major obstacle to scaling fixed-frequency transmon processors. Among the widely used all-microwave two-qubit gates, the cross-resonance (CR) gate is particularly sensitive to qubit-frequency spread because the conventional straddling regime condition constrains assignable qubit frequencies tightly and makes the system susceptible to frequency collisions. Here, we propose and analyze the CR gate outside the straddling regime, which we refer to as the far-detuned regime, and evaluate frequency collisions using a numerical method that remains accurate under high-intensity, smoothly ramped microwave drives. Based on this analysis, we perform systematic parameter sweeps and provide collision-free conditions that define designable frequency regions in which qubit frequencies can be assigned consistently with surrounding qubit frequencies. Furthermore, we formulate frequency allocation as a linear programming optimization on a unit-cell lattice with periodic boundary conditions to obtain an optimal allocation. We demonstrate that far-detuned designs significantly reduce collisions compared with designs in the straddling regime. Monte Carlo yield analysis indicates that 10% collision-free yield for a 1024-qubit square lattice at a 0.1% two-qubit-gate error threshold requires $σ_{\mathrm{f}}/2π\le 6.8~\mathrm{MHz}$. Our findings suggest that this is feasible with an approximately twofold reduction in the state-of-the-art qubit-frequency spread.

Abstract

In his 1976 paper "More about the massive Schwinger model", Coleman introduced $1+1$-dimensional Quantum Electrodynamics coupled to two charged massive fermions. By applying Abelian bosonization, he elucidated much of the physics of this two-flavor Schwinger model, but he listed three puzzles at the end of his paper. Here, we show that an external stimulus, such as a magnetic field, can relax the scaling relationships between intermediates during the OER. Spectroscopic measurements and Density Functional Theory simulations in Ni-Fe oxyhydroxides reveal that applying a magnetic field alters surface chemisorption and injects structural flexibility at the interface. We interpret these observations as a consequence of stimulated changes in the spin-lattice coupling, which allow access to quasi-degenerate oxygenated intermediates that modulate the reaction energy demands. Our findings redefine the scaling limitations as state-projected rather than intrinsic and establish external stimulation as a strategy to navigate multi-state energy landscapes in electrocatalysis and sensing applications.
