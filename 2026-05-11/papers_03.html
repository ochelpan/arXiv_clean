Tamed Feynman-Kac diffusion processes: Killing-branching intertwine

Authors: Piotr Garbaczewski, Mariusz Żaba
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07824v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: methods for driven-dissipative 3/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. The paper proposes a way to interpret Feynman-Kac diffusion processes with negative potentials by treating the negative regions as branching or cloning rates. This allows for a consistent probabilistic description of processes where killing effects are overcompensated. The authors use computer-assisted methods to demonstrate this for one-dimensional double-well systems.

Why it may be interesting. This is relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and many-body dynamics as it provides a new probabilistic framework for understanding the evolution of systems governed by Schrödinger-type operators with complex potentials.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the probabilistic interpretation of Feynman-Kac kernels when the associated potential contains negative regions, which complicates the standard interpretation of killing rates.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the 'taming' of killing effects in relaxing Feynman-Kac kernels can be interpreted through a process involving both killing and branching (cloning) rates, providing a consistent path-wise background for these diffusions.

Method. The study employs computer-assisted path-wise arguments to validate the killing/branching taming scenario for nonlinear model systems.

Model / system. The study focuses on one-dimensional nonlinear model systems, specifically focusing on Feynman-Kac potentials with double-well shapes.

Key observables. Probability density functions, Feynman-Kac kernels, and the killing/branching rates.

Important parameters / regimes. The sign and magnitude of the Feynman-Kac potential V(x), particularly the existence of negative subdomains.

Assumptions / limitations. The potential V(x) is assumed to be continuous, confining, and bounded from below.

Paper structure. The paper presents a theoretical framework for interpreting Feynman-Kac potentials with negative values, followed by computer-assisted path-wise arguments applied to one-dimensional nonlinear models, with a focus on double-well potentials.

Abstract

Relaxation to equilibrium of a drifted Brownian motion is quantified by a probability density function, whose main (multiplicative) entry is an inferred Feynman-Kac kernel of the Schrödinger semigroup operator. Although seemingly devoid of a natural probabilistic significance (except for its explicit path integral definition), the pertinent kernel relaxes to equilibrium as well. The implicit Feynman-Kac potential ${\cal{V}}(x)$, continuous, confining and bounded from below, may take negative values. If positive, ${\cal{V}}(x)$ can be interpreted as the killing rate of the decaying diffusion process. In case of relaxing F-K kernels the killing effects are tamed (often overcompensated). The taming inavoidably appears in conjunction with the existence of the negativity subdomains of ${\cal{V}}(x)$ in $R$. If locally ${\cal{V}}(x) < 0$, its sign inversion $- {\cal{V}}(x)$ can be interpreted as the branching (cloning, alternatively bifurcation) rate in the course of the other wise free random motion. The arising killed diffusion processes with branching, we interpret as the possible path-wise background of tamed (relaxing) Feynman-Kac diffusions. We present acomputer-assisted path-wise arguments, towards a consistency of the killing/branching taming scenario, for a number of nonlinear model systems in one space dimension. Special attention is paid to Feynman-Kac potential shapes, presumed to be in the double well form, where an analytic access to eigenvalues and eigenfunctions is scarce beyond the semiclassical regime.

Kochen-Specker nonlocal hidden variables must include time-ordering to allow for measurement independence of several agents

Authors: Valerio Scarani, Antoine Suarez
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07228v1

Summary. The paper shows that if one assumes a nonlocal hidden variable model where values are stored outside spacetime, the model must account for the time-ordering of measurements to prevent the loss of agent independence. This prevents 'circular signaling' and ensures that the choice of one agent does not pre-determine the freedom of another in timelike configurations. This result offers a way to maintain a single-world ontology while respecting the principle of free choice.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a fundamental ontological perspective on the nature of nonlocality and the role of time in quantum correlations, which is relevant to the foundational aspects of quantum information and the interpretation of multipartite entanglement.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the conflict between Kochen-Specker nonlocal hidden variables and the requirement of measurement independence (free choice) for multiple agents in Bell-type experiments.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that for a nonlocal ontology to remain consistent with agent independence, the measurement context must include the time-ordering of the agents' choices, not just the measurement settings.

Method. The authors use a proof by contradiction involving signaling and a decomposition of multipartite quantum correlations into conditional probabilities based on an arrow-of-time polytope.

Model / system. An 'All-Possible-Experiments' (APEX) ontology where nonlocal hidden variables are stored in a repository outside of spacetime, involving multiple agents (e.g., Alice and Bob) in spacelike and timelike configurations.

Key observables. Measurement settings (inputs), measurement outcomes, and the temporal sequence of measurement events.

Important parameters / regimes. Measurement independence (free choice), time-ordering of measurement events, and the signaling between agents.

Assumptions / limitations. The existence of a repository of pre-existing possibilities outside spacetime, a single-world ontology, and a strong form of measurement independence.

Figures summary. Table 1 shows a nonlocal deterministic assignment for CHSH that allows signaling; Table 2 shows the upgraded context including time-ordering; Table 3 illustrates circular signaling.

Paper structure. The paper begins by defining the APEX ontology, identifies the signaling conflict in Bell-type experiments, proposes time-ordering as a solution to preserve measurement independence, and generalizes the result to multipartite systems using probability decomposition.

Abstract

We consider an ontology, in which contextual nonlocal hidden variables are stored as pre-existing possibilities in a repository outside space-time; and in which the context can be chosen ``freely'' (measurement independence) by each agent, both in spacelike and timelike configurations. We show that, in Bell-type experiments involving several agents, for this ontology to be consistent, the context must include not only the measurements that can be performed, but also the time ordering of the choices of different agents.

Summary. The authors present a new Transformer-based neural network architecture designed to simulate bosonic systems in the grand canonical ensemble. This method allows for particle number fluctuations driven by a chemical potential, enabling the study of density profiles and condensate fractions from first principles. The approach demonstrates state-of-the-art accuracy for both 1D and 2D many-body models.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a powerful new numerical tool for simulating realistic cold atom and photonic systems where particle number is not fixed, bridging the gap between NQS and grand canonical physics.

Main problem. Extending Neural Network Quantum States (NQS) from fixed particle number ensembles to the grand canonical ensemble to simulate bosonic systems with variable particle numbers.

Main result. The proposed Transformer-based architecture achieves highly accurate ground-state energies and reproduces exact analytical results for 1D and 2D bosonic models, outperforming previous Deep Sets architectures.

Method. A Transformer-based ansatz using masked multi-head self-attention and Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) with Metropolis-Hastings sampling in Fock space.

Model / system. Bosonic quantum field theories in the grand canonical ensemble, specifically including the 1D Lieb-Liniger and Calogero-Sutherland models, and 2D versions of the Calogero-Sutherland and harmonically trapped bosonic systems.

Key observables. Ground-state energy, average particle number, one-body reduced density matrix (OBDM), condensate fraction, and particle number density profiles.

Important parameters / regimes. Chemical potential (mu), interaction strength (g), particle number fluctuations, and the use of a masking mechanism for variable N.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the scientific problem of GCE simulation, details the Transformer-based neural network architecture and physical constraints, presents benchmarking against 1D and 2D models, and concludes with performance comparisons and scalability discussions.

Abstract

Variational Monte Carlo calculations have recently reached state-of-the-art accuracy in the approximation of ground state properties of quantum many-body systems. Making use of flexible neural quantum states and automatic differentiation has bypassed traditional computational obstacles such as reliance on basis sets. In this paper, we propose a neural quantum state architecture capable of representing symmetric bosonic wavefunctions in Fock space, enabling the study of systems with variable particle number. By supplementing our variational state with Monte Carlo sampling and geometric optimization, we demonstrate competitive variational energies across an array of one- and two-dimensional systems, converging to the physical boson number under a set chemical potential. Our approach enables accurate estimates of one-body reduced density matrices, opening access to observables such as condensate fractions and radial density profiles from first principles. Our method opens the door to numerical predictions of key measurable quantities in practical grand canonical systems.

Fragility of Unidirectional Transport in Weakly Disordered Photonic Chern Insulators

Authors: Xiaoxuan Shi, Tiantao Qu, Xianbin Wu, Mudi Wang, Lei Zhang, Jun Chen
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.07092v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: Kondo & dissipative impurity 2/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · spintronics-quantum-optics interface 2/5

Summary. The paper reveals that weak disorder in photonic Chern insulators can disrupt unidirectional light transport by creating a trivial impurity band that enables reciprocal transport via 'necklace states'. This discovery shows that topological edge states are more sensitive to specific impurity frequencies than previously thought. This is important for designing robust photonic integrated circuits.

Why it may be interesting. It provides new insights into how local defects can fundamentally alter global transport properties in topological systems, which is critical for the stability of photonic quantum networks and topological insulators.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Understanding how weak disorder from impurities affects the robustness of unidirectional chiral edge states in photonic Chern insulators.

Main result. Discovered a mechanism where weak disorder creates a topologically trivial impurity band that couples with chiral edge states, forming reciprocal 'necklace state' channels that break unidirectional transport.

Method. Numerical simulation of a magnetic photonic crystal where magnetized rods are randomly replaced with nonmagnetized impurities to study the formation of defect states and impurity bands.

Model / system. A magnetic photonic crystal acting as a photonic Chern insulator, subject to weak disorder via the random replacement of magnetized rods with nonmagnetized impurities.

Key observables. Unidirectional propagation of chiral edge states, formation of impurity bands, and the emergence of reciprocal necklace state transport channels.

Important parameters / regimes. Excitation frequency relative to the single impurity defect state frequency; level of disorder (number of impurities).

Assumptions / limitations. The disorder is considered 'weak' and consists of replacing a small number of rods; global topological invariants are preserved.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the importance of topological protection, identifies the problem of disorder-induced fragility, presents the discovery of the impurity band mechanism, and discusses the implications for topological transport stability.

Abstract

Photonic Chern insulators enable unidirectional light transport protected by nontrivial band topology -- essential for robust photonic integrated circuits and error-free communication. However, disorder from impurities or defects inevitably exists in practical applications, yet how weak disorder affects topological chiral edge states remains insufficiently understood. Here, we reveal a previously unrecognized mechanism by which weak disorder can disrupt robust propagation of chiral edge states in photonic Chern insulators, despite the preservation of global topological invariants. By randomly replacing a small number of magnetized rods with nonmagnetized impurities in a magnetic photonic crystal, we find that when the excitation frequency approaches the single impurity defect state frequency, weak coupling between spatially extended defect states forms a topologically trivial impurity band inside the topological gap. This enables coexistence and coupling of defect states and chiral edge states. The reciprocal "necklace state" transport channels formed by coupled defect states break the expected unidirectional propagation in topological Chern insulators with weak disorder. Our work reveals that topological chiral edge state and disorder interactions are more intricate than previously understood and provides new insights into stability and control of topological transport in realistic applications.

A collider as a quantum computer

Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06907v1

Summary. This paper proposes a new framework where high-energy scattering processes are viewed as constrained quantum circuits. By decomposing the scattering T-matrix into quantum gates and Kraus operators, the authors show that the underlying dynamics of particle collisions can be understood through the lens of quantum information theory.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a novel bridge between high-energy physics and quantum information, offering a framework to study scattering dynamics using the language of quantum gates, Kraus operators, and circuit-based information loss.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper seeks to reformulate high-energy particle scattering processes, which are traditionally analyzed via final-state properties, as structured, operational quantum circuits.

Main result. The authors successfully demonstrate that the T-matrix for lepton scattering can be decomposed into a two-qubit quantum circuit consisting of Pauli gates, controlled rotations, and CNOT gates, alongside a non-unitary Kraus operator.

Method. The study employs Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to separate coherent mixing from non-unitary effects and Cosine-Sine Decomposition (CSD) to map the resulting unitary matrices onto elementary quantum gates.

Model / system. The physical system is the helicity degrees of freedom in high-energy lepton scattering, specifically the electron-positron annihilation process (e+e- to mu+mu-) involving photon and Z-boson exchange.

Key observables. Helicity indices, scattering angle (theta), and singular values (sigma_plus, sigma_minus) representing transmission strength.

Important parameters / regimes. Weinberg angle (theta_W), electroweak couplings (gL, gR), and the dimensionless momentum parameter (mu) at the 1 TeV scale.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes fixed kinematic configurations and utilizes the high-energy/massless limit where helicity coincides with chirality.

Figures summary. Figure 2 shows the dependence of circuit parameters on the scattering angle; Figure 3 illustrates the dynamical correlation between unitary and non-unitary components; Figure 4 displays the tree-level Feynman diagram for the process.

Paper structure. The paper moves from the standard S-matrix description to a T-matrix operator formulation, applies SVD and CSD for matrix decomposition, maps the results to a quantum circuit architecture, and concludes with an analytical demonstration using lepton scattering.

Abstract

Scattering processes in high-energy physics are inherently quantum mechanical, yet are typically analyzed at the level of final states, where entanglement appears as a property of the outcome rather than a consequence of the underlying dynamics. We reformulate scattering at the level of the process itself by representing helicity transition matrices as quantum circuits. Once the kinematic configuration and scattering channel are fixed, the problem reduces to a finite-dimensional quantum map, making a circuit description natural. Within this framework, an example of the process $e^+e^-\to μ^+μ^-$ is shown, which decomposes into unitary and non-unitary components, corresponding to coherent mixing and postselection effects. This representation reorganizes the amplitude into distinct operational elements, providing a perspective in which collider processes can be viewed as constrained quantum circuits and their entanglement structure can be understood in terms of the underlying circuit dynamics, opening the door to analyzing their properties using the language of quantum information.

Fluctuation-driven chiral ferromagnetism

Authors: Rokas Veitas, Ahmed Khalifa, Francisco Machado, Shubhayu Chatterjee
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.06852v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: Rydberg arrays 2/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5 · exotic spin models in light-matter 2/5

Summary. This paper shows that spin-orbit interactions can induce a chiral ferromagnetic phase stabilized by quantum fluctuations, even when classical models predict only collinear states. This mechanism generates spontaneous scalar orbital chirality and leads to an enhanced thermal Hall effect. The findings are applicable to a variety of platforms including moiré materials and Rydberg-atom arrays.

Why it may be interesting. The paper demonstrates how quantum fluctuations can actively stabilize novel topological/chiral phases rather than just destroying order, which is highly relevant to many-body dynamics and Rydberg-atom arrays.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Investigating how magnetization-non-conserving couplings (spin-orbit interactions) can overcome the suppression of quantum fluctuations in ferromagnetic materials to stabilize new phases.

Main result. The discovery of a chiral ferromagnet and a chiral stripe regime that are stabilized by quantum fluctuations, characterized by spontaneous scalar orbital chirality.

Method. Theoretical analysis of the modification of the classical phase diagram by spin-orbit interactions and the evaluation of fluctuation-induced effects.

Model / system. A ferromagnetic system featuring magnetization-non-conserving couplings and spin-orbit interactions, relevant to moire heterostructures, Rydberg-atom arrays, and non-Kramers spin materials.

Key observables. Orbital chirality, scalar orbital chirality, and the thermal Hall effect.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetization-non-conserving couplings, spin-orbit interactions, and the presence of quantum fluctuations.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes the presence of spin-orbit interactions that do not conserve magnetization.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of suppressed fluctuations in ferromagnets, presents the mechanism of fluctuation-stabilized chiral phases via spin-orbit coupling, describes the resulting phase diagram (including chiral stripes), and discusses transport signatures and experimental relevance.

Abstract

In general, quantum fluctuations are suppressed in ferromagnetic materials because they admit a simple unfrustrated ground state, greatly limiting the scope of phenomena that can be observed in these materials. In this work, we show how magnetization-non-conserving couplings fundamentally alter this paradigm by demonstrating the existence of a chiral ferromagnet that is stabilized by quantum fluctuations. More specifically, we show how these spin-orbit interactions modify the classical phase diagram; whereas a classical analysis predicts only collinear states, we observe fluctuation-stabilized phases, including a ferromagnet with large orbital chirality and a chiral stripe regime. We elucidate how such couplings spontaneously generate a scalar orbital chirality, in contrast to conventional mechanisms which rely upon a field-induced canting of vector chiral order. The resultant chiral states exhibit distinct transport signatures, namely an enhanced thermal Hall effect, and are of direct relevance to moiré heterostructures, Rydberg-atom arrays, and solid-state materials featuring non-Kramers spins.

Summary. This paper introduces a hardware-free method to stabilize polarization in MDI-QKD networks using a mathematical twirling protocol. By applying a shared random unitary transformation, the researchers transform unpredictable channel drifts into predictable white noise, significantly increasing the tolerance to fiber-induced turbulence.

Why it may be interesting. It presents an elegant application of group-theoretic twirling (unitary 2-designs) to transform complex, non-symmetric noise into a manageable, isotropic depolarizing channel, which is highly relevant to open quantum systems and quantum communication.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Polarization instability and asymmetric channel turbulence in MDI-QKD cause arbitrary unitary drifts, leading to degraded Hong-Ou-Mandel interference and increased error rates.

Main result. The proposed Correlated Twirling protocol suppresses intrinsic channel noise by a factor of 2/3 and extends the angular misalignment tolerance from 38.7 to 47.9 degrees.

Method. A hardware-free algorithmic approach using a 12-element unitary 2-design to transform asymmetric geometric rotations into an isotropic Pauli depolarizing channel via virtual post-processing.

Model / system. An MDI-QKD star-type network with Alice and Bob sending weak coherent pulses to an untrusted central node (Charlie) through noisy optical fiber channels.

Key observables. Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER), Guessing Probability (P_guess), and the depolarization parameter (eta).

Important parameters / regimes. Y-axis bias tolerance (increased from 0.68 to 0.84 rad), angular misalignment tolerance (38.7 to 47.9 degrees), and the 11% security threshold.

Assumptions / limitations. The protocol assumes a synchronized, public random beacon is available for Alice and Bob to apply the same unitary transformations.

Figures summary. Figure 1 compares the guessing probability of unprotected versus protected systems under varying levels of misalignment and noise.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of polarization drift in MDI-QKD, proposes a Correlated Twirling protocol using a unitary 2-design, provides mathematical derivations for the resulting depolarizing channel, and validates the approach through Monte Carlo simulations of error rates and transmission distances.

Abstract

Measurement-Device-Independent Quantum Key Distribution (MDI-QKD) provides unconditional security against detector vulnerabilities, but its practical deployment is severely hindered by asymmetric channel turbulence. Fluctuations in optical fibers induce arbitrary polarization drift, degrading Hong-Ou-Mandel interference and forcing extensive calibration downtime. In this work, we propose a hardware-free polarization stabilization technique utilizing a Correlated Twirling protocol based on a unitary 2-design. By applying a synchronized, public twirling supermap, Alice and Bob mathematically transform deterministic, asymmetric geometric rotations into an isotropic Pauli depolarizing channel. Executed entirely as a virtual post-processing step during classical sifting, this protocol mathematically suppresses intrinsic channel noise by a factor of 2/3. We demonstrate through exact quantum state simulations that this induced symmetry neutralizes catastrophic axis-dependent failures, extending the Y-bias tolerance from 0.68 to 0.84 radians. Furthermore, the protocol passively extends the absolute angular misalignment tolerance for the 11% security threshold from $38.7^\circ$ to $47.9^\circ$, sustaining secure key distillation over extended fiber distances in highly turbulent regimes where standard architectures fail. Inherently compatible with decoy-state weak coherent pulses, this algorithmic approach provides a highly scalable, resource-efficient framework for robust long-distance quantum networks.

Abstract

Rare-earth-ion-doped crystals has emerged as a promising platform for quantum technologies, owing to their narrow telecom-band optical emission, long spin memory, and compatibility with silicon integrated photonic architectures. However, the realization of scalable quantum devices requires host materials with intrinsically dilute spin environments to suppress decoherence. In this context, erbium (Er$^{3+}$) doped cerium oxide (CeO$_2$) is an attractive candidate due to the ultra-low concentration of nuclear spins in the host matrix and its compatibility with silicon-based technologies. Here we perform a comprehensive investigation of the coherence properties of Er$^{3+}$ electron spin qubit in CeO$_2$ via semiclassical as well as detailed cluster correlation expansion simulations. By systematically exploring magnetic field strength, pulse sequences, erbium concentration, and spin temperature, we identify regimes where decoherence from the spin bath is strongly suppressed. Moreover, we demonstrate that an additional enhancement can be obtained with conventional multi-$π$-pulse dynamical decoupling protocols. Thus, our findings establish Er$^{3+}$ doped CeO$_2$ as a front-runner for realizing spin qubits, quantum memories, and integrated quantum networks.

Commutativity from a single Bargmann invariant equality

Authors: Rafael Wagner, Ernesto F. Galvão

Summary. The paper presents a new, universal, and dimension-independent condition to check if two quantum states commute using a single equality of fourth-order Bargmann invariants. This approach bypasses the need for full state tomography and works for any finite dimension. It has direct applications in characterizing quantum nonclassicality and photon distinguishability.

Why it may be interesting. This provides a powerful, dimension-independent tool for certifying noncommutativity and nonclassicality, which is highly relevant for verifying quantum resources in photonic quantum computing and analyzing state distinguishability.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Finding a universal, dimension-independent, necessary, and sufficient condition to determine if a set of quantum states or observables is jointly diagonalizable (commutes).

Main result. The authors establish that two quantum states commute if and only if the fourth-order Bargmann invariant equality tr(rho1^2 * rho2^2) = tr(rho1 * rho2 * rho1 * rho2) holds, providing a dimension-independent criterion.

Method. The study uses the mathematical framework of Bargmann invariants and multivariate traces, specifically analyzing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the commutator and comparing the new criterion against existing dimension-dependent and necessary-only conditions.

Model / system. The work considers arbitrary pairs of quantum states (pure or mixed) and self-adjoint operators acting on finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, with specific applications discussed for qubits and photonic platforms.

Key observables. Fourth-order Bargmann invariants, purities, two-state overlaps, and the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the commutator.

Important parameters / regimes. Finite-dimensional Hilbert space dimension (d), and the rank of the Gram matrix of Bloch vectors.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary result is derived for finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces.

Figures summary. The notes mention a comparative summary table (Table I) highlighting the superiority of the new criterion over existing methods.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of set coherence, proposes a new universal criterion based on fourth-order Bargmann invariants, demonstrates its dimension-independence, provides simplified algebraic forms for qubits, proves the insufficiency of lower-order invariants for d >= 4, and compares the result to existing necessary conditions.

Abstract

Noncommutativity of states and observables is a fundamental signature of quantum theory, and a minimal requirement for nonclassicality. We provide a universal necessary and sufficient condition for pairwise commutativity of quantum states $ρ_1$ and $ρ_2$: they commute if and only if $\mathrm{tr}(ρ_1^2ρ_2^2) = \mathrm{tr}(ρ_1 ρ_2 ρ_1 ρ_2)$. For qubits the identity simplifies to an equality between polynomials of purities and of the two-state overlap $\mathrm{tr}(ρ_1ρ_2)$. These multivariate traces (known as Bargmann invariants) are directly measurable, allowing commutativity tests that bypass full state tomography. We point out possible applications to the analysis of POVM simulability and partial photonic distinguishability.

Covert Signaling for Communication and Sensing over the Bosonic Channels

Authors: Tianrui Tan, Evan J. D. Anderson, Michael S. Bullock, Boulat A. Bash
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.08066v1
Analysis basis: abstract only; PDF text extraction failed or PDF unavailable Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

Summary. This paper explores sparse signaling techniques to achieve covert communication and sensing in bosonic channels. It reveals that the most undetectable signal is a simple mixture of vacuum and a single photon, and it maps out the trade-offs between staying hidden and maintaining signal performance.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant to quantum optics and open quantum systems as it provides fundamental limits on communication and sensing using non-classical states in noisy, lossy environments.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates how to optimize sparse signaling strategies to maintain covertness while performing communication and sensing tasks over bosonic channels.

Main result. The authors identify that the optimal input state for minimizing detectability is a mixture of just two consecutive photon-number states, specifically a mixture of vacuum and a single photon in the low-brightness regime.

Method. The researchers analyze sparse signaling strategies (transmitting constant power on a subset of channel uses) and characterize the input signal state that minimizes detectability.

Model / system. The study focuses on lossy thermal-noise bosonic channels, which are applicable to optical, microwave, and radio-frequency systems.

Key observables. detectability, communication performance, and sensing performance

Important parameters / regimes. number of channel uses (n), average output power (scaling as 1/sqrt(n)), and input-power thresholds

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the use of sparse signaling strategies where power is transmitted on approximately sqrt(n) out of n channel uses.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of covertness and the square-root law, compares diffuse vs. sparse signaling, analyzes the optimal state structure for sparse signaling in bosonic channels, and explores the trade-offs between covertness and task performance.

Abstract

Preventing signal detection in communication and active sensing requires careful control of transmission power. In fact, the square-root laws (SRL) for covert classical and quantum communication and sensing prescribe that the average output power per channel use scales as $1/\sqrt{n}$ for $n$ channel uses. Two strategies for achieving this are diffuse and sparse signaling. The former transmits signals with power decaying as $1/\sqrt{n}$ on all $n$ channel uses, which is convenient for mathematical analysis. The latter transmits constant-power signals rarely, on approximately $\sqrt{n}$ out of $n$ channel uses, while remaining silent on the others. This offers significant practical advantages in compatibility with modern digital transmitters. Here, we study sparse signaling over lossy thermal-noise bosonic channels, which describe quantumly many practical channels (including optical, microwave, and radio-frequency). We characterize the input signal state that minimizes detectability. We find an unintuitive optimal quantum state structure: a mixture of just two consecutive photon-number states. In particular, in the low-brightness regime, the optimal signal state is a mixture of vacuum and a single photon. Since these states are generally suboptimal for both communication and active sensing, we explore the resulting trade-off and identify input-power thresholds for transitions between optimizing for covertness vs. performance in communication and sensing tasks.

In this work, we propose a switching protocol for unbuffered, multidrop quantum networks in a photonic synchronization domain that establishes a link between two end nodes using a shared BSA in the switched network. To achieve this, two end nodes cooperatively select the target BSA node with the lowest path cost and independently reserve each path within the network. Bi-path reservations are performed to allocate resources in a distributed manner. The proposed protocol is evaluated through simulation on Q-Fly network topologies under varying traffic conditions. The results demonstrate high link establishment success with stable performance even under increased network load. These capabilities which are driven by our proposed protocol are an essential way to realize large-scale, managed, and automated quantum networks.
