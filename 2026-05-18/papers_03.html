A reason why we do not observe Schrödinger's cats

Authors: Fabio Siringo
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16148v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: quantum measurements 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 1/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 1/5 · measurement-induced transitions 1/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 1/5

Summary. This paper proposes a dynamical resolution to the quantum measurement problem by showing that the standard Schrödinger equation leads to a stochastic equation for macroscopic variables. By analyzing this equation using Itô calculus, the authors demonstrate that any macroscopic superposition will rapidly collapse into a definite state, thereby deriving the Born rule without explicit postulation.

Why it may be interesting. This work offers a non-standard, dynamical approach to the measurement problem, providing a mechanism rooted in statistical physics (stochastic processes) that naturally enforces the Born rule for macroscopic systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the measurement problem by providing a dynamical reason why macroscopic quantum superpositions, such as Schrödinger's cat, are not observed in reality.

Main result. The standard Schrödinger equation, when analyzed through a stochastic framework, predicts that any macroscopic superposition will collapse almost instantaneously, and this collapse process naturally yields the Born rule.

Method. The analysis involves decoupling macroscopic and microscopic degrees of freedom and deriving a stochastic differential equation (SDE) for the macroscopic amplitudes, which is then analyzed using Itô calculus.

Model / system. The model uses the standard Schrödinger equation for a quantum system treated as a superposition of macrostates. The dynamics are mapped onto an effective stochastic equation whose noise term is self-generated by the microscopic ensemble average.

Key observables. The decay of off-diagonal coherence terms ($\langle p_k(t) p_m(t) angle$) and the recovery of the Born rule ($\langle p_m(t) angle = p_m(0)$).

Important parameters / regimes. The ratio of the inertial relaxation time ($ au_R$) to the noise correlation time ($ au_c$), and the macroscopic constraint $\Delta E \Delta X \gg \hbar c$.

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is that the macroscopic state can be treated as a statistical ensemble of microstates, and the derivation relies on the white noise approximation for the random matrix elements.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by deriving a stochastic equation from the Schrödinger dynamics, analyzing the required mathematical interpretation (Itô vs. Stratonovich) based on physical time scales, and finally demonstrating that the Itô interpretation leads to rapid collapse consistent with observation.

Abstract

A reason is discussed (may be not the only one) for why we do not see any superposition of macroscopic states in the real world. Under the general assumption that quantum macrostates are statistical ensembles of microstates, it is shown that any superposition of macrostates is reduced in a very short time by the unitary dynamics of the ordinary Schrödinger equation, deducing the Born rule without having to postulate it. In more detail, the macroscopic and microscopic degrees of freedom are decoupled in the Schrödinger equation, yielding an effective stochastic equation for the macroscopic variables, with the ensemble average of the microscopic amplitudes that acts as a self-generated internal white noise. The stochastic equation is shown to be a reducing Itô equation if some general causality conditions are met, predicting a very quick collapse of any macroscopic superposition upon formation, with probabilities which satisfy the Born rule. In the context of the von Neumann measurement scheme, the relevance of the result is discussed as a simple dynamical solution of the measurement problem.

Solving Classical and Quantum Spin Glasses with Deep Boltzmann Quantum States

Authors: Luca Leone, Arka Dutta, Markus Heyl, Enrico Prati, Pietro Torta
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15899v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: exotic spin models in light-matter 3/5 · analog quantum simulation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

Summary. This work proposes Deep Boltzmann Quantum States (DBQS) as a powerful variational ansatz for simulating quantum many-body systems, particularly spin glasses. By integrating DBQS into a Neural Quantum Annealing (NQA) framework, the authors overcome the sampling and optimization hurdles associated with these disordered systems. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on complex models, including solving NP-hard scheduling problems.

Why it may be interesting. The combination of deep learning architectures (DBMs) with quantum many-body physics provides a powerful, non-adiabatic route to solving hard optimization problems that challenge traditional quantum simulation methods.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the difficulty of finding ground states for classical and quantum spin glasses due to their rugged energy landscapes with exponentially many local minima.

Main result. The authors successfully solved instances of classical and quantum Ising spin-glass models and even the NP-hard Job Shop Scheduling Problem, exceeding current quantum annealing hardware limitations.

Method. They introduce Deep Boltzmann Quantum States (DBQS) as a variational ansatz, trained using Neural Quantum Annealing (NQA) combined with natural-gradient updates and efficient block-Gibbs sampling.

Model / system. The focus is on spin glass models, such as the Ising spin-glass Hamiltonian, which are characterized by disorder and energy frustration. The state is parameterized by the DBQS, which extends Boltzmann Machines to the quantum domain.

Key observables. Variational energy $E( heta)$, local energy $E_{ ext{loc}}(x; heta)$, and the ability to calculate these efficiently via matrix multiplications.

Important parameters / regimes. The annealing schedule parameter $s$ (interpolating from easy to hard Hamiltonian regimes), and optimization hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, momentum $\mu$).

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption is that the DBQS ansatz, combined with NQA, provides a sufficiently expressive and computationally tractable representation of the true ground state wavefunction.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the NQA trajectory for toy models, showing the projected parameter evolution and energy landscape minimization. Tables provide quantitative comparisons of energy errors ($\epsilon_B, \epsilon_Q$) against exact diagonalization results for various spin glass benchmarks.

Paper structure. The paper builds from general NQS methods (TDVP/VQA) to the specialized DBQS ansatz, emphasizing its computational advantages (block-Gibbs sampling, efficient local operator calculation). It then details the NQA training scheme, followed by extensive benchmarking across various spin glass and combinatorial optimization problems.

Abstract

Variational neural network models have achieved remarkable success in solving ground-state problems of quantum many-body systems. However, addressing classical and quantum spin glasses remains challenging, as disorder and energy frustration give rise to an exponentially large number of local energy minima separated by high-energy barriers, hindering the efficiency of conventional Metropolis-based Monte Carlo methods. To bridge this gap, we introduce Deep Boltzmann Quantum States, a class of neural quantum states inspired by deep Boltzmann machines that inherit efficient block Gibbs sampling. We also propose two key advances in the training algorithm. Firstly, we combine natural-gradient updates with state-of-the-art stochastic optimizers. Secondly, we gradually tune the hardness of the problem Hamiltonian by interpolating from an easy to a hard regime, without the need to closely approximate the instantaneous adiabatic state at intermediate times. We match the exact solution or the best available estimate for several instances of classical and quantum Ising spin-glass models with infinite-range interactions and hundreds of spins. We also solve instances of the NP-hard Job Shop Scheduling Problem exceeding the current limitations of quantum annealing hardware. To summarize, deep neural architectures with efficient global update rules and trained within an annealing-like scheme, provide a powerful framework for solving real-world hard combinatorial optimization and for investigating disordered quantum many-body systems.

Summary. This paper tackles the inefficiency of Monte Carlo simulations near phase transitions by employing Variational Autoregressive Networks (VANs). The key contribution is demonstrating that embedding physical knowledge, such as symmetries and local interactions, as priors into the neural network architecture drastically improves training stability and accuracy for simulating spin models like the Ising and Edwards-Anderson systems.

Why it may be interesting. The work directly addresses the intersection of advanced machine learning (autoregressive models) and complex many-body physics (spin glasses), providing a blueprint for physics-informed AI in condensed matter.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Monte Carlo simulations suffer from critical slowing down near phase transitions, and deep learning samplers struggle with training because they ignore underlying physical symmetries.

Main result. Incorporating physical priors into Variational Autoregressive Networks significantly reduces the training burden and improves simulation efficiency for discrete spin models.

Method. The authors propose a framework using VANs, approximating the true probability distribution by incorporating physical constraints (priors) derived from the system's symmetries and interactions.

Model / system. The study focuses on discrete spin systems, specifically the Ising model and the Edwards-Anderson spin glass model, defined by Hamiltonians involving nearest-neighbor interactions.

Key observables. Autocorrelation time, Free Energy (F), Magnetization (M), and Effective Sample Size (ESS).

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature parameter beta ($eta$), critical temperature ($eta_c$), and system size (e.g., $32 imes 32$ lattice).

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is that restricting the network's dependency structure using physical priors (instead of a 'blank slate' approach) is beneficial for training stability and accuracy.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate training histories (ESS, magnetization) comparing different approximation levels ($t_1, t_2, t_3$) for both the Ising and Edwards-Anderson models, showing convergence towards true values.

Paper structure. The paper systematically develops the VAN framework, applies it to the Ising model, and then extends the methodology to the more complex Edwards-Anderson spin glass model, comparing results across various temperature regimes and approximation orders.

Abstract

Monte Carlo methods are essential across diverse scientific fields, yet their efficiency is frequently hampered by critical slowing down-a sharp increase in autocorrelation times near phase transitions. Although deep learning approaches, such as neural-network-based samplers, have been proposed to alleviate this issue, they face another serious problem: the difficulty of training the models. This difficulty partially stems from the overly general nature of original machine-learning architectures, which often ignore underlying physical symmetries and force networks to relearn them from scratch. In this paper, we demonstrate that incorporating physical priors into the model significantly enhances performance. Building upon existing strategies that integrate spin-spin interactions, we propose a framework that utilizes a prior probability distribution as a starting point for training. Our results for the Ising model, as well as for the Edwards-Anderson spin glass model, suggest that moving away from `blank slate' models in favor of physics-informed priors reduces the training burden and facilitates the simulation of larger system sizes in discrete spin models.

Summary. This paper introduces Bayesian sequential verification, a statistically robust method for testing quantum programs under finite measurement budgets. It uses Bayesian Hypothesis Testing to sequentially update confidence in a program's success probability, allowing for early termination when sufficient evidence is gathered. This provides a practical, resource-aware workflow for building reliable quantum continuous-integration pipelines.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on quantum computation, the rigorous statistical framework for resource-constrained decision-making (budget-aware testing) is highly relevant for characterizing the reliability of complex quantum simulations or measurements in AMO physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of verifying quantum programs, which yield statistical distributions, by developing a method that is both statistically rigorous and mindful of limited computational resources (shot budgets).

Main result. Bayesian sequential verification significantly reduces the required measurement cost compared to fixed-budget testing when the quantum program's success probability exceeds the target threshold.

Method. It formulates the verification as a reference-based Bayesian Hypothesis Testing workflow, iteratively updating posterior distributions batch-by-batch as measurement evidence accumulates.

Model / system. The work applies to general quantum programs tested on quantum backends, using case studies like Bell-state preparation and QAOA for MaxCut optimization.

Key observables. Property success probability ($ heta$), conservative lower credible bound (LCB), and the resulting pass/fail decision.

Important parameters / regimes. Prior strength ($s$), target threshold ($ au$), batch size ($b$), and the required pass-streak ($r$).

Assumptions / limitations. The outcomes are assumed to be conditionally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) given the true probability, and the prior must be accurately estimated from reference runs.

Figures summary. Figure 1 illustrates the overall workflow from reference source to decision. Algorithm 1 details the sequential Bayesian verification procedure, including LCB calculation and stopping rules.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem, details the Bayesian sequential verification methodology (priors, sequential updates, LCB decision rule), evaluates it on Bell-state and QAOA workloads, and compares its efficiency against fixed-budget baselines.

Abstract

Quantum programs often produce probability distributions rather than deterministic outputs, making verification inherently statistical and increasingly costly on real hardware. In practice, developers still frequently rely on testing with fixed shot budgets on simulators, which are simple but time-consuming and poorly suited to noisy backends. What is missing is a verification approach that is both statistically explicit and budget-aware. This paper formulates Bayesian sequential verification as a reference-based Bayesian hypothesis testing workflow in which priors are derived from explicit reference sources, such as finite-shot reference runs or ideal/statevector-based computation, and verification decisions are updated batch by batch as measurement evidence accumulates. This approach is evaluated in Qiskit on two complementary workloads: Bell-state and QAOA-MaxCut. Across both case studies, the results show that Bayesian sequential verification can substantially reduce measurement costs compared to fixed-budget baselines when the success probability of the program exceeds the target threshold. The findings position Bayesian sequential verification as a practical verification workflow for quantum programs. The approach provides a foundation for future quantum continuous-integration pipelines that require reliable, budget-aware pass/fail decisions and motivates validation on real quantum hardware.

When Noisy Quantum Order Finding Remains Recoverable for Shor's Algorithm

Authors: Qingxin Yang, Stefano Markidis
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16074v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 3/5 · quantum measurements 3/5

Summary. This work analyzes the robustness of order finding, a key step in Shor's algorithm, when subjected to noise on current quantum hardware. By analyzing measured probability distributions, the authors find that successful recovery depends most strongly on how concentrated the verified probability mass is on the correct candidate denominator. This highlights that post-processing-aware metrics are more predictive of algorithmic success than global measures of distribution structure.

Why it may be interesting. The focus on the interplay between quantum measurement noise and classical signal processing techniques (like continued fractions) provides concrete, actionable metrics for diagnosing the performance limits of quantum algorithms on NISQ hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the conditions under which the true multiplicative order, a core subroutine of Shor's algorithm, can be recovered from a measured, noisy quantum phase estimation distribution.

Main result. Recoverability is primarily governed not by the global structure of the noise-affected distribution, but by the concentration of verified probability mass on the correct candidate denominator after classical post-processing.

Method. The authors analyzed 680 measured distributions using classical post-processing (continued-fraction) and characterized them using four features: autocorrelation peak strength, normalized entropy, dominant verified mass fraction, and verified margin fraction.

Model / system. The study uses measured precision-register output distributions collected from IBM quantum systems simulating noisy order finding circuits. The underlying process involves the ideal comb-like structure being distorted by quantum noise.

Key observables. Autocorrelation peak strength, Normalized entropy, Dominant verified mass fraction, and Verified margin fraction.

Important parameters / regimes. The true multiplicative order ($r$), the measured distribution $p(y)$, and the parameters defining the noise model (e.g., $\epsilon$ in the convolution model).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the standard classical post-processing routine (continued-fraction) is applied, and it separates global distribution structure from post-processing-aware mass competition.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate examples of recoverable/non-recoverable cases, and feature comparisons (e.g., $M_{1,frac}$ vs. $\Delta_{ver,frac}$) show trends where higher values correlate with recoverability. Random forest permutation importance confirms $M_{1,frac}$ as the most predictive feature.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the problem (noisy order finding) to developing diagnostic features based on post-processing outcomes. It then employs statistical classifiers (AUROC, Random Forest, Decision Tree) to determine which features best predict recoverability, culminating in the identification of the dominant verified mass fraction as the key indicator.

Abstract

Order finding is the core subroutine of Shor's algorithm. On NISQ hardware, phase estimation output distributions are often distorted by noise, making correct order recovery difficult. We study recoverability in noisy order finding: given a measured precision-register distribution, when does standard classical post-processing still return the true order? We analyze 680 distributions from IBM quantum systems across problem instances and circuit settings. For each distribution, we apply continued-fraction post-processing with modular verification and define recoverability as whether the recovered order equals the true one. We characterize each distribution using four features: autocorrelation peak strength, normalized entropy, dominant verified mass fraction, and verified margin fraction. We evaluate these quantities using marginal feature comparisons, single-feature AUROC analysis, and multivariate tree-based classifiers. We use random-forest permutation importance to assess which quantities contribute distinct predictive information once the other features are known. To make classification behavior interpretable, we train a decision tree that exposes threshold rules for recoverable and non-recoverable distributions. We find that recoverability is strongly associated with residual comb-like structure in the measured distribution and the way verified probability mass is organized across candidate denominators. The dominant verified mass fraction is the strongest single-feature indicator of recoverability, and tree-based analysis shows it also provides the primary split in an interpretable threshold description. Some highly distorted distributions remain recoverable when one verified denominator dominates the post-processing mass, while some visibly structured distributions fail because classical post-processing favors an incorrect verified denominator.

Summary. This theoretical paper proves the Rényi Quantum Null Energy Condition for integer parameters $n \ge 2$ within the framework of local Poincaré-invariant Quantum Field Theory. It achieves this by demonstrating the log-convexity of the Rényi relative entropy using sophisticated tools from operator algebras and non-commutative functional analysis. The result provides a powerful mathematical constraint on the structure of quantum states in QFT.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical framework connecting quantum energy conditions (a fundamental constraint in QFT) to the convexity properties of generalized entropy measures (Rényi divergences) is highly relevant for understanding quantum field theory constraints in curved or null-geometry backgrounds.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to prove the Rényi Quantum Null Energy Condition (Rényi QNEC), which is a generalization of the standard Quantum Null Energy Condition (QNEC). This conjecture relates to the non-negativity of the second null shape variation of the Sandwiched Rényi Divergence (SRD).

Main result. The authors prove the Rényi QNEC for all integer Rényi parameters $n \ge 2$ for von Neumann algebras possessing a half-sided modular inclusion structure. This is achieved by proving the log-convexity of the associated Kosaki $L_n$ norm.

Method. The proof employs advanced techniques from operator algebras, specifically utilizing von Neumann algebras, Haagerup's non-commutative $L_p$ spaces, and analyzing the dynamics generated by null translations via semigroups.

Model / system. The context is local Poincaré-invariant Quantum Field Theory (QFT) on Minkowski spacetime, analyzed using null coordinates and associated with von Neumann algebras defined over null-cut regions.

Key observables. Sandwiched Rényi Divergence (SRD), $D_n(\Psi\|\Omega; M_A)$, and the Kosaki $L_n$ norm of normal positive functionals.

Important parameters / regimes. Integer Rényi parameter $n \ge 2$, and the continuous parameter $c$ governing the null translation flow.

Assumptions / limitations. The existence of a half-sided modular inclusion (HSMI) structure on the von Neumann algebras, and the finiteness of the SRD of the excited state relative to the vacuum.

Figures summary. Figure 1 schematically illustrates null cut algebras, such as the Rindler wedge and the null cut algebra $M_b$.

Paper structure. The paper builds from defining the SRD and the RQNEC conjecture, establishing the necessary mathematical framework using non-commutative $L_p$ spaces and null translations, and culminating in the proof of log-convexity via a cyclic Ward identity.

Abstract

The Rényi quantum null energy condition conjectures that the second null shape variation of the sandwiched Rényi divergence (SRD) of an excited state relative to the vacuum is non-negative in local Poincaré-invariant quantum field theory, giving a one-parameter generalization of the quantum null energy condition (QNEC). We prove Rényi QNEC for all integer Rényi parameters $n\geq 2$ for von Neumann algebras carrying a half-sided modular inclusion structure. The only assumption on the excited state is finiteness of its SRD relative to the vacuum. Concretely, for any $σ$-finite von Neumann algebra with such an inclusion, we prove log-convexity, under the associated null-translation semigroup, of the Kosaki $L^n$ norm of any normal positive functional with finite $L^n$ norm.

Summary. This work introduces Quantum Compressed Sensing (QCS), a paradigm that uses unitary quantum evolution to reconstruct sparse signals. By encoding positional information into the quantum state evolution rather than requiring explicit measurements, QCS bypasses the classical measurement lower bound. This results in a measurement requirement scaling linearly with sparsity ($M \sim K$), offering a major leap in information acquisition efficiency for sensing and imaging.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a concrete physical pathway—using unitary transformations—to overcome fundamental information-theoretic limits in measurement-based signal processing, which has implications for quantum sensing and metrology.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the fundamental limitation of classical Compressed Sensing (CS) by proposing Quantum Compressed Sensing (QCS) to achieve superior signal acquisition efficiency.

Main result. QCS reduces the required measurement number from the classical bound of $M = O(K\log(N/K))$ to $M \sim O(K)$, achieving ultimate information acquisition efficiency.

Method. The methodology reframes signal acquisition as a unitary quantum evolution, using domain-alignment evolution to physically execute the support-set search at the quantum level.

Model / system. The system involves preparing a quantum probe state, mapping the signal onto the state, applying a unitary domain-alignment evolution, and performing projective measurements on the output state.

Key observables. Measurement number ($M$), reconstruction fidelity, and the success rate of recovering sparse coefficients.

Important parameters / regimes. Sparsity ($K$), signal dimension ($N$), and the measurement number ($M$).

Assumptions / limitations. The process relies on the existence of a physically realizable unitary operator for domain alignment and assumes a linear relationship between detection probability and signal amplitude.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the QCS workflow, compare the linear scaling of required measurements ($M \sim K$) against the classical bound, and show reconstruction examples in both time and frequency domains.

Paper structure. The paper builds from establishing the classical CS lower bound, introducing the QCS paradigm via unitary evolution, detailing the four-step physical implementation (probe state, mapping, alignment, detection), and validating the $M \sim K$ scaling experimentally.

Abstract

How many measurements are fundamentally required to capture a signal. Shannon's information theory established the bedrock of this question in 1948, the Nyquist Shannon theorem set the first answer, and compressed sensing (CS) rewrote it in 2006 by reducing the required measurement number to M = O(Klog(N/K)) for a K sparse signal. Here, we propose quantum compressed sensing (QCS), a paradigm that reframes signal acquisition as a unitary quantum evolution. By encoding high dimensional signal information into a single quantum probe state, then introducing domain-alignment evolution,a physically realizable unitary transformation that maps the sparse basis directly onto the measurement basis. QCS executes the support-set search at the quantum level without consuming measurement trials. The logarithmic penalty vanishes, compressing the required measurement number from the classical bound to M =O(K) and reducing reconstruction from ill posed optimization to linear estimation. We experimentally validate QCS using frequency and time domain sparse signals, confirming that the measurement number scales linearly with sparsity and decouples entirely from the signal dimension. Our work provides a physical pathway toward ultimate information acquisition efficiency, with broad implications for sensing, imaging, and communication.

Abstract

The honeycomb cobaltate Na$_2$Co$_2$TeO$_6$ has recently been proposed as a candidate material for hosting field-induced quantum spin liquid (QSL) behavior. Here, we present a comprehensive thermodynamic study of its low-temperature, high-field phase diagram using magnetization, specific heat, and magnetocaloric-effect measurements down to 1 K. In zero field, we observe a weak residual moment that provides further insight into the nature of the magnetic ground state. For in-plane magnetic fields ($B \parallel a^*$), we identify three field-induced transitions at $B_{c1} \simeq 6$ T, $B_{c2} \simeq 7.8$ T, and $B_{c3} \simeq 10.4$ T. Above $B_{c3}$, the absence of additional anomalies indicates a crossover to a conventional spin-polarized regime. Our results place stringent thermodynamic constraints on the proposed QSL scenario in Na$_2$Co$_2$TeO$_6$, calling for further microscopic investigations to establish the precise nature of the field-induced phases.

Abstract

Summary. This work presents a hybrid quantum-classical method to solve nonlinear differential equations by first linearizing them using Carleman linearization. The resulting linear system is then solved using a Variational Quantum Linear Solver (VQLS) implemented on quantum hardware simulators. The key finding is that optimizing the cost function (using local cost) allows the VQLS to accurately track classical solutions for these complex dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a concrete, quantum-computable recipe for simulating complex, non-linear physical systems (like those found in AMO or quantum optics) by mapping them onto linear algebra problems solvable on near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Developing a measurement-efficient Variational Quantum Linear Solver (VQLS) to solve linear systems derived from nonlinear dynamics.

Main result. The VQLS successfully recovers quantum states proportional to classical solutions for Carleman-structured systems, demonstrating high fidelity using local cost functions.

Method. The approach combines Carleman linearization to transform a nonlinear ODE into a linear system, which is then solved using a hybrid quantum-classical VQLS algorithm.

Model / system. The canonical benchmark is the damped, externally forced Duffing oscillator, which is linearized via the Carleman-Euler operator into a block-banded linear system $L Y = B$.

Key observables. Fidelity ($F_{sol}, F_{dir}$), relative residuals ($R_r$), global and local cost function values ($C_G, C_L$), and convergence rates.

Important parameters / regimes. Truncation order ($N$), stationary-extension length ($p$), and the choice between global vs. local cost formulations.

Assumptions / limitations. The method assumes the applicability of Carleman linearization and relies on the VQLS framework, which is tested on NISQ-era hardware.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate Hadamard Test circuits, the block-banded structure of the linear matrix $L$, and cost function histories comparing global and local cost approaches.

Paper structure. The paper first introduces the problem (nonlinear ODEs) and the linearization technique (Carleman). It then details the VQLS implementation, comparing global and local cost functions, and benchmarking different Hermitianization and ansatz architectures.

Abstract

We present hybrid quantum-classical pipelines for solving the Duffing equation that leverage Carleman linearization and the Variational Quantum Linear Solver (VQLS). First, we demonstrate that Carleman linearization accurately approximates the weakly nonlinear Duffing equation, with errors diminishing as the truncation order increases. Next, across IBM and Xanadu platforms, we deploy VQLS with symmetry-grouped Hadamard Test evaluations under both global and local cost formulations, compare distinct Hermitianization within a common cost framework, and benchmark hardware-efficient ansatz architectures under a fixed Hermitianization. Across block-banded test cases, each method achieves near-unity fidelity and vanishing relative residuals. These results show that topology-agnostic ansatz, optimized Hermitianization, and efficient cost formulation enable VQLS to recover quantum states proportional to classical solutions for Carleman-structured systems, providing a portable recipe for quantum-in-the-loop simulation of nonlinear dynamics.

Beyond Commutativity: Redesigning Trotter Decomposition via Local Symmetry

Authors: Naoki Negishi, Bo Yang
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16016v1

Summary. This paper proposes a novel method to improve quantum simulation accuracy by redesigning Trotter decomposition based on local SU(2) symmetries rather than just Hamiltonian commutativity. By grouping terms into symmetry-respecting local clusters, the method drastically reduces residual errors and circuit depth for models like the Kagome Heisenberg lattice. This establishes local symmetry as a practical and powerful design principle for simulating complex many-body quantum systems.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful, systematic design principle for Hamiltonian simulation that moves beyond simple algebraic commutation rules, directly impacting the feasibility of simulating complex, strongly correlated quantum materials on near-term quantum hardware.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge is improving the accuracy and efficiency of digital quantum simulation by redesigning the Trotter decomposition method.

Main result. The authors introduce a symmetry-guided decomposition that significantly suppresses commutator-induced errors and reduces circuit depth compared to conventional methods.

Method. The method exploits the underlying local SU(2) symmetry of Hamiltonian terms, grouping them into local three-site clusters rather than relying solely on direct Pauli commutativity.

Model / system. The work is demonstrated on several physically relevant spin-lattice models, including the Kagome Heisenberg model with triangular spin-chirality interactions and the 1D transverse-field Ising model.

Key observables. State infidelity, average spin-chirality bias, and the number of required quantum gates (e.g., CNOT gates).

Important parameters / regimes. The simulation time $t$ and the number of Trotter steps used for approximation.

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is that the Hamiltonian structure can be effectively decomposed based on local SU(2) symmetry, which is superior to general commutativity assumptions.

Figures summary. Figures schematically illustrate the decomposition strategies on lattices (Kagome, 1D), comparing the number of required clusters (e.g., 10 vs. 2) and showing error/fidelity comparisons.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from identifying the limitations of standard Trotter decomposition to proposing the symmetry-based decomposition principle. It then applies this principle to specific models (Heisenberg, Ising) to derive concrete, error-suppressing product formulas.

Abstract

The product formula, commonly known as Trotter decomposition, is a central tool for digital quantum simulation, whose performance depends critically on how the Hamiltonian is partitioned into tractable blocks. Standard decompositions typically rely on direct commutativity among Hamiltonian terms in a chosen operator representation, which can lead to large residual errors and deep circuits for complex, practically relevant many-body quantum systems. We address this fundamental bottleneck by introducing a new decomposition principle that goes beyond commutativity, grouping Hamiltonian terms into local three-site clusters according to the underlying SU(2) symmetry of the local dynamics. We show that three-site generators fall into at most four SU(2)-symmetry classes, each admitting an effective two-qubit SU(4) representation with exact and efficient implementations. By reducing the number of clusters, this decomposition principle substantially suppresses commutator-induced errors and circuit overhead while preserving underlying physical structures that commutativity-based decompositions may violate. We demonstrate the proposed method on several physically relevant spin-lattice models, where the reduced cluster structure can even realise the second-order product formula without doubling the circuit depth, as would be required by conventional decompositions. Numerical simulations of a Kagome Heisenberg model with triangular spin-chirality interactions show that the proposed method reduces both state infidelity and average spin-chirality bias by more than three orders of magnitude compared with conventional decompositions, while using substantially fewer gates. These results establish local symmetry as a flexible and practical design principle for product-formula simulation, opening a route to more accurate and hardware-efficient simulations of broader classes of many-body systems.
