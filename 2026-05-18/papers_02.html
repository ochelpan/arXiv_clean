Demonstration of a Multiplexing Trapped Ion Quantum Processing Unit

Authors: F. Anmasser, M. Abu Zahra, K. Schüppert, M. Pototschnig, J. Wahl, M. Dietl, M. Pfeifer, Y. Colombe, J. Repp, M. Brandl, P. Schindler, C. Rössler

Summary. This paper demonstrates a scalable Quantum Processing Unit (QPU) for trapped ions by integrating the ion trap with a time-multiplexing control unit. By using an analog multiplexer, the authors overcome the massive wiring challenge associated with large-scale ion traps. The results confirm that this multiplexing approach maintains the high fidelity required for quantum computation.

Why it may be interesting. The work directly addresses the engineering bottleneck of scaling trapped ion systems by proposing and experimentally validating a cryogenic, low-power multiplexing architecture for control electronics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. Scaling trapped ion quantum processors to thousands of qubits faces severe wiring and control line density challenges.

Main result. The demonstration shows that a time-multiplexing scheme using a surface ion trap and integrated analog multiplexers is compatible with high-fidelity quantum operations.

Method. The authors built a Quantum Processing Unit (QPU) by integrating a surface ion trap with a cryostat-based analog multiplexer using a sample-and-hold technique.

Model / system. The system is a trapped ion QPU fabricated on a surface ion trap, interfaced with an ASIC multiplexer. This setup allows routing multiple control voltages from few inputs, mitigating wiring bottlenecks.

Key observables. Motional heating rates (measured in phonons/s), gate error decay rates, and secular frequencies.

Important parameters / regimes. Multiplexing sampling intervals below 50 ms are sufficient for gate error fidelity; voltage decay rates are measured in V/min.

Assumptions / limitations. The multiplexing scheme is assumed to maintain high fidelity despite charge injection and voltage decay during the sample-and-hold process.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the QPU architecture combining the trap and multiplexer, and show measurements of heating rates and voltage decay dynamics.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from identifying the scaling challenge (wiring) to proposing and demonstrating the multiplexing solution, followed by detailed characterization of heating rates and charge injection effects.

Abstract

A fault-tolerant quantum computer is expected to require thousands of qubits. Trapped ion architectures provide a modular approach where the quantum register is divided into multiple subregisters connected by physically moving the corresponding ions. Transporting ions at scale comes with several challenges such as the need to connect thousands of control lines to an ion trap chip. Multiplexing the required control voltages from few input signals to multiple electrodes offers a solution to this wiring challenge. Here we demonstrate a quantum processing unit that combines a surface ion trap with a time multiplexer via a sample-and-hold technique that initially charges electrodes to fixed voltages and disconnects them during qubit operations. We characterize the unit's performance by measuring motional heating rates below one phonon per second in both open and closed switch configurations. We further characterize the sample and hold process and find that sampling intervals below 50 ms are sufficient to keep expected gate errors from decaying charges during the hold phase below $10^{-4}$. Our results indicate that the multiplexing scheme is compatible with high-fidelity operations.

Quantum Measurement without Ontology

Authors: Richard Healey
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16101v1

Summary. This paper argues that quantum theory does not describe objective, mind-independent facts about physical reality regarding states or probabilities. Instead, the objectivity of quantum knowledge is established by the norms and practices of the scientific community when applying the theory to predict outcomes in specific physical situations. It shifts the focus from 'what is real' to 'how we are justified in making claims.'

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep philosophical underpinning to quantum measurement, which is crucial for understanding the interpretation of quantum field theories and open quantum systems where the environment plays a defining role.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper tackles the fundamental philosophical problem of objectivity in quantum mechanics, specifically questioning whether quantum states, Born probabilities, and measurement outcomes represent objective facts about a mind-independent reality.

Main result. The objectivity of quantum knowledge is argued to be instituted not by veridical representation of reality, but by conformity to epistemic norms and the successful application of quantum theory within specific 'agent-situations' or physical procedures.

Method. The analysis employs philosophical tools, comparing ontological interpretations with pragmatic and coherence-based views (like QBism), and drawing analogies from relativistic physics and quantum metrology.

Model / system. The discussion centers on abstract quantum systems, the mathematical formalism of quantum states, and the physical context of measurement, using examples like LIGO to ground the philosophical arguments.

Key observables. Measurement outcomes, Born probabilities, quantum states, and the relative frequencies of detection events.

Important parameters / regimes. The distinction between 'absoluteness' and 'objectivity'; the structure of the 'agent-situation' relative to decoherence environments.

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is that standard physical practice requires an explanation for the objectivity of its results, leading to the rejection of strict ontological realism for quantum concepts.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the failure of classical notions of measurement objectivity in quantum theory, then contrasting various interpretations (QBism, RQM), and finally arguing that objectivity arises from normative constraints and successful application in physical procedures like metrology.

Abstract

Measurement is an important scientific activity. In most of science, including classical physics, is may be understood as a way of finding out about the physical world and representing the results numerically. No-go theorems show that measurement of quantum observables is not like that: the recorded outcome is typically created rather than revealed in a quantum measurement, in which case there is no objective fact about the observable's prior value. Other no-go theorems show that unitary quantum theory can generally neither explain nor even represent a unique recorded outcome, thereby threatening that outcome's objectivity. Methodological norms inherent in quantum physical practice nevertheless institute the objectivity, not only of unique recorded outcomes of quantum measurements, but also of non-quantum features of the world that physicists and other scientists take their models to represent.

Summary. This theoretical study models the interaction between molecular spin qubits (PEFs) and the quantized vibrations of suspended CNTs. By developing a hybrid open-system model, the authors derive an entropy balance equation to quantify how irreversibility is generated and distributed between the spin and mechanical baths. The results establish CNT-PEF hybrids as powerful platforms for studying non-equilibrium quantum thermodynamics.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant as it provides a detailed, quantitative framework for diagnosing non-equilibrium quantum processes (like decoherence and information loss) using thermodynamic quantities (entropy) in solid-state quantum devices.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper analyzes entropy generation and irreversibility in a hybrid quantum system coupling spin and mechanical (vibrational) degrees of freedom.

Main result. It demonstrates that the system exhibits crossovers between oscillator-dominated and spin-dominated entropy-production regimes, providing a quantitative basis for studying non-equilibrium quantum thermodynamics.

Method. The analysis employs a hybrid open-system model, using the Wigner function formalism for the mechanical mode and deriving an entropy balance equation from the Lindblad master equation.

Model / system. The system consists of paramagnetic endohedral fullerenes (PEFs) acting as qubits, coupled to the flexural modes of a suspended Carbon Nanotube (CNT) resonator. The dynamics are governed by a Lindblad master equation incorporating spin relaxation, mechanical damping, and the spin-vibrational interaction.

Key observables. Entropy production ($\Pi(t)$), entropy flux ($\Phi(t)$), mechanical Wigner function $W(q, p, t)$, and mutual information $I_{s:m}(t)$.

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength ($g_1$), mechanical damping ($\gamma_m$), spin relaxation rates ($\Gamma$), and external driving strength ($\epsilon$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on projecting the spin manifold to two states and treating the mechanical mode using a Gaussian propagator, which assumes linear damping and quadratic Hamiltonians.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the time evolution of the reduced mechanical Wigner function, showing oscillations between vacuum and one-phonon profiles, and plot the time evolution of mechanical and spin entropy fluxes.

Paper structure. The paper builds from deriving the effective Jaynes-Cummings Hamiltonian, to applying the Wigner function formalism for the mechanical mode, and finally deriving the entropy balance equation to quantify entropy production and flux in various coupling regimes.

Abstract

Hybrid carbon nanotube-fullerene architectures provide a controllable platform for studying irreversibility and information flow in structured quantum environments. We analyze entropy generation in a system where paramagnetic endohedral fullerenes, such as N@C$_{60}$ and P@C$_{60}$, are encapsulated inside a suspended carbon nanotube (CNT) resonator, with selected multi-level fullerene spin states forming an effective qubit coupled to quantized CNT flexural modes. Building on prior work on fullerene-filled CNTs, spin-phonon control in suspended nanotubes, and phase-space propagators for damped driven oscillators, we develop a hybrid open-system model combining driven quantum Brownian motion of the CNT with an effective Jaynes-Cummings spin-vibrational interaction. The resonator dynamics are represented by a Wigner function whose evolution is written analytically in terms of the initial Wigner distribution and a Gaussian propagator. This phase-space description separates drive-induced displacement, diffusion, and damping, and connects these processes directly to entropy flow. The coupled spin-mechanical dynamics are embedded in a Lindblad master equation including mechanical damping, spin relaxation, pure dephasing, and thermally activated excitation. Within this framework we derive the entropy balance, identify entropy flux and non-negative entropy production, and examine how spin-vibrational hybridization redistributes irreversibility between coherent exchange and dissipative channels. We show that magnetic-gradient-enhanced spin-phonon coupling, resonant driving, and moderate thermal occupation produce crossovers between oscillator-dominated and spin-dominated entropy-production regimes. The framework provides a basis for using CNT-PEF hybrids as nanoscale platforms to study nonequilibrium quantum thermodynamics, decoherence, and information loss in vibrational environments.

Summary. This work develops a theoretical framework to extract microscopic fluid-particle interaction details from single Brownian trajectories by treating equilibrium states as superpositions of non-equilibrium paths. It analyzes the short-time scaling of the mean-squared displacement, predicting crossovers between $t^{5/2}$ and $t^4$ scaling depending on the inclusion of correlated stochastic forces. This provides a powerful method to test hydrodynamic models using single-trajectory measurements.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous connection between non-equilibrium path measurements and equilibrium hydrodynamic properties is a powerful theoretical tool for characterizing complex many-body dynamics, relevant for understanding dissipation in open quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to unravel fine details of fluid-particle interactions at microscopic scales by analyzing non-equilibrium states derived from single Brownian trajectories, connecting them to macroscopic hydrodynamics.

Main result. The analysis confirms the $t^{5/2}$-law for short-time displacement statistics due to fluid inertial effects but predicts a crossover to a $t^4$-scaling at very short times when correlated stochastic forcings are properly accounted for.

Method. The approach utilizes the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation, viewing equilibrium trajectories as superpositions of non-equilibrium states, and analyzing the second-order moments of particle position via Generalized Langevin Equations (GLEs).

Model / system. The system is a Brownian particle moving in a solvent fluid, modeled using concepts from Markovian dynamics, path integrals, and memory kernels derived from hydrodynamic theory.

Key observables. Mean square displacement $\langle x^2(t) angle$, velocity autocorrelation function, and scaling exponents ($\phi, \zeta, d_v$).

Important parameters / regimes. Short time $t$, fluid viscosity ($\eta$), and parameters related to fluid inertia and correlation time.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the Markovian embedding for non-Markovian processes and involves approximations in the Laplace domain for short-time limits.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the mean-squared displacement $m_{xx}(t)$ vs $t$ for various models, showing crossovers between $t^3$, $t^{5/2}$, and $t^4$ scaling regimes depending on the physical assumptions.

Paper structure. The paper builds theory by relating equilibrium paths to non-equilibrium states, develops the mathematical framework using GLEs and memory kernels, and concludes by deriving and comparing the short-time scaling laws for $\langle x^2(t) angle$ under different hydrodynamic assumptions.

Abstract

Owing to the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation for Markovian dynamics,any equilibrium trajectory of a Brownian particle in a solvent fluid can be viewed as the superposition of an uncountable number of non-equilibrium states. This property permits the unraveling of fine details of fluid-particle interactions at microscales defined by its non-equilibrium properties from the analysis of a single Brownian trajectory and to connect them to the hydrodynamics of the solvent fluid, simply considering the lower-order (second) moments of particle position in trapped conditions. In this way, the acceleration due to thermal-hydrodynamic fluctuational forces is isolated from the other factors and the short-time displacement statistics is completely determined by the correlation properties of the fluctuational thermal-hydrodynamic force. This approach not only confirms the $t^{5/2}$-law obtained by Boynewicz et al. (2026), related to fluid inertial effects, but indicates that this scaling may be superseded by a $t^4$-scaling at very short times once the correlated nature of the stochastic forcings is taken into account. The latter result is related to the regularity properties of particle velocity realizations.

Summary. This paper analyzes entanglement dynamics in bosonic systems coupled to structured, memory-retaining reservoirs. These findings establish structured reservoirs as controllable resources for continuous-variable quantum technologies.

Why it may be interesting. Main problem. The study investigates how entanglement dynamics evolve in continuous-variable Gaussian systems when coupled to structured, finite-memory quantum reservoirs, addressing gaps left by Markovian or pre-entangled state assumptions.

Main result. The authors identify three non-Markovian mechanisms—detuning-induced freezing, entanglement revival from separable inputs, and integer-locked beating—that are absent in Markovian dynamics.

Method. The analysis employs Gaussian covariance methods, evolving the system dynamics using approximate Non-Markovian Quantum State Diffusion (QSD) and utilizing Bures distance diagnostics to quantify non-Markovianity.

Model / system. The model consists of two bosonic modes coupled to a common bosonic reservoir, whose memory effects are modeled using an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck kernel. The analysis is extended to finite temperatures via pseudomode embeddings.

Important parameters / regimes. Detuning ($\delta_{AB}, \delta_{AE}$), reservoir correlation time ($\gamma$), thermal occupation ($ar{n}$), and modulation amplitude ($\delta_0/\kappa$).

Abstract

Entanglement in continuous-variable Gaussian systems is a key resource, and common reservoirs can both suppress and generate correlations. Existing work focused on pre-entangled states or Markovian baths, leaving open whether separable squeezed inputs entangle in structured environments or under modulation. We study two bosonic modes coupled to a common reservoir, each initialized in a separable squeezed vacuum. Dynamics are analyzed utilizing Gaussian covariance methods, evolved under approximate Non-Markovian quantum state diffusion (QSD), finite-temperature pseudomode embeddings, and Bures-based non-Markovian diagnostics. We identify three mechanisms absent in Markovian dynamics: (1) A detuning condition that freezes entanglement trajectories across reservoir correlation times; (2) birth, death, and revival of entanglement from orthogonal inputs; and (3) integer-locked beating with square-wave oscillations produced by periodic detuning. All mechanisms persist at finite temperature, with deviations bounded within 5% in cryogenic regimes and 20% at moderate occupations. These deviation bounds align with cryogenic cavity, phononic, and optomechanical platforms, where structured spectral densities and detuning modulation are already accessible. Structured reservoirs are shown to emerge as tunable entanglement resources for continuous-variable quantum technologies.

Abstract

We show that the late-time dynamics of a minimal three-component mass-conserving reaction--diffusion system reduce to a scalar active field theory, Active Model B$^-$ (AMB$^-$), in which a density-dependent interfacial coefficient $κ(φ)$ turns negative at high density. This drives a finite-wavelength instability and stabilises microphase-separated patterns, in contrast to the unbounded coarsening of two-component mass-conserving systems. Unlike Active Model B$^+$, AMB$^-$ retains a chemical potential that remains a state function, inherited from the underlying conservation law, but admits no equation of state for the pressure.

Summary. This work establishes fundamental mathematical bounds on the complex eigenvalues of Markov generators by linking them to the system's non-equilibrium cycles. It proves that the oscillatory behavior of the system is constrained by a tradeoff between thermodynamic driving forces (cycle affinity) and the geometric winding number of the eigenvectors. Main problem. To understand how complex eigenvalues of Markov generators, which govern oscillatory properties of relaxation and autocorrelation, are localized by non-equilibrium cycles.

Main result. The paper proves that complex eigenvalues are confined to regions determined by the cycle affinity and the eigenvector's winding number, establishing new thermodynamic bounds on relaxation modes.

Method. The analysis uses spectral theory applied to continuous-time Markov chains, relating eigenvalue properties to cycle affinities and winding numbers via geometric and convex hull constructions.

Abstract

The complex eigenvalues of Markov generators govern oscillatory properties of relaxation, autocorrelation, and linear response. Here we show that these eigenvalues are localized by nonequilibrium cycles of the generator, thus revealing a fundamental tradeoff between thermodynamic driving, oscillation, and decay of eigenmodes. Specifically, we prove that each complex eigenvalue is confined to a region determined by the cycle affinity and the eigenvector ``winding number'' of some nonequilibrium cycle. In unicyclic systems, we also demonstrate that the winding number coincides with the ordered eigenvalue index, yielding new thermodynamic bounds on the slowest and fastest relaxation modes. In multicyclic systems, our approach unifies and extends several previous inequalities and proves the Uhl--Seifert ellipse conjecture.

Abstract

Non-Hermitian physics enables novel control over open quantum and wave systems, but selectively isolating individual modes without delicate balancing of gain and loss remains challenging. Here we introduce a gauge-engineering method in directed-graph networks that support geometry-protected pure decay modes-eigenstates exhibiting smooth exponential amplitude decay along directed paths. The approach extends to simultaneous selection of paired modes in half-connected graphs and customizable multi-mode distributions in higher dimensions via orthogonal folding. Our method enables robust, loss/gain-free control over mode profiles, advancing applications in single-mode lasers, sensors, and quantum processing.

Abstract

The main ideas and some of the most important results of the spherically symmetric self-consistent approach and a number of related theoretical algorithms are presented. These methods make it possible to study low-dimensional Heisenberg-type spin models, including frustrated ones, with careful consideration of the theoretic (Mermin-Wagner and Marshall) theorems, as well as the site spin constraint. Thus, the difficulties that may arise in the traditional analysis of low-dimensional magnetic systems are avoided. The approach can also be applied to the spin-pseudospin model, and is also embedded in more complex constructions when considering spin models with free carriers, such as the basic and three-band Hubbard models, t-J and s-d models, and the Kondo lattice.

The fractal dimension of Brownian dynamics in liquids

Summary. This paper investigates how fluid inertia modifies the expected fractal scaling of Brownian motion, moving beyond the classical Einstein-Langevin prediction. Through a combination of high-resolution experiments and advanced theoretical modeling, the authors demonstrate that the true velocity fractal dimension is $d_v = 7/4$. This work establishes a non-equilibrium universality class for particle dynamics in viscous fluids.

Why it may be interesting. The work provides a detailed, quantitative study of non-equilibrium dynamics in a classical system, using advanced scaling analysis techniques that are highly relevant to understanding memory effects in open quantum systems or complex many-body dynamics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To revise the understanding of the fractal dimension of Brownian velocity fluctuations, moving beyond the classical Einstein-Langevin theory prediction.

Main result. The inclusion of fluid-inertial memory effects fundamentally changes the scaling, establishing a distinct velocity fractal dimension of $d_v = 7/4$ instead of the classical $3/2$.

Method. The study combines experimental measurements of microspheres in liquids with theoretical analysis using generalized Langevin equations and scale-dependent fluctuation analysis.

Model / system. The system is Brownian microspheres moving in a liquid, modeled by incorporating fluid-inertial memory effects via the Basset kernel, moving beyond the simple Markovian Langevin assumption.

Key observables. Velocity fractal dimension ($d_v$), scaling exponent $\phi$ from Mean Squared Displacement ($ ext{MSD} \sim au^\phi$), and the scale-dependent absolute slope ($S_v(\Delta t)$).

Important parameters / regimes. The analysis distinguishes between the classical (Einstein-Langevin) and hydrodynamic (Basset-Boussinesq) regimes, focusing on short-time, non-equilibrium dynamics.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on relating scaling exponents ($\phi, H_v, d_v$) and requires careful handling of short-time singularities and experimental noise/resolution limits.

Figures summary. Figures compare scaling functions ($L_x, S_x, L_v, S_v$) for different models, showing crossovers in scaling behavior and confirming the $d_v = 7/4$ result from experimental data.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from establishing the classical theory, introducing the non-Markovian hydrodynamic model, developing advanced measurement techniques (like $S_v(\Delta t)$), and finally deriving the non-equilibrium universality class using scaling analysis.

Abstract

The classical Einstein-Langevin theory of Brownian motion assumes a memoryless thermal bath, establishing a universal fractal dimension of $d_v = 3/2$ for the velocity fluctuations of a particle. In this Letter, we demonstrate experimentally and theoretically that fluid-inertial memory effects fundamentally redefine the fractal scaling of these fluctuations. In analyzing highly resolved measurements of Brownian microspheres in liquids, we show that the non-Markovian hydrodynamic thermal noise establishes a distinct velocity fractal dimension of $d_v = 7/4$. Coupled with theoretical analysis of non-equilibrium short-time dynamics and the initial scaling of the velocity autocorrelation function, this result establishes the non-equilibrium universality class of Brownian motion in fluid media possessing a finite non-vanishing density.
