Beyond trace-class and Hilbert-Schmidt -- Interaction between operator ideals and von Neumann algebras in quantum physics

Authors: Frank Oertel
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16025v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: entanglement & information structure 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical paper bridges functional analysis and quantum physics by showing that concepts like operator ideals are fundamental to quantum theory. It rigorously connects the tensor product of Hilbert spaces to specific classes of bounded operators, offering a deep mathematical insight into quantum information and quantum field theory structures.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a highly abstract, mathematical underpinning for quantum information protocols (like teleportation) and quantum field theory, suggesting that the underlying mathematical structure is governed by operator ideal theory.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to reveal deep, structural connections between abstract mathematical tools—specifically operator ideals (like trace-class and $p$-nuclear operators)—and the foundational formalism of quantum physics, including AQFT and quantum information theory.

Main result. It establishes that the tensor product structure of Hilbert spaces can be rigorously identified with spaces of absolutely $p$-summing operators, providing a linear algebraic description for processes like quantum teleportation.

Method. The methodology combines advanced functional analysis (Pietsch's operator ideals, Grothendieck theory) with the mathematical framework of von Neumann algebras and the analysis of conjugate Hilbert spaces.

Model / system. The theoretical framework involves general complex Hilbert spaces, their tensor products, and the algebraic structures underpinning quantum field theories (von Neumann algebras). Applications include modeling quantum teleportation and analyzing compatibility in General Probabilistic Theories (GPTs).

Key observables. Operator ideals ($\mathcal{L}_2, \mathcal{N}, \mathcal{P}_2$), the compatibility degree $\gamma$, and the modular operator $\Delta$.

Important parameters / regimes. The parameter $p$ in $p$-nuclear operators ($1 \le p < \infty$), and the number of measurements $g$ in GPTs.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies heavily on advanced theorems regarding isometric isomorphisms between a Hilbert space and its dual, and the assumption of 'split' properties for commuting von Neumann algebras.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from analyzing the conjugate Hilbert space and its relation to the dual space, to establishing the identification of tensor products with operator ideals, and finally applying these concepts to AQFT and GPTs.

Abstract

Starting from a thorough analysis of the conjugate $\overline{H}$ of a complex Hilbert space $H$, including its significant importance regarding a representation of the tensor product of two complex Hilbert spaces and its impact to the theorem of Fréchet-Riesz over to a revisit of applications of nuclear and absolutely $p$-summing operators in algebraic quantum field theory (AQFT) in the sense of Araki, Haag and Kastler ($p=2$) and more recently in the framework of general probabilistic spaces ($p=1$), we will outline that Banach operator ideals in the sense of Pietsch, or equivalently tensor products of Banach spaces in the sense of Grothendieck are even lurking in the foundations and philosophy of quantum physics and quantum information theory. In particular, we concentrate on their importance in AQFT (Theorem 5.27). In doing so, we revisit the role of trace-class operators in quantum theory and construct the enveloping $\tup{C}^\adj$-algebra, corresponding to an arbitrarily given normed operator ideal (Proposition 5.3 and Theorem 5.5). Applications are presented, including a purely linear algebraic description of the quantum teleportation process, thereby showing a link to quantum information theory, also due to the emergence of the Hadamard-Walsh transform and the controlled NOT gate (Example 4.18). All Hilbert spaces discussed in this paper may be nonseparable (and hence infinite-dimensional).

Summary. This paper proposes a practical, partially fault-tolerant quantum computation scheme by concatenating established quantum error-correcting codes. It demonstrates that by accepting non-strict fault tolerance and using selective filtering, the scheme achieves low logical error rates efficiently at realistic noise levels. This work offers a tangible roadmap for scaling quantum computation on near-term hardware.

Why it may be interesting. The detailed analysis of error scaling, overhead management, and the comparison between different gate implementations (e.g., teleported vs. direct CNOT) provides concrete, actionable engineering guidelines for building fault-tolerant quantum processors.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a quantum computation scheme that is reliable and resource-efficient for near-term quantum hardware, bridging the gap between physical and required logical error rates.

Main result. The concatenated error-correcting scheme is shown to be reliable and resource efficient at realistic simulated noise rates, demonstrating a practical path toward scalable quantum computation.

Method. The authors develop the scheme by concatenating the five-qubit Laflamme code onto the four-qubit Iceberg code, relying on selective filtering rather than strict fault tolerance.

Model / system. The analysis focuses on quantum error correction codes (e.g., J20, 2, 6K; J36, 2, 8K) applied to near-term quantum hardware, specifically mentioning platforms like trapped ions or neutral atoms.

Key observables. Logical Error Rates ($p_L$), Rejection Rates ($p_R$), and Overhead (e.g., CNOT gate counts per accepted state).

Important parameters / regimes. Physical error rates ($p \sim 10^{-4}$ to $4 imes 10^{-3}$), required logical error rates ($< 10^{-4}$ or $10^{-6}$), and code distance ($d$).

Assumptions / limitations. The scheme is explicitly not strictly fault-tolerant, and the analysis relies on simplified circuit noise models and postselection.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the concatenation process, stabilizer generators, and circuit diagrams comparing one-stage vs. two-stage ancilla factories for state preparation and CNOT gate implementation.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the concatenated code structure, then analyzing the role of postselection in managing error rates, and finally comparing the overhead and performance of implementing logical gates (like CNOT) using different circuit designs.

Abstract

We develop an error-corrected quantum computation scheme based on concatenating the five-qubit Laflamme code onto the four-qubit Iceberg code. The approach skates a thin line: it is explicitly not fault tolerant, risking higher logical error rates, and it relies on selective filtering to prepare encoded states for error correction, risking significant overhead. Yet, at realistic simulated noise rates, the scheme is reliable and resource efficient. It forges a practical path toward scalable quantum computation.

Towards Deploying Optimistic Quantum Fourier Transforms: An Architecture-Algorithm Co-Design Study

Authors: Pedro L. S. Lopes
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15297v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: QC/QI experiment 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper presents a detailed co-design study for running the Optimistic Quantum Fourier Transform on neutral-atom quantum hardware. It introduces a 'hot-zone' architecture to manage mobile quantum resources, achieving significant speedups by optimizing the scheduling of core components like quantum adders. The work establishes critical resource requirements, showing that performance hinges on managing parallelism and reaction-limited operations.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a concrete, hardware-aware roadmap for implementing complex quantum algorithms like QFT, directly linking algorithmic complexity to physical resource constraints (mobility, parallelism) in neutral-atom platforms.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of efficiently deploying the Optimistic Quantum Fourier Transform (OQFT) algorithm on near-term, fault-tolerant quantum hardware.

Main result. By implementing a hot-zone architecture and optimizing adder circuits, the OQFT can achieve significant speedups, potentially halving the runtime compared to serial QFTs, provided sufficient parallelism is available.

Method. The authors employ an architecture-algorithm co-design study, simulating resource routing and scheduling across specialized hardware components.

Model / system. The study is scoped to reconfigurable neutral-atom hardware utilizing the surface code for fault tolerance. It models the system using a 'hot-zone' architecture that separates stationary data storage from mobile processing units.

Key observables. Time to solution, protocol volume (space-time cost), peak Degrees of Freedom (DOF), and the required number of logical ancillae.

Important parameters / regimes. Code distance (d=15), hot-zone count (2 to 4+), and the size of the quantum register (256–2048 bits).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes reaction-limited operation and is currently scoped to cultivation-only magic-state factories and surface codes.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the decomposition of OQFT into sub-blocks, compare T-counts across different implementations, and show macro-scale architectural layouts detailing the hot zone and data regions.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from analyzing the OQFT structure and resource needs, to developing the hot-zone architecture, comparing and selecting optimal adder circuits (Gidney vs. Cuccaro), and finally integrating these components to analyze the full OQFT performance scaling.

Abstract

We present an architecture-algorithm co-design study of the Optimistic Quantum Fourier Transform (OQFT) under a surface-code fault-tolerant execution model for reconfigurable neutral-atom hardware. Analyzing the OQFT structure, particularly its reliance on phase-gradient resources and small-scale blocks, highlights architectural requirements for resource mobility and parallel execution. Guided by that, we introduce a hot-zone architecture that decouples data storage from processing and dynamically routes mobile resource packages (magic-state factories, bridge qubits, and phase-gradient registers) to stationary data regions. To expose dominant costs, we route rotation insertions via catalytic phase-gradient addition and heuristically micro-schedule ripple-carry adders to patch-level moves. Under this model, leading Gidney~\cite{Gidney2018halvingcostof} and Cuccaro~\cite{cuccaro2004} adders have similar space-time volume but require different levels of parallelism. At the algorithm level, the five-layer OQFT shows a tunable parallelism/latency trade-off: two hot zones match serial-QFT latency, four hot zones roughly halve runtime, and additional hot zones asymptotically approach constant-time execution at substantial resource cost. Across 256-2048-bit instances, the requirements for half-time performance converge to about 500 additional logical ancillae and a peak parallelism of 128 logical qubits. We also identify broader algorithm-architecture bottlenecks, including endianness mismatches between phase-gradient and data registers, addressed via cyclic phase-gradient swaps and alternating QFT reflections. Scoped to surface codes and cultivation-only magic-state factories, our analysis identifies reaction-limited operation and parallelism demand as primary drivers of resource estimation and establishes a generalizable foundation for primitive-based architectural studies.

Summary. This theoretical paper generalizes the Tolman-Ehrenfest effect to coupled thermodynamic channels, deriving a universal invariant $\zeta_i T_i = C$. It applies this geometric framework to granular materials, successfully resolving long-standing puzzles regarding state-dependent material constants. The work demonstrates how geometric invariants derived from the entropy manifold can predict macroscopic physical laws in complex, coupled systems.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on granular physics, the mathematical machinery—using geometric invariants (Ruppeiner connection, curvature) to define equilibrium in coupled systems—is highly relevant to understanding non-equilibrium thermodynamics in complex quantum many-body systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the failure of single-channel equilibrium conditions when multiple thermodynamic channels are coupled, aiming to derive a universal invariant condition.

Main result. The unique equilibrium invariant is found to be $\zeta_i T_i = C$, where $\zeta_i$ is the holonomy of the Ruppeiner connection, which resolves long-standing puzzles in granular physics.

Method. The analysis extends the Tolman-Ehrenfest effect to the entropy manifold, utilizing the Ruppeiner metric and deriving the equilibrium condition as a first integral of the level-set flow.

Model / system. The framework is applied to systems with $n$ coupled thermodynamic channels, with the canonical realization being the $N=2$ volume–stress granular ensemble.

Key observables. The invariant $\zeta_i T_i = C$, the off-diagonal curvature $g_{V\sigma}$, the dilatancy ratio, and the MCTE-corrected Rowe relation $\sigma'^{1/3} = \zeta_V(\sigma) K_\mu R$.

Important parameters / regimes. The coupling coefficient $\zeta_i$, the jamming point ($\phi_J$), and the dimensionality of the system ($N=2$ being the primary focus).

Assumptions / limitations. The $N=2$ volume–stress ensemble is used as the canonical model, and the results are derived independently of the full $N=3$ non-associated flow dynamics.

Figures summary. Figure 1 contrasts classical decoupled vs. coupled (MCTE) behavior showing the invariant. Figure 2 illustrates the MCTE invariance on a toy entropy surface, showing the product $\zeta_1 T_1$ remains constant despite variations in bare compactivity.

Paper structure. The paper first establishes the failure of single-channel equilibrium, derives the MCTE invariant $\zeta_i T_i = C$ using the Ruppeiner metric, and then applies this general formalism to predict and resolve specific issues in granular plasticity (dilatancy, energy restriction, $K_\mu$ dependence).

Abstract

When multiple thermodynamic channels are coupled, single-channel equilibrium conditions fail. Extending the Tolman--Ehrenfest effect to the entropy manifold, we derive the unique $n$-channel invariant $ζ_i T_i = C$, where $ζ_i$ is the holonomy of the Ruppeiner connection. For the granular volume--stress ensemble, Rowe's dilatancy ratio and energy restriction emerge as geometric consequences of the off-diagonal curvature $g_{Vσ}$, and the 60-year puzzle of state-dependent $K_μ$ is resolved: the correction $ζ_V$ reaches $O(1)$ near jamming. The prediction $ζ_Vχ=\mathrm{const}$ across a shear band is experimentally testable.

Wave packet landscape in linear open quantum systems

Summary. This paper introduces a quantum landscape approach to analyze how wave packets evolve in open quantum systems. It shows that the long-time behavior—whether the packet collapses, localizes, or diffuses—is determined by the underlying geometry of a potential landscape in the covariance space. This geometric picture unifies these distinct dissipative quantum dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. This work provides a powerful geometric framework to unify disparate phenomena (diffusion, localization, collapse) in open quantum systems, offering deeper physical insight than standard master equation treatments.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop a quantum landscape approach to characterize the long-time behavior of wave packet spreading in linear open quantum systems.

Main result. The long-time dynamics (diffusion, localization, collapse) are unified by the geometry of a potential landscape in covariance space, showing that symmetry breaking dictates the asymptotic behavior.

Method. The dynamics of the covariance matrix are mapped onto a generalized gradient flow equation, $\dot{\sigma} = -M abla L(\sigma)$, where $L$ is the quantum landscape function.

Model / system. The system consists of linear open quantum systems, modeled by coupled harmonic oscillators interacting with a bath (Caldeira-Leggett type). The dynamics are governed by the quantum Langevin equation.

Key observables. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (or its vectorization $\sigma$), and the position width $\Delta q(t)$.

Important parameters / regimes. The oscillator frequency ($\omega$), damping rate ($\Gamma$), and the presence/strength of bath fluctuations ($\xi(t)$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on casting the covariance evolution into a gradient flow form and analyzing the zero modes of the system matrix.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the deformation of the landscape: a bowl shape for confined systems (implying collapse) versus a flat valley for free particles, which becomes tilted by fluctuations.

Paper structure. The paper first introduces the landscape approach to the covariance dynamics, then analyzes specific cases (e.g., harmonic oscillator vs. free particle) to demonstrate how the landscape geometry dictates the asymptotic state, culminating in a discussion of symmetry breaking due to fluctuations.

Abstract

We develop a quantum landscape approach to characterize the long-time behavior of wave packet spreading in linear open quantum systems. Instead of treating diffusion, localization, and collapse of the wave packet as separate dynamical phenomena, we show that they originate from the symmetry structure of an underlying landscape in covariance space. The geometry of this landscape determines these distinct long time behaviors. Trapping potentials and bath fluctuations act as distinct symmetry-breaking perturbations, leading to noncommuting long-time limits and abrupt changes in the asymptotic wave-packet width. This geometric picture provides a unified origin for wave-packet diffusion, localization, and collapse in dissipative quantum dynamics.

Summary. This work analyzes the collective dynamics of neural networks modeled by QIF neurons coupled on sparse random graphs. By combining linear stability analysis with random matrix theory, the authors predict the onset of instabilities, which manifest as high-dimensional chaos in directed networks. The findings establish a theoretical link between network topology, coupling parameters, and the emergence of complex, chaotic spatiotemporal activity.

Why it may be interesting. The rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing collective dynamics in large, sparse, and disordered systems is highly relevant to understanding complex quantum many-body systems where local interactions lead to emergent global behavior.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the emergence of spatiotemporal patterns and high-dimensional chaos in coupled neural populations modeled by exact firing rate models on sparse random networks.

Main result. The system exhibits extensive high-dimensional chaos in directed networks for both excitatory and inhibitory coupling, while undirected inhibitory networks show winner-takes-all steady states.

Method. The analysis combines linear stability analysis of homogeneous states with random matrix theory to determine bifurcation boundaries, followed by Lyapunov exponent calculations to quantify chaos.

Model / system. The model uses $N$ populations of Quadratic Integrate-and-Fire (QIF) neurons coupled via a sparse random network with row-normalized topology. The dynamics are governed by coupled differential equations.

Key observables. Lyapunov exponents ($\ell_i$), Kaplan-Yorke fractal dimension ($D_{KY}$), and the dispersion relation linking growth rates to connectivity matrix eigenvalues ($\Lambda_k$).

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength ($J$), network sparsity ($\kappa$), and the diagonal coupling parameter ($\mu$).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the system can be linearized around homogeneous states and utilizes the row-normalization constraint on the connectivity matrix.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate bifurcation diagrams showing instability boundaries, and others map the dynamical landscape using Lyapunov exponents and the scaling of the fractal dimension with system size.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from defining the QIF model and performing linear stability analysis to deriving spectral properties using random matrix theory, and finally characterizing the resulting dynamics using Lyapunov exponents to confirm high-dimensional chaos.

Abstract

Exact firing rate models, also known as next-generation neural mass models (NG-NMMs), provide a rigorous description of the dynamics of neural populations. While in its simplest form a single population only displays fixed-point activity, multi-population models may display a range of different behaviors. In this work, we study the dynamics of all-excitatory or all-inhibitory NG-NMMs coupled through sparse random networks with row-normalized network topology. Linear stability analysis of the homogeneous states of the system, representing asynchronous neural activity, provides a dispersion relation linking the emergence of spatiotemporal dynamics to the spectra of the connectivity matrix. Using bounds from random matrix theory, we identify the parameter regions where instabilities occur. In undirected networks, only inhibitory systems produce heterogeneous stationary patterns, corresponding to a winner-takes-all mechanism. In directed networks, exotic rhythmic states with high frequencies emerge in both, excitatory and inhibitory systems. Numerical simulations reveal that these hectic oscillatory states correspond to high-dimensional chaos with extensive properties.

Diagonal Adaptive Non-local Observables on Quantum Neural Networks

Authors: Huan-Hsin Tseng, Yan Li, Hsin-Yi Lin, Samuel Yen-Chi Chen
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15410v1

Summary. This paper introduces Diagonal Adaptive Non-local Observables (DANO) to enhance Variational Quantum Algorithms by making the measurement observable trainable. By restricting the observable to a diagonal form, DANO significantly reduces the classical computational cost and parameter count compared to general methods, while mathematically retaining the full expressive power of the original approach. This makes complex, high-locality quantum machine learning tasks feasible on near-term hardware.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on QML, the core technique of parameterizing observables via restricted structures (diagonalization) and proving equivalence to a larger space is relevant to understanding constraints and effective Hamiltonians in open quantum systems or when modeling complex interactions in condensed matter.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To improve Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) by enlarging the function space beyond the circuit ansatz alone, while mitigating the exponential increase in parameters and classical optimization cost associated with general trainable observables.

Main result. The proposed Diagonal Adaptive Non-local Observables (DANO) framework achieves the full expressivity of general Adaptive Non-local Observables (ANO) while reducing the observable parameter complexity from $O(4^k)$ to $O(2^k)$, making large-scale measurements practical.

Method. The authors restrict the trainable observable $H$ to a diagonal form $\Lambda$, which is mathematically shown to be equivalent in expressivity to the full ANO space under certain conditions.

Model / system. The work focuses on Quantum Neural Networks (QNNs) implemented via Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) trained on NISQ devices. The model involves training parameters $ heta$ for the unitary circuit $U( heta)$ alongside parameters $\lambda$ for the diagonal observable $\Lambda(\lambda)$.

Key observables. The expectation values of the trainable diagonal observables $\Lambda_j$, which are measured via sliding $k$-local measurements.

Important parameters / regimes. The locality $k$ of the observable, the number of qubits $n$, and the circuit parameters $ heta$ and observable parameters $\lambda$.

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is that restricting the observable to a diagonal form retains full ANO expressivity, which is supported by a density proof relying on the circuit generating set spanning $SU(K)$.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the VQC structure, the training trajectory involving observable changes, and the partitioning of the observable space. Tables summarize performance gains on MNIST and Yale B face recognition when using DANO vs. pure VQC.

Paper structure. The paper first introduces the challenge of ANO, then proposes DANO by restricting observables to diagonal forms. It provides theoretical guarantees of expressivity equivalence, followed by empirical validation on classification tasks (MNIST and Yale B) demonstrating performance gains and cost reductions.

Abstract

Adaptive Non-local Observables (ANOs) have shown that making quantum observables dynamic can substantially enlarge the function space of Variational Quantum Algorithms, partly shifting hardware demands from circuit synthesis to measurement design. However, this advantage is accompanied by a steep increase in the number of parameters, as well as the classical optimization cost for varying general Hermitian observables. We propose a special form of ANO that significantly reduces this burden by considering only diagonal observables paired with quantum circuits. Mathematically, this is equivalent to the full ANO of a large parameter space since diagonal matrices are canonical representatives of the ANO space modulo unitary similarity. As a result, Diagonal ANO retains the same capability of full ANO while reducing $k$-local observable complexity from $O(4^k)$ to $O(2^k)$ and lowering the corresponding measurement-side classical computation. In this sense, diagonal ANO preserves much of the benefit of full ANO while encompassing conventional VQCs as a special case.

Summary. This paper reports on the synthesis and characterization of Y4Co3Ag, a novel compound designed to host quasi-one-dimensional Co chains. Through magnetic and transport measurements, the authors establish that the material orders antiferromagnetically at 14.9 K, exhibiting signatures of itinerant magnetism. Why it may be interesting. The work provides an experimental realization of designing magnetic materials based on structural motifs (antagonistic pairs) to isolate and study specific magnetic interactions, which is highly relevant for understanding correlated electron physics in low dimensions.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the nature of antiferromagnetism in the novel compound Y4Co3Ag, aiming to characterize its magnetic interactions within a quasi-one-dimensional structure.

Main result. The material exhibits antiferromagnetic ordering at $T_N=14.9$ K, suggesting the presence of small, itinerant magnetic moments and strong magnetic fluctuations in a low-dimensional Co chain system.

Method. The research employed a combination of structural characterization (XRD) and multiple physical measurements, including temperature-dependent resistivity, specific heat, and high/low-field magnetization.

Model / system. The physical system is the intermetallic compound Y4Co3Ag, which crystallizes in a monoclinic structure featuring one-dimensional Co chains running along the b-axis, designed using the 'antagonistic pair' concept to avoid Co-Ag nearest-neighbor contacts.

Key observables. Antiferromagnetic ordering temperature ($T_N$), effective magnetic moment ($\mu_{ ext{eff}}$), specific heat anomaly, and magnetization isotherms $M(H)$.

Abstract

Low dimensional crystallographic motifs have long been associated with desirable physical properties. The confinement of electrons to low dimensions is thought to enhance quantum fluctuations and may promote correlated phenomena. Here, using the antagonistic pair concept, we add Y to the immiscible Co-Ag pair to discover Y$_4$Co$_3$Ag. This compound adopts a monoclinic $I$2/$m$ structure consisting of Y channels that are filled by one-dimensional zigzag and hexagonal Co chains, which extend along the crystallographic $b$-axis with no nearest neighbor contacts between Co and Ag atoms. Transport, magnetic, and specific heat measurements reveal that Y$_4$Co$_3$Ag orders antiferromagnetically at $T_N=14.9$ K with an effective magnetic moment $μ_{\text{eff}}$ = 1.4 $μ_{\text{B}}$/Co. Specific heat measurements show only a small entropy loss on the order of $0.1\,R\ln2$ associated with magnetic order, and magnetization isotherms, in DC fields up to 70 kOe at 1.8 K and in pulsed fields up to 600 kOe at 500 mK, indicate a small ordered moment of less than 0.2 $μ_B$/Co. Taken together, our results imply the presence of small, itinerant moments and strong fluctuations in Y$_4$Co$_3$Ag, suggesting that Y$_4$Co$_3$Ag may be a promising candidate material to investigate itinerant magnetic interactions in a quasi-one dimensional system.

Abstract

Sorting known approaches in this way shows why embedding frameworks fit modern tools better, while faster linear algebra waits for more advanced systems.

Abstract

Abstract

BEDT-TTF-based organic conductors host a number of ground states, tuned by electron repulsion from Mott and charge ordered insulators to superconductors. Knowing charge distribution on the molecular sites in the insulating state of these materials is a key to understanding the origin of these ground states. Abstract

Applying single-qubit Clifford unitaries to a Pauli stabilizer code produces a Clifford-deformed variant whose stabilizers remain Pauli operators, but with locally rotated Pauli axes. Such deformations provide a simple way to tailor a fixed code to anisotropic noise, and have enabled unusually high thresholds under strongly biased dephasing. In this work, we discuss zero-rate quantum low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, for which there exist Clifford-deformed variants where the number of biased logical operators scales slower than the distance, or there exists a basis of logical operators whose overlap satisfies certain scaling conditions; in this case, the code-capacity threshold for the Clifford-deformed variant under i.i.d. pure dephasing noise approaches 50%. This property provably explains previously known code examples with 50% biased noise thresholds, such as XY surface code, XZZX surface code, color code, as well as some 3D Clifford-deformed codes. As a concrete new example, we study Clifford deformations of the tile codes of Ref. [1]. Similar to the phase diagram of 50% thresholds for random Clifford deformations of the surface code in Ref. [2], we find a similar phase diagram for the tile codes. We also construct several translationally invariant deformations of the tile code with 50% thresholds, and present numerical evidence for improved performance at finite bias and under circuit-level noise. In the circuit-level setting, performance is governed by the residual bias after a full syndrome-extraction cycle, linking our simulations to phenomenological models commonly used to study Clifford-deformed codes. We estimate this residual bias for different qubit platforms by modeling microscopic implementations of tile-code syndrome extraction.

Abstract

We construct coherent states for the quantized electromagnetic field that correspond to the classical non-null torus knot solutions of Maxwell's equations in vacuum. We derive the displacement operators from the general relation between classical fields and coherent state amplitudes and verify the defining properties of coherent states through direct computation. We determine the observables of the model: field expectation values, energy density, Poynting vector, helicity, photon number, quadrature uncertainties, and correlation functions, and calculate their expectation values in the knotted coherent states in terms of the integer parameters $(n,m,l,s)$ of the classical solutions. As an example, we particularize the construction in the case of the Hopfion coherent state. These results establish the quantum-classical correspondence for this type of vacuum topological electromagnetic systems.
