Ising anyons in the $SU(2)_2$ Chern--Simons theory

Authors: Artem Belov, Andrey Morozov
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15268v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical work rigorously compares the algebraic structures of the Ising minimal model and the $SU(2)_2$ Chern-Simons theory. By analyzing tensor product decompositions and fusion rules, the authors resolve apparent mathematical contradictions, confirming that the two descriptions yield consistent physical observables for Ising anyons.

Why it may be interesting. The deep connection between topological quantum field theories (Chern-Simons) and exactly solvable models (CFTs) is fundamental to understanding topological quantum computation, which is a key area of theoretical physics.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper resolves apparent algebraic contradictions between the fusion/braiding rules of the Ising minimal model and the representation theory of the $SU(2)_2$ Chern-Simons theory.

Main result. The tensor product decomposition rules for the quantum algebra $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$ at roots of unity successfully reproduce the known fusion and braiding rules of the Ising anyons, confirming the equivalence at the level of observables.

Method. The authors compare results derived from two formalisms: Operator Product Expansions (OPEs) for the CFT side and the representation theory of the quantum group $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$ for the Chern-Simons side.

Model / system. The study concerns the mathematical equivalence between the Ising minimal model $\mathcal{M}(4,3)$ (a 2D CFT with $c=1/2$) and the $SU(2)_2$ Chern-Simons theory, which describes Ising anyons.

Key observables. Wilson loop expectation values $\langle W_K^R angle$, fusion matrices ($F$), and braiding matrices ($R$).

Important parameters / regimes. The deformation parameter $q$ is set to a root of unity, specifically $q = \exp(\pi i/4)$.

Assumptions / limitations. The comparison is restricted to low-degree tensor products, and the resolution of contradictions relies on the fact that representations with zero quantum dimension do not contribute to observable quantities.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the Ising fusion rules, the structure of the 52 knot in different closures, and the transition matrices ($F^{\otimes\otimes\otimes}$) governing tensor product decompositions.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by establishing the algebraic structures for both theories, calculating specific observables (like Wilson loops), and systematically comparing the tensor product decompositions ($\otimes^{\otimes\otimes\otimes}$) to show consistency.

Abstract

The present work is motivated by the statement that the Ising minimal model $\mathcal{M}(4,3)$ is equivalent, at the level of observables, to the $SU(2)_2$ Chern--Simons theory. At first glance, however, these two theories appear to differ substantially. For instance, the number of irreducible highest-weight representations does not match the number of Ising anyons. For tensor products of low degree, these discrepancies are examined in this work. While representation structure differs, it does not affect the observables underlying topological quantum computation algorithms.

Local Softmax and Global Weights in Non-Boolean Event Structures

Authors: Karl Svozil
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16248v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical paper analyzes probability assignments on complex, overlapping logical structures called non-Boolean event structures. It shows that when local normalization rules, such as Softmax, are forced to be globally consistent across shared contexts, the rule merely parameterizes the set of all mathematically admissible weights. The key finding is that the interesting physics—the existence of weights beyond classical or quantum bounds—is determined by the structure itself, not the specific normalizing function used.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding when local probabilistic assumptions (like those used in machine learning or cognitive models) are compatible with global physical constraints, separating the mathematical machinery (Softmax) from the underlying physical constraints (the polytope $W(L)$).

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper investigates the mathematical relationship between local normalization rules (like Softmax) applied in overlapping contexts and the requirement for a single, globally consistent probability weight in non-Boolean event structures.

Main result. Imposing global consistency (single-valuedness) forces the generalized softmax rule to merely parameterize the set of admissible weights, demonstrating that the softmax function itself does not introduce new physics beyond the structural constraints of the event structure.

Method. The authors use abstract algebraic and geometric methods to analyze the polytope of admissible weights, comparing local normalization schemes against global consistency requirements.

Model / system. The model is a non-Boolean event structure $L=(A, M)$, where $A$ is a set of atoms and $M$ is a family of overlapping contexts. The analysis focuses on characterizing the set of admissible probability weights $W(L)$ within this structure.

Key observables. Admissible weights $p \in W(L)$, the classical polytope $C(L)$, the quantum polytope $Q(L)$, and exotic weights that violate these bounds.

Important parameters / regimes. The link function $g$ used in the generalized softmax, and the structure of the context overlaps, particularly odd cycles (e.g., $n=5$ pentagon).

Assumptions / limitations. The core assumption is the requirement of single-valuedness or 'no-disturbance' across shared atoms, which restricts the general softmax framework.

Figures summary. The figures illustrate the geometry of probability assignments on odd cycles (like the pentagon), showing how the admissible half-weight violates both classical and quantum bounds.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first defining the problem in terms of local vs. global normalization, then establishing the equivalence between single-valued softmax and parametrization of $W(L)$, and finally using odd cycles to demonstrate the existence of exotic, non-classical weights.

Abstract

Softmax and related normalized response functions are widely used in choice theory, machine learning, and cognitive science. In non-Boolean event structures with overlapping contexts, however, local normalization does not automatically yield a global probability weight. We show that imposing single-valuedness on shared atoms -- equivalently, no-disturbance or consistent connectedness -- collapses generalized softmax rules to coordinate parametrizations of the strictly positive part of the admissible-weight polytope. Any strictly positive admissible weight can be represented in this way, while boundary weights arise as limits. Exotic weights that exceed classical or quantum bounds are therefore properties of the event structure and the chosen weight, not of the normalizing link. The resulting hierarchy separates local normalization, cross-context gluing, Cauchy--Gleason linearity, and physical or cognitive realizability.

Multifractal and Ergodic Properties of Conductance Fluctuations under Strong Disorder

Authors: Marcos A. A. de Sousa, Heitor R. Publio, Henrique A. de Lima, Adauto J. F. de Souza, Fernando A. Oliveira, Anderson L. R. Barbosa
Type: theory · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15447v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work analyzes how conductance fluctuations behave in disordered quantum conductors by examining their multifractal and ergodic properties. It finds a clear transition from non-ergodic to ergodic dynamics as disorder increases. Crucially, it distinguishes the physical origin of multifractality, showing it stems from temporal correlations in the weak disorder limit and from non-Gaussian probability distributions in the strong disorder limit.

Why it may be interesting. The findings provide a detailed understanding of how fundamental concepts like ergodicity and multifractality manifest in quantum transport, offering insights into the role of rare events and non-Gaussian statistics in disordered quantum systems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study investigates the multifractal and ergodic properties of conductance fluctuations in disordered mesoscopic systems.

Main result. The system undergoes a transition from non-ergodic to ergodic behavior as disorder increases, with multifractality arising from temporal correlations in the non-ergodic regime and non-Gaussian PDF effects in the ergodic regime.

Method. The analysis employs Multifractal Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (MF-DFA) on a fictitious time series of conductance, comparing results with shuffled time series to distinguish correlation sources.

Model / system. The model is a two-dimensional (2D) tight-binding lattice subjected to Anderson disorder via on-site potentials. Transport is analyzed through the conductance, calculated using the Landauer-Büttiker formalism.

Key observables. Conductance correlation function, generalized Hurst exponent h(q), singularity spectrum f(alpha), fictitious correlation time $\epsilon$, and non-Gaussian indicator $\eta$.

Important parameters / regimes. Disorder strength ($U$), which controls the transition between non-ergodic and ergodic regimes.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis treats conductance as a function of a control parameter (like Fermi energy) to generate a time series for fluctuation analysis.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the transition from non-ergodic to ergodic behavior via correlation function decay, and show how multifractal scaling parameters ($\Delta\alpha$, $h(q)$) change with disorder strength $U$ for both original and shuffled time series.

Paper structure. The paper systematically analyzes the conductance fluctuations by first establishing the physical model and observables, then applying multifractal analysis to characterize the scaling properties in different disorder regimes, culminating in a discussion of the physical origin of the observed multifractality.

Abstract

Understanding the stochastic properties of conductance fluctuations in disordered mesoscopic systems is fundamental to quantum transport. In this work, we investigate the multifractal and ergodic properties of the fictitious time series of conductance in two-dimensional tight-binding models under varying Anderson disorder. Using standard multifractal analysis, we show that conductance fluctuations exhibit a transition from non-ergodic to ergodic behavior as the disorder strength increases, as evidenced by the decay of the conductance correlation function. Remarkably, multifractality persists in both regimes; however, it becomes insensitive to shuffling in the strong-disorder (ergodic) regime, suggesting that distributional effects dominate temporal organization. On the contrary, in the weakly disordered (non-ergodic) regime, long-range correlations play a significant role. These findings are robust against changes in lead geometry (asymmetric vs. symmetric). Our results provide new insights into the interplay between ergodicity, multifractality, and rare events in disordered quantum transport.

Observation of flat-bottom U-shaped energy gap in high-Tc nickelate (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 thin films

Authors: Zhen Liang, Tianheng Wei, Wei Ren, Haoran Ji, Zheyuan Xie, Yanzhao Liu, Ziqiang Wang, Jian Wang
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15703v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work reports the first observation of a distinct, flat-bottom U-shaped energy gap in high-Tc nickelate thin films using STM/S. By analyzing the gap's temperature and magnetic field dependence, the authors argue that the material exhibits unconventional, strong-coupling superconductivity with a large gap, suggesting potential for high critical temperatures at ambient pressure.

Why it may be interesting. The observation of a large, unconventional superconducting gap ($\Delta$) and the strong-coupling ratio ($\gg$ BCS limit) in a nickelate system provides crucial experimental data for theories attempting to explain high-Tc superconductivity beyond the cuprate paradigm.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The study aims to understand the underlying mechanism of high-Tc superconductivity in nickelate superconductors and characterize the nature of the superconducting energy gap.

Main result. The researchers reported the first observation of an energy-symmetric, flat-bottom U-shaped gap in the nickelate film, whose temperature and magnetic field dependence is consistent with a superconducting gap function.

Method. The study employed ultra-low temperature Scanning Tunneling Microscopy/Spectroscopy (STM/S) and electrical transport measurements on the thin films.

Model / system. The physical system is (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 thin films grown on a SrLaAlO4 substrate. The analysis probes the superconducting gap structure, which is compared against theoretical expectations for strong-coupling superconductivity.

Key observables. Energy-symmetric, flat-bottom U-shaped energy gap; zero residual density of states at the Fermi level; gap reduction under a c-axis magnetic field; U-shaped to V-shaped gap evolution with temperature.

Important parameters / regimes. Onset $T_c$ above 40 K; Gap size $\Delta \sim 41.6 ext{ meV}$; Strong-coupling ratio $2\Delta/k_B T_c \approx 20$; Magnetic field $B = 14 ext{ T}$.

Assumptions / limitations. The observed gap structure is assumed to be superconducting, ruling out alternative origins like Coulomb blockade, and the analysis relies on careful deconvolution of thermal broadening effects.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the U-shaped gap structure, its suppression by a perpendicular magnetic field, and the temperature evolution from U-shaped to V-shaped spectra.

Paper structure. The paper progresses by first establishing the superconducting transport properties, then using STM/S to observe the characteristic U-shaped gap at low temperatures, followed by analyzing the gap's evolution with temperature and magnetic field, and finally comparing the results to theoretical models of strong-coupling superconductivity.

Abstract

The discovery of high transition temperature (high-Tc) superconductivity in Ruddlesden-Popper (R-P) bilayer nickelates under high pressure has stimulated extensive work to understand the underlying mechanism and search for superconductors with higher Tc. The recent realization of superconductivity in R-P bilayer nickelate thin films with onset Tc above 40 K at ambient-pressure enables the use of a wide array of powerful experimental tools to investigate the unconventional high-Tc superconductivity in bilayer nickelates. Here, using ultra-low temperature scanning tunneling microscopy/spectroscopy (STM/S) and electrical transport study, we report the first successful observation of an energy-symmetric, flat-bottom U-shaped gap with zero residual density of states around the Fermi level in the high-Tc nickelate (La,Pr)3Ni2O7 thin film grown on SrLaAlO4 substrate. Before and after STM/S studies, transport measurements on the same sample reveal consistent superconducting behaviors showing zero resistance, with an onset Tc above 40 K and zero resistance Tc above 20 K. The tunneling spectra exhibit highly unconventional temperature evolution, characterized by a rapid filling of the U-shaped energy gap to a V-shaped gap as the temperature increases. Furthermore, the U-shaped energy gap is reduced under a c-axis magnetic field of 14 T. The energy-symmetric U-shaped gap, taken together with its dependence on magnetic field and temperature, is consistent with the behavior of a superconducting gap, suggesting a nodeless gap function at ultra-low temperatures. Our findings shed new lights on the nature of high-Tc superconductivity and provide an encouraging and thought-provoking hint for a local superconductivity with Tc above liquid nitrogen boiling temperature in nickelate superconductors at ambient or zero pressure.

Orthogonal Polynomials and the MacWilliams Transform for Permutation-Invariant Qudit Codes

Authors: Ian Teixeira
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15372v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This theoretical paper derives the intrinsic MacWilliams transform for a specific class of quantum error-correcting codes (permutation-invariant qudit codes). It achieves this by mapping the problem onto the spectral theory of operators acting on symmetric tensor representations of SU(q). The main result is an explicit formula for the transform matrix, showing it is a finite Racah transform, which is crucial for establishing rigorous bounds on the code's performance.

Why it may be interesting. The deep connection between quantum error-correcting codes (qudit codes) and advanced mathematical structures like Racah polynomials and orthogonal polynomials provides powerful, explicit tools for bounding code performance.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper aims to derive an explicit formula for the intrinsic MacWilliams transform associated with permutation-invariant qudit codes.

Main result. The intrinsic MacWilliams matrix is shown to be a finite Racah transform, providing an explicit, closed-form matrix for computing linear programming bounds on these codes.

Method. The derivation heavily utilizes representation theory, relating the transform to the joint eigenvalues of twirling operators acting on symmetric power representations of SU(q).

Model / system. The system studied is permutation-invariant qudit codes, whose underlying structure is analyzed using the symmetric power representation $V_n = ext{Sym}^n(\mathbb{C}^q)$ of the group SU(q).

Key observables. The intrinsic MacWilliams matrix $M = (M_{ba})$, the spectrum of the degree-one twirl operator $T_1$, and the associated Racah orthogonal polynomials.

Important parameters / regimes. The qudit dimension $q$, the block length $n$, and the indices $a, b$ corresponding to irreducible sectors.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis relies on the multiplicity-free decomposition of the operator space $\mathcal{L}(V_n)$ and the properties derived from Casimir operators in the su(q) algebra.

Paper structure. The paper proceeds by establishing the spectral decomposition of the twirling operators, proving that the resulting polynomial representatives form an orthogonal system, identifying this system with Racah polynomials, and finally deriving the explicit matrix elements and proving structural identities like orthogonality and involutivity.

Abstract

We derive an explicit formula for the intrinsic MacWilliams transform for permutation-invariant qudit codes. Such codes naturally live in symmetric power representations, where the relevant error sectors are determined by the irreducible decomposition of the conjugation action on the associated operator space. Using the multiplicity-free structure of this decomposition and the corresponding intertwiner algebra, we identify the intrinsic MacWilliams matrix with a finite Racah transform. The entries are given by a terminating hypergeometric series, and the rows of the matrix are Racah orthogonal polynomials with parameters determined explicitly by the block length and local dimension. Computing the spectrum of the degree-one twirl reveals that this spectrum lies on an affine quadratic lattice. Then we derive a tridiagonal multiplication rule from the representation theory of the adjoint sector. As consequences, we obtain closed-form orthogonality, detailed-balance, and involutivity identities for the transform. The resulting formula supplies an explicit MacWilliams matrix for computing linear programming bounds on permutation-invariant qudit codes.

Performance Gains in Quantum SAT Solvers Using ESOP Encoding

Authors: Majd Assaad, Abhoy Kole, Rolf Drechsler
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16202v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This work proposes using an Exclusive-Sum-of-Products (e-CNF) encoding to represent SAT problems for quantum computation. By replacing standard CNF, the e-CNF approach significantly reduces the required quantum resources, such as qubits and non-Clifford gates. This establishes e-CNF as a quantum-aware encoding that improves the feasibility of oracle-based quantum SAT solving.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on SAT, the methodology of optimizing quantum circuit resource overhead by choosing a mathematically superior encoding structure (e-CNF) is highly relevant to designing practical quantum algorithms for complex computational problems.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The paper addresses the challenge of efficiently encoding the NP-complete Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) problem for quantum acceleration using Grover's algorithm.

Main result. The proposed Exclusive-Sum-of-Products (e-CNF) encoding significantly reduces quantum resource requirements (qubit count, T-gate complexity) compared to standard Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) encodings.

Method. The authors derive theoretical bounds and implement a systematic transformation from Boolean formulas to e-CNF, which is then mapped onto reversible quantum circuits for the oracle construction.

Model / system. The model is quantum computation, specifically utilizing Grover's search algorithm. The quantum operations are analyzed using the Clifford+T gate set, focusing on the resource cost of constructing the phase oracle.

Key observables. Quantum resource efficiency, including qubit count (#q), T-gate complexity (#T), CNOT count (#CX), and circuit depth (#D).

Important parameters / regimes. The complexity scales with the number of clauses ($m$) and the structure of the logical equivalence constraints being encoded.

Assumptions / limitations. The performance of the quantum solver is dominated by the cost of constructing the quantum oracle, and the analysis relies on decomposing multi-controlled gates into the Clifford+T gate set.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the schematic overview of Grover's algorithm and provide quantum circuit representations comparing the oracle construction for CNF versus e-CNF for sample equivalences.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the SAT problem and Grover's algorithm, contrasts the resource costs of CNF vs. e-CNF encodings, derives the e-CNF transformation, and concludes with experimental benchmarks demonstrating resource savings.

Abstract

The Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) problem is a canonical NP-complete problem and a natural candidate for quantum acceleration via search-based algorithms. In Grover-based quantum SAT solvers, the dominant computational cost stems from the construction of a reversible oracle that evaluates the Boolean formula, rendering the choice of SAT encoding crucial for overall quantum resource efficiency. Although SAT instances are conventionally expressed in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF), such encodings typically translate into quantum circuits with significant qubit overhead and high non-Clifford gate complexity. In this work, we investigate an Exclusive-Sum-of-Products (ESOP)-based CNF (e-CNF) representation tailored for quantum SAT solving and analyze its impact on oracle construction. We derive tighter upper bounds on qubit requirements and Clifford+$T$ gate counts for Grover-based SAT solvers when e-CNF encodings are employed in place of standard CNF. In addition, we propose a scalable transformation from Boolean formulas to e-CNF and present a systematic procedure for interpreting e-CNF representations as reversible quantum circuits suitable for oracle implementation. Experimental evaluation on representative SAT benchmarks demonstrates that the proposed e-CNF-based approach yields substantial and consistent reductions in quantum resources, including qubit count, T-gate complexity, and circuit depth, when compared to CNF-based oracle constructions. These results establish e-CNF as an effective quantum-aware SAT encoding that significantly improves the practicality of oracle-based quantum SAT solving.

Quantum Circuit Synthesis Using an Exact T Library

Summary. This paper presents a novel, quantum-aware approach to synthesizing quantum circuits by accurately calculating the minimum required T gate count. It overcomes the limitations of previous methods that used simple proxies for cost by formulating the problem exactly using phase polynomials. The resulting customized mapper significantly reduces the T gate count for critical quantum applications.

Why it may be interesting. While focused on digital quantum computation, the rigorous mathematical framework for minimizing non-Clifford resources (T gates) by analyzing underlying algebraic structures (phase polynomials) is highly relevant to understanding resource constraints in any complex quantum system.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The primary challenge is minimizing the T gate count during fault-tolerant quantum circuit synthesis, as T gates are the dominant resource bottleneck in quantum error correction.

Main result. The proposed method achieves significant T count reductions (up to 14.3% on EPFL benchmarks and 40% for cryptographic modules) by using an exact T cost model instead of inaccurate proxies like AND count.

Method. The authors formulate an exact T synthesis problem, canonicalize Boolean functions under Clifford equivalence, and develop a customized mapper that integrates this quantum-aware cost model into the standard XAG synthesis flow.

Model / system. The work models quantum circuits implementing Boolean functions, specifically focusing on bilinear functions, and utilizes the phase polynomial representation to capture the exact non-Clifford (T gate) cost.

Key observables. T count (the primary cost metric), AND count/Multiplicative Complexity (the inaccurate proxy), and the phase polynomial vector.

Important parameters / regimes. The maximum number of variables supported for precomputation is seven; the complexity is related to the rank of the multiplication tensor.

Assumptions / limitations. Existing methods incorrectly assume that the AND count is a reliable proxy for the true T gate cost, which the paper refutes.

Figures summary. Figures compare T-count reductions across arithmetic benchmarks (e.g., GF(2^n) multipliers) using the new mapper versus prior state-of-the-art methods, and detail the structure of the synthesis flow and cost analysis.

Paper structure. The paper progresses from identifying the T-gate bottleneck, detailing the mathematical formulation of the exact T synthesis problem via phase polynomials, proposing the customized mapper that respects bilinear structure, and finally demonstrating the end-to-end synthesis flow and performance gains.

Abstract

In fault-tolerant quantum circuit synthesis, T gates supplied via magic states dominate space-time cost, while Clifford gates incur negligible overhead. Conventional flows minimize AND count in an {XOR, AND, NOT} basis as a proxy for T, which neglects phase cancellation and can be far from T-optimal. We instead formulate an exact T synthesis problem and canonicalize Boolean functions under Clifford equivalence. By precomputing T-optimal implementations up to seven variables and developing a customized mapper, we reduce the T count by up to 14.3% on EPFL benchmarks and improve the T counts of several cryptographic modules by up to 40%.

Quantum Feature Amplification Network (QFAN) as An Autoregressive Quantum Generative Model

Authors: Jamal Slim, Saverio Monaco, Florian Rehm, Dirk Kruecker, Kerstin Borras
Type: both · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.16044v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper introduces the Quantum Feature Amplification Network (QFAN), an autoregressive quantum generative model designed to simulate large-scale images like calorimeter showers. By generating the image sequentially in fixed-size blocks, QFAN bypasses the prohibitive requirement that quantum register size must grow with the image size. The authors demonstrate this feasibility by reproducing key physical observables on both simulators and real quantum hardware.

Why it may be interesting. The core mechanism—decoupling resource scaling from output dimension via autoregression and fixed-size quantum circuits—is a major architectural advance for applying quantum computation to high-dimensional physical simulations.

Detailed structure

Main problem. To develop quantum generative models capable of simulating large-scale physical images, such as calorimeter showers, without requiring the quantum register size to scale linearly with the image dimension.

Main result. The Quantum Feature Amplification Network (QFAN) successfully establishes a hardware-compatible proof of principle by generating images block-by-block, keeping the required qubit count fixed regardless of the total image size.

Method. The model uses an autoregressive factorization, where each block is generated by reusing a small parameterized quantum circuit conditioned on a fixed-length summary (count-sketch) of previously generated pixels.

Model / system. The system simulates electromagnetic calorimeter showers, treating the energy deposition map as a high-dimensional image ($\mathbf{y}$). The model architecture is QFAN, which decomposes the image into sequential blocks ($B$) and uses a shared quantum circuit ($U(\mathbf{a}_eta, heta)$) for generation.

Key observables. Per-pixel intensity distributions, inter-pixel correlation matrices, and the total energy distribution of the simulated showers.

Important parameters / regimes. Qubit count ($n_q=3$), block size ($b$), total number of blocks ($B$), and the feature-to-target ratio ($ ho$).

Assumptions / limitations. The training uses teacher-forcing (ground-truth prefixes), and the analysis cannot causally separate optimization-budget limitations from device noise effects.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the comparison between direct-register scaling ($n_q=d$) and QFAN's fixed-qubit approach; subsequent figures show the successful reproduction of marginal distributions, correlation structures, and total energy conservation across simulated and hardware backends.

Paper structure. The paper introduces QFAN to solve the scaling bottleneck, details the autoregressive block generation process involving count-sketch conditioning and feature extraction, presents theoretical bounds on noise propagation, and validates the approach by reproducing key physical observables on both simulators and IBM quantum hardware.

Abstract

Direct-register quantum generative models for calorimeter shower simulation tie the quantum output dimension to the image dimension, so the required register size grows with the full image. Recent quantum-assisted methods reduce this pressure only by moving part of the generative task into hybrid latent-variable models. Consequently, current quantum demonstrations remain far below detector-scale geometries used in high-energy physics. We introduce the Quantum Feature Amplification Network (QFAN), which removes this register-size bottleneck by generating an image as a sequence of blocks. Each block is produced by the same small parameterized quantum circuit, conditioned on a compressed summary of the pixels already generated. Reusing the circuit fixes the qubit requirement by block size rather than full image size, while the per-step quantum processing cost is independent of image size for the Pauli-observable family used here. We derive a conservative worst-case bound on shot-noise propagation through the generation chain and give an empirical decoder-capacity heuristic for the reachable sequential depth. A three-qubit circuit with twelve shared variational parameters, closed-form ridge decoders, and a post-hoc residual sampler reproduces per-pixel intensity distributions, inter-pixel correlations, and total energy distributions of calorimeter showers on both simulator and IBM quantum hardware. At this scale, the hardware-simulator gap is consistent with optimization-budget limits dominating over device noise, although the experiments do not causally separate these effects. The results establish a hardware-compatible proof of principle and motivate, but do not validate, larger-scale extrapolations within this circuit family.

Quantum Feature Pyramid Gating for Seismic Image Segmentation

Authors: Taha Gharaibeh, Jyotsna Sharma
Type: both · Category: disordered systems and neural networks · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.15370v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Summary. This paper proposes a Quantum Feature Pyramid Gating (Q-FPN) gate to enhance seismic image segmentation by improving feature fusion at the FPN merge points. It embeds a small, parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) into a standard deep learning backbone. The results show that this quantum gating mechanism provides a significant, measurable performance boost over purely classical fusion techniques, suggesting a viable application for quantum machine learning in geophysical imaging.

Why it may be interesting. The work applies quantum circuit principles (PQC) to a deep learning task (image segmentation), demonstrating how quantum computation can be used as a trainable, non-linear feature transformation layer within a classical neural network architecture.

Detailed structure

Main problem. The core problem is achieving accurate, dense, pixel-level segmentation of salt bodies in seismic images, which is complicated by wave propagation distortions.

Main result. The proposed Quantum Feature Pyramid Gating (Q-FPN) gate significantly improves segmentation performance, showing a substantial gain (e.g., 9.85 percentage points) over classical fusion methods at the FPN merge point.

Method. The method introduces a hybrid architecture embedding a Parameterized Quantum Circuit (PQC) at feature-fusion points to compute a learned, content-dependent convex combination of features.

Model / system. The system uses a standard encoder-decoder segmentation pipeline (like U-Net/FPN) augmented by a 4-qubit, 2-layer PQC. The PQC processes compressed feature statistics to generate a gating weight, making the gate encoder-agnostic.

Key observables. Mean Intersection over Union (IoU) score, TGS-mAP, and the magnitude of performance improvement ($\Delta$) when comparing quantum vs. classical gating.

Important parameters / regimes. The quantum circuit has a fixed budget of 72 trainable parameters, operating on 4 qubits.

Assumptions / limitations. The primary assumption is that the quantum function space accessed by the PQC provides a superior mechanism for feature correlation and fusion compared to simple classical addition or attention mechanisms.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the PQC structure (4-qubit, 2-layer) and compare the performance gains (e.g., $\Delta$ of 9.85 pp) achieved by the Q-FPN gate versus classical baselines across different encoders and resolutions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the Q-FPN gate, details the PQC implementation (encoding, variational layers), evaluates its performance on the TGS challenge by comparing it against classical element-wise addition and skip-connection attention, and concludes by analyzing the source of the performance gain.

Abstract

Accurate salt-body delineation is essential for seismic interpretation because salt structures distort wave propagation, complicate velocity-model building, obscure reservoir geometry, and increase uncertainty in exploration and drilling decisions. Although hybrid quantum-classical models have shown competitive performance on small-scale image-classification tasks, their value for dense, pixel-level geophysical prediction remains largely untested. This work introduces quantum feature gating, a hybrid segmentation architecture that embeds a parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) at feature-fusion points within an encoder-decoder pipeline. A 4-qubit, 2-layer PQC with data re-uploading computes a learned convex combination of lateral and top-down features at each Feature Pyramid Network merge point. A global-average-pooling layer maps encoder features to a fixed 4-dimensional quantum input, decoupling the 72-parameter quantum budget from backbone size and image resolution. The method is evaluated on the 2018 TGS Salt Identification Challenge using 4,000 seismic images at 101 x 101 resolution, across two integration topologies, eight circuit variants, and six encoders with 8M to 118M parameters under five-fold cross-validation. In a controlled EfficientNetV2-L ablation at 256 x 256 resolution, replacing the three Quantum FPN Gates with element-wise addition while holding the encoder, loss schedule, splits, and threshold search fixed reduces mean IoU from 0.9389 to 0.8404, a 9.85 percentage-point gap. Inserting the same circuit as skip-connection attention in a custom U-Net improves IoU by 0.88 points over the SolidUNet baseline, showing that the PQC contribution depends on where and what it gates. These results provide controlled evidence that quantum feature fusion can improve dense seismic segmentation.

Magnetic topological insulators offer a platform to control electronic topology through magnetic order, yet reliable routes to tune their properties remain limited. Here, we show that ion irradiation allows to modify the magnetic and the topological properties of the van der Waals magnetic topological insulator MnBi$_2$Te$_4$. Using inert ion beams, intrinsic defects are introduced via collision cascades without chemical doping. We identify two distinct regimes. At low fluence, cation antisite disorder leads to a near-complete redistribution of Bi over cation sites while preserving long-range crystallographic order, accompanied by a transition from $p$-type to $n$-type transport. At high fluence, cation-anion intermixing drives the formation of a previously unreported layer-disordered phase characterized by a high density of van der Waals-specific planar defects, including swapped bilayers. Despite significant structural disorder, the system retains partial periodic order up to high displacement levels. Magnetometry and X-ray spectroscopy show that the Mn high-spin state and antiferromagnetic interactions persist, while magnetic anisotropy is strongly reduced. At the same time, the anomalous Hall response is suppressed by over an order of magnitude, far exceeding the change in magnetization, indicating a direct modification of Berry curvature. These results establish ion irradiation as a means to tune topology through defect engineering and reveal a disorder-driven approach to control symmetry and electronic structure in van der Waals magnetic materials.

Abstract

Crossed Andreev reflection (CAR) is a nonlocal quantum transport phenomenon that arises at the interface between a superconductor and two spatially separated metals. In this process, an electron incident from one metal combines with another electron originating from the other metal to form a Cooper pair in the superconductor. As a consequence, a hole is emitted into the second metal, establishing a nonlocal electron-hole conversion process. In contrast to local Andreev reflection -- where electron-to-hole conversion occurs within the same region -- CAR intrinsically links two spatially separated carriers, giving rise to nonlocal correlations and quantum entanglement. In bipolar magnetic semiconductors (BMSs), the conduction and valence bands possess opposite spin polarizations. We propose to achieve tunable control of CAR by independently adjusting the chemical potentials of the two regions. By engineering the alignment of spin-polarized bands in the two BMS leads, CAR can be selectively enhanced or suppressed. This tunability enables precise manipulation of nonlocal transport, and correlated electron dynamics, offering promising prospects for spintronic and superconducting device applications.
