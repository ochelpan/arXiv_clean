Anisotropy of spin waves in the field-polarized phase of Fe-doped MnSi

Authors: I. N. Khoroshiy, A. Podlesnyak, D. Menzel, M. C. Rahn, D. S. Inosov, A. S. Sukhanov, S. E. Nikitin
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.21985v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to investigate the unexpected and pronounced anisotropy of spin-wave stiffness in the field-polarized phase of the cubic chiral magnet Fe-doped MnSi, which contradicts standard theoretical models.

Main result. The researchers discovered that spin-wave stiffness is highly anisotropic, with values significantly higher parallel to the applied field (14.7 meV A^2) than perpendicular to it (7.6 meV A^2), suggesting a need to revise current theoretical understandings of cubic chiral magnets.

Method. High-resolution inelastic and elastic neutron scattering measurements were performed using the Cold-neutron Chopper Spectrometer (CNCS) at the Spallation Neutron Source.

Summary. This paper reports the discovery of significant spin-wave stiffness anisotropy in the field-polarized phase of Fe-doped MnSi. While standard models for this cubic material predict isotropic spin-wave dispersion, experimental neutron scattering reveals that stiffness is nearly twice as large parallel to the magnetic field as it is perpendicular to it. The authors suggest that this unexpected behavior might be driven by the magnetic field inducing a symmetry reduction in the electronic Fermi surface. This finding challenges existing theoretical frameworks for chiral magnets and necessitates a re-evaluation of how magnetic interactions are modeled in these systems.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system is Fe-doped MnSi (Mn0.9Fe0.1Si), a B20-type itinerant chiral magnetic material, studied in its field-polarized ferromagnetic state.

Key observables. Spin-wave dispersion, spin-wave stiffness (A), magnetic propagation vector (km), energy gap (E), and magnetic reflection intensity.

Important parameters / regimes. Magnetic field (B), temperature (T ~ 1.8-2 K), Fe concentration (x), and critical field (Bc).

Assumptions / limitations. The authors rule out single-ion anisotropy, anisotropic exchange, and dipole-dipole interactions as the primary causes, instead speculating on symmetry reduction of the Fermi surface.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows magnetic phase diagrams; Fig 2 displays magnetic reflection intensity and neutron scattering slices under different cooling protocols; Fig 3 shows the magnetic field dependence of the propagation vector and reflection intensity; Fig 6 compares extracted spin-wave stiffness values for different directions.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of skyrmion-stabilizing interactions, describes the experimental setup and sample preparation, presents the neutron scattering results regarding spin-wave dispersion and anisotropy, evaluates potential physical mechanisms for the observed anisotropy, and concludes with implications for theory.

Abstract

Chiral magnetic textures, such as skyrmions, are of great interest to the condensed matter community due to their novel transport properties. The stabilization of topologically non-trivial magnetic phases, like the skyrmion lattice in MnSi, is governed by underlying magnetic interactions which can be probed via measurements of spin-wave excitations. Here, we report high-resolution inelastic neutron scattering (INS) measurements of the spin waves in Fe-doped Mn$_{0.9}$Fe$_{0.1}$Si deep within its field-polarized ferromagnetic state. We observe non-reciprocal spin waves with a parabolic dispersion that shifts linearly with magnetic field. Crucially, the spin-wave stiffness is highly anisotropic, with values of 14.7 meV $\rm{\mathring{A}}@@MATH0MATH@@^2$ perpendicular to it. This pronounced anisotropy in a cubic material is inconsistent with standard theoretical models for MnSi and indicates a necessity to revise our theoretical understanding.

Beyond Variational Bias: Resolving Intertwined Orders in the Hubbard Model

Authors: Luciano Loris Viteritti, Riccardo Rende, Christopher Roth, Anirvan Sengupta, Giuseppe Carleo, Antoine Georges
Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.21978v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating how architectural bias in different variational ansätze leads to conflicting physical conclusions regarding competing orders in the Hubbard model.

Main result. By using symmetry restoration and variance reduction, three different neural-network ansätze (determinant, particle-hole, and Pfaffian) converge to a consistent physical picture of coexisting superconducting and stripe orders.

Method. Variational Monte Carlo using Transformer-based backflow fermionic wave functions, combined with symmetry projection (translational and rotational) and zero-variance extrapolation.

Summary. This paper addresses a critical issue in computational many-body physics: the tendency of different variational wave function architectures to favor different physical phases. By studying the 2D Hubbard model, the authors show that while different neural-network ansätze initially predict conflicting stripe or superconducting orders, applying symmetry restoration forces them to converge to a single, shared physical state. This demonstrates that energy minimization alone is insufficient to identify the ground state in systems with competing orders and highlights the necessity of tracking correlation functions during optimization.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The two-dimensional t-t' Hubbard model on an 8x8 square lattice with periodic boundary conditions, specifically focusing on the regime with U=8, t'=-0.2, and 1/8 hole doping.

Key observables. d-wave superconducting order parameter (Delta_SC), stripe spin order parameter (M^2_str), spin structure factor S(k), and energy variance.

Important parameters / regimes. U = 8.0, t' = -0.2, hole doping delta = 1/8, 8x8 lattice size.

Assumptions / limitations. The study assumes that the true ground state can be recovered through systematic variance reduction and symmetry restoration, effectively overcoming the initial mean-field bias of the chosen ansatz.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the optimization landscape and convergence of different schemes; Figure 2 plots order parameters against energy variance; Figure 4 compares real-space charge/spin patterns; Figure 7 shows energy vs. variance for extrapolation; Figure 8 and 9 show the impact of symmetry restoration on pairing and spin structure factors.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of variational bias, describes the Transformer-based backflow architecture and the three antisymmetrization schemes, presents results showing how unprojected states diverge, demonstrates convergence through symmetry restoration, and concludes with the physical implications for intertwined orders.

Abstract

The two-dimensional Hubbard model at finite doping hosts competing or intertwined orders, resulting in conflicting conclusions from different computational approaches regarding its ground state. We show that a key source of such discrepancies is the bias encoded in the variational ansatz. We consider three different Transformer backflow fermionic wave functions based on a Slater determinant, its particle-hole counterpart, and a Pfaffian, initialized without any mean-field pretraining. We show that, despite achieving nearly degenerate, state-of-the-art variational energies, each ansatz converges to a state with qualitatively different spin, charge, and pairing correlations. Upon improving accuracy via symmetry restoration and variance reduction, however, all three converge to the same physical picture: coexisting superconducting and stripe orders. These results demonstrate that variational energy alone is insufficient to identify the ground state in the presence of competing phases, and highlight the importance of tracking how correlation functions evolve as the wave function is systematically improved before drawing physical conclusions.

Boundary-Aware Stabilizer Scheduling for Distributed Quantum Error Correction

Authors: Sanidhya Gupta, Sanidhay Bhambay, Narges Alavisamani, Neil Walton, Thirupathaiah Vasantam
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22471v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Optimizing the scheduling of stabilizer measurements in distributed quantum error correction to balance the trade-off between remote-operation noise (due to waiting for entanglement) and syndrome staleness.

Main result. The proposed Skip-Seam-tau (SS-tau) and Adaptive Skip-tau (AST) policies reduce the logical error rate compared to the standard Measure-All baseline, particularly in low-to-moderate entanglement generation rate regimes.

Method. The authors develop a scheduling module for syndrome extraction and evaluate it using Stim-based circuit-level noise simulations with a concatenated MWPM decoder.

Summary. This paper addresses the performance bottleneck in distributed quantum computing caused by the slow and noisy nature of remote entanglement-based gates. By introducing a scheduling strategy that skips certain boundary (seam) measurements, the authors reduce the accumulation of idling noise on data qubits. They demonstrate that an adaptive approach, which adjusts measurement frequency based on the entanglement generation rate, can significantly improve the logical error rate and approach the performance of monolithic architectures.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A modular quantum architecture consisting of multiple QPUs connected via photonic interconnects, utilizing triangular color codes partitioned across four modules.

Key observables. Logical Error Rate (LER)

Important parameters / regimes. Entanglement Generation Rate (EGR), code distance (d), physical error rate (p), and the seam-refresh interval (tau).

Assumptions / limitations. The decoder and underlying code structure remain unchanged; the study focuses on measurement frequency rather than the classical computational overhead of the scheduler.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the triangular color code structure, the remote CNOT circuit, the performance gap between monolithic and distributed architectures, and plots of LER vs. physical error rate and EGR for various scheduling policies.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of distributed QEC overhead, describes the modular architecture and noise model, presents the proposed SS-tau and AST scheduling policies, evaluates them via Stim simulations, and analyzes performance across different EGR regimes and code distances.

Abstract

Future quantum architectures are expected to be modular, with quantum processors connecting multiple quantum processing units (QPUs) via photonic interconnects. In topological quantum error correction, such as color codes, this creates seam boundaries where parity checks require remote CNOT operations using heralded Bell pairs. These non-local checks are slower and noisier than bulk local checks because entanglement generation is probabilistic, causing data qubits to accumulate idle noise while waiting for remote operations. A natural way to reduce this overhead is to skip some seam measurements; however, doing so makes seam syndrome information stale and can degrade decoding. The central scheduling problem is therefore to determine how frequently seam checks should be measured so as to balance remote-operation and waiting noise against syndrome staleness. To address this trade-off, we develop a scheduling module that integrates directly into standard syndrome-extraction circuits. We consider two policies: Skip-Seam-$τ$ (SS-$τ$), which measures all bulk checks every round while measuring seam checks once every $τ$ rounds and copying the most recent syndrome in skipped rounds, and Adaptive Skip-$τ$ (AST), which selects $τ$ as a function of code distance and entanglement generation rate (EGR). We evaluate these policies on triangular color codes under circuit-level noise in Stim, including idling errors induced by Bell-pair generation delays. Our simulations show that SS-tau and AST reduce remote-operation overhead and can lower the logical error rate (LER) relative to the Measure-All (MA) baseline. For physical error rate $p = 10^{-3}$, we identify an EGR regime in which both SS-$τ$ and AST exhibit behavior consistent with fault-tolerant scaling, with LER decreasing as code distance increases. Across these regimes, SS-$τ$ and AST outperform MA.

Constant Factor Analysis of Optimal Quantum Linear Solvers in Practice

Authors: Pedro C. S. Costa, Alexander M. Dalzell, Dong An, Dominic W. Berry
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22185v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Evaluating the practical performance and constant factor overhead of optimal Quantum Linear System Algorithms (QLSAs), specifically comparing the 'Shortcut' method against Quantum Walk-based approaches.

Main result. The Shortcut method is significantly more efficient for non-Hermitian matrices when the solution norm is known, whereas the Quantum Walk method performs better in the unknown-norm regime.

Method. Numerical analysis and comparative benchmarking of the Shortcut method (using Kernel Reflection and Kernel Projection via QSVT) against Adiabatic and Randomized Quantum Walk methods using random linear systems.

Summary. This paper provides a practical performance analysis of state-of-the-art quantum algorithms for solving linear systems. It compares the recently developed 'Shortcut' method with established Quantum Walk-based solvers. The authors find that while the Shortcut method excels when the solution norm is known, the Quantum Walk method remains more robust and efficient when the norm is unknown. This work is crucial for understanding the real-world utility of these algorithms beyond their theoretical asymptotic complexity.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The study focuses on the Quantum Linear System Problem (QLSP) for square, sparse matrices $A$, including both Hermitian (Positive Definite) and non-Hermitian families.

Key observables. Computational cost (number of block-encoding calls), success probability, and solution error (Bures distance/infidelity).

Important parameters / regimes. Condition number ($\kappa$), target error ($\epsilon$), solution norm ($\|x\|$), and matrix dimension ($N$).

Assumptions / limitations. Assumes access to a block-encoding $U_A$ of the matrix $A$ and that the matrix is normalized such that $\|A\|=1$.

Figures summary. Circuit diagrams for the Kernel Reflection algorithm and plots showing the scaling of total cost and error as a function of condition number and error tolerance.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of constant factor analysis, presents the mathematical framework for the Shortcut and Quantum Walk methods, details the numerical simulation setup, and provides comparative results across different matrix regimes and norm-knowledge scenarios.

Abstract

Optimal quantum linear equation solvers provide complexity $O(κ\log(1/ε))$, where $κ$ is the condition number and $ε$ is the allowable error. The optimal solver using a discrete adiabatic approach [PRX Quantum \textbf{3}, 040303 (2022)] has large analytically proven constant factors for the upper bound on the complexity. The constant factors were later found to be about 1,200 times smaller in numerical testing [Quantum \textbf{9}, 1887 (2025)]. This meant it is about an order of magnitude more efficient than using a randomised approach from [PRX Quantum \textbf{6}, 040373 (2025)], which has far smaller analytically proven constant factors. Recently, a ``Shortcut'' method has been found to provide an optimal solver which also has small proven constant factors. In the present work, we conduct a comprehensive numerical analysis comparing this method with the adiabatic solver for two families of random linear systems. We find that, in the case where the solution norm is \emph{unknown}, the adiabatic solver provides slightly better performance. If the solution norm is \emph{known}, then the shortcut method provides significantly better performance for non-Hermitian matrices.

Corner Majorana states in semi-Dirac

Authors: M. García Olmos, Y. Baba, R. A. Molina, M. Amado
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22553v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The challenge of realizing robust, fault-tolerant Majorana bound states (MBSs) without the experimental difficulties of 1D nanowires or the need for complex engineered nanostructures and vortices.

Main result. The discovery that 2D semi-Dirac materials can host corner Majorana modes through proximity-induced superconductivity, where the system effectively acts as coupled Kitaev chains.

Method. A theoretical framework using the Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) formalism, including numerical diagonalization of a tight-binding model and projection onto edge-state subspaces.

Summary. This paper proposes a new theoretical platform for realizing Majorana bound states using 2D semi-Dirac materials. By applying Rashba spin-orbit coupling and a Zeeman field to these materials under s-wave superconductivity, the authors show that corner Majorana modes emerge at the edges of finite strips. The system can be effectively described as coupled Kitaev chains. Crucially, the study demonstrates that these corner states are remarkably robust against significant levels of Anderson disorder.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A 2D semi-Dirac system with anisotropic bulk dispersion (quadratic in kx, linear in ky) subject to Rashba spin-orbit coupling, a perpendicular Zeeman field, and s-wave superconducting proximity effect.

Key observables. Edge-state dispersion, Majorana polarization, Majorana zero modes at corners, Local Density of States (LDOS), and energy splitting between modes.

Important parameters / regimes. Mass term (M0), Rashba coupling (alpha), Zeeman field (BZ), chemical potential (mu), and Anderson disorder strength (w).

Assumptions / limitations. The large-width limit (W >> lambda^-1) allows treating opposite edges as decoupled; the superconducting pairing is momentum-independent; and orbital spinors of opposite edges are orthogonal.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows numerical edge-state spectra and spin/orbital components; Figure 2 compares full BdG results to a reduced decoupled model; Figure 3 displays energy spectra for semi-infinite and finite systems; Figure 6 illustrates the stability of the integrated LDOS under increasing disorder.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of Majorana realization, defines the semi-Dirac Hamiltonian and its edge-state properties, derives an effective 1D Kitaev chain model via projection, analyzes the emergence of corner modes in finite geometries, and evaluates the robustness of these modes against Anderson disorder.

Why it may be interesting. It proposes a new, naturally occurring 2D platform for Majorana modes that avoids the complexities of engineered nanostructures, which is highly relevant for developing topological qubits for fault-tolerant quantum computing.

Abstract

Proximity-induced superconductivity in low-dimensional systems offers a powerful pathway to engineer topological superconducting phases in, otherwise, non-superconducting systems. These exotic phases are of fundamental and technological interest due to the presence of robust zero-energy modes, the Majorana bound states. In this work, we propose a theoretical framework to realize Majorana bound states from the edge states of a two-dimensional semi-Dirac system. This anisotropic system, under specific conditions, can host non-chiral edge states that propagate only along particular edges, effectively forming separated one-dimensional channels. We show that the interplay between Rashba spin-orbit coupling and a Zeeman field on this setup provides the right conditions to get an effective p-wave pairing between the edge states by proximity with a s-wave superconductor. In finite geometries, each edge can independently undergo a topological phase transition into a one-dimensional topological superconductor and give rise to four zero-energy modes localized at the strip corners. At low energies, the edge states subspace admits a description in terms of coupled Kitaev chains, providing a clear picture of the origin, robustness, and tunability of the corner Majorana modes. Our results establish semi-Dirac materials as a natural platform for realizing Majorana modes in two dimensions without relying on engineered nanostructures, vortices, or crystalline higher-order topology.

Type: theory · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22343v1

Main problem. Bridging the gap between theoretical predictions and experimental observations in large-scale moire systems by accounting for how lattice dynamics and thermal fluctuations influence electronic localization and Wigner crystallization.

Main result. The inclusion of dynamical effects from lattice vibrations deepens moire potential wells and narrows the conduction band, leading to robust Wigner crystallization and the emergence of a kagome-patterned three-electron state.

Method. A machine-learning-based workflow integrating DeePMD for molecular dynamics and DeepH for machine-learned electronic Hamiltonians, combined with DFT-level accuracy (HONPAS) and DMRG simulations.

Summary. This paper presents a new machine-learning workflow to simulate large-scale moire supercells that are too large for traditional DFT. By incorporating lattice vibrations and structural relaxation, the authors show that the moire potential wells deepen, which facilitates the formation of robust Wigner crystals. They specifically identify a kagome-patterned electron state at certain doping levels. This approach provides a practical way to connect atomic-scale dynamics to large-scale emergent electronic phenomena in 2D materials.

Detailed structure

Abstract
