Tantalum Damascene Coplanar Waveguide Resonators Fabricated Using 300 mm Scale Processes

Main problem. Surface oxides on the sidewalls of superconducting tantalum devices contribute to dielectric losses, degrading the performance of transmon qubits.

Main result. The damascene fabrication process successfully replaces the metal/air interface with a metal/substrate interface, leading to a statistical improvement in quality factor of approximately 2x for pristine interfaces compared to buried oxide interfaces.

Method. The researchers used a 300 mm scale damascene process involving trench etching, Ta deposition, and chemical mechanical planarization (CMP), followed by cryogenic characterization and STEM/EDS analysis.

Summary. This paper investigates a new fabrication method called the damascene process to improve the quality of superconducting tantalum resonators. By embedding the metal within the substrate, the researchers replaced lossy metal/air sidewall interfaces with metal/substrate interfaces. The study demonstrates that avoiding native oxide formation at these interfaces can significantly increase the resonator's quality factor. This approach is highly scalable, utilizing 300 mm industrial-scale fabrication processes, which is crucial for the future of large-scale quantum computing hardware.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system consists of Tantalum (Ta) based superconducting coplanar waveguide (CPW) resonators fabricated on 300 mm Si (100) wafers, including both capacitive and inductive coupling configurations.

Key observables. Resonance frequencies, coupling quality factor (Qc), internal quality factor (Qi), kinetic inductance (Lki), and electric field distribution profiles.

Important parameters / regimes. Temperature range of 25 mK to 350 mK, power levels from -40 to -80 dBm, and 300 mm wafer scale.

Assumptions / limitations. The numerical simulations neglected kinetic inductance initially, requiring a post-hoc correction to match experimental resonance frequencies.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows the damascene fabrication process flow; Figure 2 displays SEM images of the resonator structures; Figure 3 illustrates simulated electric field distributions; Figure 12 shows Qi as a function of temperature; Figure 13 compares quality factors across different device types.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of surface oxides, describes the damascene fabrication process and experimental setup, presents numerical simulations and characterization results (STEM/EDS), discusses the performance of different device variants, and concludes with an analysis of loss mechanisms.

Abstract

Surface oxides contribute to losses in superconducting transmon devices resulting in degraded performance. We explore the use of the damascene process to replace the sidewall native oxide of a device with a metal/substrate interface. We simulate sidewall oxidation by burying an oxide layer during fabrication. We observe a modest improvement between the two types of devices, which is suggestive of a reduction in the surface participation ratio.

Spiral, target, stripe, and disordered waves in active six-state Potts models

Authors: Hiroshi Noguchi
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22353v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · dissipative systems 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to characterize various spatiotemporal wave modes (spiral, target, stripe, and disordered) and their phase transitions in a non-equilibrium six-state Potts model.

Main result. The researchers identified distinct wave modes (SP, TG, ST, and DS) and found that the transition between even- and odd-numbered state dominance is a first-order transition. They also distinguished between forward and backward three-state waves based on their boundary dynamics.

Method. Monte Carlo simulations using the Metropolis algorithm were performed on square and hexagonal lattices to observe pattern formation and coarsening dynamics.

Summary. This paper investigates the variety of wave patterns that emerge in an active six-state Potts model. By using Monte Carlo simulations, the author identifies spiral, target, stripe, and disordered waves and maps their existence across different interaction energies. The study reveals that transitions between certain wave modes are first-order and explores how these patterns evolve during coarsening. The findings provide a detailed classification of complex spatiotemporal dynamics in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An active six-state Potts model on 2D square and hexagonal lattices, which is a non-equilibrium system where local balance holds but global detailed balance is violated.

Key observables. Temporal and spatial correlation functions, oscillation amplitude and period, probability of phase coexistence, cluster size, and the wave ratio order parameter.

Important parameters / regimes. Flipping energy (h), non-flip contact energy (Jnf), contact energy for same states (Jkk), and diagonal state interactions.

Assumptions / limitations. The system is assumed to follow local balance for flips between neighboring states, and the dynamics are governed by a rock-paper-scissors-like mechanism.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows a phase diagram of modes; Figure 7 shows the dependence of various quantities on contact energy; Figure 12 demonstrates first-order transition behavior via the scaling of susceptibility.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the active Potts model, presents a phase diagram of wave modes, analyzes the quantitative transition between disordered and spiral waves, investigates the emergence of target and stripe waves, details the dynamics of three-state waves, and concludes with a discussion on lattice robustness and transition types.

Why it may be interesting. The study of non-equilibrium phase transitions and pattern formation in multi-state systems is highly relevant to understanding driven-dissipative dynamics in open quantum systems and active matter.

Abstract

Wave propagation can be observed in various nonequilibrium systems. In this study, we investigated the properties of several wave modes in active six-state Potts models using Monte Carlo simulations of square and hexagonal lattices. Disordered and spiral (SP) waves of six states are formed under weak and strong repulsions at nonflip contacts, respectively. The target (TG) and stripe (ST) waves were found to emerge under stronger repulsion. These three wave modes (SP, TG, and ST) can temporally coexist in small systems near the transition points but they do not switch in large systems or far from these transition points. During coarsening from randomly mixed states to ST waves, SP waves appear at an intermediate stage. The SP wave modes of three even- or odd-numbered states (states $s=0,2,4$ or $s=1,3,5$) emerge under two conditions: repulsion at the diagonal contact and attraction at nonflip contacts. Previously thought to be identical for both conditions, the wave types were found to differ, comprising forward and backward waves ($s=1\to 3\to 5\to 1$ or $s=1\to 5\to 3\to 1$), whose domain boundaries move by the two-step and four-step forward flips, respectively. The transition between the waves of the even- and odd-numbered states is first-order for both the forward and backward waves.

Long-Range Order in Coupled $D$-dimensional Kuramoto Oscillators

Authors: Zhongpu Qiu, Tianyi Wu, Linkai Zhang, Sheng Fang, Jun Meng, Jingfang Fan, Hugues Chaté
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22151v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · Keldysh / 2PI / non-Gaussian methods 2/5 · dissipative systems 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating whether quenched frequency disorder can stabilize long-range order (LRO) in low-dimensional (d=1, 2) vector Kuramoto oscillators, thereby circumventing the Hohenberg-Mermin-Wagner theorem.

Main result. Long-range order emerges in low-dimensional lattices only for odd-dimensional oscillators (D), where quenched noise acts as a stabilizing mechanism that maps the system to an effective ferromagnetic model.

Method. The authors use two-body dynamics analysis, large-scale numerical simulations, and a real-space renormalization group (RG) framework combined with Bogoliubov-Krylov averaging.

Summary. The paper explores the emergence of long-range order in $D$-dimensional Kuramoto oscillators on 1D and 2D lattices. It discovers a parity effect where order only appears when the oscillator dimension $D$ is odd. This happens because odd-$D$ systems possess a null space in their frequency matrices that allows for synchronization, effectively mapping the system to a ferromagnetic model. In contrast, even-$D$ systems remain disordered. The study highlights how quenched noise can serve as a stabilizing force against the fluctuations that usually prevent order in low dimensions.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A D-dimensional vector Kuramoto model (DDKM) on 1D and 2D lattices, featuring alignment coupling and quenched noise introduced via antisymmetric intrinsic frequency matrices.

Key observables. Orientational order parameter (rho) and frequency order parameter (r, measuring the fraction of entrained oscillators).

Important parameters / regimes. Oscillator dimension (D), lattice dimension (d), coupling strength (K), and the presence of quenched frequency disorder.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the use of antisymmetric matrices for frequency disorder and focuses on the thermodynamic limit via RG.

Figures summary. Figure 4 shows the decay of order parameters (rho and r) versus coupling strength (K) for even-D oscillators, demonstrating the absence of LRO.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the model and the problem of LRO in low dimensions, analyzes microscopic two-body dynamics, presents large-scale numerical simulations, performs a real-space renormalization group analysis, and concludes by comparing the results to standard continuous symmetry models.

Why it may be interesting. This work is highly relevant to many-body dynamics as it demonstrates a novel mechanism where quenched disorder, typically a source of decoherence or disorder, actually stabilizes long-range order in low-dimensional systems.

Abstract

We show that the long-range order (LRO) strikingly emerges in systems of locally coupled $D$-dimensional vector Kuramoto oscillators on low-dimensional lattices ($d=1,2$), but only for odd $D$. This parity-dependent effect is traced to two-oscillator dynamics, where odd-$D$ units synchronize for any coupling, while even-$D$ pairs require a finite threshold. This fundamental difference selectively seeds collective order in large-scale systems, a phenomenon demonstrated by our numerical simulations. A renormalization group analysis reveals a RG flow to a weak-coupling fixed point for $d \le 2$. In this limit, odd-$D$ systems effectively map to a ferromagnetic model, developing an ordered ``hemisphere" phase, whereas even-$D$ systems remain disordered. Our findings further reveal orientational LRO emerges in both $d=1$ and $d=2$, but frequency LRO requires $d=2$. We contrast these results with the established behavior of models possessing continuous symmetry, highlighting how quenched disorder provides a fundamentally new route to order.

Entanglement Enhanced Sensing with Qubits affected by non-Markovian Dephasing

Authors: Noah Kaufmann, Kasper Hede Nielsen, Anders Søndberg Sørensen
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22368v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 dissipative systems 3/5 · entanglement & information structure 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating whether entanglement, specifically via spin-squeezed states, can enhance the sensitivity of frequency estimation in Ramsey spectroscopy when qubits are subject to non-Markovian, spatially and temporally correlated dephasing noise.

Main result. The study finds that while entanglement provides no scaling advantage for noise with a non-zero zero-frequency component (like White or Gaussian noise), it enables scaling beyond the Standard Quantum Limit for noise with a vanishing zero-frequency component (like Linear or Ohmic noise), achieving uncertainties as low as N^{-3/4}.

Method. The authors use an analytical framework involving Fourier decomposition of the effective Hamiltonian, the Wiener-Khinchin theorem, and optimization of interrogation time and squeezing parameters to derive scaling laws for various noise spectra.

Summary. This paper explores how entanglement can improve the precision of frequency estimation in the presence of complex, non-Markovian noise. It demonstrates that the ability to surpass the Standard Quantum Limit depends critically on whether the noise spectrum has a non-zero component at zero frequency. For specific types of noise, such as Ohmic or Linear noise, the authors show that spin-squeezed states can achieve significantly better scaling of sensing uncertainty with the number of probes than separable states. This provides important theoretical insights for developing quantum sensors that are resilient to correlated environmental noise.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A collection of N two-level systems (qubits) undergoing Ramsey spectroscopy, subject to a classical, pure dephasing noise model that is stationary, translationally invariant, and non-Markovian.

Key observables. Estimation uncertainty (variance) of the frequency b, metrological gain (r), and dephasing rate gamma(tau).

Important parameters / regimes. Number of probes (N), total interrogation time (T), squeezing parameter (kappa), and noise power spectra (White, Gaussian, Linear, Ohmic).

Assumptions / limitations. The noise is treated as a classical stochastic process (valid for high-temperature bosonic baths), the dephasing is pure (no X or Y components), and the analysis assumes the long-time and large-N limits.

Figures summary. Figure 4 shows metrological gain as a function of N for various noise combinations; Figure 8 displays scaled uncertainty, metrological gain, and optimal interrogation time for GHZ states across different noise spectra.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of sensing under correlated noise, defines the Hamiltonian and noise models, derives a fundamental sensitivity limit based on SNR, analyzes specific scaling laws for different noise spectra (White, Gaussian, Linear, Ohmic), compares different entangled states (OAT, TAT, GHZ), and concludes with optimization results.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems and quantum metrology because it identifies specific noise-correlation regimes where entanglement-enhanced sensing can bypass the Standard Quantum Limit, providing a roadmap for designing robust quantum sensors.

Abstract

Entanglement has been proposed as a means to improve the sensitivity of sensing weak signals. While the degree of this quantum advantage is well understood in noiseless settings, the situation is more complex under realistic conditions, where the system is subject to decoherence. In this case, the enhancement depends on the specific noise characteristics. Previous treatments of colored noise typically assume that the noise is uncorrelated between successive experiments. Here, we consider the scenario in which the noise exhibits correlations across multiple shots. We derive a simple fundamental limit to the sensitivity based on the fact that the sensitivity cannot be better than the signal-to-noise ratio seen by the probe. Focusing on Ramsey spectroscopy with probes affected by pure classical dephasing, we show that, for suitable spatial and temporal noise correlations, entangled probes achieve a better scaling of the sensitivity with the number of probes than separable states. This demonstrates that entanglement can provide a substantial improvement for Ramsey spectroscopy subject to correlated noise.

Uncovering long-lived relaxation channel and exciton-phonon coupling in \textrm{Ta\textsubscript{2}NiSe\textsubscript{5}} via non-degenerate pump-probe spectroscopy

Authors: Poulami Ghosh, Anupama Chauhan, Sidhanta Sahu, Sk Kalimuddin, Mintu Mondal, N. Kamaraju
Type: experiment · Category: strongly correlated electrons · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22218v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · correlated cavity matter 2/5 · driven-dissipative phase transition 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study aims to understand the hierarchy of relaxation pathways and the role of exciton-phonon coupling in the excitonic insulator Ta2NiSe5 (TNSe), specifically investigating the nature of the long-lived relaxation channel.

Main result. The researchers discovered an unusually long-lived relaxation channel (280-600 ps) attributed to an exciton-phonon bottleneck and identified two distinct coherent phonon modes, one of which (1.0 THz) is strongly coupled to the excitonic order parameter.

Method. Temperature-dependent, non-degenerate optical pump-probe spectroscopy with an extended temporal window up to 500 ps was used to monitor transient reflectivity.

Summary. This paper investigates the ultrafast dynamics of the excitonic insulator Ta2NiSe5 using extended-window pump-probe spectroscopy. It reveals a previously hidden, long-lived relaxation channel lasting up to 600 ps, which is caused by a bottleneck between excitons and nonequilibrium phonons. The study also distinguishes between phonon modes that couple to the excitonic order parameter and those that are purely structural. These findings provide a clearer picture of how electronic and lattice degrees of freedom interact during a phase transition.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The physical system is Ta2NiSe5 (TNSe), a layered narrow-gap semiconductor exhibiting a concomitant excitonic-insulator and structural phase transition at Tc approx 327 K. The dynamics are analyzed using a modified Rothwarf-Taylor model and an exciton-phonon bottleneck framework.

Key observables. Transient differential reflectivity (delta R/R), relaxation time constants (tau1, tau2), coherent phonon frequencies and amplitudes, and the Fano asymmetry parameter.

Important parameters / regimes. Transition temperature Tc approx 327 K, pump wavelength 3.14 eV, probe wavelength 1.57 eV, and relaxation scales ranging from sub-picosecond to 600 ps.

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes the electronic background can be modeled by a multi-exponential decay and that the phonon dynamics can be described by cubic anharmonic decay or Fano-like interference.

Figures summary. Fig 1 shows the schematic of the excitonic insulator transition, resistivity anomalies, and crystal structures. Fig 2 shows temperature-dependent transient reflectivity. Fig 5 shows the temperature dependence of phonon amplitudes, frequencies, and linewidths.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the excitonic insulator phase in TNSe, describes the experimental setup and sample growth, presents the discovery of the slow relaxation channel, analyzes the temperature dependence of electronic and phonon modes, and concludes with the implications for exciton-phonon dynamics.

Why it may be interesting. The discovery of a long-lived exciton-phonon bottleneck and the observation of Higgs-like mode coupling to the condensate are highly relevant to many-body dynamics and the study of non-equilibrium phase transitions.

Abstract

An excitonic insulator represents a quantum phase in which spontaneous condensation of excitons leads to novel many-body phenomena. Ta$_2$NSi$_5$ (TNSe), a layered narrow-gap semiconductor, has emerged as a model platform to probe these correlated excitonic phases and their underlying dynamics below 327 K. In this work, we investigate the nonequilibrium dynamics of TNSe using temperature-dependent, non-degenerate optical pump-probe spectroscopy with a 3.14 eV pump and a 1.57 eV probe, extending the accessible pump-probe delay window up to 500 ps. In addition to the well-established sub-picosecond relaxation channel ($\sim$ 0.7- 0.9 ps) associated with carrier cooling and recombination, accompanied by exciton reformation, we uncover a much slower recovery process with a decay time of $\sim$ 280-600~ps, significantly longer than previously reported. We attribute this unusually prolonged recovery to enhanced scattering between excitons and nonequilibrium phonons, which delays the re-establishment of equilibrium excitonic correlations. On top of this bi-exponential background, we observe two coherent phonon modes at 1.0 and 2.9 THz with distinctly different coupling behaviors. The 1.0 THz mode exhibits an order-parameter-like temperature dependence, consistent with strong coupling to the excitonic condensate in TNSe. In contrast, the 2.9 THz mode does not exhibit any discernible coupling to the excitonic order parameter, and appears to arise from anharmonic lattice dynamics associated with the structural phase transition. Together, these results elucidate the hierarchy of relaxation pathways in TNSe and highlight the importance of extending the temporal detection window in pump-probe measurements to fully capture long-lived exciton-phonon dynamics.

Abstract

Abstract

Joint measurements on multiple copies of a quantum state provide access to nonlinear observables such as $\operatorname{tr}(ρ^t)$, but whether replica number marks a sharp information-theoretic resource boundary has remained unclear. Coherent replica number therefore acts as a genuinely discrete resource for nonlinear quantum-state estimation.
