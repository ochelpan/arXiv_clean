⭐ Entanglement and information scrambling in long-range measurement-only circuits

Highlighted author(s): Susanne F. Yelin
Authors: Abigail McClain Gomez, Fiona Abney-McPeek, Hong-Ye Hu, Susanne F. Yelin, Ceren B. Dağ
Type: theory · Category: statistical mechanics · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22022v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 measurement-induced transitions 5/5 · 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · 🔥 driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium dynamics 3/5 · 🔥 non-equilibrium universality 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · dissipative systems 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Investigating how the range, density, and structure of measurements jointly shape entanglement, information scrambling, and purification in measurement-only circuits.

Main result. The study identifies a rich phase diagram including a novel phase with volume-law entanglement but no scrambling, and establishes a theoretical mapping between the circuit's steady state and a long-range XX Hamiltonian.

Method. Large-scale Clifford stabilizer simulations combined with a replica-based method that maps the 1+1D circuit to a 2D statistical mechanics model.

Summary. This paper explores the entanglement and information dynamics in 1D measurement-only circuits with long-range interactions. By using Clifford simulations and a statistical mechanics mapping, the authors uncover various phases characterized by different entanglement scaling and scrambling properties. Notably, they find a regime in dense, single-basis circuits where volume-law entanglement exists without information scrambling, alongside rapid purification. This discovery suggests a promising method for the efficient preparation of robust, highly entangled quantum resource states.

Detailed structure

Model / system. One-dimensional measurement-only Clifford circuits with two-qubit parity checks (XX, YY, ZZ) featuring power-law decaying interaction ranges.

Key observables. Entanglement entropy (volume-law, sub-volume law, area-law), tripartite mutual information (scrambling diagnostic), mutual information, and purification timescale.

Important parameters / regimes. Measurement range (alpha), measurement density per layer, and measurement basis structure (random-basis vs. single-basis).

Assumptions / limitations. Single-qubit Clifford gates are approximated by Haar-averaged unitaries; periodic boundary conditions are assumed.

Figures summary. Schematic phase diagrams for different protocols; a diagram illustrating the mapping to a 2D statistical mechanics model; and plots showing the spatial profiles of entanglement, mutual information, and tripartite mutual information.

Paper structure. The paper introduces measurement-only circuits, develops a statistical mechanics mapping for random-basis models, presents phase diagrams for both random and single-basis protocols, explores the effective XX Hamiltonian connection, and discusses the implications for state preparation.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a deep connection between measurement-induced phase transitions and classical statistical mechanics, while identifying a regime for efficient, non-scrambling preparation of highly entangled states.

Abstract

Measurement-only circuits provide a minimal setting in which repeated local projections can either generate or suppress many-body entanglement, giving rise to measurement-induced phase transitions and dynamical regimes, that might have no unitary counterpart. Here we investigate entanglement and information transitions in one-dimensional measurement-only Clifford circuits with long-range two-qubit parity checks. By tuning both the measurement range and density per layer, we uncover a broad set of phases whose classification requires probes beyond entanglement entropy, such as mutual information, tripartite mutual information, purification from an ancilla, and Bell-cluster statistics. We map phase diagrams using large-scale Clifford simulations for two protocols: a random-basis design in which each measurement is randomly chosen from $\lbrace XX,YY,ZZ \rbrace$, and a single-basis design in which the basis is fixed within each layer but varies between layers, hence introducing more structure to the circuit. We map the trajectory-averaged entanglement entropy to a two-dimensional statistical mechanics model by extending a replica-based method to random-basis measurement-only circuits, and show that a continuous-time limit yields an effective long-range XX hamiltonian in the steady state. This connection links the observed volume-law to sub-volume-law entanglement transition to the boundary between a continuous symmetry broken phase and a critical XY phase. Strikingly, in structured (single-basis) circuits we find a phase in which volume-law and long-range entanglement coexists with rapid, size-independent purification of an ancilla qubit, and the absence of scrambling, highlighting measurement-only circuits as a promising route to efficiently preparing highly entangled and technologically useful quantum states.

Main problem. The paper investigates the extension of active matter concepts to the quantum regime, specifically focusing on the emergence and robustness of anomalous power-law scaling in the Mean-Squared Displacement (MSD).

Main result. The authors identify specific conditions for $t^6$ and $t^7$ anomalous scaling regimes in quantum active matter and demonstrate that these behaviors are robust against different initial quantum states.

Method. A hybrid Wigner phase-space framework is developed, utilizing a hybrid Wigner master equation and the Moyal product to map quantum dynamics onto a quasiclassical Langevin equation.

Summary. This paper explores how the principles of active matter—systems driven out of equilibrium by local energy influx—apply to quantum systems. By using a Wigner phase-space approach, the authors show that quantum active matter can exhibit much faster, anomalous diffusion (scaling as $t^6$ or $t^7$) than classical active matter. They demonstrate that these high-order power-law growths are robust and depend on the interplay between quantum fluctuations and the persistence of the active noise. This work establishes a new framework for studying non-equilibrium dynamics in quantum-controlled environments.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A quantum harmonic oscillator coupled to an Active Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process (AOUP), where the trap center follows a classical stochastic trajectory driven by active noise.

Key observables. Mean-Squared Displacement (MSD) and two-time correlation functions.

Important parameters / regimes. Persistence time ($ au$), activity strength ($D_u$), harmonic frequency ($\omega$), dissipation/pumping rates ($ u_-, u_+$), and the squeezing parameter ($r$).

Assumptions / limitations. The mapping to a Langevin equation assumes the Wigner master equation is quadratic in derivatives and the initial condition is Gaussian; the $t^7$ scaling is found under specific initial conditions.

Figures summary. Figure 2 shows MSD trajectories for different persistence times; Figure 3 demonstrates $t^6$ scaling; Figure 7 compares different MSD definitions to show stability; Figure 8 compares three different numerical/analytical methods for calculating MSD; Figure 9 compares exact MSD results with asymptotic forms.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of quantum active matter, develops a hybrid Wigner phase-space framework, derives the master equation and its quasiclassical Langevin mapping, analyzes specific scaling regimes ($t^6, t^7$) and oscillatory dynamics, examines the impact of initial state squeezing, and provides mathematical proofs for the Moyal product and expectation value equivalence in the appendices.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a rigorous theoretical bridge between classical active matter and open quantum systems, offering new insights into how non-equilibrium driving can lead to extreme anomalous diffusion in quantum-controlled systems like optical tweezers.

Abstract

Active matter is driven out of equilibrium by a local influx of energy. While classical active matter has been extensively studied, the extension of active matter concepts to quantum systems has been explored far less. In this work we develop a full quantum description based on the Wigner function. By introducing a hybrid Wigner master equation that incorporates classical active motion and quantum degrees of freedom, we compute the quantum mean-squared displacement (MSD) using established techniques from classical active matter. We analytically derive the time dependence of the MSD and clarify the conditions under which the characteristic scaling with time $\mathrm{MSD}\sim t^{6}$ emerges. We further show that, for certain parameter and initial conditions, the MSD can exhibit an even steeper scaling regime $\mathrm{MSD}\sim t^{7}$, and we examine the robustness of these behaviors against quantum fluctuations of the initial state.

Main problem. Developing an accurate thermometry protocol to determine temperature and entropy in Rydberg atom tweezer arrays, specifically for frustrated Kagome lattice systems where traditional numerical methods like QMC suffer from the sign problem.

Main result. The study estimates a temperature of T=0.55J and entropy of S/N=0.67 ln2, concluding that current experimental temperatures are too high to reach the predicted quantum spin liquid regime.

Method. A combination of experimental measurements of correlations and local susceptibility with a theoretical Dynamic High-Temperature Expansion (Dyn-HTE) up to 8th order.

Summary. This paper proposes a new method for measuring temperature and entropy in Rydberg atom quantum simulators. By combining experimental spin correlations with a high-temperature expansion theory, the authors analyze a Kagome lattice experiment. They find that the current experimental temperature is significantly higher than what is needed to observe quantum spin liquid physics. This work provides a way to quantify how close experimental platforms are to highly entangled quantum phases.

Detailed structure

Model / system. An anti-ferromagnetic dipolar spin-1/2 XY model realized on a 2D Kagome lattice of 114 Rubidium-87 atoms in Rydberg tweezer arrays.

Key observables. Nearest-neighbor equal-time zz correlator (Czz1), local zz spin susceptibility (zz0), and distance-resolved static zz susceptibility (zzd).

Important parameters / regimes. Coupling strength J approx 2pi x 0.77 MHz, nearest-neighbor distance a = 12 micrometers, and interaction decay 1/r^3.

Figures summary. Fig 2 shows experimental correlators and susceptibilities compared to theoretical predictions; Fig 3 displays entropy per spin as a function of temperature; Fig 4 shows convergence tests for interaction truncation; Fig 5 illustrates the diagrammatic expansion graphs.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the thermometry challenge, describes the Kagome Rydberg platform and XY model, details the Dyn-HTE method and expansion orders, presents temperature and entropy results, and concludes with implications for reaching the QSL regime.

Why it may be interesting. It provides a critical diagnostic tool (thermometry) for Rydberg simulators and addresses the fundamental difficulty of characterizing thermalization and entropy in frustrated quantum many-body systems.

Abstract

We propose an accurate thermometry approach for Rydberg atom tweezer arrays combining data from correlation and local susceptibility measurements with a theoretical high-temperature expansion method for dynamic spin correlations. We apply our approach to a recent quantum simulation experiment [Bornet et al., arXiv 2602.14323] realizing an anti-ferromagnetic dipolar spin-1/2 XY model on the Kagome lattice. We obtain T=0.55J and S/N=0.67 ln2 for temperature and entropy respectively, showing that further experimental efforts are required to reach the putative quantum spin liquid regime.

Decohered color code and emerging mixed toric code by anyon proliferation: Topological entanglement negativity perspective

Authors: Keisuke Kataoka, Yoshihito Kuno, Takahiro Orito, Ikuo Ichinose
Type: theory · Category: quantum information and computing · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22521v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 dissipative systems 4/5 · 🔥 entanglement & information structure 4/5 · 🔥 driven-dissipative phase transition 3/5 · correlated / nonlocal dissipation 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · non-equilibrium universality 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. The study investigates how local decoherence transforms a color code into an intrinsic mixed-state topological order (imTO) and seeks to characterize this transition using entanglement diagnostics.

Main result. The authors demonstrate that the decohered color code emerges as a mixed toric code, characterized by a smooth crossover in topological entanglement negativity (TEN) from 2 ln 2 to ln 2, and show that TEN is a stable, shape-independent observable for such transitions.

Method. The researchers use the stabilizer formalism and the Gottesman-Aaronson algorithm for large-scale numerical simulations, combined with an analytical 'gauging out' procedure to identify the emergent anyon theory.

Summary. This paper explores how local noise affects the topological properties of the color code. It shows that decoherence leads to the emergence of a mixed-state toric code through the proliferation of anyons. By using topological entanglement negativity (TEN), the authors demonstrate that this transition is a smooth crossover rather than a sharp phase transition. They also prove that TEN is a robust and stable measure for tracking topological evolution in noisy systems.

Detailed structure

Model / system. The system is a 2D color code on a honeycomb lattice on a torus, subjected to a stochastic XX-type decoherence channel acting on the red links of the lattice.

Key observables. Topological entanglement negativity (TEN), entanglement negativity, logarithmic purity, and anyon content/proliferation.

Important parameters / regimes. Decoherence strength (p), system size (Lx, Ly), and subsystem geometry (hexagonal partitions).

Assumptions / limitations. The analysis assumes an Abelian theory for defining the quotient group and focuses on the specific XX-type decoherence model.

Figures summary. Figures illustrate the honeycomb lattice and color scheme, the process of anyon creation via decoherence, the emergence of the triangular lattice, and numerical plots showing the smooth decrease of TEN and the peak in its variance during the crossover.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the color code decoherence model, analytically describes the emergence of the mixed toric code via anyon proliferation, presents numerical simulations of the transition using TEN, and concludes with a stability analysis of the TEN observable.

Why it may be interesting. This paper is highly relevant for researchers in open quantum systems as it provides a new diagnostic tool (TEN) to identify and characterize topological order in the presence of noise, specifically studying the transition from pure to mixed-state topological order.

Abstract

We study how the color code under decoherence gives rise to an intrinsic mixed-state topological order (imTO), which has no counterpart in pure ground states of local gapped Hamiltonians. For decoherence induced by XX-type operators on red links of the honeycomb lattice, we show that the resulting mixed state inherits half of the topological properties of the color code, including anyon content, logical operators, and topological entanglement structure. Using a gauging procedure for mixed stabilizer states, we identify the emergent phase as closely related to a single toric code. We characterize this phase by topological entanglement negativity (TEN) and perform efficient stabilizer-formalism simulations. While the pure color code has ${\rm TEN} = 2 \ln 2$, the maximally decohered state has ${\rm TEN} = \ln 2$, indicating emergence of a single toric code. By tuning the decoherence strength, we find a smooth crossover in TEN accompanied by a pronounced, nearly system-size-independent peak in its variance. We further show that the negativity exhibits characteristic scaling only for subsystem partitions commensurate with the triangular lattice of the emergent toric code. Our results demonstrate that negativity-based quantities provide powerful probes of mixed-state topological order generated by decoherence.

Quantum limits on squeezing

Authors: Xin Zhou, Francesco Massel
Type: theory · Category: other · PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22500v1
Analysis basis: full PDF text, analyzed in chunks Topic relevance: 🔥 dissipative systems 4/5 · 🔥 correlated / nonlocal dissipation 3/5 · 🔥 entanglement & information structure 3/5 · Tavis-Cummings & cavity-many-emitter 2/5 · methods for driven-dissipative 2/5 · non-equilibrium dynamics 2/5 · quantum measurements 2/5

↔ Scroll figures horizontally

Main problem. Determining the fundamental quantum limits on the amount of squeezing achievable in bosonic networks subject to canonical commutation relations and stability constraints.

Main result. The authors establish that for purely dissipative two-mode squeezing, the sum of normalized quadrature variances is bounded below by 1, but this bound can approach 1/2 when local parametric driving is added.

Method. The study uses input-output theory, Bogoliubov transformations, and the Lyapunov equation to analyze the 'commutator budget' and noise-gain balance in linear time-invariant quantum systems.

Summary. This paper investigates the fundamental limits on how much squeezing can be achieved in networks of bosonic modes. It demonstrates that the canonical commutation relations and system stability impose a strict 'noise budget' that prevents infinite squeezing. While purely dissipative schemes have a lower bound of 1 for normalized squeezing power, adding parametric driving can lower this limit toward 1/2. These findings are directly applicable to improving the performance of electromechanical and nanomechanical quantum sensors.

Detailed structure

Model / system. A network of N bosonic modes (such as cavity optomechanics or electromechanics) coupled to external Markovian baths, including specific models for two-mode and three-mode systems.

Key observables. Steady-state quadrature variances, squeezing power (sum of normalized mode-optimal quadrature variances), and the Duan inseparability criterion.

Important parameters / regimes. Strong-coupling limit, stability region, transfer parameter (eta_e), and the presence of degenerate parametric driving.

Assumptions / limitations. The framework assumes Markovian baths, linear time-invariant dynamics, and the rotating-wave approximation (RWA) for the three-mode description.

Figures summary. Figure 1 shows squeezing power as a function of coupling and parameters; Figure 2 shows squeezing power as a function of the difference in parametric driving strengths.

Paper structure. The paper introduces the problem of squeezing limits, develops a mathematical framework based on commutation relations, analyzes two-mode and three-mode systems, and discusses experimental implications.

Why it may be interesting. It provides fundamental, model-independent bounds on squeezing and entanglement based on the algebraic structure of quantum mechanics, which is highly relevant to the control of open quantum systems and optomechanical networks.

Abstract

In our work, we show how, for a network of bosonic modes, canonical commutation relations constrain the coefficients relating input and internal modes. Based on these constraints, we derive a lower bound on the total steady-state squeezing achievable in reservoir-engineered (dissipative) squeezing schemes, quantified by the sum of mode-optimal quadrature variances normalized to its corresponding input variance. The bound follows solely from canonical commutation relations and stability, and is saturated in the strong-coupling limit at 1. Furthermore, we show that adding independent parametric driving terms for each mode changes the quantum noise-gain balance and yields a distinct optimum bound, approaching 1/2. In addition, we show how these constraints allow us to reformulate the Duan inseparability criterion for a three-mode bosonic system in terms of a single parameter-dependent figure of merit. Our results apply directly to current electromechanical and nanomechanical experiments and indicate that the two-mode bounds can be experimentally approached even at room temperature.
